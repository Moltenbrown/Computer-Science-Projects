<p><b>Enlightened absolutism</b> (also called <b>enlightened <a href="/wiki/Despotism" title="Despotism">despotism</a></b> or <b>benevolent despotism</b>) refers to the conduct and policies of European <a href="/wiki/Absolute_monarchy" title="Absolute monarchy">absolute monarchs</a> during the 18th and 19th centuries who were influenced by the ideas of the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a>, espousing them to enhance their power.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPerryChaseJacob2015442_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPerryChaseJacob2015442-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The concept originated during the Enlightenment period in the <a href="/wiki/18th_century" title="18th century">18th</a> and into the early <a href="/wiki/19th_century" title="19th century">19th centuries</a>.
</p><p>An enlightened <a href="/wiki/Autocracy" title="Autocracy">absolutist</a> is a non-<a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democratic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Authoritarian" class="mw-redirect" title="Authoritarian">authoritarian</a> leader who exercises their political power based upon the principles of the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a>. 
</p><p>Enlightened monarchs distinguished themselves from ordinary rulers by claiming to rule for their subjects' well-being. <a href="/wiki/John_Stuart_Mill" title="John Stuart Mill">John Stuart Mill</a> stated that despotism is a legitimate mode of government in dealing with barbarians, provided the end be their improvement.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMill198913_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMill198913-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Enlightened absolutists' beliefs about royal power were typically similar to those of regular despots, both believing that they were destined to rule. Enlightened rulers may have played a part in the abolition of <a href="/wiki/Serfdom" title="Serfdom">serfdom</a> in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The enlightened despot <a href="/wiki/Joseph_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Joseph II, Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor Joseph II</a> of Austria summarized, "Everything for the people, nothing by the people".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enlightened_absolutism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Enlightened absolutism is the theme of an essay by <a href="/wiki/Frederick_the_Great" title="Frederick the Great">Frederick the Great</a>, who ruled <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a> from 1740 to 1786, defending this system of government.<sup id="cite_ref-Kramnick1995_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kramnick1995-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> When the prominent French Enlightenment philosopher <a href="/wiki/Voltaire" title="Voltaire">Voltaire</a> fell out of favor in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, he eagerly accepted Frederick's invitation to live at his palace. He believed that an enlightened monarchy was the only real way for society to advance. Frederick was an enthusiast of French ideas. Frederick explained: "My principal occupation is to combat ignorance and prejudice ... to enlighten minds, cultivate morality, and to make people as happy as it suits human nature, and as the means at my disposal permit."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Enlightened absolutists held that royal power emanated not from <a href="/wiki/Divine_right_of_kings" title="Divine right of kings">divine right</a> but from a <a href="/wiki/Social_contract" title="Social contract">social contract</a> whereby a despot was entrusted with the power to govern through a social contract in lieu of any other governments. The monarchs of enlightened absolutism strengthened their authority by improving the lives of their subjects. This philosophy implied that the sovereign knew that the interests of his or her subjects better than they themselves did. The monarch taking responsibility for the subjects precluded their political participation.
</p><p>The difference between an <a href="/wiki/Absolutism_(European_history)" class="mw-redirect" title="Absolutism (European history)">absolutist</a> and an <i>enlightened</i> absolutist is based on a broad analysis of the degree to which they embraced the Age of Enlightenment. Historians debate the actual implementation of enlightened absolutism. They distinguish between the "enlightenment" of the ruler personally, versus that of his or her regime. For example, Frederick the Great was tutored in the ideas of the French Enlightenment in his youth, and maintained those ideas in his private life as an adult, but in many ways was unable or unwilling to effect enlightened reforms in practice.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Other rulers such as the <a href="/wiki/Sebasti%C3%A3o_Jos%C3%A9_de_Carvalho_e_Melo" class="mw-redirect" title="Sebastião José de Carvalho e Melo">Marquis of Pombal</a>, prime minister of <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, used the ideas and practices of the Enlightenment not only to achieve reforms but also to enhance <a href="/wiki/Autocracy" title="Autocracy">autocracy</a>, crush opposition, suppress criticism, advance colonial economic exploitation, and consolidate personal control and profit.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The concept of enlightened absolutism was formally described by the German historian <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Georg_Friedrich_Roscher" title="Wilhelm Georg Friedrich Roscher">Wilhelm Roscher</a> in 1847<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and remains controversial among scholars.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Centralized control necessitated centralized systematic information on the nation. A major renovation was the collection, use and interpretation of numerical and statistical data, ranging from trade statistics, harvest reports, death notices to population censuses. Starting in the 1760s, officials in France and Germany began increasingly to rely on quantitative data for systematic planning, especially regarding long-term economic growth. It combined the utilitarian agenda of "enlightened absolutism" with the new ideas being developed in  economics. In Germany and France, the trend was especially strong in <a href="/wiki/Cameralism" title="Cameralism">Cameralism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physiocracy" title="Physiocracy">Physiocracy</a>. <sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_nations">Major nations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enlightened_absolutism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Major nations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Governmental responses to the Age of Enlightenment varied widely. In several nations with powerful rulers, called "enlightened despots" by historians, leaders of the Enlightenment were welcomed at Court and helped design laws and programs to reform the system, typically to build stronger national states.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  Government responses to the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Age of Enlightenment</a> varied widely. In France the government was hostile, and the philosophers fought against its censorship.  The British government generally  ignored the Enlightenment's leaders.  
</p><p>However in several nations with powerful rulers--called "enlightened despots" by historians--leaders of the Enlightenment were welcomed at Court and helped design laws and programs to reform the system, typically to build stronger national states.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Frederick_the_Great" title="Frederick the Great">Frederick the Great</a>, who ruled Prussia 1740–1786, was an enthusiast for French ideas (he ridiculed German culture and was unaware of the remarkable advances it was undergoing). Voltaire, who had been imprisoned and maltreated by the French government, was eager to accept Frederick's invitation to live at his palace.  Frederick explained, "My principal occupation is to combat ignorance and prejudice ... to enlighten minds, cultivate morality, and to  make people as happy as it suits human nature, and as the means at my disposal permit.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  He wrote an essay on "Benevolent Despotism" defending this system of government.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Tsarina  <a href="/wiki/Catherine_the_Great" title="Catherine the Great">Catherine II of Russia</a> sponsored the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Enlightenment" title="Russian Enlightenment">Russian Enlightenment</a>. She incorporated many ideas of Enlightenment philosophers, especially <a href="/wiki/Charles_de_Secondat,_Baron_de_Montesquieu" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles de Secondat, Baron de Montesquieu">Montesquieu</a>, in her <a href="/wiki/Nakaz" title="Nakaz">Nakaz</a>, which was intended to revise Russian law.  However inviting the famous French philosopher <a href="/wiki/Denis_Diderot" title="Denis Diderot">Denis Diderot</a> to her court worked out poorly.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Charles_III_of_Spain" title="Charles III of Spain">Charles III</a>, king of Spain from 1759 to 1788, tried to rescue his empire from decay through far-reaching reforms such as weakening the Church and its monasteries, promoting science and university research, facilitating trade and commerce, modernizing agriculture and avoiding wars. The centralization of power in Madrid angered the local nobility, and challenge the traditional autonomy of cities, so that resistance grew steadily. Consequently, Spain relapsed after his death.  <sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>   
</p><p>Emperor <a href="/wiki/Joseph_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Joseph II, Holy Roman Emperor">Joseph II</a>, ruler of Austria 1780-1790, was over-enthusiastic, announcing so many reforms that had so little support  that revolts broke out and his regime became a comedy of errors.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>  
</p><p>In some countries the initiative came not from rulers but from senior officials such as the <a href="/wiki/Sebasti%C3%A3o_Jos%C3%A9_de_Carvalho_e_Melo,_1st_Marquess_of_Pombal" class="mw-redirect" title="Sebastião José de Carvalho e Melo, 1st Marquess of Pombal">Marquis of Pombal</a>, who was <a href="/wiki/Joseph_I_of_Portugal" title="Joseph I of Portugal">Joseph I of Portugal</a>'s Secretary of State.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>  For a brief period in Denmark <a href="/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Struensee" title="Johann Friedrich Struensee">Johann Friedrich Struensee</a> attempted to govern in terms of Enlightenment principles.  After issuing 1069 decrees in 13 months covering many major reforms, his enemies overthrew him and he was decapitated and quartered.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Leaders such as <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_Bonaparte" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleon Bonaparte">Napoleon Bonaparte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fidel_Castro" title="Fidel Castro">Fidel Castro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a> (at least until the <a href="/wiki/Second_Italo-Abyssinian_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second Italo-Abyssinian War">war against Ethiopia</a>), <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_Salazar" class="mw-redirect" title="António Salazar">António Salazar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Joseph Stalin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isaias_Afwerki" title="Isaias Afwerki">Isaias Afwerki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mustafa_Kemal_Atat%C3%BCrk" title="Mustafa Kemal Atatürk">Mustafa Kemal Atatürk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lee_Kuan_Yew" title="Lee Kuan Yew">Lee Kuan Yew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">Mao Zedong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pervez_Musharraf" title="Pervez Musharraf">Pervez Musharraf</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Medici" class="mw-redirect" title="Medici">Medici dynasty</a> have been given the title. Long-seated <a href="/wiki/Dictators" class="mw-redirect" title="Dictators">dictators</a> are more likely to be regarded as enlightened because they acknowledge public interest in order to remain in power and to be regarded as <a href="/wiki/Legitimacy_(political)" title="Legitimacy (political)">legitimate</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In Spanish the word <i><a href="/wiki/Dictablanda" title="Dictablanda">dictablanda</a></i> is sometimes used for a <a href="/wiki/Dictatorship" title="Dictatorship">dictatorship</a> that preserves some of the liberties and mechanisms of democracy.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chinese_Legalism">Chinese Legalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enlightened_absolutism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chinese Legalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><p>
Xuezhi Guo contrasts the <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucian</a> ideal of a "humane ruler" (renjun) with the ideal of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Legalism" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese Legalism">Chinese Legalists</a>, who he says "intended to create a truly 'enlightened ruler' (<i>mingjun</i>) who is able to effectively rule the masses and control his bureaucracy"; this ruler would be a "skilful manipulator and successful politician who uses means or 'technique' in achieving self-protection and political control." Guo quotes <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_I._Schwartz" title="Benjamin I. Schwartz">Benjamin I. Schwartz</a> as describing the features of "a truly Legalist 'enlightened ruler<span style="padding-right:.15em;">'</span>":<sup id="cite_ref-:0_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup></p><blockquote><p>He must be anything but an arbitrary despot if one means by a despot a tyrant who follows all his impulses, whims and passions. Once the systems which maintain the entire structure are in place, he must not interfere with their operation. He may use the entire system as a means to the achievement of his national and international ambitions, but to do so he must not disrupt its impersonal workings. He must at all times be able to maintain an iron wall between his private life and public role. Concubines, friends, flatterers and charismatic saints must have no influence whatsoever on the course of policy, and he must never relax his suspicions of the motives of those who surround him.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
