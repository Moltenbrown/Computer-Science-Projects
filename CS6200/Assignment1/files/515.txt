	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Uncle</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Uncle_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Uncle (disambiguation)">Uncle (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Uncle</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <i lang="la">avunculus</i> the diminutive of <i>avus</i> "grandfather") is a male <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">family</a> relationship or <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">kinship</a> within an extended or immediate family. Uncles are <a href="/wiki/Second-degree_relative" title="Second-degree relative">second-degree relatives</a> and share 25% genetic overlap when they are the full brother of one of the biological parents. An uncle is the <a href="/wiki/Brother" title="Brother">brother</a> or a friend of a parent. A half-uncle is the half-brother of one's parent. "Uncle-in-law" can refer to the husband of one's aunt or uncle of one's spouse. A biological uncle is a second degree male relative and shares 25% genetic overlap. However people who are not a biological uncle are sometimes affectionately called as an uncle as a title of admiration and respect. The co-uncle-in-law is the husband of one's aunt of one's spouse.
</p><p>The feminine counterpart of an uncle is an <a href="/wiki/Aunt" title="Aunt">aunt</a>, and the reciprocal relationship is that of a <a href="/wiki/Nephew_and_niece" class="mw-redirect" title="Nephew and niece">nephew or niece</a>.
</p><p>A <b>grand-uncle</b><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>/<b>granduncle</b><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>/<b>great-uncle</b><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> is the brother of one's grandparent. Half-great-uncle/half-granduncle is the half-brother of one's grandparent. Great-uncle-in-law/granduncle-in-law is the husband of one's great-aunt/grandaunt. Great-uncle-in-law/granduncle-in-law and can also be great-uncle/granduncle of one's spouse. The co-great-uncle-in-law/co-granduncle-in-law is the husband of one's great-aunt/grandaunt of one's spouse.
</p><p>A great-great-uncle/great-granduncle is the brother of one's great-grandparent. Half-great-great-uncle/half-great-granduncle is the half-brother of one's great-grandparent. Great-great-uncle-in-law/great-granduncle-in-law is the husband of one's great-great-aunt/great-grandaunt. Great-great-uncle-in-law/great-granduncle-in-law and can also be great-great-uncle/great-granduncle of one's spouse. The co-great-great-uncle-in-law/co-great-granduncle-in-law is the husband of one's great-great-aunt/great-grandaunt of one's spouse.
</p><p>A cousin-uncle is the male cousin of one's parent. As they are in first degree relatives to your parents they become a second degree relative to you as you are the generation below your parents. They are called uncle simply because they lie a generation above you. Their child would be called in reference to yourself, your second cousin as they are on your generation level.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternate_uses">Alternate uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uncle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Alternate uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A male cousin of one's parent is sometimes addressed as "uncle" instead of "cousin" due to the age difference.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_variations">Cultural variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uncle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cultural variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In some cultures and families, children may refer to the cousins of their parents as "aunt" or "uncle". It is also a <a href="/wiki/Title_of_respect" class="mw-redirect" title="Title of respect">title of respect</a> for elders (for example older cousins, neighbours, acquaintances, close family friends, and even sometimes total strangers). Using the term in this way is a form of <a href="/wiki/Fictive_kinship" title="Fictive kinship">fictive kinship</a>.
Aboriginal Australians have adopted the terms ‘uncle’ and ‘aunty’ as prefixes or titles of great respect. An Aboriginal individual who is titled ’Uncle’ or ’Aunty’ to an audience beyond their community is considered an elder of the community and a person of great knowledge or importance.
</p>
<h3><span id="Albanian.2C_Slavic_and_Persian"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Albanian,_Slavic_and_Persian">Albanian, Slavic and Persian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uncle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Albanian, Slavic and Persian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In some cultures, like Albanian, Slavic, or Persian, no single inclusive term describing both a person's <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">kinship</a> to their parental male sibling or parental male in-law exists. Instead, there are specific terms describing a person's kinship to their mother's brother ("dajë" in <a href="/wiki/Albanian_language" title="Albanian language">Albanian</a> language, "daiyee" in <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>, "wuj" (diminutive: "wujek") in <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>) or a person's kinship to their father's brother ("xhajë" in Albanian, "amou" in Persian, "stryj" (diminutive: "stryjek") in <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>). An analogous differentiation exists using separate terms to describe a person's kinship to their mother's female sibling, ("teze" in Albanian, "khaleh" in Persian, "ciotka" (diminutive: "ciocia") in <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>), and a person's kinship to their father's female sibling, ("hallë" in Albanian, "ammeh" in Persian, "stryjna" (diminutive: "stryjenka") in <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>).
</p><p>Furthermore, in Persian culture the terms used to describe a person's kinship to their maternal or paternal in-laws bear clear and unambiguous descriptions of that relationship, differentiating the parental in-laws from blood-relatives. For example, there is a specific term describing a person's kinship to the spouse of their paternal uncle (i.e. "zan-amou", literally 'wife-of-' amou). This clarifies that kinship is to the spouse of the person's paternal male sibling, as opposed to a blood-relationship.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Irish">Irish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uncle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Irish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Uncles and aunts are considered important in modern Irish culture and are usually chosen to be <a href="/wiki/Godparent" title="Godparent">godfather or godmother</a> of children during <a href="/wiki/Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">baptism</a>. A young Irish person might seek the counsel of their favourite aunt or uncle before making an important decision, and the opinion of the respective aunt or uncle is treated seriously.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Asian">South Asian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uncle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: South Asian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, unambiguous names are used for various uncles such as father’s brother chacha (or kaka). If the brother of your father is elder than your father then he is called Tauji ( or bapuji). Your mother’s brother is called Mama. Your paternal aunt’s husband is called Fufa (or Fuva) and your maternal aunt’s husband is called Mausa (or Masa) in Hindi (or Gujarati).
</p><p>Likewise, in neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>, mother's brother is also Mama as well father's brother as Chacha. A paternal aunt's husband is Phupha and maternal aunt's husband is Khalu.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uncles_in_popular_culture">Uncles in popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uncle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Uncles in popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to the loving image of an old but wise and friendly uncle in many cultures the word has been used as a loving nickname for many people. In <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_mythology" title="Tibetan mythology">Tibetan mythology</a> <a href="/wiki/Akhu_T%C3%B6npa" title="Akhu Tönpa">Akhu Tönpa</a> (<i>Uncle Tompa</i>) is a familiar and well-beloved figure. The American national personification <a href="/wiki/Uncle_Sam" title="Uncle Sam">Uncle Sam</a> serves as an allegorical fatherly figure to many Americans. Various children's TV hosts have used <i>uncle</i> as their nickname, including <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney" title="Walt Disney">Walt Disney</a> (<i>Uncle Walt</i>), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bob_Davidse&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bob Davidse (page does not exist)">Bob Davidse</a> (<i>Nonkel Bob</i>, literally <i>Uncle Bob</i>), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwin_Rutten&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Edwin Rutten (page does not exist)">Edwin Rutten</a> (who hosted a children's show named <i>De Show van Ome Willem</i> (<i>The Show of Uncle Willem</i>). The Dutch poet Ome Ko also used <i>uncle</i> as part of his pseudonym.
</p><p>Rich, wise or otherwise eccentric uncles are also popular in works of fiction.
</p>
