<p><b>Hans</b> is a masculine <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a>. In German, Danish, Dutch, Faroese, Norwegian, Icelandic and Swedish, it was originally short for Hanselot but is now also recognized as a name in its own right for official purposes.
</p><p>The earliest documented usage was in 1356 in Sweden,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> 1360 in Norway,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and the 14th century in Denmark.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>"<a href="/wiki/Hansel_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Hansel (disambiguation)">Hansel</a>" (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">Hänsel</i>) is a variant, meaning "little Hans". Another variant with the same meaning is <i lang="de" title="German language text">Hänschen</i>, found in the German <a href="/wiki/Proverb" title="Proverb">proverb</a> "<i lang="de" title="German language text">Was Hänschen nicht lernt, lernt Hans nimmermehr</i>", which translates roughly as: "What Hansel doesn't learn, Hans will never learn".
</p><p>Other variants include: Han, Hawns, Hanns, Hannes, Hanse, Hansi (also female), Hansele, Hansal, Hensal, Hanserl, Hännschen, Hennes, Hännes, Hänneschen, Henning, Henner, Honsa, Johan, Johann, Jan, Jannes, Jo, Joha, Hanselmann, Hansje.
</p>
