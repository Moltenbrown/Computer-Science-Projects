<p><b>Pluralism</b> as a political philosophy is the recognition and affirmation of diversity within a political body, which permits the peaceful coexistence of different interests, convictions, and lifestyles.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>While not all political pluralists advocate for a <a href="/wiki/Pluralist_democracy" title="Pluralist democracy">pluralist democracy</a>, this is most common as <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democracy</a> is often viewed as the most fair and effective way to moderate between the discrete values.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As put by arch-pluralist <a href="/wiki/Isaiah_Berlin" title="Isaiah Berlin">Isaiah Berlin</a>, "let us have the courage of our admitted ignorance, of our doubts and uncertainties. At least we can try to discover what others [...] require, by [...] making it possible for ourselves to know men as they truly are, by listening to them carefully and sympathetically, and understanding them and their lives and their needs... ."<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Pluralism thus tries to encourage members of society to accommodate their differences by avoiding <a href="/wiki/Extremism" title="Extremism">extremism</a> (adhering solely to one value, or at the very least refusing to recognize others as legitimate) and engaging in <a href="/wiki/Good_faith" title="Good faith">good faith</a> dialogue. Pluralists also seek the construction or reform of social institutions in order to reflect and balance competing principles. 
</p><p>One of the more famous arguments for institutional pluralism came from <a href="/wiki/James_Madison" title="James Madison">James Madison</a> in <a href="/wiki/Federalist_No._10" title="Federalist No. 10"><i>The Federalist</i> paper number 10</a>. Madison feared that factionalism would lead to in-fighting in the new American republic and devotes this paper to questioning how best to avoid such an occurrence. He posits that to avoid factionalism, it is best to allow many competing factions (advocating different primary principles) to prevent any one from dominating the <a href="/wiki/Political_system" title="Political system">political system</a>. This relies, to a degree, on a series of disturbances changing the influences of groups so as to avoid institutional dominance and ensure competition.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Like <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Burke" title="Edmund Burke">Edmund Burke</a>, this view concerns itself with balance, and subordinating any single abstract principle to a plurality or realistic harmony of interests.
</p><p>Pluralism recognizes that certain conditions may make good faith negotiation impossible, and therefore also focuses on what institutional structures can best modify or prevent such a situation. Pluralism advocates institutional design in keeping with a form of pragmatic <a href="/wiki/Realism_(international_relations)" title="Realism (international relations)">realism</a> here, with the preliminary adoption of suitable existing socio-historical structures where necessary.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_common_good">The common good</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pluralism_(political_philosophy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The common good">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pluralism is connected with the hope that this process of conflict and <a href="/wiki/Dialogue" title="Dialogue">dialogue</a> will result in a quasi-<a href="/wiki/Common_good" title="Common good">common good</a>. This common good is not an abstract value or set in stone, however, but an attempt at balancing competing social interests, and will thus constantly shift given present social conditions. Proponents in contemporary political philosophy of such a view include <a href="/wiki/Isaiah_Berlin" title="Isaiah Berlin">Isaiah Berlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stuart_Hampshire" title="Stuart Hampshire">Stuart Hampshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Williams" title="Bernard Williams">Bernard Williams</a>. An earlier version of political pluralism was a strong current in the formation of modern <a href="/wiki/Social_democracy" title="Social democracy">social democracy</a> (to balance socialist and capitalist ideals), with theorists such as the early <a href="/wiki/Harold_Laski" title="Harold Laski">Harold Laski</a> and <a href="/wiki/G._D._H._Cole" title="G. D. H. Cole">G. D. H. Cole</a>, as well as other leading members of the British <a href="/wiki/Fabian_Society" title="Fabian Society">Fabian Society</a>. In the United States, President <a href="/wiki/Eisenhower" class="mw-redirect" title="Eisenhower">Eisenhower</a>'s "middle way" was arguably motivated by a belief in political pluralism.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While advocated by many pluralists, pluralism need not embrace social democracy given it does not <a href="/wiki/A_priori_knowledge" class="mw-redirect" title="A priori knowledge">a priori</a> assume a desirable political system. Rather, pluralists advocate one based on the pre-existing traditions and cognizable interests of a given society, and the political structure most likely to harmonize these factors. Thus, pluralists have also included <a href="/wiki/Michael_Oakeshott" title="Michael Oakeshott">Michael Oakeshott</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Kekes" title="John Kekes">John Kekes</a>, proponents of something close to <a href="/wiki/Liberal_conservatism" title="Liberal conservatism">liberal conservatism</a> (although will often reject such political labels). What pluralists certainly do have in common is the notion that a single vision or ideological schema, whether <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxism</a> or unbridled <a href="/wiki/Neoliberalism" title="Neoliberalism">neoliberalism</a>, is likely too simplistic and rigid to advocate human beings' natural plurality of values. Pluralists likewise reject <a href="/wiki/Historicism" title="Historicism">historicism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Utopian" class="mw-redirect" title="Utopian">utopian</a> thinking. While some, like <a href="/wiki/John_Gray_(philosopher)" title="John Gray (philosopher)">John N. Gray</a>, repudiate historical progress altogether, others, like <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Burke" title="Edmund Burke">Edmund Burke</a>, indicate that human progress has occurred, as a function of improved social harmony.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conditions">Conditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pluralism_(political_philosophy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Conditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For pluralism to function and to be successful in defining the common good, all groups have to agree to a minimal consensus that shared values are at least worth pursuing. The most important baseline value is thus that of mutual <a href="/wiki/Respect" title="Respect">respect</a> or <a href="/wiki/Toleration" title="Toleration">tolerance</a>. If no such dialogue is possible, <a href="/wiki/Extremism" title="Extremism">extremism</a> and physical coercion are likely inevitable.
</p>
