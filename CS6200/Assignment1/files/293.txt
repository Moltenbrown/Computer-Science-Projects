<p><b>Murray</b> (<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap;margin-right:.25em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Murray.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" decoding="async" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a></span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c8/Murray.ogg" class="internal" title="Murray.ogg">listen</a></span>&#160;<small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">(<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Media help"><span style="cursor:help;">help</span></a>·<a href="/wiki/File:Murray.ogg" title="File:Murray.ogg"><span style="cursor:help;">info</span></a>)</small></span>) is both a Scottish and an Irish surname with two distinct respective etymologies. The Scottish version is a common variation of the word <a href="/wiki/Moray" title="Moray">Moray</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Anglicisation_of_names#Non-English-language_areas_of_the_British_Isles" title="Anglicisation of names">anglicisation</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Irish_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Middle Irish language">Medieval Gaelic</a> word <i>Muireb</i> (or <i>Moreb</i>); the <i>b</i> here was pronounced as <i>v</i>, hence the <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Latinization</a> to <i>Moravia</i>. These names denote the district on the south shore of the <a href="/wiki/Moray_Firth" title="Moray Firth">Moray Firth</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. <i>Murray</i> is a direct <a href="/wiki/Transliteration" title="Transliteration">transliteration</a> of how Scottish people pronounce the word <i>Moray</i>. The <i>Murray</i> spelling is not used for the geographical area, which is <i>Moray</i>, but it became the commonest form of the <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a>, especially among <a href="/wiki/Scottish_emigrants" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish emigrants">Scottish emigrants</a>, to the extent that the surname <i>Murray</i> is now much more common than the original surname <i>Moray</i>. See also <a href="/wiki/Clan_Murray" title="Clan Murray">Clan Murray</a>.
</p><p>The Irish version derives from Ó <a href="/wiki/Muireadhaigh" title="Muireadhaigh">Muireadhaigh</a>. However, there were many Scottish Murray immigrants into Ireland during the Middle Ages and so a Murray of Irish heritage today could be descended from either source of the name.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_(surname)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A considerable number of present bearers of this surname are of Scottish origin, especially in Ulster. Possible etymologies are:
</p>
<ul><li>From <a href="/wiki/Moray" title="Moray">Moray</a> in northeast Scotland, which came from the Scottish Gaelic for "sea settlement".</li>
<li>As a native Irish of this name, from Mac Muireadhaigh or Ó Muireadhaigh "descendant of Muireadhach" or Mac Giolla Mhuire "descendant of the servant of the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Mary" class="mw-redirect" title="Virgin Mary">Virgin Mary</a>".</li></ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Motto" title="Motto">motto</a> for Murray is <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/imperio" class="extiw" title="wikt:imperio"><i>Imperio</i></a>. "Murrays" trace their heritage back to the 12th century and take their name from the province of Moray, once a local kingdom. It was during this time that the <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flemish</a> lords crossed the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> and established themselves in the <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scottish realm</a>. Among them was <a href="/wiki/Freskin" title="Freskin">Freskin</a>. It is possible that either Freskin or his son William intermarried with the ancient <a href="/wiki/Monarchy" title="Monarchy">royal</a> house of Moray. The senior line of the Murrays took the surname of <a href="/wiki/Sutherland_(surname)" title="Sutherland (surname)">Sutherland</a> and became <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Sutherland" title="Earl of Sutherland">Earls of Sutherland</a> by 1235.
</p><p>Thereafter, the chiefs of the Murrays were the Lords of Petty in Moray, who also became Lords of <a href="/wiki/Bothwell" title="Bothwell">Bothwell</a> in <a href="/wiki/Clydesdale" title="Clydesdale">Clydesdale</a> before 1253. An heir of this line, <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Moray" title="Andrew Moray">Sir Andrew Moray</a>, was the brilliant young general who led the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_people" title="Scottish people">Scots</a> in 1297 in their first uprising against English rule. He was mortally wounded while winning his famous victory at <a href="/wiki/Stirling_Bridge" class="mw-redirect" title="Stirling Bridge">Stirling Bridge</a>.
</p><p>His son, <a href="/wiki/Sir_Andrew_Murray" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Andrew Murray">Sir Andrew Murray</a>, 4th Lord of Bothwell, third <a href="/wiki/Regent" title="Regent">Regent</a> of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, married Christian Bruce, a sister of King <a href="/wiki/Robert_the_Bruce" title="Robert the Bruce">Robert the Bruce</a>. He was captured at <a href="/wiki/Roxburgh" title="Roxburgh">Roxburgh</a> early in 1333 and was a prisoner in England at the time of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Halidon_Hill" title="Battle of Halidon Hill">Battle of Halidon Hill</a>. He obtained his freedom in time to march to the relief of his wife, who was defending <a href="/wiki/Kildrummy_Castle" title="Kildrummy Castle">Kildrummy Castle</a>. Sir Andrew commenced with unabated spirit to struggle in the cause of independence and died in 1338.
</p><p>The last Murray Lord of <a href="/wiki/Bothwell" title="Bothwell">Bothwell</a> died in 1360 of the <a href="/wiki/Bubonic_plague" title="Bubonic plague">plague</a>. By the 16th century, the Murrays of Tullibardine in <a href="/wiki/Strathearn" title="Strathearn">Strathearn</a> had assumed the leadership of the Murrays. This was formally confirmed by Bands of Association in 1586 and 1589.
</p><p>Sir John became the 1st <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Tullibardine" class="mw-redirect" title="Earl of Tullibardine">Earl of Tullibardine</a> in 1606. Thus, the Tullibardine hegemony was firmly established among the Murrays; and George Iain Murray, 10th <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Atholl" title="Duke of Atholl">Duke of Atholl</a> was also Marquis of Tullibardine as recognized in Lyon Register as Chief of the Murrays. The 2nd <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Tullibardine" class="mw-redirect" title="Earl of Tullibardine">Earl of Tullibardine</a> William Murray, 1574 circa – 1628 , married Lady Dorothea <a href="/wiki/House_of_Stuart" title="House of Stuart">Stewart</a>, heiress of the Earls of Atholl in 1629 and Marquises from 1676. To their <a href="/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">medieval</a> peacock's head crest (motto-Praite), they added the <a href="/wiki/Mermaid" title="Mermaid">mermaid</a> (motto-Tout Pret), as Lords of Balquidder; and in the seventeenth century, they took the demi-savage holding a sword and a key commemorating the capture of the last <a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_Isles" title="Lord of the Isles">Lord of the Isles</a> by the 1st Stewart Earl of Atholl in 1475: hence the motto Furth, Fortune, and Fill the Fetters. (Go forth against your enemies, have good fortune, and return with hostages and booty).
</p><p>Since 1703, the Murray's chiefs have been <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Atholl" title="Duke of Atholl">Dukes of Atholl</a>. For a time in the 18th century, the Murray dukes were also Sovereign Lords of the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>, with their own coinage and parliament, The House of Keys. The 1st Duke's younger son, Lord George Murray, was the <a href="/wiki/Jacobitism" title="Jacobitism">Jacobite</a> general responsible for the highlander's successes through the early part of the <a href="/wiki/Jacobite_rising_of_1745" title="Jacobite rising of 1745">1745 uprising</a>.
</p><p>Much of the above information about the Murrays was taken from the book <i>The Highland Clans</i>, by <a href="/wiki/Iain_Moncreiffe_of_that_Ilk" class="mw-redirect" title="Iain Moncreiffe of that Ilk">Iain Moncreiffe of that Ilk</a>.
</p><p>Lord George's descendant <a href="/wiki/George_Murray,_10th_Duke_of_Atholl" class="mw-redirect" title="George Murray, 10th Duke of Atholl">George Murray, 10th Duke of Atholl</a>, died in February 1996. The new <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Atholl" title="Duke of Atholl">Duke of Atholl</a> is <a href="/wiki/John_Murray,_11th_Duke_of_Atholl" title="John Murray, 11th Duke of Atholl">John Murray, 11th Duke of Atholl</a>, a <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South African</a>. The new Duke has taken the chiefship of the Murrays.
</p>
