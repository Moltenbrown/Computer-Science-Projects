<p><b>Ancestry.com LLC</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Privately_held_company" title="Privately held company">privately held</a> <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_company" title="Dot-com company">online</a> company based in <a href="/wiki/Lehi,_Utah" title="Lehi, Utah">Lehi, Utah</a>. The largest <a href="/wiki/For-profit" class="mw-redirect" title="For-profit">for-profit</a> <a href="/wiki/Genealogy" title="Genealogy">genealogy</a> company in the world, it operates a network of genealogical, historical record and <a href="/wiki/Genetic_genealogy" title="Genetic genealogy">genetic genealogy</a> websites.
</p><p>As of November 2018, the company claimed to provide access to approximately 10 billion historical records, to have 3 million paying subscribers and to have sold 15 million DNA kits to customers.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancestry.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancestry.com">Ancestry.com</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancestry.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancestry.com">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1990, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Brent_Allen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Paul Brent Allen (page does not exist)">Paul B. Allen</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and Dan Taggart, two <a href="/wiki/Brigham_Young_University" title="Brigham Young University">Brigham Young University</a> graduates, founded Infobases and began offering <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">Latter-day Saints</a> (LDS) publications on <a href="/wiki/Floppy_disk" title="Floppy disk">floppy disks</a>. In 1988, Allen had worked at <a href="/wiki/Folio_Corporation" title="Folio Corporation">Folio Corporation</a>, founded by his brother Curt and his brother-in-law <a href="/wiki/Brad_Pelo" title="Brad Pelo">Brad Pelo</a>.
</p><p>Infobases' first products were floppy disks and compact disks sold from the back seat of the founders' car. In 1994, Infobases was named among <i><a href="/wiki/Inc._(magazine)" title="Inc. (magazine)">Inc.</a></i> magazine's 500 fastest-growing companies.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Their first offering on CD was the LDS Collectors Edition, released in April 1995, selling for $299.95,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> which was offered in an online version in August 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Ancestry officially went online with the launch of Ancestry.com in 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On January 1, 1997, Infobases' parent company, Western Standard Publishing, purchased Ancestry, Inc.,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> publisher of <i><a href="/wiki/Ancestry_Magazine" title="Ancestry Magazine">Ancestry</a></i> magazine and genealogy books. Western Standard Publishing's CEO was <a href="/wiki/Joseph_A._Cannon" title="Joseph A. Cannon">Joe Cannon</a>, one of the principal owners of <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Steel" title="Geneva Steel">Geneva Steel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 1997, Allen and Taggart purchased Western Standard's interest in Ancestry, Inc. At the time, <a href="/wiki/Brad_Pelo" title="Brad Pelo">Brad Pelo</a> was president and CEO of Infobases, and president of Western Standard. Less than six months earlier, he had been president of Folio Corporation, whose digital technology Infobases was using. In March 1997, Folio was sold to <a href="/wiki/Open_Market" title="Open Market">Open Market</a> for $45&#160;million.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The first public evidence of the change in ownership of <i>Ancestry</i> magazine came with the July/August 1997 issue, which showed a newly reorganized Ancestry, Inc., as its publisher. That issue's masthead also included the first use of the Ancestry.com web address.
</p><p>More growth for Infobases occurred in July 1997, when Ancestry, Inc. purchased <a href="/wiki/Bookcraft,_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Bookcraft, Inc.">Bookcraft, Inc.</a>, a publisher of books written by leaders and officers of the LDS Church.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Infobases had published many of Bookcraft's books as part of its LDS Collector's Library. Pelo also announced that Ancestry's product line would be greatly expanded in both CDs and online. <a href="/wiki/Alan_Ashton_(executive)" class="mw-redirect" title="Alan Ashton (executive)">Alan Ashton</a>, a longtime investor in Infobases and founder of <a href="/wiki/WordPerfect" title="WordPerfect">WordPerfect</a>, was its chairman of the board.
</p><p>Allen and Taggart began running Ancestry, Inc. independently from Infobases in July 1997, and began creating one of the largest online subscription-based genealogy database services.<sup id="cite_ref-infobase_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-infobase-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In April 1999, to better focus on its Ancestry.com and MyFamily.com Internet businesses, Infobases sold the Bookcraft brand name and its catalog of print books to its major competitor in the LDS book market, <a href="/wiki/Deseret_Book" class="mw-redirect" title="Deseret Book">Deseret Book</a>. Included in the sale were the rights to Infobases' LDS Collectors Library on CD. A year earlier, Deseret Book had released a competing product called GospeLink, and the two products were combined as a single product by Deseret Book.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The MyFamily.com website launched in December 1998, with additional free sites beginning in March 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The site generated one million registered users within its first 140 days.<sup id="cite_ref-infobase_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-infobase-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The company raised more than US$90&#160;million in venture capital from investors<sup id="cite_ref-infobase_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-infobase-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and changed its name on November 17, 1999, from Ancestry.com, Inc. to MyFamily.com, Inc. Its three Internet genealogy sites were then called Ancestry.com, FamilyHistory.com, and MyFamily.com.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Sales were about US$62&#160;million for 2002 and US$99&#160;million for 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-pallencompanies_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pallencompanies-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:272px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ancestry_dot_com_headquarters.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Ancestry_dot_com_headquarters.jpg/270px-Ancestry_dot_com_headquarters.jpg" decoding="async" width="270" height="189" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Ancestry_dot_com_headquarters.jpg/405px-Ancestry_dot_com_headquarters.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Ancestry_dot_com_headquarters.jpg/540px-Ancestry_dot_com_headquarters.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2766" data-file-height="1934" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ancestry_dot_com_headquarters.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Former Ancestry.com headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Provo,_Utah" title="Provo, Utah">Provo, Utah</a></div></div></div>
<p>In March 2004, the company, which had outgrown its call center in <a href="/wiki/Orem,_Utah" title="Orem, Utah">Orem, Utah</a>, opened a new <a href="/wiki/Call_center" class="mw-redirect" title="Call center">call center</a>, which accommodates about 700 agents at a time, in <a href="/wiki/Provo,_Utah" title="Provo, Utah">Provo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Heritage_Makers" title="Heritage Makers">Heritage Makers</a> was acquired by MyFamily.com in September 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While the company had been offering free access to Ancestry.com at LDS <a href="/wiki/Family_History_Center_(LDS_Church)" title="Family History Center (LDS Church)">Family History Centers</a>, that service was terminated on March 17, 2007, because the company and the LDS Church were unable to reach a mutually agreeable licensing agreement. In 2010, Ancestry restored access to its site at Family History Centers.
</p><p>In 2010, Ancestry sold its book publishing assets to <a href="/wiki/Turner_Publishing_Company" title="Turner Publishing Company">Turner Publishing Company</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ancestry.com became a publicly traded company on <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ" class="mw-redirect" title="NASDAQ">NASDAQ</a> (symbol: ACOM) on November 5, 2009, with an initial public offering of 7.4 million shares priced at $13.50 per share, underwritten by <a href="/wiki/Morgan_Stanley" title="Morgan Stanley">Morgan Stanley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_America" title="Bank of America">Bank of America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Merrill_Lynch" title="Merrill Lynch">Merrill Lynch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jefferies_%26_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Jefferies &amp; Company">Jefferies &amp; Company</a>, <a href="/wiki/Piper_Jaffray" title="Piper Jaffray">Piper Jaffray</a>, and <a href="/wiki/BMO_Capital_Markets" title="BMO Capital Markets">BMO Capital Markets</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2010, Ancestry.com expanded its domestic operations with the opening of an office in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco,_California" class="mw-redirect" title="San Francisco, California">San Francisco, California</a>, staffed with brand new engineering, product, and marketing teams geared toward developing some of Ancestry's cutting-edge technology and services.  In 2011, Ancestry launched an <a href="/wiki/Android_(OS)" class="mw-redirect" title="Android (OS)">Android</a> and <a href="/wiki/IOS" title="IOS">iOS</a> app.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In December 2011, Ancestry.com moved the <a href="/wiki/Social_Security_Death_Index" title="Social Security Death Index">Social Security Death Index</a> search behind a <a href="/wiki/Paywall" title="Paywall">paywall</a> and stopped displaying the Social Security information of people who had died within the past 10 years, because of <a href="/wiki/Identity_theft" title="Identity theft">identity theft</a> concerns.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2012, Ancestry.com acquired the collection of DNA assets from <a href="/wiki/GeneTree" title="GeneTree">GeneTree</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In September 2012, Ancestry.com expanded its international operations with the opening of its European headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Dublin,_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Dublin, Ireland">Dublin, Ireland</a>. The Dublin office includes a new call centre for international customers, as well as product, marketing, and engineering teams.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2012, Ancestry.com agreed to be acquired by a private equity group consisting of <a href="/wiki/Permira" title="Permira">Permira</a> Advisers LLP, members of Ancestry.com's management team, including CEO Tim Sullivan and CFO Howard Hochhauser, and <a href="/wiki/Spectrum_Equity" title="Spectrum Equity">Spectrum Equity</a>, for $32 per share or around $1.6 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>  At the same time, Ancestry.com purchased a photo digitization and sharing service called <a href="/wiki/1000Memories" title="1000Memories">1000Memories</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On July 16, 2015, Ancestry launched AncestryHealth, and announced the appointment of Cathy A. Petti as its Chief Health Officer.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In April 2016 <a href="/wiki/GIC_Private_Limited" title="GIC Private Limited">GIC Private Limited</a> (a sovereign wealth fund owned by the Government of Singapore) and <a href="/wiki/Silver_Lake_Partners" title="Silver Lake Partners">Silver Lake</a> (a private equity fund manager) bought equity stakes in Ancestry.com.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The  estimated market value of Ancestry.com in 2017 was more than $3 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 2018 Ancestry claimed to have over 10 billion digitised records and over three million paying customers.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="AncestryDNA">AncestryDNA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancestry.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: AncestryDNA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>AncestryDNA is a subsidiary of Ancestry LLC. AncestryDNA offers a direct-to-consumer <a href="/wiki/Genealogical_DNA_test" title="Genealogical DNA test">genealogical DNA test</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Consumers provide a sample of their DNA to the company for analysis. AncestryDNA then uses DNA sequences to infer family relationships with other Ancestry DNA users and to provide what it calls an "ethnicity estimate". Previously, Ancestry.com also offered paternal <a href="/wiki/YDNA" class="mw-redirect" title="YDNA">Y-chromosome DNA</a> and maternal <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a> tests, but those were discontinued in June 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The company describes the technical process of testing in a scientific <a href="/wiki/White_paper" title="White paper">white paper</a>. In May 2019 the company claimed that their database contained 15 million completed DNA kits bought by customers.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ancestry DNA is commonly used for <a href="/wiki/Donor_conceived_person" title="Donor conceived person">donor conceived persons</a> to find their biological siblings and in some cases their <a href="/wiki/Sperm_donor" class="mw-redirect" title="Sperm donor">sperm</a> or <a href="/wiki/Egg_donor" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg donor">egg donor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The testing itself is performed by <a href="/wiki/Quest_Diagnostics" title="Quest Diagnostics">Quest Diagnostics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="FindAGrave">FindAGrave</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancestry.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: FindAGrave">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Find_a_Grave" title="Find a Grave">Find a Grave</a></div>
<p>On September 30, 2013, Ancestry.com announced its acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Find_a_Grave" title="Find a Grave">Find a Grave</a>. Site editor Jim Tipton said of the purchase that Ancestry.com had "been linking and driving traffic to the site for several years. Burial information is a wonderful source for people researching their family history". Ancestry.com launched a mobile app in March 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Newspapers.com">Newspapers.com</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancestry.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Newspapers.com">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2012, Ancestry spun off its digitized online newspaper components into a standalone service Newspapers.com with newspaper.com only pricing as well as a bundled Ancestry.com pricing.
</p><p>Before the Newspapers.com launch, Ancestry.com acquired the following newspaper-oriented components, including scanning and digital technologies and posting on the web:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/IArchives,_Inc." title="IArchives, Inc.">iArchives, Inc.</a> (and its footnote.com service) acquired in 2010<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> for 1.022 million Common Stock shares.  The purchase brought in assets including processes for digitalizing documents on <a href="/wiki/Microfilm" class="mw-redirect" title="Microfilm">microfilm</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>  Footnote would be rebranded Fold3 in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Archives.com acquired for $100 million in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> As of June 23, 2019 the archive claimed its index comprised online newspapers dating from 1700 worldwide, covering 12,100+ newspapers and a total of more than 509 million pages.<sup id="cite_ref-newspapers.com_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newspapers.com-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The website's principal competitor is <a href="/wiki/Newspaperarchive.com" class="mw-redirect" title="Newspaperarchive.com">newspaperarchive.com</a> which claims it has online newspapers dating from 1607 worldwide and its index in June 2018 includes 9,829 newspapers.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Both websites have similar models for increasing their databases: striking deals with libraries, publishers and historical organizations to scan the publications for free to include in their database. Participants note that the process of free scanning is easier, cheaper and quicker to get their publications online rather than working through the government operated <a href="/wiki/National_Digital_Newspaper_Program" title="National Digital Newspaper Program">National Digital Newspaper Program</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="RootsWeb">RootsWeb</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancestry.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: RootsWeb">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>RootsWeb, acquired by Ancestry in June 2000, is a free genealogy community that uses online forums, mailing lists, and other resources to help people research their family history. Users can upload <a href="/wiki/GEDCOM" title="GEDCOM">GEDCOM</a> files of their information for others to search at the WorldConnect portion of the site. Trees uploaded to WorldConnect are searchable at both the RootsWeb and Ancestry websites.
</p><p>On December 20, 2017, a file containing 300,000 RootsWeb user names, passwords, and email addresses was exposed to the internet.  The 300,000 records were from RootsWeb surname list service with 55,000 of those records were also Ancestry.com login credentials.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Past_products">Past products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancestry.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Past products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Ancestry Family Tree, Ancestral Quest by Incline Software, licensed by Ancestry.com in 2001 and branded for their use as Ancestry Family Tree. Available for free from Ancestry.com from 2001 to 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Family Origins<sup id="cite_ref-tmjre_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tmjre-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Family_Tree_Maker" title="Family Tree Maker">Family Tree Maker</a>, sold in 2017.</li>
<li>Genealogy.com - Genealogy.com, which maintains a genealogy research website, was acquired by MyFamily.com in 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Generations Family Tree (originally called "<a href="/wiki/Reunion_(genealogy_software)" title="Reunion (genealogy software)">Reunion for Windows</a>")<sup id="cite_ref-pastproducts_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pastproducts-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>MyFamily.com - allowed members to create private family, or group, websites. In May 2010, MyFamily closed its <a href="/wiki/Bellevue,_Washington" title="Bellevue, Washington">Bellevue, Washington</a>, development office, effectively letting its entire staff go since none of the staff accepted an offer to move to Provo. Ancestry shut down MyFamily.com on September 5, 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> At the time of the shutdown, MyFamily had not resolved discontent with the downloading process, which consisted of capturing miscellaneous uncatalogued photos, with alphanumeric names and no data attached, and various calendar documents, thus leaving behind the associated data, File Cabinet documents, family recipes, and all other information.<sup id="cite_ref-slate_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-slate-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>ROOTS software series by CommSoft<sup id="cite_ref-uft_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uft-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> was one of the first publishers of series of <a href="/wiki/Genealogy_software" title="Genealogy software">genealogy software</a> programs, created in the 1980s, and available until 1997. Commsoft released the following: ROOTS89 for the <a href="/wiki/HDOS" title="HDOS">Heath H-8</a> series of personal computers; ROOTS/M for the <a href="/wiki/CP/M" title="CP/M">CP/M</a> operating system; and ROOTS II for <a href="/wiki/MS-DOS" title="MS-DOS">MS-DOS</a>, followed by ROOTS III and ROOTS IV; and ROOTS V for Windows along with Visual ROOTS for <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a>.</li>
<li>Ultimate Family Tree (UFT)<sup id="cite_ref-uft_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uft-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Partnerships">Partnerships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancestry.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Partnerships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2013, Ancestry partnered with <a href="/wiki/FamilySearch" title="FamilySearch">FamilySearch</a>. The two genealogical organisations agreed to co-operate in digitizing records and to allow members of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter_Day_Saints" class="mw-redirect" title="Church of Jesus Christ of Latter Day Saints">Church of Jesus Christ of Latter Day Saints</a> to have free Ancestry accounts. <sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2015, Ancestry partnered with the <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> subsidiary, <a href="/wiki/Calico_(company)" title="Calico (company)">Calico</a>, to focus on longevity research and therapeutics, in an effort to investigate human heredity of lifespan.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Data_sharing_policy_.28government_agencies.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Data_sharing_policy_(government_agencies)">Data sharing policy (government agencies)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancestry.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Data sharing policy (government agencies)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the arrest of the accused <a href="/wiki/Golden_State_Killer" title="Golden State Killer">Golden State Killer</a>, Ancestry.com and <a href="/wiki/23andMe" title="23andMe">23andMe</a> made a data policy stating that they won't allow their DNA profiles to be used for crime solving unless pressured to do so by law enforcement, as they believe it violates users' privacy.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Privacy_controversy">Privacy controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancestry.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Privacy controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2019, Ancestry.com was a awarded a German <a href="/wiki/Big_Brother_Awards" title="Big Brother Awards">Big Brother Award</a>, a negative award, "for exploiting an interest in genealogy to entice people into submitting saliva samples … to pile up a treasure trove of genome data for commercial research, because that is their actual business model."<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
