	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Fletcher (surname)</h1>

<p><b>Fletcher</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a> of <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Gaelic language">Scottish</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a> origin. The name is an occupational name for an <a href="/wiki/Fletching" title="Fletching">arrowsmith</a> or seller of arrows, derived from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> "Fulcher" or <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> <i>flech(i)er</i> (in turn from <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> <i>fleche</i> "arrow").<sup id="cite_ref-Reaney_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reaney-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Fletcher_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fletcher-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The English word was borrowed into the <a href="/wiki/Goidelic_languages" title="Goidelic languages">Goidelic languages</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, leading to the development of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish</a> name "Mac an Fhleisteir" (also spelt "Mac an Fhleisdeir"),<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> "the arrowsmith's son".
</p><p>Early bearers of the name include Robert <i>le Flecher</i> in 1203 (in the "Assize Court Rolls of Staffordshire", during the reign of <a href="/wiki/John_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="John I of England">King John</a>), William <i>Flecher’</i> also in 1203, and Peter <i>le flechier</i> in 1227. The name is often confused with <i>Flesher</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Reaney_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reaney-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> due to the phonetic similarity.
</p>
