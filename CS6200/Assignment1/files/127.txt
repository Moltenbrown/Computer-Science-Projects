<p>The <b>Council of Europe</b> (<b>CoE</b>; <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Conseil de l'Europe, CdE</i>; <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">Europarat</i>) is an <a href="/wiki/International_organization" title="International organization">international organisation</a> whose stated aim<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> is to uphold <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">human rights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democracy</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Law_in_Europe" title="Law in Europe">rule of law</a> in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Founded in 1949, it has 47 member states, covers approximately 820 million people and operates with an annual budget of approximately 500 million <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euros</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The organisation is distinct from the 28-nation <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> (EU), although it is sometimes confused with it, partly because the EU has adopted the original <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Europe" title="Flag of Europe">European Flag</a> which was created by the Council of Europe in 1955,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Anthem_of_Europe" title="Anthem of Europe">European Anthem</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> No country has ever joined the EU without first belonging to the Council of Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The Council of Europe is an official United Nations Observer.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Unlike the EU, the Council of Europe cannot make binding laws, but it does have the power to enforce select international agreements reached by European states on various topics. The best known body of the Council of Europe is the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a>, which enforces the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a>.
</p><p>The Council's two statutory bodies are the <a href="/wiki/Committee_of_Ministers" class="mw-redirect" title="Committee of Ministers">Committee of Ministers</a>, comprising the foreign ministers of each member state, and the <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Assembly_of_the_Council_of_Europe" title="Parliamentary Assembly of the Council of Europe">Parliamentary Assembly</a>, composed of members of the national parliaments of each member state. The <a href="/wiki/Commissioner_for_Human_Rights" title="Commissioner for Human Rights">Commissioner for Human Rights</a> is an independent institution within the Council of Europe, mandated to promote awareness of and respect for human rights in the member states. The <a href="/wiki/Secretary_General_of_the_Council_of_Europe" title="Secretary General of the Council of Europe">Secretary General</a> heads the secretariat of the organisation. Other major CoE bodies include the <a href="/wiki/European_Directorate_for_the_Quality_of_Medicines" title="European Directorate for the Quality of Medicines">European Directorate for the Quality of Medicines</a> and the <a href="/wiki/European_Audiovisual_Observatory" title="European Audiovisual Observatory">European Audiovisual Observatory</a>.
</p><p>The headquarters of the Council of Europe are in <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> are its two <a href="/wiki/Official_language" title="Official language">official languages</a>. The Committee of Ministers, the Parliamentary Assembly and the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_the_Council_of_Europe" title="Congress of the Council of Europe">Congress</a> also use <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a> for some of their work.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Council_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Palais_Universitaire_de_Strasbourg-10_ao%C3%BBt_1949.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Palais_Universitaire_de_Strasbourg-10_ao%C3%BBt_1949.jpg/170px-Palais_Universitaire_de_Strasbourg-10_ao%C3%BBt_1949.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="212" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Palais_Universitaire_de_Strasbourg-10_ao%C3%BBt_1949.jpg/255px-Palais_Universitaire_de_Strasbourg-10_ao%C3%BBt_1949.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Palais_Universitaire_de_Strasbourg-10_ao%C3%BBt_1949.jpg/340px-Palais_Universitaire_de_Strasbourg-10_ao%C3%BBt_1949.jpg 2x" data-file-width="768" data-file-height="956" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Palais_Universitaire_de_Strasbourg-10_ao%C3%BBt_1949.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Plaque commemorating the first session of the Council of Europe Assembly at <a href="/wiki/Palais_Universitaire,_Strasbourg" title="Palais Universitaire, Strasbourg">Strasbourg University</a></div></div></div>
<p>In a speech in 1929, French Foreign Minister <a href="/wiki/Aristide_Briand" title="Aristide Briand">Aristide Briand</a> floated the idea of an organisation which would gather European nations together in a "federal union" to resolve common problems.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="https://fresques.ina.fr/jalons/fiche-media/InaEdu02042/discours-d-aristide-briand-devant-la-sdn-du-7-septembre-1929-audio.html">[3]</a> But it was Britain's wartime leader Sir <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a> who first publicly suggested the creation of "a Council of Europe" in a <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.winstonchurchill.org/resources/speeches/1941-1945-war-leader/national-address/">BBC radio broadcast</a> on 21 March 1943, while the second world war was still raging. In his own <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.ibiblio.org/pha/policy/1943/1943-03-21a.html">words</a>, he tried to "peer through the mists of the future to the end of the war," once victory had been achieved, and think about how to re-build and maintain peace on a shattered continent. Given that Europe had been at the origin of two world wars, the creation of such a body would be, he suggested, "a stupendous business". He returned to the idea during a well-known speech at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Zurich" title="University of Zurich">University of Zurich</a> on 19 September 1946,<sup id="cite_ref-COE_Churchill_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-COE_Churchill-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ENA_Churchill_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ENA_Churchill-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> throwing the full weight of his considerable post-war prestige behind it.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_B_145_Bild-F023908-0002,_Stra%C3%9Fburg,_Tagung_des_Europarates.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Bundesarchiv_B_145_Bild-F023908-0002%2C_Stra%C3%9Fburg%2C_Tagung_des_Europarates.jpg/220px-Bundesarchiv_B_145_Bild-F023908-0002%2C_Stra%C3%9Fburg%2C_Tagung_des_Europarates.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="160" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Bundesarchiv_B_145_Bild-F023908-0002%2C_Stra%C3%9Fburg%2C_Tagung_des_Europarates.jpg/330px-Bundesarchiv_B_145_Bild-F023908-0002%2C_Stra%C3%9Fburg%2C_Tagung_des_Europarates.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Bundesarchiv_B_145_Bild-F023908-0002%2C_Stra%C3%9Fburg%2C_Tagung_des_Europarates.jpg/440px-Bundesarchiv_B_145_Bild-F023908-0002%2C_Stra%C3%9Fburg%2C_Tagung_des_Europarates.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="583" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_B_145_Bild-F023908-0002,_Stra%C3%9Fburg,_Tagung_des_Europarates.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Session of the Council of Europe's Parliamentary Assembly in the former <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Europe" title="Palace of Europe">House of Europe</a> in Strasbourg in 1967. <a href="/wiki/Willy_Brandt" title="Willy Brandt">Willy Brandt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_Foreign_Affairs_(Germany)" title="Minister for Foreign Affairs (Germany)">German Minister for Foreign Affairs</a>, is speaking.</div></div></div>
<p>The future structure of the Council of Europe was discussed at a specific congress of several hundred leading politicians, government representatives and civil society in <a href="/wiki/The_Hague" title="The Hague">The Hague</a>, <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, in 1948. There were two schools of thought competing: some favoured a classical international organisation with representatives of governments, while others preferred a political forum with parliamentarians. Both approaches were finally combined through the creation of a Committee of Ministers (in which governments were represented) and a Consultative Assembly (in which parliaments were represented), the two main bodies mentioned in the Statute of the Council of Europe. This dual intergovernmental and inter-parliamentary structure was later copied for the <a href="/wiki/European_Communities" title="European Communities">European Communities</a>, <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">North Atlantic Treaty Organization</a>  and the <a href="/wiki/Organization_for_Security_and_Co-operation_in_Europe" title="Organization for Security and Co-operation in Europe">Organization for Security and Co-operation in Europe</a>.
</p><p>The Council of Europe was founded on 5 May 1949 by the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_London_(1949)" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaty of London (1949)">Treaty of London</a>. The Statute was signed in London on that day by ten states: <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, though Turkey and Greece joined three months later. On 10 August 1949, 100 members of the Council's Consultative Assembly, parliamentarians drawn from the twelve member nations, met in Strasbourg for its first plenary session, held over 18 sittings and lasting nearly a month. They debated how to reconcile and reconstruct a continent still reeling from war, yet already facing a new East-West divide, launched the concept of a trans-national court to protect the basic human rights of every European citizen, and took the first steps towards what would in time become the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>.
</p><p>In August 1949, <a href="/wiki/Paul-Henri_Spaak" title="Paul-Henri Spaak">Paul-Henri Spaak</a> of Belgium was elected as the first president of the Assembly, steering its early work. However, in December 1951, after nearly three years in the role, Spaak resigned in disappointment after the Assembly rejected proposals for a "European political authority".<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.assembly.coe.int/nw/xml/Speeches/Speech-XML2HTML-EN.asp?SpeechID=271">[4]</a> Convinced the Council of Europe was never going to be strong enough to achieve his long-term goal of European unification,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  he soon tried again in a different format, becoming one of the founders of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historic_speeches_at_the_Council_of_Europe">Historic speeches at the Council of Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Council_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historic speeches at the Council of Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2018 an archive of all speeches made to the <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Assembly_of_the_Council_of_Europe" title="Parliamentary Assembly of the Council of Europe">Parliamentary Assembly of the Council of Europe</a> by heads of state or government since the Council of Europe's creation in 1949 appeared online, the fruit of a two-year project entitled <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.assembly.coe.int/nw/xml/Speeches/Speech-By-Country-EN.asp">"Voices of Europe"</a>. At the time of its <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://assembly.coe.int/nw/xml/News/News-View-en.asp?newsid=7112&amp;lang=2">launch</a>, the archive comprised 263 speeches delivered over a 70-year period by some 216 Presidents, Prime Ministers, monarchs and religious leaders from 45 countries - though it continues to expand, as new speeches are added every few months.
</p><p>Some very early speeches by individuals considered to be "founding figures" of the European institutions, even if they were not heads of state or government at the time, are also included (such as Sir <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a> or <a href="/wiki/Robert_Schuman" title="Robert Schuman">Robert Schuman</a>). Addresses by eight monarchs appear in the list (such as King <a href="/wiki/Juan_Carlos_I_of_Spain" title="Juan Carlos I of Spain">Juan Carlos I of Spain</a>, King <a href="/wiki/Albert_II_of_Belgium" title="Albert II of Belgium">Albert II of Belgium</a> and Grand Duke <a href="/wiki/Henri,_Grand_Duke_of_Luxembourg" title="Henri, Grand Duke of Luxembourg">Henri of Luxembourg</a>) as well as the speeches given by religious figures (such as <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">Pope John Paul II</a>) and several leaders from countries in the Middle East and North Africa (such as <a href="/wiki/Shimon_Peres" title="Shimon Peres">Shimon Peres</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yasser_Arafat" title="Yasser Arafat">Yasser Arafat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hosni_Mubarak" title="Hosni Mubarak">Hosni Mubarak</a>, <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9opold_S%C3%A9dar_Senghor" title="Léopold Sédar Senghor">Léopold Sédar Senghor</a> or King <a href="/wiki/Hussein_of_Jordan" title="Hussein of Jordan">Hussein of Jordan</a>).
</p><p>The full text of the speeches is given in both English and <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.assembly.coe.int/nw/xml/Speeches/Speech-By-Country-FR.asp">French</a>, regardless of the original language used. The archive is searchable by country, by name, and chronologically.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aims_and_achievements">Aims and achievements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Council_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aims and achievements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Article 1(a) of the Statute states that "The aim of the Council of Europe is to achieve a greater unity between its members for the purpose of safeguarding and realising the ideals and principles which are their common heritage and facilitating their economic and social progress."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Membership is open to all European states who seek harmony, cooperation, good governance and human rights, accepting the principle of the rule of law and are able and willing to guarantee democracy, fundamental human rights and freedoms.
</p><p>Whereas the member states of the European Union transfer part of their national legislative and executive powers to the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> and the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a>, Council of Europe member states maintain their sovereignty but commit themselves through conventions/treaties (<a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international law</a>) and co-operate on the basis of common values and common political decisions. Those conventions and decisions are developed by the member states working together at the Council of Europe. Both organisations function as concentric circles around the common foundations for European co-operation and harmony, with the Council of Europe being the geographically wider circle. The European Union could be seen as the smaller circle with a much higher level of integration through the transfer of powers from the national to the EU level. "The Council of Europe and the European Union: different roles, shared values."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Council of Europe conventions/treaties are also open for signature to non-member states, thus facilitating equal co-operation with countries outside Europe.
</p><p>The Council of Europe's most famous achievement is the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a>, which was adopted in 1950 following a report by the Council of Europe's Parliamentary Assembly, and followed on from the United Nations '<a href="/wiki/Universal_Declaration_of_Human_Rights" title="Universal Declaration of Human Rights">Universal Declaration of Human Rights</a>' (UDHR).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The Convention created the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a> in Strasbourg. The Court supervises compliance with the European Convention on Human Rights and thus functions as the highest European court. It is to this court that Europeans can bring cases if they believe that a member country has violated their fundamental rights and freedoms.
</p><p>The various activities and achievements of the Council of Europe can be found in detail on its official website. The Council of Europe works in the following areas:
</p>
<ul><li>Protection of the <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_law" title="Rule of law">rule of law</a> and fostering legal co-operation through some 200 conventions and other treaties,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> including such leading instruments as the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_Cybercrime" title="Convention on Cybercrime">Convention on Cybercrime</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Europe_Convention_on_the_Prevention_of_Terrorism" title="Council of Europe Convention on the Prevention of Terrorism">Convention on the Prevention of Terrorism</a>, Conventions against Corruption and Organised Crime,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_Action_against_Trafficking_in_Human_Beings" class="mw-redirect" title="Convention on Action against Trafficking in Human Beings">Convention on Action against Trafficking in Human Beings</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Convention_for_the_Protection_of_Human_Rights_and_Dignity_of_the_Human_Being_with_regard_to_the_Application_of_Biology_and_Medicine" title="Convention for the Protection of Human Rights and Dignity of the Human Being with regard to the Application of Biology and Medicine">Convention on Human Rights and Biomedicine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/CODEXTER" class="mw-redirect" title="CODEXTER">CODEXTER</a>, designed to co-ordinate counter-terrorism measures</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_European_Commission_for_the_Efficiency_of_Justice_(CEPEJ)" class="mw-redirect" title="The European Commission for the Efficiency of Justice (CEPEJ)">The European Commission for the Efficiency of Justice (CEPEJ)</a></li>
<li>Protection of <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a>, notably through:
<ul><li>the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/European_Committee_for_the_Prevention_of_Torture" class="mw-redirect" title="European Committee for the Prevention of Torture">European Committee for the Prevention of Torture</a></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission_against_Racism_and_Intolerance" title="European Commission against Racism and Intolerance">European Commission against Racism and Intolerance</a></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_Action_against_Trafficking_in_Human_Beings" class="mw-redirect" title="Convention on Action against Trafficking in Human Beings">Convention on Action against Trafficking in Human Beings</a><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Convention_for_the_protection_of_individuals_with_regard_to_automatic_processing_of_personal_data" title="Convention for the protection of individuals with regard to automatic processing of personal data">Convention for the protection of individuals with regard to automatic processing of personal data</a></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_Protection_of_Children_against_Sexual_Exploitation_and_Sexual_Abuse" class="mw-redirect" title="Convention on the Protection of Children against Sexual Exploitation and Sexual Abuse">Convention on the Protection of Children against Sexual Exploitation and Sexual Abuse</a><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_preventing_and_combating_violence_against_women_and_domestic_violence" title="Convention on preventing and combating violence against women and domestic violence">Convention on preventing and combating violence against women and domestic violence</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>social rights under the <a href="/wiki/European_Social_Charter" title="European Social Charter">European Social Charter</a></li>
<li>linguistic rights under the <a href="/wiki/European_Charter_for_Regional_or_Minority_Languages" title="European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages">European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages</a></li>
<li>minority rights under the <a href="/wiki/Framework_Convention_for_the_Protection_of_National_Minorities" title="Framework Convention for the Protection of National Minorities">Framework Convention for the Protection of National Minorities</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_the_press" title="Freedom of the press">Media freedom</a> under Article&#160;10 of the European Convention on Human Rights and the European Convention on Transfrontier Television</li></ul></li>
<li>Protection of <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democracy</a> through parliamentary scrutiny and <a href="/wiki/Election_monitoring" title="Election monitoring">election monitoring</a> by its Parliamentary Assembly as well as assistance in democratic reforms, in particular by the <a href="/wiki/Venice_Commission" title="Venice Commission">Venice Commission</a>.</li>
<li>Promotion of cultural co-operation and diversity under the Council of Europe's Cultural Convention of 1954 and several conventions on the protection of cultural heritage as well as through its Centre for Modern Languages in <a href="/wiki/Graz" title="Graz">Graz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, and its <a href="/wiki/North-South_Centre" class="mw-redirect" title="North-South Centre">North-South Centre</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>.</li>
<li>Promotion of the right to education under Article&#160;2 of the first Protocol to the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a> and several conventions on the recognition of university studies and diplomas (see also <a href="/wiki/Bologna_Process" title="Bologna Process">Bologna Process</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lisbon_Recognition_Convention" title="Lisbon Recognition Convention">Lisbon Recognition Convention</a>).</li>
<li>Promotion of fair sport through the <a href="/wiki/World_Anti-Doping_Agency#Council_of_Europe_Anti-Doping_Convention" title="World Anti-Doping Agency">Anti-Doping Convention</a><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Promotion of European youth exchanges and co-operation through European Youth Centres in <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Budapest" title="Budapest">Budapest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>.</li>
<li>Promotion of the quality of medicines throughout Europe by the <a href="/wiki/European_Directorate_for_the_Quality_of_Medicines" title="European Directorate for the Quality of Medicines">European Directorate for the Quality of Medicines</a> and its <a href="/wiki/European_Pharmacopoeia" title="European Pharmacopoeia">European Pharmacopoeia</a>.</li></ul>

<p>The institutions of the Council of Europe are:
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Secretary_General_of_the_Council_of_Europe" title="Secretary General of the Council of Europe">Secretary General</a>, who is elected for a term of five years by the <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Assembly_of_the_Council_of_Europe" title="Parliamentary Assembly of the Council of Europe">Parliamentary Assembly</a> and heads the Secretariat of the Council of Europe. Thorbjørn Jagland, the former Prime Minister of Norway, was elected Secretary General of the Council of Europe on 29 September 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> In June 2014, he became the first Secretary General to be re-elected, commencing his second term in office on 1 October 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Europe_Committee_of_Ministers" class="mw-redirect" title="Council of Europe Committee of Ministers">Committee of Ministers</a>, comprising the Ministers of Foreign Affairs of all 47 member states who are represented by their <a href="/wiki/Ministers_Deputies" title="Ministers Deputies">Permanent Representatives and Ambassadors</a> accredited to the Council of Europe. Committee of Ministers' presidencies are held in alphabetical order for six months following the English alphabet: <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> 11/2010-05/2011, <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> 05/2011-11/2011, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> 11/2011-05/2012, <a href="/wiki/Albania" title="Albania">Albania</a> 05/2012-11/2012, <a href="/wiki/Andorra" title="Andorra">Andorra</a> 11/2012-05/2013, <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a> 05/2013-11/2013, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> 11/2013-05/2014, and so on.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>

<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Assembly_of_the_Council_of_Europe" title="Parliamentary Assembly of the Council of Europe">Parliamentary Assembly</a> (PACE), which comprises national parliamentarians from all member states.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Adopting resolutions and recommendations to governments, the Assembly holds a dialogue with its governmental counterpart, the <a href="/wiki/Committee_of_Ministers_of_the_Council_of_Europe" title="Committee of Ministers of the Council of Europe">Committee of Ministers</a>, and is often regarded as the "motor" of the organisation. The national parliamentary delegations to the Assembly must reflect the political spectrum of their national parliament, i.e. comprise government and opposition parties. The Assembly appoints members as rapporteurs with the mandate to prepare parliamentary reports on specific subjects. The British MP <a href="/wiki/Sir_David_Maxwell-Fyfe" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir David Maxwell-Fyfe">Sir David Maxwell-Fyfe</a> was rapporteur for the drafting of the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a>. <a href="/wiki/Dick_Marty" title="Dick Marty">Dick Marty</a>'s reports on secret <a href="/wiki/CIA" class="mw-redirect" title="CIA">CIA</a> detentions and rendition flights in Europe became quite famous in 2006 and 2007. Other Assembly reports were instrumental in, for example, the abolition of the death penalty in Europe, highlighting the political and human rights situation in <a href="/wiki/Chechnya" title="Chechnya">Chechnya</a>, identifying who was responsible for disappeared persons in <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a>, chronicling threats to freedom of expression in the media and many other subjects.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_the_Council_of_Europe" title="Congress of the Council of Europe">Congress of the Council of Europe</a> (Congress of Local and Regional Authorities of Europe), which was created in 1994 and comprises political representatives from local and regional authorities in all member states. The most influential instruments of the Council of Europe in this field are the <a href="/wiki/European_Charter_of_Local_Self-Government" title="European Charter of Local Self-Government">European Charter of Local Self-Government</a> of 1985 and the European Outline Convention on Transfrontier Co-operation between Territorial Communities or Authorities of 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a>, created under the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a> of 1950, is composed of a judge from each member state elected for a single, non-renewable term of nine years by the <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Assembly_of_the_Council_of_Europe" title="Parliamentary Assembly of the Council of Europe">Parliamentary Assembly</a> and is headed by the elected President of the Court. The current President of the Court is Guido Raimondi from Italy. Under the recent Protocol No. 14 to the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a>, the Court's case-processing was reformed and streamlined. Ratification of Protocol No. 14 was delayed by Russia for a number of years, but won support to be passed in January 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Commissioner_for_Human_Rights" title="Commissioner for Human Rights">Commissioner for Human Rights</a> is elected by the <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Assembly_of_the_Council_of_Europe" title="Parliamentary Assembly of the Council of Europe">Parliamentary Assembly</a> for a non-renewable term of six years since the creation of this position in 1999. Since April 2018, this position has been held by <a href="/wiki/Dunja_Mijatovi%C4%87" title="Dunja Mijatović">Dunja Mijatović</a> from Bosnia and Herzegovina.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.coe.int/T/NGO/default_en.asp">Conference of INGOs.</a> NGOs can participate in the <a href="/wiki/INGOs_Conference_of_the_Council_of_Europe" title="INGOs Conference of the Council of Europe">INGOs Conference of the Council of Europe</a>. Since the [Resolution (2003)8] adopted by the Committee of Ministers on 19 November 2003, they are given a "participatory status".<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.coe.int/en/web/youth/about-us">Joint Council on Youth of the Council of Europe.</a> The European Steering Committee (CDEJ) on Youth and the Advisory Council (CCJ) on Youth of the Council of Europe form together the Joint Council on Youth (CMJ). The CDEJ brings together representatives of ministries or bodies responsible for youth matters from the 50 States Parties to the European Cultural Convention. The CDEJ fosters co-operation between governments in the youth sector and provides a framework for comparing national youth policies, exchanging best practices and drafting standard-setting texts. The Advisory Council on Youth comprises 30 representatives of non-governmental youth organisations and networks. It provides opinions and input from youth NGOs on all youth sector activities and ensures that young people are involved in the Council's other activities.</li>
<li>Information Offices of the Council of Europe in many member states.</li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Headquarters_and_buildings">Headquarters and buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Council_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Headquarters and buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/European_Institutions_in_Strasbourg" class="mw-redirect" title="European Institutions in Strasbourg">European Institutions in Strasbourg</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Council_of_Europe_Palais_de_l%27Europe_aerial_view.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Council_of_Europe_Palais_de_l%27Europe_aerial_view.JPG/220px-Council_of_Europe_Palais_de_l%27Europe_aerial_view.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Council_of_Europe_Palais_de_l%27Europe_aerial_view.JPG/330px-Council_of_Europe_Palais_de_l%27Europe_aerial_view.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Council_of_Europe_Palais_de_l%27Europe_aerial_view.JPG/440px-Council_of_Europe_Palais_de_l%27Europe_aerial_view.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2835" data-file-height="1878" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Council_of_Europe_Palais_de_l%27Europe_aerial_view.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aerial shot of the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Europe" title="Palace of Europe">Palais de l'Europe</a> in Strasbourg</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Council_of_Europe_Agora_building_in_Strasbourg.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Council_of_Europe_Agora_building_in_Strasbourg.JPG/220px-Council_of_Europe_Agora_building_in_Strasbourg.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Council_of_Europe_Agora_building_in_Strasbourg.JPG/330px-Council_of_Europe_Agora_building_in_Strasbourg.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Council_of_Europe_Agora_building_in_Strasbourg.JPG/440px-Council_of_Europe_Agora_building_in_Strasbourg.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4851" data-file-height="3213" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Council_of_Europe_Agora_building_in_Strasbourg.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Council of Europe's Agora building</div></div></div>
<p>The seat of the Council of Europe is in <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a>, France. First meetings were held in Strasbourg's <a href="/wiki/University_of_Strasbourg" title="University of Strasbourg">University</a> Palace in 1949, but the Council of Europe soon moved into its own buildings. The Council of Europe's eight main buildings are situated in the <i>Quartier européen</i>, an area in the northeast of Strasbourg spread over the three districts of Le Wacken, La Robertsau and Quartier de l'Orangerie, where are also located the four buildings of the <a href="/wiki/Seat_of_the_European_Parliament_in_Strasbourg" title="Seat of the European Parliament in Strasbourg">seat of the European Parliament in Strasbourg</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Arte" title="Arte">Arte</a> headquarters and the seat of the <a href="/wiki/International_Institute_of_Human_Rights" title="International Institute of Human Rights">International Institute of Human Rights</a>.
</p><p>Building in the area started in 1949 with the predecessor of the Palais de l'Europe, the House of Europe (demolished in 1977), and came to a provisional end in 2007 with the opening of the New General Office Building, later named "Agora", in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The Palais de l'Europe (<a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Europe" title="Palace of Europe">Palace of Europe</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Art_Nouveau" title="Art Nouveau">Art Nouveau</a> Villa Schutzenberger (seat of the <a href="/wiki/European_Audiovisual_Observatory" title="European Audiovisual Observatory">European Audiovisual Observatory</a>) are in the Orangerie district, and the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a>, the <a href="/wiki/European_Directorate_for_the_Quality_of_Medicines" title="European Directorate for the Quality of Medicines">European Directorate for the Quality of Medicines</a> and the Agora Building are in the Robertsau district. The Agora building has been voted "best international business center real estate project of 2007" on 13 March 2008, at the <a href="/wiki/MIPIM" title="MIPIM">MIPIM</a> 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The European Youth Centre is located in the Wacken district.
</p><p>Besides its headquarters in Strasbourg, the Council of Europe is also present in other cities and countries. The Council of Europe Development Bank has its seat in Paris, the <a href="/wiki/North-South_Centre" class="mw-redirect" title="North-South Centre">North-South Centre</a> of the Council of Europe is established in <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, and the Centre for Modern Languages is in <a href="/wiki/Graz" title="Graz">Graz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>. There are <a href="/wiki/European_Youth_Centres" title="European Youth Centres">European Youth Centres</a> in <a href="/wiki/Budapest" title="Budapest">Budapest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, and in Strasbourg. The European Wergeland Centre, a new Resource Centre on education for intercultural dialogue, human rights and democratic citizenship, operated in cooperation with the Norwegian Government, opened in <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, in February 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Council of Europe has offices in <a href="/wiki/Albania" title="Albania">Albania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bosnia_and_Herzegovina" title="Bosnia and Herzegovina">Bosnia and Herzegovina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Georgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moldova" title="Moldova">Moldova</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montenegro" title="Montenegro">Montenegro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>; information offices in Albania, Armenia, Azerbaijan, <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a>, Georgia, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a>, Moldova, <a href="/wiki/North_Macedonia" title="North Macedonia">North Macedonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russian_Federation" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian Federation">Russian Federation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a>, and Ukraine; and a projects office in <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>. All these offices are establishments of the Council of Europe and they share its juridical personality with privileges and immunities.
</p><p>Due to persistent budgetary shortages, the Council of Europe is expected to cut down significantly the number of its activities, and thus the number of its employees, from 2011 on. This will notably affect the economy of the city of Strasbourg, where a total of 2,321 people (on 1 January 2010) are doing salaried work for the CoE. Most offices in foreign countries are expected to be closed as well.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Member_states.2C_observers.2C_partners"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Member_states,_observers,_partners">Member states, observers, partners</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Council_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Member states, observers, partners">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Member_states_of_the_Council_of_Europe" title="Member states of the Council of Europe">Member states of the Council of Europe</a></div>
<p>The Council of Europe was founded on 5 May 1949 by <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> joined three months later, and <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> the next year. It now has 47 member states, with <a href="/wiki/Montenegro" title="Montenegro">Montenegro</a> being the latest to join.
</p><p>Article 4 of the <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_the_Council_of_Europe" title="Statute of the Council of Europe">Council of Europe Statute</a> specifies that membership is open to any "European" State.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> This has been interpreted liberally from the beginning, when Turkey was admitted, to include <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Europe#Countries_bordering_or_spanning_another_continent" title="Geography of Europe">transcontinental states</a> (such as Georgia and Azerbaijan) and states that are geographically Asian but socio-politically European (such as Armenia and Cyprus).
</p><p>Nearly all <a href="/wiki/List_of_sovereign_states_and_dependent_territories_in_Europe" title="List of sovereign states and dependent territories in Europe">European states</a> have acceded to the Council of Europe, with the exceptions of <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a> (<a href="/wiki/Human_rights_in_Belarus" title="Human rights in Belarus">human rights concerns</a> including <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment_in_Belarus" title="Capital punishment in Belarus">active use of the death penalty</a>), <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> (<a href="/wiki/Human_rights_in_Kazakhstan" title="Human rights in Kazakhstan">human rights concerns</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Vatican_City" title="Vatican City">Vatican City</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Theocracy" title="Theocracy">theocracy</a>), as well as some of the territories <a href="/wiki/List_of_states_with_limited_recognition" title="List of states with limited recognition">with limited recognition</a>.
</p><p>Besides the status as a full member, the Council of Europe has established <a href="/wiki/Member_states_of_the_Council_of_Europe#Observers" title="Member states of the Council of Europe">other instruments for cooperation and participation</a> of non-member states: observer, applicant, special guest, and partner for democracy.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Co-operation">Co-operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Council_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Co-operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-member_states">Non-member states</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Council_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Non-member states">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Council of Europe works mainly through conventions. By drafting conventions or international treaties, common legal standards are set for its member states. However, several conventions have also been opened for signature to non-member states. Important examples are the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_Cybercrime" title="Convention on Cybercrime">Convention on Cybercrime</a> (signed for example, by Canada, Japan, South Africa and the United States), the <a href="/wiki/Lisbon_Recognition_Convention" title="Lisbon Recognition Convention">Lisbon Recognition Convention</a> on the recognition of study periods and degrees (signed for example, by Australia, Belarus, Canada, the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a>, Israel, Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, New Zealand and the United States), the Anti-<a href="/wiki/Doping_(sport)" class="mw-redirect" title="Doping (sport)">doping</a> Convention (signed, for example, by Australia, Belarus, Canada and Tunisia) and the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_Conservation_of_European_Wildlife_and_Natural_Habitats" class="mw-redirect" title="Convention on the Conservation of European Wildlife and Natural Habitats">Convention on the Conservation of European Wildlife and Natural Habitats</a> (signed for example, by Burkina Faso, Morocco, Tunisia and Senegal as well as the <a href="/wiki/European_Community" class="mw-redirect" title="European Community">European Community</a>). Non-member states also participate in several partial agreements, such as the <a href="/wiki/Venice_Commission" title="Venice Commission">Venice Commission</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Group_of_States_Against_Corruption" title="Group of States Against Corruption">Group of States Against Corruption</a> (GRECO), the <a href="/wiki/European_Pharmacopoeia" title="European Pharmacopoeia">European Pharmacopoeia</a> Commission and the <a href="/wiki/North-South_Centre" class="mw-redirect" title="North-South Centre">North-South Centre</a>.
</p><p>Invitations to sign and ratify relevant conventions of the Council of Europe on a case-by-case basis are sent to three groups of non-member entities:<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Non-European states: Algeria, Argentina, Australia, Bahamas, Bolivia, Brazil, Burkina Faso, Chile, China, Colombia, Costa Rica, Dominican Republic, Ecuador, El Salvador, Honduras, South Korea, Kyrgyzstan, Lebanon, Malaysia, Mauritius, Morocco, New Zealand, Panama, Peru, Philippines, Senegal, South Africa, Syria, Tajikistan, Tonga, Trinidad and Tobago, Tunisia, Uruguay, Venezuela and the observers Canada, Israel, Japan, Mexico, United States.</li>
<li>European states: Kosovo, Kazakhstan, Belarus and the observer Vatican City.</li>
<li>the European Community and later the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> after its <a href="/wiki/Legal_personality" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal personality">legal personality</a> was established by the ratification of the EU's <a href="/wiki/Lisbon_Treaty" class="mw-redirect" title="Lisbon Treaty">Lisbon Treaty</a>.</li></ul>

<p>The Council of Europe is not to be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_the_European_Union" title="Council of the European Union">Council of the European Union</a> (the "Council of Ministers") or the <a href="/wiki/European_Council" title="European Council">European Council</a>. These belong to the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, which is separate from the Council of Europe, although they have shared the same European flag and anthem since the 1980s because they both work for <a href="/wiki/European_integration" title="European integration">European integration</a>. The Council of Europe is not to be confused with the European Union itself.
</p><p>The Council of Europe is an entirely separate body<sup id="cite_ref-COE_not_EU_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-COE_not_EU-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> from the European Union. It is not controlled by it.
</p><p>Cooperation between the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> and the Council of Europe has recently been reinforced, notably on culture and education as well as on the international enforcement of justice and Human Rights.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The European Union is expected to accede to the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a> (the Convention). There are also concerns about consistency in case law – the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Justice" title="European Court of Justice">European Court of Justice</a> (the EU's court in <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a>) is treating the Convention as part of the legal system of all <a href="/wiki/Member_state_of_the_European_Union" title="Member state of the European Union">EU member states</a> in order to prevent conflict between its judgements and those of the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a> (the court in <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a> interpreting the Convention). Protocol No. 14 of the Convention is designed to allow the EU to accede to it and the EU <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Lisbon" title="Treaty of Lisbon">Treaty of Lisbon</a> contains a protocol binding the EU to join. The EU would thus be subject to its human rights law and external monitoring as its member states currently are.<sup id="cite_ref-Juncker_Council_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Juncker_Council-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Reform_Draft_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reform_Draft-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Nations">United Nations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Council_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: United Nations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The beginning of co-operation between the CoE and the UN started with the agreement signed by the Secretariats of these institutions on 15 December 1951. On 17 October 1989, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">General Assembly of the United Nations</a> approved a resolution on granting observer status to the Council of Europe which was proposed by several member states of the CoE.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Currently Council of Europe holds <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly_observers" title="United Nations General Assembly observers">observer status</a> with the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> and is regularly represented in the <a href="/wiki/UN_General_Assembly" class="mw-redirect" title="UN General Assembly">UN General Assembly</a>. It has organised the regional UN conferences against racism and on women and co-operates with the United Nations at many levels, in particular in the areas of human rights, minorities, migration and counter-terrorism. In November 2016, the UN General Assembly adopted by consensus Resolution (A/Res/71/17) on Cooperation between the United Nations and the Council of Europe whereby it acknowledged the contribution of Council of Europe to the protection and strengthening of human rights and fundamental freedoms, democracy and the rule of law, welcomed the ongoing co-operation in a variety of fields.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-governmental_organisations">Non-governmental organisations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Council_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Non-governmental organisations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Non-governmental organisations (<a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">NGOs</a>) can participate in the <a href="/wiki/INGOs_Conference_of_the_Council_of_Europe" title="INGOs Conference of the Council of Europe">INGOs Conference of the Council of Europe</a> and become observers to inter-governmental committees of experts. The Council of Europe drafted the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_the_Recognition_of_the_Legal_Personality_of_International_Non-Governmental_Organisations" title="European Convention on the Recognition of the Legal Personality of International Non-Governmental Organisations">European Convention on the Recognition of the Legal Personality of International Non-Governmental Organisations</a> in 1986, which sets the legal basis for the existence and work of NGOs in Europe. Article 11 of the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a> protects the right to freedom of association, which is also a fundamental norm for NGOs. The rules for <a href="/wiki/Consultative_Status" class="mw-redirect" title="Consultative Status">Consultative Status</a> for INGOs appended to the resolution (93)38 "On relation between the Council of Europe and <a href="/wiki/International_nongovernmental_organizations" class="mw-redirect" title="International nongovernmental organizations">non-governmental organisations</a>", adopted by the Committee of Ministers on 18 October 1993 at the 500th meeting of the Ministers' Deputies.
On 19 November 2003 the Committee of Ministers changed the consultative status into a participatory status, "considering that it is indispensable that the rules governing the relations between the Council of Europe and NGOs evolve to reflect the active participation of international non-governmental organisations (INGOs) in the Organisation's policy and work programme".<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Others">Others</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Council_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Others">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 30 May 2018, the Council of Europe signed a <a href="/wiki/Memorandum_of_understanding" title="Memorandum of understanding">Memorandum of understanding</a> with the European <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> confederation <a href="/wiki/UEFA" title="UEFA">UEFA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Council of Europe also signed an agreement with FIFA in which the two agreed to strengthen future cooperation in areas of common interests. The deal which included cooperation between member states in the sport of football and safety and security at football matches, was finalized in October 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Council_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Privileges_and_immunities">Privileges and immunities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Council_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Privileges and immunities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The General Agreement on Privileges and Immunities of the Council of Europe grants the organisation certain privileges and immunities.<sup id="cite_ref-priv-immu-EC_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-priv-immu-EC-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The working conditions of staff are governed by the Council's staff regulations, which are public.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Salaries and emoluments paid by the Council of Europe to its officials are tax-exempt on the basis of Article&#160;18 of the General Agreement on Privileges and Immunities of the Council of Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-priv-immu-EC_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-priv-immu-EC-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Symbol_and_anthem">Symbol and anthem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Council_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Symbol and anthem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Symbols_of_Europe" title="Symbols of Europe">Symbols of Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Europe" title="Flag of Europe">Flag of Europe</a></div>
<p>The Council of Europe created, and has since 1955 used as its official symbol, the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Europe" title="Flag of Europe">European Flag</a> with 12 golden stars arranged in a circle on a blue background.
</p><p>Its musical anthem since 1972, the "<a href="/wiki/European_Anthem" class="mw-redirect" title="European Anthem">European Anthem</a>", is based on the "<a href="/wiki/Ode_to_Joy" title="Ode to Joy">Ode to Joy</a>" theme from <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Ludwig van Beethoven</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._9_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 9 (Beethoven)">Ninth symphony</a>.
</p><p>On 5 May 1964, the 15th anniversary of its founding, the Council of Europe established 5 May as <a href="/wiki/Europe_Day" title="Europe Day">Europe Day</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-symbols_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-symbols-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The wide private and public use of the European Flag is encouraged to symbolise a European dimension. To avoid confusion with the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> which subsequently adopted the same flag in the 1980s, as well as other European institutions, the Council of Europe often uses a modified version with a lower-case "e" surrounding the stars which is referred to as the "Council of Europe Logo".<sup id="cite_ref-symbols_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-symbols-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_and_controversies">Criticism and controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Council_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Criticism and controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In recent years,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (March 2019)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> the Council of Europe has been criticised for doing too little to stand up to the transgressions of some of its members. In 2013 <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i> agreed, saying that the "Council of Europe's credibility is on the line".<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> Both Human Rights Watch and the European Stability Initiative have called on the Council of Europe to undertake concrete actions to show that it is willing and able to return to its "original mission to protect and ensure human rights".<sup id="cite_ref-ESI_HavelPrize_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ESI_HavelPrize-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Issues have been raised regarding <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a>'s relationship to the Council of Europe, including allegations that Azerbaijan has, over a sustained period, provided bribes to Council members to vote down criticism of the authoritarian rule of the <a href="/wiki/Ilham_Aliyev" title="Ilham Aliyev">Aliyev</a> regime and support motions advantageous to Azerbaijan. Azerbaijan joined the Council of Europe in 2001. Since September 2014 <a href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Watch" title="Human Rights Watch">Human Rights Watch</a>, said that Azerbaijan's "systematic crackdown on human rights defenders and other perceived government critics shows sheer contempt for its commitments to the Council of Europe".<sup id="cite_ref-HRW_Criticism_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HRW_Criticism-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> In 2017 Council member and Italian politician Luca Volontè was accused by Italian prosecutors of receiving over 2.3 million euros in bribes in exchange for working for Azerbaijan in the parliamentary assembly, and that in 2013 he played a key role in orchestrating the defeat of a highly critical report on the abuse of political prisoners in Azerbaijan.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> The money was paid to Volontè in monthly instalments of 100,000 euros, starting in 2013, via four anonymous offshore companies. The payments stopped in 2014 when Volontè's bank reported them as suspicious transactions to the Milan prosecutor's office.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Arif_Mammadov_(ambassador)" title="Arif Mammadov (ambassador)">Arif Mammadov</a>, former head of the Azerbaijan representation at the Council of Europe, has stated that Azerbaijan's delegation at the Council had 25 million dollars available to "bribe members of the delegations and <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Assembly_of_the_Council_of_Europe" title="Parliamentary Assembly of the Council of Europe">PACE</a> generally".
</p><p>From 2014, Russia's voting rights were temporarily suspended by the Council due to Moscow's annexation of Crimea from Ukraine. In response, Russia withheld its annual membership dues in the amount of 33 million euros,<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> placing the institution under financial strain.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> Russia claimed that its suspension by the Council is unfair and demands the restoration of voting rights.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> European Council secretary-general <a href="/wiki/Thorbj%C3%B8rn_Jagland" title="Thorbjørn Jagland">Thorbjørn Jagland</a> organized a special committee to find a compromise with Russia in early 2018, a move that was criticized as giving in to alleged Russian pressure by Council members and academic observers, especially if voting sanctions were lifted.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_70-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_71-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> In May 2019, Russia's voting rights were restored after members of the human rights watchdog reached agreement to resolve dispute and the overwhelming majority of the Council voted in favour of the restoration.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
