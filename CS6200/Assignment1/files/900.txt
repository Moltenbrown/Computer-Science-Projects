<p><b>Francisco de Vitoria</b> <span class="noexcerpt" style="font-size:85%;"><a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">OP</a></span> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;1483</span> – 12 August 1546; also known as <b>Francisco de Victoria</b>) was a Spanish Roman Catholic <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosopher</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theologian</a>, and jurist of <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> Spain. He is the founder of the tradition in <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> known as the <a href="/wiki/School_of_Salamanca" title="School of Salamanca">School of Salamanca</a>, noted especially for his contributions to the theory of <a href="/wiki/Just_war" class="mw-redirect" title="Just war">just war</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international law</a>. He has in the past been described by some scholars as one of the "fathers of international law", along with <a href="/wiki/Alberico_Gentili" title="Alberico Gentili">Alberico Gentili</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Grotius" title="Hugo Grotius">Hugo Grotius</a>, though contemporary academics have suggested that such a description is <a href="/wiki/Anachronistic" class="mw-redirect" title="Anachronistic">anachronistic</a>, since the concept of international law did not truly develop until much later.<sup id="cite_ref-Woods_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Woods-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pagden_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pagden-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> American <a href="/wiki/Jurist" title="Jurist">jurist</a> <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Nussbaum" title="Arthur Nussbaum">Arthur Nussbaum</a> noted that Vitoria was "the first to set forth the notions (though not the terms) of <a href="/wiki/Free_trade" title="Free trade">freedom of commerce</a> and <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_the_seas" title="Freedom of the seas">freedom of the seas</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-Nussbaum1947_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nussbaum1947-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_de_Vitoria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vitoria was born <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;1483</span> in <a href="/wiki/Burgos" title="Burgos">Burgos</a> or <a href="/wiki/Vitoria-Gasteiz" title="Vitoria-Gasteiz">Vitoria-Gasteiz</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and was raised in <a href="/wiki/Burgos" title="Burgos">Burgos</a>, the son of Pedro de Vitoria, of Alava, and Catalina de Compludo, both of noble families.<sup id="cite_ref-Hernandez_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hernandez-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> As per modern scholarship, he had <a href="/wiki/Jewish" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> ancestry on his maternal side (the Compludos), being related to famous converts like <a href="/wiki/Paul_of_Burgos" title="Paul of Burgos">Paul of Burgos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_de_Cartagena" title="Alfonso de Cartagena">Alfonso de Cartagena</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> He became a <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominican</a> in 1504, and was educated at the College Saint-Jacques in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, where he was influenced by the work of <a href="/wiki/Desiderius_Erasmus" class="mw-redirect" title="Desiderius Erasmus">Desiderius Erasmus</a>. He went on to teach theology from 1516 (under the influences of <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Crockaert" class="mw-redirect" title="Pierre Crockaert">Pierre Crockaert</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Cajetan" title="Thomas Cajetan">Thomas Cajetan</a>). In 1522 he returned to <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> to teach theology at the college of Saint Gregory at <a href="/wiki/Valladolid" title="Valladolid">Valladolid</a>, where many young Dominicans were being trained for missionary work in the New World. In 1524, he was elected to the Chair of theology at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Salamanca" title="University of Salamanca">University of Salamanca</a>, where he was instrumental in promoting <a href="/wiki/Thomism" title="Thomism">Thomism</a> (the philosophy and theology of <a href="/wiki/St._Thomas_Aquinas" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Thomas Aquinas">St. Thomas Aquinas</a>). Francisco de Vitoria died on 12 August 1546<sup id="cite_ref-Schroeder_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schroeder-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Salamanca" title="Salamanca">Salamanca</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Defense_of_Amerindians">Defense of Amerindians</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_de_Vitoria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Defense of Amerindians">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Francisco_de_Vitoria.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Francisco_de_Vitoria.jpg/220px-Francisco_de_Vitoria.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Francisco_de_Vitoria.jpg/330px-Francisco_de_Vitoria.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Francisco_de_Vitoria.jpg/440px-Francisco_de_Vitoria.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Francisco_de_Vitoria.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Francisco de Vitoria, Statue before San Esteban, <a href="/wiki/Salamanca" title="Salamanca">Salamanca</a></div></div></div>
<p>A noted scholar, he was publicly consulted by <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor</a> and King of Spain. He worked to limit the type of power the Spanish Empire imposed on the Native Peoples. He said, "The upshot of all the preceding is this, then, that the aborigines undoubtedly had true dominion in both public and private matters, just like Christians, and that neither their princes nor private persons could be despoiled of their property on the ground of their not being true owners."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Vitoria denied that the native peoples could be understood as slaves by nature in Aristotelian terms.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> He adopted from Aquinas the Roman law concept of <i>ius gentium</i> ("the law of nations"). His defense of American Indians was based on a Scholastic understanding of the intrinsic dignity of man, a dignity he found being violated by Spain's policies in the New World.
</p><p>In three lectures (relectiones) held between 1537 and 1539 Vitoria concluded that the Indians were rightful owners of their property and that their chiefs validly exercised jurisdiction over their tribes. This had already been the position of <a href="/wiki/Juan_L%C3%B3pez_de_Palacios_Rubios" title="Juan López de Palacios Rubios">Palacios Rubios</a>. Neither the pope nor Charles V had a rightful claim over Indian lives or property. No violent action could be taken against them, nor could their lands or property be seized, unless the Indians had caused harm or injury to the Spanish by violating the latter's lawful rights.<sup id="cite_ref-Pagden_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pagden-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A supporter of the <a href="/wiki/Just_war_theory" title="Just war theory">just war theory</a>, in <i>De iure belli</i> Francisco pointed out that the underlying predicate conditions for a "just war" were "wholly lacking in the Indies".<sup id="cite_ref-Salas_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salas-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The only area where he saw justification for Spanish 
intervention in native affairs was to protect victims seized for human sacrifice, and because of the inherent human dignity of the victims themselves—whose rights were being violated and thus in need of defense.<sup id="cite_ref-Salas_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salas-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Woods" title="Thomas Woods">Thomas E. Woods</a> goes on to describe how some wished to argue that the natives lacked reason, but the evidence was against this because the natives had obvious customs, laws, and a form of government.<sup id="cite_ref-Woods_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Woods-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Spaniards were in the practice of invoking in their American conquests the so-called "Requerimiento", a document read to the Indians before the commencement of any hostilities. The "Requerimiento", declared the universal authority of the Pope, and the
authority the Spanish monarchs had received from the Pope over this part of the New World for the purpose of colonizing and evangelizing it. The Indians had to accept the sovereignty of the Spanish monarchs or be compelled to submit by force. Vitoria denied the legitimacy of this document.<sup id="cite_ref-Hernandez_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hernandez-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>His works are known only from his lecture notes, as he has published nothing in his lifetime.  Nevertheless, his influence such as that on the Dutch legal philosopher <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Grotius" title="Hugo Grotius">Hugo Grotius</a> was significant.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> <i>Relectiones XII Theologicae in duo libros distinctae</i> was published posthumously (Antwerp, 1604).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Francisco de Vitoria's writings have been interpreted by various scholars to support contrary policies.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Antony Anghie and others argue that Vitoria's humanitarianism legitimized conquest.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
