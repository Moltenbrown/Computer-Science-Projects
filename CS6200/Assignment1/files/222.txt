<p><b>Thatching</b> is the craft of building a <a href="/wiki/Roof" title="Roof">roof</a> with dry vegetation such as <a href="/wiki/Straw" title="Straw">straw</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phragmites" title="Phragmites">water reed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyperaceae" title="Cyperaceae">sedge</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Cladium_mariscus" title="Cladium mariscus">Cladium mariscus</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Juncus" title="Juncus">rushes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calluna" title="Calluna">heather</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Palm_branch" title="Palm branch">palm branches</a>, layering the vegetation so as to shed water away from the inner roof. Since the bulk of the vegetation stays dry and is densely packed—trapping air—thatching also functions as insulation. It is a very old roofing method and has been used in both <a href="/wiki/Tropical" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical">tropical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Temperate_climates" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperate climates">temperate climates</a>. Thatch is still employed by builders in developing countries, usually with low-cost local vegetation. By contrast, in some developed countries it is  the choice of some affluent people who desire a rustic look for their home, would like a more ecologically friendly roof, or who have purchased an originally thatched abode.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thatching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Thatching methods have traditionally been passed down from generation to generation, and numerous descriptions of the materials and methods used in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> over the past three centuries survive in archives and early publications.
</p><p>In some equatorial countries, thatch is the prevalent local material for <a href="/wiki/Roof" title="Roof">roofs</a>, and often <a href="/wiki/Wall" title="Wall">walls</a>. There are diverse building techniques from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Hawaiian" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Hawaiian">ancient Hawaiian</a> <i>hale</i> shelter made from the local <a href="/wiki/Cordyline_fruticosa" title="Cordyline fruticosa">ti leaves</a> (<i>Cordyline fruticosa</i>), <a href="/wiki/Lauhala" title="Lauhala">lauhala</a> (<i>Pandanus tectorius</i>) <sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Heteropogon_contortus" title="Heteropogon contortus">pili grass</a> (<i>Heteropogon contortus</i>).
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PrayerAtBesakih.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/PrayerAtBesakih.jpg/220px-PrayerAtBesakih.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/PrayerAtBesakih.jpg/330px-PrayerAtBesakih.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/PrayerAtBesakih.jpg/440px-PrayerAtBesakih.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2288" data-file-height="1712" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PrayerAtBesakih.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The multi-tiered <a href="/wiki/Meru_tower" title="Meru tower">Meru towers</a> of <a href="/wiki/Besakih" class="mw-redirect" title="Besakih">Besakih</a> temple, <a href="/wiki/Bali" title="Bali">Bali</a>, uses black <i><a href="/wiki/Arenga_pinnata" title="Arenga pinnata">ijuk</a></i> fibres.</div></div></div>
<p>Palm leaves are also often used. For example, in Na Bure, <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a>, thatchers combine fan palm leave roofs with layered reed walls. Feathered palm leaf roofs are used in Dominica.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Imperata_cylindrica" title="Imperata cylindrica">Alang-alang</a> (<i>Imperata cylindrica</i>) thatched roofs are used in Hawaii and <a href="/wiki/Bali" title="Bali">Bali</a>. In Southeast Asia, mangrove <a href="/wiki/Nypa_fruticans" title="Nypa fruticans">nipa palm</a> leaves are used as thatched roof material known as <a href="/wiki/Attap_dwelling" title="Attap dwelling">attap dwelling</a>. In Bali, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, the black fibres of <a href="/wiki/Arenga_pinnata" title="Arenga pinnata">Arenga pinnata</a> called <i>ijuk</i> is also used as thatched roof materials, usually used in <a href="/wiki/Balinese_temple" title="Balinese temple">Balinese temple</a> roof and meru towers.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sugar_cane" class="mw-redirect" title="Sugar cane">Sugar cane</a> leaf roofs are used in Kikuyu tribal homes in <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Wild vegetation such as water reed (<i>Phragmites australis</i>), bulrush/cat tail (<i>Typha</i> spp.), broom (<i>Cytisus scoparius</i>), heather (<i>Calluna vulgaris</i>), and rushes (<i>Juncus</i> spp. and <i>Schoenoplectus lacustris</i>) was probably used to cover shelters and primitive dwellings in Europe in the late Palaeolithic period, but so far no direct archaeological evidence for this has been recovered. People probably began to use straw in the Neolithic period when they first grew cereals—but once again, no direct archaeological evidence of straw for thatching in Europe prior to the early medieval period survives.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELetts2000_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELetts2000-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (February 2013)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Many <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_people_of_the_Americas" class="mw-redirect" title="Indigenous people of the Americas">indigenous</a> people of the Americas, such as the former Maya civilization, Mesoamerica, the Inca empire, and the Triple Alliance (Aztec), lived in thatched buildings. It is common to spot thatched buildings in rural areas of the Yucatán Peninsula as well as many settlements in other parts of Latin America, which closely resemble the method of construction from distant ancestors. After the collapse of most extant American societies due to diseases introduced by Europeans, wars, enslavement, and genocide, the first Americans encountered by Europeans lived in structures roofed with bark or skin set in panels that could be added or removed for ventilation, heating, and cooling.  Evidence of the many complex buildings with fiber-based roofing material was not rediscovered until the early 2000s. French and British settlers built temporary thatched dwellings with local vegetation as soon as they arrived in New France and New England, but covered more permanent houses with wooden shingles.
</p><p>In most of England, thatch remained the only roofing material available to the bulk of the population in the countryside, in many towns and villages, until the late 1800s.<sup id="cite_ref-MoirLetts_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MoirLetts-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Slate_industry_in_Wales" title="Slate industry in Wales">Commercial distribution of Welsh slate</a> began in 1820, and the mobility provided by <a href="/wiki/Canals" class="mw-redirect" title="Canals">canals</a> and then <a href="/wiki/Railways" class="mw-redirect" title="Railways">railways</a> made other materials readily available. Still, the number of thatched properties actually increased in the UK during the mid-1800s as agriculture expanded, but then declined again at the end of the 19th century because of agricultural recession and rural depopulation. A 2013 report estimated that there were 60,000 properties in the UK with a thatched roof; they are usually made of long straw, combed wheat reed or water reed.
<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Gradually, thatch became a mark of poverty, and the number of thatched properties gradually declined, as did the number of professional thatchers. Thatch has become much more popular in the UK over the past 30 years, and is now a symbol of wealth rather than poverty. There are approximately 1,000 full-time thatchers at work in the UK,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and thatching is becoming popular again because of the renewed interest in preserving historic buildings and using more sustainable building materials.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Material">Material</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thatching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Material">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ogi_Shirakawa06n3200.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Ogi_Shirakawa06n3200.jpg/220px-Ogi_Shirakawa06n3200.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Ogi_Shirakawa06n3200.jpg/330px-Ogi_Shirakawa06n3200.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Ogi_Shirakawa06n3200.jpg/440px-Ogi_Shirakawa06n3200.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3200" data-file-height="2133" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ogi_Shirakawa06n3200.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Straw-thatched house at <a href="/wiki/Historic_Villages_of_Shirakawa-g%C5%8D_and_Gokayama" title="Historic Villages of Shirakawa-gō and Gokayama">historic village of Shirakawa-go</a> (<a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>) in Japan.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Korea_south_traditional_house.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Korea_south_traditional_house.jpg/220px-Korea_south_traditional_house.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="143" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Korea_south_traditional_house.jpg/330px-Korea_south_traditional_house.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/57/Korea_south_traditional_house.jpg 2x" data-file-width="361" data-file-height="234" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Korea_south_traditional_house.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Korean traditional straw thatched house.</div></div></div>
<p>Although thatch is popular in Germany, The Netherlands, Denmark, parts of France, Sicily, Belgium and Ireland, there are more thatched roofs in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> than in any other European country.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="reliable source needed for the whole sentence (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Good quality straw thatch can last for more than 50 years when applied by a skilled thatcher. Traditionally, a new layer of straw was simply applied over the weathered surface, and this "spar coating" tradition has created accumulations of thatch over 7’ (2.1&#160;m) thick on very old buildings. The straw is bundled into "yelms" before it is taken up to the roof and then is attached using staples, known as "spars", made from twisted hazel sticks. Over 250 roofs in Southern England have base coats of thatch that were applied over 500 years ago, providing direct evidence of the types of materials that were used for thatching in the medieval period.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELetts2000_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELetts2000-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (February 2013)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  Almost all of these roofs are thatched with wheat, rye, or a "maslin" mixture of both.  Medieval wheat grew to almost 6 feet (1.8&#160;m) tall in very poor soils and produced durable straw for the roof and grain for baking bread.
</p><p>Technological change in the <a href="/wiki/Farming" class="mw-redirect" title="Farming">farming</a> <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industry</a> significantly affected the popularity of thatching. The availability of good quality thatching straw declined in England after the introduction of the combine harvester in the late 1930s and 1940s, and the release of short-stemmed wheat varieties. Increasing use of nitrogen fertiliser in the 1960s–70s also weakened straw and reduced its longevity. Since the 1980s, however, there has been a big increase in straw quality as specialist growers have returned to growing older, tall-stemmed, "heritage" varieties of wheat such as Squareheads Master (1880), N59 (1959), Rampton Rivet (1937), Victor (1910) and April Bearded (early 1800s)] in low input/organic conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the UK it is illegal under the <a href="/wiki/Plant_Variety_and_Seeds_Act_1964" class="mw-redirect" title="Plant Variety and Seeds Act 1964">Plant Variety and Seeds Act 1964</a> (with many amendments) for an individual or organisation to give, trade or sell seed of an older variety of wheat (or any other agricultural crop) to a third party for growing purposes, subject to a significant fine.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  Because of this legislation, thatchers in the UK can no longer obtain top quality thatching straw grown from traditional, tall-stemmed varieties of wheat.
</p><p>All evidence indicates that water reed was rarely used for thatching outside of East Anglia.<sup id="cite_ref-MoirLetts_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MoirLetts-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> It has traditionally been a "one coat" material applied in a similar way to how it is used in continental Europe. Weathered reed is usually stripped and replaced by a new layer. It takes 4–5 acres of well-managed reed bed to produce enough reed to thatch an average house, and large reed beds have been uncommon in most of England since the Anglo-Saxon period. Over 80% of the water reed used in the UK is now imported from <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>, China and South Africa. Though water reed might last for 50 years or more on a steep roof in a dry climate, modern imported water reed on an average roof in England does not last any longer than good quality wheat straw. The lifespan of a thatched roof also depends on the skill of the thatcher, but other factors must be considered—such as <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a>, quality of materials, and the roof pitch.
</p><p>In areas where <a href="/wiki/Arecaceae" title="Arecaceae">palms</a> are abundant, palm leaves are used to thatch walls and roofs. Many species of palm trees are called "<a href="/wiki/Thatch_palm" title="Thatch palm">thatch palm</a>", or have "thatch" as part of their common names. In the <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_United_States" title="Southeastern United States">southeastern United States</a>, Native and pioneer houses were often constructed of <a href="/wiki/Arecaceae" title="Arecaceae">palmetto</a>-leaf thatch.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Chickee" title="Chickee">chickees</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Seminole" title="Seminole">Seminole</a> and <a href="/wiki/Miccosukee" title="Miccosukee">Miccosukee</a> are still thatched with palmetto leaves.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Maintenance_in_temperate_climates">Maintenance in temperate climates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thatching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Maintenance in temperate climates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Good thatch does not require frequent maintenance. In England a ridge normally lasts 8–14 years, and re-ridging is required several times during the lifespan of a thatch. Thatched roofs generally need replacement when the horizontal wooden 'sways' and hair-pin 'spars', also known as 'gads' (twisted hazel staples) that fix each course become visible near the surface. It is not total depth of the thatch within a new layer applied to a new roof that determines its longevity, but rather how much weathering thatch covers the fixings of each overlapping course. “A roof is as good as the amount of correctly laid thatch covering the fixings.”<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flammability">Flammability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thatching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Flammability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:House_river_kwai.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/House_river_kwai.jpg/220px-House_river_kwai.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/House_river_kwai.jpg/330px-House_river_kwai.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/House_river_kwai.jpg/440px-House_river_kwai.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2560" data-file-height="1920" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:House_river_kwai.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>River houses on the <a href="/wiki/Khwae_Noi_River" title="Khwae Noi River">River Kwai</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Thatch is not as flammable as many people believe. It burns slowly, "like a closed book," thatchers say. The vast majority of fires are linked to the use of wood burners and faulty chimneys with degraded or poorly installed or maintained flues. Sparks from paper or burned rubbish can ignite dry thatch on the surface around a chimney. Fires can also begin when sparks or flames work their way through a degraded chimney and ignite the surrounding semi-charred thatch. This can be avoided by ensuring that the chimney is in good condition, which may involve stripping thatch immediately surrounding the chimney to the full depth of the stack. This can easily be done without stripping thatch over the entire roof. Insurance premiums on thatched houses are higher than average in part because of the perception that thatched roofs are a fire hazard, but also because a thatch fire can cause extensive smoke damage and a thatched roof is more expensive to replace than a standard tiled or slate roof. Workmen should never use open flame near thatch, and nothing should be burnt that could fly up the chimney and ignite the surface of the thatch. <a href="/wiki/Spark_arrestor" title="Spark arrestor">Spark arrestors</a> usually cause more harm than good, as they are easily blocked and reduce air flow. All thatched roofs should have smoke detectors in the roof space. Spray-on fire retardant or pressure impregnated fire retardants can reduce the spread of flame and radiated heat output.
</p><p>On new buildings, a solid fire retardant barrier over the rafters can make the thatch sacrificial in case of fire. If fireboards are used, they require a ventilation gap between boarding and thatch so that the roof can breathe, as condensation can be a significant problem in thin, single layer thatch. Condensation is much less of a problem on thick straw roofs, which also provide much better insulation since they do not need to be ventilated.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Performance">Performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thatching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The performance of thatch depends on roof shape and design, pitch of roof, position—its <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a>—the quality of material and the expertise of the thatcher.
</p><p>Thatch has some natural properties that are advantageous to its performance. It is naturally weather-resistant, and when properly maintained does not absorb a lot of water. There should not be a significant increase to roof weight due to water retention. A roof pitch of at least 50 degrees allows precipitation to travel quickly down slope so that it runs off the roof before it can penetrate the structure.
</p><p>Thatch is also a natural insulator, and air pockets within straw thatch insulate a building in both warm and cold weather. A thatched roof ensures that a building is cool in summer and warm in winter.
</p><p>Thatch also has very good resistance to wind damage when applied correctly.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages">Advantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thatching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Advantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Thatching materials range from plains grasses to waterproof leaves found in equatorial regions. It is the most common roofing material in the world, because the materials are readily available.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Partially_thatched_roof.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Partially_thatched_roof.jpg/220px-Partially_thatched_roof.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Partially_thatched_roof.jpg/330px-Partially_thatched_roof.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Partially_thatched_roof.jpg/440px-Partially_thatched_roof.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1954" data-file-height="1465" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Partially_thatched_roof.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Farm-house in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> near <a href="/wiki/Alkmaar" title="Alkmaar">Alkmaar</a>. The combination of thatch and <a href="/wiki/Roof_tiles" title="Roof tiles">roof tiles</a> is quite common in that area.</div></div></div>
<p>Because thatch is lighter, less timber is required in the roof that supports it.
</p><p>Thatch is a versatile material when it comes to covering irregular roof structures. This fact lends itself to the use of second-hand, <a href="/wiki/Recycled" class="mw-redirect" title="Recycled">recycled</a> and natural materials that are not only more <a href="/wiki/Sustainable" class="mw-redirect" title="Sustainable">sustainable</a>, but need not fit exact standard dimensions to perform well.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disadvantages">Disadvantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thatching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Disadvantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Thatched houses are harder to insure because of the perceived fire risk, and because thatching is labor-intensive, it is much more expensive to thatch a roof than to cover it with slate or tiles. Birds can damage a roof while they are foraging for grubs, and rodents are attracted by residual grain in straw.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lucy_in_the_sky.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Lucy_in_the_sky.jpg/220px-Lucy_in_the_sky.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Lucy_in_the_sky.jpg/330px-Lucy_in_the_sky.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Lucy_in_the_sky.jpg/440px-Lucy_in_the_sky.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lucy_in_the_sky.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Thatched hut in <a href="/wiki/Lesotho" title="Lesotho">Lesotho</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Thatch has fallen out of favor in much of the industrialized world not because of fire, but because thatching has become very expensive and alternative 'hard' materials are cheaper—but this situation is slowly changing. There are about 60,000 thatched roofs in the UK, of which 50–80 suffer a serious fire each year, most of these being completely destroyed. The cost to the Fire Brigade is £1.3m per annum.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Many more thatched roofs are being built every year.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>New thatched roofs were forbidden in London in 1212 following a major fire,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and existing roofs had to have their surfaces plastered to reduce the risk of fire. The modern <a href="/wiki/Shakespeare%27s_Globe" title="Shakespeare&#39;s Globe">Globe Theatre</a> is one of the few thatched buildings in London (others can be found in the suburb of <a href="/wiki/Kingsbury,_London" title="Kingsbury, London">Kingsbury</a>), but the Globe's modern, water reed thatch is purely for decorative purpose and actually lies over a fully waterproofed roof built with modern materials. The Globe Theatre, opened in 1997, was modelled on the Rose, which was destroyed by a fire on a dry June night in 1613 when a burning wad of cloth ejected from a special effects cannon during a performance set light to the surface of the thatch. The original Rose Theatre was actually thatched with cereal straw, a sample of which was recovered by Museum of London archaeologists during the excavation of the site in the 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some claim thatch cannot cope with regular snowfall but, as with all roofing materials, this depends on the strength of the underlying roof structure and the pitch of the surface. A law passed in 1640 in <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> outlawed the use of thatched roofs in the colony for this reason. Thatch is lighter than most other roofing materials, typically around 7&#160;lb/sq&#160;ft (34&#160;kg/m<sup>2</sup>), so the roof supporting it does not need to be so heavily constructed, but if snow accumulates on a lightly constructed thatched roof, it could collapse. A thatched roof is usually pitched between 45–55 degrees and under normal circumstances this is sufficient to shed snow and water. In areas of extreme snowfall, such as <a href="/wiki/Minka#Roofing" title="Minka">parts of Japan</a>, the pitch is increased further.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Some thatched roofs in the UK are extremely old and preserve evidence of traditional materials and methods that had long been lost. In northern Britain this evidence is often preserved beneath corrugated sheet materials and frequently comes to light during the development of smaller rural properties. <a href="/wiki/Historic_Scotland" title="Historic Scotland">Historic Scotland</a> have funded several research projects into thatching techniques and these have revealed a wide range of materials including broom, heather, rushes, cereals, bracken, turf and clay and highlighted significant regional variation <sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>More recent examples include the <a href="/wiki/Moirlanich_Longhouse" title="Moirlanich Longhouse">Moirlanich Longhouse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Killin" title="Killin">Killin</a> owned by the <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_for_Scotland" title="National Trust for Scotland">National Trust for Scotland</a> (rye, bracken &amp; turf) <sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> and Sunnybrae Cottage, <a href="/wiki/Pitlochry" title="Pitlochry">Pitlochry</a> owned by <a href="/wiki/Historic_Scotland" title="Historic Scotland">Historic Scotland</a> (rye, broom &amp; turf) <sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
