<p>The <b>Clash of Civilizations</b> is a hypothesis that people's <a href="/wiki/Cultural_identity" title="Cultural identity">cultural</a> and <a href="/wiki/Religious_identity" title="Religious identity">religious</a> <a href="/wiki/Identity_(social_science)" title="Identity (social science)">identities</a> will be the primary source of conflict in the post-<a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> world. The American political scientist <a href="/wiki/Samuel_P._Huntington" title="Samuel P. Huntington">Samuel P. Huntington</a> argued that future wars would be fought not between countries, but between cultures. It was proposed in a 1992 lecture<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> at the <a href="/wiki/American_Enterprise_Institute" title="American Enterprise Institute">American Enterprise Institute</a>, which was then developed in a 1993 <i><a href="/wiki/Foreign_Affairs" title="Foreign Affairs">Foreign Affairs</a></i> article titled "The Clash of Civilizations?",<sup id="cite_ref-FAarticle_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAarticle-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> in response to his former student <a href="/wiki/Francis_Fukuyama" title="Francis Fukuyama">Francis Fukuyama</a>'s 1992 book, <i><a href="/wiki/The_End_of_History_and_the_Last_Man" title="The End of History and the Last Man">The End of History and the Last Man</a></i>. Huntington later expanded his thesis in a 1996 book <i>The Clash of Civilizations and the Remaking of World Order</i>.
</p><p>The phrase itself was earlier used by <a href="/wiki/Albert_Camus" title="Albert Camus">Albert Camus</a> in 1946,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Girilal_Jain" title="Girilal Jain">Girilal Jain</a> in his analysis of the <a href="/wiki/Ayodhya_dispute" title="Ayodhya dispute">Ayodhya dispute</a> in 1988,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Lewis" title="Bernard Lewis">Bernard Lewis</a> in an article in the September 1990 issue of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Atlantic" title="The Atlantic">The Atlantic Monthly</a></i> titled "The Roots of Muslim Rage"<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and by <a href="/wiki/Mahdi_Elmandjra" title="Mahdi Elmandjra">Mahdi El Mandjra</a> in his book "La première guerre civilisationnelle" published in 1992.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Even earlier, the phrase appears in a 1926 book regarding the Middle East by Basil Mathews: <i>Young Islam on Trek:  A Study in the Clash of Civilizations</i> (p.&#160;196). This expression derives from "clash of cultures", already used during the colonial period and the <a href="/wiki/Belle_%C3%89poque" title="Belle Époque">Belle Époque</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Huntington began his thinking by surveying the diverse theories about the nature of global politics in the post-Cold War period. Some theorists and writers argued that <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liberal_democracy" title="Liberal democracy">liberal democracy</a>, and the capitalist free market economy had become the only remaining ideological alternative for nations in the post-Cold War world. Specifically, <a href="/wiki/Francis_Fukuyama" title="Francis Fukuyama">Francis Fukuyama</a> argued that the world had reached the '<a href="/wiki/End_of_history" title="End of history">end of history</a>' in a <a href="/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_Friedrich_Hegel" title="Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel">Hegelian</a> sense.
</p><p>Huntington believed that while the age of <a href="/wiki/Ideology" title="Ideology">ideology</a> had ended, the world had only reverted to a normal state of affairs characterized by cultural conflict. In his thesis, he argued that the primary axis of conflict in the future will be along cultural lines.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> As an extension, he posits that the concept of different <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilizations</a>, as the highest rank of <a href="/wiki/Cultural_identity" title="Cultural identity">cultural identity</a>, will become increasingly useful in analyzing the potential for conflict. At the end of his 1993 <i>Foreign Affairs</i> article, "The Clash of Civilizations?", Huntington writes, "This is not to advocate the desirability of conflicts between civilizations. It is to set forth descriptive hypothesis as to what the future may be like."<sup id="cite_ref-FAarticle_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAarticle-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition, the clash of civilizations, for Huntington, represents a development of history. In the past, world history was mainly about the struggles between monarchs, nations and ideologies, such as seen within Western civilization. But after the end of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, world politics moved into a new phase, in which non-Western civilizations are no longer the exploited recipients of Western civilization but have become additional important actors joining the West to shape and move world history.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_civilizations_according_to_Huntington">Major civilizations according to Huntington</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clash_of_Civilizations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Major civilizations according to Huntington">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:752px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Clash_of_Civilizations_mapn2.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Clash_of_Civilizations_mapn2.png/750px-Clash_of_Civilizations_mapn2.png" decoding="async" width="750" height="364" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Clash_of_Civilizations_mapn2.png/1125px-Clash_of_Civilizations_mapn2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/34/Clash_of_Civilizations_mapn2.png 2x" data-file-width="1260" data-file-height="612" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Clash_of_Civilizations_mapn2.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The clash of civilizations according to Huntington (1996), as presented in the book.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div></div>
<p>Huntington divided the world into the "major civilizations" in his thesis as such:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western</a> civilization, comprising the United States and Canada, Western and Central Europe, Australia, Oceania and most of the Philippines. Whether Latin  America and the <a href="/wiki/Post-Soviet_states" title="Post-Soviet states">former member states</a> of the Soviet Union are included, or are instead their own separate civilizations, will be an important future consideration for those regions, according to Huntington. The traditional Western viewpoint identified Western Civilization with the <a href="/wiki/Western_Christianity" title="Western Christianity">Western Christian</a> (<a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic</a>-<a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant</a>) countries and culture.<sup id="cite_ref-google.com_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-google.com-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin American</a>. Includes Central America, South America (excluding <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a>), <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>. May be considered a part of Western civilization. Many people in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> regard themselves as full members of Western civilization.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Orthodox</a> world of the <a href="/wiki/Post-Soviet_states" title="Post-Soviet states">former Soviet Union</a>, the former <a href="/wiki/Yugoslavia" title="Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>.
<ul><li>Countries with a non-Orthodox majority are usually excluded e.g. Muslim <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a> and Muslim <a href="/wiki/Albania" title="Albania">Albania</a> and most of <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>, as well as majority Muslim regions in the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> and central Russian regions such as <a href="/wiki/Tatarstan" title="Tatarstan">Tatarstan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bashkortostan" title="Bashkortostan">Bashkortostan</a>, Roman Catholic <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a>, Protestant and Catholic <a href="/wiki/Baltic_states" title="Baltic states">Baltic states</a>). However, <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a> is included, despite its dominant faith, the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Apostolic_Church" title="Armenian Apostolic Church">Armenian Apostolic Church</a>, being a part of <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodoxy" class="mw-redirect" title="Oriental Orthodoxy">Oriental Orthodoxy</a> rather than the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Church</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> is also included, despite its dominant faith being <a href="/wiki/Sunni_Islam" title="Sunni Islam">Sunni Islam</a>.</li></ul></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Eastern_world" title="Eastern world">Eastern world</a> is the mix of the <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greater_China" title="Greater China">Chinese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hindu" class="mw-redirect" title="Hindu">Hindu</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japonic</a> civilizations.
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhist</a> areas of <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">Laos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> are identified as separate from other civilizations, but Huntington believes that they do not constitute a major civilization in the sense of international affairs.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucian</a> civilization of China, the <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Koreas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>. This group also includes the <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Chinese" title="Overseas Chinese">Chinese diaspora</a>, especially in relation to Southeast Asia.</li>
<li>Hindu civilization, located chiefly in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, and culturally adhered to by the global <a href="/wiki/Non-resident_Indian_and_person_of_Indian_origin" title="Non-resident Indian and person of Indian origin">Indian diaspora</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, considered a hybrid of Chinese civilization and older <a href="/wiki/Altaic_languages" title="Altaic languages">Altaic</a> patterns.</li></ul></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Muslim_world" title="Muslim world">Muslim world</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Middle_East" title="Greater Middle East">Greater Middle East</a> (excluding <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Georgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Sudan" title="South Sudan">South Sudan</a>), northern <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Albania" title="Albania">Albania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>, parts of <a href="/wiki/Bosnia_and_Herzegovina" title="Bosnia and Herzegovina">Bosnia and Herzegovina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brunei" title="Brunei">Brunei</a>, <a href="/wiki/Comoros" title="Comoros">Comoros</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a> and southern Philippines.</li>
<li>The civilization of <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a> located in southern Africa, Middle Africa (excluding Chad), East Africa (excluding <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Comoros" title="Comoros">Comoros</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Swahili_coast" title="Swahili coast">Swahili coast</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>), <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ivory_Coast" title="Ivory Coast">Ivory Coast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liberia" title="Liberia">Liberia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Leone" title="Sierra Leone">Sierra Leone</a>. Considered as a possible eighth civilization by Huntington.</li>
<li>Instead of belonging to one of the "major" civilizations, <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> are labeled as "Lone" countries. <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> could be considered a unique state with its own civilization, Huntington writes, but one which is extremely similar to the West. Huntington also believes that the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Caribbean" title="Commonwealth Caribbean">Anglophone Caribbean</a>, former British colonies in the Caribbean, constitutes a distinct entity.</li>
<li>There are also others which are considered "cleft countries" because they contain very large groups of people identifying with separate civilizations. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> ("cleft" between its <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_Greek_Catholic_Church" title="Ukrainian Greek Catholic Church">Eastern Rite Catholic</a>-dominated western section and its <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_Orthodox_Church_(Moscow_Patriarchate)" title="Ukrainian Orthodox Church (Moscow Patriarchate)">Orthodox</a>-dominated east), <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a> (cleft between Latin America, and the West), <a href="/wiki/Benin" title="Benin">Benin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Togo" title="Togo">Togo</a> (all cleft between Islam and Sub-Saharan Africa), <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a> (cleft between Hindu and Sub-Saharan African), <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> (cleft between Hindu and Buddhist), <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> (cleft between Sinic and Buddhist, in the case of <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a>; and the West, in the case of <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> (cleft between Islam, in the case of <a href="/wiki/Mindanao" title="Mindanao">Mindanao</a>; Sinic, in the case of <a href="/wiki/Cordillera_Administrative_Region" title="Cordillera Administrative Region">Cordillera</a>; and the West). <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a> was also included as "cleft" between Islam and Sub-Saharan Africa; this division became a formal split in July 2011 following an overwhelming vote for independence by <a href="/wiki/South_Sudan" title="South Sudan">South Sudan</a> in a <a href="/wiki/South_Sudanese_independence_referendum,_2011" class="mw-redirect" title="South Sudanese independence referendum, 2011">January 2011 referendum</a>.</li></ul>
<h2><span id="Huntington.27s_thesis_of_civilizational_clash"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Huntington's_thesis_of_civilizational_clash">Huntington's thesis of civilizational clash</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clash_of_Civilizations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Huntington&#039;s thesis of civilizational clash">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Samuel_P._Huntington_(2004_World_Economic_Forum).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Samuel_P._Huntington_%282004_World_Economic_Forum%29.jpg/220px-Samuel_P._Huntington_%282004_World_Economic_Forum%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="155" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Samuel_P._Huntington_%282004_World_Economic_Forum%29.jpg/330px-Samuel_P._Huntington_%282004_World_Economic_Forum%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Samuel_P._Huntington_%282004_World_Economic_Forum%29.jpg/440px-Samuel_P._Huntington_%282004_World_Economic_Forum%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2150" data-file-height="1514" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Samuel_P._Huntington_(2004_World_Economic_Forum).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Huntington at the 2004 World Economic Forum</div></div></div>
<p>Huntington argues that the trends of global conflict after the end of the Cold War are increasingly appearing at these civilizational divisions. Wars such as those following the break up of <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Federal_Republic_of_Yugoslavia" title="Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Chechnya" title="Chechnya">Chechnya</a>, and between India and Pakistan were cited as evidence of inter-civilizational conflict. He also argues that the widespread Western belief in the universality of the West's values and political systems is naïve and that continued insistence on democratization and such "universal" norms will only further antagonize other civilizations. Huntington sees the West as reluctant to accept this because it built the international system, wrote its laws, and gave it substance in the form of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>.
</p><p>Huntington identifies a major shift of economic, military, and political power from the West to the other civilizations of the world, most significantly to what he identifies as the two "challenger civilizations", Sinic and Islam.
</p><p>In Huntington's view, East Asian Sinic civilization is culturally asserting itself and its values relative to the West due to its rapid economic growth. Specifically, he believes that China's goals are to reassert itself as the regional <a href="/wiki/Hegemon" class="mw-redirect" title="Hegemon">hegemon</a>, and that other countries in the region will 'bandwagon' with China due to the history of hierarchical command structures implicit in the <a href="/wiki/Confucian" class="mw-redirect" title="Confucian">Confucian</a> Sinic civilization, as opposed to the individualism and pluralism valued in the West. Regional powers such as the two Koreas and Vietnam will acquiesce to Chinese demands and become more supportive of China rather than attempting to oppose it. Huntington therefore believes that the rise of China poses one of the most significant problems and the most powerful long-term threat to the West, as Chinese cultural assertion clashes with the American desire for the lack of a regional hegemony in East Asia.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Huntington argues that the Islamic civilization has experienced a massive population explosion which is fueling instability both on the <a href="/wiki/Divisions_of_the_world_in_Islam" title="Divisions of the world in Islam">borders of Islam</a> and in its interior, where <a href="/wiki/Islamic_fundamentalism" title="Islamic fundamentalism">fundamentalist</a> movements are becoming increasingly popular. Manifestations of what he terms the "<a href="/wiki/Islamic_revival" title="Islamic revival">Islamic Resurgence</a>" include the <a href="/wiki/1979_Iranian_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="1979 Iranian revolution">1979 Iranian revolution</a> and the first <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Gulf War</a>. Perhaps the most controversial statement Huntington made in the <i>Foreign Affairs</i> article was that "Islam has bloody borders". Huntington believes this to be a real consequence of several factors, including the previously mentioned Muslim <a href="/wiki/Youth_bulge" class="mw-redirect" title="Youth bulge">youth bulge</a> and population growth and Islamic proximity to many civilizations including Sinic, Orthodox, Western, and African.
</p><p>Huntington sees Islamic civilization as a potential ally to China, both having more revisionist goals and sharing common conflicts with other civilizations, especially the West. Specifically, he identifies common Chinese and Islamic interests in the areas of weapons proliferation, human rights, and democracy that conflict with those of the West, and feels that these are areas in which the two civilizations will cooperate.
</p><p>Russia, Japan, and India are what Huntington terms 'swing civilizations' and may favor either side. Russia, for example, clashes with the many Muslim ethnic groups on its southern border (such as <a href="/wiki/Chechnya" title="Chechnya">Chechnya</a>) but—according to Huntington—cooperates with Iran to avoid further Muslim-Orthodox violence in Southern Russia, and to help continue the flow of oil. Huntington argues that a "<a href="/w/index.php?title=Sino-Islamic_connection&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sino-Islamic connection (page does not exist)">Sino-Islamic connection</a>" is emerging in which China will cooperate more closely with <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, Pakistan, and other states to augment its international position.
</p><p>Huntington also argues that civilizational conflicts are "particularly prevalent between Muslims and non-Muslims", identifying the "bloody borders" between Islamic and non-Islamic civilizations. This conflict dates back as far as the <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_conquest_of_Hispania" title="Umayyad conquest of Hispania">initial thrust of Islam into Europe</a>, its eventual expulsion in the <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Iberian reconquest</a>, the attacks of the Ottoman Turks on Eastern Europe and Vienna, and the European imperial division of the Islamic nations in the 1800s and 1900s.
</p><p>Huntington also believes that some of the factors contributing to this conflict are that both Christianity (upon which Western civilization is based) and Islam are:
</p>
<ul><li>Missionary religions, seeking conversion of others</li>
<li>Universal, "all-or-nothing" religions, in the sense that it is believed by both sides that only their faith is the correct one</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Teleological" class="mw-redirect" title="Teleological">Teleological</a> religions, that is, that their values and beliefs represent the goals of existence and purpose in human existence.</li></ul>
<p>More recent factors contributing to a Western–Islamic clash, Huntington wrote, are the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_revival" title="Islamic revival">Islamic Resurgence</a> and demographic explosion in Islam, coupled with the values of Western universalism—that is, the view that all civilizations should adopt Western values—that infuriate Islamic fundamentalists. All these historical and modern factors combined, Huntington wrote briefly in his <i>Foreign Affairs</i> article and in much more detail in his 1996 book, would lead to a bloody clash between the Islamic and Western civilizations.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Why_civilizations_will_clash">Why civilizations will clash</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clash_of_Civilizations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Why civilizations will clash">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Huntington offers six explanations for why civilizations will clash:
</p>
<ol><li>Differences among civilizations are too basic in that civilizations are differentiated from each other by history, language, culture, tradition, and, most importantly, religion. These fundamental differences are the product of centuries and the foundations of different civilizations, meaning they will not be gone soon.</li>
<li>The world is becoming a smaller place. As a result, interactions across the world are increasing, which intensify "civilization consciousness" and the awareness of differences between civilizations and commonalities within civilizations.</li>
<li>Due to economic modernization and social change, people are separated from longstanding local identities. Instead, religion has replaced this gap, which provides a basis for identity and commitment that transcends national boundaries and unites civilizations.</li>
<li>The growth of civilization-consciousness is enhanced by the dual role of the West. On the one hand, the West is at a peak of power. At the same time, a return-to-the-roots phenomenon is occurring among non-Western civilizations. A West at the peak of its power confronts non-Western countries that increasingly have the desire, the will and the resources to shape the world in non-Western ways.</li>
<li>Cultural characteristics and differences are less mutable and hence less easily compromised and resolved than political and economic ones.</li>
<li>Economic regionalism is increasing. Successful economic regionalism will reinforce civilization-consciousness. Economic regionalism may succeed only when it is rooted in a common civilization.</li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_West_versus_the_Rest">The West versus the Rest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clash_of_Civilizations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The West versus the Rest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Huntington suggests that in the future the central axis of world politics tends to be the conflict between Western and non-Western civilizations, in [Stuart Hall]'s phrase, the conflict between "the West and the Rest". He offers three forms of general actions that non-Western civilization can take in response to Western countries.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>Non-Western countries can attempt to achieve isolation in order to preserve their own values and protect themselves from Western invasion. However, Huntington argues that the costs of this action are high and only a few states can pursue it.</li>
<li>According to the theory of "<a href="/wiki/Bandwagoning" title="Bandwagoning">band-wagoning</a>" non-Western countries can join and accept Western values.</li>
<li>Non-Western countries can make an effort to balance Western power through modernization. They can develop economic, military power and cooperate with other non-Western countries against the West while still preserving their own values and institutions. Huntington believes that the increasing power of non-Western civilizations in international society will make the West begin to develop a better understanding of the cultural fundamentals underlying other civilizations. Therefore, Western civilization will cease to be regarded as "universal" but different civilizations will learn to coexist and join to shape the future world.</li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Core_state_and_fault_line_conflicts">Core state and fault line conflicts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clash_of_Civilizations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Core state and fault line conflicts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Huntington's view, intercivilizational conflict manifests itself in two forms: fault line conflicts and core state conflicts.
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Fault_line_war" title="Fault line war">Fault line conflicts</a></i> are on a local level and occur between adjacent states belonging to different civilizations or within states that are home to populations from different civilizations.
</p><p><i>Core state conflicts</i> are on a global level between the major states of different civilizations. Core state conflicts can arise out of fault line conflicts when core states become involved.<sup id="cite_ref-Huntington1_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huntington1-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>These conflicts may result from a number of causes, such as: relative influence or power (military or economic), discrimination against people from a different civilization, intervention to protect kinsmen in a different civilization, or different values and culture, particularly when one civilization attempts to impose its values on people of a different civilization.<sup id="cite_ref-Huntington1_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huntington1-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Modernization.2C_Westernization.2C_and_.22torn_countries.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Modernization,_Westernization,_and_&quot;torn_countries&quot;">Modernization, Westernization, and "torn countries"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clash_of_Civilizations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Modernization, Westernization, and &quot;torn countries&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Japan, China and the <a href="/wiki/Four_Asian_Tigers" title="Four Asian Tigers">Four Asian Tigers</a> have modernized in many respects while maintaining traditional or authoritarian societies which distinguish them from the West. Some of these countries have clashed with the West and some have not.
</p><p>Perhaps the ultimate example of non-Western modernization is Russia, the core state of the Orthodox civilization. Huntington argues that Russia is primarily a non-Western state although he seems to agree that it shares a considerable amount of cultural ancestry with the modern West. According to Huntington, the West is distinguished from Orthodox Christian countries by its experience of the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformation</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a>; by overseas <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">colonialism</a> rather than contiguous expansion and colonialism; and by the infusion of <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">Classical</a> culture through <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greece</a> rather than through the continuous trajectory of the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a>.
</p><p>Huntington refers to countries that are seeking to affiliate with another civilization as "torn countries". <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, whose political leadership has systematically tried to Westernize the country since the 1920s, is his chief example. Turkey's history, culture, and traditions are derived from Islamic civilization, but Turkey's elite, beginning with <a href="/wiki/Mustafa_Kemal_Atat%C3%BCrk" title="Mustafa Kemal Atatürk">Mustafa Kemal Atatürk</a> who took power as first President in 1923, imposed Western institutions and dress, embraced the <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Latin alphabet</a>, joined <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>, and has sought to <a href="/wiki/European_Union%E2%80%93Turkey_relations" title="European Union–Turkey relations">join the European Union</a>.
</p><p>Mexico and Russia are also considered to be torn by Huntington. He also gives the example of Australia as a country torn between its Western civilizational heritage and its growing economic engagement with Asia.
</p><p>According to Huntington, a torn country must meet three requirements to redefine its civilizational identity. Its political and economic elite must support the move. Second, the public must be willing to accept the redefinition. Third, the elites of the civilization that the torn country is trying to join must accept the country.
</p><p>The book claims that to date no torn country has successfully redefined its civilizational identity, this mostly due to the elites of the 'host' civilization refusing to accept the torn country, though if Turkey gained membership in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, it has been noted that many of its people would support Westernization, as in the following quote by EU Minister Egemen Bağış: "This is what Europe needs to do: they need to say that when Turkey fulfills all requirements, Turkey will become a member of the EU on date X. Then, we will regain the Turkish public opinion support in one day."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> If this were to happen, it would, according to Huntington, be the first to redefine its civilizational identity.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clash_of_Civilizations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Huntington has fallen under the stern critique of various academic writers, who have either empirically, historically, logically, or ideologically challenged his claims (Fox, 2005; Mungiu Pippidi &amp; Mindruta, 2002; Henderson &amp; Tucker, 2001; Russett, Oneal, &amp; Cox, 2000; Harvey, 2000).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>
In an article explicitly referring to Huntington, scholar <a href="/wiki/Amartya_Sen" title="Amartya Sen">Amartya Sen</a> (1999) argues:</p><blockquote><p>diversity is a feature of most cultures in the world. Western civilization is no exception. The practice of democracy that has won out in the modern West is largely a result of a consensus that has emerged since the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, and particularly in the last century or so. To read in this a historical commitment of the West—over the millennia—to democracy, and then to contrast it with non-Western traditions (treating each as monolithic) would be a great mistake.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>16</span></sup></p></blockquote><p>In his 2003 book <i>Terror and Liberalism</i>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Berman" title="Paul Berman">Paul Berman</a> argues that distinct cultural boundaries do not exist in the present day. He argues there is no "Islamic civilization" nor a "Western civilization", and that the evidence for a civilization clash is not convincing, especially when considering relationships such as that between the United States and <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>. In addition, he cites the fact that many Islamic extremists spent a significant amount of time living or studying in the Western world. According to Berman, conflict arises because of <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophical</a> beliefs various groups share (or do not share), regardless of cultural or religious identity.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Timothy_Garton_Ash" title="Timothy Garton Ash">Timothy Garton Ash</a> objects to the ‘extreme cultural determinism… crude to the point of parody’ of Huntington’s idea that Catholic and Protestant Europe is headed for democracy but that Orthodox Christian and Islamic Europe must accept dictatorship.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Edward_Said" title="Edward Said">Edward Said</a> issued a response to Huntington's thesis in his 2001 article, "<a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Clash_of_Ignorance&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="The Clash of Ignorance (page does not exist)">The Clash of Ignorance</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Said argues that Huntington's categorization of the world's fixed "civilizations" omits the dynamic interdependency and interaction of culture. A longtime critic of the Huntingtonian paradigm, and an outspoken proponent of Arab issues, Edward Said (2004) also argues that the clash of civilizations thesis is an example of "the purest invidious racism, a sort of parody of Hitlerian science directed today against Arabs and Muslims" (p.&#160;293).<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Noam_Chomsky" title="Noam Chomsky">Noam Chomsky</a> has criticized the concept of the clash of civilizations as just being a new justification for the United States "for any atrocities that they wanted to carry out", which was required after the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> was no longer a viable threat.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intermediate_Region">Intermediate Region</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clash_of_Civilizations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Intermediate Region">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Huntington's geopolitical model, especially the structures for North Africa and Eurasia, is largely derived from the "<a href="/wiki/Intermediate_Region" title="Intermediate Region">Intermediate Region</a>" geopolitical model first formulated by <a href="/wiki/Dimitri_Kitsikis" title="Dimitri Kitsikis">Dimitri Kitsikis</a> and published in 1978.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The Intermediate Region, which spans the <a href="/wiki/Adriatic_Sea" title="Adriatic Sea">Adriatic Sea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Indus_River" title="Indus River">Indus River</a>, is neither Western nor Eastern (at least, with respect to the Far East) but is considered distinct. Concerning this region, Huntington departs from Kitsikis contending that a civilizational fault line exists between the two dominant yet differing religions (<a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodoxy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sunni_Islam" title="Sunni Islam">Sunni Islam</a>), hence a dynamic of external conflict. However, Kitsikis establishes an integrated civilization comprising these two peoples along with those belonging to the less dominant religions of <a href="/wiki/Shia_Islam" title="Shia Islam">Shia Islam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alevism" title="Alevism">Alevism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a>. They have a set of mutual cultural, social, economic and political views and norms which radically differ from those in the West and the Far East. In the Intermediate Region, therefore, one cannot speak of a civilizational clash or external conflict, but rather an internal conflict, not for cultural domination, but for political succession. This has been successfully demonstrated by documenting the rise of Christianity from the Hellenized <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, the rise of the <a href="/wiki/Caliphate" title="Caliphate">Islamic caliphates</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Christianized Roman Empire</a> and the rise of <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a> rule from the Islamic caliphates and the Christianized Roman Empire.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Opposing_concepts">Opposing concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clash_of_Civilizations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Opposing concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In recent years, the theory of <a href="/wiki/Dialogue_Among_Civilizations" title="Dialogue Among Civilizations">Dialogue Among Civilizations</a>, a response to Huntington's Clash of Civilizations, has become the center of some international attention. The concept was originally coined by Austrian philosopher <a href="/wiki/Hans_K%C3%B6chler" title="Hans Köchler">Hans Köchler</a> in an essay on cultural identity (1972).<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> In a letter to <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a>, Köchler had earlier proposed that the cultural organization of the United Nations should take up the issue of a "dialogue between different civilizations" (<i>dialogue entre les différentes civilisations</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> In 2001, Iranian president <a href="/wiki/Mohammad_Khatami" title="Mohammad Khatami">Mohammad Khatami</a> introduced the concept at the global level. At his initiative, the United Nations proclaimed the year 2001 as the "United Nations Year of Dialogue among Civilizations".<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Alliance_of_Civilizations" title="Alliance of Civilizations">Alliance of Civilizations</a> (AOC) initiative was proposed at the 59th <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">General Assembly of the United Nations</a> in 2005 by the Spanish Prime Minister, <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Luis_Rodr%C3%ADguez_Zapatero" title="José Luis Rodríguez Zapatero">José Luis Rodríguez Zapatero</a> and co-sponsored by the Turkish Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Recep_Tayyip_Erdo%C4%9Fan" title="Recep Tayyip Erdoğan">Recep Tayyip Erdoğan</a>. The initiative is intended to galvanize collective action across diverse societies to combat <a href="/wiki/Extremism" title="Extremism">extremism</a>, to overcome cultural and social barriers between mainly the Western and predominantly Muslim worlds, and to reduce the tensions and polarization between societies which differ in religious and cultural values.
</p>
