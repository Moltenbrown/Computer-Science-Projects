<p><b>Sanaʽa</b> (<a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl">صَنْعَاء</span>&#8206;, <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Ṣanʿāʾ</i></i> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Arabic" title="Help:IPA/Arabic">[sˤɑnʕaːʔ]</a></span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Yemeni_Arabic" title="Yemeni Arabic">Yemeni Arabic</a>:</small> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Arabic" title="Help:IPA/Arabic">[ˈsˤɑnʕɑ]</a></span>), also spelled <b>Sanaa</b> or <b>Sana</b>, is the <a href="/wiki/Largest_city" class="mw-redirect" title="Largest city">largest city</a> in <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a> and the centre of <a href="/wiki/Sana%CA%BDa_Governorate" title="Sanaʽa Governorate">Sanaʽa Governorate</a>. The city is not part of the Governorate, but forms the separate administrative district of "Amanat Al-Asemah". Under the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Yemen" title="Constitution of Yemen">Yemeni constitution</a>, Sanaʽa is the capital of the country,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> although the seat of the internationally recognised government moved to <a href="/wiki/Aden" title="Aden">Aden</a> in the aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/Houthi_takeover_in_Yemen" title="Houthi takeover in Yemen">September 21 Revolution</a>. Aden was declared as the temporary capital by President <a href="/wiki/Abdrabbuh_Mansur_Hadi" title="Abdrabbuh Mansur Hadi">Abdrabbuh Mansur Hadi</a> in March 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sanaʽa is one of the <a href="/wiki/Oldest_continuously_inhabited_cities" class="mw-redirect" title="Oldest continuously inhabited cities">oldest continuously inhabited cities</a> in the world. At an elevation of 2,300 metres (7,500&#160;ft),<sup id="cite_ref-Laughlin2008_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Laughlin2008-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> it is also one of the highest capital cities in the world, and is next to the <a href="/wiki/Sarawat_Mountains" title="Sarawat Mountains">Sarawat Mountains</a> of <a href="/wiki/Jabal_An-Nabi_Shu%27ayb" title="Jabal An-Nabi Shu&#39;ayb">Jabal An-Nabi Shu'ayb</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jabal_Tiyal" title="Jabal Tiyal">Jabal Tiyal</a>, considered to be the highest mountains in the country and amongst the highest in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">region</a>. Sanaʽa has a population of approximately 3,937,500 (2012), making it Yemen's largest city.
</p><p>The Old City of Sanaʽa, a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>, has a distinctive architectural character, most notably expressed in its multi-storey buildings decorated with geometric patterns. In the conflict that raged in 2015, bombs hit UNESCO sites in the old city.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HestlerSpilling2010_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HestlerSpilling2010-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Located here is the <a href="/wiki/Al_Saleh_Mosque" title="Al Saleh Mosque">Al Saleh Mosque</a>, the largest in the city.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sana%CA%BDa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_Sana%CA%BDa" title="Timeline of Sanaʽa">Timeline of Sanaʽa</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_period">Ancient period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sana%CA%BDa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancient period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to popular legend, it was founded at the base of <a href="/wiki/Jabal_Nuqum" class="mw-redirect" title="Jabal Nuqum">Jabal Nuqum</a><sup id="cite_ref-Laughlin2008_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Laughlin2008-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Shem" title="Shem">Shem</a>, the son of <a href="/wiki/Noah" title="Noah">Noah</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Aithe30_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aithe30-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> after the latter's death. It was known as "Azal" in ancient times, which has been connected to <a href="/wiki/Uzal" title="Uzal">Uzal</a>, a son of <a href="/wiki/Qahtanite" title="Qahtanite">Qahtan</a>, a great-grandson of Shem, in the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">biblical</a> accounts of <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">Genesis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EB_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Its name is related to the <a href="/wiki/Sabaic" class="mw-redirect" title="Sabaic">Sabaic</a> word for "well-fortified".<sup id="cite_ref-Bosworth_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bosworth-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Arab" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab">Arab</a> historian <a href="/wiki/Ab%C5%AB_Muhammad_al-Hasan_al-Hamd%C4%81n%C4%AB" class="mw-redirect" title="Abū Muhammad al-Hasan al-Hamdānī">al-Hamdani</a> wrote that Sanaʽa was walled by the <a href="/wiki/Sabaeans" title="Sabaeans">Sabaeans</a> under their ruler <a href="/wiki/Sha%27r_Awtar" title="Sha&#39;r Awtar">Sha'r Awtar</a>, who also arguably built the <a href="/wiki/Ghumdan_Palace" title="Ghumdan Palace">Ghumdan Palace</a> in the city. Because of its location, Sanaʽa has served as an urban center for the surrounding tribes of the region, and as a nucleus of regional trade in <a href="/wiki/South_Arabia" title="South Arabia">southern Arabia</a>. It was positioned at the crossroad of two major ancient trade routes linking <a href="/wiki/Ma%27rib" title="Ma&#39;rib">Ma'rib</a> in the east to the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> in the west.<sup id="cite_ref-Aithe30_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aithe30-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When King Yousef Athar (or <a href="/wiki/Dhu_Nuwas" title="Dhu Nuwas">Dhu Nuwas</a>), the last of the Himyarite kings, was in power, Sanaʽa was also the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Aksum" title="Kingdom of Aksum">Ethiopian</a> viceroys.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Islamic_era">Islamic era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sana%CA%BDa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Islamic era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SanaaQuoranDoubleVersions.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/SanaaQuoranDoubleVersions.jpg/220px-SanaaQuoranDoubleVersions.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/SanaaQuoranDoubleVersions.jpg/330px-SanaaQuoranDoubleVersions.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/SanaaQuoranDoubleVersions.jpg/440px-SanaaQuoranDoubleVersions.jpg 2x" data-file-width="460" data-file-height="347" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SanaaQuoranDoubleVersions.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Sana%CA%BDa_manuscript" title="Sanaʽa manuscript">Sanaʽa manuscript</a>, found in Sanaʽa in 1972, is one of the oldest <a href="/wiki/Qur%27an" class="mw-redirect" title="Qur&#39;an">Quranic</a> manuscripts in existence</div></div></div>
<p>From the era of <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a> (ca. 622 CE) until the founding of independent sub-states in many parts of the Yemen Islamic <a href="/wiki/Caliph" class="mw-redirect" title="Caliph">Caliphate</a>, Sanaʽa persisted as the governing seat. The <a href="/wiki/Caliph" class="mw-redirect" title="Caliph">Caliph</a>'s deputy ran the affairs of one of Yemen's three <a href="/wiki/Mikhlaf" title="Mikhlaf">Makhalifs</a>: Mikhlaf Sanaʽa, Mikhlaf al-Janad and Mikhlaf <a href="/wiki/Hadhramaut" title="Hadhramaut">Hadhramaut</a>. The city of Sanaʽa regularly regained an important status and all Yemenite States competed to control it.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Imam <a href="/wiki/Al-Shafi%27i" class="mw-redirect" title="Al-Shafi&#39;i">Al-Shafi'i</a>, the 8th-century Islamic jurist and founder of the <a href="/wiki/Shafi%27i" class="mw-redirect" title="Shafi&#39;i">Shafi'i</a> school of jurisprudence, visited Sanaʽa several times. He praised the city, writing <i>La budda min Ṣanʻāʼ</i>, or "Sanaʽa must be seen." In the 9th–10th centuries, the Yemeni geographer <a href="/wiki/Ab%C5%AB_Muhammad_al-Hasan_al-Hamd%C4%81n%C4%AB" class="mw-redirect" title="Abū Muhammad al-Hasan al-Hamdānī">al-Hamdani</a> took note of the city's cleanliness, saying "The least dwelling there has a well or two, a garden and long cesspits separate from each other, empty of ordure, without smell or evil odors, because of the hard concrete (<a href="/wiki/Adobe" title="Adobe">adobe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cob_(material)" title="Cob (material)">cob</a>, probably) and fine pastureland and clean places to walk." Later in the 10th-century, the Persian geographer <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Rustah" class="mw-redirect" title="Ibn Rustah">Ibn Rustah</a> wrote of Sanaʽa "It is the city of Yemen — there cannot be found ... a city greater, more populous or more prosperous, of nobler origin or with more delicious food than it."
</p><p>In 1062 Sanaʽa was taken over by the <a href="/wiki/Sulayhid_dynasty" title="Sulayhid dynasty">Sulayhid dynasty</a> led by <a href="/wiki/Ali_al-Sulayhi" title="Ali al-Sulayhi">Ali al-Sulayhi</a> and his wife, the popular <a href="/wiki/Asma_bint_Shihab" title="Asma bint Shihab">Queen Asma</a>. He made the city capital of his relatively small kingdom, which also included the <a href="/wiki/Haraz_Mountains" class="mw-redirect" title="Haraz Mountains">Haraz Mountains</a>. The Sulayhids were aligned with the <a href="/wiki/Ismaili_Muslim" class="mw-redirect" title="Ismaili Muslim">Ismaili Muslim</a>-leaning <a href="/wiki/Fatimid_Caliphate" title="Fatimid Caliphate">Fatimid Caliphate</a> of <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, rather than the <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">Baghdad</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Abbasid_Caliphate" title="Abbasid Caliphate">Abbasid Caliphate</a> that most of <a href="/wiki/Arabia" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabia">Arabia</a> followed. Al-Sulayhi ruled for about 20 years but he was assassinated by his principal local rivals, the <a href="/wiki/Zabid" title="Zabid">Zabid</a>-based Najahids. Following his death, al-Sulayhi's daughter, <a href="/wiki/Arwa_al-Sulayhi" title="Arwa al-Sulayhi">Arwa al-Sulayhi</a>, inherited the throne. She withdrew from Sanaʽa, transferring the Sulayhid capital to <a href="/wiki/Jibla,_Yemen" title="Jibla, Yemen">Jibla</a>, where she ruled much of Yemen from 1067 to 1138. As a result of the Sulayhid departure, the <a href="/wiki/Hamdanid" class="mw-redirect" title="Hamdanid">Hamdanid</a> dynasty took control of Sanaʽa.<sup id="cite_ref-McLaughlin16_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLaughlin16-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1173 <a href="/wiki/Saladin" title="Saladin">Saladin</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ayyubid" class="mw-redirect" title="Ayyubid">Ayyubid</a> sultan of Egypt, sent his brother <a href="/wiki/Turan-Shah" title="Turan-Shah">Turan-Shah</a> on an expedition to conquer Yemen. The Ayyubids gained control of Sanaʽa in 1175 and united the various Yemeni tribal states, except for the northern mountains controlled by the <a href="/wiki/Zaydi" class="mw-redirect" title="Zaydi">Zaydi</a> imams, into one entity.<sup id="cite_ref-McLaughlin16_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLaughlin16-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The Ayyubids switched the country's official religious allegiance to the <a href="/wiki/Sunni_Muslim" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunni Muslim">Sunni Muslim</a> Abbasids. During the reign of the Ayyubid <i><a href="/wiki/Emir" title="Emir">emir</a></i> Tughtekin ibn Ayyub, the city underwent significant improvements. These included the incorporation of the garden lands on the western bank of the Sa'ilah, known as Bustan al-Sultan, where the Ayyubids built one of their palaces.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Despite Sanaʽa's strategic position, the Ayyubids chose <a href="/wiki/Ta%27izz" class="mw-redirect" title="Ta&#39;izz">Ta'izz</a> as their capital while <a href="/wiki/Aden" title="Aden">Aden</a> was their principal income-producing city.
</p><p>While the <a href="/wiki/Rasulid" class="mw-redirect" title="Rasulid">Rasulids</a> controlled most of Yemen, followed by their successors the <a href="/wiki/Tahirid" class="mw-redirect" title="Tahirid">Tahirids</a>, Sanaʽa largely remained in the political orbit of the <a href="/wiki/Zaydi" class="mw-redirect" title="Zaydi">Zaydi</a> imams from 1323 to 1454 and outside the former two dynasties' rule.<sup id="cite_ref-Bosworth463_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bosworth463-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Mamelukes" class="mw-redirect" title="Mamelukes">Mamelukes</a> arrived in Yemen in 1517.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ottoman_era">Ottoman era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sana%CA%BDa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ottoman era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> entered Yemen in 1538 when <a href="/wiki/Suleiman_the_Magnificent" title="Suleiman the Magnificent">Suleiman the Magnificent</a> was <a href="/wiki/Sultan" title="Sultan">Sultan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dumper330_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dumper330-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Under the military leadership of <a href="/wiki/%C3%96zdemir_Pasha" title="Özdemir Pasha">Özdemir Pasha</a>, the Ottomans conquered Sanaʽa in 1547.<sup id="cite_ref-Bosworth463_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bosworth463-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> With Ottoman approval, European captains based in the Yemeni port towns of <a href="/wiki/Aden" title="Aden">Aden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mocha,_Yemen" title="Mocha, Yemen">Mocha</a> frequented Sanaʽa to maintain special privileges and capitulations for their trade. In 1602 the local Zaydi imams led by <a href="/wiki/Al-Mu%27ayyad_Muhammad" title="Al-Mu&#39;ayyad Muhammad">Imam al-Mu'ayyad</a> reasserted their control over the area,<sup id="cite_ref-Dumper330_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dumper330-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and forced out Ottoman troops in 1629. Although the Ottomans fled during al-Mu'ayyad's reign, his predecessor <a href="/wiki/Al-Mansur_al-Qasim" title="Al-Mansur al-Qasim">al-Mansur al-Qasim</a> had already vastly weakened the Ottoman army in Sanaʽa and Yemen.<sup id="cite_ref-Bosworth463_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bosworth463-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Consequently, European traders were stripped of their previous privileges.<sup id="cite_ref-Dumper330_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dumper330-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Zaydi imams maintained their rule over Sanaʽa until the mid 19th-century, when the Ottomans relaunched their campaign to control the region. In 1835, Ottoman troops arrived on the Yemeni coast under the guise of <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Ali_of_Egypt" title="Muhammad Ali of Egypt">Muhammad Ali of Egypt</a>'s troops.<sup id="cite_ref-Dumper330_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dumper330-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> They did not capture Sanaʽa until 1872, when their troops led by <a href="/wiki/Ahmed_Muhtar_Pasha" title="Ahmed Muhtar Pasha">Ahmed Muhtar Pasha</a> entered the city.<sup id="cite_ref-Bosworth463_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bosworth463-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The Ottoman Empire instituted the <a href="/wiki/Tanzimat" title="Tanzimat">Tanzimat</a> reforms throughout the lands they governed.
</p><p>In Sanaʽa, city planning was initiated for the first time, new roads were built, and schools and hospitals were established. The reforms were rushed by the Ottomans in order to solidify their control of Sanaʽa to compete with an expanding <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, British influence in <a href="/wiki/Aden" title="Aden">Aden</a> and imperial Italian and French influence along the coast of <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a>, particularly in the towns of <a href="/wiki/Djibouti" title="Djibouti">Djibouti</a> and <a href="/wiki/Berbera" title="Berbera">Berbera</a>. The modernization reforms in Sanaʽa were still very limited, however.<sup id="cite_ref-Dumper331_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dumper331-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_Yemen_period">North Yemen period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sana%CA%BDa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: North Yemen period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dar_al_hajar_edit.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Dar_al_hajar_edit.jpg/170px-Dar_al_hajar_edit.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="250" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Dar_al_hajar_edit.jpg/255px-Dar_al_hajar_edit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Dar_al_hajar_edit.jpg/340px-Dar_al_hajar_edit.jpg 2x" data-file-width="541" data-file-height="797" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dar_al_hajar_edit.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Dar_al-Hajar" title="Dar al-Hajar">Dar al-Hajar</a>, the residence of <a href="/wiki/Imam_Yahya" class="mw-redirect" title="Imam Yahya">Imam Yahya</a> in the Wādī Ẓahr (<span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">وادى ظهر</span>) near Sanaʽa</div></div></div>
<p>In 1904, as Ottoman influence was waning in Yemen, <a href="/wiki/Yahya_Muhammad_Hamid_ed-Din" title="Yahya Muhammad Hamid ed-Din">Imam Yahya</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Zaydi" class="mw-redirect" title="Zaydi">Zaydi imams</a> took power in Sanaʽa. In a bid to secure <a href="/wiki/North_Yemen" title="North Yemen">North Yemen</a>'s independence, Yahya embarked on a policy of isolationism, avoiding international and Arab world politics, cracking down on embryonic liberal movements, not contributing to the development of infrastructure in Sanaʽa and elsewhere and closing down the Ottoman girls' school. As a consequence of Yahya's measures, Sanaʽa increasingly became a center of anti-government organization and intellectual revolt.<sup id="cite_ref-Dumper331_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dumper331-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1930s, several organizations opposing or demanding reform of the Zaydi imamate sprung up in the city, particularly Fatat al-Fulayhi, a group of various Yemeni <a href="/wiki/Muslim_scholars" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim scholars">Muslim scholars</a> based in Sanaʽa's Fulayhi Madrasa, and Hait al-Nidal ("Committee of the Struggle.") By 1936 most of the leaders of these movements were imprisoned. In 1941 another group based in the city, the Shabab al-Amr bil-Maruf wal-Nahian al-Munkar, called for a <i><a href="/wiki/Nahda" title="Nahda">nahda</a></i> ("renaissance") in the country as well as the establishment of a parliament with Islam being the instrument of Yemeni revival. Yahya largely repressed the Shabab and most of its leaders were executed following his son, <a href="/wiki/Ahmad_bin_Yahya" title="Ahmad bin Yahya">Imam Ahmad</a>'s inheritance of power in 1948.<sup id="cite_ref-Dumper331_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dumper331-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> That year, Sanaʽa was replaced with <a href="/wiki/Ta%27izz" class="mw-redirect" title="Ta&#39;izz">Ta'izz</a> as capital following Ahmad's new residence there. Most government offices followed suit. A few years later, most of the city's Jewish population emigrated to <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-R&amp;S631_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-R&amp;S631-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ahmad began a process of gradual economic and political liberalization, but by 1961 Sanaʽa was witnessing major demonstrations and riots demanding quicker reform and change. Pro-republican officers in the North Yemeni military sympathetic of <a href="/wiki/Gamal_Abdel_Nasser" title="Gamal Abdel Nasser">Gamal Abdel Nasser</a> of <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>'s government and <a href="/wiki/Pan-Arabism" title="Pan-Arabism">pan-Arabist</a> policies staged a coup overthrowing the Imamate government in September 1962, a week after Ahmad's death.<sup id="cite_ref-Dumper331_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dumper331-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Sanaʽa's role as capital was restored afterward.
<sup id="cite_ref-R&amp;S631_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-R&amp;S631-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Neighboring <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a> opposed this development and actively supported North Yemen's rural tribes, pitting large parts of the country against the urban and largely pro-republican inhabitants of Sanaʽa.<sup id="cite_ref-Dumper331_16-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dumper331-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/North_Yemen_Civil_War" title="North Yemen Civil War">North Yemen Civil War</a> resulted in the destruction of some parts of the city's ancient heritage and continued until 1968 when a deal between the republicans and the royalists was reached,<sup id="cite_ref-R&amp;S631_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-R&amp;S631-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> establishing a presidential system. Instability in Sanaʽa continued due to continuing coups and political assassinations until the situation in the country stabilized in the late 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-Dumper331_16-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dumper331-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>British writer <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Raban" title="Jonathan Raban">Jonathan Raban</a> visited in the 1970s and described the city as fortress-like, its architecture and layout resembling a <a href="/wiki/Labyrinth" title="Labyrinth">labyrinth</a>", further noting "It was like stepping out into the middle of a vast pop-up picturebook. Away from the street, the whole city turned into a maze of another kind, a dense, jumbled alphabet of signs and symbols."
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_era">Contemporary era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sana%CA%BDa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Contemporary era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Alsalh-24-2-2014_(16481824622).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Alsalh-24-2-2014_%2816481824622%29.jpg/220px-Alsalh-24-2-2014_%2816481824622%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="129" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Alsalh-24-2-2014_%2816481824622%29.jpg/330px-Alsalh-24-2-2014_%2816481824622%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Alsalh-24-2-2014_%2816481824622%29.jpg/440px-Alsalh-24-2-2014_%2816481824622%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2825" data-file-height="1656" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Alsalh-24-2-2014_(16481824622).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Al_Saleh_Mosque" title="Al Saleh Mosque">Al Saleh Mosque</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Attabari_Elementary_School_is_situated_in_the_middle_of_the_Old_City_of_Sana%27a,_a_UNESCO_World_Heritage_site.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Attabari_Elementary_School_is_situated_in_the_middle_of_the_Old_City_of_Sana%27a%2C_a_UNESCO_World_Heritage_site.jpg/220px-Attabari_Elementary_School_is_situated_in_the_middle_of_the_Old_City_of_Sana%27a%2C_a_UNESCO_World_Heritage_site.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Attabari_Elementary_School_is_situated_in_the_middle_of_the_Old_City_of_Sana%27a%2C_a_UNESCO_World_Heritage_site.jpg/330px-Attabari_Elementary_School_is_situated_in_the_middle_of_the_Old_City_of_Sana%27a%2C_a_UNESCO_World_Heritage_site.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Attabari_Elementary_School_is_situated_in_the_middle_of_the_Old_City_of_Sana%27a%2C_a_UNESCO_World_Heritage_site.jpg/440px-Attabari_Elementary_School_is_situated_in_the_middle_of_the_Old_City_of_Sana%27a%2C_a_UNESCO_World_Heritage_site.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3504" data-file-height="2336" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Attabari_Elementary_School_is_situated_in_the_middle_of_the_Old_City_of_Sana%27a,_a_UNESCO_World_Heritage_site.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Attabari Elementary School, Old City of Sanaʽa</div></div></div>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Yemeni_unification" title="Yemeni unification">unification of Yemen</a>, Sanaʽa was designated capital of the new Republic of Yemen. It houses the presidential palace, the <a href="/wiki/Assembly_of_Representatives_of_Yemen" class="mw-redirect" title="Assembly of Representatives of Yemen">parliament</a>, the supreme court and the country's government ministries. The largest source of employment is provided by the governmental civil service. Due to massive rural immigration, Sanaʽa has grown far outside its Old City, but this has placed a huge strain on the city's underdeveloped infrastructure and municipal services, particularly water.<sup id="cite_ref-Dumper331_16-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dumper331-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sanaʽa was chosen as the 2004 <a href="/wiki/Arab_Cultural_Capital" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab Cultural Capital">Arab Cultural Capital</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Arab_League" title="Arab League">Arab League</a>. In 2008, the <a href="/wiki/Al_Saleh_Mosque" title="Al Saleh Mosque">Al Saleh Mosque</a> was completed. It holds over 40,000 worshipers.
</p><p>In 2011, Sanaʽa, as the Yemeni capital, was the center of the <a href="/wiki/Yemeni_Revolution" title="Yemeni Revolution">Yemeni Revolution</a> in which <a href="/wiki/President_of_Yemen" title="President of Yemen">President</a> <a href="/wiki/Ali_Abdullah_Saleh" title="Ali Abdullah Saleh">Ali Abdullah Saleh</a> was ousted. Between May and November, the city was a battleground, in what became known as the <a href="/w/index.php?title=2011_Battle_of_Sana%CA%BDa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="2011 Battle of Sanaʽa (page does not exist)">2011 Battle of Sanaʽa</a>.
</p><p>On 21 May 2012, Sanaʽa was <a href="/wiki/2012_Sana%CA%BDa_bombing" title="2012 Sanaʽa bombing">attacked</a> by a suicide bomber, resulting in the deaths of 120 soldiers.
</p><p>On 23 January 2013, a drone strike near Al-Masna'ah village killed two civilians, according to a report<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> issued by <a href="/wiki/Radhya_Al-Mutawakel" title="Radhya Al-Mutawakel">Radhya Al-Mutawakel</a> and Abdulrasheed Al-Faqih and <a href="/wiki/Open_Society_Foundations" title="Open Society Foundations">Open Societies Foundations.</a>
</p><p>On 21 September 2014, during the <a href="/wiki/Houthi_insurgency_in_Yemen" title="Houthi insurgency in Yemen">Houthi insurgency</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Houthis" class="mw-redirect" title="Houthis">Houthis</a> <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Sana%CA%BDa_(2014)" title="Battle of Sanaʽa (2014)">seized</a>  control of Sanaʽa.
</p><p>On 12 June 2015,  <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabian-led_intervention_in_Yemen" title="Saudi Arabian-led intervention in Yemen">Saudi-led airstrikes</a> targeting Shiite rebels and their allies in Yemen destroyed historic houses in the center of the capital. A <a href="/wiki/UNESCO_World_Heritage_site" class="mw-redirect" title="UNESCO World Heritage site">UNESCO World Heritage site</a> was severely damaged.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 8 October 2016, <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabian-led_intervention_in_Yemen" title="Saudi Arabian-led intervention in Yemen">Saudi-led airstrikes</a> targeted a hall in Sanaʽa where a funeral was taking place. At least 140 people were killed and about 600 were wounded. After initially denying it was behind the attack, the Coalition's Joint Incidents Assessment Team admitted that it had bombed the hall but claimed that this attack had been a mistake caused by bad information.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On May 2017, according to the <a href="/wiki/International_Committee_of_the_Red_Cross" title="International Committee of the Red Cross">International Committee of the Red Cross</a> an outbreak of <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a> killed 115 people and left 8,500 ill.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In late 2017, another <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Sana%CA%BDa_(2017)" title="Battle of Sanaʽa (2017)">Battle of Sanaʽa</a> broke out between the Houthis and forces loyal to former President Saleh, who was killed.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography_and_climate">Geography and climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sana%CA%BDa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Geography and climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:%D8%AC%D8%A8%D9%84_%D9%86%D9%82%D9%85_-_panoramio.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/%D8%AC%D8%A8%D9%84_%D9%86%D9%82%D9%85_-_panoramio.jpg/220px-%D8%AC%D8%A8%D9%84_%D9%86%D9%82%D9%85_-_panoramio.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/%D8%AC%D8%A8%D9%84_%D9%86%D9%82%D9%85_-_panoramio.jpg/330px-%D8%AC%D8%A8%D9%84_%D9%86%D9%82%D9%85_-_panoramio.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/%D8%AC%D8%A8%D9%84_%D9%86%D9%82%D9%85_-_panoramio.jpg/440px-%D8%AC%D8%A8%D9%84_%D9%86%D9%82%D9%85_-_panoramio.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:%D8%AC%D8%A8%D9%84_%D9%86%D9%82%D9%85_-_panoramio.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Jabal Nuqm (<a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl">جَبَل نُقْم</span>&#8206;) or Jabal Nuqum (<a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl">جَبَل نُقُم</span>&#8206;) of the Yemeni <i><a href="/wiki/Sarawat_Mountains" title="Sarawat Mountains">Sarawat</a></i> in the area of Sana'a. Local legend has it that after the death of <a href="/wiki/Noah" title="Noah">Noah</a>, his son <a href="/wiki/Shem" title="Shem">Shem</a> built the city at the base of this mountain.<sup id="cite_ref-Laughlin2008a_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Laughlin2008a-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Districts">Districts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sana%CA%BDa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Districts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Generally, Sanaʽa is divided into two parts: the <a href="/wiki/Old_City_District" title="Old City District">Old City District</a> ("al-Qadeemah") and the new city ("al-Jadid.") The former is much smaller and retains the city's ancient heritage and mercantile way-of-living while the latter is an urban sprawl with many suburbs and modern buildings. The newer parts of the city were largely developed in the 1960s and onward when Sanaʽa was chosen as the republican capital.<sup id="cite_ref-R&amp;S631_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-R&amp;S631-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sana%CA%BDa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sanaʽa features the very rare mild version of a <a href="/wiki/Semi-arid_climate" title="Semi-arid climate">semi-arid climate</a> (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen-Geiger_climate_classification_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Köppen-Geiger climate classification system">Köppen</a>: BSk).<sup id="cite_ref-Climate-Data.org_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Climate-Data.org-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Sanaʽa sees on average 265&#160;mm (10.43&#160;in) of <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">precipitation</a> per year. Due to its high elevation, however, temperatures are much more moderate than many other cities on the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a>; average temperatures remain relatively constant throughout the year in Sanaʽa, with its coldest month being January and its warmest month July. Even considering this, as a result of its lower latitude and higher elevation, <a href="/wiki/UV_radiation" class="mw-redirect" title="UV radiation">UV radiation</a> from the sun is much stronger than in the hotter climates farther north on the <a href="/wiki/Arab_peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab peninsula">Arab peninsula</a>.
</p><p>The city seldom experiences extreme heat or cold. Some areas around the city, however, can see temperatures fall to around −9&#160;°C (16&#160;°F) or −7&#160;°C (19&#160;°F) during winter. <a href="/wiki/Frost" title="Frost">Frost</a> usually occurs in the early winter mornings, and there is a slight <a href="/wiki/Wind_chill" title="Wind chill">wind chill</a> in the city at elevated areas that causes the cold mornings to be bitter, including low <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a>. The sun warms the city to the high 15–20&#160;°C (59–68&#160;°F) and low 21–26&#160;°C (70–79&#160;°F) during the noontime but it drops drastically as night falls in.
</p><p>The city experiences many <a href="/wiki/Microclimate" title="Microclimate">microclimates</a> from district to district because of its location in the Sanaʽa basin and uneven elevations throughout the city. Summers are warm and can cool rapidly at night, especially after rainfall. Sanaʽa receives half of its annual rainfall during the months of July and August. Rainfall amounts vary from year to year; some years could see 500–600&#160;mm (20–24 inches) of rainfall, while others can barely get 150&#160;mm (5.9 inches). High temperatures have increased slightly during the summer over the past few years, but low temperatures and winter temperatures have dramatically fallen over the same period.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Old_City">Old City</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sana%CA%BDa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Old City">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sana,_Yemen_(4325153574).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Sana%2C_Yemen_%284325153574%29.jpg/220px-Sana%2C_Yemen_%284325153574%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Sana%2C_Yemen_%284325153574%29.jpg/330px-Sana%2C_Yemen_%284325153574%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Sana%2C_Yemen_%284325153574%29.jpg/440px-Sana%2C_Yemen_%284325153574%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3888" data-file-height="2592" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sana,_Yemen_(4325153574).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Night streetscene in Sanaʽa, Yemen</div></div></div>
<p>The Old City of Sanaʽa<sup id="cite_ref-Laughlin2008a_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Laughlin2008a-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl">مَدِيْنَة صَنْعَاء ٱلْقَدِيْمَة</span>&#8206;, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Arabic" title="Romanization of Arabic">romanized</a>:&#160;</small><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Madīnat Ṣanʿāʾ Al-Qadīmah</i>) is recognised as a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> World Heritage Site. The old fortified city has been inhabited for more than 2,500 years and contains many intact architectural gems. The oldest, partially standing architectural structure in the Old City of Sanaʽa is <a href="/wiki/Ghumdan_Palace" title="Ghumdan Palace">Ghumdan Palace</a>. The city was declared a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> by the United Nations in 1986. Efforts are underway to preserve some of the oldest buildings some of which, such as the Samsarh and the <a href="/wiki/Great_Mosque_of_Sana%CA%BDa" title="Great Mosque of Sanaʽa">Great Mosque of Sanaʽa</a>, are more than 1,400 years old. Surrounded by ancient clay walls that stand 9–14 metres (30–46&#160;ft) high, the Old City contains more than 100 mosques, 12 <i>hammams</i> (baths) and 6,500 houses. Many of the houses resemble ancient skyscrapers, reaching several stories high and topped with flat roofs. They are decorated with elaborate friezes and intricately carved frames and stained-glass windows.
</p><p>One of the most popular attractions is <i>Suq al-Milh</i> (Salt Market), where it is possible to buy salt along with bread, spices, raisins, cotton, copper, pottery, silverware, and antiques. The 7th-century <a href="/wiki/Great_Mosque_of_Sana%CA%BDa" title="Great Mosque of Sanaʽa"><i>Jāmiʿ al-Kabīr</i></a> (the Great Mosque) is one of the oldest mosques in the world. The <i><a href="/wiki/Bab_al-Yaman" title="Bab al-Yaman">Bāb al-Yaman</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-Laughlin2008a_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Laughlin2008a-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> ("Gate of the Yemen") is an iconized entry point through the city walls and is more than 1,000 years old.
</p><p>A commercial area of the Old City is known as <i>Al Madina</i> where development is proceeding rapidly. In addition to three large hotels, there are numerous stores and restaurants. The area also contains three parks and <a href="/wiki/Ali_Abdullah_Saleh" title="Ali Abdullah Saleh">the President</a>'s palace. The <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_Yemen" title="National Museum of Yemen">National Museum of Yemen</a> is located here.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sana%CA%BDa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">Football</a> (soccer) is the most popular sport in Sanaʽa. The city is home to the <a href="/wiki/Ali_Muhesen_Stadium" class="mw-redirect" title="Ali Muhesen Stadium">Ali Muhesen Stadium</a>, home of the <a href="/wiki/Yemen_national_football_team" title="Yemen national football team">Yemen national football team</a>, and is mostly used for football matches. The stadium holds 25,000 people.
</p>

<p>The city's population growth soared from the 1960s onward as a result of mass rural migration to the city in search of employment and improved standard of living.<sup id="cite_ref-R&amp;S631_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-R&amp;S631-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Sanaʽa is the fastest growing capital city in the world with a growth rate of 7%,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> while the growth rate of the nation as a whole is 3.2%.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> About 10% of the population resides in the Old City, while the remainder live in the outside districts.<sup id="cite_ref-R&amp;S631_17-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-R&amp;S631-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jewish_community">Jewish community</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sana%CA%BDa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Jewish community">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Abraham_b._Abraham_Yitzhak_Halevi_and_family.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Abraham_b._Abraham_Yitzhak_Halevi_and_family.jpg/220px-Abraham_b._Abraham_Yitzhak_Halevi_and_family.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="169" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Abraham_b._Abraham_Yitzhak_Halevi_and_family.jpg/330px-Abraham_b._Abraham_Yitzhak_Halevi_and_family.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Abraham_b._Abraham_Yitzhak_Halevi_and_family.jpg/440px-Abraham_b._Abraham_Yitzhak_Halevi_and_family.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="460" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Abraham_b._Abraham_Yitzhak_Halevi_and_family.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Yemeni Jewish family from Sanaʽa, ca. 1940</div></div></div>
<p>Jews have been present in Yemen since the 5th century BCE and form one of the most historic <a href="/wiki/Jewish_diaspora" title="Jewish diaspora">Jewish diasporas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> In Sanaʽa, Jews had initially settled within the enclosed citadel, known as <i>al-Qaṣr</i>, near the ruins of the old tower known as <a href="/wiki/Ghumdan_Palace" title="Ghumdan Palace">Ghumdan Palace</a>, but were evicted from there in the late 6th century by the ruling monarch, and moved to a different section of the city, known as <i>al-Marbaki</i> (also called the Falayhi Quarter). From there, they again uprooted and were made to settle in the section of the city known as <i>al-Quzali</i>, and eventually moved from there and settled in the neighborhood of <i>al-Sa'ilah</i>. In 1679, during the <a href="/wiki/Mawza_Exile" title="Mawza Exile">Mawza Exile</a>, they were once again evicted from their place of residence. Upon returning to the city in 1680, they were given a plot of land outside of the city walls, where they built the new Jewish Quarter, <i>al-Qāʻ</i> (now <i>Qāʻ al-ʻUlufi</i>), and where they remained until the community's demise in the mid-20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> After the creation of the political State of Israel in 1948, about 49,000 (of an estimated 51,000) of <a href="/wiki/Yemenite_Jews" title="Yemenite Jews">Yemenite Jews</a> were airlifted to Israel, almost 10,000 of whom were from Sanaʽa (see the English-language book <i>Jews and Muslims in lower Yemen: a study in protection and restraint, 1918–1949</i>). There was essentially no Jewish population in Sanaʽa until the <a href="/wiki/Shia_insurgency_in_Yemen" class="mw-redirect" title="Shia insurgency in Yemen">Shia insurgency</a> broke out in northern Yemen in 2004. The <a href="/wiki/Houthis" class="mw-redirect" title="Houthis">Houthis</a> directly threatened the Jewish community in 2007, prompting the government of <a href="/wiki/President_Saleh" class="mw-redirect" title="President Saleh">President Saleh</a> to offer them refuge in Sanaʽa. As of 2010<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sana%CA%BDa&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, there were around 700 Jews living in the capital under government protection.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sana%CA%BDa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Historically, Sanaʽa had a <a href="/wiki/Mining_industry" class="mw-redirect" title="Mining industry">mining industry</a>. The hills around Sanaʽa were mined for <a href="/wiki/Onyx" title="Onyx">onyx</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chalcedony" title="Chalcedony">chalcedony</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cornelian" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornelian">cornelian</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Prothero98_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero98-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The city was also known for its <a href="/wiki/Metalwork" class="mw-redirect" title="Metalwork">metalwork</a>, which the <a href="/wiki/British_people" title="British people">British</a> described as "famous" in the early 20th century, but declining in popularity.<sup id="cite_ref-Prothero99_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero99-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> As of 1920, Sanaʽa was described by the British as being "well supplied with fruit and <a href="/wiki/Grape" title="Grape">grapes</a>, and has good water."<sup id="cite_ref-Prothero103_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero103-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As the capital city of Yemen, 40% of jobs in Sanaʽa are in the public sector. Other primary sources of formal employment in the city are trade and industry. Like many other cities in the developing world, Sanaʽa has a large <a href="/wiki/Informal_sector" class="mw-redirect" title="Informal sector">informal sector</a> that is estimated to constitute 32% of nongovernmental employment.
While there is a greater variety of jobs in Sanaʽa as compared to other cities in Yemen, there is also greater poverty and unemployment. It is estimated that 25% of the labor force in Sanaʽa is unemployed.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sana%CA%BDa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Yemenia" title="Yemenia">Yemenia</a>, the national airline of Yemen, has its head office in Sanaʽa.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sana%CA%BDa_International_Airport" title="Sanaʽa International Airport">Sanaʽa International Airport</a> is Yemen's main domestic and international airport. A primary means of transport in the city is via dababs, minibuses that carry about 10 people. Taxis are also a common form of public transport and there are coaches to major cities such as <a href="/wiki/Aden" title="Aden">Aden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taiz" title="Taiz">Taiz</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Zoo">Zoo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sana%CA%BDa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Zoo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like <a href="/wiki/Ta%27izz_Zoo" class="mw-redirect" title="Ta&#39;izz Zoo">Ta'izz Zoo</a>, this zoo held fauna caught in the wild, such as the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_leopard" title="Arabian leopard">Arabian leopard</a>, as well as imported animals such as <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">African</a> <a href="/wiki/Lion" title="Lion">lions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gazelle" title="Gazelle">gazelles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BCFEW2001_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BCFEW2001-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The lions were thought to be of <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopian</a> origin, but a <a href="/wiki/Phylogeography" title="Phylogeography">phylogeographic</a> test demonstrated them to be different from <a href="/wiki/Panthera_leo_leo#Genetic_research" title="Panthera leo leo">captive Ethiopian lions</a> kept at <a href="/wiki/Addis_Ababa_Zoo" title="Addis Ababa Zoo">Addis Ababa Zoo</a>, and more similar to <a href="/wiki/Panthera_leo_melanochaita" title="Panthera leo melanochaita">lions</a> from <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">Eastern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Africa" title="Southern Africa">Southern Africa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bruche_al2012_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bruche_al2012-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
