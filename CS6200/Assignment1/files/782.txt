<p>A <b>counterculture</b> (also written <b>counter-culture</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Subculture" title="Subculture">subculture</a> whose values and norms of behavior differ substantially from those of mainstream society, often in opposition to mainstream cultural <a href="/wiki/Mores" title="Mores">mores</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MWebster_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MWebster-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Eric_Donald_Hirsch_1993_p_419_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eric_Donald_Hirsch_1993_p_419-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  A countercultural movement expresses the ethos and aspirations of a specific population during a well-defined era. When oppositional forces reach <a href="/wiki/Critical_mass_(sociodynamics)" title="Critical mass (sociodynamics)">critical mass</a>, countercultures can trigger dramatic cultural changes.  Prominent examples of <a href="/wiki/Late_modernism" title="Late modernism">Late Modern</a> countercultures in the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western world</a> include <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romanticism</a> (1790–1840), <a href="/wiki/Bohemianism" title="Bohemianism">Bohemianism</a> (1850–1910), the more fragmentary counterculture of the <a href="/wiki/Beat_Generation" title="Beat Generation">Beat Generation</a> (1944–1964), followed by the globalized <a href="/wiki/Counterculture_of_the_1960s" title="Counterculture of the 1960s">counterculture of the 1960s</a> (1964–1974), usually associated with the <a href="/wiki/Hippie" title="Hippie">hippie</a> subculture<sup id="cite_ref-HarvardTR_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HarvardTR-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and the diversified <a href="/wiki/Punk_subculture" title="Punk subculture">punk subculture</a> of the 1970s and 1980s.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition_and_characteristics">Definition and characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counterculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition and characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Milton_Yinger" title="John Milton Yinger">John Milton Yinger</a> originated the term "contraculture" in his 1960 article in <i><a href="/wiki/American_Sociological_Review" title="American Sociological Review">American Sociological Review</a></i>. Yinger suggested the use of the term contraculture "wherever the normative system of a group contains, as a primary element, a theme of conflict with the values of the total society, where personality variables are directly involved in the development and maintenance of the group's values, and wherever its norms can be understood only by reference to the relationships of the group to a surrounding dominant culture." <sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some scholars have attributed the <i>counterculture</i> to <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roszak_(scholar)" title="Theodore Roszak (scholar)">Theodore Roszak</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-HarvardTR_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HarvardTR-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TRbook_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TRbook-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> author of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Making_of_a_Counter_Culture" title="The Making of a Counter Culture">The Making of a Counter Culture</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  It became prominent in the news media amid the <a href="/wiki/Social_revolution" title="Social revolution">social revolution</a> that swept the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> during the 1960s.<sup id="cite_ref-MWebster_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MWebster-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HarvardTR_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HarvardTR-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TRbook_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TRbook-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Scholars differ in the characteristics and specificity they attribute to "counterculture". "Mainstream" culture is of course also difficult to define, and in some ways becomes identified and understood through contrast with counterculture. Counterculture might oppose mass culture (or "media culture"),<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> or middle-class culture and values.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Counterculture is sometimes conceptualized in terms of generational conflict and rejection of older or adult values.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Counterculture may or may not be explicitly political. It typically involves criticism or rejection of currently powerful institutions, with accompanying hope for a better life or a new society.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> It does not look favorably on party politics or <a href="/wiki/Authoritarianism" title="Authoritarianism">authoritarianism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cultural development can also be affected by way of counterculture. Scholars such as Joanne Martin and Caren Siehl, deem counterculture and cultural development as "a balancing act, [that] some core values of a counterculture should present a direct challenge to the core values of a dominant culture". Therefore, a prevalent culture and a counterculture should coexist in an uneasy symbiosis, holding opposite positions on valuable issues that are essentially important to each of them. According to this theory, a counterculture can contribute a plethora of useful functions for the prevalent culture, such as "articulating the foundations between appropriate and inappropriate behavior and providing a safe haven for the development of innovative ideas".<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Typically, a  "fringe culture" expands and grows into a counterculture by defining its own values in opposition to mainstream norms.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Countercultures tend to peak, then go into decline, leaving a lasting impact on mainstream cultural values. Their life cycles include phases of rejection, growth, partial acceptance and absorption into the mainstream.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  During the late 1960s, hippies became the largest and most visible countercultural group in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-Yablonsky,_Lewis_1968_pp_21-37_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yablonsky,_Lewis_1968_pp_21-37-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The "cultural shadows" left by the Romantics, Bohemians, Beats and Hippies remain visible in contemporary Western culture.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>According to Sheila Whiteley, "recent developments in sociological theory complicate and problematize theories developed in the 1960s, with digital technology, for example, providing an impetus for new understandings of counterculture".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Andy Bennett writes that "despite the theoretical arguments that can be raised against the sociological value of counterculture as a meaningful term for categorising social action, like <a href="/wiki/Subculture" title="Subculture">subculture</a>, the term lives on as a concept in social and <a href="/wiki/Cultural_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Cultural theory">cultural theory</a>… [to] become part of a received, mediated memory". However, "this involved not simply the utopian but also the dystopian and that while festivals such as those held at <a href="/wiki/Monterey" class="mw-redirect" title="Monterey">Monterey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Woodstock" title="Woodstock">Woodstock</a> might appear to embrace the former, the deaths of such iconic figures as <a href="/wiki/Brian_Jones" title="Brian Jones">Brian Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jimi_Hendrix" title="Jimi Hendrix">Jimi Hendrix</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Morrison" title="Jim Morrison">Jim Morrison</a> and <a href="/wiki/Janis_Joplin" title="Janis Joplin">Janis Joplin</a>, the nihilistic mayhem at <a href="/wiki/Altamont_Free_Concert" title="Altamont Free Concert">Altamont</a>, and the shadowy figure of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Manson" title="Charles Manson">Charles Manson</a> cast a darker light on its underlying agenda, one that reminds us that ‘pathological issues [are] still very much at large in today's world".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counterculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The counterculture of the 1960s and early 1970s generated its own unique brand of notable literature, including comics and cartoons, and sometimes referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Underground_press" title="Underground press">underground press</a>. In the United States, this includes the work of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Crumb" title="Robert Crumb">Robert Crumb</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Shelton" title="Gilbert Shelton">Gilbert Shelton</a>, and includes <a href="/wiki/Mr._Natural_(comics)" title="Mr. Natural (comics)">Mr. Natural</a>; <a href="/wiki/Keep_on_Truckin%27_(comics)" title="Keep on Truckin&#39; (comics)">Keep on Truckin'</a>; <i><a href="/wiki/Fritz_the_Cat" title="Fritz the Cat">Fritz the Cat</a></i>; <a href="/wiki/Fat_Freddy%27s_Cat" title="Fat Freddy&#39;s Cat">Fat Freddy's Cat</a>; <a href="/wiki/Fabulous_Furry_Freak_Brothers" class="mw-redirect" title="Fabulous Furry Freak Brothers">Fabulous Furry Freak Brothers</a>; the album cover art for <i><a href="/wiki/Cheap_Thrills_(Big_Brother_and_the_Holding_Company_album)" title="Cheap Thrills (Big Brother and the Holding Company album)">Cheap Thrills</a></i>; and in several countries contributions to <i><a href="/wiki/International_Times" title="International Times">International Times</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a></i>, and <a href="/wiki/Oz_(magazine)" title="Oz (magazine)"><i>Oz</i> magazine</a>. During the late 1960s and early 1970s, these comics and magazines were available for purchase in <a href="/wiki/Head_shop" title="Head shop">head shops</a> along with items like beads, incense, cigarette papers, tie-dye clothing, <a href="/wiki/Blacklight_paint" title="Blacklight paint">Day-Glo</a> posters, books, etc.
</p><p>During the late 1960s and early 1970s, some of these shops selling hippie items also became cafés where hippies could hang out, chat, smoke <a href="/wiki/Marijuana" class="mw-redirect" title="Marijuana">marijuana</a>, read books, etc., e.g. <a href="/wiki/Gandalf%27s_Garden" title="Gandalf&#39;s Garden">Gandalf's Garden</a> in the <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Road" title="King&#39;s Road">King's Road</a>, London, which also published a magazine of the same name.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Another such hippie/anarchist bookshop was <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_mushroom" class="mw-redirect" title="Psychedelic mushroom">Mushroom</a> Books, tucked away in the <a href="/wiki/Lace_Market" title="Lace Market">Lace Market</a> area of <a href="/wiki/Nottingham" title="Nottingham">Nottingham</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counterculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some genres tend to challenge societies with their content that is meant to outright question the norms within cultures and even create change usually towards a more modern way of thought. More often than not, sources of these controversies can be found in art such as <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Duchamp" title="Marcel Duchamp">Marcel Duchamp</a> whose piece <i><a href="/wiki/Fountain_(Duchamp)" title="Fountain (Duchamp)">Fountain</a></i> was meant to be "a calculated attack on the most basic conventions of art"<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> in 1917. Contentious artists like <a href="/wiki/Banksy" title="Banksy">Banksy</a> base most of their works off of mainstream media and culture to bring pieces that usually shock viewers into thinking about their piece in more detail and the themes behind them. A great example can be found in <a href="/wiki/Dismaland" title="Dismaland">Dismaland</a>, the biggest project of "<a href="/wiki/Anarchism" title="Anarchism">anarchism</a>" to be organised and exhibited which showcases multiple works such as an "iconic <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">Disney</a> princess's horse-drawn pumpkin carriage, [appearing] to re-enact the death of <a href="/wiki/Diana,_Princess_of_Wales" title="Diana, Princess of Wales">Princess Diana</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counterculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Counterculture is very much evident in music particularly on the basis of the separation of genres into those considered acceptable and within the status quo and those not. Since many minorities groups are already considered counterculture, the music they create and produce may reflect their sociopolitical realities and their musical culture may be adopted as a social expression of their counterculture. This is reflected in dancehall with the concept of base frequencies and base culture in Henriques's "Sonic diaspora", where he expounds that "base denotes crude, debased, unrefined, vulgar, and even animal" for the Jamaican middle class and is associated with the "bottom-end, low frequencies…basic lower frequencies and embodied resonances distinctly inferior to the higher notes" that appear in dancehall.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
According to Henriques, "base culture is bottom-up popular, street culture, generated by an urban underclass surviving almost entirely outside the formal economy".<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> That the music is low frequency sonically and regarded as reflective of a lower culture shows the influential connection between counterculture and the music produced. Although music may be considered base and counter culture, it may actually enjoy a lot of popularity which can be seen by the labelling of hip hop as a counter culture genre, despite it being one of the most commercially successful and high charting genres.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Assimilation">Assimilation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counterculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Assimilation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many of these artists though once being taboo, have been assimilated into culture and are no longer a source of moral panic since they don't cross overtly controversial topics or challenge staples of current culture.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Instead of being a topic to fear, they have initiated subtle trends that other artists and sources of media may follow.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="LGBT">LGBT</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counterculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: LGBT">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gay_liberation" title="Gay liberation">Gay liberation</a> (considered a <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/precursor" class="extiw" title="wikt:precursor">precursor</a> of various modern <a href="/wiki/LGBT_social_movements" title="LGBT social movements">LGBT social movements</a>) was known for its links to the counterculture of the time (e.g. groups like the <a href="/wiki/Radical_Faeries" title="Radical Faeries">Radical Faeries</a>), and for the gay liberationists' intent to transform or abolish fundamental institutions of society such as <a href="/wiki/Gender" title="Gender">gender</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_family" title="Nuclear family">nuclear family</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-HoffmanIntro_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HoffmanIntro-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> in general, the politics were radical, <a href="/wiki/Anti-racism" title="Anti-racism">anti-racist</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anti-capitalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-capitalist">anti-capitalist</a> in nature.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> In order to achieve such liberation, <a href="/wiki/Consciousness_raising" title="Consciousness raising">consciousness raising</a> and <a href="/wiki/Direct_action" title="Direct action">direct action</a> were employed.<sup id="cite_ref-HoffmanIntro_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HoffmanIntro-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At the outset of the 20th century, <a href="/wiki/Homosexual" class="mw-redirect" title="Homosexual">homosexual</a> acts were punishable offenses in these countries.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The prevailing public attitude was that homosexuality was a moral failing that should be punished, as exemplified by <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Wilde" title="Oscar Wilde">Oscar Wilde</a>'s 1895 trial and imprisonment for "gross indecency". But even then, there were dissenting views. <a href="/wiki/Sigmund_Freud" title="Sigmund Freud">Sigmund Freud</a> publicly expressed his opinion that homosexuality was "assuredly no advantage, but it is nothing to be ashamed of, no vice, no degradation; it cannot be classified as an illness; we consider it to be a variation of the sexual function, produced by a certain arrest of sexual development".<sup id="cite_ref-ErnstLFreud_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ErnstLFreud-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> According to Charles Kaiser's <i>The Gay Metropolis</i>, there were already semi-public gay-themed gatherings by the mid-1930s in the United States (such as the annual <a href="/wiki/Drag_(clothing)" title="Drag (clothing)">drag</a> balls held during the <a href="/wiki/Harlem_Renaissance" title="Harlem Renaissance">Harlem Renaissance</a>). There were also <a href="/wiki/Gay_bar" title="Gay bar">bars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gay_bathhouse" title="Gay bathhouse">bathhouses</a> that catered to gay clientele and adopted warning procedures (similar to those used by <a href="/wiki/Prohibition" title="Prohibition">Prohibition</a>-era <a href="/wiki/Speakeasy" title="Speakeasy">speakeasies</a>) to warn customers of police raids. But homosexuality was typically subsumed into <a href="/wiki/Bohemianism" title="Bohemianism">bohemian</a> culture, and was not a significant movement in itself.<sup id="cite_ref-Gay_Metro_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gay_Metro-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Eventually, a genuine <a href="/wiki/Gay_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Gay culture">gay culture</a> began to take root, albeit very discreetly, with its own styles, attitudes and behaviors and industries began catering to this growing demographic group. For example, publishing houses cranked out <a href="/wiki/Pulp_novel" class="mw-redirect" title="Pulp novel">pulp novels</a> like <i><a href="/wiki/The_Velvet_Underground_(book)" title="The Velvet Underground (book)">The Velvet Underground</a></i> that were targeted directly at gay people. By the early 1960s, openly gay political organizations such as the <a href="/wiki/Mattachine_Society" title="Mattachine Society">Mattachine Society</a> were formally protesting abusive treatment toward gay people, challenging the entrenched idea that homosexuality was an aberrant condition, and calling for the decriminalization of homosexuality. Despite very limited sympathy, American society began at least to acknowledge the existence of a sizable population of gays. The film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boys_in_the_Band_(1970_film)" title="The Boys in the Band (1970 film)">The Boys in the Band</a></i>, for example, featured negative portrayals of gay men, but at least recognized that they did in fact fraternize with each other (as opposed to being isolated, solitary predators who "victimized" straight men).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Disco" title="Disco">Disco music</a> in large part rose out of the New York gay club scene of the early 1970s as a reaction to the stigmatization of gays and other outside groups such as blacks by the counterculture of that era.<sup id="cite_ref-alternative_to_rock&#39;n&#39;roll_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alternative_to_rock&#39;n&#39;roll-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Partylikeits1975_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Partylikeits1975-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cambridge_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Traces_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Traces-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Italian-americans_and_disco_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Italian-americans_and_disco-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> By later in the decade Disco was dominating the pop charts.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> The popular <a href="/wiki/Village_People" title="Village People">Village People</a> and the critically acclaimed <a href="/wiki/Sylvester_(singer)" title="Sylvester (singer)">Sylvester</a> had gay-themed lyrics and presentation.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another element of <a href="/wiki/LGBT" title="LGBT">LGBT</a> counter-culture that began in the 1970s—and continues today—is the <a href="/wiki/Womyn%27s_land" title="Womyn&#39;s land">lesbian land</a>, landdyke movement, or <a href="/wiki/Womyn%27s_land" title="Womyn&#39;s land">womyn's land</a> movement.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Radical feminists inspired by the <a href="/wiki/Back-to-the-land" class="mw-redirect" title="Back-to-the-land">back-to-the-land</a> initiative and migrated to rural areas to create communities that were often female-only and/or lesbian communes.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> "Free Spaces" are defined by Sociologist Francesca Polletta as "small-scale settings within a community or movement that are removed from the direct control of dominant groups, are voluntarily participated in, and generate the cultural challenge that precedes or accompanies political mobilization.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Women came together in Free Spaces like music festivals, activist groups and collectives to share ideas with like-minded people and to explore the idea of the lesbian land movement. The movement is closely tied to <a href="/wiki/Eco-feminism" class="mw-redirect" title="Eco-feminism">eco-feminism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The four tenets of the Landdyke Movement are relationship with the land, liberation and transformation, living the politics, and bodily Freedoms.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>  Most importantly, members of these communities seek to live outside of a <a href="/wiki/Patriarchal_society" class="mw-redirect" title="Patriarchal society">patriarchal society</a> that puts emphasis on "beauty ideals that discipline the female body, compulsive heterosexuality, competitiveness with other women, and dependence".<sup id="cite_ref-AnahitaSine_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AnahitaSine-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Instead of adhering typical female <a href="/wiki/Gender_roles" class="mw-redirect" title="Gender roles">gender roles</a>, the women of Landdyke communities value "self-sufficiency, bodily strength, autonomy from men and patriarchal systems, and the development of lesbian-centered community".<sup id="cite_ref-AnahitaSine_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AnahitaSine-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Members of the Landdyke movement enjoy bodily freedoms that have been deemed unacceptable in the modern Western world—such as the freedom to expose their breasts, or to go without any clothing at all.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>  An awareness of their impact on the Earth, and connection to nature is essential members of the Landdyke Movement's way of life.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The watershed event in the American gay rights movement was the 1969 <a href="/wiki/Stonewall_riots" title="Stonewall riots">Stonewall riots</a> in New York City. Following this event, gays and lesbians began to adopt the militant protest tactics used by <a href="/wiki/Opposition_to_the_Vietnam_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Opposition to the Vietnam War">anti-war</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Black power">black power</a> radicals to confront anti-gay ideology. Another major turning point was the 1973 decision by the <a href="/wiki/American_Psychiatric_Association" title="American Psychiatric Association">American Psychiatric Association</a> to remove homosexuality from the official list of <a href="/wiki/Mental_disorder" title="Mental disorder">mental disorders</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> Although gay radicals used pressure to force the decision, Kaiser notes that this had been an issue of some debate for many years in the psychiatric community, and that one of the chief obstacles to normalizing homosexuality was that therapists were profiting from offering dubious, unproven "cures".<sup id="cite_ref-Gay_Metro_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gay_Metro-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS_in_the_United_States" title="HIV/AIDS in the United States">AIDS epidemic</a> was initially an unexpected blow to the movement, especially in North America. There was speculation that the disease would permanently drive gay life underground. Ironically, the tables were turned. Many of the early victims of the disease had been openly gay only within the confines of insular "gay ghettos" such as New York City's <a href="/wiki/Greenwich_Village" title="Greenwich Village">Greenwich Village</a> and San Francisco's <a href="/wiki/The_Castro" class="mw-redirect" title="The Castro">Castro</a>; they remained closeted in their professional lives and to their families. Many heterosexuals who thought they didn't know any gay people were confronted by friends and loved ones dying of "the gay plague" (which soon began to infect <a href="/wiki/Heterosexuality" title="Heterosexuality">heterosexual</a> people also). LGBT communities were increasingly seen not only as victims of a disease, but as victims of ostracism and hatred. Most importantly, the disease became a rallying point for a previously complacent gay community. AIDS invigorated the community politically to fight not only for a medical response to the disease, but also for wider acceptance of homosexuality in mainstream America. Ultimately, <a href="/wiki/Coming_out" title="Coming out">coming out</a> became an important step for many LGBT people.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>During the early 1980s what was dubbed "<a href="/wiki/New_Music_(music_industry)" class="mw-redirect" title="New Music (music industry)">New Music</a>", <a href="/wiki/New_wave_music" title="New wave music">New wave</a>, "New pop"  popularized by <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a> and associated with <a href="/wiki/Gender_bending" class="mw-redirect" title="Gender bending">gender bending</a> <a href="/wiki/Second_British_Invasion" title="Second British Invasion">Second British Music Invasion</a> stars such as <a href="/wiki/Boy_George" title="Boy George">Boy George</a> and <a href="/wiki/Annie_Lennox" title="Annie Lennox">Annie Lennox</a> became what was described by <a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a> at the time as an alternate mainstream to the traditional masculine/heterosexual rock music in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-Sullivan_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sullivan-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Reynolds_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reynolds-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2003, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Supreme Court">United States Supreme Court</a> officially declared all <a href="/wiki/Sodomy" title="Sodomy">sodomy</a> laws unconstitutional in <i><a href="/wiki/Lawrence_v._Texas" title="Lawrence v. Texas">Lawrence v. Texas</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counterculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bill Osgerby argues that:
</p>
<blockquote><p>the counterculture's various strands developed from earlier artistic and political movements. On both sides of the Atlantic the 1950s "Beat Generation" had fused existentialist philosophy with jazz, poetry, literature, Eastern mysticism and drugs – themes that were all sustained in the 1960s counterculture.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counterculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Counterculture_of_the_1960s" title="Counterculture of the 1960s">Counterculture of the 1960s</a> and <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_1960s_counterculture" title="Timeline of 1960s counterculture">Timeline of 1960s counterculture</a></div>
<p>In the United States, the counterculture of the 1960s became identified with the rejection of conventional <a href="/wiki/Social_norms" class="mw-redirect" title="Social norms">social norms</a> of the 1950s. Counterculture youth rejected the cultural standards of their parents, especially with respect to racial <a href="/wiki/Racial_segregation" title="Racial segregation">segregation</a> and initial widespread support for the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Eric_Donald_Hirsch_1993_p_419_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eric_Donald_Hirsch_1993_p_419-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> and, less directly, the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>—with many young people fearing that America's <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_arms_race" title="Nuclear arms race">nuclear arms race</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, coupled with its involvement in Vietnam, would lead to a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_holocaust" title="Nuclear holocaust">nuclear holocaust</a>.
</p><p>In the United States, widespread tensions developed in the 1960s in American society that tended to flow along generational lines regarding the <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">war</a> in Vietnam, <a href="/wiki/Race_relations" title="Race relations">race relations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sexual_revolution" title="Sexual revolution">sexual mores</a>, women's rights, traditional modes of authority, and a <a href="/wiki/Economic_materialism" title="Economic materialism">materialist</a> interpretation of the <a href="/wiki/American_Dream" title="American Dream">American Dream</a>. White, <a href="/wiki/Middle_class" title="Middle class">middle class</a> youth—who made up the bulk of the counterculture in western countries—had sufficient leisure time, thanks to widespread economic <a href="/wiki/Prosperity" title="Prosperity">prosperity</a>, to turn their attention to <a href="/wiki/Social_issues" class="mw-redirect" title="Social issues">social issues</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Conscience_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conscience-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> These social issues included support for <a href="/wiki/Civil_rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil rights">civil rights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_rights#Modern_movements" title="Women&#39;s rights">women's rights</a>, and <a href="/wiki/LGBT_social_movements" title="LGBT social movements">gay rights</a> movements, and a rejection of the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>. The counterculture also had access to a media which was eager to present their concerns to a wider public. Demonstrations for <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">social justice</a> created far-reaching changes affecting many aspects of society.  <a href="/wiki/Hippies" class="mw-redirect" title="Hippies">Hippies</a> became the largest countercultural group in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-Yablonsky,_Lewis_1968_pp_21-37_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yablonsky,_Lewis_1968_pp_21-37-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:30%;&#32;;"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r887775652">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%;max-width:100%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{margin:0.5em auto 0.8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft p,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright p{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:larger;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" “ ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" ” ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:360px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{min-width:100%;margin:0 0 0.8em!important;float:none!important}}</style>
<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">"The 60s were a leap in <a href="/wiki/Human_consciousness" class="mw-redirect" title="Human consciousness">human consciousness</a>. <a href="/wiki/Mahatma_Gandhi" title="Mahatma Gandhi">Mahatma Gandhi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_X" title="Malcolm X">Malcolm X</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King" class="mw-redirect" title="Martin Luther King">Martin Luther King</a>, <a href="/wiki/Che_Guevara" title="Che Guevara">Che Guevara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mother_Teresa" title="Mother Teresa">Mother Teresa</a>, they led a <a href="/wiki/Revolution#Political_and_socioeconomic_revolutions" title="Revolution">revolution</a> of conscience. <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Doors" title="The Doors">The Doors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jimi_Hendrix" title="Jimi Hendrix">Jimi Hendrix</a> created revolution and evolution themes. The music was like <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Dal%C3%AD" title="Salvador Dalí">Dalí</a>, with many colors and revolutionary ways. The youth of today must go there to find themselves."</div>
<p><cite class="left-aligned" style="">— <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Santana" title="Carlos Santana">Carlos Santana</a><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup></cite>
</p>
</div>
<p>Rejection of mainstream culture was best embodied in the new genres of <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_rock" title="Psychedelic rock">psychedelic rock</a> music, <a href="/wiki/Pop-art" class="mw-redirect" title="Pop-art">pop-art</a> and new explorations in <a href="/wiki/Spirituality" title="Spirituality">spirituality</a>. Musicians who exemplified this era in the United Kingdom and United States included <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Lennon" title="John Lennon">John Lennon</a>, &#160;<a href="/wiki/Neil_Young" title="Neil Young">Neil Young</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dylan" title="Bob Dylan">Bob Dylan</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Grateful_Dead" class="mw-redirect" title="The Grateful Dead">The Grateful Dead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_Airplane" title="Jefferson Airplane">Jefferson Airplane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jimi_Hendrix" title="Jimi Hendrix">Jimi Hendrix</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Doors" title="The Doors">The Doors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Zappa" title="Frank Zappa">Frank Zappa</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stones" title="The Rolling Stones">The Rolling Stones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Velvet_Underground" class="mw-redirect" title="Velvet Underground">Velvet Underground</a>, <a href="/wiki/Janis_Joplin" title="Janis Joplin">Janis Joplin</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Who" title="The Who">The Who</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joni_Mitchell" title="Joni Mitchell">Joni Mitchell</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Kinks" title="The Kinks">The Kinks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sly_and_the_Family_Stone" title="Sly and the Family Stone">Sly and the Family Stone</a><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> and, in their early years, <a href="/wiki/Chicago_(band)" title="Chicago (band)">Chicago</a>. New forms of musical presentation also played a key role in spreading the counterculture, with large outdoor rock festivals being the most noteworthy.  The climactic live statement on this occurred from August 15–18, 1969, with the <i><a href="/wiki/Woodstock" title="Woodstock">Woodstock</a> Music Festival</i> held in <a href="/wiki/Bethel,_New_York" title="Bethel, New York">Bethel, New York</a>—with 32 of <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a>'s and <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_rock" title="Psychedelic rock">psychedelic rock</a>'s most popular acts performing live outdoors during the sometimes rainy weekend to an audience of half a million people. (<a href="/wiki/Michael_Lang_(producer)" title="Michael Lang (producer)">Michael Lang</a> stated 400,000 attended, half of which did not have a ticket.)<sup id="cite_ref-NY_Times_Aug_27,_1969_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NY_Times_Aug_27,_1969-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> It is widely regarded as a pivotal moment in popular music history—with <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> calling it one of the <i>50 Moments That Changed the History of Rock and Roll</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> According to Bill Mankin, "It seems fitting… that one of the most enduring labels for the entire generation of that era was derived from a rock festival: the ‘Woodstock Generation’."<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sentiments were expressed in song lyrics and popular sayings of the period, such as "do your own thing", "<a href="/wiki/Turn_on,_tune_in,_drop_out" title="Turn on, tune in, drop out">turn on, tune in, drop out</a>", "whatever turns you on", "<a href="/wiki/Eight_miles_high" class="mw-redirect" title="Eight miles high">Eight miles high</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Wine,_women_and_song" title="Wine, women and song">sex, drugs, and rock 'n' roll</a>", and "<a href="/wiki/Light_My_Fire" title="Light My Fire">light my fire</a>". Spiritually, the counterculture included interest in <a href="/wiki/Astrology" title="Astrology">astrology</a>, the term "<a href="/wiki/Age_of_Aquarius" title="Age of Aquarius">Age of Aquarius</a>" and knowing people's <a href="/wiki/Astrological_signs" class="mw-redirect" title="Astrological signs">astrological signs</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Zodiac" title="Zodiac">Zodiac</a>. This led Theodore Roszak to state "A &#32;&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>&#93; eclectic taste for <a href="/wiki/Mysticism" title="Mysticism">mystic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Occult" title="Occult">occult</a>, and magical phenomena has been a marked characteristic of our <a href="/wiki/Postwar" class="mw-redirect" title="Postwar">postwar</a> youth culture since the days of the <a href="/wiki/Beatnik" title="Beatnik">beatniks</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-TRbook_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TRbook-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In the United States, even actor <a href="/wiki/Charlton_Heston" title="Charlton Heston">Charlton Heston</a> contributed to the movement, with the statement "Don't trust anyone over thirty" (a saying coined in 1965 by activist <a href="/wiki/Jack_Weinberg" title="Jack Weinberg">Jack Weinberg</a>) in the 1968 film <i><a href="/wiki/Planet_of_the_Apes_(1968_film)" title="Planet of the Apes (1968 film)">Planet of the Apes</a></i>; the same year, actress and social activist <a href="/wiki/Jane_Fonda" title="Jane Fonda">Jane Fonda</a> starred in the sexually-themed <i><a href="/wiki/Barbarella_(film)" title="Barbarella (film)">Barbarella</a></i>. Both actors opposed the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a> during its duration, and Fonda would eventually become controversially active in the <a href="/wiki/Peace_movement" title="Peace movement">peace movement</a>.
</p><p>The counterculture in the United States has been interpreted as lasting roughly from 1964 to 1972<sup id="cite_ref-Reason_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reason-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>—coincident with America's involvement in Vietnam—and reached its peak in August 1969 at the Woodstock Festival, New York, characterized in part by the film <i>Easy Rider</i> (1969). Unconventional or psychedelic dress; political activism; public protests; campus uprisings; pacifist then loud, defiant music; drugs; <a href="/wiki/Communitarianism" title="Communitarianism">communitarian experiments</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sexual_liberation" class="mw-redirect" title="Sexual liberation">sexual liberation</a> were hallmarks of the sixties counterculture—most of whose members were young, white and middle-class.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States, the movement divided the  population. To some Americans, these attributes reflected American ideals of <a href="/wiki/Free_speech" class="mw-redirect" title="Free speech">free speech</a>, equality, <a href="/wiki/World_peace" title="World peace">world peace</a>, and the pursuit of happiness; to others, they reflected a self-indulgent, pointlessly rebellious, unpatriotic, and destructive assault on the country's traditional <a href="/wiki/Argument_from_morality" title="Argument from morality">moral order</a>. Authorities banned the psychedelic drug <a href="/wiki/LSD" class="mw-redirect" title="LSD">LSD</a>, restricted political gatherings, and tried to enforce bans on what they considered <a href="/wiki/Obscenity" title="Obscenity">obscenity</a> in books, music, theater, and other media.
</p><p>The counterculture has been argued  to have diminished in the early 1970s, and some have attributed two reasons for this. First, it has been suggested that the most popular of its political goals—<a href="/wiki/Civil_rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil rights">civil rights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Civil_liberties" title="Civil liberties">civil liberties</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gender_equality" title="Gender equality">gender equality</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalism</a>, and the end of the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>—were  "accomplished" (to at least  some degree); and also that its most popular social attributes—particularly a "<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/live_and_let_live" class="extiw" title="wikt:live and let live">live and let live</a>" mentality in personal lifestyles (the "<a href="/wiki/Sexual_revolution" title="Sexual revolution">sexual revolution</a>")—were co-opted by mainstream society.<sup id="cite_ref-Conscience_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conscience-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Beatles_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beatles-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> Second, a decline of idealism and <a href="/wiki/Hedonism" title="Hedonism">hedonism</a> occurred as many notable counterculture figures died, the rest settled into mainstream society and started their own families, and the "magic economy" of the 1960s gave way to the <a href="/wiki/1973%E2%80%9375_recession" class="mw-redirect" title="1973–75 recession">stagflation of the 1970s</a><sup id="cite_ref-Conscience_57-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conscience-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>—the latter costing many in the  middle-classes the luxury of being able to live outside conventional <a href="/wiki/Social_institution" class="mw-redirect" title="Social institution">social institutions</a>. The counterculture, however, continues to influence <a href="/wiki/Social_movement" title="Social movement">social movements</a>, art, music, and society in general, and the post-1973 mainstream society has been in many ways a hybrid of the 1960s establishment and counterculture.<sup id="cite_ref-Beatles_65-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beatles-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The counterculture movement has been said to be rejuvenated in a way that maintains some similarities from the Counterculture of the 1960s, but it is different as well. Photographer Steve Schapiro investigated and documented these contemporary hippie communities from 2012 to 2014. He traveled the country with his son, attending festival after festival. These findings were compiled in Schapiro’s book <i>Bliss: Transformational Festivals &amp; the Neo Hippie.</i> One of his most valued findings was that these “Neo Hippies” experience and encourage such a spiritual commitment to the community.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counterculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Australia's countercultural trend followed the one burgeoning in the US, and to a lesser extend the one in Great Britain. Political scandals in the country, such as the <a href="/wiki/Disappearance_of_Harold_Holt" title="Disappearance of Harold Holt">disappearance of Harold Holt</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/1975_Australian_constitutional_crisis" title="1975 Australian constitutional crisis">1975 constitutional crisis</a>, as well as Australia's involvement in <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>, led to a disillusionment or disengagement with political figures and the government. Large protests were held in the countries most populated cities such as <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a>, one prominent march was held in Sydney in 1971 on <a href="/wiki/George_Street,_Sydney" title="George Street, Sydney">George Street</a>. The photographer <a href="/wiki/Roger_Scott_(photographer)" title="Roger Scott (photographer)">Roger Scott</a>, who captured the protest in front of the <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria_Building" title="Queen Victoria Building">Queen Victoria Building</a>, remarked: "I knew I could make a point with my camera. It was exciting. The old conservative world was ending and a new Australia was beginning. The demonstration was almost silent. The atmosphere was electric. The protesters were committed to making their presence felt … It was clear they wanted to show the government that they were mighty unhappy".<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Political upheaval made its way into art in the country: film, music and literature were shaped by the ongoing changes both within the country, the Southern Hemisphere and the rest of the world. Bands such as <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Master%E2%80%99s_Apprentices&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="The Master’s Apprentices (page does not exist)">The Master’s Apprentices</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Pink_Finks" title="The Pink Finks">The Pink Finks</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Normie_Rowe_%26_The_Playboys&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Normie Rowe &amp; The Playboys (page does not exist)">Normie Rowe &amp; The Playboys</a>, along with Sydney’s <a href="/wiki/The_Easybeats" title="The Easybeats">The Easybeats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Billy_Thorpe_%26_The_Aztecs" class="mw-redirect" title="Billy Thorpe &amp; The Aztecs">Billy Thorpe &amp; The Aztecs</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Missing_Links" title="The Missing Links">The Missing Links</a> began to emerge in the 1960s.
</p><p>One of Australia's most noted literary voices of the counter-culture movement was <a href="/wiki/Frank_Moorhouse" title="Frank Moorhouse">Frank Moorhouse</a>, whose collection of short stories, <i>Futility and Other Animals</i>, was first published in <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a> 1969.<sup id="cite_ref-Rickard_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rickard-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> Its "discontinuous narrative" was said to reflect the "ambience of the counter-culture".<sup id="cite_ref-Rickard_67-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rickard-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Helen_Garner" title="Helen Garner">Helen Garner</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Monkey_Grip_(novel)" title="Monkey Grip (novel)">Monkey Grip</a></i> (1977), released eight years later, is considered a classic example of the contemporary Australian novel, and captured the thriving countercultural movement in Melbourne's inner-city in the mid 1970s, specifically <a href="/wiki/Open_relationships" class="mw-redirect" title="Open relationships">open relationships</a> and recreational drug use.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PostColonial_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PostColonial-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> Years later, Garner revealed it was strongly autobiographical and based on her own diaries.<sup id="cite_ref-PostColonial_69-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PostColonial-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, from the 1960s, <a href="/wiki/Surf_culture" title="Surf culture">surf culture</a> took rise in Australia given the abundance of beaches in the country, and this was reflected in art, from bands such as <a href="/wiki/The_Atlantics" title="The Atlantics">The Atlantics</a> and novels like <i><a href="/wiki/Puberty_Blues_(novel)" title="Puberty Blues (novel)">Puberty Blues</a></i> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Puberty_Blues" title="Puberty Blues">film of the same name</a>.
</p><p>As delineations of gender and sexuality have been dismantled, counter-culture in contemporary Melbourne is heavily influenced by the LGBT club scene.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Britain">Great Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counterculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Great Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Starting in the late 1960s the <a href="/wiki/Counterculture_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Counterculture movement">counterculture movement</a> spread from the US like a wildfire.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>  Britain did not experience the intense social turmoil produced in America by the  <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a> and racial tensions. Nevertheless, British youth readily identified with their American counterparts' desire to cast off the older generation's social mores. The new music was a powerful weapon. In this case, it took the form of a wholesale revolt against the class system, which was now being questioned for the first time in the nation's history. Rock music, which had first been introduced from the US in the 1950s, became a key instrument in the social uprisings of the young generation and Britain soon became a groundswell of musical talent thanks to groups like <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">the Beatles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stones" class="mw-redirect" title="Rolling Stones">Rolling Stones</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Who" title="The Who">the Who</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a>, and more in coming years.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The antiwar movement in Britain closely collaborated with their American counterparts, supporting peasant insurgents in the Asian jungles.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>  The  "<a href="/wiki/Campaign_for_Nuclear_Disarmament" title="Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament">Ban the Bomb</a>" protests centered around opposition to <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weaponry" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear weaponry">nuclear weaponry</a>; the campaign gave birth to what  was to  become the <a href="/wiki/Peace_symbol" class="mw-redirect" title="Peace symbol">peace symbol</a> of the 1960s.
</p>
<h3><span id="Russia.2FSoviet_Union"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Russia/Soviet_Union">Russia/Soviet Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counterculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Russia/Soviet Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although not exactly equivalent to the English definition, the term <i>Контркультура</i> (<i>Kontrkul'tura</i>) became common in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> to define a 1990s <a href="/wiki/Cultural_movement" title="Cultural movement">cultural movement</a> that promoted acting outside of cultural conventions: the use of explicit language; graphical descriptions of sex, violence and illicit activities; and uncopyrighted use of "safe" characters involved in such activities.
</p><p>During the early 1970s, the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_government" class="mw-redirect" title="Soviet government">Soviet government</a> rigidly promoted optimism in Russian culture. Divorce and alcohol abuse were viewed as taboo by the media. However, Russian society grew weary of the gap between real life and the creative world,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and underground culture became "forbidden fruit". General satisfaction with the quality of existing works led to parody, such as how the <a href="/wiki/Russian_joke" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian joke">Russian anecdotal joke</a> tradition turned the setting of <i><a href="/wiki/War_and_Peace" title="War and Peace">War and Peace</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Leo_Tolstoy" title="Leo Tolstoy">Leo Tolstoy</a> into a grotesque world of sexual excess. Another well-known example is <a href="/wiki/Russian_humour#Black_humour" title="Russian humour">black humor</a> (mostly in the form of short poems) that dealt exclusively with funny deaths and/or other mishaps of small, innocent children.
</p><p>In the mid-1980s, the <a href="/wiki/Glasnost" title="Glasnost">Glasnost</a> policy permitted the production of less optimistic works. As a consequence, Russian cinema during the late 1980s and the early 1990s was <a href="/wiki/Action_movie" class="mw-redirect" title="Action movie">action movies</a> with explicit (but not necessarily graphic) scenes of ruthless violence and social dramas about <a href="/wiki/Drug_abuse" class="mw-redirect" title="Drug abuse">drug abuse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prostitution" title="Prostitution">prostitution</a> and failing relationships. Although Russian movies of the time would be <a href="/wiki/MPAA_rating_system" class="mw-redirect" title="MPAA rating system">rated "R"</a> in the United States due to violence, the use of explicit language was much milder than in American cinema.
</p><p>In the late 1990s, Russian counterculture became increasingly popular on the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>. Several websites appeared that posted user-created short stories dealing with sex, drugs and violence. The following features are considered the most popular topics in such works:
</p>
<ul><li>Wide use of explicit language;</li>
<li>Deliberate misspelling;</li>
<li>Descriptions of drug use and consequences of abuse;</li>
<li>Negative portrayals of alcohol use;</li>
<li>Sex and violence: nothing is a taboo – in general, violence is rarely advocated, while all types of sex are considered good;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Parody" title="Parody">Parody</a>: media advertising, classic movies, <a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">pop culture</a> and children's books are considered fair game;</li>
<li>Non-conformance; and</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Politically_incorrect" class="mw-redirect" title="Politically incorrect">Politically incorrect</a> topics, mostly <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xenophobia" title="Xenophobia">xenophobia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Homophobia" title="Homophobia">homophobia</a>.</li></ul>
<p>A notable aspect of counterculture at the time was the influence of contra-cultural developments on Russian pop culture. In addition to traditional Russian styles of music, such as songs with jail-related lyrics, new music styles with explicit language were developed.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counterculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the recent past, Dr. <a href="/wiki/Sebastian_Kappen" title="Sebastian Kappen">Sebastian Kappen</a>, an Indian theologian, has tried to redefine counterculture in the Asian context. In March 1990, at a seminar in Bangalore, he presented his countercultural perspectives (Chapter 4 in S. Kappen, <i>Tradition, modernity, counterculture: an Asian perspective</i>, Visthar, Bangalore, 1994). Dr. Kappen envisages counterculture as a new culture that has to negate the two opposing cultural phenomena in Asian countries:
</p>
<ol><li>invasion by Western <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalist</a> culture, and</li>
<li>the emergence of <a href="/wiki/Revivalism" class="mw-redirect" title="Revivalism">revivalist</a> movements.</li></ol>
<p>Kappen writes, "Were we to succumb to the first, we should be losing our identity; if to the second, ours would be a false, obsolete identity in a mental universe of dead symbols and delayed myths".
</p><p>The most important countercultural movement in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> had taken place in the state of <a href="/wiki/West_Bengal" title="West Bengal">West Bengal</a> during the 1960s by a group of poets and artists who called themselves <a href="/wiki/Hungry_Generation" class="mw-redirect" title="Hungry Generation">Hungryalists</a>.
</p>
