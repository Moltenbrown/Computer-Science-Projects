	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Kouros</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For other uses of "Kouros", see <a href="/wiki/Kouros_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Kouros (disambiguation)">Kouros (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kouros_anavissos.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Kouros_anavissos.jpg/200px-Kouros_anavissos.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="497" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Kouros_anavissos.jpg/300px-Kouros_anavissos.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Kouros_anavissos.jpg/400px-Kouros_anavissos.jpg 2x" data-file-width="612" data-file-height="1522" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kouros_anavissos.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Kroisos_Kouros" title="Kroisos Kouros">Kroisos Kouros</a>, c. 530 BC</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>kouros</b> (<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Greek language">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc">κοῦρος</span>, <small>pronounced&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Greek" title="Help:IPA/Greek">[kûːros]</a></span>, plural <b>kouroi</b>) is the modern term<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> given to free-standing ancient Greek sculptures that first appear in the <a href="/wiki/Archaic_period_in_Greece" class="mw-redirect" title="Archaic period in Greece">Archaic period in Greece</a> and represent nude male youths. In Ancient Greek <i>kouros</i> means "youth, boy, especially of noble rank". Although Kouroi have been found in many ancient Greek territories, they were especially prominent in Attica and Boiotia.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The term <i>kouros</i> was first proposed for what were previously thought to be depictions of Apollo by V. I. Leonardos in 1895 in relation to the youth from <a href="/wiki/Keratea" title="Keratea">Keratea</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and adopted by Henri Lechat as a generic term for the standing male figure in 1904.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Such statues are found across the Greek-speaking world; the preponderance of these were found in sanctuaries of Apollo with more than one hundred from the sanctuary of Apollo Ptoion, Boeotia, alone.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> These free-standing sculptures were typically <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a>, but the form is also rendered in limestone, wood, bronze, ivory and terracotta. They are typically life-sized, though early colossal examples are up to 3 meters tall. 
</p><p>The female sculptural counterpart of the <i>kouros</i> is the <i><a href="/wiki/Kore_(sculpture)" title="Kore (sculpture)">kore</a></i>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kouros&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Ancient Greek <i>kouros</i> means "youth, boy, especially of noble rank."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> When a pubescent was received into the body of grown men, as a grown <i>Kouros</i>, he could enter the initiation fest of the brotherhood (<span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">φρατρία</span>). <i>Apellaios</i> was the month of these rites, and <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a> (<i>Apellon</i>) was the "megistos  kouros" (the greatest Kouros).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Purpose">Purpose</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kouros&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Purpose">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ac.kleobisandbiton.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Ac.kleobisandbiton.jpg/200px-Ac.kleobisandbiton.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="272" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Ac.kleobisandbiton.jpg/300px-Ac.kleobisandbiton.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Ac.kleobisandbiton.jpg/400px-Ac.kleobisandbiton.jpg 2x" data-file-width="965" data-file-height="1310" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ac.kleobisandbiton.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Kleobis" class="mw-redirect" title="Kleobis">Kleobis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kleobis_and_Biton" title="Kleobis and Biton">Biton</a> (<a href="/wiki/Delphi" title="Delphi">Delphi</a>: <a href="/wiki/Delphi_Archaeological_Museum" title="Delphi Archaeological Museum">Archaeological Museum</a>)</div></div></div>
<p>The kouros type appears to have served several functions. It was previously thought that it was used only to represent the god <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a>, as attested by its depiction on a vase painting in the presence of supplicants.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This association with Apollo was supported by the description of the statue of the Pythian Apollo at Samos by Diodoros<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> as "Egyptian in style, with his arms hanging by his sides and his legs parted". However, not all kouroi are images of a deity; many have been discovered in cemeteries where they most likely served as commemorative tombstones of the deceased, also the type was used as a memorial for victors in the games (like trophies), kouroi were used as offerings to the gods, (Pausanias describes the statue of <a href="/wiki/Arrhichion" title="Arrhichion">Arrhichion</a>, an Olympic pankratiast, as in the kouros scheme),<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and some kouroi have been found in sanctuaries other than that of Apollo. Indeed, some kouroi placed in sanctuaries were not inscribed with the name of the god but with a mortal, for example the 'Delphi Twins' Kleobis and Biton were honoured for their piety with matching kouroi.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A direct influence between Egyptian sculptures (in particular the figure of <a href="/wiki/Horus" title="Horus">Horus</a>) and the kouros type has long been conjectured, not least because of trade and cultural relations that are known to have existed since the mid-seventh century <a href="/wiki/BCE" class="mw-redirect" title="BCE">BCE</a>. A 1978 study by Eleanor Guralnick applied <a href="/wiki/Photogrammetry" title="Photogrammetry">stereophotogrammetric</a> measurement and <a href="/wiki/Cluster_analysis" title="Cluster analysis">cluster analysis</a> to a number of Greek and Egyptian statues and found the correlation between the Second Canon of the 26th Dynasty and Greek kouroi to be widely distributed but not universal.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_and_evolution">Origin and evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kouros&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origin and evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Louvre_E5345.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Louvre_E5345.jpg/200px-Louvre_E5345.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="267" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Louvre_E5345.jpg/300px-Louvre_E5345.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Louvre_E5345.jpg/400px-Louvre_E5345.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="1600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Louvre_E5345.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Saite" class="mw-redirect" title="Saite">Saite</a> sculpture from the 26th dynasty similar in proportion and form to the early kouros type, <a href="/wiki/Louvre" title="Louvre">Louvre</a> E5345.</div></div></div>
<p>The problem of the evolution of the kouros type is inevitably linked to that of the overall development of monumental Archaic Greek sculpture. There are fundamentally two schools of thought on how those Daedalic forms, some of which we know of only from the literature (kolossos, bretas, andrias and xoanon), became the free-standing sculpture in the round of the 6th century; namely, that it was a response to the internal development of Greek types and religious needs<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> or a product of foreign influence. For an external cause for change possible sources of influence have been cited as Egypt, Anatolia and Syria, with the strongest case made for Egypt. It is known that the Greeks had longstanding trade relations with Egypt prior to the founding of the Greek entrepôt of <a href="/wiki/Naukratis" class="mw-redirect" title="Naukratis">Naukratis</a> in the mid-7th century<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> from where the Greeks could have learned Egyptian sculpting methods.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The work of Guralnick along with the previous studies by Erik Iversen and Kim Levin<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> have added considerably to the argument for an imitation by Greek sculptors of Egyptian sculpture. The system of proportion in the second Egyptian canon of the <a href="/wiki/Saite" class="mw-redirect" title="Saite">Saite</a> period consisted of a grid of twenty-one and one fourth parts, with twenty-one squares from the soles of the feet to a line drawn through the centres of the eyes. The grid was applied to the surface of the block being carved, allowing the major anatomical features to be located at fixed grid points. Iversen has shown that the New York kouros conforms to this ratio of proportion. It was Guralnick, however, who developed this discovery by comparing other kouroi by means of <a href="/wiki/Cluster_analysis" title="Cluster analysis">cluster</a> and <a href="/wiki/Standard_score" title="Standard score">Z-score</a> profile analysis to the Egyptian Canon II and a control group composed of statistically average Mediterranean men. As a result, she has identified two strains within methods of proportioning in sixth century kouroi, where the majority follow the general line of evolution  from the foreign model towards an idealized human norm.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Attributes_and_meaning">Attributes and meaning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kouros&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Attributes and meaning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Drawing_of_a_bronze_statue_from_the_Temple_of_Apollo_Patroos._AGMA.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Drawing_of_a_bronze_statue_from_the_Temple_of_Apollo_Patroos._AGMA.jpg/220px-Drawing_of_a_bronze_statue_from_the_Temple_of_Apollo_Patroos._AGMA.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="112" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Drawing_of_a_bronze_statue_from_the_Temple_of_Apollo_Patroos._AGMA.jpg/330px-Drawing_of_a_bronze_statue_from_the_Temple_of_Apollo_Patroos._AGMA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Drawing_of_a_bronze_statue_from_the_Temple_of_Apollo_Patroos._AGMA.jpg/440px-Drawing_of_a_bronze_statue_from_the_Temple_of_Apollo_Patroos._AGMA.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="2042" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Drawing_of_a_bronze_statue_from_the_Temple_of_Apollo_Patroos._AGMA.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Left: Drawing of a bronze statue from the <a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Apollo_Patroos" title="Temple of Apollo Patroos">Temple of Apollo Patroos</a> (6th century BC), showing the parts preserved in the <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> <a href="/wiki/Lost-wax_casting" title="Lost-wax casting">molds</a>. Right: Bronze-casting pit and <a href="/wiki/Furnace_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Furnace (disambiguation)">furnace</a>. Museum of the Ancient Agora of Athens.</div></div></div>
<p>Kouroi are beardless, take a formulaic advancing posture, and are most often nude.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Taking from the style of Egyptian figures, Greek kouroi often have their left leg extended forward as though walking; however, the figurine looks as though it could be either standing still or taking a long stride.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> A small number of early kouroi are belted around their waists, a practice that died out at the turn of the sixth century. Such belts have traditionally been assumed to be an abbreviated symbol of a more complex costume,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> however fully clothed contemporary figures also exist, suggesting that it was not just a sculptor's shorthand for clothing but a signifier in itself. Art historian <a href="/wiki/Brunilde_Sismondo_Ridgway" title="Brunilde Sismondo Ridgway">Brunilde Ridgway</a><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> suggests in her 1977 <i>The Archaic Style in Greek Sculpture</i> that this may have been an attribute of Apollo, athleticism or magical powers, though its iconography remains obscure. Further, there is the question of the nudity of the kouros and if this is also an attribute. Again this may have represented athletic or heroic nudity – immortalising the youth as he appeared in the <a href="/wiki/Palaestra" title="Palaestra">palaestra</a>, but no examples have been found at Olympia nor do they bear any allusion to athletic equipment.
</p><p>As well as being found in the sanctuaries of Apollo at Delphi, Delos and Mt. Ptoion, kouroi have been found dedicated at the sanctuaries of Hera at Samos, and of Athena and Poseidon at Sounion,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> so the contention that they depict Apollo is at the very least problematic. However, the majority are from Apollonian sites and dedicated to that god, which has led Ridgway to suggest that the early, belted form of the kouros-type statue was introduced in the late seventh century as a replacement for the colossal representation of Apollo.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Over time, the votive and funerary functions of the sculpture became divorced whilst its attributes were shed and its form became more generic until, in the late sixth century, it could serve a number of uses depending on context and location. This 'polyvalent' argument, initially put forth by historian Jean Ducat,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> was elaborated by art historian Andrew Stewart, who contends the distribution of kouroi coincides with city-states where the aristocracy were in ascendancy and that this alternation between the divine and the memorial was an identification of aristocratic <i><a href="/wiki/Arete_(moral_virtue)" class="mw-redirect" title="Arete (moral virtue)">arete</a></i> with the immortal.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The earliest extant examples may be the two life-sized marble figures from the Ionic sanctuary on the island of Delos<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> dating from the second or third quarter of the seventh century. The canonical form of the kouros persists until the beginning of the classical period, by which time artists had achieved a high degree of anatomical verisimilitude, if not naturalism,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> as can be observed on such transitional works as the <a href="/wiki/Kritios_Boy" title="Kritios Boy">Kritios Boy</a>, c. 480 BC.
</p><p>The absolute chronology of the kouros form is uncertain; none of the sculptures have secure dates. Further there is a strong homogeneity across the various regional schools: where anatomical innovations were adopted they seem to have spread quickly amongst the different workshops so that "regional distinctions become merged in a common progression".<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Consequently, the development of the kouros type as we now understand it is based on the relative chronology delineated by Gisela Richter. She distinguishes six groups by their common anatomical features, with particular reference to the major muscle groups as illustrated in the <a href="/wiki/%C3%89corch%C3%A9" title="Écorché">écorchés</a> to the right.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sounion_group">Sounion group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kouros&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sounion group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>C. 615–590 BC: the dates of this period are tentative, roughly late seventh-early sixth century, which Richter<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> infers from the duration of development necessary for the previous generations from the more securely dated Tenea-Volomandra group. Additionally she notes a similarity of sculpture from this time to <a href="/wiki/Black-figure_pottery" title="Black-figure pottery">early Athenian pottery</a>, particularly the <a href="/wiki/Nessos_Painter" title="Nessos Painter">Nessos amphora</a><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> and the human figures on the Horse amphorae.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> She also detects<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> a resemblance between the New York-Sounion kouroi and an early Corinthian pyxis<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> of the last quarter of the seventh century. Notable works of the time include the <a href="/wiki/New_York_kouros" class="mw-redirect" title="New York kouros">New York kouros</a> (Met 32.11.1), Dermys and Kittylos (<a href="/wiki/National_Archaeological_Museum_of_Athens" class="mw-redirect" title="National Archaeological Museum of Athens">NAMA</a> 56), <a href="/wiki/Delphi" title="Delphi">Delphi</a> Twins (Delphi Mus. 467 and 1524), the <a href="/wiki/Sounion_kouros" class="mw-redirect" title="Sounion kouros">Sounion kouros</a>, and the Delos colossus.
</p><p>The conception of form in this period is abstract and geometrical, emphasis is on architectural shape and the interrelation of parts which favoured expressive pattern over realism. Figures display the four faces of the block from which they are carved, their form is cubic with details incised, and their anatomy is only partially understood. Harmony and expressive pattern are the goal, and as such the proportions are abnormal. The torso is four-sided and flat, the back is higher than chest with the vertebral column expressed as a  straight line. The skull is undeveloped; flat at the back and often on top. The ear is carved in one plane, and highly stylized. <a href="/wiki/Tragus_(ear)" title="Tragus (ear)">Tragus</a> is knob like, either on cheek or lobe. <a href="/wiki/Antitragus" title="Antitragus">Antitragus</a> is not indicated. The eyes are large and flat, <a href="/wiki/Canthus_(anatomy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Canthus (anatomy)">canthus</a> is not marked, <a href="/wiki/Ciaccio%27s_glands" title="Ciaccio&#39;s glands">lachrymal caruncle</a> is not indicated. The mouth is horizontal, with lips on same plane, and corners of mouth forming triangular depressions. Hair is arranged in parallel beaded tresses, which rarely radiates from the <a href="/wiki/Vertex_(anatomy)" title="Vertex (anatomy)">vertex</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Sternocleidomastoid_muscle" title="Sternocleidomastoid muscle">Sterno-mastoids</a>, when marked, are indicated by grooves running to the <a href="/wiki/Suprasternal_notch" title="Suprasternal notch">sternal notch</a>. There is no indication of swelling of <a href="/wiki/Trapezius" title="Trapezius">trapezius</a> on the outline of shoulders. The <a href="/wiki/Clavicles" class="mw-redirect" title="Clavicles">clavicles</a> are flat ridges along whole course of shoulders. <a href="/wiki/Median_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Median line">Median line</a> is sometimes marked by a groove from sternal notch to navel. The lower boundary of the <a href="/wiki/Thorax" title="Thorax">thorax</a> has the shape of a pointed arch. <a href="/wiki/Rectus_abdominis" class="mw-redirect" title="Rectus abdominis">Rectus abdominis</a> is formed by three or more transverse divisions above navel. The navel is generally a knob in a circular groove. <a href="/wiki/Serratus_anterior_muscle" title="Serratus anterior muscle">Serratus magnus</a> is not indicated. The shoulder blades are outlined by grooves on the surface of back. The <a href="/wiki/Erector_spinae" class="mw-redirect" title="Erector spinae">erector spinae</a> attachment to posterior part of the <a href="/wiki/Iliac_crest" title="Iliac crest">iliac crest</a> is sometimes indicated by grooves in the <a href="/wiki/Lumbar" title="Lumbar">lumbar</a> regions. Forearm is <a href="/wiki/Supination" class="mw-redirect" title="Supination">supinated</a>, with palm towards the body. Arms often separated from body between armpit and hand. Thumbs are large. <a href="/wiki/Vastus_medialis" title="Vastus medialis">Vastus internus</a> descends to about the same level as <a href="/wiki/Vastus_lateralis_muscle" title="Vastus lateralis muscle">vastus externus</a>, the shin is vertical, and the <a href="/wiki/Malleoli" class="mw-redirect" title="Malleoli">malleoli</a> are level. Weight is evenly distributed on both legs and the flanks are level.
</p>

<h3><span id="Orchomenos.E2.80.93Thera_group"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Orchomenos–Thera_group">Orchomenos–Thera group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kouros&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Orchomenos–Thera group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>C. 590–570 BC: this period witnesses a lull in Attica with perhaps only two identifiable works from the beginning of the era until the second quarter of century (NAMA 3858 and 4181), this might be due to the Solonic reforms and their restriction on the extravagance of private funerals. Activity is more vigorous in Boeotia, especially those from the Ptoan sanctuary and the Orchomenos kouros (NAMA 9), early work there is probably native.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Also Corinth, Actium produces one of the best examples of the period (Louvre MNB 767), detailing still of in the form of grooves and ridges but there is the beginning of modeling in the full roundness of natural form. One of the more accomplished products of the time is the Thera kouros (NAMA 8), softer and less muscular in modeling it is more Ionian than Dorian though Thera was a Dorian colony. We may deduce the chronology of this period only if the dates for the Sounion and Volodmera groups are correct since there is no external evidence for the dates of this style; however, we can usefully compare the heads<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> on vase painting of middle Corinthian 600-575<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> which share the same stolid expression, flat skull, large eyes and horizontal mouth.
</p><p>The characteristics of this style are as follows. The ear is still carved in one plane, but less stylised. Eyes are not so large as before and more rounded. Mouth is horizontal but no longer always in one plane. The slight protrusions of flanks are sometimes prolonged into a girdle-like ridge, the sculptor occasionally marks the anterior spine of the crest. Shoulder blades are now separate raised planes. The erector spinae sometimes indicated as raised planes. Arms are generally joined to body. The depression over <a href="/wiki/Greater_trochanter" title="Greater trochanter">great trochanter</a> is generally omitted. Shin sometimes curves inwards. Left flank is occasionally placed slightly forward.
</p>

<h3><span id="Tenea.E2.80.93Volomandra_group"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Tenea–Volomandra_group">Tenea–Volomandra group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kouros&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Tenea–Volomandra group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>C. 575–550 BC: named after an Attic kouros found at Volomandra (NAMA 1906) and a Corinthian specimen from Tenea (Munich 168) this period marks the flowering of the Middle Archaic, and these kouroi are contemporary with such works as the Berlin Standing Kore, the <a href="/wiki/Moschophoros" title="Moschophoros">Moschophoros</a> and the Bluebeard Pediment. There is a tension observable in this group between the solid, architectonic quality of early styles and the expressive possibilities of a vigorous, fluid naturalism . The anatomical novelties of this time are as follows. The ear is carved in more than one plane. A roundness of the eye is indicated henceforth. Lips curve upwards and meet more or less at corners, the upper lip protrudes over lower. Construction of neck is generalized, sterno-mastoids when indicated are marked by slightly modelled shapes. On the median line a groove along sternum is generally replaced by modelled shapes and only the linea alba is marked by only a groove. The lower boundary of thorax assumes the shape of a somewhat rounded arch. There is a slight indication of the <a href="/wiki/External_oblique" class="mw-redirect" title="External oblique">external oblique</a> bulging over the <a href="/wiki/Iliac_crest" title="Iliac crest">iliac crest</a>. Shoulder blades are indicated as modelled shapes. The erector spinae is sometimes modelled. Size of thumb is normal. The <a href="/wiki/Vastus_medialis" title="Vastus medialis">vastus internus</a> descends lower than <a href="/wiki/Vastus_lateralis_muscle" title="Vastus lateralis muscle">vastus externus</a>. Shins curve inwards. The external malleolus is lower and further back than the internal one. The little toes slant inwards. The <a href="/wiki/Metatarsal" class="mw-redirect" title="Metatarsal">metatarsal</a> bones are lightly indicated.
</p><p>The absolute chronology of this period is provided by the dedication of Rhombos on the Moschophoros, which may belong to the same time as a decree referring to the Panathenaia of 566. The Moschophoros is stylistically similar to early in this group giving us an approximate upper limit of 570.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>  Additionally the terracotta kneeling boy<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> found in a well in the Agora and dated by its black-figure pottery sherd stratum to circa 550 shares the flat almond eyes, absence of the trapezium and pointed arch of the lower thorax that characterizes the late Tenea-Volomandra, furnishing us with a tentative lower boundary for the style.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Melos_group">Melos group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kouros&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Melos group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>C. 555–540 BC: figures of this period are simpler than before; their muscles are no longer separately accentuated. There is a tendency to flowing contour and a generalization of form. The tragus now sometimes assumes its natural form. The anterior part of the <a href="/wiki/Helix_(ear)" title="Helix (ear)">helix</a>, which is directed backwards (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Crus_helicis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Crus helicis (page does not exist)">crus helicis</a>), is often prominent, and joined with the upper end of tragus. The antitragus is sometimes tentatively indicated, though wrongly placed. The anterior triangle of the neck is now better understood. Navel generally modelled as a depression. Indication of external oblique bulging over iliac crest. The <a href="/wiki/Human_abdomen" class="mw-redirect" title="Human abdomen">lower boundary of abdomen</a> occasionally forms a deep curve. Forearm and arm sometimes correctly semi-<a href="/wiki/Pronated" class="mw-redirect" title="Pronated">pronated</a>; both directed towards body. Arms sometimes arched towards body below the armpit. Big toe projects a little further or same as second toe. Four smaller toes and toe nails curve gently downwards.
</p><p>"Astonishingly uniform"<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> the products of this period are found across the Greek world in large quantities. This group is named after the best preserved example of the era (NAMA 1558). The date of this group is conjectured on the basis that one generation would be required for the development of the Melos group style prior to the more securely dated Anavysos-Ptoon style. However Richter argues there may be some relationship to other contemporary Greek art works, namely: the figures on Late Corinthian pottery circa 550 BC. exhibit the same degree of naturalism,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> and the archaic column sculptures from the Temple of Artemis Ephesos, thought to have been supplied by Croesus of Lydia, share some anatomical features.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>  Of the important works that come done to us there is the colossal kouros from Megera (NAMA 13), a transitional early piece from Boeotia (Thebes 3) and an early Parian example (Louvre MND 888).
</p>

<h3><span id="Anavysos.E2.80.93Ptoon_12_group"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Anavysos–Ptoon_12_group">Anavysos–Ptoon 12 group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kouros&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Anavysos–Ptoon 12 group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>C. 540–520 BC: this is the era of the Peisistratos dynasty and marks the assumption of Athens as the centre of artistic activity in Greece. In this period of great development the anatomical proportions become normal, the forms modelled and the spine clearly S-shaped. The head is now spherical and well-developed. The tragus takes on its natural form, the antitragus is also indicated. Hair occasionally descends as far as nape of neck. The sterno-mastoids when marked are indicated by modelled shapes. Their attachment to <a href="/wiki/Human_sternum" class="mw-redirect" title="Human sternum">sternum</a> and clavicles is often not indicated, this results in a continuous hollow groove or run above the clavicle. There is an attempt to indicate the backward curve of clavicle. Groove along <a href="/wiki/Linea_alba_(abdomen)" title="Linea alba (abdomen)">linea alba</a> is sometimes continued below the navel. The lower boundary of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thorax_arch&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Thorax arch (page does not exist)">thorax arch</a> is indicated. In the flanks the swelling of the external part is well developed. Lower boundary of abdomen assumes shape of small semicircle or deep curve. The erector spinae always indicated as modelled shape. Generally hand and forearm is semi-pronated. Hands are no longer attached to body but joined by short supports. The <a href="/wiki/Metacarpal" class="mw-redirect" title="Metacarpal">metacarpal</a> bones are sometimes indicated. The bulge of the vastus internus increases. Toes are no longer parallel but do not recede along a continuous curve. Toes and nails point upwards. The articulation of joints is well rendered. Sometimes the flank of the advanced leg is placed forward and higher than receding leg.
</p><p>The characteristics of this group can be observed on the <a href="/wiki/Siphnian_Treasury" title="Siphnian Treasury">Siphnian Treasury</a> which is dated on external evidence before 525 BC,<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> therefore allowing time for the maturation of the style we can date the beginning of this group to, roughly, a generation prior.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> The earliest is perhaps the Munich kouros (Glyptothek 169) judging by the rendering of some of the muscles. Other significant Attic kouroi in this style are the Anavyssos (Base reads: "Stand and mourn Kroisos, first in line of battle and whom Ares [God of War] killed")(NAMA 3851), the akropolis torso (Akropolis 665, 596), and the Rayet head (Carlsberg Glyptothek 418). The island of Keos supplies us with one of the best examples of the time (NAMA 3686), notable for its advanced rendering of the back where the greatest protrusion of the back is level with that of the chest. Keos was likely under the cultural influence of Athens at this time and this kouros is comparable to and chronologically close to the Anavyssos kouros and akropolis head. From the Ptoan sanctuary in Boeotia we have the Ptoon 12 kouros (NAMA), "softer, less sturdy" suggests Richter<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> it is, she asserts, a native Boeotian product and not an Athenian import.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ptoon_20_group">Ptoon 20 group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kouros&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ptoon 20 group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>C. 520–485 BC: the last stage in the development of the kouros type is the period in which the Greek sculptor attained a full knowledge of human anatomy and used it to create a harmonious, proportionate whole. The features that now become expressed are as follows. The lachrymal caruncle is sometimes indicated. Lips curved upwards only in early examples, the upper lip protrudes markedly over the lower and lips are well shaped. Hair is generally short or rolled up behind, it radiates from a point near vertex and carved in wavy strands. The structure of neck is now correct. There is an indication of swelling of trapezium on the outline of shoulder, becoming more pronounced over time. Clavicles assume an s-shape and lose themselves in shoulders. The lower boundary of thorax assumes a semicircular arch. The rectus abdominis, now reduced in number to two, with the top one incorporated into lower boundary of thorax. There is a small raised plane caused by projection of <a href="/wiki/Xiphoid_process" title="Xiphoid process">xiphoid appendage</a> sometimes observable at lower end of sternum. Navel has fold of skin above in most examples. The lower boundary of abdomen assumes shape of semicircle, and the upper edge of torso with two concave curves becomes regular in form. Forearm and hand correctly pronated. Arms sometimes held free from body. Flanks; occasionally at first later regularly, flank and buttock of supporting leg rise in conformity with action.
</p><p>This period is framed by the stasis of the Peisistratid era and the beginning of Athenian democracy and the Persian war. The upper limit of this group may be fixed by the sculpture of the temple of Apollo, Delphi.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Architecturally earlier than the Hekatompedon of Athens the Delphi temple has a probable date of c.520, thus the kouroi of its pediment which betray the swelling trapezium and semicircular lower boundary of the abdomen can be associated with later examples of the group. Yet these same youths have a grooved, narrow lower boundary to the thorax and their flanks are level, suggesting that they are early specimens of the style. Richter names this group after the kouros Ptoon 20, NAMA 20, which is likely a Boeotian work dedicated by Pythias of <a href="/wiki/Akraiphia" class="mw-redirect" title="Akraiphia">Akraiphia</a> and Aischrion to Apollo of the silver bow.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> This along with the torso form Eutresis (Thebes 7) indicate a vigorous Boeotian school of sculpture which may have existed to serve the Ptoan sanctuary. Attic production is considerable up to c.500 BC after which it seems to peter out.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Important late kouroi from Athens include the Aristodikos kouros (Ptoon 20 group, NAMA 3938), an akropolis statuette (NAMA 6445) and the bronze Apollo from Piraeus.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kouros&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
