<p>A <b>father</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Male" title="Male">male</a> parent of a <a href="/wiki/Child" title="Child">child</a>. Besides the paternal bonds of a father to his children, the father may have a parental, legal, and social relationship with the child that carries with it certain rights and obligations. An <b>adoptive father</b> is a male who has become the child's parent through the legal process of <a href="/wiki/Adoption" title="Adoption">adoption</a>. A <b>biological father</b> is the male genetic contributor to the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Infant" title="Infant">infant</a>, through <a href="/wiki/Sexual_intercourse" title="Sexual intercourse">sexual intercourse</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sperm_donation" title="Sperm donation">sperm donation</a>. A biological father may have legal obligations to a child not raised by him, such as an obligation of monetary support. A <b><a href="/wiki/Putative_father" title="Putative father">putative father</a></b> is a man whose biological relationship to a child is alleged but has not been established. A <b><a href="/wiki/Stepfather" title="Stepfather">stepfather</a></b> is a male who is the husband of a child's mother and they may form a family unit, but who generally does not have the legal rights and responsibilities of a parent in relation to the child.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adjective</a> "paternal" refers to a father and comparatively to "maternal" for a <a href="/wiki/Mother" title="Mother">mother</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">verb</a> "to father" means to procreate or to sire a child from which also derives the noun "fathering". Biological fathers determine the sex of their child through a <a href="/wiki/Sperm" title="Sperm">sperm</a> cell which either contains an <a href="/wiki/X_chromosome" title="X chromosome">X chromosome</a> (female), or <a href="/wiki/Y_chromosome" title="Y chromosome">Y chromosome</a> (male).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Related <a href="/wiki/Term_of_endearment" title="Term of endearment">terms of endearment</a> are <i>dad</i> (<i>dada</i>, <i>daddy</i>), <i>papa</i>, <i>pappa</i>, papasita, (<i>pa</i>, <i>pap</i>) and <i>pop</i>. A male role model that children can look up to is sometimes referred to as a <i>father-figure</i>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paternal_rights">Paternal rights</span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Paternity_(law)" class="mw-redirect" title="Paternity (law)">paternity</a> rights of a father with regard to his children differ widely from country to country often reflecting the level of involvement and roles expected by that society.
</p>
<dl><dt>Paternity leave</dt></dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Parental_leave" title="Parental leave">Parental leave</a> is when a father takes time off to support his newly born or adopted baby.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Paid paternity leave first began in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> in 1976, and is paid in more than half of <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> countries.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In the case of male same-sex couples the law often makes no provision for either one or both fathers to take paternity leave.
</p>
<dl><dt>Child custody</dt></dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fathers%27_rights_movement" title="Fathers&#39; rights movement">Fathers' rights movements</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Fathers_4_Justice" title="Fathers 4 Justice">Fathers 4 Justice</a> argue that family courts are biased against fathers.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Child support</dt></dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Child_support" title="Child support">Child support</a> is an ongoing periodic payment made by one parent to the other; it is normally paid by the parent who does not have custody.
</p>
<dl><dt>Paternity fraud</dt></dl>
<p>An estimated 2% of British fathers experiences <a href="/wiki/Paternity_fraud" title="Paternity fraud">paternity fraud</a> during a <a href="/wiki/Non-paternity_event" title="Non-paternity event">non-paternity event</a>, bringing up a child they wrongly believe to be their biological <a href="/wiki/Offspring" title="Offspring">offspring</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_of_the_father">Role of the father</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Father_and_child,_Dhaka.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Father_and_child%2C_Dhaka.jpg/220px-Father_and_child%2C_Dhaka.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Father_and_child%2C_Dhaka.jpg/330px-Father_and_child%2C_Dhaka.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Father_and_child%2C_Dhaka.jpg/440px-Father_and_child%2C_Dhaka.jpg 2x" data-file-width="720" data-file-height="1080" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Father_and_child,_Dhaka.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Father and child, Dhaka, Bangladesh</div></div></div>
<p>In almost all cultures fathers are regarded as secondary caregivers. This perception is slowly changing with more and more fathers becoming primary caregivers, while mothers go to work or in single parenting situations, male same-sex parenting couples.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fatherhood_in_the_Western_World">Fatherhood in the Western World</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aiden-Seth-Haley-Singleton.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Aiden-Seth-Haley-Singleton.jpg/220px-Aiden-Seth-Haley-Singleton.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Aiden-Seth-Haley-Singleton.jpg/330px-Aiden-Seth-Haley-Singleton.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Aiden-Seth-Haley-Singleton.jpg/440px-Aiden-Seth-Haley-Singleton.jpg 2x" data-file-width="960" data-file-height="720" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aiden-Seth-Haley-Singleton.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A father and his children in Florida</div></div></div>
<p>In the West, the image of the married father as the primary wage-earner is changing. The social context of fatherhood plays an important part in the well-being of men and all their children.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> 16% of single parents were men as of 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Importance_of_father_or_father-figure">Importance of father or father-figure</span></h3>
<p>Involved fathers offer developmentally specific provisions to their children and are impacted themselves by doing so. Active father figures may play a role in reducing behavior and psychological problems in young adults.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> An increased amount of father–child involvement may help increase a child's social stability, educational achievement,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>5</span></sup> and their potential to have a solid marriage as an adult. Their children may also be more curious about the world around them and develop greater problem solving skills.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  Children who were raised with fathers perceive themselves to be more cognitively and physically competent than their peers without a father.<sup id="cite_ref-Fatherless_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fatherless-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  Mothers raising children together with a father reported less severe disputes with their child.<sup id="cite_ref-Fatherless_Followup_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fatherless_Followup-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The father-figure is not always a child's biological father and some children will have a biological father as well as a step- or nurturing father. When a child is conceived through sperm donation, the donor will be the "biological father" of the child.
</p><p>Fatherhood as legitimate identity can be dependent on domestic factors and behaviors. For example, a study of the relationship between fathers, their sons, and home computers found that the construction of fatherhood and masculinity required that fathers display computer expertise.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Determination_of_parenthood">Determination of parenthood</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rosenzweig_Paternal_love.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Rosenzweig_Paternal_love.jpg/200px-Rosenzweig_Paternal_love.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="151" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Rosenzweig_Paternal_love.jpg/300px-Rosenzweig_Paternal_love.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Rosenzweig_Paternal_love.jpg/400px-Rosenzweig_Paternal_love.jpg 2x" data-file-width="958" data-file-height="723" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rosenzweig_Paternal_love.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Paternal love</i> (1803) by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanette_Rosenzweig&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nanette Rosenzweig (page does not exist)">Nanette Rosenzweig</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_Museum,_Warsaw" title="National Museum, Warsaw">National Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">Roman law</a> defined fatherhood as "Mater semper certa; pater est quem nuptiae demonstrant" ("The [identity of the] mother is always certain; the father is whom the marriage vows indicate"). The recent emergence of accurate scientific testing, particularly <a href="/wiki/DNA_testing" class="mw-redirect" title="DNA testing">DNA testing</a>, has resulted in the <a href="/wiki/Family_law" title="Family law">family law</a> relating to fatherhood experiencing rapid changes.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_fatherhood">History of fatherhood</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BritaAndI_Selfportrait.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/BritaAndI_Selfportrait.jpg/220px-BritaAndI_Selfportrait.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="533" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/BritaAndI_Selfportrait.jpg/330px-BritaAndI_Selfportrait.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/BritaAndI_Selfportrait.jpg/440px-BritaAndI_Selfportrait.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1651" data-file-height="4000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BritaAndI_Selfportrait.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Painter <a href="/wiki/Carl_Larsson" title="Carl Larsson">Carl Larsson</a> playing with his laughing <a href="/wiki/Daughter" title="Daughter">daughter</a> Brita</div></div></div>
<p>In medieval and most of modern European history, caring for children was predominantly the domain of mothers, whereas fathers in many societies provide for the family as a whole. Since the 1950s, social scientists and feminists have increasingly challenged gender roles in Western countries, including that of the male breadwinner. Policies are increasingly targeting fatherhood as a tool of changing gender relations.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Patricide">Patricide</span></h2>
<p>In early human history there have been notable instances of <a href="/wiki/Patricide" class="mw-redirect" title="Patricide">patricide</a>. For example:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Tukulti-Ninurta_I" title="Tukulti-Ninurta I">Tukulti-Ninurta I</a> (r. 1243–1207 B.C.E.), <a href="/wiki/Assyria" title="Assyria">Assyrian</a> king, was killed by his own son after sacking <a href="/wiki/Babylon" title="Babylon">Babylon</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sennacherib" title="Sennacherib">Sennacherib</a> (r. 704–681 B.C.E.), <a href="/wiki/Assyria" title="Assyria">Assyrian</a> king, was killed by two of his sons for his desecration of <a href="/wiki/Babylon" title="Babylon">Babylon</a>.</li>
<li>King <a href="/wiki/Kassapa_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Kassapa I">Kassapa I</a> (473 to 495 CE) creator of the Sigiriya citadel of ancient Sri Lanka killed his father king Dhatusena for the throne.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Emperor_Yang_of_Sui#Did_Emperor_Yang_kill_Emperor_Wen.3F" title="Emperor Yang of Sui">Emperor Yang of Sui</a> in Chinese history allegedly killed his father, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Wen_of_Sui" title="Emperor Wen of Sui">Emperor Wen of Sui</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Beatrice_Cenci" title="Beatrice Cenci">Beatrice Cenci</a>, Italian noblewoman who, according to legend, killed her father after he imprisoned and raped her. She was condemned and beheaded for the crime along with her brother and her stepmother in 1599.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lizzie_Borden" title="Lizzie Borden">Lizzie Borden</a> (1860–1927) allegedly killed her father and her stepmother with an axe in Fall River, Massachusetts, in 1892. She was acquitted, but her innocence is still disputed.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Iyasus_I_of_Ethiopia" class="mw-redirect" title="Iyasus I of Ethiopia">Iyasus I of Ethiopia</a> (1682–1706), one of the great warrior emperors of Ethiopia, was deposed by his son <a href="/wiki/Tekle_Haymanot_I_of_Ethiopia" class="mw-redirect" title="Tekle Haymanot I of Ethiopia">Tekle Haymanot</a> in 1706 and subsequently assassinated.</li></ul>
<p>In more contemporary history there have also been instances of father–offspring conflicts, such as:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Chiyo_Aizawa" class="mw-redirect" title="Chiyo Aizawa">Chiyo Aizawa</a> murdered her own father who had been <a href="/wiki/Rape" title="Rape">raping</a> her for fifteen years, on October 5, 1968, in Japan. The incident changed the <a href="/wiki/Criminal_Code_of_Japan" class="mw-redirect" title="Criminal Code of Japan">Criminal Code of Japan</a> regarding patricide.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kip_Kinkel" class="mw-redirect" title="Kip Kinkel">Kip Kinkel</a> (1982- ), an <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a> boy who was convicted of killing his parents at home and two fellow students at school on May 20, 1998.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sarah_Marie_Johnson" class="mw-redirect" title="Sarah Marie Johnson">Sarah Marie Johnson</a> (1987- ), an <a href="/wiki/Idaho" title="Idaho">Idaho</a> girl who was convicted of killing both parents on the morning of September 2, 2003.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dipendra_of_Nepal" title="Dipendra of Nepal">Dipendra of Nepal</a> (1971–2001) reportedly massacred much of his family at a royal dinner on June 1, 2001, including his father <a href="/wiki/King_Birendra" class="mw-redirect" title="King Birendra">King Birendra</a>, mother, brother, and sister.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Christopher_Porco" title="Christopher Porco">Christopher Porco</a> (1983- ), was convicted on August 10, 2006, of the murder of his father and attempted murder of his mother with an axe.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_fathers">Biological fathers</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Paternal_bonding_between_father_and_newborn_daughter.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Father holding daughter in swaddling clothes" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Paternal_bonding_between_father_and_newborn_daughter.jpg/280px-Paternal_bonding_between_father_and_newborn_daughter.jpg" decoding="async" width="280" height="186" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Paternal_bonding_between_father_and_newborn_daughter.jpg/420px-Paternal_bonding_between_father_and_newborn_daughter.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Paternal_bonding_between_father_and_newborn_daughter.jpg/560px-Paternal_bonding_between_father_and_newborn_daughter.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4256" data-file-height="2832" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Paternal_bonding_between_father_and_newborn_daughter.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Paternal_bond" title="Paternal bond">Paternal bonding</a> between a father and his newborn daughter</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Father_and_son_27.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Father_and_son_27.jpg/280px-Father_and_son_27.jpg" decoding="async" width="280" height="186" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Father_and_son_27.jpg/420px-Father_and_son_27.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Father_and_son_27.jpg/560px-Father_and_son_27.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3008" data-file-height="2000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Father_and_son_27.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Father and son</div></div></div>
<ul><li><b>Baby Daddy</b>&#160;&#8211;&#32;A biological father who bears financial responsibility for a child, but with whom the mother has little or no contact.</li>
<li><b>Birth father</b>&#160;&#8211;&#32;the biological father of a child who, due to adoption or parental separation, does not raise the child or cannot take care of one.</li>
<li><b>Biological father</b>&#160;&#8211;&#32;or just "Father" is the genetic father of a child</li>
<li><b>Posthumous father</b>&#160;&#8211;&#32;father died before children were born (or even conceived in the case of artificial insemination)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Putative_father" title="Putative father">Putative father</a></b>&#160;&#8211;&#32;unwed man whose legal relationship to a child has not been established but who is alleged to be or claims that he may be the biological father of a child</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Sperm_donor" class="mw-redirect" title="Sperm donor">Sperm donor</a></b>&#160;&#8211;&#32;an anonymous or known biological father who provides his sperm to be used in <a href="/wiki/Artificial_insemination" title="Artificial insemination">artificial insemination</a> or <a href="/wiki/In_vitro_fertilisation" title="In vitro fertilisation">in vitro fertilisation</a> in order to father a child for a third party female. Also used as a slang term meaning "baby daddy".</li>
<li><b>Surprise father</b>&#160;&#8211;&#32;where the men did not know that there was a child until possibly years afterward</li>
<li><b>Teenage father/youthful father</b>&#160;&#8211;&#32; Father who is still a teenager.</li></ul>
<h3><span id="Non-biological_.28social_and_legal_relationship.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-biological_(social_and_legal_relationship)">Non-biological (social and legal relationship)</span></h3>
<ul><li><b>Adoptive father</b>&#160;&#8211;&#32;the father who has <a href="/wiki/Adopted" class="mw-redirect" title="Adopted">adopted</a> a child</li>
<li><b>Cuckolded father</b>&#160;&#8211;&#32;where the child is the product of the mother's adulterous relationship</li>
<li><b>DI Dad</b>&#160;&#8211;&#32;social/legal father of children produced via Donor Insemination (where a donor's sperm were used to impregnate the DI Dad's spouse)</li>
<li><b>Father-in-law</b>&#160;&#8211;&#32;the father of one's spouse</li>
<li><b>Foster father</b>&#160;&#8211;&#32;child is raised by a man who is not the biological or adoptive father usually as part of a couple.</li>
<li><b>Mother's partner</b>&#160;&#8211;&#32;assumption that current partner fills father role</li>
<li><b>Mother's husband</b>&#160;&#8211;&#32;under some jurisdictions (e.g. in <a href="/wiki/Civil_Code_of_Quebec" title="Civil Code of Quebec">Quebec civil law</a>), if the mother is married to another man, the latter will be defined as the father</li>
<li><b>Presumed father</b>&#160;&#8211;&#32;Where a <a href="/wiki/Presumption_of_paternity" title="Presumption of paternity">presumption of paternity</a> has determined that a man is a child's father regardless of if he actually is or is not the biological father</li>
<li><b>Social father</b>&#160;&#8211;&#32;where a man takes <i><a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">de facto</a></i> responsibility for a child, such as caring for one who has been abandoned or orphaned (the child is known as a "child of the family" in English law)</li>
<li><b>Stepfather</b>&#160;&#8211;&#32;a married non-biological father where the child is from a previous relationship</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fatherhood_defined_by_contact_level">Fatherhood defined by contact level</span></h3>
<ul><li><b>Absent father</b>&#160;&#8211;&#32;father who cannot or will not spend time with his child(ren)</li>
<li><b>Second father</b>&#160;&#8211;&#32;a non-parent whose contact and support is robust enough that near parental bond occurs (often used for older male siblings who significantly aid in raising a child, sometimes for older men who took care of younger friends who have no families)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Stay-at-home_dad" title="Stay-at-home dad">Stay-at-home dad</a></b>&#160;&#8211;&#32;the male equivalent of a <a href="/wiki/Housewife" title="Housewife">housewife</a> with child, where his spouse is breadwinner</li>
<li><b>Weekend/holiday father</b>&#160;&#8211;&#32;where child(ren) only stay(s) with father on weekends, holidays, etc.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-human_fatherhood">Non-human fatherhood</span></h2>
<p>For some animals, it is the fathers who take care of the young.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Darwin%27s_frog" title="Darwin&#39;s frog">Darwin's frog</a> (<i>Rhinoderma darwini</i>) fathers carry eggs in the vocal pouch.</li>
<li>Most male <a href="/wiki/Waterfowl" class="mw-redirect" title="Waterfowl">waterfowls</a> are very protective in raising their offspring, sharing scout duties with the female. Examples are the <a href="/wiki/Geese" class="mw-redirect" title="Geese">geese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swan" title="Swan">swans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Loon" title="Loon">loons</a>, and a few species of <a href="/wiki/Duck" title="Duck">ducks</a>. When the families of most of these waterfowls travel, they usually travel in a line and the fathers are usually the ones guarding the offspring at the end of the line while the mothers lead the way.</li>
<li>The female <a href="/wiki/Seahorse" title="Seahorse">seahorse</a> (<i>hippocampus</i>) deposits eggs into the pouch on the male's abdomen. The male releases sperm into the pouch, fertilizing the eggs. The embryos develop within the male's pouch, nourished by their individual yolk sacs.</li>
<li>Male <a href="/wiki/Catfish" title="Catfish">catfish</a> keep their eggs in their mouth, foregoing eating until they hatch.</li>
<li>Male <a href="/wiki/Emperor_penguin" title="Emperor penguin">emperor penguins</a> alone incubate their <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a>; females do no incubation. Rather than building a <a href="/wiki/Nest" title="Nest">nest</a>, each male protects his egg by balancing it on the tops of his feet, enclosed in a special brood pouch. Once the eggs are hatched however, the females will rejoin the family.</li>
<li>Male <a href="/wiki/Beaver" title="Beaver">beavers</a> secure their offspring along with the females during their first few hours of their lives. As the young beavers mature, their fathers will teach them how to search for materials to build and repair their own dams, before they disperse to find their own mates.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wolf" title="Wolf">Wolf</a> fathers help feed, protect, and play with their pups. In some cases, several generations of wolves live in the pack, giving pups the care of grandparents, aunts/uncles, and siblings, in addition to parents. The father wolf is also the one who does most of the hunting when the females are securing their newborn pups.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Coyote" title="Coyote">Coyotes</a> are monogamous and male coyotes hunt and bring food to their young.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dolphin" title="Dolphin">Dolphin</a> fathers help in the care of the young. Newborns are held on the surface of the water by both parents until they are ready to swim on their own.</li>
<li>A number of <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> species have active, caring fathers who assist the mothers, such as the waterfowls mentioned above.</li>
<li>Apart from <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a>, fathers in few <a href="/wiki/Primate" title="Primate">primate</a> species care for their young. Those that do are <a href="/wiki/Tamarin" title="Tamarin">tamarins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marmoset" title="Marmoset">marmosets</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Fernandez-Duque_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fernandez-Duque-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Particularly strong care is also shown by <a href="/wiki/Siamang" title="Siamang">siamangs</a> where fathers carry infants after their second year.<sup id="cite_ref-Fernandez-Duque_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fernandez-Duque-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Titi_monkey" class="mw-redirect" title="Titi monkey">titi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Owl_monkey" class="mw-redirect" title="Owl monkey">owl monkeys</a> fathers carry their infants 90% of the time with "titi monkey infants developing a preference for their fathers over their mothers".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Silverback_gorilla" class="mw-redirect" title="Silverback gorilla">Silverback gorillas</a> have less role in the families but most of them serve as an extra protecting the families from harm and sometimes approaching enemies to distract them so that his family can escape unnoticed.</li></ul>
<p>Many species,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> though, display little or no paternal role in caring for offspring. The male leaves the female soon after mating and long before any offspring are born. It is the females who must do all the work of caring for the young.
</p>
<ul><li>A male <a href="/wiki/Bear" title="Bear">bear</a> leaves the female shortly after mating and will kill and sometimes eat any bear cub he comes across, even if the cub is his. Bear mothers spend much of their cubs' early life protecting them from males. (Many artistic works, such as <a href="/wiki/Advertisement" class="mw-redirect" title="Advertisement">advertisements</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cartoon" title="Cartoon">cartoons</a>, depict kindly "papa bears" when this is the exact opposite of reality.)</li>
<li>Domesticated <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dog</a> fathers show little interest in their offspring, and unlike wolves, are not monogamous with their mates and are thus likely to leave them after mating.</li>
<li>Male <a href="/wiki/Lion" title="Lion">lions</a> will tolerate cubs, but only allow them to eat meat from dead prey after they have had their fill. A few are quite cruel towards their young and may hurt or kill them with little provocation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> A male who kills another male to take control of his pride will also usually kill any cubs belonging to that competing male. However, it is also the males who are responsible for guarding the pride while the females hunt. However the male lions are the only felines that actually have a role in fatherhood.</li>
<li>Male rabbits generally tolerate kits but unlike the females, they often show little interest in the kits and are known to play rough with their offspring when they are mature, especially towards their sons. This behaviour may also be part of an instinct to drive the young males away to prevent incest matings between the siblings. The females will eventually disperse from the warren as soon as they mature but the father does not drive them off like he normally does to the males.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">Horse</a> stallions and <a href="/wiki/Domestic_pig" title="Domestic pig">pig</a> boars have little to no role in parenting, nor are they monogamous with their mates. They will tolerate young to a certain extent, but due to their aggressive male nature, they are generally annoyed by the energetic exuberance of the young, and may hurt or even kill the young. Thus, stud stallions and boars are not kept in the same pen as their young or other females.</li></ul>
<p>Finally, in some species neither the father nor the mother provides any care.
</p>
<ul><li>This is true for most <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>.</li></ul>
