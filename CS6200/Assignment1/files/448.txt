<p>A <b>son</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Male" title="Male">male</a> <a href="/wiki/Reproduction" title="Reproduction">offspring</a>; a <a href="/wiki/Boy" title="Boy">boy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Man" title="Man">man</a> in relation to his <a href="/wiki/Parent" title="Parent">parents</a>. The female counterpart is a <a href="/wiki/Daughter" title="Daughter">daughter</a>.  From a biological perspective, a son constitutes a <a href="/wiki/First_degree_relative" class="mw-redirect" title="First degree relative">first degree relative</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_issues_regarding_sons">Social issues regarding sons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Son&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Social issues regarding sons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In pre-industrial societies and some current countries with agriculture-based economies, a higher value was, and still is, assigned to sons rather than daughters, giving males higher <a href="/wiki/Social_status" title="Social status">social status</a>, because males were <a href="/wiki/Physical_strength" title="Physical strength">physically stronger</a>, and could perform farming tasks more effectively. For Example - Siddarth Nandi is Sonu's son.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, a <a href="/wiki/One-child_Policy" class="mw-redirect" title="One-child Policy">One-child Policy</a> was in effect until 2015 in order to address rapid <a href="/wiki/Population_growth" title="Population growth">population growth</a>. Official birth records showed a rise in the level of male births since the policy was brought into <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a>. This was attributed to a number of factors, including the illegal practice of <a href="/wiki/Sex-selective_abortion" title="Sex-selective abortion">sex-selective abortion</a> and widespread under-reporting of female births.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Patrilineality" title="Patrilineality">patrilineal</a> societies, sons will customarily <a href="/wiki/Inheritance" title="Inheritance">inherit</a> an estate before daughters.<sup id="cite_ref-PeoplesBailey2011_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PeoplesBailey2011-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In some cultures, the eldest son has special privileges. For example, in Biblical times, the first-born male was bequeathed the most goods from their father.  Some Japanese social norms involving the eldest son are: "that parents are more likely to live with their eldest child if their eldest child is a son" and "that parents are most likely to live with their eldest son even if he is not the eldest child".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
Example: A male Leopon is a female lion's son and Emmanuel is the son of Helene from Cameroon
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Specialized_use_of_the_term_son">Specialized use of the term <i>son</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Son&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Specialized use of the term son">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Christian_symbolism">Christian symbolism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Son&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Christian symbolism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Folio_109v_-_The_Baptism_of_Christ.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Folio_109v_-_The_Baptism_of_Christ.jpg/220px-Folio_109v_-_The_Baptism_of_Christ.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Folio_109v_-_The_Baptism_of_Christ.jpg/330px-Folio_109v_-_The_Baptism_of_Christ.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Folio_109v_-_The_Baptism_of_Christ.jpg/440px-Folio_109v_-_The_Baptism_of_Christ.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="2133" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Folio_109v_-_The_Baptism_of_Christ.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Miniature in <i>Les Très Riches Heures du Duc de Berry</i> depicting the <a href="/wiki/Baptism_of_Jesus" title="Baptism of Jesus">Baptism of Jesus</a>, where <a href="/wiki/God_the_Father" title="God the Father">God the Father</a> proclaimed <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> to be his Son.</div></div></div>
<p>Among <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christians</a>, "the Son" or <a href="/wiki/Son_of_God" title="Son of God">Son of God</a> refers to <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus Christ</a>. Trinitarian Christians view Jesus as the human incarnation of <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a> the second person of the <a href="/wiki/Trinity" title="Trinity">Trinity</a>, known as <a href="/wiki/God_the_Son" title="God the Son">God the Son</a>. In the Gospels, <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> sometimes refers to himself as the <a href="/wiki/Son_of_Man" class="mw-redirect" title="Son of Man">Son of Man</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Semitic_names">In Semitic names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Son&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In Semitic names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a> word for son is <i>ibn.</i> Because <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">family</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancestry" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancestry">ancestry</a> are important <a href="/wiki/Cultural" class="mw-redirect" title="Cultural">cultural</a> <a href="/wiki/Value_(personal_and_cultural)" class="mw-redirect" title="Value (personal and cultural)">values</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Arab_world" title="Arab world">Arab world</a> and <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arab" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab">Arabs</a> and most <a href="/wiki/Muslim" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim">Muslims</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Brunei" title="Brunei">Bruneian</a>) often use <i>bin,</i> which is a form of <i>ibn,</i> in their full names. The <i>bin</i> here means "son of." For example, the Arab name "Saleh bin Tarif bin Khaled Al-Fulani" translates as "Saleh, son of Tarif, son of Khaled; of the family Al-Fulani" (cf. <a href="/wiki/Arabic_name#Arab_family_naming_convention" title="Arabic name">Arab family naming conventions</a>). Accordingly, the opposite of <i>ibn</i>/<i>bin</i> is <i>abu,</i> meaning "the father of." It is a <a href="/wiki/Retronym" title="Retronym">retronym</a>, given upon the birth of one's first-born son, and is used as a <a href="/wiki/Moniker" class="mw-redirect" title="Moniker">moniker</a> to indicate the newly acquired fatherhood status, rather than a family name. For example, if Mahmoud's first-born son is named Abdullah, from that point on Mahmoud can be called "Abu Abdullah."
</p><p>This is <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">cognate</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew language</a> <i>ben,</i> as in "Judah ben Abram HaLevi," which means "Judah, son of Abram, the Levite." Ben is also a standalone name.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indications_in_names">Indications in names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Son&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Indications in names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">Patronymic</a></div>
<p>In many cultures, the <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">surname</a> of the family means "son of", indicating a possible ancestry—i.e., that the whole family descends from a common ancestor. It may vary between the beginning or the termination of the surname.
</p>

<ul><li><i><b>bin</b></i> or <i><b>ibn</b></i>. Example: "Ibn Sina" ("son of Sina"), "Ibn Khaldun" ("son of Khaldun"), etc.</li></ul>

<li><i><b>N ayt</b></i> or <i><b>Nayt</b></i> (often misspelled as: <i><b>nait</b></i> or <i><b>naït</b></i>). Examples: "N ayt Ndir" ("son of the Ndir tribe/family"), "Naït Zerrad" ("son of the Zerrad tribe or family").</li></ul>

<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">Romanian</a></dt></dl>
<ul><li><i><b>a</b></i> as prefix (except for female names that start in a and probably for others that start in vowels) &amp; <i><b>ei</b></i> as suffix. Example: "Amariei" ("son of Mary"), "Adomnitei" ("son of Domnita"), "Alenei" ("son of Elena/Leana"), etc.</li>
<li><i><b>escu</b></i> or sometimes <i><b>aşcu</b></i> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i><b>-iscus</b></i> which means "belonging to the people". Example: "Petrescu" ("Petre's son"), "Popescu" ("Popa's son" Popa meaning Priest), "Constantinescu" ("son of Constantin"), etc.</li></ul>
<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a></dt></dl>
<ul><li><i><b>ov</b></i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ɒf/</span>, <i><b>ovich</b></i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/əvɪtʃ/</span>. Example: "Ivanov" ("son of Ivan"), etc.</li>
<li><i><b>ev</b></i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ɛf/</span>, <i><b>evich</b></i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ɨvɪtʃ/</span>. Example: "Dmitriev" ("son of Dmitri"), etc.</li></ul>
<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a></dt></dl>
<ul><li><i><b>Ez</b></i>. Example: "González" ("son of Gonzalo"), "Henríquez" ("son of Henrique"), "Fernández" ("son of Fernando"), Gómez ("son of Gome"), Sánchez ("son of Sancho"), etc.</li></ul>
<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a></dt></dl>
<ul><li><i><b>oğlu</b></i>. Examples: "Elbeyioğlu" ("son of foreigner <a href="/wiki/Bey" title="Bey">Bey</a>"), "Ağaoğlu" ("son of <a href="/wiki/A%C4%9Fa" title="Ağa">Ağa</a>"), "Yusufoğlu" ("son of <a href="/wiki/Yusuf" title="Yusuf">Yusuf</a>"), etc.</li>
<li><i><b>zade</b></i>. Examples: "<a href="/wiki/Begzada" title="Begzada">Beyzade</a>" (son of a <a href="/wiki/Bey" title="Bey">Bey</a>), "<a href="/wiki/A%C5%9F%C4%B1kpa%C5%9Fazade" title="Aşıkpaşazade">Aşıkpaşazade</a>" ("son of <a href="/wiki/Ashik" title="Ashik">Ashik</a> <a href="/wiki/Pa%C5%9Fa" class="mw-redirect" title="Paşa">Paşa</a>), "Mehmedzade" (son of <a href="/wiki/Mehmet" class="mw-redirect" title="Mehmet">Mehmet</a>), etc.</li></ul>
<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">Ukrainian</a></dt></dl>
<ul><li><i><b>-enko</b></i> or <i><b>-ko</b></i>, meaning simply "son of". Example: "<a href="/wiki/Kovalenko" title="Kovalenko">Kovalenko</a>" ("son of Koval")</li>
<li><i><b>sky</b></i> . Example: "Stanislavsky" ("son of Stanislav"), "Chaykovsky" ("son of Chayko"), "Petrovsky" ("son of Petro").</li>
<li><i><b>shyn</b></i>. Example: "Petryshyn" ("son of Petro"), "Danylyshyn" ("son of Danylo"), etc.</li>
<li><i><b>chuk</b></i>. Example: "Ivanchuk" ("son of Ivan").</li></ul>
<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a></dt></dl>
<ul><li><i><b>ap</b></i> or <i><b>ab</b></i>. Example: "ap Rhys" ("son of Rhys", <a href="/wiki/Anglicization" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglicization">anglicized</a> to "<a href="/wiki/Price_(surname)" title="Price (surname)">Price</a>"), "ab Owain" ("son of Owen", anglicized to <a href="/wiki/Bowen_(surname)" title="Bowen (surname)">Bowen</a>) etc.</li></ul>
