<p><b>Benjamin</b> (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span class="nowrap"><span lang="he">בנימין</span></span>, <i> "Son of the right side"</i>) was the last-born of <a href="/wiki/Jacob" title="Jacob">Jacob</a>'s thirteen children (12 sons and 1 daughter), and the second and last son of <a href="/wiki/Rachel" title="Rachel">Rachel</a> in Jewish, Christian and Islamic tradition. He was the progenitor of the <a href="/wiki/Israelites" title="Israelites">Israelite</a> <a href="/wiki/Tribe_of_Benjamin" title="Tribe of Benjamin">Tribe of Benjamin</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a> unlike Rachel's first son, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_(Genesis)" title="Joseph (Genesis)">Joseph</a>, Benjamin was born in <a href="/wiki/Canaan" title="Canaan">Canaan</a>.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Samaritan_Pentateuch" title="Samaritan Pentateuch">Samaritan Pentateuch</a>, Benjamin's name appears as "Binyaamem" (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span class="nowrap"><span lang="he">בנימים</span></span>, <i> "Son of my days"</i>). In the <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a>, Benjamin is referred to as righteous young child, who remained with Jacob when the older brothers plotted against Joseph. Later rabbinic traditions name him as one of four ancient Israelites who died without sin, the other three being <a href="/wiki/Chileab" title="Chileab">Chileab</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jesse" title="Jesse">Jesse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amram" title="Amram">Amram</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Benjamin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a>, Benjamin's name arose when Jacob deliberately changed the name "Benoni", the original name of Benjamin, since Benoni was an allusion to Rachel's dying just after she had given birth, as it means "son of my pain".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Textual scholars regard these two names as fragments of naming narratives coming from different sources - one being the <a href="/wiki/Jahwist" title="Jahwist">Jahwist</a> and the other being the <a href="/wiki/Elohist" title="Elohist">Elohist</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Unusually for one of the <a href="/wiki/12_tribes_of_Israel" class="mw-redirect" title="12 tribes of Israel">12 tribes of Israel</a>, the Bible does not explain the  <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymology</a> of Benjamin's name. Medieval commentator <a href="/wiki/Rashi" title="Rashi">Rashi</a> gives two different explanations, based on <a href="/wiki/Midrash" title="Midrash">Midrashic</a> sources. "Son of the south", with south derived from the word for the right hand side, referring to the birth of Benjamin in <a href="/wiki/Canaan" title="Canaan">Canaan</a>, as compared with the birth of all the other sons of Jacob in <a href="/wiki/Aram-Naharaim" title="Aram-Naharaim">Aram</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rashi_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rashi-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bdmtze_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bdmtze-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  Modern scholars have proposed that "son of the south" / "right" is a reference to the tribe being subordinate to the more dominant tribe of Ephraim.<sup id="cite_ref-bdmtze_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bdmtze-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Alternatively, Rashi suggests it means "son of days", meaning a son born in Jacob's old age. The <a href="/wiki/Samaritan_Pentateuch" title="Samaritan Pentateuch">Samaritan Pentateuch</a> consistently spells his name "בן ימים", with a terminal <a href="/wiki/Mem" title="Mem">mem</a>, ("Binyamim"), which could be translated literally as "spirit man" but is in line with the interpretation that the name was a reference to the advanced age of Jacob when Benjamin was born.<sup id="cite_ref-bdmtze_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bdmtze-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to classical rabbinical sources, Benjamin was only born after <a href="/wiki/Rachel" title="Rachel">Rachel</a> had <a href="/wiki/Fasted" class="mw-redirect" title="Fasted">fasted</a> for a long time, as a religious devotion with the hope of a new child as a reward. By then <a href="/wiki/Jacob" title="Jacob">Jacob</a> had become over 100 years old.<sup id="cite_ref-bdmtze_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bdmtze-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Benjamin is treated as a young child in most of the Biblical narrative,<sup id="cite_ref-bdmtze_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bdmtze-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> but at one point is abruptly described as the father of ten sons.<sup id="cite_ref-xhbyqx_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-xhbyqx-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Textual scholars believe that this is the result of the genealogical passage, in which his children are named, being from a much later source than the <a href="/wiki/Jahwist" title="Jahwist">Jahwist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elohist" title="Elohist">Elohist</a> narratives, which make up most of the <a href="/wiki/Joseph_(Genesis)" title="Joseph (Genesis)">Joseph</a> narrative, and which consistently describe Benjamin as a child.<sup id="cite_ref-bdmtze_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bdmtze-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Israelites_in_Egypt">Israelites in Egypt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Benjamin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Israelites in Egypt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BenjaminandJoseph.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/BenjaminandJoseph.jpg/220px-BenjaminandJoseph.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="277" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d9/BenjaminandJoseph.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="250" data-file-height="315" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BenjaminandJoseph.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Benjamin (right) embracing his brother <a href="/wiki/Joseph_(Hebrew_Bible)" class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph (Hebrew Bible)">Joseph</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The Torah's Joseph narrative, at a stage when Joseph is unrecognised by his brothers, describes Joseph as testing whether his brothers have reformed by secretly planting a silver cup in Benjamin's bag. Then, publicly searching the bags for it, and after <i>finding</i> it in Benjamin's possession, demanding that Benjamin become his <a href="/wiki/Slave" class="mw-redirect" title="Slave">slave</a> as a punishment.<sup id="cite_ref-Gen44_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gen44-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The narrative goes on to state that when <a href="/wiki/Judah_(son_of_Jacob)" title="Judah (son of Jacob)">Judah</a> (on behalf of the other brothers) begged Joseph not to enslave Benjamin and instead enslave him, since enslavement of Benjamin would break Jacob's heart. This caused Joseph to recant and reveal his identity.<sup id="cite_ref-Gen44_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gen44-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Sefer_haYashar_(midrash)" title="Sefer haYashar (midrash)">midrashic book of Jasher</a> argues that prior to revealing his identity, Joseph asked Benjamin to find his missing brother (i.e. Joseph) via <a href="/wiki/Astrology" title="Astrology">astrology</a>, using an <a href="/wiki/Astrolabe" title="Astrolabe">astrolabe</a>-like tool.<sup id="cite_ref-bdmtze_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bdmtze-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It continues by stating that Benjamin <a href="/wiki/Divination" title="Divination">divined</a> that the <i>man on the throne</i> was Joseph, so Joseph identified himself to Benjamin (but not the other brothers), and revealed his scheme (as in the Torah) to test how fraternal the other brothers were.<sup id="cite_ref-bdmtze_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bdmtze-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some classical rabbinical sources argue that Joseph identified himself for other reasons.<sup id="cite_ref-bdmtze_5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bdmtze-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In these sources, Benjamin swore an oath, on the memory of Joseph, that he was innocent of theft, and, when challenged about how believable the oath would be, explained that remembering Joseph was so important to him that he had named his sons in Joseph's honour.<sup id="cite_ref-bdmtze_5-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bdmtze-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> These sources go on to state that Benjamin's oath touched Joseph so deeply that Joseph was no longer able to pretend to be a stranger.<sup id="cite_ref-bdmtze_5-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bdmtze-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Morgan-bible-fl06.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Morgan-bible-fl06.jpg/300px-Morgan-bible-fl06.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Morgan-bible-fl06.jpg/450px-Morgan-bible-fl06.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Morgan-bible-fl06.jpg/600px-Morgan-bible-fl06.jpg 2x" data-file-width="851" data-file-height="484" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Morgan-bible-fl06.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An illustration from the <a href="/wiki/Morgan_Bible" title="Morgan Bible">Morgan Bible</a> of Benjamin being returned to Egypt (Genesis 44).</div></div></div>
<p>In the narrative, just prior to this test, when Joseph had first met all of his brothers (but not identified himself to them), he had held a feast for them;<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> the narrative heavily implies that Benjamin was Joseph's favorite brother, since he is overcome with tears when he first meets Benjamin in particular,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and he gives Benjamin five times as much food as he apportions to the others.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> According to textual scholars, this is really the Jahwist's account of the reunion after Joseph identifies himself, and the account of the threat to enslave Benjamin is just the Elohist's version of the same event, with the Elohist being more terse about Joseph's emotions towards Benjamin, merely mentioning that Benjamin was given five times as many gifts as the others.<sup id="cite_ref-bdmtze_5-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bdmtze-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Benjamin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kever_Binyamin.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Kever_Binyamin.jpg/300px-Kever_Binyamin.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="207" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Kever_Binyamin.jpg/450px-Kever_Binyamin.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Kever_Binyamin.jpg/600px-Kever_Binyamin.jpg 2x" data-file-width="997" data-file-height="687" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kever_Binyamin.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An exterior view of a <a href="/wiki/Mamluk" title="Mamluk">Mamluk</a> <a href="/wiki/Caravanserai" title="Caravanserai">caravanserai</a> complex, including the mausoleum of Nabi Yamin, traditionally believed to be the tomb of Benjamin, located outside <a href="/wiki/Kfar_Saba" title="Kfar Saba">Kfar Saba</a>, Israel.</div></div></div>
<p>Biblical scholars believe, due to their geographic overlap and their treatment in older passages, that Ephraim and Manasseh were originally considered one tribe, that of <i>Joseph</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-gzuhsh_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gzuhsh-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> According to several biblical scholars, Benjamin was also originally part of this single tribe, but the biblical account of Joseph as his father became lost.<sup id="cite_ref-gzuhsh_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gzuhsh-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-fxeide_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fxeide-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The description of Benjamin being born after the arrival in Canaan is thought by some scholars to refer to the tribe of Benjamin coming into existence by branching from the Joseph group after the tribe had settled in Canaan.<sup id="cite_ref-fxeide_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fxeide-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> A number of biblical scholars suspect that the distinction of the <i>Joseph tribes</i> (including Benjamin) is that they were the only Israelites which went to <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> <a href="/wiki/The_Exodus" title="The Exodus">and returned</a>, while the main Israelite tribes simply emerged as a subculture from the Canaanites and had remained in <a href="/wiki/Canaan" title="Canaan">Canaan</a> throughout.<sup id="cite_ref-fxeide_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fxeide-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to this view, the story of Jacob's visit to <a href="/wiki/Laban_(Bible)" title="Laban (Bible)">Laban</a> to obtain a wife originated as a <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">metaphor</a> for this migration, with the property and family which were gained from Laban representing the gains of the Joseph tribes by the time they returned from Egypt.<sup id="cite_ref-fxeide_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fxeide-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> According to textual scholars, the <a href="/wiki/Jahwist" title="Jahwist">Jahwist</a> version of the Laban narrative only mentions the Joseph tribes, and Rachel, and does not mention the other tribal <a href="/wiki/Matriarch" class="mw-redirect" title="Matriarch">matriarchs</a> whatsoever.<sup id="cite_ref-fxeide_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fxeide-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Benjamin.27s_sons"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Benjamin's_sons">Benjamin's sons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Benjamin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Benjamin&#039;s sons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt0146.htm#21">Genesis 46:21</a>, Benjamin had ten sons: Bela, Becher, Ashbel, Gera, Naaman, Ehi, Rosh, Muppim, Huppim, and Ard. The name of his wife/wives are not given. However the Book of Jubilees calls his wife Ijasaka and the Book of Jasher mentions two wives Mechalia the daughter of Aram and Aribath the daughter of Shomron.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The Classical rabbinical tradition adds that each son's name honors Joseph:<sup id="cite_ref-bdmtze_5-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bdmtze-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>Belah</i> (meaning <i>swallow</i>), in reference to Joseph disappearing (<i>being swallowed up</i>)</li>
<li><i>Becher</i> (meaning <i>first born</i>), in reference to Joseph being the first child of Rachel</li>
<li><i>Ashbel</i> (meaning <i>capture</i>), in reference to Joseph having suffered captivity</li>
<li><i>Gera</i> (meaning <i>grain</i>), in reference to Joseph living in a <i>foreign</i> land (Egypt)</li>
<li><i>Naaman</i> (meaning <i>grace</i>), in reference to Joseph having graceful speech</li>
<li><i>Ehi</i> (meaning <i>my brother</i>), in reference to Joseph being Benjamin's only full-brother (as opposed to half-brothers)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Rosh_(biblical_figure)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rosh (biblical figure)">Rosh</a></i> (meaning <i>elder</i>), in reference to Joseph being older than Benjamin</li>
<li><i>Muppim</i> (meaning <i>double mouth</i>), in reference to Joseph passing on what he had been taught by Jacob</li>
<li><i>Huppim</i> (meaning <i><a href="/wiki/Chuppah" title="Chuppah">marriage canopies</a></i>), in reference to Joseph being married in Egypt, while Benjamin was not there</li>
<li><i>Ard</i> (meaning <i>wanderer</i>/<i>fugitive</i>), in reference to Joseph being like a rose</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Islam">In Islam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Benjamin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In Islam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Though not named in the <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Cite_quran&#124;12&#124;4&#124;e=102&#124;s=ns_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cite_quran|12|4|e=102|s=ns-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Benjamin is referred to as the righteous youngest son of <a href="/wiki/Jacob" title="Jacob">Jacob</a>, in the narrative of <a href="/wiki/Joseph_in_Islam" title="Joseph in Islam">Joseph in Islamic</a> tradition. Apart from that, however, Islamic tradition does not provide much detail regarding Benjamin's life, and refers to him as being born from Jacob's wife <a href="/wiki/Rachel" title="Rachel">Rachel</a>. As with Jewish tradition, it also further links a connection between the names of Benjamin's children and Joseph.<sup id="cite_ref-EOIBinyamin_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EOIBinyamin-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
