<p><b>Pre-Socratic philosophy</b> is <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_philosophy" title="Ancient Greek philosophy">ancient Greek philosophy</a> before <a href="/wiki/Socrates" title="Socrates">Socrates</a> and schools contemporary to Socrates that were not influenced by him.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">Classical antiquity</a>, the Presocratic philosophers were called <i><a href="/wiki/Physiologoi" class="mw-redirect" title="Physiologoi">physiologoi</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el">φυσιολόγοι</span>; in English, physical or <a href="/wiki/Natural_philosophy" title="Natural philosophy">natural philosophers</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Their inquiries spanned the workings of the natural world as well as human society, ethics, and religion, seeking explanations based on natural principles rather than the actions of supernatural gods. They introduced to the West the notion of the world as a <i><a href="/wiki/Kosmos" class="mw-redirect" title="Kosmos">kosmos</a></i>, an ordered arrangement that could be understood via rational inquiry.<sup id="cite_ref-SEP_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SEP-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> was the first to make a clear distinction between these <i>physiologoi</i> or <i>physikoi</i> ("physicists", after <i><a href="/wiki/Physis" title="Physis">physis</a></i>, "<a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">nature</a>") who sought natural explanations for <a href="/wiki/Phenomena" class="mw-redirect" title="Phenomena">phenomena</a>, and the earlier <i><a href="/wiki/Theology#Etymology" title="Theology">theologoi</a></i> (theologians), or <i>mythologoi</i> (story tellers and bards) who attributed these phenomena to various gods.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Diogenes_La%C3%ABrtius" title="Diogenes Laërtius">Diogenes Laërtius</a> divides the <i>physiologoi</i> into two groups: <a href="/wiki/Ionian_School_(philosophy)" title="Ionian School (philosophy)">Ionian</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/Anaximander" title="Anaximander">Anaximander</a>, and Italiote, led by <a href="/wiki/Pythagoras" title="Pythagoras">Pythagoras</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pre-Socratic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Modern interest in early Greek philosophy can be traced back to 1573, when <a href="/wiki/Henri_Estienne" title="Henri Estienne">Henri Estienne</a> collected a number of pre-Socratic fragments in <i>Poesis Philosophica</i> (<i>Ποίησις Φιλόσοφος</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Diels" class="mw-redirect" title="Hermann Diels">Hermann Diels</a> popularized the term "pre-Socratic" in <i>Die Fragmente der Vorsokratiker</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/The_Fragments_of_the_Pre-Socratics" class="mw-redirect" title="The Fragments of the Pre-Socratics">The Fragments of the Pre-Socratics</a></i>) in 1903. However, the term "pre-Sokratic" &#32;&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>&#93; was in use as early as <a href="/wiki/George_Grote" title="George Grote">George Grote</a>'s <i>Plato and the Other Companions of Sokrates</i> in 1865. <a href="/wiki/Edouard_Zeller" class="mw-redirect" title="Edouard Zeller">Edouard Zeller</a> was also important in dividing thought before and after Socrates.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Major analyses of pre-Socratic thought have been made by <a href="/wiki/Gregory_Vlastos" title="Gregory Vlastos">Gregory Vlastos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Barnes" title="Jonathan Barnes">Jonathan Barnes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Nietzsche" title="Friedrich Nietzsche">Friedrich Nietzsche</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Philosophy_in_the_Tragic_Age_of_the_Greeks" title="Philosophy in the Tragic Age of the Greeks">Philosophy in the Tragic Age of the Greeks</a></i>.
</p><p>It may sometimes be difficult to determine the actual line of argument some pre-Socratics used in supporting their particular views. While most of them produced significant texts, none of the texts have survived in complete form. All that is available are quotations by later philosophers (often biased) and historians, and the occasional textual fragment.
</p><p>The pre-Socratic philosophers rejected <a href="/wiki/Religious_cosmology" title="Religious cosmology">traditional mythological explanations of the phenomena</a> they saw around them in favor of more rational explanations. These philosophers asked questions about "the <a href="/wiki/Essence" title="Essence">essence</a> of things":<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>From where does everything come?</li>
<li>From what is everything created?</li>
<li>How do we explain the plurality of things found in nature?</li>
<li>How might we describe nature mathematically?</li></ul>
<p>Others concentrated on defining problems and paradoxes that became the basis for later mathematical, scientific and philosophic study.
</p><p>Later philosophers rejected many of the answers the early Greek philosophers provided, but continued to place importance on their questions. Furthermore, the <a href="/wiki/Cosmology" title="Cosmology">cosmologies</a> proposed by them have been updated by later developments in <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pre-Socratic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_metaphysical_naturalism" class="mw-redirect" title="History of metaphysical naturalism">History of metaphysical naturalism</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Presocratic_graph.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Presocratic_graph.svg/500px-Presocratic_graph.svg.png" decoding="async" width="500" height="260" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Presocratic_graph.svg/750px-Presocratic_graph.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Presocratic_graph.svg/1000px-Presocratic_graph.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="2177" data-file-height="1133" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Presocratic_graph.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Graphical relationship among the various pre-socratic <a href="/wiki/Philosopher" title="Philosopher">philosophers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Intellectual" title="Intellectual">thinkers</a>; red arrows indicate a relationship of opposition.</div></div></div>
<p>Coming from the eastern or western fringes of the Greek world, the pre-Socratics were the forerunners of what became Western philosophy as well as <a href="/wiki/Natural_philosophy" title="Natural philosophy">natural philosophy</a>, which later developed into the natural sciences (such as physics, chemistry, geology, and astronomy).<sup id="cite_ref-SEP_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SEP-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Their efforts were directed to the investigation of the ultimate basis and essential nature of the external world.<sup id="cite_ref-seyffert480_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seyffert480-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> They sought the material principle (<i><a href="/wiki/Arche" title="Arche">archê</a></i>) of things, and the method of their origin and disappearance.<sup id="cite_ref-seyffert480_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seyffert480-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> As the first philosophers, they emphasized the rational unity of things and rejected supernatural explanations, instead seeking natural principles at work in the world and human society. The pre-Socratics saw the world as a <i><a href="/wiki/Kosmos" class="mw-redirect" title="Kosmos">kosmos</a></i>, an ordered arrangement that could be understood via rational inquiry.<sup id="cite_ref-SEP_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SEP-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Pre-Socratic thinkers present a discourse concerned with key areas of philosophical inquiry such as being, the primary stuff of the universe, the structure and function of the human soul, and the underlying principles governing perceptible phenomena, human knowledge and morality.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Writings">Writings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pre-Socratic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Writings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Only fragments of the original writings of the pre-Socratics survive (many entitled <i>Peri Physeos</i>, or <i><a href="/wiki/On_Nature_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="On Nature (disambiguation)">On Nature</a></i>, a title probably attributed later by other authors).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The knowledge we have of them derives from accounts - known as <a href="/wiki/Doxography" title="Doxography">doxography</a> - of later philosophical writers (especially <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diogenes_La%C3%ABrtius" title="Diogenes Laërtius">Diogenes Laërtius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stobaeus" title="Stobaeus">Stobaeus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Simplicius_of_Cilicia" title="Simplicius of Cilicia">Simplicius</a>), and some early theologians (especially <a href="/wiki/Clement_of_Alexandria" title="Clement of Alexandria">Clement of Alexandria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hippolytus_of_Rome" title="Hippolytus of Rome">Hippolytus of Rome</a>).
</p><p>The translation of <i>Peri Physeos</i> as <i>On Nature</i> may be misleading: the "on" normally gives the idea of an "erudite dissertation", while "<i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/peri" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:peri">peri</a></i>" may refer in fact to a "circular approach"; and the traditional meanings of "<a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">nature</a>" for us (as opposition to <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">supernatural</a>, or as <a href="/wiki/Essence" title="Essence">essence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Substance_theory" title="Substance theory">substance</a>, opposed to <a href="/wiki/Accident" title="Accident">accident</a>, etc.) may be in contrast with the meaning of "<i>physeos</i>" or "<i><a href="/wiki/Physis" title="Physis">physis</a></i>" for the Greeks (referring to an "originary source", or "process of emergence and development").<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Milesian_school">Milesian school</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pre-Socratic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Milesian school">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first pre-Socratic philosophers were from <a href="/wiki/Miletus" title="Miletus">Miletus</a> on the western coast of Anatolia. <a href="/wiki/Thales" class="mw-redirect" title="Thales">Thales</a> (624-546 BC) is reputedly the father of Greek philosophy; he declared water to be the basis of all things.<sup id="cite_ref-seyffert480_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seyffert480-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Next came <a href="/wiki/Anaximander" title="Anaximander">Anaximander</a> (610-546 BC), the first writer on philosophy. He assumed as the first principle an undefined, unlimited substance without qualities (<i><a href="/wiki/Apeiron_(cosmology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Apeiron (cosmology)">apeiron</a></i>), out of which the primary opposites, hot and cold, moist and dry, became differentiated.<sup id="cite_ref-seyffert480_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seyffert480-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> His younger contemporary, <a href="/wiki/Anaximenes_of_Miletus" title="Anaximenes of Miletus">Anaximenes</a> (585-525 BC), took for his principle air, conceiving it as modified, by thickening and thinning, into fire, wind, clouds, water, and earth.<sup id="cite_ref-seyffert480_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seyffert480-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pythagoreanism">Pythagoreanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pre-Socratic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pythagoreanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The practical side of philosophy was introduced by <a href="/wiki/Pythagoras" title="Pythagoras">Pythagoras</a> (582-496 BC). Regarding the world as perfect harmony, dependent on number, he aimed at inducing humankind likewise to lead a harmonious life. His doctrine was adopted and extended by a large following of Pythagoreans who gathered at his school in south Italy in the town of <a href="/wiki/Crotone" title="Crotone">Croton</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-seyffert480_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seyffert480-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> His followers included <a href="/wiki/Philolaus" title="Philolaus">Philolaus</a> (470-380 BC), <a href="/wiki/Alcmaeon_of_Croton" title="Alcmaeon of Croton">Alcmaeon of Croton</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Archytas" title="Archytas">Archytas</a> (428-347 BC).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ephesian_school">Ephesian school</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pre-Socratic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ephesian school">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ephesian_school" title="Ephesian school">Ephesian school</a></div>
<p>The Ephesian philosophers were interested in the natural world and the properties by which it is ordered. <a href="/wiki/Xenophanes" title="Xenophanes">Xenophanes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heraclitus" title="Heraclitus">Heraclitus</a> were able to push philosophical inquiry further than the Milesian school by examining the nature of philosophical inquiry itself. In addition, they were also invested in furthering observations and explanations regarding natural and physical process and also the functions and processes of the human subjective experience.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Heraclitus and Xenophanes both shared interests in analyzing philosophical inquiry as they contemplated morality and religious belief. This was because they wanted to figure out the proper methods of understanding human knowledge and the ways humans fit into the world. This was much different than natural philosophy that was being done by other philosophers as it questioned how the operations of the universe as well as the human positions within the universe.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Heraclitus posited that all things in nature are in a state of perpetual flux, connected by logical structure or pattern, which he termed <i><a href="/wiki/Logos" title="Logos">Logos</a></i>. To Heraclitus, fire, one of the four <a href="/wiki/Classical_element" title="Classical element">classical elements</a>, motivates and substantiates this eternal pattern. From fire all things originate, and return to it again in a process of eternal cycles.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eleatic_school">Eleatic school</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pre-Socratic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Eleatic school">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Eleatics" title="Eleatics">Eleatics</a> emphasized the doctrine of the One. Xenophanes (570-470 BC) declared God to be the eternal unity, permeating the universe, and governing it by his thought.<sup id="cite_ref-seyffert480_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seyffert480-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Parmenides" title="Parmenides">Parmenides</a> (510-440 BC) affirmed the one unchanging existence to be alone true and capable of being conceived, and multitude and change to be an appearance without reality.<sup id="cite_ref-seyffert480_10-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seyffert480-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> This doctrine was defended by his younger countryman <a href="/wiki/Zeno_of_Elea" title="Zeno of Elea">Zeno of Elea</a> (490-430 BC) in a polemic against the common opinion which sees in things multitude, becoming, and change. Zeno propounded a number of celebrated paradoxes, much debated by later philosophers, which try to show that supposing that there is any change or multiplicity leads to contradictions.<sup id="cite_ref-seyffert480_10-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seyffert480-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Melissus_of_Samos" title="Melissus of Samos">Melissus of Samos</a> (born c. 470 BC) was another eminent member of this school.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pluralist_school">Pluralist school</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pre-Socratic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pluralist school">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pluralist_school" title="Pluralist school">Pluralist school</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Empedocles" title="Empedocles">Empedocles</a> appears to have been partly in agreement with the Eleatic School, partly in opposition to it. On the one hand, he maintained the unchangeable nature of substance; on the other, he supposes a plurality of such substances - i.e. four <a href="/wiki/Classical_elements" class="mw-redirect" title="Classical elements">classical elements</a>, earth, water, air, and fire. Of these the world is built up, by the agency of two ideal motive forces - love as the cause of union, strife as the cause of separation.<sup id="cite_ref-seyffert480_10-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seyffert480-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Anaxagoras" title="Anaxagoras">Anaxagoras</a> (500-428 BC) in Asia Minor also maintained the existence of an ordering principle as well as a material substance, and while regarding the latter as an infinite multitude of imperishable primary elements, he conceived divine reason or Mind (<i><a href="/wiki/Nous" title="Nous">nous</a></i>) as ordering them. He referred all generation and disappearance to mixture and resolution respectively. To him belongs the credit of first establishing philosophy at <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-seyffert480_10-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seyffert480-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atomist_school">Atomist school</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pre-Socratic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Atomist school">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first explicitly materialistic system was formed by <a href="/wiki/Leucippus" title="Leucippus">Leucippus</a> (5th century BC) and his pupil <a href="/wiki/Democritus" title="Democritus">Democritus</a> (460-370 BC) from <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thrace</a>. This was the doctrine of atoms - small primary bodies infinite in number, indivisible and imperishable, qualitatively similar, but distinguished by their shapes. Moving eternally through the infinite void, they collide and unite, thus generating objects which differ in accordance with the varieties, in number, size, shape, and arrangement, of the atoms which compose them.<sup id="cite_ref-seyffert480_10-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seyffert480-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Others">Others</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pre-Socratic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Others">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Diogenes_of_Apollonia" title="Diogenes of Apollonia">Diogenes of Apollonia</a> from <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thrace</a> (born c. 460 BC) was an eclectic philosopher who adopted many principles of the <a href="/wiki/Milesian_school" title="Milesian school">Milesian school</a>, especially the single material principle, which he identified as air. He explained natural processes in reference to the rarefactions and condensations of this primary substance. He also adopted <a href="/wiki/Anaxagoras" title="Anaxagoras">Anaxagoras</a>' cosmic thought.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sophists">Sophists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pre-Socratic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Sophists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sophist" title="Sophist">sophists</a> held that all thought rests solely on the apprehensions of the senses and on subjective impression, and that therefore we have no other standards of action than convention for the individual.<sup id="cite_ref-seyffert480_10-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seyffert480-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Specializing in <a href="/wiki/Rhetoric" title="Rhetoric">rhetoric</a>, the sophists were typically seen more as professional educators than philosophers. The sophists traveled extensively educating people throughout Greece. Unlike philosophical schools, the sophists had no common set of philosophical doctrines that connected them to each other. They did, however, focus on teaching techniques of debate and persuasion which centered around the study of language, semantics, and grammar for use in convincing people of certain viewpoints. They also taught students their own interpretations of the social sciences, mathematics, history, among others.<b><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></b> They flourished as a result of a special need at that time for Greek education. Prominent sophists include <a href="/wiki/Protagoras" title="Protagoras">Protagoras</a> (490-420 BC) from Abdera in Thrace, <a href="/wiki/Gorgias" title="Gorgias">Gorgias</a> (487-376 BC) from Leontini in Sicily, <a href="/wiki/Hippias" title="Hippias">Hippias</a> (485-415 BC) from <a href="/wiki/Elis" title="Elis">Elis</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Peloponnese" title="Peloponnese">Peloponnesos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prodicus" title="Prodicus">Prodicus</a> (465-390 BC) from the island of <a href="/wiki/Kea_(island)" title="Kea (island)">Ceos</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thrasymachus" title="Thrasymachus">Thrasymachus</a> (459-400 BC) from <a href="/wiki/Chalcedon" title="Chalcedon">Chalcedon</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Bosporus" title="Bosporus">Bosphorus</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pre-Socratic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>The Pre-Socratic method of critical reasoning deployed in the examination of the natural world was applied by Socrates to an examination of the human individual and his social <a href="/wiki/Institution" title="Institution">institutions</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_Friedrich_Hegel" title="Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel">Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel</a> deeply studied the Pre-Socratics, crediting the philosopher Parmenides with introducing the concepts of Being and Non-Being (or Nothing).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a>'s doctoral thesis <i><a href="/wiki/The_Difference_Between_the_Democritean_and_Epicurean_Philosophy_of_Nature" title="The Difference Between the Democritean and Epicurean Philosophy of Nature">The Difference Between the Democritean and Epicurean Philosophy of Nature</a></i> evaluates the thought of the Pre-Socratic philosopher Democritus, one of the founders of Atomic theory.</li>
<li>Within the Marxist philosophical tradition the Pre-Socratics are recognized as the first <a href="/wiki/Materialist" class="mw-redirect" title="Materialist">materialists</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Nietzsche" title="Friedrich Nietzsche">Friedrich Nietzsche</a> described the Pre-Socratics as "the tyrants of the spirit",<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and says of Socrates that "the hitherto so wonderfully regular, although certainly too rapid, development of the philosophical science was destroyed in one night".</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Oswald_Spengler" title="Oswald Spengler">Oswald Spengler</a>'s doctoral thesis <i>The Fundamental Metaphysical Idea of the Philosophy of Heraclitus</i> evaluates the thought of the Pre-Socratic philosopher Heraclitus, dubbed "the obscure".</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Karl_Popper" title="Karl Popper">Karl Popper</a>, one of the 20th century's most influential philosophers of science, placed great importance on the critical tradition embodied in the development of Pre-Socratic thought, the analysis of which contributed to his own epistemological theories. His well-known essay on the subject, "Back to the Pre-Socratics", can be found in the anthology of his essays <i>Conjectures and Refutations - The Growth of Scientific Knowledge</i>, 2nd Edition. Routledge Publishing. 2002.</li></ul>
