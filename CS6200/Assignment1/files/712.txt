<p><b>Marcus Vitruvius Pollio</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;v&#39; in &#39;vie&#39;">v</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span><span title="&#39;r&#39; in &#39;rye&#39;">r</span><span title="/uː/: &#39;oo&#39; in &#39;goose&#39;">uː</span><span title="&#39;v&#39; in &#39;vie&#39;">v</span><span title="/i/: &#39;y&#39; in &#39;happy&#39;">i</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span></span><span class="wrap"> </span><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;p&#39; in &#39;pie&#39;">p</span><span title="/ɒ/: &#39;o&#39; in &#39;body&#39;">ɒ</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span><span title="/i/: &#39;y&#39; in &#39;happy&#39;">i</span><span title="/oʊ/: &#39;o&#39; in &#39;code&#39;">oʊ</span></span>/</a></span></span>; c. 80–70 BC – after c. 15&#160;BC), commonly known as <b>Vitruvius</b>, was a <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> <a href="/wiki/Author" title="Author">author</a>, <a href="/wiki/Architect" title="Architect">architect</a>, <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineer" title="Civil engineer">civil engineer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Military_engineering" title="Military engineering">military engineer</a> during the 1st century BC, known for his multi-volume work entitled <i><a href="/wiki/De_architectura" title="De architectura">De architectura</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> His discussion of perfect proportion in architecture and the human body led to the famous <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> drawing by Leonardo da Vinci of <i><a href="/wiki/Vitruvian_Man" title="Vitruvian Man">Vitruvian Man</a></i>. He was also the one who, in 40 BCE, invented the idea that all buildings should have three attributes: <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">firmitas</i>, <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">utilitas</i>, and <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">venustas</i>, meaning: strength, utility, and beauty. These principles were later adopted by the Romans.
</p><p>By his own description<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Vitruvius served as an artilleryman, the third class of arms in the military offices. He probably served as a senior officer of artillery in charge of <i>doctores ballistarum</i> (artillery experts) and <i>libratores</i> who actually operated the machines.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_career">Life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vitruvius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Little is known about Vitruvius' life. Most inferences about him are extracted from his only surviving work <i><a href="/wiki/De_Architectura" class="mw-redirect" title="De Architectura">De Architectura</a></i>. Even his first name <i>Marcus</i> and his <a href="/wiki/Cognomen" title="Cognomen">cognomen</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Pollio" title="Pollio">Pollio</a></i> are uncertain. Marcus Cetius Faventinus writes of "Vitruvius Polio aliique auctores"; this can be read as "Vitruvius Polio, and others" or, less likely, as "Vitruvius, Polio, and others". An inscription in Verona, which names a <i><a href="/wiki/Lucius_Vitruvius_Cordo" class="mw-redirect" title="Lucius Vitruvius Cordo">Lucius Vitruvius Cordo</a></i>, and an inscription from <a href="/wiki/Numidia" title="Numidia">Thilbilis</a> in North Africa, which names a <i>Marcus Vitruvius Mamurra</i> have been suggested as evidence that Vitruvius and <a href="/wiki/Mamurra" title="Mamurra">Mamurra</a> (who was a military <i>praefectus fabrum</i> under <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a>) were from the same family;<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> or were even the same individual.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Neither association, however, is borne out by <i>De Architectura</i> (which Vitruvius dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a>), nor by the little that is known of Mamurra.
</p><p>Vitruvius was a military engineer (<i><a href="/wiki/Roman_military_engineering" title="Roman military engineering">praefectus fabrum</a></i>), or a <i><a href="/wiki/Praefect" class="mw-redirect" title="Praefect">praefect</a> architectus armamentarius</i> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Apparitor" title="Apparitor">apparitor</a></i> status group (a branch of the Roman civil service). He is mentioned in <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a>'s table of contents for <i><a href="/wiki/Naturalis_Historia" class="mw-redirect" title="Naturalis Historia">Naturalis Historia</a></i> (Natural History), in the heading for <a href="/wiki/Mosaic" title="Mosaic">mosaic</a> techniques.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Frontinus" title="Frontinus">Frontinus</a> refers to "Vitruvius the architect" in his late 1st-century work <i><a href="/wiki/De_aquaeductu" title="De aquaeductu">De aquaeductu</a></i>.
</p><p>Likely born a free Roman citizen, by his own account, Vitruvius served in the <a href="/wiki/Roman_army" title="Roman army">Roman army</a> under Caesar with the otherwise poorly identified Marcus Aurelius, Publius Minidius, and Gnaeus Cornelius. These names vary depending on the edition of <i>De architectura</i>. Publius Minidius is also written as Publius Numidicus and Publius Numidius, speculated as the same Publius Numisius inscribed on the <a href="/wiki/Heraclea_Lyncestis#Roman_Theater" title="Heraclea Lyncestis">Roman Theatre at Heraclea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As an <a href="/wiki/Army_engineer" class="mw-redirect" title="Army engineer">army engineer</a> he specialized in the construction of <i><a href="/wiki/Ballista" title="Ballista">ballista</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Scorpio_(dart-thrower)" class="mw-redirect" title="Scorpio (dart-thrower)">scorpio</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Artillery" title="Artillery">artillery</a> <a href="/wiki/Roman_siege_engines" title="Roman siege engines">war machines</a> for <a href="/wiki/Siege" title="Siege">sieges</a>. It is speculated that Vitruvius served with Caesar's chief engineer <a href="/wiki/Lucius_Cornelius_Balbus_(major)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lucius Cornelius Balbus (major)">Lucius Cornelius Balbus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The locations where he served can be reconstructed from, for example, descriptions of the building methods of various "foreign tribes". Although he describes places throughout <i>De Architectura</i>, he does not say he was present. His service likely included <a href="/wiki/Africa_Province" class="mw-redirect" title="Africa Province">north Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hispania" title="Hispania">Hispania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Gallia_Aquitania" title="Gallia Aquitania">Aquitaine</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Pontus_(region)" title="Pontus (region)">Pontus</a>.
</p><p>To place the role of Vitruvius the military engineer in context, a description of "The Prefect of the camp" or army engineer is quoted here as given by <a href="/wiki/Flavius_Vegetius_Renatus" class="mw-redirect" title="Flavius Vegetius Renatus">Flavius Vegetius Renatus</a> in <i>The Military Institutions of the Romans</i>:
</p>
<blockquote><p>The Prefect of the camp, though inferior in rank to the [Prefect], had a post of no small importance. The position of the camp, the direction of the entrenchments, the inspection of the tents or huts of the soldiers and the baggage were comprehended in his province. His authority extended over the sick, and the physicians who had the care of them; and he regulated the expenses relative thereto. He had the charge of providing carriages, bathhouses and the proper tools for sawing and cutting wood, digging trenches, raising parapets, sinking wells and bringing water into the camp. He likewise had the care of furnishing the troops with wood and straw, as well as the rams, <i><a href="/wiki/Onager_(siege_weapon)" class="mw-redirect" title="Onager (siege weapon)">onagri</a>,</i> <i>balistae</i> and all the other engines of war under his direction. This post was always conferred on an officer of great skill, experience and long service, and who consequently was capable of instructing others in those branches of the profession in which he had distinguished himself.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>At various locations described by Vitruvius,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> battles and <a href="/wiki/Siege#Greco-Roman_and_medieval_siege_warfare" title="Siege">sieges</a> occurred. He is the only source for the siege of <a href="/wiki/Larino" title="Larino">Larignum</a> in 56 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Of the battlegrounds of the <a href="/wiki/Gallic_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Gallic War">Gallic War</a> there are references to: the siege and massacre of the 40,000 residents at <a href="/wiki/Avaricum" title="Avaricum">Avaricum</a> in 52 BC; <a href="/wiki/Vercingetorix" title="Vercingetorix">Vercingetorix</a> commented that "the Romans did not conquer by valor nor in the field, but by a kind of art and skill in assault, with which they [Gauls] themselves were unacquainted."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The broken siege at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Gergovia" title="Battle of Gergovia">Gergovia</a> in 52 BC. The circumvallation and <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Alesia#Siege_and_battle" title="Battle of Alesia">Battle of Alesia</a> in 52 BC; the women and children of the encircled city were evicted to conserve food, where they starved to death between the opposing walls of the defenders and besiegers. And the siege of <a href="/wiki/Uxellodunum" title="Uxellodunum">Uxellodunum</a> in 51 BC. These are all sieges of large Gallic <i><a href="/wiki/Oppida" class="mw-redirect" title="Oppida">oppida</a></i>. Of the sites involved in <a href="/wiki/Caesar%27s_civil_war" class="mw-redirect" title="Caesar&#39;s civil war">Caesar's civil war</a>, we find the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Massilia" title="Siege of Massilia">Siege of Massilia</a> in 49 BC,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Dyrrhachium_(48_BC)" title="Battle of Dyrrhachium (48 BC)">Battle of Dyrrhachium</a> of 48 BC (modern Albania), the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Pharsalus" title="Battle of Pharsalus">Battle of Pharsalus</a> in 48 BC (Hellas – Greece), the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Zela" title="Battle of Zela">Battle of Zela</a> of 47 BC (modern Turkey) and the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Thapsus" title="Battle of Thapsus">Battle of Thapsus</a> in 46 BC in Caesar's <a href="/wiki/Africa_Province" class="mw-redirect" title="Africa Province">African</a> campaign.<sup id="cite_ref-bandw_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bandw-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Roman_legion" title="Roman legion">legion</a> that fits the same sequence of locations is the <a href="/wiki/Legio_VI_Ferrata" title="Legio VI Ferrata">Legio VI Ferrata</a>, of which <i>ballista</i> would be an auxiliary unit.
</p><p>Mainly known for his writings, Vitruvius was himself an architect. In Roman times architecture was a broader subject than at present including the modern fields of architecture, <a href="/wiki/Construction_management" title="Construction management">construction management</a>, <a href="/wiki/Construction_engineering" title="Construction engineering">construction engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemical_engineering" title="Chemical engineering">chemical engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineering" title="Civil engineering">civil engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Materials_engineering" class="mw-redirect" title="Materials engineering">materials engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_engineering" title="Mechanical engineering">mechanical engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Military_engineering" title="Military engineering">military engineering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">urban planning</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Architectural_engineering" title="Architectural engineering">architectural engineers</a> consider him the first of their discipline, a specialization previously known as technical architecture. <a href="/wiki/Frontinus" title="Frontinus">Frontinus</a> mentions him in connection with the standard sizes of <a href="/wiki/Pipe_(material)#Sizes" class="mw-redirect" title="Pipe (material)">pipes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> He is often credited as father of <a href="/wiki/Architectural_acoustics" title="Architectural acoustics">architectural acoustics</a> for describing the technique of <i><a href="/wiki/Echea" title="Echea">echeas</a></i> placement in theaters.<sup id="cite_ref-Information1974_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Information1974-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The only building, however, that we know Vitruvius to have worked on is one he tells us about,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> a <i><a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">basilica</a></i> completed in 19 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> It was built at Fanum Fortunae, now the modern town of <a href="/wiki/Fano" title="Fano">Fano</a>. The <i>Basilica di Fano</i> (to give the building its Italian name) has disappeared so completely that its very site is a matter of conjecture, although various attempts have been made to visualise it.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The early Christian practice of converting Roman <i>basilicae</i> (public buildings) into cathedrals implies the <i>basilica</i> may be incorporated into the cathedral in Fano.
</p><p>In later years the emperor Augustus, through his sister <a href="/wiki/Octavia_Minor" class="mw-redirect" title="Octavia Minor">Octavia Minor</a>, sponsored Vitruvius, entitling him with what may have been a pension to guarantee financial independence.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Whether <i>De architectura</i> was written by one author or is a compilation completed by subsequent librarians and copyists, remains an open question. The date of his death is unknown, which suggests that he had enjoyed only little popularity during his lifetime.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Gerolamo_Cardano" title="Gerolamo Cardano">Gerolamo Cardano</a>, in his 1552 book <i>De subtilitate rerum</i>, ranks Vitruvius as one of the 12 persons whom he supposes to have excelled all men in the force of genius and invention; and would not have scrupled to have given him the first place, if it could be imagined that he had delivered nothing but his own discoveries.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="De_architectura"><i>De architectura</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vitruvius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: De architectura">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Greekhse1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Greekhse1.jpg/220px-Greekhse1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="125" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Greekhse1.jpg/330px-Greekhse1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Greekhse1.jpg/440px-Greekhse1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1060" data-file-height="603" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Greekhse1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Roman house plan after Vitruvius</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Mathematics_and_architecture" title="Mathematics and architecture">Mathematics and architecture</a></div>
<p>Vitruvius is the author of <i>De architectura</i>, known today as <i>The Ten Books on Architecture</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Vitruvius,_Pollio_1960_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vitruvius,_Pollio_1960-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> a treatise written in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> on architecture, dedicated to the emperor Augustus. In the preface of Book I, Vitruvius dedicates his writings so as to give personal knowledge of the quality of buildings to the emperor. Likely Vitruvius is referring to <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Vipsanius_Agrippa#Life_in_public_service" title="Marcus Vipsanius Agrippa">Marcus Agrippa</a>'s campaign of public repairs and improvements. This work is the only surviving major book on architecture from <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">classical antiquity</a>. According to Petri Liukkonen, this text "influenced deeply from the <a href="/wiki/Early_Renaissance" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Renaissance">Early Renaissance</a> onwards artists, thinkers, and architects, among them <a href="/wiki/Leon_Battista_Alberti" title="Leon Battista Alberti">Leon Battista Alberti</a> (1404–1472), <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a> (1452–1519), and <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a> (1475–1564)."<sup id="cite_ref-bandw_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bandw-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The next major book on architecture, <a href="/wiki/Leone_Battista_Alberti" class="mw-redirect" title="Leone Battista Alberti">Alberti's</a> reformulation of <i>Ten Books</i>, was not written until 1452.
</p><p>Vitruvius is famous for asserting in his book <i>De architectura</i> that a structure must exhibit the three qualities of <i>firmitatis, utilitatis, venustatis</i> – that is, stability, utility, beauty. These are sometimes termed the <b>Vitruvian virtues</b> or the <b>Vitruvian Triad</b>. According to Vitruvius, architecture is an imitation of nature. As birds and bees built their nests, so humans constructed housing from natural materials, that gave them shelter against the elements. When perfecting this art of building, the Greeks invented the architectural orders: <a href="/wiki/Doric_order" title="Doric order">Doric</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ionic_order" title="Ionic order">Ionic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Corinthian_order" title="Corinthian order">Corinthian</a>. It gave them a sense of proportion, culminating in understanding the proportions of the greatest work of art: the human body. This led Vitruvius in defining his <a href="/wiki/Vitruvian_Man" title="Vitruvian Man">Vitruvian Man</a>, as drawn later by <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>: the human body inscribed in the circle and the square (the fundamental geometric patterns of the cosmic order). In this book series, Vitruvius, also wrote about <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> in relation to housing architecture and how to choose locations for cities.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scope">Scope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vitruvius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Scope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vitruvius_the_Ten_Books_on_Architecture_Basilica_at_Fano.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Vitruvius_the_Ten_Books_on_Architecture_Basilica_at_Fano.png/220px-Vitruvius_the_Ten_Books_on_Architecture_Basilica_at_Fano.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="370" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Vitruvius_the_Ten_Books_on_Architecture_Basilica_at_Fano.png/330px-Vitruvius_the_Ten_Books_on_Architecture_Basilica_at_Fano.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Vitruvius_the_Ten_Books_on_Architecture_Basilica_at_Fano.png/440px-Vitruvius_the_Ten_Books_on_Architecture_Basilica_at_Fano.png 2x" data-file-width="1239" data-file-height="2081" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vitruvius_the_Ten_Books_on_Architecture_Basilica_at_Fano.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Vitruvius designed and supervised the construction of this <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">basilica</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fano" title="Fano">Fano</a> (reconstruction). However, many of the other things he did would not now be considered the realm of architecture</div></div></div>
<p>Vitruvius is sometimes loosely referred to as the first architect, but it is more accurate to describe him as the first Roman architect to have written surviving records of his field. He himself cites older but less complete works. He was less an original thinker or creative intellect than a codifier of existing architectural practice. It should also be noted that Vitruvius had a much wider scope than modern architects. <a href="/wiki/Roman_architecture" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman architecture">Roman architects</a> practised a wide variety of disciplines; in modern terms, they could be described as being engineers, architects, <a href="/wiki/Landscape_architects" class="mw-redirect" title="Landscape architects">landscape architects</a>, surveyors, artists, and <a href="/wiki/Artisan" title="Artisan">craftsmen</a> combined. Etymologically the word architect derives from Greek words meaning 'master' and 'builder'. The first of the <i>Ten Books</i> deals with many subjects which now come within the scope of <a href="/wiki/Landscape_architecture" title="Landscape architecture">landscape architecture</a>.
</p><p>In Book I, Chapter 1, titled The Education of the Architect, Vitruvius instructs...
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>1. Architecture is a science arising out of many other sciences, and adorned with much and varied learning; by the help of which a judgment is formed of those works which are the result of other arts. Practice and theory are its parents. Practice is the frequent and continued contemplation of the mode of executing any given work, or of the mere operation of the hands, for the conversion of the material in the best and readiest way. Theory is the result of that reasoning which demonstrates and explains that the material wrought has been so converted as to answer the end proposed.
</p></blockquote>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>2. Wherefore the mere practical architect is not able to assign sufficient reasons for the forms he adopts; and the theoretic architect also fails, grasping the shadow instead of the substance. He who is theoretic as well as practical, is therefore doubly armed; able not only to prove the propriety of his design, but equally so to carry it into execution.<sup id="cite_ref-penelope.uchicago.edu_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-penelope.uchicago.edu-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>He goes on to say that the architect should be versed in drawing, geometry, optics (lighting), history, philosophy, music, theatre, medicine, and law.
</p><p>In Book I, Chapter 3 (<i>The Departments of Architecture</i>), Vitruvius divides architecture into three branches, namely; building; the construction of <a href="/wiki/Sundial" title="Sundial">sundials</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_clocks" class="mw-redirect" title="Water clocks">water clocks</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> and the design and use of machines in construction and warfare.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> He further divides building into public and private. Public building includes city planning, public security structures such as walls, gates and towers; the convenient placing of public facilities such as theatres, forums and markets, baths, roads and pavings; and the construction and position of shrines and temples for religious use.<sup id="cite_ref-penelope.uchicago.edu_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-penelope.uchicago.edu-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Later books are devoted to the understanding, design and construction of each of these.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proportions_of_man">Proportions of man</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vitruvius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Proportions of man">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:De_Architectura030.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/De_Architectura030.jpg/220px-De_Architectura030.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="313" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/De_Architectura030.jpg/330px-De_Architectura030.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/De_Architectura030.jpg/440px-De_Architectura030.jpg 2x" data-file-width="647" data-file-height="921" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:De_Architectura030.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"Vitruvian Man", illustration in the edition of <i>De architectura</i> by Vitruvius; illustrated edition by Cesare Cesariano (1521)</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Da_Vinci_Vitruve_Luc_Viatour.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Da_Vinci_Vitruve_Luc_Viatour.jpg/220px-Da_Vinci_Vitruve_Luc_Viatour.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="299" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Da_Vinci_Vitruve_Luc_Viatour.jpg/330px-Da_Vinci_Vitruve_Luc_Viatour.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Da_Vinci_Vitruve_Luc_Viatour.jpg/440px-Da_Vinci_Vitruve_Luc_Viatour.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2258" data-file-height="3070" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Da_Vinci_Vitruve_Luc_Viatour.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Vitruvian_Man" title="Vitruvian Man">Vitruvian Man</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>, an illustration of the human body inscribed in the circle and the square derived from a passage about geometry and human proportions in Vitruvius' writings</div></div></div>
<p>In Book III, Chapter 1, Paragraph 3, Vitruvius writes about the proportions of man:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>3. Just so the parts of Temples should correspond with each other, and with the whole. The navel is naturally placed in the centre of the human body, and, if in a man lying with his face upward, and his hands and feet extended, from his navel as the centre, a circle be described, it will touch his fingers and toes. It is not alone by a circle, that the human body is thus circumscribed, as may be seen by placing it within a square. For measuring from the feet to the crown of the head, and then across the arms fully extended, we find the latter measure equal to the former; so that lines at right angles to each other, enclosing the figure, will form a square.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>It was upon these writings that <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a> based his <a href="/wiki/Vitruvian_Man" title="Vitruvian Man">Vitruvian Man</a>.
</p><p>Vitruvius described the human figure as being the principal source of proportion.
</p><p>The drawing itself is often used as an implied symbol of the essential <a href="/wiki/Symmetry" title="Symmetry">symmetry</a> of the human body, and by extension, of the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> as a whole.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lists_of_names_given_in_Book_VII_Introduction">Lists of names given in Book VII Introduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vitruvius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lists of names given in Book VII Introduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the introduction to book seven, Vitruvius goes to great lengths to present why he is qualified to write <i>De Architectura</i>. This is the only location in the work where Vitruvius specifically addresses his personal breadth of knowledge. Similar to a modern reference section, the author's position as one who is knowledgeable and educated is established. The topics range across many fields of expertise reflecting that in Roman times as today construction is a diverse field. Vitruvius is clearly a well-read man.
</p><p>In addition to providing his qualification, Vitruvius summarizes a recurring theme throughout the 10 books, a non-trivial and core contribution of his treatise beyond simply being a construction book. Vitruvius makes the point that the work of some of the most talented are unknown, while many of those of lesser talent but greater political position are famous.<sup id="cite_ref-Vitruvius,_Pollio_1960_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vitruvius,_Pollio_1960-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> This theme runs through Vitruvius’s ten books repeatedly – echoing an implicit prediction that he and his works will also be forgotten.
</p><p>Vitruvius illustrates this point by naming what he considers are the most talented individuals in history.<sup id="cite_ref-Vitruvius,_Pollio_1960_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vitruvius,_Pollio_1960-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Implicitly challenging the reader that they have never heard of some of these people, Vitruvius goes on and predicts that some of these individuals will be forgotten and their works <a href="/wiki/Lost_works" class="mw-redirect" title="Lost works">lost</a>, while other, less deserving political characters of history will be forever remembered with pageantry.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rediscovery">Rediscovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vitruvius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Rediscovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Battle_of_Thapsus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Battle_of_Thapsus.jpg/200px-Battle_of_Thapsus.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="128" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Battle_of_Thapsus.jpg/300px-Battle_of_Thapsus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Battle_of_Thapsus.jpg/400px-Battle_of_Thapsus.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1638" data-file-height="1046" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Battle_of_Thapsus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Thapsus" title="Battle of Thapsus">Battle of Thapsus</a> as depicted in an engraving after <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Palladio" title="Andrea Palladio">Andrea Palladio</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Giovanni_Paolo_Panini_-_Interior_of_the_Pantheon,_Rome_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Giovanni_Paolo_Panini_-_Interior_of_the_Pantheon%2C_Rome_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/200px-Giovanni_Paolo_Panini_-_Interior_of_the_Pantheon%2C_Rome_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="261" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Giovanni_Paolo_Panini_-_Interior_of_the_Pantheon%2C_Rome_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/300px-Giovanni_Paolo_Panini_-_Interior_of_the_Pantheon%2C_Rome_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Giovanni_Paolo_Panini_-_Interior_of_the_Pantheon%2C_Rome_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/400px-Giovanni_Paolo_Panini_-_Interior_of_the_Pantheon%2C_Rome_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5401" data-file-height="7045" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Giovanni_Paolo_Panini_-_Interior_of_the_Pantheon,_Rome_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The interior of the <a href="/wiki/Pantheon,_Rome" title="Pantheon, Rome">Pantheon</a> (from an 18th-century painting by <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Paolo_Panini" title="Giovanni Paolo Panini">Panini</a>). Although built after Vitruvius' death, its excellent state of preservation makes it of great importance to those interested in Vitruvian architecture</div></div></div>
<p>Vitruvius' <i><a href="/wiki/De_architectura" title="De architectura">De architectura</a></i> was "rediscovered" in 1414 by the Florentine humanist <a href="/wiki/Poggio_Bracciolini" title="Poggio Bracciolini">Poggio Bracciolini</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Abbey_library_of_Saint_Gall" title="Abbey library of Saint Gall">library of Saint Gall Abbey</a>. <a href="/wiki/Leon_Battista_Alberti" title="Leon Battista Alberti">Leon Battista Alberti</a> (1404–1472) publicised it in his seminal treatise on architecture, <i><a href="/wiki/De_re_aedificatoria" title="De re aedificatoria">De re aedificatoria</a></i> (c. 1450). The first known Latin printed edition was by Fra Giovanni Sulpitius in Rome, 1486.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>  Translations followed in Italian (<a href="/wiki/Cesare_Cesariano" title="Cesare Cesariano">Cesare Cesariano</a>, 1521), French (Jean Martin, 1547<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>), English, German (<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walther_Hermann_Ryff" class="extiw" title="de:Walther Hermann Ryff">Walther H. Ryff</a>, 1543) and Spanish and several other languages. The original illustrations had been lost and the first illustrated edition was published in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> in 1511 with <a href="/wiki/Woodcut" title="Woodcut">woodcut</a> illustrations, based on descriptions in the text by <a href="/wiki/Fra_Giovanni_Giocondo" class="mw-redirect" title="Fra Giovanni Giocondo">Fra Giovanni Giocondo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Later in the 16th-century <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Palladio" title="Andrea Palladio">Andrea Palladio</a> provided illustrations for <a href="/wiki/Daniele_Barbaro" title="Daniele Barbaro">Daniele Barbaro</a>'s commentary on Vitruvius, published in Italian and Latin versions. The most famous illustration is probably Da Vinci's <i><a href="/wiki/Vitruvian_Man" title="Vitruvian Man">Vitruvian Man</a></i>.
</p><p>The surviving ruins of Roman antiquity, the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Forum" title="Roman Forum">Roman Forum</a>, temples, theatres, triumphal arches and their reliefs and statues offered visual examples of the descriptions in the Vitruvian text. Printed and illustrated editions of <i>De Architectura</i> inspired <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_architecture" title="Neoclassical architecture">Neoclassical architecture</a>. <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Brunelleschi" title="Filippo Brunelleschi">Filippo Brunelleschi</a>, for example, invented a new type of <a href="/wiki/Hoist_(device)" title="Hoist (device)">hoist</a> to lift the large stones for the dome of the cathedral in <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a> and was inspired by <i>De Architectura</i> as well as surviving Roman monuments such as the <a href="/wiki/Pantheon_(Rome)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pantheon (Rome)">Pantheon</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Baths_of_Diocletian" title="Baths of Diocletian">Baths of Diocletian</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_technology">Roman technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vitruvius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Roman technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Archscrew2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Archscrew2.jpg/200px-Archscrew2.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="258" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Archscrew2.jpg/300px-Archscrew2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Archscrew2.jpg/400px-Archscrew2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1526" data-file-height="1966" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Archscrew2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Drainage wheel from <a href="/wiki/Rio_Tinto_(river)" title="Rio Tinto (river)">Rio Tinto</a> mines</div></div></div>
<p>Books VIII, IX and X form the basis of much of what we know about Roman technology, now augmented by archaeological studies of extant remains, such as the <a href="/wiki/Water_mill" class="mw-redirect" title="Water mill">water mills</a> at <a href="/wiki/Barbegal" class="mw-redirect" title="Barbegal">Barbegal</a> in France. The other major source of information is the <i><a href="/wiki/Naturalis_Historia" class="mw-redirect" title="Naturalis Historia">Naturalis Historia</a></i> compiled by <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a> much later in c. 75 AD.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Machines">Machines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vitruvius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Machines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The work is important for its descriptions of the many different machines used for engineering structures such as <a href="/wiki/Hoist_(device)" title="Hoist (device)">hoists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crane_(machine)" title="Crane (machine)">cranes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pulley" title="Pulley">pulleys</a>, as well as war machines such as <a href="/wiki/Catapult" title="Catapult">catapults</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ballistae" class="mw-redirect" title="Ballistae">ballistae</a>,</i> and <a href="/wiki/Siege_engine" title="Siege engine">siege engines</a>. As a practising engineer, Vitruvius must be speaking from personal experience rather than simply describing the works of others. He also describes the construction of <a href="/wiki/Sundials" class="mw-redirect" title="Sundials">sundials</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_clocks" class="mw-redirect" title="Water clocks">water clocks</a>, and the use of an <a href="/wiki/Aeolipile" title="Aeolipile">aeolipile</a> (the first <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engine</a>) as an experiment to demonstrate the nature of atmospheric air movements (wind).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aqueducts">Aqueducts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vitruvius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Aqueducts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>His description of <a href="/wiki/Aqueduct_(watercourse)" class="mw-redirect" title="Aqueduct (watercourse)">aqueduct</a> construction includes the way they are surveyed, and the careful choice of materials needed, although <a href="/wiki/Frontinus" title="Frontinus">Frontinus</a> (a general who was appointed in the late 1st century AD to administer the many aqueducts of Rome), writing a century later, gives much more detail of the practical problems involved in their construction and maintenance. Surely Vitruvius' book would have been of great assistance in this. Vitruvius was writing in the 1st century BC when many of the finest <a href="/wiki/Roman_aqueducts" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman aqueducts">Roman aqueducts</a> were built, and survive to this day, such as those at <a href="/wiki/Segovia" title="Segovia">Segovia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pont_du_Gard" title="Pont du Gard">Pont du Gard</a>. The use of the <a href="/wiki/Inverted_siphon" class="mw-redirect" title="Inverted siphon">inverted siphon</a> is described in detail, together with the problems of high pressures developed in the pipe at the base of the siphon, a practical problem with which he seems to be acquainted.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Materials">Materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vitruvius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>He describes many different <a href="/wiki/Construction_material" class="mw-redirect" title="Construction material">construction materials</a> used for a wide variety of different structures, as well as such details as <a href="/wiki/Stucco" title="Stucco">stucco</a> painting. Concrete and <a href="/wiki/Lime_(material)" title="Lime (material)">lime</a> receive in-depth descriptions.
</p><p>Vitruvius is cited as one of the earliest sources to connect lead mining and manufacture, its use in drinking water pipes, and its adverse effects on health. For this reason, he recommended the use of clay pipes and masonry channels in the provision of piped drinking-water.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Vitruvius is the source for the anecdote that credits <a href="/wiki/Archimedes" title="Archimedes">Archimedes</a> with the discovery of the <a href="/wiki/Archimedes%27_principle" title="Archimedes&#39; principle">mass-to-volume ratio</a> while relaxing in his bath. Having been asked to investigate the suspected adulteration of the gold used to make a crown, Archimedes realised that the crown's volume could be measured exactly by its displacement of water, and ran into the street with the cry of<i> <a href="/wiki/Eureka_(word)" title="Eureka (word)">Eureka!</a></i>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dewatering_machines">Dewatering machines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vitruvius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Dewatering machines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:VitruviusTenBooksMHMorgan1914p295.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/VitruviusTenBooksMHMorgan1914p295.gif/200px-VitruviusTenBooksMHMorgan1914p295.gif" decoding="async" width="200" height="80" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/VitruviusTenBooksMHMorgan1914p295.gif/300px-VitruviusTenBooksMHMorgan1914p295.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/81/VitruviusTenBooksMHMorgan1914p295.gif 2x" data-file-width="310" data-file-height="124" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:VitruviusTenBooksMHMorgan1914p295.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Design for an Archimedean water-screw</div></div></div>
<p>He describes the construction of <a href="/wiki/Archimedes%27_screw" title="Archimedes&#39; screw">Archimedes' screw</a> in Chapter X (without mentioning Archimedes by name). It was a device widely used for raising water to irrigate fields and drain mines. Other lifting machines he mentions include the endless chain of buckets and the <a href="/wiki/Reverse_overshot_water-wheel" title="Reverse overshot water-wheel">reverse overshot water-wheel</a>. Remains of the water wheels used for lifting water were discovered when old mines were re-opened at <a href="/wiki/Rio_Tinto_(river)" title="Rio Tinto (river)">Rio Tinto</a> in Spain, <a href="/wiki/Rosia_Montana" class="mw-redirect" title="Rosia Montana">Rosia Montana</a> in Romania and <a href="/wiki/Dolaucothi" class="mw-redirect" title="Dolaucothi">Dolaucothi</a> in west <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>. The Rio Tinto wheel is now shown in the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a>, and the Dolaucothi specimen in the <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="National Museum of Wales">National Museum of Wales</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Surveying_instruments">Surveying instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vitruvius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Surveying instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>That he must have been well practised in surveying is shown by his descriptions of surveying instruments, especially the water level or <i><a href="/wiki/Chorobates" title="Chorobates">chorobates</a>,</i> which he compares favourably with the <i><a href="/wiki/Groma_surveying" title="Groma surveying">groma</a>,</i> a device using <a href="/wiki/Plumb_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Plumb line">plumb lines</a>. They were essential in all building operations, but especially in aqueduct construction, where a uniform gradient was important to the provision of a regular supply of water without damage to the walls of the channel. He also developed one of the first <a href="/wiki/Odometer" title="Odometer">odometers</a>, consisting of a wheel of known circumference that dropped a pebble into a container on every rotation.
</p>

<p>He describes the many innovations made in building design to improve the living conditions of the inhabitants. Foremost among them is the development of the <i><a href="/wiki/Hypocaust" title="Hypocaust">hypocaust</a>,</i> a type of <a href="/wiki/Central_heating" title="Central heating">central heating</a> where hot air developed by a fire was channelled under the floor and inside the walls of <a href="/wiki/Public_bath" class="mw-redirect" title="Public bath">public baths</a> and <a href="/wiki/Villa" title="Villa">villas</a>. He gives explicit instructions how to design such buildings so that <a href="/wiki/Fuel_efficiency" title="Fuel efficiency">fuel efficiency</a> is maximised, so that for example, the <i><a href="/wiki/Caldarium" title="Caldarium">caldarium</a></i> is next to the <i><a href="/wiki/Tepidarium" title="Tepidarium">tepidarium</a></i> followed by the <i><a href="/wiki/Frigidarium" title="Frigidarium">frigidarium</a></i>. He also advises on using a type of regulator to control the heat in the hot rooms, a <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a> disc set into the roof under a circular aperture which could be raised or lowered by a <a href="/wiki/Pulley" title="Pulley">pulley</a> to adjust the ventilation. Although he does not suggest it himself, it is likely that his dewatering devices such as the <a href="/wiki/Reverse_overshot_water-wheel" title="Reverse overshot water-wheel">reverse overshot water-wheel</a> were used in the larger baths to lift water to header tanks at the top of the larger <i>thermae</i>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Baths_of_Diocletian" title="Baths of Diocletian">Baths of Diocletian</a>. The one which was used in Bath of Caracalla for grinding flour.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vitruvius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Vitruvian_Man" title="Vitruvian Man">Vitruvian Man</a> – a drawing by <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Colen_Campbell#Vitruvius_Britannicus" title="Colen Campbell"><i>Vitruvius Britannicus</i></a> – 18th century work on British architecture named after Vitruvius.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Den_Danske_Vitruvius" title="Den Danske Vitruvius">Den Danske Vitruvius</a></i> – 18th century work on Danish architecture – inspired by Vitruvius Britannicus.<sup id="cite_ref-denstoredanske.dk_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-denstoredanske.dk-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>The American Vitruvius</i> – 20th century work on civil architecture by <a href="/wiki/Werner_Hegemann" title="Werner Hegemann">Werner Hegemann</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/William_Vitruvius_Morrison" title="William Vitruvius Morrison">William Vitruvius Morrison</a> (1794–1838), the son of Irish architect <a href="/wiki/Sir_Richard_Morrison" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Richard Morrison">Sir Richard Morrison</a> and himself a noted architect of great houses, bridges, court houses and prisons.</li>
<li>A small <a href="/wiki/Vitruvius_(crater)" title="Vitruvius (crater)">lunar crater</a> has been named after Vitruvius and also an elongated lunar mountain <a href="/wiki/Mons_Vitruvius" title="Mons Vitruvius">Mons Vitruvius</a> close-by.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Design_Quality_Indicator" class="mw-redirect" title="Design Quality Indicator">Design Quality Indicator</a> (DQI) tool for buildings uses Vitruvius's principles.</li></ul>
