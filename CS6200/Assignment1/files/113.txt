<p><b>Haida</b> (<span class="nowrap"><small>English:  </small><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;h&#39; in &#39;hi&#39;">h</span><span title="/aɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;tide&#39;">aɪ</span><span title="&#39;d&#39; in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <a href="/wiki/Haida_language" title="Haida language">Haida</a>: <i lang="hai">X̱aayda</i>, <i lang="hai" title="Haida language text">X̱aadas</i>, <i lang="hai" title="Haida language text">X̱aad</i>, <i lang="hai" title="Haida language text">X̱aat</i>) are a <a href="/wiki/Nation" title="Nation">nation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_group" title="Ethnic group">ethnic group</a> native to, or otherwise associated with, <span lang="hai" style="font-style:normal;" title="Haida language text"><a href="/wiki/Haida_Gwaii" title="Haida Gwaii">Haida Gwaii</a></span> (a Canadian archipelago) and the <a href="/wiki/Haida_language" title="Haida language">Haida language</a>. Haida language, which is an <a href="/wiki/Isolate_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Isolate language">isolate language</a>, has historically been spoken across Haida Gwaii and certain islands on the <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Alaska" title="Southeast Alaska">Alaska Panhandle</a>, where it has been spoken for at least 14,000 years. Prior to the 19th century, Haida would speak a number of <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Pacific_Northwest_Coast" title="Indigenous peoples of the Pacific Northwest Coast">coastal First Nations languages</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Tlingit_language" title="Tlingit language">Tlingit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nisga%27a_language" title="Nisga&#39;a language">Nisg̱a'a</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coast_Tsimshian_dialect" title="Coast Tsimshian dialect">Coast Tsimshian</a>. After <a href="/wiki/History_of_Canada" title="History of Canada">settlers' arrival and colonisation</a> of the Haida through <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Indian_residential_school_system" title="Canadian Indian residential school system">residential schools</a>, few Haida speak the <a href="/wiki/Haida_language" title="Haida language">Haida language</a>, though there are many efforts to revive the language.
</p><p>The Haida national government, the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_the_Haida_Nation" title="Council of the Haida Nation">Council of the Haida Nation (CHN)</a>, is based in the archipelago of <a href="/wiki/Haida_Gwaii" title="Haida Gwaii">Haida Gwaii</a> (formerly the <a href="/wiki/Queen_Charlotte_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen Charlotte Islands">Queen Charlotte Islands</a>) in northern <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, Canada. A group known as the <b>Kaigani Haida</b> live across the international border of the <a href="/wiki/Dixon_Entrance" title="Dixon Entrance">Dixon Entrance</a> on <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Wales_Island_(Alaska)" title="Prince of Wales Island (Alaska)">Prince of Wales Island</a> (<a href="/wiki/Tlingit_language" title="Tlingit language">Tlingit</a>: <i lang="tli">Taan</i>) in <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Alaska" title="Southeast Alaska">Southeast Alaska</a>, United States; <i>Taan</i> was traditionally and still is in Tlingit territory. The Kaigani Haida migrated there in the late 18th century. Haida have occupied Haida Gwaii since at least 14,000 <a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">BP</a>. Pollen fossils and oral histories both confirm that Haida ancestors were present when the first tree, a <a href="/wiki/Pinus_contorta" title="Pinus contorta">Lodgepole pine</a>, arrived at <span lang="hai" style="font-style:normal;" title="Haida language text">SG̱uuluu Jaads Saahlawaay</span>, the westernmost of the Swan Islands located in <span lang="hai" style="font-style:normal;" title="Haida language text"><a href="/wiki/Gwaii_Haanas_National_Park_Reserve_and_Haida_Heritage_Site" title="Gwaii Haanas National Park Reserve and Haida Heritage Site">Gwaii Haanas</a></span>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In British Columbia, the term "Haida Nation" can refer both to Haida people as a whole and their government, the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_the_Haida_Nation" title="Council of the Haida Nation">Council of the Haida Nation</a>. While all people of Haida ancestry are entitled to Haida citizenship, the Kaigani are also part of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Council_Tlingit_Haida_Indian_Tribes_of_Alaska&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Central Council Tlingit Haida Indian Tribes of Alaska (page does not exist)">Central Council Tlingit Haida Indian Tribes of Alaska</a> government.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Haida_language" title="Haida language">Haida language</a> has sometimes been classified as one of the <a href="/wiki/Na-Dene_languages" title="Na-Dene languages">Na-Dene group</a>, but is usually considered to be an <a href="/wiki/Language_isolate" title="Language isolate">isolate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Schoonmaker_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schoonmaker-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Haida society continues to produce a robust and highly stylized art form, a leading component of <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Coast_art" title="Northwest Coast art">Northwest Coast art</a>. While artists frequently have expressed this in large wooden carvings (<a href="/wiki/Totem_poles" class="mw-redirect" title="Totem poles">totem poles</a>), <a href="/wiki/Chilkat_weaving" title="Chilkat weaving">Chilkat weaving</a>, or ornate jewellery, in the 21st century, younger people are also making art in popular expression such as <a href="/wiki/Haida_manga" title="Haida manga">Haida manga</a>.
</p><p>In 2018 the first feature-length Haida-language film, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Edge_of_the_Knife" class="mw-redirect" title="The Edge of the Knife">The Edge of the Knife</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Haida_language" title="Haida language">Haida</a>: <i lang="hai">SG̲aawaay Ḵʹuuna</i>), was released, with an all-Haida cast. The actors learned Haida for their performances in the film, with a 2-week training camp followed by lessons throughout the 5 weeks of filming. Haida artist <a href="/wiki/Gwaai_Edenshaw" title="Gwaai Edenshaw">Gwaai Edenshaw</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tsilhqot%27in" title="Tsilhqot&#39;in">Tsilhqot'in</a> filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Helen_Haig-Brown" title="Helen Haig-Brown">Helen Haig-Brown</a> directed, with Edenshaw and his brother being co-screenwriters, with Graham Richard and <a href="/wiki/Leonie_Sandercock" title="Leonie Sandercock">Leonie Sandercock</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-haidafilm_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-haidafilm-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Haida_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gaahlaay.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Gaahlaay.jpg/220px-Gaahlaay.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Gaahlaay.jpg/330px-Gaahlaay.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Gaahlaay.jpg/440px-Gaahlaay.jpg 2x" data-file-width="960" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gaahlaay.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Haida hereditary leader of the eagle moiety awaits guests with his nephew on the shores of his territory.</div></div></div>
<p>Traditional Haida territory spans the current international boundary between <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, Canada and <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, United States. Their heartland is the two large and many smaller islands known as <a href="/wiki/Haida_Gwaii" title="Haida Gwaii">Haida Gwaii</a>, which means "island of the people" in Haida. This archipelago was surveyed in 1787 by Captain <a href="/wiki/George_Dixon_(captain)" class="mw-redirect" title="George Dixon (captain)">George Dixon</a> of the <a href="/wiki/British_Navy" class="mw-redirect" title="British Navy">British Navy</a>, who named them after one of his ships, the <i><a href="/wiki/Queen_Charlotte_(1785_ship)" title="Queen Charlotte (1785 ship)">Queen Charlotte</a></i>, which was in turn named after <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_of_Mecklenburg-Strelitz" title="Charlotte of Mecklenburg-Strelitz">Charlotte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Queen_consort" title="Queen consort">queen consort</a> of <a href="/wiki/George_III_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George III of the United Kingdom">George III of the United Kingdom</a>. The name "Queen Charlotte Islands" was subsequently "given back" to <a href="/wiki/The_Crown" title="The Crown">the Crown</a> in a ceremony between the British Columbia government and the Council of the Haida Nation.
</p><p>Haida also live in <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Alaska" title="Southeast Alaska">Southeast Alaska</a>, particularly on the southern half of <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Wales_Island_(Alaska)" title="Prince of Wales Island (Alaska)">Prince of Wales Island</a> in communities such as <a href="/wiki/Hydaburg,_Alaska" title="Hydaburg, Alaska">Hydaburg</a>, and in large cities elsewhere in the region such as <a href="/wiki/Ketchikan,_Alaska" title="Ketchikan, Alaska">Ketchikan</a>. Haida also live in various cities in mainland British Columbia and the western United States.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Haida_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
 <div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Haida_Houses.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Haida_Houses.jpg/220px-Haida_Houses.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="189" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Haida_Houses.jpg/330px-Haida_Houses.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/79/Haida_Houses.jpg 2x" data-file-width="440" data-file-height="378" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Haida_Houses.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Houses and totem poles, 1878</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_west_coast_of_North_America" title="History of the west coast of North America">History of the west coast of North America</a></div>
<p>The Haida are known for their craftsmanship, trading skills, and seamanship. They are thought to have been warlike and <a href="/wiki/History_of_slavery#Among_indigenous_peoples" title="History of slavery">to practise slavery</a>. <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Museum_of_Civilization" class="mw-redirect" title="Canadian Museum of Civilization">Canadian Museum of Civilization</a> anthropologist Diamond Jenness has compared the tribe to <a href="/wiki/Vikings" title="Vikings">Vikings</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Warfare_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Warfare-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Oral_history" title="Oral history">Oral histories</a> and archaeological evidence indicate that the Haida have occupied Haida Gwaii for more than 17,000 years. In that time they have established an intimate connection with the islands' lands and oceans, established highly structured societies, and constructed many villages.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The Haida have also occupied present-day southern Alaska for more than the last 200 years, the modern group having emigrated from Haida Gwaii in the 18th century.
</p><p>The Haida conducted regular trade with Russian, Spanish, British, and American fur traders and whalers. According to sailing records, they diligently maintained strong trade relationships with Westerners, coastal people, and among themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Like other groups on the Northwest Coast, the Haida defended themselves with fortifications, including <a href="/wiki/Palisade" title="Palisade">palisades</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trapdoor" title="Trapdoor">trapdoors</a> and platforms. They took to water in <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest_canoes" title="Pacific Northwest canoes">large ocean-going canoes</a>, each created from a single <a href="/wiki/Thuja_plicata" title="Thuja plicata">Western red cedar</a> tree, and big enough to accommodate as many as 60 paddlers. The aggressive tribe were particularly feared in sea battles, although they did respect <a href="/wiki/Rules_of_engagement" title="Rules of engagement">rules of engagement</a> in their conflicts.<sup id="cite_ref-Warfare_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Warfare-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The Haida developed effective weapons for boat-based battle, including a special system of stone rings weighing 18 to 23&#160;kg (40 to 51&#160;lb) which could destroy an enemy's <a href="/wiki/Dugout_(boat)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dugout (boat)">dugout canoe</a> and be reused after the attacker pulled it back with the attached cedar bark rope. The Haida took captives from defeated enemies. Between 1780 and 1830, the Haida turned their aggression towards European and American traders. Among the half-dozen ships the tribe captured were the <i>Eleanor</i> and the <i>Susan Sturgis</i>. The tribe made use of the weapons they so acquired, using cannons and canoe-mounted <a href="/wiki/Swivel_gun" title="Swivel gun">swivel guns</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Warfare_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Warfare-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Welcoming_Party.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Welcoming_Party.jpg/220px-Welcoming_Party.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Welcoming_Party.jpg/330px-Welcoming_Party.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Welcoming_Party.jpg/440px-Welcoming_Party.jpg 2x" data-file-width="960" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Welcoming_Party.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Haida drummers and singers greet guests on the shores of Ḵay Linagaay, a thousands-of-years-old village in Haida Gwaii.</div></div></div>
<p>In 1856, an expedition in search of a route across <a href="/wiki/Vancouver_Island" title="Vancouver Island">Vancouver Island</a> was at the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Qualicum_River" title="Qualicum River">Qualicum River</a> when they observed a large fleet of Haida canoes approaching and hid in the forest. They observed these attackers holding human heads. When the explorers reached the mouth of the river, they came upon the charred remains of the village of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Qualicum_people&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Qualicum people (page does not exist)">Qualicum people</a> and the mutilated bodies of its inhabitants, with only one survivor, an elderly woman, hiding terrified inside a tree stump.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Also in 1857, the <a href="/wiki/USS_Massachusetts_(1845)" title="USS Massachusetts (1845)">USS&#160;<i>Massachusetts</i></a> was sent from Seattle to nearby Port Gamble, where indigenous raiding parties made up of Haida (from territory claimed by the British) and Tongass (from territory claimed by the Russians) had been attacking and enslaving the <a href="/wiki/Coast_Salish_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Coast Salish people">Coast Salish people</a> there. When the Haida and Tongass (sea lion tribe <a href="/wiki/Tlingit" title="Tlingit">Tlingit</a>) warriors refused to acknowledge American jurisdiction and to hand over those among them who had attacked the <a href="/wiki/Puget_Sound" title="Puget Sound">Puget Sound</a> communities, a battle ensued in which 26 natives and one government soldier were killed. In the aftermath of this, <a href="/wiki/Colonel_Isaac_Ebey" class="mw-redirect" title="Colonel Isaac Ebey">Colonel Isaac Ebey</a>, a US military officer and the first settler on <a href="/wiki/Whidbey_Island" title="Whidbey Island">Whidbey Island</a>, was shot and beheaded on 11 August 1857 by a small Tlingit group from <a href="/wiki/Kake,_Alaska" title="Kake, Alaska">Kake, Alaska</a>, in retaliation for the killing of a respected Kake chief in the raid the year before. Ebey's scalp was purchased from the Kake by an American trader in 1860.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/History_of_smallpox#Epidemics_in_the_Americas" title="History of smallpox">The introduction of smallpox</a> among the Haida at Victoria in March 1862 significantly reduced their sovereignty over their traditional territories, and opened the doorway to colonial power.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> As many as nine in ten Haidas died of smallpox and <a href="/wiki/Population_history_of_indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas#Depopulation_from_disease" title="Population history of indigenous peoples of the Americas">many villages were completely depopulated</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Model_of_House_of_Contentment,_late_19th_century,_05.589.7791.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Model_of_House_of_Contentment%2C_late_19th_century%2C_05.589.7791.jpg/220px-Model_of_House_of_Contentment%2C_late_19th_century%2C_05.589.7791.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="268" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Model_of_House_of_Contentment%2C_late_19th_century%2C_05.589.7791.jpg/330px-Model_of_House_of_Contentment%2C_late_19th_century%2C_05.589.7791.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Model_of_House_of_Contentment%2C_late_19th_century%2C_05.589.7791.jpg/440px-Model_of_House_of_Contentment%2C_late_19th_century%2C_05.589.7791.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1259" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Model_of_House_of_Contentment,_late_19th_century,_05.589.7791.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Model of House of Contentment, late 19th century, <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Museum" title="Brooklyn Museum">Brooklyn Museum</a></div></div></div>
<p>In 1885 the Haida <a href="/wiki/Potlatch" title="Potlatch">potlatch</a> (<a href="/wiki/Haida_language" title="Haida language">Haida</a>: <i lang="hai">waahlgahl</i>) was outlawed under the <a href="/wiki/Potlatch_Ban" class="mw-redirect" title="Potlatch Ban">Potlatch Ban</a>. The elimination of the potlatch system destroyed financial relationships and seriously interrupted the cultural heritage of coastal people.
</p><p>The Haida also created "notions of wealth", and Jenness credits them with the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Totem_pole" title="Totem pole">totem pole</a> (Haida: <i><i lang="hai" title="Haida language text">ǥyaagang</i></i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Bentwood" title="Bentwood">bentwood</a> box.<sup id="cite_ref-Warfare_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Warfare-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Missionaries regarded the carved poles as <a href="/wiki/Idolatry" title="Idolatry">graven images</a> rather than representations of the family histories that wove Haida society together. Chiefly families showed their histories by erecting totems outside their homes, or on house posts forming the building.  Their social organization was matrilineal.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> As the islands were Christianized, many cultural works such as totem posts were destroyed or taken to museums around the world. This significantly undermined Haida self-knowledge and further diminished morale.
</p><p>The government began forcibly sending some Haida children to <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Indian_residential_school_system" title="Canadian Indian residential school system">residential schools</a> as early as 1911. Haida children were sent as far away as Alberta to live among English-speaking families where they were to be assimilated into the dominant culture.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Warfare">Warfare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Haida_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Warfare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prior to contact with <a href="/wiki/Europeans" class="mw-redirect" title="Europeans">Europeans</a>, other Indigenous communities regarded the Haida as aggressive warriors and made attempts to avoid sea battles with them. Archeological evidence shows that Northwest coast tribes, to which the Haida belong, engaged in warfare as early as 10 000 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Though the Haida were more likely to participate in sea battles, it was not uncommon for them to engage in hand-to-hand combat or long-range attacks.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Archeological_and_written_evidence_of_warfare">Archeological and written evidence of warfare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Haida_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Archeological and written evidence of warfare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Analyses of skeletal injuries dating from the <a href="/wiki/Archaic_period_(North_America)" title="Archaic period (North America)">Archaic period</a> show that Northwest coast nations, particularly in the North where most Haida communities were situated, engaged in battles of some sort, though the number of battles is unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The presence of defensive <a href="/wiki/Fortification" title="Fortification">fortifications</a> dating from the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Middle_Pacific_period&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Middle Pacific period (page does not exist)">Middle Pacific period</a> show that the incidence of battles rose somewhere between 1800 BC and AD 500.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> These fortifications continued to be in use during the 18th century as evidenced by <a href="/wiki/James_Cook" title="James Cook">Captain James Cook’</a>s discovery of one such hilltop fortification in a Haida village. Numerous other sightings of such fortifications were recorded by other European explorers during this century.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes_of_warfare">Causes of warfare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Haida_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Causes of warfare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There were multiple reasons that motivated Haida people to commit warfare. Various accounts explain that the Haida went to battle more for revenge and slaves than for anything else.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> According to the anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Margaret_B._Blackman" title="Margaret B. Blackman">Margaret Blackman</a>, who has done research on the Haida since the 1970s, warfare on Haida Gwaii was primarily motivated by revenge. Many Northwest coast legends tell of Haida communities raiding and fighting with neighbouring communities because of insults.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Other causes included disputes over property, territory, resources, trade routes and even women. However, a battle between a Haida community and another often did not have simply one cause. In fact, many battles were the result of decades old disputes.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The Haida, like many of the Northwest coast Indigenous communities, engaged in <a href="/wiki/Slave_raiding" title="Slave raiding">slave-raiding</a> as slaves were highly sought after for their use as labour as well as bodyguards and warriors.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> During the 19th century, the Haida fought physically with other Indigenous communities to ensure domination of <a href="/wiki/North_American_fur_trade" title="North American fur trade">the fur trade</a> with European merchants.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Haida groups also had feuds with these European merchants that could last years. In 1789, some Haidas were accused of stealing items from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Captain_Kendrick&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Captain Kendrick (page does not exist)">Captain Kendrick</a>, most of which included drying linen. Kendrick seized two Haida chiefs and threatened to kill them via cannon-fire if they did not return the stolen items. Though the Haida community complied at the time, less than two years later 100 to 200 of its people attacked the same ship.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="War_Parties">War Parties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Haida_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: War Parties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The missionary <a href="/wiki/William_Collison" title="William Collison">W.H. Collison</a> describes having seen a Haida fleet of around forty canoes.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> However, he does not provide the number of warriors in these canoes, and there are no other known accounts that describe the number of warriors in a war party. The structure of a Haida war party generally followed that of the community itself, the only difference being that the chief took the lead during battles; otherwise his title was more or less meaningless.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Medicine_man" title="Medicine man">Medicine men</a> were often brought along raids or before battles to “destroy the souls of enemies” and ensure victory.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_in_battle">Death in battle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Haida_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Death in battle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Battles between a group of Haida warriors and another community sometimes resulted in the annihilation of either one or both of the groups involved.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Entire villages would be burned down during a battle which was a common practice during Northwest coast battles.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The Haida burned their warriors who died in battles, though it is not known if this act was done after each battle or only after battles in which they were victorious.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The Haida believed that fallen warriors went to the <a href="/w/index.php?title=House_of_Sun&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="House of Sun (page does not exist)">House of Sun</a>, which was considered a highly honourable death. For this reason, a specially made military suit for chiefs was prepared if they fell in battle. The slaves belonging to the chiefs who died in battle were burned with them.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weapons_used_in_battles">Weapons used in battles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Haida_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Weapons used in battles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Haida used the <a href="/wiki/Bow_and_arrow" title="Bow and arrow">bow and arrow</a> until it was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Firearm" title="Firearm">firearms</a> acquired from Europeans in the 19th century, but other traditional weapons were still preferred.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The weapons that the Haida used were often multi-functional; they were used not only in battle, but during other activities as well. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Dagger" title="Dagger">daggers</a> were very common and almost always the choice of weapon for hand-to-hand combat, and were also used during hunting and to create other tools. One medicine man's dagger that <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Mackenzie_(explorer)" title="Alexander Mackenzie (explorer)">Alexander Mackenzie</a> came across during his exploration of Haida Gwaii, was used both for fights and to hold the medicine man's hair up.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Another dagger that Mackenzie obtained from a Haida village was said to be connected to a Haida legend; many daggers had individual histories which made them unique from one another.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Battle_armour">Battle armour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Haida_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Battle armour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Haida wore <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rod-and-slat&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rod-and-slat (page does not exist)">rod-and-slat</a> <a href="/wiki/Armour" title="Armour">armour</a>. This meant <a href="/wiki/Greave" title="Greave">greaves</a> for the thighs and lower back and slats (a long strip of wood) in the side pieces to allow for more flexibility during movement. They wore <a href="/wiki/Elk" title="Elk">elk</a> hide <a href="/wiki/Tunic" title="Tunic">tunics</a> under their armour and wooden helmets. Arrows could not penetrate this armour, and <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> explorers found that bullets could only penetrate the armour if shot from a distance of less than 20 feet. The Haida rarely used shields because of their developed armour.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Calendar">Calendar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Haida_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Calendar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lisa_telford_haida.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Lisa_telford_haida.jpg/220px-Lisa_telford_haida.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/ab/Lisa_telford_haida.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="288" data-file-height="432" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lisa_telford_haida.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Lisa_Telford" title="Lisa Telford">Lisa Telford</a>, Haida basket weaver</div></div></div>
<p>The Haidas' <a href="/wiki/Calendar" title="Calendar">calendar</a>:
</p>
<ul><li>April/May- Gansgee 7laa kongaas</li>
<li>May/Early June- Wa.aay gwaalgee</li>
<li>June/July- Kong koaas</li>
<li>July/August- Sgaana gyaas</li>
<li>August/September- K'ijaas</li>
<li>September/October- K'alayaa Kongaas</li>
<li>October/November- K'eed adii</li>
<li>November/December- Jid Kongaas</li>
<li>December/January- Kong gyaangaas</li>
<li>January/February- Hlgiduum kongaas</li>
<li>February/March- Taan kongaas</li>
<li>March- Xiid gayaas</li>
<li>April- Wiid gyaas</li></ul>
