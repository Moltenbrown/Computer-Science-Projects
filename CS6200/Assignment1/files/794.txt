<p>In <a href="/wiki/Art_history" title="Art history">art history</a>, <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cultural_studies" title="Cultural studies">cultural studies</a>, <b>Orientalism</b> is the imitation or depiction of aspects in the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_world" title="Eastern world">Eastern world</a>. These depictions are usually done by writers, designers, and artists from the West. In particular, <b>Orientalist painting</b>, depicting more specifically "the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>",<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> was one of the many specialisms of 19th-century <a href="/wiki/Academic_art" title="Academic art">academic art</a>, and the literature of Western countries took a similar interest in Oriental themes.
</p><p>Since the publication of <a href="/wiki/Edward_Said" title="Edward Said">Edward Said</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Orientalism_(book)" title="Orientalism (book)">Orientalism</a></i> in 1978, much academic discourse has begun to use the term "Orientalism" to refer to a general patronizing Western attitude towards Middle Eastern, Asian, and North African societies. In Said's analysis, the West <a href="/wiki/Essentialism" title="Essentialism">essentializes</a> these societies as static and undeveloped—thereby fabricating a view of Oriental culture that can be studied, depicted, and reproduced. Implicit in this fabrication, writes Said, is the idea that Western society is developed, rational, flexible, and superior.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orientalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Orientalism refers to the <a href="/wiki/Orient" title="Orient">Orient</a>, in reference and opposition to the <a href="/wiki/Occident" class="mw-redirect" title="Occident">Occident</a>; the East and the West, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The word Orient entered the English language as the <a href="/wiki/Middle_French" title="Middle French">Middle French</a> <i>orient</i>. The root word <i>oriēns</i>, from the Latin <i>Oriēns</i>, has synonymous denotations: The eastern part of the world; the sky whence comes the sun; the east; the rising sun, etc.; yet the denotation changed as a term of geography. In the "<a href="/wiki/Monk%27s_Tale" class="mw-redirect" title="Monk&#39;s Tale">Monk's Tale</a>" (1375), <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Chaucer" title="Geoffrey Chaucer">Geoffrey Chaucer</a> wrote: "That they conquered many regnes grete / In the orient, with many a fair citee." The term "orient" refers to countries east of the Mediterranean Sea and Southern Europe. <i>In Place of Fear</i> (1952), <a href="/wiki/Aneurin_Bevan" title="Aneurin Bevan">Aneurin Bevan</a> used an expanded denotation of the Orient that comprehended <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a>: "the awakening of the Orient under the impact of Western ideas". Edward Said said that Orientalism "enables the political, economic, cultural and social domination of the West, not just during colonial times, but also in the present."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Art">Art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orientalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In art history, the term Orientalism refers to the works of the Western artists who specialized in Oriental subjects, produced from their travels in <a href="/wiki/Western_Asia" title="Western Asia">Western Asia</a>, during the 19th century. In that time, artists and scholars were described as Orientalists, especially in France, where the dismissive use of the term "Orientalist" was made popular by the art critic <a href="/wiki/Jules-Antoine_Castagnary" title="Jules-Antoine Castagnary">Jules-Antoine Castagnary</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Despite such social disdain for a style of <a href="/wiki/Representation_(arts)" title="Representation (arts)">representational art</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Soci%C3%A9t%C3%A9_des_Peintres_Orientalistes_Fran%C3%A7ais" title="Société des Peintres Orientalistes Français">French Society of Orientalist Painters</a> was founded in 1893, with <a href="/wiki/Jean-L%C3%A9on_G%C3%A9r%C3%B4me" title="Jean-Léon Gérôme">Jean-Léon Gérôme</a> as the honorary president;<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> whereas in Britain, the term Orientalist identified "an artist".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The formation of the French Orientalist Painters Society changed the consciousness of practitioners towards the end of the 19th century, since artists could now see themselves as part of a distinct art movement.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> As an art movement, Orientalist painting is generally treated as one of the many branches of 19th-century <a href="/wiki/Academic_art" title="Academic art">academic art</a>; however, many different styles of Orientalist art were in evidence. Art historians tend to identify two broad types of Orientalist artist: the realists who carefully painted what they observed and those who imagined Orientalist scenes without ever leaving the studio.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> French painters such as <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix" title="Eugène Delacroix">Eugène Delacroix</a> (1798–1863) and <a href="/wiki/Jean-L%C3%A9on_G%C3%A9r%C3%B4me" title="Jean-Léon Gérôme">Jean-Léon Gérôme</a> (1824–1904) are widely regarded as the leading luminaries of the Orientalist movement.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oriental_studies">Oriental studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orientalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Oriental studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 18th and 19th centuries, the term <a href="/wiki/Oriental_studies" title="Oriental studies">Orientalist</a> identified a scholar who specialized in the languages and literatures of the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_world" title="Eastern world">Eastern world</a>. Among such scholars were British officials of the <a href="/wiki/East_India_Company" title="East India Company">East India Company</a>, who said that the <a href="/wiki/Arab_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab culture">Arab culture</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_India" title="Culture of India">culture of India</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_culture" title="Islamic culture">Islamic cultures</a> should be studied as equal to the cultures of Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Among such scholars is the philologist <a href="/wiki/William_Jones_(philologist)" title="William Jones (philologist)">William Jones</a>, whose studies of <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European languages</a> established modern <a href="/wiki/Philology" title="Philology">philology</a>. British <a href="/wiki/Company_rule_in_India" title="Company rule in India">imperial strategy in India</a> favored Orientalism as a technique for developing good relations with the natives—until the 1820s, when the influence of "anglicists" such as <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Babington_Macaulay" title="Thomas Babington Macaulay">Thomas Babington Macaulay</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Stuart_Mill" title="John Stuart Mill">John Stuart Mill</a> led to the promotion of Anglocentric education.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Additionally, <a href="/wiki/Hebraists" class="mw-redirect" title="Hebraists">Hebraism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jewish_studies" title="Jewish studies">Jewish studies</a> gained popularity among British and German scholars in the 19th and 20th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The academic field of <a href="/wiki/Oriental_studies" title="Oriental studies">Oriental studies</a>, which comprehended the cultures of the <a href="/wiki/Near_East" title="Near East">Near East</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Far_East" title="Far East">Far East</a>, became the fields of <a href="/wiki/Asian_studies" title="Asian studies">Asian studies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Middle_Eastern_studies" title="Middle Eastern studies">Middle Eastern studies</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_studies">Critical studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orientalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Critical studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the book <i><a href="/wiki/Orientalism_(book)" title="Orientalism (book)">Orientalism</a></i> (1978), the cultural critic Edward Said redefined the term Orientalism to describe a pervasive Western tradition — academic and artistic — of prejudiced outsider-interpretations of the Eastern world, which was shaped by the cultural attitudes of European <a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">imperialism</a> in the 18th and 19th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The thesis of <i>Orientalism</i> develops <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Gramsci" title="Antonio Gramsci">Antonio Gramsci</a>'s theory of <a href="/wiki/Cultural_hegemony" title="Cultural hegemony">cultural hegemony</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Michel_Foucault" title="Michel Foucault">Michel Foucault</a>'s theorisation of <a href="/wiki/Discourse" title="Discourse">discourse</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Knowledge" title="Knowledge">knowledge</a>-and-<a href="/wiki/Power_(social_and_political)" title="Power (social and political)">power</a> relation) to criticise the scholarly tradition of Oriental studies. Said criticised contemporary scholars who perpetuated the tradition of outsider-interpretation of <a href="/wiki/Arab" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab">Arabo</a>-Islamic cultures, especially <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Lewis" title="Bernard Lewis">Bernard Lewis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fouad_Ajami" title="Fouad Ajami">Fouad Ajami</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The analyses are of Orientalism in European literature, especially French literature, and do not analyse <a href="/wiki/Visual_art" class="mw-redirect" title="Visual art">visual art</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orientalist_painting" class="mw-redirect" title="Orientalist painting">Orientalist painting</a>. In that vein, the art historian <a href="/wiki/Linda_Nochlin" title="Linda Nochlin">Linda Nochlin</a> applied Said's methods of critical analysis to art, "with uneven results".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Academy" title="Academy">academy</a>, the book <i>Orientalism</i> (1978) became a foundational text of <a href="/wiki/Postcolonialism" title="Postcolonialism">post-colonial cultural studies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Moreover, in relation to the cultural institution of <a href="/wiki/Citizenship" title="Citizenship">citizenship</a>, Orientalism has rendered the concept of <i>citizenship</i> as a problem of <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemology</a>, because citizenship originated as a social institution of the Western world; as such, the problem of defining citizenship reconfigures the idea of <i>Europe</i> in time of crises.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Furthermore, Said said that Orientalism, as an "idea of representation is a theoretical one: The Orient is a stage on which the whole East is confined" in order to make the Eastern world "less fearsome to the West";<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> that the developing world, primarily the West, is the cause of <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">colonialism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Moreover, in <i>Empire: A Very Short Introduction</i> (2000), Stephen Howe agreed with Said that Western nations and their empires were created by the exploitation of underdeveloped countries, by the extraction of wealth and labour from one country to another country.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There's also a critical trend within the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_world" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic world">Islamic world</a>, and in 2002 it was estimated that in <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a> alone there have been, penned by local or foreign scholars, around 200 books critical of Orientalism as well as some 2000 articles.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_European_architecture_and_design">In European architecture and design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orientalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In European architecture and design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Orientalism_in_early_modern_France" title="Orientalism in early modern France">Orientalism in early modern France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turquerie" title="Turquerie">Turquerie</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Moresque" title="Moresque">Moresque</a> style of <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> <a href="/wiki/Ornament_(art)" title="Ornament (art)">ornament</a> is a European adaptation of the Islamic <a href="/wiki/Arabesque" title="Arabesque">arabesque</a> that began in the late 15th century and was to be used in some types of work, such as <a href="/wiki/Bookbinding" title="Bookbinding">bookbinding</a>, until almost the present day. Early architectural use of motifs lifted from the Indian subcontinent is known as <a href="/wiki/Indo-Saracenic_Revival_architecture" class="mw-redirect" title="Indo-Saracenic Revival architecture">Indo-Saracenic Revival architecture</a>. One of the earliest examples is the façade of <a href="/wiki/Guildhall,_London" title="Guildhall, London">Guildhall, London</a> (1788–1789). The style gained momentum in the west with the publication of views of India by <a href="/wiki/William_Hodges" title="William Hodges">William Hodges</a>, and <a href="/wiki/William_Daniell" title="William Daniell">William</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Daniell" title="Thomas Daniell">Thomas Daniell</a> from about 1795. Examples of "Hindoo" architecture are <a href="/wiki/Sezincote_House" title="Sezincote House">Sezincote House</a> (c. 1805) in <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a>, built for a <a href="/wiki/Nabob" title="Nabob">nabob</a> returned from <a href="/wiki/Bengal" title="Bengal">Bengal</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Pavilion" title="Royal Pavilion">Royal Pavilion</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brighton" title="Brighton">Brighton</a>.
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Turquerie" title="Turquerie">Turquerie</a></i>, which began as early as the late 15th century, continued until at least the 18th century, and included both the use of "Turkish" styles in the decorative arts, the adoption of Turkish costume at times, and interest in art depicting the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> itself. Venice, the traditional trading partner of the Ottomans, was the earliest centre, with France becoming more prominent in the 18th century.
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Chinoiserie" title="Chinoiserie">Chinoiserie</a></i> is the catch-all term for the fashion for Chinese themes in decoration in Western Europe, beginning in the late 17th century and peaking in waves, especially <a href="/wiki/Rococo" title="Rococo">Rococo</a> Chinoiserie, <i>c.</i> 1740–1770. From the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> to the 18th century, Western designers attempted to imitate the technical sophistication of Chinese ceramics with only partial success. Early hints of Chinoiserie appeared in the 17th century in nations with active East India companies: England (the <a href="/wiki/East_India_Company" title="East India Company">East India Company</a>), Denmark (the <a href="/wiki/Danish_East_India_Company" title="Danish East India Company">Danish East India Company</a>), the Netherlands (the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_India_Company" title="Dutch East India Company">Dutch East India Company</a>) and France (the <a href="/wiki/French_East_India_Company" title="French East India Company">French East India Company</a>). Tin-glazed pottery made at <a href="/wiki/Delft" title="Delft">Delft</a> and other Dutch towns adopted genuine <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming</a>-era <a href="/wiki/Blue_and_white_porcelain" class="mw-redirect" title="Blue and white porcelain">blue and white porcelain</a> from the early 17th century. Early ceramic wares made at <a href="/wiki/Meissen" title="Meissen">Meissen</a> and other centers of true <a href="/wiki/Porcelain" title="Porcelain">porcelain</a> imitated Chinese shapes for dishes, vases and <a href="/wiki/Teaware" title="Teaware">teawares</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Chinese_export_porcelain" title="Chinese export porcelain">Chinese export porcelain</a>).
</p><p>Pleasure pavilions in "Chinese taste" appeared in the formal parterres of late Baroque and Rococo German palaces, and in tile panels at <a href="/wiki/Aranjuez" title="Aranjuez">Aranjuez</a> near <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a>. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Chippendale" title="Thomas Chippendale">Thomas Chippendale</a>'s mahogany tea tables and china cabinets, especially, were embellished with fretwork glazing and railings, <i>c.</i> 1753–70. Sober <i>homages</i> to early Xing scholars' furnishings were also naturalized, as the <i>tang</i> evolved into a mid-Georgian side table and squared slat-back armchairs that suited English gentlemen as well as Chinese scholars. Not every adaptation of Chinese design principles falls within mainstream "chinoiserie". Chinoiserie media included imitations of lacquer and painted tin (tôle) ware that imitated japanning, early painted wallpapers in sheets, and ceramic figurines and table ornaments. Small <a href="/wiki/Pagoda" title="Pagoda">pagodas</a> appeared on chimneypieces and full-sized ones in gardens. <a href="/wiki/Kew" title="Kew">Kew</a> has a magnificent garden pagoda designed by <a href="/wiki/William_Chambers_(architect)" title="William Chambers (architect)">William Chambers</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Wilhelma" title="Wilhelma">Wilhelma</a> (1846) in <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a> is an example of <a href="/wiki/Moorish_Revival_architecture" title="Moorish Revival architecture">Moorish Revival architecture</a>. <a href="/wiki/Leighton_House_Museum" title="Leighton House Museum">Leighton House</a>, built for the artist <a href="/wiki/Frederic_Leighton" title="Frederic Leighton">Frederic Leighton</a>, has a conventional facade but elaborate Arab-style interiors, including original Islamic tiles and other elements as well as Victorian Orientalizing work.
</p><p>After 1860, <i><a href="/wiki/Japonism" title="Japonism">Japonism</a>,</i> sparked by the importing of <a href="/wiki/Ukiyo-e" title="Ukiyo-e">ukiyo-e</a>, became an important influence in the western arts. In particular, many modern French artists such as <a href="/wiki/Claude_Monet" title="Claude Monet">Claude Monet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Degas" title="Edgar Degas">Edgar Degas</a> were influenced by the Japanese style. <a href="/wiki/Mary_Cassatt" title="Mary Cassatt">Mary Cassatt</a>, an American artist who worked in France, used elements of combined patterns, flat planes and shifting perspective of Japanese prints in her own images.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The paintings of <a href="/wiki/James_Abbott_McNeill_Whistler" title="James Abbott McNeill Whistler">James Abbott McNeill Whistler</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Peacock_Room" title="The Peacock Room">The Peacock Room</a></i> demonstrated how he used aspects of Japanese tradition and are some of the finest works of the genre. California architects <a href="/wiki/Greene_and_Greene" title="Greene and Greene">Greene and Greene</a> were inspired by Japanese elements in their design of the <a href="/wiki/Gamble_House_(Pasadena,_California)" title="Gamble House (Pasadena, California)">Gamble House</a> and other buildings.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Egyptian_Revival_architecture" title="Egyptian Revival architecture">Egyptian Revival architecture</a> became popular in the early and mid-19th century and continued as a minor style into the early 20th century. <a href="/wiki/Moorish_Revival_architecture" title="Moorish Revival architecture">Moorish Revival architecture</a> began in the early 19th century in the German states and was particularly popular for building synagogues. <a href="/wiki/Indo-Saracenic_Revival_architecture" class="mw-redirect" title="Indo-Saracenic Revival architecture">Indo-Saracenic Revival architecture</a> was a genre that arose in the late 19th century in the <a href="/wiki/British_Raj" title="British Raj">British Raj</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orientalist_art">Orientalist art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orientalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Orientalist art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-19th_century">Pre-19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orientalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pre-19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gentile_Bellini_Madonna_and_Child_Enthroned_late_15th_century.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Gentile_Bellini_Madonna_and_Child_Enthroned_late_15th_century.jpg/220px-Gentile_Bellini_Madonna_and_Child_Enthroned_late_15th_century.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="322" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Gentile_Bellini_Madonna_and_Child_Enthroned_late_15th_century.jpg/330px-Gentile_Bellini_Madonna_and_Child_Enthroned_late_15th_century.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Gentile_Bellini_Madonna_and_Child_Enthroned_late_15th_century.jpg/440px-Gentile_Bellini_Madonna_and_Child_Enthroned_late_15th_century.jpg 2x" data-file-width="582" data-file-height="853" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gentile_Bellini_Madonna_and_Child_Enthroned_late_15th_century.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A "Bellini type" <a href="/wiki/Islamic" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic">Islamic</a> <a href="/wiki/Prayer_rug" title="Prayer rug">prayer rug</a>, seen from the top, at the feet of the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Mary" class="mw-redirect" title="Virgin Mary">Virgin Mary</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Gentile_Bellini" title="Gentile Bellini">Gentile Bellini</a>'s <i>Madonna and Child Enthroned</i>, late 15th century</div></div></div>
<p>Depictions of Islamic "<a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moors</a>" and "Turks" (imprecisely named <a href="/wiki/Muslim" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> groups of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Europe" title="Southern Europe">southern Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Asia" class="mw-redirect" title="West Asia">West Asia</a>) can be found in Medieval, Renaissance, and Baroque art. In Biblical scenes in <a href="/wiki/Early_Netherlandish_painting" title="Early Netherlandish painting">Early Netherlandish painting</a>, secondary figures, especially Romans, were given exotic costumes that distantly reflected the clothes of the <a href="/wiki/Near_East" title="Near East">Near East</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Biblical_Magi" title="Biblical Magi">Three Magi</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nativity_of_Jesus_in_art" title="Nativity of Jesus in art">Nativity scenes</a> were an especial focus for this. In general art with Biblical settings would not be considered as Orientalist except where contemporary or historicist Middle Eastern detail or settings is a feature of works, as with some paintings by <a href="/wiki/Gentile_Bellini" title="Gentile Bellini">Gentile Bellini</a> and others, and a number of 19th-century works. Renaissance <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> had a phase of particular interest in depictions of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> in painting and <a href="/wiki/Old_master_print" title="Old master print">prints</a>. Gentile Bellini, who travelled to <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> and painted the Sultan, and <a href="/wiki/Vittore_Carpaccio" title="Vittore Carpaccio">Vittore Carpaccio</a> were the leading painters. By then the depictions were more accurate, with men typically dressed all in white. The depiction of <a href="/wiki/Oriental_carpets_in_Renaissance_painting" title="Oriental carpets in Renaissance painting">Oriental carpets in Renaissance painting</a> sometimes draws from Orientalist interest, but more often just reflects the prestige these expensive objects had in the period.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Jean-%C3%89tienne_Liotard" title="Jean-Étienne Liotard">Jean-Étienne Liotard</a> (1702–1789) visited <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a> and painted numerous <a href="/wiki/Pastel" title="Pastel">pastels</a> of Turkish domestic scenes; he also continued to wear Turkish attire for much of the time when he was back in Europe. The ambitious Scottish 18th-century artist <a href="/wiki/Gavin_Hamilton_(artist)" title="Gavin Hamilton (artist)">Gavin Hamilton</a> found a solution to the problem of using modern dress, considered unheroic and inelegant, in <a href="/wiki/History_painting" title="History painting">history painting</a> by using Middle Eastern settings with Europeans wearing local costume, as travelers were advised to do. His huge <i>James Dawkins and Robert Wood Discovering the Ruins of <a href="/wiki/Palmyra" title="Palmyra">Palmyra</a></i> (1758, now Edinburgh) elevates tourism to the heroic, with the two travelers wearing what look very like <a href="/wiki/Toga" title="Toga">togas</a>. Many travelers had themselves painted in exotic Eastern dress on their return, including <a href="/wiki/Lord_Byron" title="Lord Byron">Lord Byron</a>, as did many who had never left Europe, including <a href="/wiki/Madame_de_Pompadour" title="Madame de Pompadour">Madame de Pompadour</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The growing French interest in exotic Oriental luxury and lack of liberty in the 18th century to some extent reflected a pointed analogy with France's own <a href="/wiki/Absolute_monarchy" title="Absolute monarchy">absolute monarchy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-McCabe2008_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McCabe2008-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Byron's poetry was highly influential in introducing Europe to the heady cocktail of <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romanticism</a> in exotic Oriental settings which was to dominate 19th century Oriental art.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="French_Orientalism">French Orientalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orientalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: French Orientalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Leon_Cogniet_-_L_Expedition_D_Egypte_Sous_Les_Ordres_De_Bonaparte.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Leon_Cogniet_-_L_Expedition_D_Egypte_Sous_Les_Ordres_De_Bonaparte.jpg/240px-Leon_Cogniet_-_L_Expedition_D_Egypte_Sous_Les_Ordres_De_Bonaparte.jpg" decoding="async" width="240" height="154" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Leon_Cogniet_-_L_Expedition_D_Egypte_Sous_Les_Ordres_De_Bonaparte.jpg/360px-Leon_Cogniet_-_L_Expedition_D_Egypte_Sous_Les_Ordres_De_Bonaparte.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Leon_Cogniet_-_L_Expedition_D_Egypte_Sous_Les_Ordres_De_Bonaparte.jpg/480px-Leon_Cogniet_-_L_Expedition_D_Egypte_Sous_Les_Ordres_De_Bonaparte.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1400" data-file-height="899" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Leon_Cogniet_-_L_Expedition_D_Egypte_Sous_Les_Ordres_De_Bonaparte.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9on_Cogniet" title="Léon Cogniet">Léon Cogniet</a>, <i>The 1798 Egyptian Expedition Under the Command of Bonaparte</i> (1835; <a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_du_Louvre" class="mw-redirect" title="Musée du Louvre">Musée du Louvre</a>).</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ferdinand-Victor-Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix,_French_-_The_Death_of_Sardanapalus_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Ferdinand-Victor-Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix%2C_French_-_The_Death_of_Sardanapalus_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/240px-Ferdinand-Victor-Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix%2C_French_-_The_Death_of_Sardanapalus_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" decoding="async" width="240" height="189" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Ferdinand-Victor-Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix%2C_French_-_The_Death_of_Sardanapalus_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/360px-Ferdinand-Victor-Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix%2C_French_-_The_Death_of_Sardanapalus_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Ferdinand-Victor-Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix%2C_French_-_The_Death_of_Sardanapalus_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/480px-Ferdinand-Victor-Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix%2C_French_-_The_Death_of_Sardanapalus_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5593" data-file-height="4409" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ferdinand-Victor-Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix,_French_-_The_Death_of_Sardanapalus_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix" title="Eugène Delacroix">Eugène Delacroix</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Death_of_Sardanapalus" title="The Death of Sardanapalus">The Death of Sardanapalus</a></i> (1827; <a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_du_Louvre" class="mw-redirect" title="Musée du Louvre">Musée du Louvre</a>).</div></div></div>
<p>French Orientalist painting was transformed by <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>'s ultimately unsuccessful <a href="/wiki/French_campaign_in_Egypt_and_Syria" title="French campaign in Egypt and Syria">invasion of Egypt and Syria</a> in 1798–1801, which stimulated great public interest in <a href="/wiki/Egyptology" title="Egyptology">Egyptology</a>, and was also recorded in subsequent years by Napoleon's court painters, especially <a href="/wiki/Antoine-Jean_Gros" title="Antoine-Jean Gros">Antoine-Jean Gros</a>, although the Middle Eastern campaign was not one on which he accompanied the army. Two of his most successful paintings, <i><a href="/wiki/Bonaparte_Visiting_the_Plague_Victims_of_Jaffa" title="Bonaparte Visiting the Plague Victims of Jaffa">Bonaparte Visiting the Plague Victims of Jaffa</a></i> (1804) and <i><a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Abukir_(1799)" title="Battle of Abukir (1799)">Battle of Abukir</a></i> (1806) focus on the Emperor, as he was by then, but include many Egyptian figures, as does the less effective <i>Napoleon at the Battle of the Pyramids</i> (1810). <a href="/wiki/Anne-Louis_Girodet_de_Roussy-Trioson" title="Anne-Louis Girodet de Roussy-Trioson">Anne-Louis Girodet de Roussy-Trioson</a>'s <i>La Révolte du Caire</i> (1810) was another large and prominent example. A well-illustrated <i>Description de l'Égypte</i> was published by the French Government in twenty volumes between 1809 and 1828, concentrating on <a href="/wiki/Antiquities" title="Antiquities">antiquities</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix" title="Eugène Delacroix">Eugène Delacroix</a>'s first great success, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Massacre_at_Chios" title="The Massacre at Chios">The Massacre at Chios</a></i> (1824) was painted before he visited Greece or the East, and followed his friend <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9odore_G%C3%A9ricault" title="Théodore Géricault">Théodore Géricault</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Raft_of_the_Medusa" title="The Raft of the Medusa">The Raft of the Medusa</a></i> in showing a recent incident in distant parts that had aroused public opinion. <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> was still fighting for independence from the Ottomans, and was effectively as exotic as the more Near Eastern parts of the empire. Delacroix followed up with <i><a href="/wiki/Greece_on_the_Ruins_of_Missolonghi" title="Greece on the Ruins of Missolonghi">Greece on the Ruins of Missolonghi</a></i> (1827), commemorating a siege of the previous year, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Death_of_Sardanapalus" title="The Death of Sardanapalus">The Death of Sardanapalus</a></i>, inspired by <a href="/wiki/Lord_Byron" title="Lord Byron">Lord Byron</a>, which although set in antiquity has been credited with beginning the mixture of sex, violence, lassitude and exoticism which runs through much French Orientalist painting.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> In 1832, Delacroix finally visited what is now <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>, recently conquered by the French, and <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, as part of a diplomatic mission to the <a href="/wiki/Sultan_of_Morocco" class="mw-redirect" title="Sultan of Morocco">Sultan of Morocco</a>. He was greatly struck by what he saw, comparing the North African way of life to that of the Ancient Romans, and continued to paint subjects from his trip on his return to France. Like many later Orientalist painters, he was frustrated by the difficulty of sketching women, and many of his scenes featured <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a> or warriors on horses. However, he was apparently able to get into the women's' quarters or <a href="/wiki/Harem" title="Harem">harem</a> of a house to sketch what became <i><a href="/wiki/Women_of_Algiers" title="Women of Algiers">Women of Algiers</a></i>; few later harem scenes had this claim to authenticity.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Le_Bain_Turc,_by_Jean_Auguste_Dominique_Ingres,_from_C2RMF_retouched.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Le_Bain_Turc%2C_by_Jean_Auguste_Dominique_Ingres%2C_from_C2RMF_retouched.jpg/240px-Le_Bain_Turc%2C_by_Jean_Auguste_Dominique_Ingres%2C_from_C2RMF_retouched.jpg" decoding="async" width="240" height="239" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Le_Bain_Turc%2C_by_Jean_Auguste_Dominique_Ingres%2C_from_C2RMF_retouched.jpg/360px-Le_Bain_Turc%2C_by_Jean_Auguste_Dominique_Ingres%2C_from_C2RMF_retouched.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Le_Bain_Turc%2C_by_Jean_Auguste_Dominique_Ingres%2C_from_C2RMF_retouched.jpg/480px-Le_Bain_Turc%2C_by_Jean_Auguste_Dominique_Ingres%2C_from_C2RMF_retouched.jpg 2x" data-file-width="15556" data-file-height="15488" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Le_Bain_Turc,_by_Jean_Auguste_Dominique_Ingres,_from_C2RMF_retouched.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Jean Auguste Dominique Ingres, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Turkish_Bath" title="The Turkish Bath">The Turkish Bath</a></i>, 1862</div></div></div>
<p>When Ingres, the director of the French <i>Académie de peinture</i>, painted a highly colored vision of a <i><a href="/wiki/Turkish_bath" title="Turkish bath">Turkish bath</a></i>, he made his eroticized Orient publicly acceptable by his diffuse generalizing of the female forms (who might all have been the same model). More open sensuality was seen as acceptable in the exotic Orient.<sup id="cite_ref-Tromans,_135_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tromans,_135-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> This imagery persisted in art into the early 20th century, as evidenced in <a href="/wiki/Henri_Matisse" title="Henri Matisse">Henri Matisse</a>'s orientalist semi-nudes from his Nice period, and his use of Oriental costumes and patterns. Ingres' pupil <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9odore_Chass%C3%A9riau" title="Théodore Chassériau">Théodore Chassériau</a> (1819–1856) had already achieved success with his nude <i><a href="/wiki/The_Toilette_of_Esther" title="The Toilette of Esther">The Toilette of Esther</a></i> (1841, <a href="/wiki/Louvre" title="Louvre">Louvre</a>) and equestrian portrait of <i>Ali-Ben-Hamet, Caliph of Constantine and Chief of the Haractas, Followed by his Escort</i> (1846) before he first visited the East, but in later decades the <a href="/wiki/Steamship" title="Steamship">steamship</a> made travel much easier and increasing numbers of artists traveled to the Middle East and beyond, painting a wide range of Oriental scenes.
</p><p>In many of these works, they portrayed the Orient as exotic, colorful and sensual, not to say <a href="/wiki/Stereotype" title="Stereotype">stereotyped</a>. Such works typically concentrated on Arab, Jewish, and other Semitic cultures, as those were the ones visited by artists as France became more engaged in North Africa. French artists such as <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix" title="Eugène Delacroix">Eugène Delacroix</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean-L%C3%A9on_G%C3%A9r%C3%B4me" title="Jean-Léon Gérôme">Jean-Léon Gérôme</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jean-Auguste-Dominique_Ingres" title="Jean-Auguste-Dominique Ingres">Jean-Auguste-Dominique Ingres</a> painted many works depicting Islamic culture, often including lounging <a href="/wiki/Odalisque" title="Odalisque">odalisques</a>. They stressed both lassitude and visual spectacle. Other scenes, especially in <a href="/wiki/Genre_painting" title="Genre painting">genre painting</a>, have been seen as either closely comparable to their equivalents set in modern-day or historical Europe, or as also reflecting an Orientalist mind-set in the Saidian sense of the term. Gérôme was the precursor, and often the master, of a number of French painters in the later part of the century whose works were often frankly salacious, frequently featuring scenes in harems, public baths and slave auctions (the last two also available with classical decor), and responsible, with others, for "the equation of Orientalism with the nude in pornographic mode";<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> (<i>Gallery, below</i>)
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_Orientalism">British Orientalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orientalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: British Orientalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:192px;"><a href="/wiki/File:William_Holman_Hunt_002.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/William_Holman_Hunt_002.jpg/190px-William_Holman_Hunt_002.jpg" decoding="async" width="190" height="304" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/William_Holman_Hunt_002.jpg/285px-William_Holman_Hunt_002.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/William_Holman_Hunt_002.jpg/380px-William_Holman_Hunt_002.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="1600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:William_Holman_Hunt_002.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/William_Holman_Hunt" title="William Holman Hunt">William Holman Hunt</a>, <i>A Street Scene in Cairo; The Lantern-Maker's Courtship</i>, 1854–61</div></div></div>
<p>Though British political interest in the territories of the unravelling Ottoman Empire was as intense as in France, it was mostly more discreetly exercised. The origins of British Orientalist 19th-century painting owe more to religion than military conquest or the search for plausible locations for naked women. The leading British <a href="/wiki/Genre_painter" class="mw-redirect" title="Genre painter">genre painter</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Wilkie_(artist)" title="David Wilkie (artist)">Sir David Wilkie</a> was 55 when he travelled to <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a> in 1840, dying off <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a> during the return voyage. Though not noted as a religious painter, Wilkie made the trip with a <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> agenda to reform religious painting, as he believed that: "a <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a> in painting is as much called for as in theology, to sweep away the abuses by which our divine pursuit is encumbered", by which he meant traditional Christian <a href="/wiki/Iconography" title="Iconography">iconography</a>. He hoped to find more authentic settings and decor for Biblical subjects at their original location, though his death prevented more than studies being made. Other artists including the <a href="/wiki/Pre-Raphaelite" class="mw-redirect" title="Pre-Raphaelite">Pre-Raphaelite</a> <a href="/wiki/William_Holman_Hunt" title="William Holman Hunt">William Holman Hunt</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Roberts_(painter)" title="David Roberts (painter)">David Roberts</a> had similar motivations,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> giving an emphasis on realism in British Orientalist art from the start.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The French artist <a href="/wiki/James_Tissot" title="James Tissot">James Tissot</a> also used contemporary Middle Eastern landscape and decor for Biblical subjects, with little regard for historical costumes or other fittings.
</p><p>William Holman Hunt produced a number of major paintings of Biblical subjects drawing on his Middle Eastern travels, improvising variants of contemporary Arab costume and furnishings to avoid specifically Islamic styles, and also some landscapes and genre subjects. The biblical subjects included <i><a href="/wiki/The_Scapegoat_(painting)" title="The Scapegoat (painting)">The Scapegoat</a></i> (1856), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Finding_of_the_Saviour_in_the_Temple" title="The Finding of the Saviour in the Temple">The Finding of the Saviour in the Temple</a></i> (1860), and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Shadow_of_Death" title="The Shadow of Death">The Shadow of Death</a></i> (1871). <i><a href="/wiki/The_Miracle_of_the_Holy_Fire_(painting)" title="The Miracle of the Holy Fire (painting)">The Miracle of the Holy Fire</a></i> (1899) was intended as a picturesque satire on the local Eastern Christians, of whom, like most English visitors, Hunt took a very dim view. His <i>A Street Scene in Cairo; The Lantern-Maker's Courtship</i> (1854–61) is a rare contemporary narrative scene, as the young man feels his fiancé's face, which he is not allowed to see, through her veil, as a Westerner in the background beats his way up the street with his stick.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> This a rare intrusion of a clearly contemporary figure into an Orientalist scene; mostly they claim the picturesqueness of the historical painting so popular at the time, without the trouble of researching authentic costumes and settings.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Geromeslavemarket.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Geromeslavemarket.jpg/220px-Geromeslavemarket.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="295" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Geromeslavemarket.jpg/330px-Geromeslavemarket.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Geromeslavemarket.jpg/440px-Geromeslavemarket.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1162" data-file-height="1560" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Geromeslavemarket.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/The_Slave_Market_(G%C3%A9r%C3%B4me_painting)" title="The Slave Market (Gérôme painting)">The Slave Market</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Jean-L%C3%A9on_G%C3%A9r%C3%B4me" title="Jean-Léon Gérôme">Jean-Léon Gérôme</a>, 1866; a popular subject, normally with a sexual element.</div></div></div>  
<p>When Gérôme exhibited <i>For Sale; Slaves at Cairo</i> at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Academy">Royal Academy</a> in London in 1871, it was "widely found offensive", perhaps partly because the British liked to think they had successfully suppressed the <a href="/wiki/Slave_trade" class="mw-redirect" title="Slave trade">slave trade</a> in Egypt, also for cruelty and "representing fleshiness for its own sake".<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> But Rana Kabbani believes that "French Orientalist painting, as exemplified by the works of Gérôme, may appear more sensual, gaudy, gory and sexually explicit than its British counterpart, but this is a difference of style not substance ... Similar strains of fascination and repulsion convulsed their artists"<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Nonetheless, nudity and violence are more evident in British paintings set in the ancient world, and "the iconography of the <i><a href="/wiki/Odalisque" title="Odalisque">odalisque</a></i> ... the Oriental <a href="/wiki/Sex_slave" class="mw-redirect" title="Sex slave">sex slave</a> whose image is offered up to the viewer as freely as she herself supposedly was to her master – is almost entirely French in origin",<sup id="cite_ref-Tromans,_135_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tromans,_135-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> though taken up with enthusiasm by Italian and other painters.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/John_Frederick_Lewis" title="John Frederick Lewis">John Frederick Lewis</a>, who lived for several years in a traditional mansion in <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a>, painted highly detailed works showing both realistic <a href="/wiki/Genre_painting" title="Genre painting">genre scenes</a> of Middle Eastern life and more idealized scenes in upper class Egyptian interiors with no traces of Western cultural influence yet apparent. His careful and seemingly affectionate representation of Islamic architecture, furnishings, screens, and costumes set new standards of realism, which influenced other artists, including Gérôme in his later works. He "never painted a nude", and his wife modelled for several of his harem scenes,<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> which, with the rare examples by the classicist painter <a href="/wiki/Lord_Leighton" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Leighton">Lord Leighton</a>, imagine "the harem as a place of almost English domesticity, ... [where]... women's fully clothed respectability suggests a moral healthiness to go with their natural good looks".<sup id="cite_ref-Tromans,_135_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tromans,_135-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Other artists concentrated on <a href="/wiki/Landscape_painting" title="Landscape painting">landscape painting</a>, often of desert scenes, including <a href="/wiki/Richard_Dadd" title="Richard Dadd">Richard Dadd</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edward_Lear" title="Edward Lear">Edward Lear</a>. <a href="/wiki/David_Roberts_(painter)" title="David Roberts (painter)">David Roberts</a> (1796–1864) produced architectural and landscape views, many of <a href="/wiki/Antiquities" title="Antiquities">antiquities</a>, and published very successful books of <a href="/wiki/Lithograph" class="mw-redirect" title="Lithograph">lithographs</a> from them.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>

<p>Russian Orientalist art was largely concerned with the areas of <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> that Russia was conquering during the century, and also in historical painting with the <a href="/wiki/Mongol" class="mw-redirect" title="Mongol">Mongols</a> who had dominated Russia for much of the Middle Ages, who were rarely shown in a good light. <a href="/wiki/Nationalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Nationalist">Nationalist</a> historical painting in <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a> dwelt on Turkish oppression, with <a href="/wiki/Battle_scene" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle scene">battle scenes</a> and maidens about to be raped.
</p><p>The Saidian analysis has not prevented a strong revival of interest in, and collecting of, 19th century Orientalist works since the 1970s, the latter was in large part led by Middle Eastern buyers.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Authors and composers are not commonly referred to as "Orientalist" in the way that artists are, and relatively few specialized in Oriental topics or styles, or are even best known for their works including them. But many major figures, from Mozart to Flaubert, have produced significant works with Oriental subjects or treatments. <a href="/wiki/Lord_Byron" title="Lord Byron">Lord Byron</a> with his four long "Turkish tales" in poetry, is one of the most important writers to make exotic fantasy Oriental settings a significant theme in the literature of <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romanticism</a>. Verdi's opera <i>Aida</i> (1871) is set in Egypt as portrayed through the content and the visual spectacle. "Aida" depicts a militaristic Egypt's tyranny over Ethiopia.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Irish Orientalism had a particular character, drawing on various beliefs about early historical links between Ireland and the East, few of which are now regarded as historically correct. The mythical <a href="/wiki/Milesians_(Irish)" title="Milesians (Irish)">Milesians</a> are one example of this. The Irish were also conscious of the views of other nations seeing them as comparably backward to the East, and Europe's "backyard Orient".<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_music">In music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orientalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: In music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Turkish_music_(style)" title="Turkish music (style)">Turkish music (style)</a></div>
<p>In music, Orientalism may be applied to styles occurring in different periods, such as the <i>alla Turca</i>, used by multiple composers including <a href="/wiki/Mozart" class="mw-redirect" title="Mozart">Mozart</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beethoven" class="mw-redirect" title="Beethoven">Beethoven</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Beard_and_Gloag_2005,_129_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beard_and_Gloag_2005,_129-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> The American musicologist <a href="/wiki/Richard_Taruskin" title="Richard Taruskin">Richard Taruskin</a> has identified in 19th-century Russian music a strain of Orientalism: "the East as a sign or metaphor, as imaginary geography, as historical fiction, as the reduced and totalized other against which we construct our (not less reduced and totalized) sense of ourselves".<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Taruskin concedes Russian composers, unlike those in France and Germany, felt an "ambivalence" to the theme since "Russia was a contiguous empire in which Europeans, living side by side with 'orientals', identified (and intermarried) with them far more than in the case of other colonial powers".<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Nonetheless, Taruskin characterizes Orientalism in Romantic Russian music as having melodies "full of close little ornaments and melismas",<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> chromatic accompanying lines, drone bass<sup id="cite_ref-taruskin165_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taruskin165-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>—characteristics which were used by <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Glinka" title="Mikhail Glinka">Glinka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mily_Balakirev" title="Mily Balakirev">Balakirev</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Borodin" title="Alexander Borodin">Borodin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Rimsky-Korsakov" title="Nikolai Rimsky-Korsakov">Rimsky-Korsakov</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Lyapunov" title="Sergei Lyapunov">Lyapunov</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Rachmaninoff" title="Sergei Rachmaninoff">Rachmaninov</a>. These musical characteristics evoke "not just the East, but the seductive East that emasculates, enslaves, renders passive. In a word, it signifies the promise of the experience of <i>nega</i>, a prime attribute of the orient as imagined by the Russians. ... In opera and song, <i>nega</i> often simply denotes S-E-X a la russe, desired or achieved."<sup id="cite_ref-taruskin165_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taruskin165-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Orientalism is also traceable in music that is considered to have effects of <a href="/wiki/Exoticism" title="Exoticism">exoticism</a>, including the <i><a href="/wiki/Japonisme" class="mw-redirect" title="Japonisme">japonisme</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/Claude_Debussy" title="Claude Debussy">Claude Debussy</a>'s piano music all the way to the <a href="/wiki/Sitar" title="Sitar">sitar</a> being used in recordings by <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">the Beatles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Beard_and_Gloag_2005,_129_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beard_and_Gloag_2005,_129-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United Kingdom, <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Holst" title="Gustav Holst">Gustav Holst</a> composed <i><a href="/wiki/Beni_Mora" title="Beni Mora">Beni Mora</a></i> evoking a languid, heady Arabian atmosphere.
</p><p>Orientalism, in a more <i>camp</i> fashion also found its way into <a href="/wiki/Exotica" title="Exotica">exotica</a> music in the late 1950s, especially the works of <a href="/wiki/Les_Baxter" title="Les Baxter">Les Baxter</a>, for example, his composition "City of Veils".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_literature">In literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orientalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: In literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Romantic movement in literature began in 1785 and ended around 1830. The term "Romantic" references the ideas and culture that writers of the time reflected in their work. During this time, the culture and objects of the East began to have a profound effect on Europe. Extensive traveling by artists and members of the European elite brought travelogues and sensational tales back to the West creating a great interest in all things "foreign". Romantic Orientalism incorporates African and Asian geographic locations, well-known colonial and "native" personalities, folklore, and philosophies to create a literary environment of colonial exploration from a distinctly European worldview. The current trend in analysis of this movement references a belief in this literature as a mode to justify European colonial endeavors with the expansion of territory.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In his novel <i><a href="/wiki/Salammb%C3%B4_(novel)" class="mw-redirect" title="Salammbô (novel)">Salammbô</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Flaubert" title="Gustave Flaubert">Gustave Flaubert</a> used ancient <a href="/wiki/Carthage" title="Carthage">Carthage</a> in North Africa as a <a href="/wiki/Foil_(literature)" title="Foil (literature)">foil</a> to ancient <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>. He portrayed its culture as morally corrupting and suffused with dangerously alluring eroticism. This novel proved hugely influential on later portrayals of ancient <a href="/wiki/Semitic_cultures" class="mw-redirect" title="Semitic cultures">Semitic cultures</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_film">In film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orientalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: In film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Said argues that the continuity of Orientalism into the present can be found in influential images, particularly through the <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_the_United_States" title="Cinema of the United States">Cinema of the United States</a>, as the West has now grown to include the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> Many <a href="/wiki/Blockbuster_(entertainment)" title="Blockbuster (entertainment)">blockbuster</a> feature film, such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Indiana_Jones_(franchise)" title="Indiana Jones (franchise)">Indiana Jones</a></i> series, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mummy_(franchise)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Mummy (franchise)">The Mummy</a></i> films, and Disney's <i><a href="/wiki/Aladdin_(franchise)" title="Aladdin (franchise)">Aladdin</a></i> film series demonstrate the imagined geographies of the East.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> The films usually portray the lead heroic characters as being from the Western world, while the villains often come from the East.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_50-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> The representation of the Orient has continued in film, although this representation does not necessarily have any truth to it.
</p><p>The overly sexualized character of <a href="/wiki/Princess_Jasmine" title="Princess Jasmine">Princess Jasmine</a> in <i>Aladdin</i> is simply a continuation of the paintings from the 19th century, where women were represented as erotic, sexualized fantasies.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Teahouse_of_the_August_Moon_(film)" title="The Teahouse of the August Moon (film)">The Tea House of the August Moon</a></i> (1956), as argued by Pedro Iacobelli, there are tropes of orientalism. He notes, that the film "tells us more about the Americans and the American's image of Okinawa rather than about the Okinawan people".<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> The film characterizes the Okinawans as "merry but backward" and "de-politicized", which ignored the real-life Okinawan political protests over forceful land acquisition by the American military at the time.
</p><p>Kimiko Akita, in "Orientalism and the Binary of Fact and Fiction in Memoirs of a Geisha", argues that <i><a href="/wiki/Memoirs_of_a_Geisha_(film)" title="Memoirs of a Geisha (film)">Memoirs of a Geisha</a></i> (2005) contains orientalist tropes and deep "cultural misrepresentations". She states that <i>Memoirs of a Geisha</i> "reinforces the idea of Japanese culture and geisha as exotic, backward, irrational, dirty, profane, promiscuous, bizarre, and enigmatic".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_dance">In dance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orientalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: In dance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the Romantic period of the 19th century, <a href="/wiki/Ballet" title="Ballet">ballet</a> developed a preoccupation with the exotic. This exoticism ranged from ballets set in Scotland to those based on ethereal creatures. By the later part of the century, ballets were capturing the presumed essence of the mysterious East. These ballets often included sexual themes and tended to be based on assumptions of people rather than on concrete facts. Orientalism is apparent in numerous ballets.
</p><p>The Orient motivated several major ballets, which have survived since the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Corsaire" title="Le Corsaire">Le Corsaire</a></i> premiered in 1856 at the Paris Opera, with choreography by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Mazilier" title="Joseph Mazilier">Joseph Mazilier</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-abt_corsaire_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abt_corsaire-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Marius_Petipa" title="Marius Petipa">Marius Petipa</a> re-choreographed the ballet for the Maryinsky Ballet in St. Petersburg, Russia in 1899.<sup id="cite_ref-abt_corsaire_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abt_corsaire-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> Its complex storyline, loosely based on Lord Byron's <a href="/wiki/The_Corsair" title="The Corsair">poem</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> takes place in Turkey and focuses on a love story between a pirate and a beautiful slave girl. Scenes include a bazaar where women are sold to men as slaves, and the Pasha's Palace, which features his harem of wives.<sup id="cite_ref-abt_corsaire_53-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abt_corsaire-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> In 1877, Marius Petipa choreographed <i><a href="/wiki/La_Bayad%C3%A8re" title="La Bayadère">La Bayadère</a></i>, the love story of an Indian temple dancer and Indian warrior. This ballet was based on Kalidasa's play <i><a href="/wiki/Shakuntala_(play)" title="Shakuntala (play)">Sakuntala</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Au_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Au-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> <i>La Bayadere</i> used vaguely Indian costuming, and incorporated Indian inspired hand gestures into classical ballet. In addition, it included a 'Hindu Dance,' motivated by Kathak, an Indian dance form.<sup id="cite_ref-Au_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Au-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Another ballet, <i>Sheherazade</i>, choreographed by <a href="/wiki/Michel_Fokine" title="Michel Fokine">Michel Fokine</a> in 1910 to music by <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Rimsky-Korsakov" title="Nikolai Rimsky-Korsakov">Nikolai Rimsky-Korsakov</a>, is a story involving a shah's wife and her illicit relations with a Golden Slave, originally played by <a href="/wiki/Vaslav_Nijinsky" title="Vaslav Nijinsky">Vaslav Nijinsky</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Au_55-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Au-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> The ballet's controversial fixation on sex includes an orgy in an oriental harem. When the shah discovers the actions of his numerous wives and their lovers, he orders the deaths of those involved.<sup id="cite_ref-Au_55-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Au-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> <i>Sheherazade</i> was loosely based on folktales of questionable authenticity.
</p><p>Several lesser-known ballets of the late nineteenth and early twentieth century also reveal Orientalism. For instance, in Petipa's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pharaoh%27s_Daughter" title="The Pharaoh&#39;s Daughter">The Pharaoh's Daughter</a></i> (1862), an Englishman imagines himself, in an opium-induced dream, as an Egyptian boy who wins the love of the Pharaoh's daughter, Aspicia.<sup id="cite_ref-Au_55-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Au-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Aspicia's costume consisted of 'Egyptian' décor on a <a href="/wiki/Tutu_(clothing)" title="Tutu (clothing)">tutu</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Au_55-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Au-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Another ballet, Hippolyte Monplaisir's <i>Brahma</i>, which premiered in 1868 in La Scala, Italy,<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> is a story that involves romantic relations between a slave girl and <a href="/wiki/Brahma" title="Brahma">Brahma</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hindu_deities" title="Hindu deities">Hindu god</a>, when he visits earth.<sup id="cite_ref-Au_55-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Au-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> In addition, in 1909, Serge Diagilev included <i><a href="/wiki/Cl%C3%A9op%C3%A2tre_(ballet)" title="Cléopâtre (ballet)">Cléopâtre</a></i> in the <a href="/wiki/Ballets_Russes" title="Ballets Russes">Ballets Russes</a>' repertory. With its theme of sex, this revision of Fokine's <i>Une Nuit d'Egypte</i> combined the "exoticism and grandeur" that audiences of this time craved.<sup id="cite_ref-Au_55-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Au-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As one of the pioneers of modern dance in America, <a href="/wiki/Ruth_St_Denis" class="mw-redirect" title="Ruth St Denis">Ruth St Denis</a> also explored Orientalism in her dancing. Her dances were not authentic; she drew inspiration from photographs, books, and later from museums in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> Yet, the exoticism of her dances catered to the interests of society women in America.<sup id="cite_ref-Au_55-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Au-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> She included <i>Radha</i> and <i>The Cobras</i> in her 'Indian' program in 1906. In addition, she found success in Europe with another Indian-themed ballet, <i>The Nautch</i> in 1908. In 1909, upon her return to America, St Denis created her first 'Egyptian' work, <i>Egypta</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Au_55-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Au-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Her preference for Orientalism continued, culminating with <i>Ishtar of the Seven Gates</i> in 1923, about a Babylonian goddess.<sup id="cite_ref-Au_55-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Au-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While Orientalism in dance climaxed in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, it is still present in modern times. For instance, major ballet companies regularly perform <i>Le Corsaire</i>, <i>La Bayadere</i>, and <i>Sheherazade</i>. Furthermore, Orientalism is also found within newer versions of ballets. In versions of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Nutcracker" title="The Nutcracker">The Nutcracker</a></i>, such as the 2010 <a href="/wiki/American_Ballet_Theatre" title="American Ballet Theatre">American Ballet Theatre</a> production, the Chinese dance uses an arm position with the arms bent at a ninety-degree angle and the index fingers pointed upwards, while the Arabian dance uses two dimensional bent arm movements. Inspired by ballets of the past, stereotypical 'Oriental' movements and arm positions have developed and remain.
</p>

<p>An exchange of Western and Eastern ideas about spirituality developed as the West traded with and established colonies in Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcMahan2008_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcMahan2008-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> The first Western translation of a Sanskrit text appeared in 1785,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERenard2010176_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERenard2010176-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> marking the growing interest in Indian culture and languages.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERenard2010177_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERenard2010177-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Translations of the <i>Upanishads</i>, which <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Schopenhauer" title="Arthur Schopenhauer">Arthur Schopenhauer</a> called "the consolation of my life", first appeared in 1801 and 1802.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERenard2010177-178_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERenard2010177-178-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;note 1&#93;</a></sup> Early translations also appeared in other European languages.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERenard2010183-184_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERenard2010183-184-64">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> 19th-century <a href="/wiki/Transcendentalism" title="Transcendentalism">transcendentalism</a> was influenced by Asian spirituality, prompting <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Waldo_Emerson" title="Ralph Waldo Emerson">Ralph Waldo Emerson</a> (1803–1882) to pioneer the idea of spirituality as a distinct field.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A major force in the mutual influence of Eastern and Western <a href="/wiki/Spirituality" title="Spirituality">spirituality</a> and religiosity was the <a href="/wiki/Theosophical_Society" title="Theosophical Society">Theosophical Society</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERenard2010185-188_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERenard2010185-188-66">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESinari2000_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESinari2000-67">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> a group searching for ancient wisdom from the East and spreading Eastern religious ideas in the West.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavoie2012_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavoie2012-68">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcMahan2008_58-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcMahan2008-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> One of its salient features was the belief in "<a href="/wiki/Masters_of_the_Ancient_Wisdom_(Theosophy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Masters of the Ancient Wisdom (Theosophy)">Masters of Wisdom</a>",<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGilchrist199632_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGilchrist199632-69">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;note 2&#93;</a></sup> "beings, human or once human, who have transcended the normal frontiers of knowledge, and who make their wisdom available to others".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGilchrist199632_69-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGilchrist199632-69">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> The Theosophical Society also spread Western ideas in the East, contributing to its modernisation and a growing nationalism in the Asian colonies.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcMahan2008_58-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcMahan2008-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Theosophical Society had a major influence on <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_modernism" title="Buddhist modernism">Buddhist modernism</a><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcMahan2008_58-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcMahan2008-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Hindu_reform_movements" title="Hindu reform movements">Hindu reform movements</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESinari2000_67-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESinari2000-67">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcMahan2008_58-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcMahan2008-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> Between 1878 and 1882, the Society and the <a href="/wiki/Arya_Samaj" title="Arya Samaj">Arya Samaj</a> were united as the <a href="/wiki/Theosophical_Society_of_the_Arya_Samaj" title="Theosophical Society of the Arya Samaj">Theosophical Society of the Arya Samaj</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJohnson1994107_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJohnson1994107-71">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Helena_Blavatsky" title="Helena Blavatsky">Helena Blavatsky</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Henry_Steel_Olcott" title="Henry Steel Olcott">H. S. Olcott</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anagarika_Dharmapala" title="Anagarika Dharmapala">Anagarika Dharmapala</a>, was instrumental in the Western transmission and revival of <a href="/wiki/Theravada_Buddhism" class="mw-redirect" title="Theravada Buddhism">Theravada Buddhism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcMahan200898_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcMahan200898-72">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGombrich1996185-188_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGombrich1996185-188-73">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFields199283-118_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFields199283-118-74">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another major influence was <a href="/wiki/Vivekananda" class="mw-redirect" title="Vivekananda">Vivekananda</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERenard2010189-193_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERenard2010189-193-75">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMichaelson200979-81_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMichaelson200979-81-76">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> who popularised his modernised interpretation<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERambachan1994_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERambachan1994-77">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Advaita_Vedanta" title="Advaita Vedanta">Advaita Vedanta</a> during the later 19th and early 20th century in both India and the West,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMichaelson200979-81_76-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMichaelson200979-81-76">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> emphasising <i>anubhava</i> ("personal experience") over scriptural authority.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERambachan19941_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERambachan19941-78">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_views_of_the_West_and_Western_views_of_the_East">Eastern views of the West and Western views of the East</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orientalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Eastern views of the West and Western views of the East">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "re-orientalism" was used by Lisa Lau and Ana Cristina Mendes<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> to refer to how Eastern self-representation is based on western referential points:
</p><p>"Re-Orientalism differs from Orientalism in its manner of and reasons for referencing the West: while challenging the metanarratives of Orientalism, re-Orientalism sets up alternative metanarratives of its own in order to articulate eastern identities, simultaneously deconstructing and reinforcing Orientalism."<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The term "<a href="/wiki/Occidentalism" title="Occidentalism">Occidentalism</a>" is often used to refer to negative views of the Western world found in Eastern societies and is founded on the sense of nationalism that spread in reaction to colonialism.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The action of "<a href="/wiki/Othering" class="mw-redirect" title="Othering">othering</a>" cultures occurs when groups are labeled as different due to characteristics that distinguish them from the perceived norm.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-83">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Edward_Said" title="Edward Said">Edward Said</a>, the author of the book <i>Orientalism</i>, argued that western powers and influential individuals such as social scientists and artists othered "the Orient".<sup id="cite_ref-:1_83-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-83">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> The evolution of ideologies is often initially embedded in the language, and continues to ripple through the fabric of society by taking over the culture, economy and political sphere.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Much of Said's criticism of Western Orientalism is based on what he describes as articularizing trends. These ideologies are present in Asian works by Indian, Chinese, and Japanese writers and artists, in their views of Western culture and tradition.
</p><p>A particularly significant development is the manner in which Orientalism has taken shape in non-Western cinema, as for instance in Hindi cinema.
</p><p>Said has been accused of Occidentalizing the west in his critique Orientalism, <i>i.e.</i> of being guilty of falsely characterizing the West in the same way that he accuses Western scholars of falsely characterizing the East.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-85">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> Said essentialized the west by creating a homogeneous image of the area. Currently, the west consists not only of Europe, but also the United States, which has become more influential and dominant over the years.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_85-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-85">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
