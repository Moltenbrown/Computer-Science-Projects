<p>In <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a>, <b>kinship</b> is the web of social relationships that form an important part of the lives of all humans in all societies, although its exact meanings even within this discipline are often debated. Anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Robin_Fox" title="Robin Fox">Robin Fox</a> states that "the study of kinship is the study of what man does with these basic facts of life&#160;&#8211;&#32;mating, gestation, parenthood, <a href="/wiki/Socialization" title="Socialization">socialization</a>, siblingship etc." Human society is unique, he argues, in that we are "working with the same raw material as exists in the animal world, but [we] can conceptualize and categorize it to serve social ends."<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> These social ends include the socialization of children and the formation of basic economic, political and religious groups.
</p><p>Kinship can refer both to the patterns of social relationships themselves, or it can refer to the study of the patterns of social relationships in one or more human cultures (i.e. kinship studies). Over its history, anthropology has developed a number of related concepts and terms in the study of kinship, such as <a href="/wiki/Descent_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Descent (disambiguation)">descent</a>, descent group, <a href="/wiki/Lineage_(anthropology)" title="Lineage (anthropology)">lineage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Affinity_(law)" title="Affinity (law)">affinity/affine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Consanguinity" title="Consanguinity">consanguinity/cognate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fictive_kinship" title="Fictive kinship">fictive kinship</a>. Further, even within these two broad usages of the term, there are different theoretical approaches.
</p><p>Broadly, kinship patterns may be considered to include people related by both descent –&#160;i.e. social relations during development&#160;– and by <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">marriage</a>. Human kinship relations through <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">marriage</a> are commonly called "affinity" in contrast to the relationships that arise in one's group of origin, which may be called one's descent group. In some cultures, kinship relationships may be considered to extend out to people an individual has economic or political relationships with, or other forms of social connections. Within a culture, some descent groups may be considered to lead back to <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">gods</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> or animal ancestors (<a href="/wiki/Totem" title="Totem">totems</a>). This may be conceived of on a more or less literal basis.
</p><p>Kinship can also refer to a principle by which individuals or groups of individuals are organized into <a href="/wiki/Social_groups" class="mw-redirect" title="Social groups">social groups</a>, roles, categories and <a href="/wiki/Genealogy" title="Genealogy">genealogy</a> by means of <a href="/wiki/Kinship_terminology" title="Kinship terminology">kinship terminologies</a>. <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">Family</a> relations can be represented concretely (mother, brother, grandfather) or abstractly by degrees of relationship (kinship distance). A relationship may be relative (e.g. a father in relation to a child) or reflect an absolute (e.g. the difference between a mother and a childless woman). Degrees of relationship are not identical to <a href="/wiki/Inheritance" title="Inheritance">heirship</a> or legal succession. Many codes of <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethics</a> consider the bond of kinship as creating obligations between the related persons stronger than those between strangers, as in <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucian</a> <a href="/wiki/Filial_piety" title="Filial piety">filial piety</a>.
</p><p>In a more general sense, kinship may refer to a similarity or <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/affinity" class="extiw" title="wikt:affinity">affinity</a> between entities on the basis of some or all of their characteristics that are under focus. This may be due to a shared <a href="/wiki/Ontology" title="Ontology">ontological</a> origin, a shared historical or cultural connection, or some other perceived shared features that connect the two entities. For example, a person studying the ontological roots of human languages (<a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymology</a>) might ask whether there is kinship between the English word <i>seven</i> and the German word <i>sieben</i>. It can be used in a more diffuse sense as in, for example, the news headline "<a href="/wiki/Madonna_(entertainer)" title="Madonna (entertainer)">Madonna</a> feels kinship with vilified <a href="/wiki/Wallis_Simpson" title="Wallis Simpson">Wallis Simpson</a>", to imply a felt similarity or <a href="/wiki/Empathy" title="Empathy">empathy</a> between two or more entities.
</p><p>In biology, "kinship" typically refers to the degree of genetic relatedness or <a href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_relationship" title="Coefficient of relationship">coefficient of relationship</a> between individual members of a species (e.g. as in <a href="/wiki/Kin_selection" title="Kin selection">kin selection</a> theory). It may also be used in this specific sense when applied to human relationships, in which case its meaning is closer to <a href="/wiki/Consanguinity" title="Consanguinity">consanguinity</a> or <a href="/wiki/Genealogy" title="Genealogy">genealogy</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_concepts">Basic concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Basic concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_types">Family  types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Family types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">Family</a></div>
<p>Family is a <a href="/wiki/Social_group" title="Social group">group</a> of <a href="/wiki/People" title="People">people</a> affiliated by <a href="/wiki/Consanguinity" title="Consanguinity">consanguinity</a> (by recognized birth), <a href="/wiki/Affinity_(law)" title="Affinity (law)">affinity</a> (by marriage), or co-residence/shared consumption (see <a href="/wiki/Nurture_kinship" title="Nurture kinship">Nurture kinship</a>). In most societies it is the principal institution for the socialization of children. As the basic unit for raising children, Anthropologists most generally classify family organization as  <a href="/wiki/Matrifocal_family" title="Matrifocal family">matrifocal</a> (a mother and her children); conjugal (a husband, his wife, and children; also called <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_family" title="Nuclear family">nuclear family</a>); <a href="/wiki/Avuncular" class="mw-redirect" title="Avuncular">avuncular</a> (a brother, his sister, and her children); or <a href="/wiki/Extended_family" title="Extended family">extended family</a> in which parents and children co-reside with other members of one parent's family.
</p><p>However, producing children is not the only function of the family; in societies with a sexual division of labor, <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">marriage</a>, and the resulting relationship between two people, it is necessary for the formation of an economically productive <a href="/wiki/Household" title="Household">household</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kinship_terminologies">Kinship terminologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Kinship terminologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kinship_terminology" title="Kinship terminology">Kinship terminology</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:160px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Beowulf_-_cynne.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/39/Beowulf_-_cynne.jpg" decoding="async" width="158" height="66" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="158" data-file-height="66" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Beowulf_-_cynne.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A mention of "cȳnne" (kinsmen) in the <i><a href="/wiki/Beowulf" title="Beowulf">Beowulf</a></i></div></div></div>
<p>Different societies classify kinship relations differently and therefore use different systems of kinship terminology – for example some languages distinguish between <a href="/wiki/Affinal_kin" class="mw-redirect" title="Affinal kin">affinal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Consanguine" class="mw-redirect" title="Consanguine">consanguine</a> uncles, whereas others
have only one word to refer to both a father and his brothers. Kinship terminologies include the terms of address used in different languages or communities for different relatives and the terms of reference used to identify the relationship of these relatives to ego or to each other.
</p><p>Kin terminologies can be either descriptive or <a href="/wiki/Classificatory_kinship" title="Classificatory kinship">classificatory</a>. When a descriptive terminology is used, a term refers to only one specific type of relationship, while a classificatory terminology groups many different types of relationships under one term. For example, the word <i><a href="/wiki/Brother" title="Brother">brother</a></i> in English-speaking societies indicates a son of one's same parent; thus, English-speaking societies use the word <i>brother</i> as a descriptive term referring to this relationship only.  In many other classificatory kinship terminologies, in contrast, a person's male first cousin ( whether mother's brother's son, mother's sister's son, father's brother's son, father's sister's son) may also be referred to as brothers.
</p><p>The major patterns of kinship systems that are known which <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Henry_Morgan" class="mw-redirect" title="Lewis Henry Morgan">Lewis Henry Morgan</a> identified through kinship terminology in his 1871 work <i>Systems of Consanguinity and Affinity of the Human Family</i> are:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Iroquois_kinship" title="Iroquois kinship">Iroquois kinship</a> (also known as "bifurcate merging")</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Crow_kinship" title="Crow kinship">Crow kinship</a> (an expansion of bifurcate merging)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Omaha_kinship" title="Omaha kinship">Omaha kinship</a> (also an expansion of bifurcate merging)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Eskimo_kinship" title="Eskimo kinship">Eskimo kinship</a> (also referred to as "lineal kinship")</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_kinship" title="Hawaiian kinship">Hawaiian kinship</a> (also referred to as the "generational system")</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sudanese_kinship" title="Sudanese kinship">Sudanese kinship</a> (also referred to as the "descriptive system")<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li></ul>
<p>There is a seventh type of system only identified as distinct later:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Dravidian_kinship" class="mw-redirect" title="Dravidian kinship">Dravidian kinship</a> (the classical type of <a href="/wiki/Classificatory_kinship" title="Classificatory kinship">classificatory kinship</a>, with bifurcate merging but totally distinct from Iroquois). Most <a href="/wiki/Australian_Aboriginal_kinship" title="Australian Aboriginal kinship">Australian Aboriginal kinship</a> is also classificatory.</li></ul>
<p>The six types (Crow, Eskimo, Hawaiian, Iroquois, Omaha, Sudanese) that are not fully classificatory (Dravidian, Australian) are those identified by Murdock (1949) prior to Lounsbury's (1964) rediscovery of the linguistic principles of classificatory kin terms.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Descent">Descent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Descent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Descent_rules">Descent rules</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Descent rules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In many societies where kinship connections are important, there are rules, though they may be expressed or be taken for granted. There are four main headings that anthropologists use to categorize rules of descent. They are bilateral, unilineal, ambilineal and double descent.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bilateral" class="extiw" title="wikt:bilateral">Bilateral</a> descent or two-sided descent affiliates an individual more or less equally with relatives on his father's and mother's sides. A good example is the <a href="/wiki/Yakurr_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Yakurr people">Yakurr</a> of the Crossriver state of Nigeria.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Unilineal" class="mw-redirect" title="Unilineal">Unilineal</a> rules affiliates an individual through the descent of one sex only, that is, either through males or through females. They are subdivided into two: <a href="/wiki/Patrilineal" class="mw-redirect" title="Patrilineal">patrilineal</a> (male) and <a href="/wiki/Matrilineal" class="mw-redirect" title="Matrilineal">matrilineal</a> (female). Most societies are patrilineal. Examples of a matrilineal system of descent are the <a href="/wiki/Nyakyusa_people" title="Nyakyusa people">Nyakyusa</a> of Tanzania and the Nair of <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a>, India. Many societies that practise a matrilineal system often have a patrilocal residence and men still exercise significant authority.</li>
<li>Ambilineal (or Cognatic) rule affiliates an individual with <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/kinsman" class="extiw" title="wikt:kinsman">kinsmen</a> through the father's or mother's line. Some people in societies that practise this system affiliate with a group of relatives through their fathers and others through their mothers. The individual can choose which side he wants to affiliate to. The <a href="/wiki/Samoans" title="Samoans">Samoans</a> of the South Pacific are an excellent example of an ambilineal society. The core members of the Samoan descent group can live together in the same compound.</li>
<li>Double descent refers to societies in which both the patrilineal and matrilineal descent group are recognized. In these societies an individual affiliates for some purposes with a group of patrilineal kinsmen and for other purposes with a group of matrilineal kinsmen.  The most widely known case of double descent is the <a href="/wiki/Afikpo" class="mw-redirect" title="Afikpo">Afikpo</a> of Imo state in Nigeria. Although patrilineage is considered an important method of organization, the Afikpo considers matrilineal ties to be more important.</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Descent_groups">Descent groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Descent groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A descent group is a <a href="/wiki/Group_(sociology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Group (sociology)">social group</a> whose members talk about common ancestry. A <a href="/wiki/Unilineal_descent" class="mw-redirect" title="Unilineal descent">unilineal</a> society is one in which the descent of an individual is reckoned either from the mother's or the father's line of descent. With <a href="/wiki/Matrilineality" title="Matrilineality">matrilineal descent</a> individuals belong to their mother's descent group. Matrilineal descent includes the mother's brother, who in some societies may pass along inheritance to the sister's children or succession to a sister's son. With <a href="/wiki/Patrilineality" title="Patrilineality">patrilineal descent</a>, individuals belong to their father's descent group. Societies with the <a href="/wiki/Iroquois_kinship" title="Iroquois kinship">Iroquois kinship</a> system, are typically uniliineal, while the Iroquois proper are specifically matrilineal.
</p><p>In a society which reckons descent bilaterally (bilineal), descent is reckoned through both father and mother, without unilineal descent groups. Societies with the <a href="/wiki/Eskimo_kinship" title="Eskimo kinship">Eskimo kinship</a> system, like the <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yupik_peoples" title="Yupik peoples">Yupik</a>, and most Western societies, are typically bilateral. The egocentric kindred group is also typical of bilateral societies.
</p><p>Some societies reckon descent patrilineally for some purposes, and matrilineally for others. This arrangement is sometimes called double descent. For instance, certain property and titles may be inherited through the male line, and others through the female line.
</p><p>Societies can also consider descent to be <a href="/wiki/Ambilineal_descent" class="mw-redirect" title="Ambilineal descent">ambilineal</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_kinship" title="Hawaiian kinship">Hawaiian kinship</a>) where offspring determine their lineage through the <a href="/wiki/Matrilineality" title="Matrilineality">matrilineal line</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Patrilineality" title="Patrilineality">patrilineal line</a>.
</p>
<h4><span id="Lineages.2C_clans.2C_phratries.2C_moieties.2C_and_matrimonial_sides"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Lineages,_clans,_phratries,_moieties,_and_matrimonial_sides">Lineages, clans, phratries, moieties, and matrimonial sides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lineages, clans, phratries, moieties, and matrimonial sides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A lineage is a <a href="/wiki/Unilineal_descent" class="mw-redirect" title="Unilineal descent">unilineal descent</a> group that can demonstrate their common descent from a known <a href="/wiki/Most_recent_common_ancestor" title="Most recent common ancestor">apical ancestor</a>. Unilineal lineages can be matrilineal or patrilineal, depending on whether they are traced through mothers or fathers, respectively. Whether matrilineal or patrilineal descent is considered most significant differs from culture to culture.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Clan" title="Clan">clan</a> is generally a descent group claiming common descent from an apical ancestor. Often, the details of parentage are not important elements of the clan tradition. Non-human apical ancestors are called <a href="/wiki/Totem" title="Totem">totems</a>. Examples of clans are found in <a href="/wiki/Teip" title="Teip">Chechen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Consort_clan" class="mw-redirect" title="Consort clan">Chinese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irish_clans" title="Irish clans">Irish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_clans" title="Japanese clans">Japanese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polish_clans" class="mw-redirect" title="Polish clans">Polish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_clan" title="Scottish clan">Scottish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tlingit_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Tlingit people">Tlingit</a>, and  <a href="/wiki/Somali_clan" class="mw-redirect" title="Somali clan">Somali</a> societies.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Phratry" title="Phratry">phratry</a> is a descent group composed of two or more clans each of whose apical ancestors are descended from a further common ancestor.
</p><p>If a society is divided into exactly two descent groups, each is called a <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/moiety" class="extiw" title="wikt:moiety">moiety</a>, after the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> word for <i>half</i>. If the two halves are each obliged to marry out, and into the other, these are called matrimonial <a href="/wiki/Moiety_(kinship)" title="Moiety (kinship)">moieties</a>. Houseman and White (1998b, bibliography) have discovered numerous societies where kinship network analysis shows that two halves marry one another, similar to matrimonial moieties, except that the two halves—which they call matrimonial <i>sides</i><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>—are neither named nor descent groups, although the egocentric kinship terms may be consistent with the pattern of sidedness, whereas the sidedness is culturally evident but imperfect.<sup id="cite_ref-Houseman1998a_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Houseman1998a-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The word <i>deme</i> refers to an endogamous local population that does not have unilineal descent.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Thus, a deme is a local endogamous community without internal segmentation into clans.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="House_societies">House societies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: House societies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/House_society" title="House society">House society</a></div>
<p>In some societies kinship and political relations are organized around membership in corporately organized dwellings rather than around <a href="/wiki/Descent_group" class="mw-redirect" title="Descent group">descent groups</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lineage_(anthropology)" title="Lineage (anthropology)">lineages</a>, as in the "<a href="/wiki/House_of_Windsor" title="House of Windsor">House of Windsor</a>". The concept of a house society was originally proposed by <a href="/wiki/Claude_L%C3%A9vi-Strauss" title="Claude Lévi-Strauss">Claude Lévi-Strauss</a> who called them "<i>sociétés à maison</i>".<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The concept has been applied to understand the organization of societies from <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">Mesoamerica</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Moluccas" class="mw-redirect" title="Moluccas">Moluccas</a> to <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> and medieval Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Lévi-Strauss introduced the concept as an alternative to 'corporate kinship group' among the cognatic kinship groups of the Pacific region. The socially significant groupings within these societies have variable membership because kinship is reckoned bilaterally (through both father's and mother's kin) and come together for only short periods. Property, genealogy and residence are not the basis for the group's existence.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Marriage_.28affinity.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_(affinity)">Marriage (affinity)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Marriage (affinity)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">Marriage</a></div>
<p>Marriage is a socially or ritually recognized union or legal contract between <a href="/wiki/Spouse" title="Spouse">spouses</a> that establishes rights and obligations between them, between them and their children, and between them and their in-laws.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The definition of marriage varies according to different cultures, but it is principally an <a href="/wiki/Institution" title="Institution">institution</a> in which interpersonal relationships, usually <a href="/wiki/Intimate_relationship" title="Intimate relationship">intimate</a> and sexual, are acknowledged. When defined broadly, marriage is considered a <a href="/wiki/Cultural_universal" title="Cultural universal">cultural universal</a>. A broad definition of marriage includes those that are <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamous</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polygamy" title="Polygamy">polygamous</a>, <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage" title="Same-sex marriage">same-sex</a> and temporary.
</p><p>The act of marriage usually creates <a href="/wiki/Normative" title="Normative">normative</a> or legal obligations between the individuals involved, and any offspring they may produce. Marriage may result, for example, in "a union between a man and a woman such that children born to the woman are the recognized legitimate offspring of both partners."<sup id="cite_ref-Notes_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Notes-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Edmund Leach argued that no one definition of marriage applied to all cultures, but offered a list of ten rights frequently associated with marriage, including sexual monopoly and rights with respect to children (with specific rights differing across cultures).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There is wide cross-cultural variation in the social rules governing the selection of a partner for marriage. In many societies the choice of partner is limited to suitable persons from specific social groups. In some societies the rule is that a partner is selected from an individual's own social group – <a href="/wiki/Endogamy" title="Endogamy">endogamy</a>, this is the case in many class and caste based societies. But in other societies a partner must be chosen from a different group than one's own – <a href="/wiki/Exogamy" title="Exogamy">exogamy</a>, this is the case in many societies practicing <a href="/wiki/Totem" title="Totem">totemic</a> religion where society is divided into several exogamous totemic clans, such as most <a href="/wiki/Aboriginal_Australian" class="mw-redirect" title="Aboriginal Australian">Aboriginal Australian</a> societies. Marriages between parents and children, or between full siblings, with few exceptions,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> have been considered <a href="/wiki/Incest" title="Incest">incest</a> and forbidden. However, <a href="/wiki/Cousin_marriage" title="Cousin marriage">marriages between more distant relatives</a> have been much more common, with one estimate being that 80% of all marriages in history have been between second cousins or closer.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Alliance_.28marital_exchange_systems.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Alliance_(marital_exchange_systems)">Alliance (marital exchange systems)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Alliance (marital exchange systems)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Alliance_theory" title="Alliance theory">Alliance theory</a></div>
<p>Systemic forms of preferential marriage may have wider social implications in terms of economic and political organization. In a wide array of lineage-based societies with a <a href="/wiki/Kinship_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Kinship system">classificatory kinship system</a>, potential spouses are sought from a specific class of relative as determined by a prescriptive marriage rule. Insofar as regular marriages following prescriptive rules occur, lineages are linked together in fixed relationships; these ties between lineages may form political alliances in kinship dominated societies.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> French <a href="/wiki/Structuralism_(sociology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Structuralism (sociology)">structural</a> anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Claude_L%C3%A9vi-Strauss" title="Claude Lévi-Strauss">Claude Lévi-Strauss</a> developed <a href="/wiki/Alliance_theory" title="Alliance theory">alliance theory</a> to account for the "elementary" kinship structures created by the limited number of prescriptive marriage rules possible.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Claude Lévi-Strauss argued in <i>The Elementary Structures of Kinship</i> (1949), that the <a href="/wiki/Incest_taboo" title="Incest taboo">incest taboo</a> necessitated the exchange of women between kinship groups. Levi-Strauss thus shifted the emphasis from descent groups to the stable <i>structures</i> or relations between groups that preferential and prescriptive marriage rules created.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_kinship_studies">History of kinship studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: History of kinship studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of the foundational works in the anthropological study of kinship was <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Henry_Morgan" class="mw-redirect" title="Lewis Henry Morgan">Morgan's</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Systems_of_Consanguinity_and_Affinity_of_the_Human_Family" title="Systems of Consanguinity and Affinity of the Human Family">Systems of Consanguinity and Affinity of the Human Family</a></i> (1871). As is the case with other social sciences, Anthropology and kinship studies emerged at a time when the understanding of the Human species' comparative place in the world was somewhat different from today's. Evidence that life in stable social groups is not just a feature of humans, but also of many other <a href="/wiki/Primate#Behavior" title="Primate">primates</a>, was yet to emerge and <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a> was considered to be a uniquely human affair. As a result, early kinship theorists saw an apparent need to explain not only the details of <i>how</i> human social groups are constructed, their patterns, meanings and obligations, but also <i>why</i> they are constructed at all. The <i>why</i> explanations thus typically presented the fact of life in social groups (<i>which appeared to be unique to humans</i>) as being largely a result of human ideas and values.
</p>
<h3><span id="Morgan.27s_early_influence"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Morgan's_early_influence">Morgan's early influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Morgan&#039;s early influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kinship_Systems.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Kinship_Systems.svg/220px-Kinship_Systems.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="116" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Kinship_Systems.svg/330px-Kinship_Systems.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Kinship_Systems.svg/440px-Kinship_Systems.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1330" data-file-height="700" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kinship_Systems.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A broad comparison of (left, top-to-bottom) <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_kinship" title="Hawaiian kinship">Hawaiian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sudanese_kinship" title="Sudanese kinship">Sudanese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eskimo_kinship" title="Eskimo kinship">Eskimo</a>, (right, top-to-bottom) <a href="/wiki/Iroquois_kinship" title="Iroquois kinship">Iroquois</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crow_kinship" title="Crow kinship">Crow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Omaha_kinship" title="Omaha kinship">Omaha kinship</a> systems.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kinship_terminology" title="Kinship terminology">Kinship terminology</a></div>
<p>Morgan's explanation for why humans live in groups was largely based on the notion that all humans have an inherent <i>natural valuation</i> of genealogical ties (an unexamined assumption that would remain at the heart of kinship studies for another century, see below), and therefore also an inherent desire to construct social groups around these ties. Even so, Morgan found that members of a society who are <i>not</i> close genealogical relatives may nevertheless use what he called <i>kinship terms</i> (which he considered to be originally based on genealogical ties). This fact was already evident in his use of the term <i>affinity</i> within his concept of the <i>system of kinship</i>. The most lasting of Morgan's contributions was his discovery of the difference between descriptive and <a href="/wiki/Classificatory_kinship" title="Classificatory kinship">classificatory kinship</a> terms, which situated broad kinship classes on the basis of imputing abstract social patterns of relationships having little or no overall relation to genetic closeness but instead cognition about kinship, social distinctions as they affect linguistic usages in <a href="/wiki/Kinship_terminology" title="Kinship terminology">kinship terminology</a>, and strongly relate, if only by approximation, to patterns of marriage.<sup id="cite_ref-Houseman1998a_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Houseman1998a-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Kinship_networks_and_social_process.5B30.5D"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Kinship_networks_and_social_process[30]">Kinship networks and social process<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Kinship networks and social process[30]">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A more flexible view of kinship was formulated in British <a href="/wiki/Social_anthropology" title="Social anthropology">social anthropology</a>. Among the attempts to break out of universalizing assumptions and theories about kinship, <a href="/wiki/Radcliffe-Brown" class="mw-redirect" title="Radcliffe-Brown">Radcliffe-Brown</a> (1922, The <a href="/wiki/Andaman_Islands" title="Andaman Islands">Andaman Islands</a>; 1930, The social organization of Australian tribes) was the first to assert that kinship relations are best thought of as concrete networks of relationships among individuals. He then described these relationships, however, as typified by interlocking interpersonal roles. <a href="/wiki/Malinowski" title="Malinowski">Malinowski</a> (1922, Argonauts of the Western Pacific) described patterns of events with concrete individuals as participants stressing the relative stability of institutions and communities, but without insisting on abstract systems or models of kinship. <a href="/wiki/Max_Gluckman" title="Max Gluckman">Gluckman</a> (1955, The judicial process among the Barotse of Northern Rhodesia) balanced the emphasis on stability of institutions against processes of change and conflict, inferred through detailed analysis of instances of social interaction to infer rules and assumptions.  <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Barnes_(anthropologist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="John Barnes (anthropologist) (page does not exist)">John Barnes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Victor_Turner" title="Victor Turner">Victor Turner</a>, and others, affiliated with Gluckman's Manchester school of anthropology, described patterns of actual network relations in communities and fluid situations in urban or migratory context, as with the work of <a href="/wiki/J._Clyde_Mitchell" title="J. Clyde Mitchell">J. Clyde Mitchell</a> (1965, Social Networks in Urban Situations). Yet, all these approaches clung to a view of stable <a href="/wiki/Structural_functionalism" title="Structural functionalism">functionalism</a>, with kinship as one of the central stable institutions.
</p>
<h3><span id=".22Kinship_system.22_as_systemic_pattern"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Kinship_system&quot;_as_systemic_pattern">"Kinship system" as systemic pattern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: &quot;Kinship system&quot; as systemic pattern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The concept of “system of kinship” tended to dominate anthropological studies of kinship in the early 20th century. Kinship systems as defined in anthropological texts and ethnographies were seen as constituted by patterns of behavior and attitudes in relation to the differences in terminology, listed above, for referring to relationships as well as for addressing others. Many anthropologists went so far as to see, in these patterns of kinship, strong relations between <a href="/wiki/Kinship_terminology" title="Kinship terminology">kinship categories</a> and patterns of marriage, including forms of marriage, restrictions on marriage, and cultural concepts of the boundaries of <a href="/wiki/Incest" title="Incest">incest</a>. A great deal of inference was necessarily involved in such constructions as to “systems” of kinship, and attempts to construct systemic patterns and reconstruct kinship evolutionary histories on these bases were largely invalidated in later work. However, anthropologist Dwight Read later argued that the way in which kinship categories are defined by individual researchers are substantially inconsistent.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> This occurs when working within a systemic cultural model that can be elicited in fieldwork, but also allowing considerable individual variability in details, such as when they are recorded through relative products.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Conflicting_theories_of_the_mid_20th_century.5B33.5D"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Conflicting_theories_of_the_mid_20th_century[33]">Conflicting theories of the mid 20th century<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Conflicting theories of the mid 20th century[33]">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In trying to resolve the problems of dubious inferences about kinship "systems", <a href="/wiki/George_P._Murdock" class="mw-redirect" title="George P. Murdock">George P. Murdock</a> (1949, Social Structure) compiled kinship data to test a theory about universals in human kinship in the way that terminologies were influenced by the behavioral similarities or social differences among pairs of kin, proceeding on the view that the psychological ordering of kinship systems radiates out from ego and the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_family" title="Nuclear family">nuclear family</a> to different forms of <a href="/wiki/Extended_family" title="Extended family">extended family</a>. <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9vi-Strauss" class="mw-redirect" title="Lévi-Strauss">Lévi-Strauss</a> (1949, Les Structures Elementaires), on the other hand, also looked for global patterns to kinship, but viewed the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_theory" title="Alliance theory">“elementary” forms</a> of kinship as lying in the ways that families were connected by marriage in different fundamental forms resembling those of <a href="/wiki/Alliance_theory" title="Alliance theory">modes of exchange</a>: symmetric and direct, reciprocal delay, or generalized exchange.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recognition_of_fluidity_in_kinship_meanings_and_relations">Recognition of fluidity in kinship meanings and relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Recognition of fluidity in kinship meanings and relations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Building on Lévi-Strauss's (1949) notions of kinship as caught up with the fluid languages of exchange, <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Leach" title="Edmund Leach">Edmund Leach</a> (1961, Pul Eliya) argued that kinship was a flexible idiom that had something of the grammar of a language, both in the uses of terms for kin but also in the fluidities of language, meaning, and networks. His field studies criticized the ideas of structural-functional stability of kinship groups as corporations with charters that lasted long beyond the lifetimes of individuals, which had been the orthodoxy of <a href="/wiki/British_Social_Anthropology" class="mw-redirect" title="British Social Anthropology">British Social Anthropology</a>. This sparked debates over whether kinship could be resolved into specific organized sets of rules and components of meaning, or whether kinship meanings were more fluid, symbolic, and independent of grounding in supposedly determinate relations among individuals or groups, such as those of descent or prescriptions for marriage.
</p><p>From the 1950s onwards, reports on kinship patterns in the New Guinea Highlands added some momentum to what had until then been only occasional fleeting suggestions that living together (co-residence) might underlie social bonding, and eventually contributed to the general shift away from a genealogical approach (see below section). For example, on the basis of his observations, Barnes suggested:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>[C]learly, genealogical connexion of some sort is one criterion for membership of many social groups. But it may not be the only criterion; birth, or residence, or a parent's former residence, or utilization of garden land, or participation in exchange and feasting activities or in house-building or raiding, may be other relevant criteria for group membership.”(Barnes 1962,6)<sup id="cite_ref-B1962_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B1962-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>Similarly, Langness' ethnography of the <i>Bena Bena</i> also emphasized the primacy of residence patterns in 'creating' kinship ties:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p><i>The sheer fact of residence in a Bena Bena group can and does determine kinship.</i>  People do not necessarily reside where they do because they are kinsmen: rather they become kinsmen because they reside there.”  (Langness 1964, 172 emphasis in original)<sup id="cite_ref-L1964_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-L1964-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>In 1972 <a href="/wiki/David_M._Schneider" title="David M. Schneider">David M. Schneider</a> raised <sup id="cite_ref-S1972_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S1972-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> deep problems with the notion that human social bonds and 'kinship' was a natural category built upon genealogical ties and made a fuller argument in his 1984 book <i>A critique of the study of Kinship</i><sup id="cite_ref-S1984_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S1984-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> which had a major influence on the subsequent study of kinship.
</p>
<h3><span id="Schneider.27s_critique_of_genealogical_concepts"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Schneider's_critique_of_genealogical_concepts">Schneider's critique of genealogical concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Schneider&#039;s critique of genealogical concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Before the questions raised within anthropology about the study of 'kinship' by <a href="/wiki/David_M._Schneider" title="David M. Schneider">David M. Schneider</a> <sup id="cite_ref-S1984_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S1984-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> and others from the 1960s onwards, anthropology itself had paid very little attention to the notion that kinship bonds were anything other than connected to consanguineal (or genealogical) relatedness (or its local cultural conceptions). Schneider's 1968 study<sup id="cite_ref-S1968_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S1968-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> of the symbolic meanings surrounding ideas of kinship in American Culture found that Americans ascribe a special significance to 'blood ties' as well as related symbols like the naturalness of marriage and raising children within this culture.  In later work (1972 and 1984) Schneider argued that unexamined genealogical notions of kinship had been embedded in anthropology since Morgan's early work<sup id="cite_ref-M1870_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M1870-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> because American anthropologists (and anthropologists in western Europe) had made the mistake of assuming these <i>particular cultural values</i> of 'blood is thicker than water', common in their own societies, were 'natural' and universal for all human cultures (i.e. a form of ethnocentrism). He concluded that, due to these unexamined assumptions, the whole enterprise of 'kinship' in anthropology may have been built on faulty foundations. His 1984 book <i>A Critique of The Study of Kinship</i> gave his fullest account of this critique.
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Certainly for Morgan (1870:10) the actual bonds of blood relationship had a force and vitality of their own quite apart from any social overlay which they may also have acquired, and it is this biological relationship itself which accounts for what Radcliffe-Brown called "the source of social cohesion". (Schneider 1984, 49)
</p></blockquote>
<p>Schneider himself emphasised a distinction between the notion of a social relationship as intrinsically <i>given</i> and inalienable (<i>from birth</i>), and a social relationship as created, constituted and maintained by a process of interaction, or <i>doing</i> (Schneider 1984, 165). Schneider used the example of the citamangen / fak relationship in Yap society, that his own early research had previously glossed over as a <i>father / son</i> relationship, to illustrate the problem;
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The crucial point is this: in the relationship between <i>citamangen</i> and <i>fak</i> the stress in the definition of the relationship is more on doing than on being. That is, it is more what the <i>citamangen</i> does for <i>fak</i> and what <i>fak</i> does for <i>citamangen</i> that makes or constitutes the relationship. This is demonstrated, first, in the ability to terminate absolutely the relationship where there is a failure in the doing, when the <i>fak</i> fails to do what he is supposed to do; and second, in the reversal of terms so that the old, dependent man becomes <i>fak</i>, to the young man, <i>tam</i>. The European and the anthropological notion of consanguinity, of blood relationship and descent, rest on precisely the opposite kind of value. It rests more on the state of being... on the biogenetic relationship which is represented by one or another variant of the symbol of 'blood' (consanguinity), or on 'birth', on qualities rather than on performance. We have tried to impose this definition of a kind of relation on all peoples, insisting that kinship consists in relations of consanguinity and that kinship as consanguinity is a universal condition.(Schneider 1984, 72)
</p></blockquote>
<p>Schneider preferred to focus on these often ignored processes of "performance, forms of doing, various codes for conduct, different roles" (p.&#160;72) as the most important constituents of kinship. His critique quickly prompted a new generation of anthropologists to reconsider how they conceptualized, observed and described social relationships ('kinship') in the cultures they studied.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-Schneider">Post-Schneider</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Post-Schneider">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Nurture_kinship" title="Nurture kinship">nurture kinship</a></div>
<p>Schneider's critique is widely acknowledged <sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> to have marked a turning point in anthropology's study of social relationships and interactions. Some anthropologists moved forward with kinship studies by teasing apart biological and social aspects, prompted by Schneider's question;
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The question of whether kinship is a privileged system and if so, why, remains without a satisfactory answer. If it is privileged because of its relationship to the functional prerequisites imposed by the nature of physical kinship, this remains to be spelled out in even the most elementary detail. (Schneider 1984, 163)
</p></blockquote>
<p>Schneider also dismissed the sociobiological account of biological influences, maintaining that these did not fit the ethnographic evidence (see more below). <a href="/wiki/Janet_Carsten" title="Janet Carsten">Janet Carsten</a> employed her studies with the Malays<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> to reassess kinship. She uses the idea of <i>relatedness</i> to move away from a pre-constructed analytic opposition between the biological and the social. Carsten argued that relatedness should be described in terms of indigenous statements and practices, some of which fall outside what anthropologists have conventionally understood as kinship;
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Ideas about relatedness in Langkawi show how culturally specific is the separation of the 'social' from the 'biological' and the latter to sexual reproduction. In Langkawi relatedness is derived both from acts of procreation and from living and eating together. It makes little sense in indigenous terms to label some of these activities as social and others as biological. (Carsten 1995, 236)
</p></blockquote>
<p>Philip Thomas' work with the Temanambondro of <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a> highlights that nurturing processes are considered to be the 'basis' for kinship ties in this culture, notwithstanding genealogical connections;
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Yet just as fathers are not simply made by birth,  neither are mothers,  and although mothers are not made by  "custom" they,  like fathers,  can make themselves through another type of performatively constituted relation,  the giving of "nurture". Relations of ancestry are particularly important in contexts of ritual,  inheritance and the defining of marriageability and incest;  they are in effect the  "structuring structures" (Bourdieu  1977)  of social reproduction and intergenerational continuity.  Father, mother and children are, however,  also performatively related through the giving and receiving of "nurture" (fitezana). Like ancestry, relations of "nurture" do not always coincide with relations by birth; but unlike ancestry, "nurture" is a largely ungendered relation, constituted in contexts of everyday practical existence, in the intimate, familial and familiar world of the household, and in ongoing relations of work and consumption, of feeding and farming. (Thomas 1999, 37)<sup id="cite_ref-T1999_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-T1999-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>Similar ethnographic accounts have emerged from a variety of cultures since Schneider's intervention. The concept of <a href="/wiki/Nurture_kinship" title="Nurture kinship">nurture kinship</a> highlights the extent to which kinship relationships may be brought into being through the performance of various acts of nurture between individuals. Additionally the concept highlights ethnographic findings that, in a wide swath of human societies, people understand, conceptualize and symbolize their relationships predominantly in terms of giving, receiving and sharing nurture. These approaches were somewhat forerun by <a href="/wiki/Bronis%C5%82aw_Malinowski" title="Bronisław Malinowski">Malinowski</a>, in his <a href="/wiki/Ethnography" title="Ethnography">ethnographic</a> study of <a href="/wiki/Human_sexual_behavior" class="mw-redirect" title="Human sexual behavior">sexual behaviour</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Trobriand_Islands" title="Trobriand Islands">Trobriand Islands</a> which noted that the Trobrianders did not believe pregnancy to be the result of <a href="/wiki/Sexual_intercourse" title="Sexual intercourse">sexual intercourse</a> between the man and the woman, and they denied that there was any physiological relationship between father and child.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Nevertheless, while paternity was unknown in the "full biological sense", for a woman to have a child without having a husband was considered socially undesirable. Fatherhood was therefore recognised as a social and nurturing role; the woman's husband is the "man whose role and duty it is to take the child in his arms and to help her in nursing and bringing it up";<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> "Thus, though the natives are ignorant of any physiological need for a male in the constitution of the family, they regard him as indispensable socially".<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Biology.2C_psychology_and_kinship"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology,_psychology_and_kinship">Biology, psychology and kinship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Biology, psychology and kinship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Human_inclusive_fitness" class="mw-redirect" title="Human inclusive fitness">Human inclusive fitness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Attachment_theory" title="Attachment theory">Attachment theory</a></div>
<p>Like Schneider, other anthropologists of kinship have largely rejected sociobiological accounts of human social patterns as being both reductionistic and also empirically incompatible with ethnographic data on human kinship. Notably, <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Sahlins" title="Marshall Sahlins">Marshall Sahlins</a> strongly critiqued the sociobiological approach through reviews of ethnographies in his 1976 <i>The Use and Abuse of Biology</i><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> noting that for humans "the categories of 'near' and 'distant' [kin] vary independently of consanguinal distance and that these categories organize actual social practice" (p.&#160;112).
</p><p>Independently from anthropology, biologists studying organisms' social behaviours and relationships have been interested to understand under what conditions significant social behaviors can evolve to become a typical feature of a species (see <a href="/wiki/Inclusive_fitness" title="Inclusive fitness">inclusive fitness</a> theory). Because complex social relationships and cohesive social groups are common not only to humans, but also to most primates, biologists maintain that these biological theories of sociality should in principle be generally applicable. The more challenging question arises as to how such ideas can be applied to the human species whilst fully taking account of the extensive ethnographic evidence that has emerged from anthropological research on kinship patterns.
</p><p>Early developments of biological <a href="/wiki/Inclusive_fitness" title="Inclusive fitness">inclusive fitness</a> theory and the derivative field of <a href="/wiki/Sociobiology" title="Sociobiology">Sociobiology</a>, encouraged some <a href="/wiki/Sociobiology" title="Sociobiology">sociobiologists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_psychology" title="Evolutionary psychology">evolutionary psychologists</a> to approach human kinship with the assumption that <a href="/wiki/Inclusive_fitness" title="Inclusive fitness">inclusive fitness</a> theory predicts that kinship relations in humans <i>are indeed</i> expected to depend on genetic relatedness, which they readily connected with the <i>genealogy</i> approach of early anthropologists such as Morgan (see above sections). However, this is the position that Schneider, Sahlins and other anthropologists explicitly reject.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nonreductive_biology_and_nurture_kinship">Nonreductive biology and nurture kinship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Nonreductive biology and nurture kinship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Social_bonding_and_nurture_kinship" class="mw-redirect" title="Social bonding and nurture kinship">Social bonding and nurture kinship</a></div>
<p>In agreement with Schneider, <a href="/wiki/Social_bonding_and_nurture_kinship" class="mw-redirect" title="Social bonding and nurture kinship">Holland</a> argued<sup id="cite_ref-SBNK_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SBNK-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> that an accurate account of biological theory and evidence supports the view that social bonds (and kinship) are indeed mediated by a shared social environment and processes of frequent interaction, care and nurture, rather than by genealogical relationships <i>per se</i> (even if genealogical relationships frequently correlate with such processes). In his 2012 book <i>Social bonding and nurture kinship</i> Holland argues that sociobiologists and later evolutionary psychologists misrepresent biological theory, mistakenly believing that <a href="/wiki/Inclusive_fitness" title="Inclusive fitness">inclusive fitness</a> theory predicts that genetic relatedness <i>per se</i> is the condition that <i>mediates</i> social bonding and social cooperation in organisms. Holland points out that the biological theory (see <a href="/wiki/Inclusive_fitness" title="Inclusive fitness">inclusive fitness</a>) only specifies that a statistical relationship between social behaviors and genealogical relatedness is a criterion for the <i>evolution</i> of social behaviors. The theory's originator, <a href="/wiki/W.D.Hamilton" class="mw-redirect" title="W.D.Hamilton">W.D.Hamilton</a> considered that organisms' social behaviours were likely to be mediated by general conditions that typically <i>correlate</i> with genetic relatedness, but are not likely to be mediated by genetic relatedness <i>per se</i><sup id="cite_ref-H1987_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H1987-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> (see <a href="/wiki/Human_inclusive_fitness" class="mw-redirect" title="Human inclusive fitness">Human inclusive fitness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kin_recognition" title="Kin recognition">Kin recognition</a>). Holland reviews fieldwork from social mammals and primates to show that social bonding and cooperation in these species is indeed mediated through processes of shared living context, familiarity and <a href="/wiki/Attachment_theory" title="Attachment theory">attachments</a>, not by genetic relatedness <i>per se</i>. Holland thus argues that both the biological <i>theory</i> and the biological <i>evidence</i> is nondeterministic and nonreductive, and that biology as a theoretical and empirical endeavor (as opposed to 'biology' as a cultural-symbolic nexus as outlined in Schneider's 1968 book) actually supports the <i><a href="/wiki/Nurture_kinship" title="Nurture kinship">nurture kinship</a></i> perspective of cultural anthropologists working post-Schneider (see above sections). Holland argues that, whilst there is nonreductive compatibility around human kinship between anthropology, biology and psychology, for a full account of kinship in any <i>particular</i> human culture, ethnographic methods, including accounts of the people themselves, the analysis of historical contingencies, symbolic systems, economic and other cultural influences, remain centrally important.
</p><p>Holland's position is widely supported by both cultural anthropologists and biologists as an approach which, according to Robin Fox, <i>"gets to the heart of the matter concerning the contentious relationship between kinship categories, genetic relatedness and the prediction of behavior"</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-RF2012_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RF2012-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolutionary_psychology">Evolutionary psychology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Evolutionary psychology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_psychology" title="Evolutionary psychology">Evolutionary psychology</a></div>
<p>The other approach, that of Evolutionary psychology, continues to take the view that genetic relatedness (or genealogy) is key to understanding human kinship patterns. In contrast to Sahlin's position (above), Daly and Wilson argue that "the categories of 'near' and 'distant' do not 'vary independently of consanguinal distance', not in any society on earth." (Daly et al. 1997,<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> p282). A current view is that humans have an inborn but culturally affected system for detecting certain forms of genetic relatedness. One important factor for <a href="/wiki/Sibling" title="Sibling">sibling</a> detection, especially relevant for older siblings, is that if an infant and one's mother are seen to care for the infant, then the infant and oneself are assumed to be related. Another factor, especially important for younger siblings who cannot use the first method, is that persons who grew up together see one another as related. Yet another may be genetic detection based on the <a href="/wiki/Major_histocompatibility_complex" title="Major histocompatibility complex">major histocompatibility complex</a> (See <a href="/wiki/Major_Histocompatibility_Complex_and_Sexual_Selection" class="mw-redirect" title="Major Histocompatibility Complex and Sexual Selection">Major Histocompatibility Complex and Sexual Selection</a>). This kinship detection system in turn affects other genetic predispositions such as the <a href="/wiki/Incest_taboo" title="Incest taboo">incest taboo</a> and a tendency for <a href="/wiki/Altruism" title="Altruism">altruism</a> towards relatives.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>One issue within this approach is why many societies organize according to descent (see below) and not exclusively according to kinship. An explanation is that kinship does not form clear boundaries and is centered differently for each individual. In contrast, descent groups usually do form clear boundaries and provide an easy way to create cooperative groups of various sizes.<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford2007_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford2007-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to an evolutionary psychology hypothesis that assumes that descent systems are optimized to assure high genetic probability of relatedness between lineage members, males should prefer a patrilineal system if paternal certainty is high; males should prefer a matrilineal system if paternal certainty is low. Some research supports this association with one study finding no patrilineal society with low paternity confidence and no matrilineal society with high paternal certainty. Another association is that <a href="/wiki/Pastoral" title="Pastoral">pastoral</a> societies are relatively more often patrilineal compared to <a href="/wiki/Horticultural" class="mw-redirect" title="Horticultural">horticultural</a> societies. This may be because wealth in pastoral societies in the form of mobile cattle can easily be used to pay <a href="/wiki/Bride_price" title="Bride price">bride price</a> which favor concentrating resources on sons so they can marry.<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford2007_54-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford2007-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The evolutionary psychology account of biology continues to be rejected by most cultural anthropologists.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fosterage_and_adoption">Fosterage and adoption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Fosterage and adoption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Detailed_terms_for_parentage">Detailed terms for parentage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Detailed terms for parentage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As social and biological concepts of parenthood are not necessarily coterminous, the terms "pater" and "genitor" have been used in anthropology to distinguish between the man who is socially recognised as father (pater) and the man who is believed to be the physiological parent (genitor); similarly the terms "mater" and "genitrix" have been used to distinguish between the woman socially recognised as mother (mater) and the woman believed to be the physiological parent (genitrix).<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Such a distinction is useful when the individual who is considered the legal parent of the child is not the individual who is believed to be the child's biological parent. For example, in his ethnography of the <a href="/wiki/Nuer_people" title="Nuer people">Nuer</a>, <a href="/wiki/E._E._Evans-Pritchard" title="E. E. Evans-Pritchard">Evans-Pritchard</a> notes that if a <a href="/wiki/Widow" title="Widow">widow</a>, following the death of her husband, chooses to live with a lover outside of her deceased husband's kin group, that lover is only considered genitor of any subsequent children the widow has, and her deceased husband continues to be considered the pater. As a result, the lover has no legal control over the children, who may be taken away from him by the kin of the pater when they choose.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> The terms "pater" and "genitor" have also been used to help describe the relationship between children and their parents in the context of divorce in Britain. Following the divorce and remarriage of their parents, children find themselves using the term "mother" or "father" in relation to more than one individual, and the pater or mater who is legally responsible for the child's care, and whose <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family name</a> the child uses, may not be the genitor or genitrix of the child, with whom a separate parent-child relationship may be maintained through arrangements such as <a href="/wiki/Contact_(law)" title="Contact (law)">visitation rights</a> or <a href="/wiki/Joint_custody" title="Joint custody">joint custody</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is important to note that the terms "genitor" or "genetrix" do not necessarily imply actual biological relationships based on <a href="/wiki/Consanguinity" title="Consanguinity">consanguinity</a>, but rather refer to the socially held belief that the individual is physically related to the child, derived from culturally held ideas about how biology works. So, for example, the <a href="/wiki/Ifugao_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Ifugao people">Ifugao</a> may believe that an illegitimate child might have more than one physical father, and so nominate more than one genitor.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> J.A. Barnes therefore argued that it was necessary to make a further distinction between genitor and genitrix (the supposed biological mother and father of the child), and the actual <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetic</a> father and mother of the child.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition_of_relations">Composition of relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Composition of relations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Composition_of_relations" title="Composition of relations">Composition of relations</a></div>
<p>The study of kinship may be abstracted to <a href="/wiki/Binary_relation" title="Binary relation">binary relations</a> between people. For example, if <i>x</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Parent" title="Parent">parent</a> of <i>y</i>, the relation may be symbolized as <i>xPy</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Converse_relation" title="Converse relation">converse relation</a>, that <i>y</i> is the child of <i>x</i>, is written <i>yP</i><sup>T</sup><i>x</i>. Suppose that <i>z</i> is another child of <i>x</i>: <i>zP</i><sup>T</sup><i>x</i>. Then <i>y</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Sibling" title="Sibling">sibling</a> of <i>z</i> as they share the parent <i>x</i>: <span class="nowrap"><i>zP</i><sup>T</sup><i>xPy</i> → <i>zP</i><sup>T</sup><i>Py</i></span>. Here the relation of siblings is expressed as the composition <i>P</i><sup>T</sup><i>P</i> of the parent relation with its inverse.
</p><p>The relation of <a href="/wiki/Grandparent" title="Grandparent">grandparent</a> is the composition of the parent relation with itself: <span class="nowrap"><i>G</i> = <i>PP</i></span>. The relation of <a href="/wiki/Uncle" title="Uncle">uncle</a> is the composition of parent with brother, while the relation of <a href="/wiki/Aunt" title="Aunt">aunt</a> composes parent with sister. Suppose <i>x</i> is the grandparent of <i>y</i>: <i>xGy</i>. Then <i>y</i> and <i>z</i> are <a href="/wiki/Cousin" title="Cousin">cousins</a> if <i>yG</i><sup>T</sup><i>xGz</i>.
</p><p>The symbols applied here to express kinship are used more generally in <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_logic" title="Algebraic logic">algebraic logic</a> to develop a calculus of relations with sets other than human beings.
</p>
