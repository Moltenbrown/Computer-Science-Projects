	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Armstrong (surname)</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><table class="infobox" style="width:22em"><caption>Armstrong</caption><tbody><tr><th colspan="2" style="text-align:center;background:#ADD8E6">Origin</th></tr><tr><th scope="row">Meaning</th><td>Son of a strong man</td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Region of origin</th><td>North East England, Scotland,Ireland</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><b>Armstrong</b> is a surname of <a href="/wiki/Scottish_borders" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish borders">Scottish borders</a> origin. It derives from a <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> nickname which meant someone with strong arms.  In Ireland the name was adopted as an <a href="/wiki/Anglicisation" title="Anglicisation">Anglicization</a> of two <a href="/wiki/Gaels" title="Gaels">Gaelic</a> names from <a href="/wiki/Ulster" title="Ulster">Ulster</a>: <i>Mac Thréinfhir</i> (meaning "son of the strong man") and <i>Ó Labhraidh Tréan</i> (meaning "strong O'Lavery").<sup id="cite_ref-dictamfam_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dictamfam-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Clan_Armstrong" title="Clan Armstrong">Clan Armstrong</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Clan" title="Clan">clan</a> from the border area between England and Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-dobson2003_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dobson2003-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  The Scottish Armstrong is reputed to have been originally bestowed by "an antient (sic) king of Scotland" upon "Fairbairn, his armour-bearer" following an act of strength in battle.<sup id="cite_ref-Lower1844_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lower1844-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In the UK this surname is well represented in <a href="/wiki/North_East_England" title="North East England">North East England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cumbria" title="Cumbria">Cumbria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Borders" title="Scottish Borders">Scottish Borders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lanarkshire" title="Lanarkshire">Lanarkshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ayrshire" title="Ayrshire">Ayrshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dumfries_%26_Galloway" class="mw-redirect" title="Dumfries &amp; Galloway">Dumfries &amp; Galloway</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, and in the US it is well represented in the <a href="/wiki/Deep_south" class="mw-redirect" title="Deep south">Deep south</a>, and other <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">southern states</a>.
</p><p>From the name <i>Ó Labhraidh Tréan</i> (meaning "strong O'Lavery" and sometimes written in AngloIrish as "Tréanlámagh") the following surnames survive: "Trainor", Traynor", O'Lavery", "McLavery", and "MacLavery". Although the name "Armstrong" is quite common in the Aghagallon and Glenavy area of County Antrim in Northern Ireland, the other names are to be found within the 9 Ulster Counties and Scotland, especially along the west coast.
</p>
