<p><b>17th century philosophy</b> is generally regarded as seeing the start of <a href="/wiki/Modern_philosophy" title="Modern philosophy">modern philosophy</a>, and the shaking off of the medieval approach, especially <a href="/wiki/Scholasticism" title="Scholasticism">scholasticism</a>. It succeeded the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> and preceded the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Age of Enlightenment</a>. It is often considered to be part of <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_philosophy" title="Early modern philosophy">early modern philosophy</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=17th-century_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The period is usually taken to start in the seventeenth century with the work of <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes" title="René Descartes">René Descartes</a>, who set much of the agenda as well as much of the methodology for those who came after him.  The period is typified in Europe by the great system-builders &#8212;  philosophers who present unified systems of <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">metaphysics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethics</a>, and often <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a> and the physical sciences too. <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">Immanuel Kant</a> classified his predecessors into two schools: the <a href="/wiki/Rationalism" title="Rationalism">rationalists</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Empiricism" title="Empiricism">empiricists</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_philosophy" title="Early modern philosophy">early modern philosophy</a> is often characterised in terms of a conflict between these schools.
</p><p>The three main rationalists are normally taken to have been <a href="/wiki/Descartes" class="mw-redirect" title="Descartes">Descartes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baruch_Spinoza" title="Baruch Spinoza">Baruch Spinoza</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_Leibniz" class="mw-redirect" title="Gottfried Leibniz">Gottfried Leibniz</a>. Building upon their English predecessors <a href="/wiki/Francis_Bacon" title="Francis Bacon">Francis Bacon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hobbes" title="Thomas Hobbes">Thomas Hobbes</a>, the three main empiricists were <a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">John Locke</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Berkeley" title="George Berkeley">George Berkeley</a>, and <a href="/wiki/David_Hume" title="David Hume">David Hume</a>.  The former were distinguished by the belief that, in principle (though not in practice), all knowledge can be gained by the power of our reason alone; the latter rejected this, believing that all knowledge has to come through the senses, from experience.  Thus the rationalists took <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> as their model for knowledge, and the empiricists took the <a href="/wiki/Physical_sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Physical sciences">physical sciences</a>. 
</p><p>This period also saw the birth of some of the classics of <a href="/wiki/Political_philosophy" title="Political philosophy">political philosophy</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hobbes" title="Thomas Hobbes">Thomas Hobbes</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Leviathan_(Hobbes)" class="mw-redirect" title="Leviathan (Hobbes)">Leviathan</a></i>, and <a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">John Locke</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Two_Treatises_of_Government" title="Two Treatises of Government">Two Treatises of Government</a></i>.
</p><p>The seventeenth century in Europe saw the culmination of the slow process of detachment of philosophy from <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theology</a>.  Thus, while philosophers still talked about &#8211; and even offered arguments for the existence of &#8211; a <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">deity</a>, this was done in the service of philosophical argument and thought.  (In the <a href="/wiki/The_Age_of_Enlightenment" class="mw-redirect" title="The Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a>, the "Age of Reason", <a href="/wiki/18th-century_philosophy" class="mw-redirect" title="18th-century philosophy">18th-century philosophy</a> was to go still further, leaving theology and <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religion</a> behind altogether.)
</p>
