	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Ó Creachmhaoil</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Ó Creachmhaoil</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Irish_people" title="Irish people">Irish</a> <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a>, often <a href="/wiki/Anglicisation_of_names" title="Anglicisation of names">anglicised</a> as <b>Craughwell</b>, <b>Croughwell</b>, <b>Crockwell</b>, and <b>Crowell</b><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. It was largely unknown outside of the south-east of <a href="/wiki/Counties_of_Ireland" title="Counties of Ireland">County</a> <a href="/wiki/Galway" title="Galway">Galway</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/Village" title="Village">village</a> of <a href="/wiki/Craughwell" title="Craughwell">Creachmhaoil</a> is also found, until the latter end of the 19th century when emigres established branches of the family which still thrive in <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Berkshire_County,_Massachusetts" title="Berkshire County, Massachusetts">Berkshire County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, among other places. The surname was found in <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a> in the 19th Century, having evidently arrived in the 17th Century (probably as part of the involuntary <a href="/wiki/Irish_immigration_to_Barbados" title="Irish immigration to Barbados">Irish immigration to Barbados</a> that followed the <a href="/wiki/Cromwellian_invasion_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Cromwellian invasion of Ireland">Cromwellian invasion of Ireland</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> but is now extinct there, possibly as a result of re-emigration (the Crockwells of Bermuda descend from a single white Barbadian who settled there in the 19th Century). Documentation on the origin of the surname is not recorded, but it is doubtless connected to the village.
</p><p><i>Ó</i>, in Irish surnames, indicates a grandson or descendant of the person whose <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> it precedes (as in <i>Ó Briain</i>: <i>grandson of Brian</i>). Creachmhaoil is not used as a given name in Ireland, and is actually a <a href="/wiki/Toponym" class="mw-redirect" title="Toponym">toponym</a>, composed of two Gaelic words.
</p><p><i>Creach</i>, which is related to <i>craig</i>, and <i>creag</i>, and the English word <i><a href="/wiki/Cliff" title="Cliff">crag</a></i>, refers to a rock (with which word it rhymes), or the bare rock crest of a hill (related words are <i>cruach</i>, for a mountain, pinnacle, or a rounded hill that stands apart...or for any type of pile, or heap, and 'cnoc', for a hill or eminence). An alternate etymology of <i>creach</i> is <i>plunder</i>, presumably in reference to herds of cattle, which were often targets of thefts and cattle raids amongst the Gaels. The usual Gaelic word for cattle is <i>crodh</i>, often Anglicised in place-names as <i>crow</i>, although the words <i>cro</i>, <i>crocharsach</i>, and <i>crò</i> are all connected with sheep, sheep enclosures or meadows.
</p><p>A <i>maol</i> is a round-shaped hill or mountain, bare of trees. It is anglicised as <i>mull</i>, and is common in Irish and Scottish place names such as the <a href="/wiki/Mull_of_Kintyre" title="Mull of Kintyre">Mull of Kintyre</a>. Gaelic spelling rules require that maol, following creach, be lenited; that is, an h is inserted after the first letter, providing the first letter is a consonant (and not an <i>l</i>, <i>n</i>, or <i>r</i>). This h makes the preceding consonant silent, or changes its sound (<i>mh</i>, or <i>bh</i>, for instance, are silent or sound like an English <i>v</i> or <i>w</i>). Gaelic spelling rules also require that, with the first letter lenited, the last vowel should be slender (an <i>i</i>, or an <i>e</i>). As both vowels in maol are broad (<i>a</i>, <i>o</i>, <i>u</i>), an <i>i</i> is inserted after. These two changes alter the sound of <i>maol</i> (rhymes with <i>mull</i>) to <i>mhaoil</i> (rhymes with <i>uell</i>, or <i>well</i>). The sound of the two word together, therefore, sounds to an English ear like <i>Crockwell</i>, or <i>Craughwell</i>, and it is Anglicised thus (the Gaelic personal names <i>Seán</i> (John) and <i>Seamus</i> (James) became <i>Iain</i> and <i>Hamish</i> in Scotland by similar means).
</p><p>The complete toponym is used, today, to connote the village in Galway, but presumably was adopted from a nearby hill. The village is too small to have been known far afield, and the surname is largely restricted in Ireland to County Galway. <i>Ó Creachmhaoil</i>, therefore, is presumably a <a href="/wiki/Toponymic_surname" title="Toponymic surname">Toponymic surname</a> adopted by villagers from Creachmhaoil upon their moving to other parts of Galway.
</p><p>Notable bearers of the name include American painter <a href="/wiki/Douglass_Crockwell" title="Douglass Crockwell">Douglass Crockwell</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> of the <a href="/wiki/Seanad_%C3%89ireann" title="Seanad Éireann">Seanad Éireann</a>, Bermudian parliamentarian <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shawn_Crockwell&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Shawn Crockwell (page does not exist)">Shawn Crockwell</a>, JP, MP,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/FIFA" title="FIFA">FIFA</a>-certified Bermudian <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> <a href="/wiki/Referee" title="Referee">referee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carlyle_Crockwell" title="Carlyle Crockwell">Carlyle McNeil Eugene Crockwell</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Bermudian footballers <i>Denzel Crockwell</i> (of <i>Ireland Rangers FC</i>),<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Mikkail_Crockwell" title="Mikkail Crockwell">Mikkail Crockwell</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-9" href="/wiki/Leslie_Crockwell" title="Leslie Crockwell">Leslie Crockwell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Record" class="mw-redirect" title="Guinness World Record">Guinness World Record</a> holding Craughwell (page does not exist)">Thomas J. Craughwell</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> film director and actor Charles Croughwell, <sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> sword-maker Michael "Irish Mike" Craughwell (star of the <a href="/wiki/Discovery_Channel" title="Discovery Channel">Discovery Channel</a> television series <a href="/wiki/Big_Giant_Swords" title="Big Giant Swords">Big Giant Swords</a>), <sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and educator and <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a> film critic Kathleen Craughwell, <sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>.
</p>
