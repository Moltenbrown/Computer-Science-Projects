<p>The <b>Shang dynasty</b>  (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%95%86%E6%9C%9D" class="extiw" title="wikt:商朝">商朝</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>&#58; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Shāngcháo</span></i>), also historically known as the <b>Yin dynasty</b> (<span lang="zh"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%AE%B7%E4%BB%A3" class="extiw" title="wikt:殷代">殷代</a></span>; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Yīndài</span></i>), was a <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a> <a href="/wiki/Chinese_dynasties" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese dynasties">dynasty</a> that ruled in the Lower <a href="/wiki/Yellow_River" title="Yellow River">Yellow River</a> Valley in the second millennium&#160;BC, succeeding the semi-mythical <a href="/wiki/Xia_dynasty" title="Xia dynasty">Xia dynasty</a> and followed by the <a href="/wiki/Zhou_dynasty" title="Zhou dynasty">Zhou dynasty</a>. The classic account of the Shang comes from texts such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Documents" title="Book of Documents">Book of Documents</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bamboo_Annals" title="Bamboo Annals">Bamboo Annals</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Records_of_the_Grand_Historian" title="Records of the Grand Historian">Records of the Grand Historian</a></i>.  According to the traditional chronology based on calculations made approximately 2,000 years ago by <a href="/wiki/Liu_Xin_(scholar)" title="Liu Xin (scholar)">Liu Xin</a>, the Shang ruled from 1766 to 1122&#160;BC, but according to the chronology based upon the "current text" of <i>Bamboo Annals</i>, they ruled from 1556 to 1046&#160;BC. The state-sponsored <a href="/wiki/Xia%E2%80%93Shang%E2%80%93Zhou_Chronology_Project" title="Xia–Shang–Zhou Chronology Project">Xia–Shang–Zhou Chronology Project</a> dated them from c. 1600 to 1046&#160;BC based on the carbon 14 dates of the Erligang site.
</p><p>The Shang dynasty is the earliest dynasty of traditional Chinese history firmly supported by archaeological evidence. Excavation at the <a href="/wiki/Yinxu" title="Yinxu">Ruins of Yin</a> (near modern-day <a href="/wiki/Anyang" title="Anyang">Anyang</a>), which has been identified as the last Shang capital, uncovered eleven major royal tombs and the foundations of palaces and ritual sites, containing weapons of war and remains from both animal and human sacrifices.  Tens of thousands of <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jade" title="Jade">jade</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stone" class="mw-redirect" title="Stone">stone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bone</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramic</a> artifacts have been found.
</p><p>The Anyang site has yielded the earliest known body of <a href="/wiki/Written_Chinese" title="Written Chinese">Chinese writing</a>, mostly <a href="/wiki/Divination" title="Divination">divinations</a> inscribed on <a href="/wiki/Oracle_bone" title="Oracle bone">oracle bones</a> – turtle shells, ox <a href="/wiki/Scapula" title="Scapula">scapulae</a>, or other bones. More than 20,000 were discovered in the initial scientific excavations during the 1920s and 1930s, and over four times as many have been found since.  The inscriptions provide critical insight into many topics from the politics, economy, and religious practices to the art and medicine of this early stage of <a href="/wiki/History_of_China" title="History of China">Chinese civilization</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKeightley2000_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeightley2000-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_accounts">Traditional accounts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shang_dynasty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Traditional accounts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many events concerning the Shang dynasty are mentioned in various <a href="/wiki/Chinese_classics" title="Chinese classics">Chinese classics</a>, including the <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Documents" title="Book of Documents">Book of Documents</a></i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Mencius_(book)" title="Mencius (book)">Mencius</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Zuo_Zhuan" class="mw-redirect" title="Zuo Zhuan">Zuo Zhuan</a></i>.  Working from all the available documents, the <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasty</a> historian <a href="/wiki/Sima_Qian" title="Sima Qian">Sima Qian</a> assembled a sequential account of the Shang dynasty as part of his <i><a href="/wiki/Records_of_the_Grand_Historian" title="Records of the Grand Historian">Records of the Grand Historian</a></i>.  His history describes some events in detail, while in other cases only the name of a king is given.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKeightley1999233–235_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeightley1999233–235-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  A closely related, but slightly different, account is given by the <i><a href="/wiki/Bamboo_Annals" title="Bamboo Annals">Bamboo Annals</a></i>.  The <i>Annals</i> were interred in 296&#160;BC, but the text has a complex history and the authenticity of the surviving versions is controversial.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKeightley1978b_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeightley1978b-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The name <i>Yīn</i> (殷) is used by Sima Qian for the dynasty, and in the "current text" version of the <i>Bamboo Annals</i> for both the dynasty and its final capital. It has been a popular name for the Shang throughout history. Since the <i>Records of Emperors and Kings</i> by <a href="/wiki/Huangfu_Mi" title="Huangfu Mi">Huangfu Mi</a> (3rd century AD), it has often been used specifically to describe the later half of the Shang dynasty. In Japan and Korea, the Shang are still referred to almost exclusively as the Yin (<i>In</i>) dynasty. However, it seems to have been a <a href="/wiki/Zhou_dynasty" title="Zhou dynasty">Zhou</a> name for the earlier dynasty.  The word does not appear in the oracle bones, which refer to the state as <i>Shāng</i> (商), and the capital as <i>Dàyì Shāng</i> (大邑商 "Great Settlement Shang").<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKeightley1999232_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeightley1999232-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  It also does not appear in securely-dated Western Zhou bronze inscriptions.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKeightley1978axiv_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeightley1978axiv-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding_myth">Founding myth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shang_dynasty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Founding myth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The founding myth of the Shang dynasty is described by Sima Qian in the <i>Annals of the Yin</i>.  In the text, a woman named <a href="/wiki/Jiandi" title="Jiandi">Jiandi</a> (簡狄), who was the second wife of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Ku" title="Emperor Ku">Emperor Ku</a>, swallowed an egg dropped by a black bird (玄鳥) and subsequently gave birth miraculously to <a href="/wiki/Xie_of_Shang" title="Xie of Shang">Xie</a> (偰) — also appearing as Qi (契).  Xie is said to have helped <a href="/wiki/Yu_the_Great" title="Yu the Great">Yu the Great</a> to control the <a href="/wiki/Great_Flood_(China)" title="Great Flood (China)">Great Flood</a> and for his service to have been granted a place called Shang as a fief.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dynastic_course">Dynastic course</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shang_dynasty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dynastic course">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <i>Annals of the Yin</i>, Sima Qian writes that the dynasty was founded 13 generations after <a href="/wiki/Xie_of_Shang" title="Xie of Shang">Xie</a>, when Xie's descendant <a href="/wiki/Tang_of_Shang" title="Tang of Shang">Tang</a> overthrew the impious and cruel final <a href="/wiki/Xia_dynasty" title="Xia dynasty">Xia</a> ruler in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Mingtiao" title="Battle of Mingtiao">Battle of Mingtiao</a>.  The <i>Records</i> recount events from the reigns of Tang, <a href="/wiki/Tai_Jia" title="Tai Jia">Tai Jia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tai_Wu" title="Tai Wu">Tai Wu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pan_Geng" title="Pan Geng">Pan Geng</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wu_Ding" title="Wu Ding">Wu Ding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wu_Yi_of_Shang" title="Wu Yi of Shang">Wu Yi</a> and the depraved final king <a href="/wiki/King_Zhou_of_Shang" title="King Zhou of Shang">Di Xin</a>, but the rest of the Shang rulers are merely mentioned by name.  According to the <i>Records</i>, the Shang moved their capital five times, with the final move to Yin in the reign of Pan Geng inaugurating the golden age of the dynasty.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKeightley1999233_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeightley1999233-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Di Xin, the last Shang king, is said to have committed suicide after his army was defeated by <a href="/wiki/King_Wu_of_Zhou" title="King Wu of Zhou">Wu of Zhou</a>. Legends say that his army and his equipped slaves betrayed him by joining the Zhou rebels in the decisive <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Muye" title="Battle of Muye">Battle of Muye</a>. According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Yi_Zhou_Shu" title="Yi Zhou Shu">Yi Zhou Shu</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Mencius" title="Mencius">Mencius</a> the battle was very bloody. The classic, <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming</a>-era novel <i><a href="/wiki/Fengshen_Yanyi" class="mw-redirect" title="Fengshen Yanyi">Fengshen Yanyi</a></i> retells the story of the war between Shang and Zhou as a conflict with rival factions of gods supporting different sides in the war.
</p><p>After the Shang were defeated, King Wu allowed Di Xin's son <a href="/wiki/Wu_Geng" title="Wu Geng">Wu Geng</a> to rule the Shang as a <a href="/wiki/Vassal" title="Vassal">vassal</a> kingdom.  However, Zhou Wu sent three of his brothers and an army to ensure that Wu Geng would not rebel.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> After Zhou Wu's death, the Shang joined the <a href="/wiki/Rebellion_of_the_Three_Guards" title="Rebellion of the Three Guards">Rebellion of the Three Guards</a> against the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Zhou" title="Duke of Zhou">Duke of Zhou</a>, but the rebellion collapsed after three years, leaving Zhou in control of Shang territory.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Descendants_of_the_Shang_royal_family">Descendants of the Shang royal family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shang_dynasty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Descendants of the Shang royal family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After Shang's collapse, Zhou's rulers forcibly relocated "Yin diehards" (殷頑) and scattered them throughout Zhou territory.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Some surviving members of the Shang royal family collectively changed their surname from the ancestral name Zi (子) to the name of their fallen dynasty, Yin. The family retained an aristocratic standing and often provided needed administrative services to the succeeding Zhou dynasty. The <i><a href="/wiki/Records_of_the_Grand_Historian" title="Records of the Grand Historian">Records of the Grand Historian</a></i> states that <a href="/wiki/King_Cheng_of_Zhou" title="King Cheng of Zhou">King Cheng of Zhou</a>, with the support of his regent and uncle, the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Zhou" title="Duke of Zhou">Duke of Zhou</a>, <a href="/wiki/Feoffment" title="Feoffment">enfeoffed</a> Weiziqi (微子啟), a brother of Di Xin, as the Duke of <a href="/wiki/Song_(state)" title="Song (state)">Song</a>, with its capital at <a href="/wiki/Shangqiu" title="Shangqiu">Shangqiu</a>. This practice was known as <a href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E4%BA%8C%E7%8E%8B%E4%B8%89%E6%81%AA" class="extiw" title="zh:二王三恪">二王三恪</a> ("enfeoffment of three generations for two kings"). The Dukes of Song would maintain rites honoring the Shang kings until <a href="/wiki/Qi_(state)" title="Qi (state)">Qi</a> conquered Song in 286&#160;BC. <a href="/wiki/Confucius" title="Confucius">Confucius</a> was possibly a descendant of the Shang Kings through the Dukes of Song.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Eastern Han dynasty bestowed the title of Duke of Song and <i>"Duke Who Continues and Honours the Yin"</i> (<a href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E6%AE%B7%E7%B4%B9%E5%98%89%E5%85%AC" class="extiw" title="zh:殷紹嘉公">殷紹嘉公</a>) upon Kong An (<a href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E5%AD%94%E5%AE%89_(%E6%9D%B1%E6%BC%A2)" class="extiw" title="zh:孔安 (東漢)">孔安 (東漢)</a>) because he was part of the Shang dynasty's legacy.<sup id="cite_ref-Crespigny2006_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crespigny2006-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> This branch of the Confucius family is a separate branch from the line that held the title of Marquis of Fengsheng village and later Duke Yansheng.
</p><p>Another remnant of the Shang established the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Chinese_states" title="Ancient Chinese states">vassal state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Guzhu" title="Guzhu">Guzhu</a> (located in present-day <a href="/wiki/Tangshan" title="Tangshan">Tangshan</a>), which <a href="/wiki/Duke_Huan_of_Qi" title="Duke Huan of Qi">Duke Huan of Qi</a> destroyed.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Many Shang clans that migrated northeast after the dynasty's collapse were integrated into <a href="/wiki/Yan_(state)" title="Yan (state)">Yan</a> culture during the <a href="/wiki/Western_Zhou" title="Western Zhou">Western Zhou</a> period.  These clans maintained an élite status and continued practicing the sacrificial and burial traditions of the Shang.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESun2006_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESun2006-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Both Korean and Chinese legends, including reports in the <i>Book of Documents</i> and the <i>Bamboo Annals</i>, state that a disgruntled Shang prince named <a href="/wiki/Jizi" title="Jizi">Jizi</a>, who had refused to cede power to the Zhou, left China with a small army. According to these legends, he founded a state known as <a href="/wiki/Gija_Joseon" title="Gija Joseon">Gija Joseon</a> in northwest Korea during the <a href="/wiki/Gojoseon" title="Gojoseon">Gojoseon</a> period of ancient Korean history. However, scholars debate the historical accuracy of these legends.
</p>

<p>Before the 20th century, the <a href="/wiki/Zhou_dynasty" title="Zhou dynasty">Zhou dynasty</a> (1046–256&#160;BC) was the earliest Chinese dynasty that could be verified from its own records.  However, during the <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">Song dynasty</a> (960–1279 AD), <a href="/wiki/Antiquarian" title="Antiquarian">antiquarians</a> collected <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a> ritual vessels attributed to the Shang era, some of which bore inscriptions.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFairbankGoldman200633_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFairbankGoldman200633-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Yellow_River_valley">Yellow River valley</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shang_dynasty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Yellow River valley">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1899, several scholars noticed that Chinese pharmacists were selling "<a href="/wiki/Oracle_bone" title="Oracle bone">dragon bones</a>" marked with curious and archaic characters.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFairbankGoldman200633_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFairbankGoldman200633-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> These were finally traced back in 1928 to a site (now called <a href="/wiki/Yinxu" title="Yinxu">Yinxu</a>) near <a href="/wiki/Anyang" title="Anyang">Anyang</a>, north of the <a href="/wiki/Yellow_River" title="Yellow River">Yellow River</a> in modern <a href="/wiki/Henan" title="Henan">Henan</a> province, where the <a href="/wiki/Academia_Sinica" title="Academia Sinica">Academia Sinica</a> undertook archeological excavation until the <a href="/wiki/Second_Sino-Japanese_War" title="Second Sino-Japanese War">Japanese invasion</a> in 1937.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFairbankGoldman200633_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFairbankGoldman200633-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Archaeologists focused on the Yellow River valley in Henan as the most likely site of the states described in the traditional histories.
After 1950, the remnants of the earlier walled settlement of <a href="/wiki/Zhengzhou_Shang_City" title="Zhengzhou Shang City">Shang City</a> were discovered near <a href="/wiki/Zhengzhou" title="Zhengzhou">Zhengzhou</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFairbankGoldman200633_22-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFairbankGoldman200633-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
It has been determined that the earth walls at Zhengzhou, erected in the 15th century&#160;BC, would have been 20&#160;m (66&#160;ft) wide at the base, rising to a height of 8&#160;m (26&#160;ft), and formed a roughly rectangular wall 7&#160;km (4&#160;mi) around the ancient city.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFairbankGoldman200634_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFairbankGoldman200634-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-needham_volume_4_part_3_43_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-needham_volume_4_part_3_43-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The rammed earth construction of these walls was an inherited tradition, since much older fortifications of this type have been found at Chinese <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> sites of the <a href="/wiki/Longshan_culture" title="Longshan culture">Longshan culture</a> <span class="nowrap">(c. 3000–2000 BC)</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFairbankGoldman200634_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFairbankGoldman200634-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1959, the site of the <a href="/wiki/Erlitou_culture" title="Erlitou culture">Erlitou culture</a> was found in Yanshi, south of the Yellow River near <a href="/wiki/Luoyang" title="Luoyang">Luoyang</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFairbankGoldman200634_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFairbankGoldman200634-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">Radiocarbon dating</a> suggests that the Erlitou culture flourished ca. 2100&#160;BC to 1800&#160;BC. They built large palaces, suggesting the existence of an organized state.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFairbankGoldman200634–35_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFairbankGoldman200634–35-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
In 1983, <a href="/wiki/Yanshi_Shang_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Yanshi Shang City">Yanshi Shang City</a> was discovered 6 kilometres (3.7&#160;mi) north-east of the Erlitou site in Yanshi's Shixianggou Township. This was a large walled city dating from 1600&#160;BC. It had an area of nearly 200 hectares (490 acres) and featured pottery characteristic of the <a href="/wiki/Erligang_culture" title="Erligang culture">Erligang culture</a>.
</p><p>The remains of a walled city of about 470 hectares (1,200 acres) were discovered in 1999 across the <a href="/wiki/Huan_River" title="Huan River">Huan River</a> from the well explored Yinxu site.  The city, now known as <a href="/wiki/Huanbei" title="Huanbei">Huanbei</a>, was apparently occupied for less than a century and destroyed shortly before the construction of the Yinxu complex.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Chinese historians were accustomed to the notion of one dynasty succeeding another, and readily identified the Erligang and Erlitou sites with the early Shang and <a href="/wiki/Xia_dynasty" title="Xia dynasty">Xia dynasty</a> of traditional histories.
The actual political situation in early China may have been more complicated, with the Xia and Shang being political entities that existed concurrently, just as the early <a href="/wiki/Zhou_dynasty" title="Zhou dynasty">Zhou</a>, who established the successor state of the Shang, are known to have existed at the same time as the Shang.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESun2006_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESun2006-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> It has also been suggested the Xia legend originated as a Shang myth of an earlier people who were their opposites.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAllan199163_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAllan199163-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_sites">Other sites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shang_dynasty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other sites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Erligang_culture" title="Erligang culture">Erligang culture</a> centred on the Zhengzhou site is found across a wide area of China, even as far northeast as the area of modern <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>, where at least one burial in this region during this period contained both Erligang-style <a href="/wiki/Bronzes" class="mw-redirect" title="Bronzes">bronzes</a> and local-style <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> jewelry.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESun2006_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESun2006-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>  The discovery of a <a href="/wiki/Chenggu" class="mw-redirect" title="Chenggu">Chenggu</a>-style <i>ge</i> <a href="/wiki/Dagger-axe" title="Dagger-axe">dagger-axe</a> at Xiaohenan demonstrates that even at this early stage of Chinese history, there were some ties between the distant areas of north China.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESun2006_21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESun2006-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
The <a href="/wiki/Panlongcheng" title="Panlongcheng">Panlongcheng</a> site in the middle <a href="/wiki/Yangtze_valley" class="mw-redirect" title="Yangtze valley">Yangtze valley</a> was an important regional center of the <a href="/wiki/Erligang_culture" title="Erligang culture">Erligang culture</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBagley1999168–171_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBagley1999168–171-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Accidental finds elsewhere in China have revealed advanced civilizations contemporaneous with but culturally unlike the settlement at Anyang, such as the walled city of <a href="/wiki/Sanxingdui" title="Sanxingdui">Sanxingdui</a> in Sichuan. Western scholars are hesitant to designate such settlements as belonging to the Shang dynasty.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBagley1999124–125_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBagley1999124–125-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Also unlike the Shang, there is no known evidence that the Sanxingdui culture had a system of writing.  The late Shang state at Anyang is thus generally considered the first verifiable civilization in Chinese history.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKeightley1999232_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeightley1999232-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In contrast, the earliest layers of the <a href="/wiki/Wucheng_culture" title="Wucheng culture">Wucheng site</a>, pre-dating Anyang, have yielded pottery fragments containing short sequences of symbols, suggesting that they may be a form of writing quite different in form from <a href="/wiki/Oracle_bone_script" title="Oracle bone script">oracle bone characters</a>, but the sample is too small for decipherment.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilkinson2013669_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilkinson2013669-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWagner199320_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWagner199320-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECheung1983_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECheung1983-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetic_studies">Genetic studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shang_dynasty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Genetic studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A study of <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a> (inherited in the maternal line) from Yinxu graves showed similarity with modern northern <a href="/wiki/Han_Chinese" title="Han Chinese">Han Chinese</a>, but significant differences from southern Han Chinese.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Absolute_chronology">Absolute chronology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shang_dynasty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Absolute chronology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The earliest securely dated event in Chinese history is the start of the <a href="/wiki/Gonghe_Regency" title="Gonghe Regency">Gonghe Regency</a> in 841 BC, early in the <a href="/wiki/Zhou_dynasty" title="Zhou dynasty">Zhou dynasty</a>, a date first established by the <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasty</a> historian <a href="/wiki/Sima_Qian" title="Sima Qian">Sima Qian</a>.  Attempts to establish earlier dates have been plagued by doubts about the origin and transmission of traditional texts and the difficulties in their interpretation.  More recent attempts have compared the traditional histories with archaeological and astronomical data.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELee200216–17_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELee200216–17-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
At least 44 dates for the end of the dynasty have been proposed, ranging from 1130 BC to 1018 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELee200232_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELee200232-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>The traditional dates of the dynasty, from 1766 BC to 1222 BC, were calculated by <a href="/wiki/Liu_Xin_(scholar)" title="Liu Xin (scholar)">Liu Xin</a> during the Han dynasty.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKeightley1999248_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeightley1999248-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>A calculation based on the "old text" of the <i><a href="/wiki/Bamboo_Annals" title="Bamboo Annals">Bamboo Annals</a></i> yields dates of 1523 BC to 1027 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKeightley1999248_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeightley1999248-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>David Pankenier, by attempting to identify astronomical events mentioned in Zhou texts, dated the beginning of the dynasty at 1554 BC and its overthrow at 1046 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKeightley1999248_37-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeightley1999248-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Xia%E2%80%93Shang%E2%80%93Zhou_Chronology_Project" title="Xia–Shang–Zhou Chronology Project">Xia–Shang–Zhou Chronology Project</a> identified the establishment of the dynasty with the foundation of an <a href="/wiki/Erligang_culture" title="Erligang culture">Erligang culture</a> walled city at <a href="/wiki/Yanshi" title="Yanshi">Yanshi</a>, dated at c. 1600 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELee200228_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELee200228-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>  The project also arrived at an end date of 1046 BC, based on a combination of the astronomical evidence considered by Pankenier and dating of arcaeological layers.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELee200231–34_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELee200231–34-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_Shang_at_Anyang">Late Shang at Anyang</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shang_dynasty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Late Shang at Anyang">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Oracle_bones_pit.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Oracle_bones_pit.JPG/220px-Oracle_bones_pit.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="180" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Oracle_bones_pit.JPG/330px-Oracle_bones_pit.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Oracle_bones_pit.JPG/440px-Oracle_bones_pit.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2743" data-file-height="2240" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Oracle_bones_pit.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Oracle bones pit at Yin</div></div></div>
<p>The oldest extant direct records date from approximately 1200&#160;BC at Anyang, covering the reigns of the last nine Shang kings.
The Shang had a fully developed system of writing, preserved on <a href="/wiki/Chinese_bronze_inscriptions" title="Chinese bronze inscriptions">bronze inscriptions</a> and a small number of other writings on pottery, jade and other stones, horn, etc., but most prolifically on <a href="/wiki/Oracle_bones" class="mw-redirect" title="Oracle bones">oracle bones</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEQiu200060_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEQiu200060-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> The complexity and sophistication of this writing system indicates an earlier period of development, but direct evidence of that development is still lacking.
Other advances included the invention of many musical instruments and celestial observations of <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> and various <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comets</a> by Shang astronomers.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Their civilization was based on <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> and augmented by <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">hunting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animal_husbandry" title="Animal husbandry">animal husbandry</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-PoI_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PoI-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
In addition to war, the Shang also practiced <a href="/wiki/Human_sacrifice" title="Human sacrifice">human sacrifice</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NGC_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NGC-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
Crania of sacrificial victims have been found to be similar to modern Chinese ones (based on comparisons with remains from <a href="/wiki/Hainan" title="Hainan">Hainan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Cowry" class="mw-redirect" title="Cowry">Cowry shells</a> were also excavated at <a href="/wiki/Anyang" title="Anyang">Anyang</a>, suggesting trade with coast-dwellers, but there was very limited sea trade since China was isolated from other large civilizations during the Shang period.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFairbankGoldman200635_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFairbankGoldman200635-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Trade relations and diplomatic ties with other formidable powers via the <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road" title="Silk Road">Silk Road</a> and Chinese voyages to the Indian Ocean did not exist until the reign of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Wu_of_Han" title="Emperor Wu of Han">Emperor Wu</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasty</a> (206&#160;BC–221 AD).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESun1989161–167_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESun1989161–167-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChen200267–71_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChen200267–71-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Court_life">Court life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shang_dynasty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Court life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Shang_dynasty_inscribed_tortoise_plastron.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Shang_dynasty_inscribed_tortoise_plastron.jpg/150px-Shang_dynasty_inscribed_tortoise_plastron.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="247" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Shang_dynasty_inscribed_tortoise_plastron.jpg/225px-Shang_dynasty_inscribed_tortoise_plastron.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Shang_dynasty_inscribed_tortoise_plastron.jpg/300px-Shang_dynasty_inscribed_tortoise_plastron.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1186" data-file-height="1954" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Shang_dynasty_inscribed_tortoise_plastron.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Oracle shell with inscriptions.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tomb_Fu_Hao_YinXu.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Tomb_Fu_Hao_YinXu.jpg/220px-Tomb_Fu_Hao_YinXu.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Tomb_Fu_Hao_YinXu.jpg/330px-Tomb_Fu_Hao_YinXu.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Tomb_Fu_Hao_YinXu.jpg/440px-Tomb_Fu_Hao_YinXu.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tomb_Fu_Hao_YinXu.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bronzewares from the excavated <a href="/wiki/Tomb_of_Fu_Hao" title="Tomb of Fu Hao">tomb of Fu Hao</a></div></div></div>
<p>At the excavated royal palace of <a href="/wiki/Yinxu" title="Yinxu">Yinxu</a>, large stone pillar bases were found along with <a href="/wiki/Rammed_earth" title="Rammed earth">rammed earth</a> foundations and platforms, which according to Fairbank, were "as hard as cement".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFairbankGoldman200633_22-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFairbankGoldman200633-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>  These foundations in turn originally supported 53 buildings of wooden <a href="/wiki/Timber_framing" title="Timber framing">post-and-beam</a> construction.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFairbankGoldman200633_22-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFairbankGoldman200633-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> In close proximity to the main palatial complex, there were underground pits used for storage, servants' quarters, and housing quarters.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFairbankGoldman200633_22-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFairbankGoldman200633-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many Shang royal tombs had been tunneled into and ravaged by <a href="/wiki/Grave_robbing" class="mw-redirect" title="Grave robbing">grave robbers</a> in ancient times,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThorp1981239_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThorp1981239-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> but in the spring of 1976, the <a href="/wiki/Tomb_of_Fu_Hao" title="Tomb of Fu Hao">discovery of Tomb 5</a> at Yinxu revealed a tomb that was not only undisturbed, but one of the most richly furnished Shang tombs that archaeologists had yet come across.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThorp1981240_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThorp1981240-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> With over 200 bronze ritual vessels and 109 inscriptions of <a href="/wiki/Fu_Hao" title="Fu Hao">Lady Fu Hao</a>'s name, <a href="/wiki/Zheng_Zhenxiang" title="Zheng Zhenxiang">Zheng Zhenxiang</a> and other archaeologists realized they had stumbled across the tomb of <a href="/wiki/Wu_Ding" title="Wu Ding">King Wu Ding</a>'s most famous consort, Fu Hao, who is mentioned in 170 to 180 Shang oracle bone inscriptions, and who was also renowned as a military general.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThorp1981240,_245_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThorp1981240,_245-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Along with bronze vessels, <a href="/wiki/Stoneware" title="Stoneware">stoneware</a> and pottery vessels, bronze weapons, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_jade" title="Chinese jade">jade</a> figures and hair combs, and bone hairpins were found.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThorp1981242,_245_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThorp1981242,_245-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELi1980393–394_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELi1980393–394-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELernerMurckFordHearn198577_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELernerMurckFordHearn198577-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> Historian Robert L. Thorp states that the large assortment of weapons and ritual vessels in her tomb correlate with the <a href="/wiki/Oracle_bone_script" title="Oracle bone script">oracle bone accounts</a> of her military career and involvement in Wu Ding's <a href="/wiki/Ancestor_worship" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancestor worship">ritual ancestral sacrifices</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThorp1981245_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThorp1981245-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The capital was the center of court life. Over time, court rituals to appease spirits developed, and in addition to his secular duties, the king would serve as the head of the <a href="/wiki/Ancestor_worship" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancestor worship">ancestor worship</a> cult. Often, the king would even perform oracle bone divinations himself, especially near the end of the dynasty. Evidence from excavations of the royal tombs indicates that royalty were buried with articles of value, presumably for use in the afterlife. Perhaps for the same reason, hundreds of commoners, who may have been <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slaves</a>, were buried alive with the royal corpse.
</p><p>A line of hereditary Shang kings ruled over much of northern China, and Shang troops fought frequent wars with neighboring settlements and nomadic herdsmen from the inner Asian <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppes</a>. The Shang king, in his oracular divinations, repeatedly showed concern about the <i>fang</i> groups, the barbarians living outside of the civilized <i>tu</i> regions, which made up the center of Shang territory.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="are tu areas all of Shang or only the Shang heartland? (September 2010)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  In particular, the <i>tufang</i> group of the <a href="/wiki/Yan_Mountains" title="Yan Mountains">Yanshan</a> region were regularly mentioned as hostile to the Shang.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESun2006_21-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESun2006-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Apart from their role as the head military commanders, Shang kings also asserted their social supremacy by acting as the high priests of society and leading the divination ceremonies.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEbreyWalthallPalais200614_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEbreyWalthallPalais200614-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> As the oracle bone texts reveal, the Shang kings were viewed as the best qualified members of society to offer sacrifices to their royal ancestors and to the high god <a href="/wiki/Shangdi" title="Shangdi">Di</a>, who in their beliefs was responsible for the rain, wind, and thunder.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEbreyWalthallPalais200614_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEbreyWalthallPalais200614-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shang_dynasty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Shang_bronze_masks,_16-14th.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Shang_bronze_masks%2C_16-14th.JPG/220px-Shang_bronze_masks%2C_16-14th.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Shang_bronze_masks%2C_16-14th.JPG/330px-Shang_bronze_masks%2C_16-14th.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Shang_bronze_masks%2C_16-14th.JPG/440px-Shang_bronze_masks%2C_16-14th.JPG 2x" data-file-width="576" data-file-height="384" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Shang_bronze_masks,_16-14th.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chinese Shang dynasty bronze face masks, 16th–14th century BC</div></div></div>
<p>Shang religious rituals featured divination and sacrifice.  The degree to which <a href="/wiki/Shamanism" title="Shamanism">shamanism</a> was a central aspect of Shang religion is a subject of debate.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChang1994_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChang1994-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKeightley1998_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeightley1998-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There were six main recipients of sacrifice: (1) Di (the High God), (2) nature powers like the sun and mountain powers, (3) former lords, deceased humans who had been added to the dynastic pantheon, (4) pre-dynastic ancestors, (5) dynastic ancestors, and (6) royal wives who were ancestors of the present king.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKeightley1999253–254_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeightley1999253–254-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Shang believed that their ancestors held power over them and performed divination rituals to secure their approval for planned actions.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKeightley2004_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeightley2004-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>  Divination involved cracking a turtle carapace or ox scapula to answer a question, and to then record the response to that question on the bone itself.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChang1994_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChang1994-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>  It is unknown what criteria the diviners used to determine the response, but it is believed to be the sound or pattern of the cracks on the bone.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The Shang also seem to have believed in an afterlife, as evidenced by the elaborate burial tombs built for deceased rulers.  Often "carriages, utensils, sacrificial vessels, [and] weapons" would be included in the tomb.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith1961_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith1961-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>  A king's burial involved the burial of up to several hundred humans and horses as well to accompany the king into the afterlife, in some cases even numbering four hundred.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith1961_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith1961-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>  Finally, tombs included ornaments such as jade, which the Shang may have believed to protect against decay or confer immortality.
</p><p>The Shang religion was highly bureaucratic and meticulously ordered.  Oracle bones contained descriptions of the date, ritual, person, ancestor, and questions associated with the divination.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChang1994_57-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChang1994-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>  Tombs displayed highly ordered arrangements of bones, with groups of skeletons laid out facing the same direction.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bronze_working">Bronze working</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shang_dynasty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Bronze working">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chinese_ritual_bronzes" title="Chinese ritual bronzes">Chinese ritual bronzes</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bronze_square_ding_(cauldron)_with_human_faces.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Bronze_square_ding_%28cauldron%29_with_human_faces.jpg/220px-Bronze_square_ding_%28cauldron%29_with_human_faces.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="272" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Bronze_square_ding_%28cauldron%29_with_human_faces.jpg/330px-Bronze_square_ding_%28cauldron%29_with_human_faces.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Bronze_square_ding_%28cauldron%29_with_human_faces.jpg/440px-Bronze_square_ding_%28cauldron%29_with_human_faces.jpg 2x" data-file-width="793" data-file-height="981" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bronze_square_ding_(cauldron)_with_human_faces.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Shang dynasty bronze vessel ding (sacrificial vessel)</div></div></div>
<p>Chinese bronze casting and pottery advanced during the Shang dynasty, with bronze typically being used for ritually significant, rather than primarily utilitarian, items.
As far back as c. 1500&#160;BC, the early Shang dynasty engaged in large-scale production of <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a>-ware vessels and weapons.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEbreyWalthallPalais200617_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEbreyWalthallPalais200617-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> This production required a large labor force that could handle the mining, refining, and transportation of the necessary <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> ores. This in turn created a need for official managers that could oversee both hard-laborers and skilled artisans and craftsmen.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEbreyWalthallPalais200617_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEbreyWalthallPalais200617-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> The Shang royal court and aristocrats required a vast number of different bronze vessels for various ceremonial purposes and events of religious <a href="/wiki/Divination" title="Divination">divination</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEbreyWalthallPalais200617_62-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEbreyWalthallPalais200617-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>  Ceremonial rules even decreed how many bronze containers of each type a nobleman or noblewoman of a certain rank could own. With the increased amount of bronze available, the army could also better equip itself with an assortment of bronze weaponry. Bronze was also used for the fittings of <a href="/wiki/Spoke" title="Spoke">spoke</a>-wheeled <a href="/wiki/Chariot_(Ancient_China)" class="mw-redirect" title="Chariot (Ancient China)">chariots</a>, which appeared in China around 1200&#160;BC.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEbreyWalthallPalais200614_56-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEbreyWalthallPalais200614-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military">Military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shang_dynasty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CMOC_Treasures_of_Ancient_China_exhibit_-_bronze_battle_axe.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/CMOC_Treasures_of_Ancient_China_exhibit_-_bronze_battle_axe.jpg/220px-CMOC_Treasures_of_Ancient_China_exhibit_-_bronze_battle_axe.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="198" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/CMOC_Treasures_of_Ancient_China_exhibit_-_bronze_battle_axe.jpg/330px-CMOC_Treasures_of_Ancient_China_exhibit_-_bronze_battle_axe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/CMOC_Treasures_of_Ancient_China_exhibit_-_bronze_battle_axe.jpg/440px-CMOC_Treasures_of_Ancient_China_exhibit_-_bronze_battle_axe.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2844" data-file-height="2564" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CMOC_Treasures_of_Ancient_China_exhibit_-_bronze_battle_axe.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A bronze <a href="/wiki/Axe" title="Axe">axe</a> of the Shang dynasty</div></div></div>
<p>Bronze weapons were an integral part of Shang society.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESawyerSawyer1994_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESawyerSawyer1994-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> 
Shang infantry were armed with a variety of stone and bronze weaponry, including <i>máo</i> (矛) spears, <i>yuè</i> (鉞) pole-axes, <i>gē</i> (戈) pole-based <a href="/wiki/Dagger-axe" title="Dagger-axe">dagger-axes</a>, composite bows, and bronze or leather helmets.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWang1993_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWang1993-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESawyerSawyer199435_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESawyerSawyer199435-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Chariot" title="Chariot">chariot</a> first appeared in China around 1200 BC, during the reign of Wu Ding. There is little doubt that the chariot entered China through the Central Asia and the Northern Steppe, possibly indicating some form of contact with the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-Europeans" title="Proto-Indo-Europeans">Indo-Europeans</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShaughnessy1988190_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShaughnessy1988190-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> Recent archaeological finds have shown that the late Shang used horses, chariots, bows and practiced <a href="/wiki/Horse_burial" title="Horse burial">horse burials</a> that are similar to the steppe peoples to the west.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMair2011100_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMair2011100-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> These influences led <a href="/wiki/Christopher_I._Beckwith" title="Christopher I. Beckwith">Christopher I. Beckwith</a> to speculate that Indo-Europeans "may even have been responsible for the foundation of the Shang Dynasty", though he admits there is no direct evidence.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeckwith200943–48_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeckwith200943–48-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> Oracle bone inscriptions suggest that the Shang used chariots in royal hunts and in battle only as mobile command vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShaughnessy1988213–221_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShaughnessy1988213–221-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> In contrast, the western enemies of the Shang, such as the Zhou, began to use limited numbers of chariots in battle towards the end of the Shang period.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShaughnessy1988218–221_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShaughnessy1988218–221-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the Shang depended upon the military skills of their nobility, Shang rulers could mobilize the masses of town-dwelling and rural commoners as conscript laborers and soldiers for both campaigns of defense and conquest.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESawyerSawyer199433_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESawyerSawyer199433-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>  Aristocrats and other state rulers were obligated to furnish their local garrisons with all necessary equipment, armor, and armaments. The Shang king maintained a force of about a thousand troops at his capital and would personally lead this force into battle.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESawyerSawyer199434_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESawyerSawyer199434-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> A rudimentary military bureaucracy was also needed in order to muster forces ranging from three to five thousand troops for border campaigns to thirteen thousand troops for suppressing rebellions against the Shang dynasty.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kings">Kings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shang_dynasty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Kings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For a more comprehensive list, see <a href="/wiki/List_of_rulers_of_China#Shang_dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="List of rulers of China">List of rulers of China §&#160;Shang dynasty</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Family_tree_of_ancient_Chinese_emperors#Shang_dynasty" title="Family tree of ancient Chinese emperors">Family tree of ancient Chinese emperors §&#160;Shang dynasty</a>.</div>
<p><span id="list"></span><span id="kings"></span><span id="king_list"></span><span id="emperors"></span>
</p><p>The earliest records are the <a href="/wiki/Oracle_bone" title="Oracle bone">oracle bones</a> inscribed during the reigns of the Shang kings from <a href="/wiki/Wu_Ding" title="Wu Ding">Wu Ding</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilkinson2013684_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilkinson2013684-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
The oracle bones do not contain king lists, but they do record the sacrifices to previous kings and the ancestors of the current king, which follow a standard schedule that scholars have reconstructed. From this evidence, scholars have assembled the implied king list and genealogy, finding that it is in substantial agreement with the later accounts, especially for later kings.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKeightley1999235_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeightley1999235-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>
According to this implied king list, Wu Ding was the twenty-first Shang king.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKeightley1999235_74-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeightley1999235-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Shang kings were referred to in the oracle bones by <a href="/wiki/Posthumous_name" title="Posthumous name">posthumous names</a>.
The last character of each name is one of the 10 <a href="/wiki/Celestial_stem" class="mw-redirect" title="Celestial stem">celestial stems</a>, which also denoted the day of the 10-day Shang week on which sacrifices would be offered to that ancestor within the ritual schedule.
There were more kings than stems, so the names have distinguishing prefixes such as 大 <i>Dà</i> (greater), 中 <i>Zhōng</i> (middle), 小 <i>Xiǎo</i> (lesser), 卜 <i>Bǔ</i> (outer), 祖 <i>Zǔ</i> (ancestor) and a few more obscure names.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith20113–5_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith20113–5-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The kings, in the order of succession derived from the oracle bones, are here grouped by generation.  Later reigns were assigned to oracle bone diviner groups by <a href="/wiki/Dong_Zuobin" title="Dong Zuobin">Dong Zuobin</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKeightley1999234–235,_240–241_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeightley1999234–235,_240–241-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: lower-roman;">
<div class="mw-references-wrap mw-references-columns"><ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-77"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-77">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The first king is known as Tang in the <i>Historical Records</i>.  The oracle bones also identify six pre-dynastic ancestors: 上甲 Shàng Jiǎ, 報乙 Bào Yǐ, 報丙 Bào Bǐng, 報丁 Bào Dīng, 示壬 Shì Rén and 示癸 Shì Guǐ.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-78"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-78">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">There is no firm evidence of oracle bone inscriptions before the reign of Wu Ding.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-79"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-79">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">According to the <i>Historical Records</i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Mencius_(book)" title="Mencius (book)">Mencius</a></i>, Da Ding (there called Tai Ding) died before he could ascend to the throne.  However in the oracle bones he receives rituals like any other king.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-80"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-80">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">According to the <i>Historical Records</i>, Bu Bing (there called Wai Bing) and 仲壬 <a href="/wiki/Zhong_Ren" title="Zhong Ren">Zhong Ren</a> (not mentioned in the oracle bones) were younger brothers of Dai Ting and preceded Da Jia (also known as Dai Jia).  However the <i>Mencius</i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Commentary_of_Zuo" class="mw-redirect" title="Commentary of Zuo">Commentary of Zuo</a></i> and the <i>Book of History</i> state that he reigned after Da Jia, as also implied by the oracle bones.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-81"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-81">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The <i>Historical Records</i> include a king <a href="/wiki/Wo_Ding" title="Wo Ding">Wo Ding</a> not mentioned in the oracle bones.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-82"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-82">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The <i>Historical Records</i> have Xiao Jia as the son of Da Geng (known as Tai Geng) in the "Annals of Yin", but as a younger brother (as implied by the oracle bones) in the "Genealogical Table of the Three Ages".</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-83"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-83">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">According to the <i>Historical Records</i>, Lü Ji (there called Yong Ji) reigned before Da Wu (there called Tai Wu).</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-84"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-84">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The kings from Zhong Ding to Nan Geng are placed in the same order by the <i>Historical Records</i> and the oracle bones, but there are some differences in genealogy, as described in the articles on individual kings.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-85"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-85">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The status of Qiang Jia varies over the history of the oracle bones.  During the reigns of Wu Ding, Di Yi and Di Xin, he was not included in the main line of descent, a position also held by the <i>Historical Records</i>, but in the intervening reigns he was included as a direct ancestor.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-86"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-86">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">According to the <i>Historical Records</i>, Nan Geng was the son of Qiang Jia (there called Wo Jia).</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-87"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-87">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The oracle bones and the <i>Historical Records</i> include an older brother 祖己 <a href="/wiki/Zu_Ji" title="Zu Ji">Zǔ Jǐ</a> who did not reign.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-89"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-89">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Lin Xin is named as a king in the <i>Historical Records</i> and oracle bones of succeeding reigns, but not those of the last two kings.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKeightley1978a187_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeightley1978a187-88">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup></span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-91"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-91">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">There are no ancestral sacrifices to the last two kings on the oracles bones, due to the fall of Shang.  Their names, including the character 帝 <i>Dì</i> "emperor", come from the much later <i><a href="/wiki/Bamboo_Annals" title="Bamboo Annals">Bamboo Annals</a></i> and <i>Historical Records</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKeightley1978a187,_207,_209_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeightley1978a187,_207,_209-90">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup></span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-92"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-92">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">also referred to as Zhòu (紂), Zhòu Xīn (紂辛) or Zhòu Wáng (紂王) or by adding "Shāng" (商) in front of any of these names.</span>
</li>
