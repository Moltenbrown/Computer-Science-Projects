<p><a href="/wiki/David_and_Jonathan" title="David and Jonathan">David and Jonathan</a>, sworn friends and confidants, became brothers-in-law when David married Jonathan's sister Michal.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</div>
<p><b>Sibling-in-law</b> is a word phrase referring to the sibling of one's spouse, or the spouse of one's sibling, or (more rarely)<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> taken one step further by referring to the spouse of one's spouse's sibling. All of these are relations which do not relate to the person directly by blood.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Alternatively, the spouse of your spouse's sibling may be called a co-sibling-in-law<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>, or co-sibling.
</p><p>More commonly this is referred to by the gendered terms: <b>brother-in-law</b>, or co-brother-in-law for a male non-blood sibling, and <b>sister-in-law</b> or co-sister-in-law for a female non-blood sibling.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Just like other affines, or "in-laws", siblings-in-law are related by a type of <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">kinship</a> called <i><a href="/wiki/Affinity_(law)" title="Affinity (law)">affinity</a></i>. Just like the children of one's siblings, the children of one's siblings-in-law are called simply <a href="/wiki/Nephew_and_niece" class="mw-redirect" title="Nephew and niece"><i>nieces</i> and <i>nephews</i></a>  – if necessary, specified whether "by marriage", as opposed to "<a href="/wiki/Consanguinity" title="Consanguinity">by blood</a>" or "<a href="/wiki/Adoption" title="Adoption">by adoption</a>".
</p><p>One study, examining the issue of <a href="/wiki/Envy" title="Envy">envy</a> in the triadic system of sibling, sibling-in-law and spouse, concluded that "The sibling-in-law relationship shared similarities with both <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">spousal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sibling_relationship" title="Sibling relationship">sibling relationships</a>" and that "Relational closeness and satisfaction for all relationships in the triad were correlated."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Islamic_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic law">Islamic law</a> (<i>shariʿa</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Jewish_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish law">Jewish law</a> (<i>halakhah</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> sexual relations between siblings-in-law are prohibited as <a href="/wiki/Incest" title="Incest">incestuous</a>, unless the spouse is no longer married. Conversely, in Judaism there was the custom of <a href="/wiki/Yibbum" title="Yibbum">yibbum</a>, whereby a man had a non-obligatory duty to wed his deceased brother's childless widow so she might have progeny by him.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>If one pair of siblings is married to another pair of siblings, the siblings-in-law are thus doubly related, each of the four both through one's spouse and through one's sibling, while the children of the two couples are <a href="/wiki/Double_cousins" class="mw-redirect" title="Double cousins">double cousins</a>.
</p>
