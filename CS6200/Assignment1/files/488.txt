<p><b>Iroquois kinship</b> (also known as <b>bifurcate merging</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Kinship_and_descent" class="mw-redirect" title="Kinship and descent">kinship</a> system named after the <a href="/wiki/Haudenosaunee" class="mw-redirect" title="Haudenosaunee">Haudenosaunee</a> people that were previously known as <i>Iroquois</i> and whose kinship system was the first one described to use this particular type of system.  Identified by <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Henry_Morgan" class="mw-redirect" title="Lewis Henry Morgan">Lewis Henry Morgan</a> in his 1871 work <i><a href="/wiki/Systems_of_Consanguinity_and_Affinity_of_the_Human_Family" title="Systems of Consanguinity and Affinity of the Human Family">Systems of Consanguinity and Affinity of the Human Family</a></i>, the Iroquois system is one of the six major kinship systems (<a href="/wiki/Eskimo_kinship" title="Eskimo kinship">Eskimo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_kinship" title="Hawaiian kinship">Hawaiian</a>, Iroquois, <a href="/wiki/Crow_kinship" title="Crow kinship">Crow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Omaha_kinship" title="Omaha kinship">Omaha</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sudanese_kinship" title="Sudanese kinship">Sudanese</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kinship_system">Kinship system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iroquois_kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Kinship system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The system has both <a href="/wiki/Classificatory_kinship" title="Classificatory kinship">classificatory</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Descriptive_kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Descriptive kinship (page does not exist)">descriptive</a> terms.  In addition to gender and generation, Iroquois kinship also distinguishes 'same-sex' and 'cross-sex' parental siblings: the brothers of Ego's (the subject from whose perspective the kinship is based) father, and the sisters of Ego's mother, are referred to by the same parental kinship terms used for Ego's Father and Mother. The sisters of Ego's father, and the brothers of Ego's mother, on the other hand, are referred to by non-parental kinship terms, commonly translated into English as "Aunt" and "Uncle".
</p><p>The children of one's parents' same-sex siblings, i.e. <a href="/wiki/Parallel_cousin" class="mw-redirect" title="Parallel cousin">parallel cousins</a>, are referred to by sibling kinship terms. The children of Aunts or Uncles, i.e. <a href="/wiki/Cross_cousin" class="mw-redirect" title="Cross cousin">cross cousins</a>, are not considered siblings, and are referred to by kinship terms commonly translated into English as "cousin".  In some systems, the kinship terms applied to cross-cousins are the same as those applied to brothers-in-law and sisters-in-law, due to preferential marriage practices (see below).
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a href="/wiki/File:Iriquois-kinship-chart.svg" class="image" title="Egocentric genealogical diagram of the Iroquois kinship system."><img alt="Egocentric genealogical diagram of the Iroquois kinship system." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Iriquois-kinship-chart.svg/660px-Iriquois-kinship-chart.svg.png" decoding="async" width="660" height="215" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Iriquois-kinship-chart.svg/990px-Iriquois-kinship-chart.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Iriquois-kinship-chart.svg/1320px-Iriquois-kinship-chart.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="920" data-file-height="300" /></a></div></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage">Marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iroquois_kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ego (the subject from whose perspective the kinship is based) is encouraged to marry his <a href="/wiki/Cross_cousin" class="mw-redirect" title="Cross cousin">cross cousins</a> but discouraged or prohibited from marrying his <a href="/wiki/Parallel_cousin" class="mw-redirect" title="Parallel cousin">parallel cousins</a>.  In many societies with Iroquois kinship terminologies, the preferred marriage partners include not only first cousins (mother's brother's children and father's sister's children), but more remote relatives who are also classified as cross cousins by the logic of the kinship system.  Preferential cross-cousin marriage can be useful in reaffirming alliances between unilineal <a href="/wiki/Lineage_(anthropology)" title="Lineage (anthropology)">lineages</a> or <a href="/wiki/Clan" title="Clan">clans</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iroquois_kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term Iroquois comes from the six <a href="/wiki/Iroquois" title="Iroquois">Iroquois</a> tribes of northeastern North America. Another aspect of their kinship was that the six tribes all had <a href="/wiki/Matrilineal" class="mw-redirect" title="Matrilineal">matrilineal</a> systems, in which children were born into the mother's <a href="/wiki/Clan" title="Clan">clan</a> and gained status through it. Women controlled some property, and hereditary leadership passed through the maternal line. A woman's eldest brother was more important as a mentor to her children than their father, who was always of a different clan.
</p><p>Some groups in other countries also happen to be independently organized for kinship by the Iroquois system.  It is commonly found in <a href="/wiki/Unilineality" title="Unilineality">unilineal descent</a> groups.  These include:
</p>
<ol><li>The <a href="/wiki/Anishinaabe" title="Anishinaabe">Anishinaabe</a> of North America, who include the <a href="/wiki/Algonquin_people" title="Algonquin people">Algonquin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nipissing_First_Nation" title="Nipissing First Nation">Nipissing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mississauga" title="Mississauga">Mississauga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ojibwe" title="Ojibwe">Ojibwe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saulteaux" title="Saulteaux">Saulteaux</a>, <a href="/wiki/Odawa_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Odawa people">Odawa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Potawatomi" title="Potawatomi">Potawatomi</a> peoples.  Many of these people were traditional neighbors to the Iroquois, but they spoke languages of the <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_languages" title="Algonquian languages">Algonquian</a> family.</li></ol>
<p>Other populations found to have the Iroquois system are
</p>
<ol><li>Most of the <a href="/wiki/Bantu_languages" title="Bantu languages">Bantu</a>-speaking cultures of <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Africa" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Africa">Eastern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Africa" title="Southern Africa">Southern</a> <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a> &#8212; see <a href="/wiki/Sesotho_kinship" title="Sesotho kinship">Sesotho kinship</a> for an example.</li>
<li>Many <a href="/wiki/Melanesian" class="mw-redirect" title="Melanesian">Melanesian</a> societies.</li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_India_and_Sri_Lanka">South India and Sri Lanka</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iroquois_kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: South India and Sri Lanka">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some communities in  <a href="/wiki/South_India" title="South India">South India</a> use the kinship tradition described above.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Melanesia">Melanesia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iroquois_kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Melanesia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many of the cultures of <a href="/wiki/Vanuatu" title="Vanuatu">Vanuatu</a> use this type of kinship system. In <a href="/wiki/Bislama" title="Bislama">Bislama</a> (Vanuatu pidgin), paternal uncles and maternal aunts are referred to as <i>smol papa</i> "small father" and <i>smol mama</i> "small mother" respectively.
</p>
