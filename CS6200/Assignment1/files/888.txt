<p><b>Orthodoxy</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> ὀρθοδοξία <i>orthodoxía</i> "right opinion")<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is adherence to correct or accepted <a href="/wiki/Creed" title="Creed">creeds</a>, especially in religion.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> sense the term means "conforming to the Christian faith as represented in the creeds of the <a href="/wiki/Early_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Church">early Church</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/First_seven_ecumenical_councils" title="First seven ecumenical councils">first seven ecumenical councils</a> were held between the years of 325 and 787 with the aim of formalizing accepted doctrines.
</p><p>In some English-speaking countries, Jews who adhere to all the traditions and commandments as legislated in the <a href="/wiki/Talmud" title="Talmud">Talmud</a> are often called <a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Judaism" title="Orthodox Judaism">Orthodox Jews</a>, although the term "orthodox" historically first described Christian beliefs.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religions">Religions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orthodoxy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Religions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Buddhism">Buddhism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orthodoxy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Buddhism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Theravada" title="Theravada">Theravada</a></div>
<p>The historical <a href="/wiki/Gautama_Buddha" title="Gautama Buddha">Buddha</a> was known to denounce mere attachment to scriptures or <a href="/wiki/Dogmatic" class="mw-redirect" title="Dogmatic">dogmatic</a> principles, as it was mentioned in the <a href="/wiki/Kalama_Sutta" title="Kalama Sutta">Kalama Sutta</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Moreover, the <a href="/wiki/Theravada" title="Theravada">Theravada</a> school of <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a> follows strict adherence to the <a href="/wiki/P%C4%81li_Canon" title="Pāli Canon">Pāli Canon</a> (tripitaka) and the commentaries such as the <a href="/wiki/Visuddhimagga" title="Visuddhimagga">Visuddhimagga</a>. Hence, the Theravada school came to be considered the most orthodox of all Buddhist schools<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, as it is known to be highly conservative especially within the discipline and practice of the <a href="/wiki/Vinaya" title="Vinaya">Vinaya</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Christianity">Christianity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orthodoxy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Christianity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Church</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodox_Churches" title="Oriental Orthodox Churches">Oriental Orthodox Churches</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ICXC_NIKA.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/ICXC_NIKA.svg/220px-ICXC_NIKA.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/ICXC_NIKA.svg/330px-ICXC_NIKA.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/ICXC_NIKA.svg/440px-ICXC_NIKA.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="180" data-file-height="180" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ICXC_NIKA.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Greek_Cross" class="mw-redirect" title="Greek Cross">Greek Cross</a>, flanked by the Greek letters "ICXC NIKA" which means "JESUS CHRIST CONQUERS".</div></div></div>
<p>In classical Christian usage, the term <i>orthodox</i> refers to the set of doctrines which were believed by the <a href="/wiki/Early_Christians" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Christians">early Christians</a>. A series of <a href="/wiki/Ecumenical_council" title="Ecumenical council">ecumenical councils</a> were held over a period of several centuries to try to formalize these doctrines. The most significant of these early decisions was that between the <a href="/wiki/Homoousian" class="mw-redirect" title="Homoousian">Homoousian</a> doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Athanasius_of_Alexandria" title="Athanasius of Alexandria">Athanasius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eustathius_of_Antioch" title="Eustathius of Antioch">Eustathius</a> (which became <a href="/wiki/Trinity" title="Trinity">Trinitarianism</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Heteroousian" class="mw-redirect" title="Heteroousian">Heteroousian</a> doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Arius" title="Arius">Arius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eusebius_of_Nicomedia" title="Eusebius of Nicomedia">Eusebius</a> (called <a href="/wiki/Arianism" title="Arianism">Arianism</a>). The Homoousian doctrine, which defined Jesus as both God and man with the canons of the 431 <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Ephesus" title="Council of Ephesus">Council of Ephesus</a>, won out in the Church and was referred to as <i>orthodoxy</i> in most Christian contexts, since this was the viewpoint of previous Christian Church Fathers and was reaffirmed at these councils. (The minority of <a href="/wiki/Nontrinitarianism" title="Nontrinitarianism">nontrinitarian Christians</a> object to this terminology).
</p><p>Following the 1054 <a href="/wiki/East%E2%80%93West_Schism" title="East–West Schism">Great Schism</a>, both the <a href="/wiki/Western_Christianity" title="Western Christianity">Western</a> Church and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodoxy" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Orthodoxy">Eastern</a> Church continued to consider themselves uniquely <i>orthodox</i> and <a href="/wiki/Catholicity" title="Catholicity"><i>catholic</i></a>. <a href="/wiki/Augustine" class="mw-redirect" title="Augustine">Augustine</a> wrote in On True Religion: “Religion is to be sought . . . only among those who are called Catholic or orthodox Christians, that is, guardians of truth and followers of right.” <sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Over time, the Western Church gradually identified with the "Catholic" label, and people of Western Europe gradually associated the "Orthodox" label with the Eastern Church (in some languages the "Catholic" label is not necessarily identified with the Western Church). This was in note of the fact that both Catholic and Orthodox were in use as ecclesiastical adjectives as early as the 2nd and 4th centuries respectively.
</p><p>Much earlier, the earliest <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodox_Churches" title="Oriental Orthodox Churches">Oriental Orthodox Churches</a> had split from <a href="/wiki/Chalcedonian_Christianity" title="Chalcedonian Christianity">Chalcedonian Christianity</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Chalcedon" title="Council of Chalcedon">Council of Chalcedon</a> (AD 451), because of several <a href="/wiki/Christological" class="mw-redirect" title="Christological">christological</a> differences.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMeyendorff1989_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMeyendorff1989-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Since then, Oriental Orthodox Churches are maintaining the <i>orthodox</i> designation as a symbol of their theological traditions.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKrikorian2010_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKrikorian2010-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hinduism">Hinduism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orthodoxy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hinduism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Hinduism" class="mw-redirect" title="Orthodox Hinduism">Orthodox Hinduism</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/%C4%80stika_and_n%C4%81stika" title="Āstika and nāstika">Āstika and nāstika</a></div>
<p>Orthodox Hinduism commonly refers to the religious teachings and practices of <a href="/wiki/San%C4%81tan%C4%AB" title="Sanātanī">Sanātanī</a>, one of the traditionalist branches of <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Islam">Islam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orthodoxy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Islam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sunni_Islam" title="Sunni Islam">Sunni Islam</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sunni_Islam" title="Sunni Islam">Sunni Islam</a> is sometimes referred to as "orthodox Islam".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> As of 2009<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Orthodoxy&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, Sunni Muslims constituted 87–90% of the world's Muslim population.<sup id="cite_ref-PEW2009_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PEW2009-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> However, other scholars of Islam, such as John Burton believe that there is no such thing as "orthodox Islam".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Judaism">Judaism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orthodoxy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Judaism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Judaism" title="Orthodox Judaism">Orthodox Judaism</a></div>
<p>Orthodox Judaism is a collective term for the traditionalist branches of <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a>, which seek to fully maintain the received Jewish beliefs and observances and which coalesced in opposition to the various challenges of <a href="/wiki/Modernity" title="Modernity">modernity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Secularization" title="Secularization">secularization</a>. <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">Theologically</a>, it is chiefly defined by regarding the <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a>, both <a href="/wiki/Written_Torah" class="mw-redirect" title="Written Torah">Written</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oral_Torah" title="Oral Torah">Oral</a>, as literally <a href="/wiki/Revelation" title="Revelation">revealed</a> by God on <a href="/wiki/Biblical_Mount_Sinai" title="Biblical Mount Sinai">biblical Mount Sinai</a> and faithfully transmitted ever since. The movement advocates a strict observance of <i><a href="/wiki/Halakha" title="Halakha">halakha</a></i> (Jewish Law), which is to be interpreted only according to received methods due to its divine character. Orthodoxy considers <i>halakha</i> as eternal and beyond historical influence, being applied differently to changing circumstances but basically unchangeable in itself.
</p><p>Orthodox Judaism is not a centralized denomination. Relations between its different subgroups are sometimes strained and the exact limits of Orthodoxy are subject to intense debate. Very roughly, it may be divided between <a href="/wiki/Haredi_Judaism" title="Haredi Judaism">Haredi Judaism</a>, which is more conservative and reclusive, and <a href="/wiki/Modern_Orthodox_Judaism" title="Modern Orthodox Judaism">Modern Orthodox Judaism</a>, which is relatively open to outer society. Each of those is itself formed of independent streams. They are almost uniformly exclusionist, regarding Orthodoxy as the only authentic form of Judaism and rejecting all non-Orthodox interpretations as illegitimate.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kemeticism">Kemeticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orthodoxy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Kemeticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kemetic_Orthodoxy" title="Kemetic Orthodoxy">Kemetic Orthodoxy</a></div>
<p>Kemetic Orthodoxy is a <a href="/wiki/Kemeticism" class="mw-redirect" title="Kemeticism">Kemetic</a> denomination, which is a reform reconstruction of <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_polytheism" class="mw-redirect" title="Egyptian polytheism">Egyptian polytheism</a> for modern followers. It claims to derive a spiritual lineage from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_religion" title="Ancient Egyptian religion">Ancient Egyptian religion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rodnovery">Rodnovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orthodoxy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Rodnovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Slavic_Native_Faith" title="Slavic Native Faith">Slavic Native Faith</a></div>
<p>There are organizations of Slavic Native Faith (Rodnovery) which characterize the religion as Orthodoxy (Russian: <i>Pravoslavie</i>), and by other terms.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_concepts_in_religion">Related concepts in religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orthodoxy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Related concepts in religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Orthodoxy is opposed to <i><a href="/wiki/Heterodoxy" title="Heterodoxy">heterodoxy</a></i> ("other teaching") or <i><a href="/wiki/Heresy" title="Heresy">heresy</a></i>. People who <a href="/wiki/Deviationism" title="Deviationism">deviate</a> from orthodoxy by professing a <a href="/wiki/Doctrine" title="Doctrine">doctrine</a> considered to be false are called heretics, while those who, perhaps without professing heretical beliefs, break from the perceived main body of believers are called <a href="/wiki/Schism_(religion)" class="mw-redirect" title="Schism (religion)">schismatics</a>. The term employed sometimes depends on the aspect most in view: if one is addressing corporate unity, the emphasis may be on schism; if one is addressing doctrinal coherence, the emphasis may be on heresy. A deviation lighter than heresy is commonly called error, in the sense of not being grave enough to cause total estrangement, while yet seriously affecting communion. Sometimes error is also used to cover both full heresies and minor errors.
</p><p>The concept of orthodoxy is prevalent in many forms of organized <a href="/wiki/Monotheism" title="Monotheism">monotheism</a>. However, orthodox belief is not usually overly emphasized in <a href="/wiki/Polytheism" title="Polytheism">polytheistic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Animism" title="Animism">animist</a> religions, in which there is often little or no concept of <a href="/wiki/Dogma" title="Dogma">dogma</a>, and varied interpretations of doctrine and <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theology</a> are tolerated and sometimes even encouraged within certain contexts. <a href="/wiki/Syncretism" title="Syncretism">Syncretism</a>, for example, plays a much wider role in non-monotheistic (and particularly, non-scriptural) religion. The prevailing governing norm within polytheism is often <a href="/wiki/Orthopraxy" title="Orthopraxy">orthopraxy</a> ("right practice") rather than the "right belief" of orthodoxy.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-religious_contexts">Non-religious contexts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orthodoxy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Non-religious contexts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Outside the context of religion, the term "orthodoxy" is often used to refer to any commonly held belief or set of beliefs in some field, in particular when these tenets, possibly referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Dogma" title="Dogma">dogmas</a>", are being challenged. In this sense, the term has a mildly <a href="/wiki/Pejorative" title="Pejorative">pejorative</a> connotation.
</p><p>Among various "orthodoxies" in distinctive fields, the most commonly used terms are: 
</p>
<ul><li>Political orthodoxy</li>
<li>Social orthodoxy</li>
<li>Economic orthodoxy</li>
<li>Scientific orthodoxy</li>
<li>Artistic orthodoxy</li></ul>
<p>The terms "orthodox" and "orthodoxy" are also used more broadly to refer to things other than ideas and beliefs. A new and unusual way of solving a problem could be referred to as "unorthodox", while a common and 'normal' way of solving a problem would be referred to as "orthodox".
</p>
