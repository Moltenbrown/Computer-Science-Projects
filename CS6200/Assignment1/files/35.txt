<p><b>Mrs.</b> (<a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a>) or <b>Mrs</b> (<a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a>; standard English pronunciation: <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;z&#39; in &#39;zoom&#39;">z</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is a commonly used <a href="/wiki/English_honorific" class="mw-redirect" title="English honorific">English honorific</a> used for <a href="/wiki/Woman" title="Woman">women</a>, usually for those who are married and who do not instead use another title (or rank), such as <i><a href="/wiki/Doctor_(title)" title="Doctor (title)">Dr</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Professor" title="Professor">Professor</a></i>, &#160;<i><a href="/wiki/President_(government_title)" title="President (government title)">President</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Dame_(title)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dame (title)">Dame</a></i>, etc. In most <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a> countries, a full stop (period) is usually not used with the title. In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> a period is usually used (see <a href="/wiki/Abbreviation#History" title="Abbreviation">Abbreviation</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Mrs</i> originated as a <a href="/wiki/Contraction_(grammar)" title="Contraction (grammar)">contraction</a> of the honorific <i><a href="/wiki/Mistress_(form_of_address)" title="Mistress (form of address)">Mistress</a></i>, the feminine of <i><a href="/wiki/Mister_(Mr)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mister (Mr)">Mister</a></i>, or <i><a href="/wiki/Master_(form_of_address)" title="Master (form of address)">Master</a></i>, which was originally applied to both married and unmarried women. The split into <i>Mrs</i> for married women and <i><a href="/wiki/Miss" title="Miss">Miss</a></i> for unmarried began during the 17th century;<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> the 17th century also saw the coinage of a new <a href="/wiki/Unmarked" class="mw-redirect" title="Unmarked">unmarked</a> option 
<i><a href="/wiki/Ms." title="Ms.">Ms</a></i> with a return of this usage appearing in the 20th century. 
</p><p>It is rare for <i>Mrs</i> to be written in a non-abbreviated form, and the unabbreviated word lacks a standard spelling. In literature it may appear as <i>missus</i> or <i>missis</i> in dialogue. A variant in the works of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hardy" title="Thomas Hardy">Thomas Hardy</a> and others is "Mis'ess", reflecting its <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymology</a>. <i>Misses</i> has been used but is ambiguous, as this is a commonly used plural for <i>Miss</i>. The plural of <i>Mrs</i> is from the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i>Mesdames</i>. This may be used as-is in written correspondence, or it may be abbreviated <i>Mmes</i>. 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_usage">Traditional usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mrs.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Traditional usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Mrs</i> was most often used by a woman when married, in conjunction with her husband's first and last names (e.g., <i>Mrs John Smith</i>). A <a href="/wiki/Widow" title="Widow">widow</a> was and still is addressed with the same title as when she was married. <i>Mrs</i> was rarely used before a woman's first name, her birth name, or a hyphenated surname her husband was not using. For example, <i>Mrs Jane Smith</i>, <i>Mrs Miller</i> (wife of John Smith), or <i>Mrs Miller-Smith</i> were considered incorrect by many etiquette writers, especially of the early 20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In several languages, the title for married women such as <i>Madame</i>, <i>Señora</i>, <i>Signora</i>, or <i>Frau</i>, is the direct feminine equivalent of the title used for men; the title for unmarried women is a diminutive: <i>Mademoiselle</i>, <i>Señorita</i>, <i>Signorina</i>, or <i><a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A4ulein" title="Fräulein">Fräulein</a></i>. For this reason, usage had shifted toward using the married title as the default for all women in professional usage. This had long been followed in the United Kingdom for some high-ranking household staff, such as housekeepers, cooks, and <a href="/wiki/Nanny" title="Nanny">nannies</a>, who were called <i>Mrs</i> as a mark of respect regardless of marital status. However, in the late 20th century the marital-neutral <a href="/wiki/Ms." title="Ms.">Ms</a> became more common for women professionally and socially.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In the United Kingdom, the traditional form for a <a href="/wiki/Divorce" title="Divorce">divorcée</a> was <i>Mrs Jane Smith</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In the U.S., the divorcée originally retained her full married name unless she remarried.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Later,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> the form <i>Mrs Miller Smith</i> was sometimes used, with the <a href="/wiki/Maiden_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Maiden name">birth</a> surname in place of the first name. However, the form <i>Mrs Jane Miller</i> eventually became widely used for divorcées, even in formal correspondence; that is, <i>Mrs</i> preceded the divorcée's maiden name. 
</p><p>Before social mores relaxed to the point where single women with children were socially acceptable, the unwed mother was often advised by <a href="/wiki/Etiquette" title="Etiquette">etiquette</a> mavens like <a href="/wiki/Emily_Post" title="Emily Post">Emily Post</a> to use <i>Mrs</i> with her maiden name to avoid scrutiny. 
</p><p>The separation of <i>Miss</i> and <i>Mrs</i> became problematic as more women entered the <a href="/wiki/White-collar_worker" title="White-collar worker">white-collar</a> workforce. Women who became famous or well known in their professional circles before marriage often kept their birth names, stage names, or <i>noms de plume</i>. <i>Miss</i> became the appellation for celebrities (e.g., Miss <a href="/wiki/Helen_Hayes" title="Helen Hayes">Helen Hayes</a>, or Miss <a href="/wiki/Amelia_Earhart" title="Amelia Earhart">Amelia Earhart</a>) but this also proved problematic, as when a married woman <i>did</i> use her husband’s last name but was still referred to as <i>Miss</i>; see more at <a href="/wiki/Ms." title="Ms.">Ms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Miss" title="Miss">Miss</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_usage">Modern usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mrs.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Modern usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is now very uncommon for a woman to be addressed by her husband's first name; however, this still sometimes occurs if a couple is being addressed jointly, such as in <i>Mr and Mrs John Smith</i>.
</p><p>Many married women still use the title with their spouse's last name but retaining their first name (e.g., <i>Mrs Jane Smith</i>). Other married women choose not to adopt their spouse's last name at all. It is generally considered polite to address a woman by <i>Ms</i> rather than <i>Mrs</i>, unless the preference of the woman in question is clearly known. This is especially true in written communication, as dictated by professional etiquette. <sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Modern <a href="/wiki/Etiquette" title="Etiquette">etiquette</a> provides various options in addressing married couples in which the wife uses her own last name, or uses a title such as <i>Dr</i>, <i>Mayor</i>, or <i>Ms</i>. Etiquette writer <a href="/wiki/Judith_Martin" title="Judith Martin">Judith Martin</a> ("Miss Manners") generally advises that, in non-standard situations, the individuals be addressed on separate lines when writing invitations (e.g., "Dr Sue Martin/Mr John Martin").
</p><p>In direct address, a woman with the title <i>Mrs</i> may be addressed <i>Mrs [Lastname]</i>, or with the stand-alone <i><a href="/wiki/Madam" title="Madam">Madam</a></i> or <i>Ma'am</i>, although the latter two are more often used for any adult woman, regardless of marital status, in modern conversation. It is normally considered correct to address a woman as <i>Ms [Lastname]</i>, regardless of her marital status. 
</p><p>A woman who has a long-term sexual relationship with a partner who is married may be referred to as that person's "<a href="/wiki/Mistress_(lover)" title="Mistress (lover)">mistress</a>".
</p>
