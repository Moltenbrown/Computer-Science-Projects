	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Greek scholars in the Renaissance</h1>

		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ghirlandaio_-_Tornabuoni_Chapel_-_a_Humanist_philosopher.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Ghirlandaio_-_Tornabuoni_Chapel_-_a_Humanist_philosopher.jpg/180px-Ghirlandaio_-_Tornabuoni_Chapel_-_a_Humanist_philosopher.jpg" decoding="async" width="180" height="235" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Ghirlandaio_-_Tornabuoni_Chapel_-_a_Humanist_philosopher.jpg/270px-Ghirlandaio_-_Tornabuoni_Chapel_-_a_Humanist_philosopher.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b0/Ghirlandaio_-_Tornabuoni_Chapel_-_a_Humanist_philosopher.jpg 2x" data-file-width="326" data-file-height="426" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ghirlandaio_-_Tornabuoni_Chapel_-_a_Humanist_philosopher.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Demetrios_Chalkokondyles" title="Demetrios Chalkokondyles">Demetrios Chalkokondyles</a> (brother of <a href="/wiki/Laonikos_Chalkokondyles" title="Laonikos Chalkokondyles">Laonikos Chalkokondyles</a>) (1424–1511) was a <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greek</a> Renaissance scholar,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Humanist and teacher of Greek and Platonic philosophy.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Argyropoulos_(detail)_Calling_of_the_Apostles.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Argyropoulos_%28detail%29_Calling_of_the_Apostles.JPG/180px-Argyropoulos_%28detail%29_Calling_of_the_Apostles.JPG" decoding="async" width="180" height="355" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/98/Argyropoulos_%28detail%29_Calling_of_the_Apostles.JPG 1.5x" data-file-width="260" data-file-height="513" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Argyropoulos_(detail)_Calling_of_the_Apostles.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/John_Argyropoulos" title="John Argyropoulos">John Argyropoulos</a> (1415–1487) was a Greek Renaissance scholar who played a prominent role in the revival of <a href="/wiki/Greek_philosophy" class="mw-redirect" title="Greek philosophy">Greek philosophy</a> in Italy.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The migration waves of <a href="/wiki/Byzantine" class="mw-redirect" title="Byzantine">Byzantine</a> scholars and émigrés in the period following the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Crusade" title="Fourth Crusade">Crusader</a> <a href="/wiki/Sack_of_Constantinople_(1204)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sack of Constantinople (1204)">sacking of Constantinople</a> in 1204 and the <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Constantinople" title="Fall of Constantinople">end of the Byzantine Empire</a> in 1453, is considered by many scholars key to the revival of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Greek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> studies that led to the development of the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_humanism" title="Renaissance humanism">Renaissance humanism</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_science_in_the_Renaissance" title="History of science in the Renaissance">science</a>. These émigrés brought to Western Europe the relatively well-preserved remnants and accumulated knowledge of their own (Greek) civilization, which had mostly not survived the <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">Dark Ages</a> in the West.
</p><p>Their main role within <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_humanism" title="Renaissance humanism">Renaissance humanism</a> was the teaching of the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek language</a> to their western counterparts in universities or privately together with the spread of ancient texts. Their forerunners were <a href="/wiki/Barlaam_of_Calabria" class="mw-redirect" title="Barlaam of Calabria">Barlaam of Calabria</a> (Bernardo Massari) and <a href="/wiki/Leonzio_Pilato" class="mw-redirect" title="Leonzio Pilato">Leonzio Pilato</a>, two translators who were both born in Calabria in southern Italy and who were both educated in the Greek language. The impact of these two scholars on the very first Renaissance humanists was indisputable.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By 1500 there was a Greek-speaking community of about 5,000 in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>. The Venetians also ruled <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dalmatia" title="Dalmatia">Dalmatia</a>, and scattered islands and port cities of the former empire, the populations of which were augmented by refugees from other Byzantine provinces who preferred Venetian to Ottoman governance. Crete was especially notable for the <a href="/wiki/Cretan_School" title="Cretan School">Cretan School</a> of <a href="/wiki/Icon" title="Icon">icon</a>-painting, which after 1453 became the most important in the Greek world.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the peak of the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance" title="Italian Renaissance">Italian Renaissance</a> in the first decades of the 16th century, the flow of information reversed, and Greek scholars in Italy were employed to oppose Turkish expansion into former Byzantine lands in Greece, prevent the Protestant <a href="/wiki/Reformation" title="Reformation">Reformation</a> spreading there and help bring the Eastern Churches back into communion with Rome. In 1577, <a href="/wiki/Gregory_XIII" class="mw-redirect" title="Gregory XIII">Gregory XIII</a> founded the <a href="/wiki/Collegio_Pontifico_Greco" class="mw-redirect" title="Collegio Pontifico Greco">Collegio Pontifico Greco</a> as a college in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> to receive young Greeks belonging to any nation in which the <a href="/wiki/Greek_Rite" class="mw-redirect" title="Greek Rite">Greek Rite</a> was used, and consequently for Greek refugees in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Ruthenians" title="Ruthenians">Ruthenians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Melkite" title="Melkite">Malchites</a> of <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Syria_(region)" title="Syria (region)">Syria</a>. The construction of the College and Church of S. Atanasio, joined by a bridge over the <i>Via dei Greci</i>, was begun in that year.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contribution_of_Greek_scholars_to_the_Italian_Renaissance">Contribution of Greek scholars to the Italian Renaissance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greek_scholars_in_the_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Contribution of Greek scholars to the Italian Renaissance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Plethon_autograph.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Plethon_autograph.jpg/150px-Plethon_autograph.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="256" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Plethon_autograph.jpg/225px-Plethon_autograph.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3d/Plethon_autograph.jpg 2x" data-file-width="261" data-file-height="446" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Plethon_autograph.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>One of <a href="/wiki/Gemistus_Pletho" title="Gemistus Pletho">Georgius Gemistus (Plethon)</a>'s manuscripts, in Greek, written in the early 15th century.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Johannes_Bessarion_aport012.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Johannes_Bessarion_aport012.png/180px-Johannes_Bessarion_aport012.png" decoding="async" width="180" height="242" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Johannes_Bessarion_aport012.png/270px-Johannes_Bessarion_aport012.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Johannes_Bessarion_aport012.png/360px-Johannes_Bessarion_aport012.png 2x" data-file-width="403" data-file-height="542" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Johannes_Bessarion_aport012.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Cardinal_Bessarion" class="mw-redirect" title="Cardinal Bessarion">Cardinal Bessarion</a> (1395–1472) of <a href="/wiki/Trabzon" title="Trabzon">Trebizond</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pontus_(region)" title="Pontus (region)">Pontus</a> was a Greek scholar, statesman, and cardinal and one of the leading figures in the rise of the intellectual Renaissance.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Manuel_Chrysoloras_-_Imagines_philologorum.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Manuel_Chrysoloras_-_Imagines_philologorum.jpg/180px-Manuel_Chrysoloras_-_Imagines_philologorum.jpg" decoding="async" width="180" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Manuel_Chrysoloras_-_Imagines_philologorum.jpg/270px-Manuel_Chrysoloras_-_Imagines_philologorum.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Manuel_Chrysoloras_-_Imagines_philologorum.jpg/360px-Manuel_Chrysoloras_-_Imagines_philologorum.jpg 2x" data-file-width="449" data-file-height="549" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Manuel_Chrysoloras_-_Imagines_philologorum.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Manuel_Chrysoloras" title="Manuel Chrysoloras">Manuel Chrysoloras</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Although ideas from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">ancient Rome</a> already enjoyed popularity with the scholars of the 14th century and their importance to the Renaissance was undeniable, the lessons of Greek learning brought by Byzantine intellectuals changed the course of <a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">humanism</a> and the Renaissance itself.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> While Greek learning affected all the subjects of the <i><a href="/wiki/Studia_humanitatis" class="mw-redirect" title="Studia humanitatis">studia humanitatis</a></i>, history and philosophy in particular were profoundly affected by the texts and ideas brought from <a href="/wiki/Byzantium" title="Byzantium">Byzantium</a>. History was changed by the re-discovery and spread of Greek historians’ writings, and this knowledge of Greek historical treatises helped the subject of history become a guide to virtuous living based on the study of past events and people. The effects of this renewed knowledge of Greek history can be seen in the writings of humanists on virtue, which was a popular topic. Specifically, these effects are shown in the examples provided from Greek antiquity that displayed virtue as well as vice. 
</p><p>The philosophy of not only <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> but also <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> affected the Renaissance by causing debates over man’s place in the universe, the immortality of the soul, and the ability of man to improve himself through virtue. The flourishing of philosophical writings in the 15th century revealed the impact of <a href="/wiki/Greek_philosophy" class="mw-redirect" title="Greek philosophy">Greek philosophy</a> and science on the Renaissance. The resonance of these changes lasted through the centuries following the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> not only in the writing of humanists, but also in the education and values of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and western society even to the present day.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Deno Geanakopoulos in his work on the contribution of Byzantine scholars to Renaissance has summarised their input into three major shifts to Renaissance thought: 
</p>
<ul><li>in early 14th century <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a> from the early, central emphasis on rhetoric to one on  metaphysical philosophy by means of introducing and reinterpretation of the Platonic texts,</li></ul>
<ul><li>in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>-<a href="/wiki/Padua" title="Padua">Padua</a> by reducing the dominance of <a href="/wiki/Averroist" class="mw-redirect" title="Averroist">Averroist</a> <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> in science and philosophy by supplementing but not completely replacing it with Byzantine traditions which utilised ancient and <a href="/wiki/Commentaries_on_Aristotle#Byzantine_commentators" title="Commentaries on Aristotle">Byzantine commentators on Aristotle</a>,</li></ul>
<ul><li>and earlier in the mid 15th century in Rome, through emphasis not on any philosophical school but through the production of more authentic and reliable versions of Greek texts relevant to all fields of humanism and science and with respect to the Greek fathers of the church. Hardly less important was their direct or indirect influence on <a href="/wiki/Exegesis" title="Exegesis">exegesis</a> of the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> itself through <a href="/wiki/Bessarion" class="mw-redirect" title="Bessarion">Bessarion</a>'s inspiration of <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_Valla" title="Lorenzo Valla">Lorenzo Valla</a>'s biblical emendations of the Latin <a href="/wiki/Vulgate" title="Vulgate">vulgate</a> in the light of the Greek text.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scholars">Scholars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greek_scholars_in_the_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Scholars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Leo_Allatius" title="Leo Allatius">Leo Allatius</a>, Rome, librarian of the <a href="/wiki/Vatican_Library" title="Vatican Library">library of Vatican</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/George_Amiroutzes" title="George Amiroutzes">George Amiroutzes</a>, Florence, Aristotelian</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Henry_Aristippus" title="Henry Aristippus">Henry Aristippus</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Michael_Apostolius" title="Michael Apostolius">Michael Apostolius</a>, Rome</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aristobulus_Apostolius" class="mw-redirect" title="Aristobulus Apostolius">Aristobulus Apostolius</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Arsenius_Apostolius" title="Arsenius Apostolius">Arsenius Apostolius</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/John_Argyropoulos" title="John Argyropoulos">John Argyropoulos</a>, Universities of Florence, Rome</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Simon_Atumano" title="Simon Atumano">Simon Atumano</a>, Bishop of Gerace in Calabria</li>
