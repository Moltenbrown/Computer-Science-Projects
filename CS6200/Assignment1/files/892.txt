	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Monastery</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

Built in 1563–1584.</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>monastery</b> is a building or complex of buildings comprising the domestic quarters and workplaces of <a href="/wiki/Monasticism" title="Monasticism">monastics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">monks</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nun" title="Nun">nuns</a>, whether living in <a href="/wiki/Cenobitic_monasticism" title="Cenobitic monasticism">communities</a> or alone (<a href="/wiki/Hermits" class="mw-redirect" title="Hermits">hermits</a>). A monastery generally includes a place reserved for prayer which may be a chapel, <a href="/wiki/Church_(building)" title="Church (building)">church</a>, or temple, and may also serve as an <a href="/wiki/Oratory_(worship)" title="Oratory (worship)">oratory</a>, or in the case of <a href="/wiki/Cenobium" class="mw-redirect" title="Cenobium">communities</a> anything from a single building housing only one senior and two or three junior monks or nuns, to vast complexes and estates housing tens or hundreds. A monastery complex typically comprises a number of buildings which include a church, <a href="/wiki/Dormitory" title="Dormitory">dormitory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cloister" title="Cloister">cloister</a>, <a href="/wiki/Refectory" title="Refectory">refectory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Library" title="Library">library</a>, <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/balneary" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:balneary">balneary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hospital" title="Hospital">infirmary</a>. Depending on the location, the monastic order and the occupation of its inhabitants, the complex may also include a wide range of buildings that facilitate self-sufficiency and service to the community. These may include a <a href="/wiki/Hospice" title="Hospice">hospice</a>, a <a href="/wiki/School" title="School">school</a>, and a range of agricultural and manufacturing buildings such as a <a href="/wiki/Barn" title="Barn">barn</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Forge" title="Forge">forge</a>, or a <a href="/wiki/Brewery" title="Brewery">brewery</a>.
</p><p>In English usage, the term <i>monastery</i> is generally used to denote the buildings of a community of monks. In modern usage, <i><a href="/wiki/Convent" title="Convent">convent</a></i> tends to be applied only to institutions of female monastics (nuns), particularly communities of teaching or nursing <a href="/wiki/Nun" title="Nun">religious sisters</a>. Historically, a convent denoted a house of <a href="/wiki/Friar" title="Friar">friars</a> (reflecting the Latin), now more commonly called a <i>friary</i>. Various religions may apply these terms in more specific ways.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monastery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St_gall_plan.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/St_gall_plan.jpg/220px-St_gall_plan.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="317" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/St_gall_plan.jpg/330px-St_gall_plan.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/St_gall_plan.jpg/440px-St_gall_plan.jpg 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="720" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:St_gall_plan.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Plan_of_Saint_Gall" title="Plan of Saint Gall">Plan of Saint Gall</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ground_plan" class="mw-redirect" title="Ground plan">ground plan</a> of an unbuilt abbey, providing for all of the needs of the monks within the confines of the monastery walls</div></div></div>
<p>The word <i>monastery</i> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> word <i>μοναστήριον</i>, neut. of <i>μοναστήριος</i> – <i>monasterios</i> from <i>μονάζειν</i> – <i>monazein</i> "to live alone"<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> from the root <i>μόνος</i> – <i>monos</i> "alone" (originally all Christian monks were hermits); the suffix "-terion" denotes a "place for doing something". The earliest extant use of the term <i>monastērion</i> is by the 1st century AD <a href="/wiki/Jew" class="mw-redirect" title="Jew">Jewish</a> philosopher <a href="/wiki/Philo" title="Philo">Philo</a> in <i>On The Contemplative Life,</i> ch. III.
</p><p>In England, the word <i>monastery</i> was also applied to the habitation of a <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishop</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cathedral" title="Cathedral">cathedral</a> <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">clergy</a> who lived apart from the lay community. Most cathedrals were not monasteries, and were served by <a href="/wiki/Canon_(priest)" title="Canon (priest)">canons secular</a>, which were communal but not monastic. However, some were run by monasteries orders, such as <a href="/wiki/York_Minster" title="York Minster">York Minster</a>. <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Abbey" title="Westminster Abbey">Westminster Abbey</a> was for a short time a cathedral, and was a <a href="/wiki/Benedictine" class="mw-redirect" title="Benedictine">Benedictine</a> monastery until the <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">Reformation</a>, and its <a href="/wiki/Chapter_(religion)" title="Chapter (religion)">Chapter</a> preserves elements of the Benedictine tradition. See the entry <a href="/wiki/Cathedral" title="Cathedral">cathedral</a>. They are also to be distinguished from collegiate churches, such as <a href="/wiki/St_George%27s_Chapel,_Windsor" class="mw-redirect" title="St George&#39;s Chapel, Windsor">St George's Chapel, Windsor</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terms">Terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monastery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The term <i>monastery</i> is used generically to refer to any of a number of types of religious community. In the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> religion and to some extent in certain branches of <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, there is a somewhat more specific definition of the term and many related terms.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Buddhist" class="mw-redirect" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a> monasteries are generally called <b><a href="/wiki/Vihara" title="Vihara">vihara</a></b> (<a href="/wiki/Pali" title="Pali">Pali</a> language). Viharas may be occupied by men or women, and in keeping with common English usage, a vihara populated by females may often be called a nunnery or a convent. However, vihara can also refer to a <a href="/wiki/Temple" title="Temple">temple</a>. In Tibetan Buddhism, monasteries are often called <b><a href="/wiki/Gompa" title="Gompa">gompa</a></b>. In <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">Laos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>, a monastery is called a <i><a href="/wiki/Wat" title="Wat">wat</a></i>. In <a href="/wiki/Burma" class="mw-redirect" title="Burma">Burma</a>, a monastery is called a <i><a href="/wiki/Kyaung" title="Kyaung">kyaung</a></i>.
</p><p>A Christian monastery may be an <b><a href="/wiki/Abbey" title="Abbey">abbey</a></b> (i.e., under the rule of an <a href="/wiki/Abbot" title="Abbot">abbot</a>), or a <b><a href="/wiki/Priory" title="Priory">priory</a></b> (under the rule of a <a href="/wiki/Prior" title="Prior">prior</a>), or conceivably a <b><a href="/wiki/Hermitage_(religious_retreat)" title="Hermitage (religious retreat)">hermitage</a></b> (the dwelling of a <a href="/wiki/Hermit" title="Hermit">hermit</a>). It may be a community of men (<a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">monks</a>) or of women (<a href="/wiki/Nun" title="Nun">nuns</a>). A <a href="/wiki/Charterhouse_(monastery)" title="Charterhouse (monastery)">charterhouse</a> is any monastery belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Carthusian" class="mw-redirect" title="Carthusian">Carthusian</a> order. In <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Christianity" title="Eastern Christianity">Eastern Christianity</a>, a very small monastic community can be called a <b><a href="/wiki/Skete" title="Skete">skete</a></b>, and a very large or important monastery can be given the dignity of a <b><a href="/wiki/Lavra" title="Lavra">lavra</a></b>.
</p><p>The great communal life of a Christian monastery is called <a href="/wiki/Cenobitic" class="mw-redirect" title="Cenobitic">cenobitic</a>, as opposed to the <a href="/wiki/Anchorite" title="Anchorite">anchoretic</a> (or anchoritic) life of an <a href="/wiki/Anchorite" title="Anchorite">anchorite</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Eremitical" class="mw-redirect" title="Eremitical">eremitic</a> life of a <a href="/wiki/Hermit" title="Hermit">hermit</a>. There has also been, mostly under the <a href="/wiki/Osmanli" class="mw-redirect" title="Osmanli">Osmanli</a> occupation of Greece and Cyprus, an "idiorrhythmic" lifestyle where monks come together but being able to own things individually and not being obliged to work for the common good.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a> monasteries are called <a href="/wiki/Matha" title="Matha">matha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mandir" class="mw-redirect" title="Mandir">mandir</a>, <a href="/wiki/Koil" title="Koil">koil</a>, or most commonly an <a href="/wiki/Ashram" title="Ashram">ashram</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">Jains</a> use the Buddhist term <a href="/wiki/Vihara" title="Vihara">vihara</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Monastic_life">Monastic life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monastery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Monastic life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In most religions, the life inside monasteries is governed by community rules that stipulate the gender of the inhabitants and require them to remain <a href="/wiki/Celibacy" title="Celibacy">celibate</a> and own little or no personal property. The degree to which life inside a particular monastery is socially separate from the surrounding populace can also vary widely; some religious traditions mandate isolation for purposes of contemplation removed from the everyday world, in which case members of the monastic community may spend most of their time isolated even from each other. Others focus on interacting with the local communities to provide services, such as teaching, medical care, or <a href="/wiki/Evangelism" title="Evangelism">evangelism</a>. Some monastic communities are only occupied seasonally, depending both on the traditions involved and the local weather, and people may be part of a monastic community for periods ranging from a few days at a time to almost an entire lifetime.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The life within the walls of a monastery may be supported in several ways: by manufacturing and selling goods, often <a href="/wiki/Agricultural" class="mw-redirect" title="Agricultural">agricultural</a> products, by donations or <a href="/wiki/Alms" title="Alms">alms</a>, by rental or investment incomes, and by funds from other organizations within the religion, which in the past formed the traditional support of monasteries. There has been a long tradition of Christian monasteries providing hospitable, charitable and hospital services. Monasteries have often been associated with the provision of education and the encouragement of scholarship and research, which has led to the establishment of schools and colleges and the association with universities. Christian monastic life has adapted to modern society by offering computer services, <a href="/wiki/Accounting" title="Accounting">accounting</a> services and management as well as modern hospital and educational administration.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

<p>Buddhist monasteries, known as <b><a href="/wiki/Vihara" title="Vihara">vihāra</a></b> in <a href="/wiki/Pali" title="Pali">Pali</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a>, emerged sometime around the fourth century BCE from the practice of <a href="/wiki/Vassa" title="Vassa">vassa</a>, a retreat undertaken by Buddhist monastics during the South Asian <a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">wet season</a>. To prevent wandering <a href="/wiki/Bhikkhu" title="Bhikkhu">monks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bhikkhuni" class="mw-redirect" title="Bhikkhuni">nuns</a> from disturbing new plant growth or becoming stranded in inclement weather, they were instructed to remain in a fixed location for the roughly three-month period typically beginning in mid-July.
</p><p>These early fixed <i>vassa</i> retreats were held in pavilions and parks that had been donated to the <a href="/wiki/Sangha" title="Sangha">sangha</a> by wealthy supporters. Over the years, the custom of staying on property held in common by the <i>sangha</i> as a whole during the <i>vassa</i> retreat evolved into <a href="/wiki/Cenobitic_monasticism" title="Cenobitic monasticism">cenobitic monasticism</a>, in which monks and nuns resided year-round in monasteries.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Buddhism_in_India" class="mw-redirect" title="Buddhism in India">India</a>, Buddhist monasteries gradually developed into centres of learning where philosophical principles were developed and debated; this tradition is currently preserved by monastic universities of <a href="/wiki/Vajrayana" title="Vajrayana">Vajrayana Buddhists</a>, as well as religious schools and universities founded by religious orders across the Buddhist world. In modern times, living a settled life in a monastery setting has become the most common lifestyle for Buddhist monks and nuns across the globe.
</p><p>Whereas early monasteries are considered to have been held in common by the entire sangha, in later years this tradition diverged in a number of countries. Despite <i><a href="/wiki/Vinaya" title="Vinaya">vinaya</a></i> prohibitions on possessing wealth, many monasteries became large landowners, much like monasteries in medieval Christian Europe. In <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Buddhism" title="Chinese Buddhism">Chinese Buddhism</a>, peasant families worked monastic-owned land in exchange for paying a portion of their yearly crop to the resident monks in the monastery, just as they would to a <a href="/wiki/Feudal" class="mw-redirect" title="Feudal">feudal</a> landlord. In <a href="/wiki/Buddhism_in_Sri_Lanka" title="Buddhism in Sri Lanka">Sri&#160;Lanka</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Buddhism" title="Tibetan Buddhism">Tibetan Buddhism</a>, the ownership of a monastery often became vested in a single monk, who would often keep the property within the family by passing it on to a nephew who ordained as a monk. In <a href="/wiki/Buddhism_in_Japan" title="Buddhism in Japan">Japan</a>, where civil authorities permitted Buddhist monks to marry, being the head of a temple or monastery sometimes became a hereditary position, passed from father to son over many generations.
</p><p>Forest monasteries – most commonly found in the <a href="/wiki/Theravada" title="Theravada">Theravada</a> traditions of Southeast Asia and Sri&#160;Lanka – are monasteries dedicated primarily to the study of Buddhist <a href="/wiki/Meditation" title="Meditation">meditation</a>, rather than scholarship or ceremonial duties. Forest monasteries often function like early Christian monasteries, with small groups of monks living an essentially hermit-like life gathered loosely around a respected elder teacher. While the wandering lifestyle practised by the Buddha and his disciples continues to be the ideal model for forest tradition monks in <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> and elsewhere, practical concerns- including shrinking wilderness areas, lack of access to lay supporters, dangerous wildlife, and dangerous border conflicts- dictate that increasing numbers of "meditation" monks live in monasteries, rather than wandering.
</p><p>Tibetan Buddhist monasteries or <a href="/wiki/Gompa" title="Gompa">gompas</a> are sometimes known as <b>lamaseries</b> and the monks are sometimes (mistakenly) known as <a href="/wiki/Lama" title="Lama">lamas</a>. <a href="/wiki/Helena_Blavatsky" title="Helena Blavatsky">Helena Blavatsky</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Theosophical_Society" title="Theosophical Society">Theosophical Society</a> named its initial New York City meeting place "the Lamasery."<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some famous Buddhist monasteries include:

<p>According to tradition, <a href="/wiki/Christian_monasticism" title="Christian monasticism">Christian monasticism</a> began in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> with <a href="/wiki/Anthony_the_Great" title="Anthony the Great">Anthony the Great</a>. Originally, all Christian monks were <a href="/wiki/Hermit" title="Hermit">hermits</a> seldom encountering other people. But because of the extreme difficulty of the solitary life, many monks failed, either returning to their previous lives, or becoming spiritually deluded.
</p><p>A transitional form of monasticism was later created by <a href="/wiki/Saint_Amun" title="Saint Amun">Saint Amun</a> in which "solitary" monks lived close enough to one another to offer mutual support as well as gathering together on Sundays for common services.
</p><p>It was <a href="/wiki/Pachomius_the_Great" title="Pachomius the Great">Pachomius the Great</a> who developed the idea of <a href="/wiki/Cenobitic_monasticism" title="Cenobitic monasticism">cenobitic monasticism</a>: having renunciates live together and worship together under the same roof. Some attribute his mode of communal living to the <a href="/wiki/Barracks" title="Barracks">barracks</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Army" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Army">Roman Army</a> in which Pachomios served as a young man.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Soon the Egyptian desert blossomed with monasteries, especially around Nitria (<a href="/wiki/Wadi_El_Natrun" title="Wadi El Natrun">Wadi El Natrun</a>), which was called the "Holy City". Estimates are that upwards of 50,000 monks lived in this area at any one time. Hermitism never died out though, but was reserved only for those advanced monks who had worked out their problems within a cenobitic monastery.
</p><p>The idea caught on, and other places followed:
</p>
<ul><li>Upon his return from the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Serdica" title="Council of Serdica">Council of Serdica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Athanasius_of_Alexandria" title="Athanasius of Alexandria">Athanasius of Alexandria</a> established <a href="/wiki/Monastery_of_Saint_Athanasius" title="Monastery of Saint Athanasius">the first Christian monastery in Europe</a> circa 344 near modern-day <a href="/wiki/Chirpan" title="Chirpan">Chirpan</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mar_Awgin" class="mw-redirect" title="Mar Awgin">Mar Awgin</a> founded a monastery on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Izla" title="Mount Izla">Mount Izla</a> above <a href="/wiki/Nusaybin" title="Nusaybin">Nusaybin</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> (~350), and from this monastery the cenobitic tradition spread in Mesopotamia, <a href="/wiki/Persia" class="mw-redirect" title="Persia">Persia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a>, Georgia and even India and China.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mar_Saba" title="Mar Saba">Mar Saba</a> organized the monks of the <a href="/wiki/Judaean_Desert" title="Judaean Desert">Judaean Desert</a> in a monastery close to <a href="/wiki/Bethlehem" title="Bethlehem">Bethlehem</a> (483), and this is considered the mother of all monasteries of <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodoxy" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Orthodoxy">Eastern Orthodoxy</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Benedict_of_Nursia" title="Benedict of Nursia">Benedict of Nursia</a> founded the monastery of <a href="/wiki/Monte_Cassino" title="Monte Cassino">Monte Cassino</a> in Italy (529), which was the seed of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> monasticism in general, and of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Saint_Benedict" class="mw-redirect" title="Order of Saint Benedict">Order of Saint Benedict</a> in particular.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Carthusians" title="Carthusians">Carthusians</a> were founded by <a href="/wiki/Bruno_of_Cologne" title="Bruno of Cologne">Bruno of Cologne</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Grande_Chartreuse" title="Grande Chartreuse">Grande Chartreuse</a>, from which the religious Order takes its name, in the eleventh century as an eremitical community, and remains the <a href="/wiki/Motherhouse" title="Motherhouse">motherhouse</a> of the Order.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jerome" title="Jerome">Jerome</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paula_of_Rome" title="Paula of Rome">Paula of Rome</a> decided to go live a hermit's life in <a href="/wiki/Bethlehem" title="Bethlehem">Bethlehem</a> and founded several monasteries in the Holy Land. This way of life inspired the foundation of the <a href="/wiki/Hieronymites" title="Hieronymites">Hieronymites</a> in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Monastery_of_Santa_Mar%C3%ADa_del_Parral" title="Monastery of Santa María del Parral">Monastery of Santa María del Parral</a> in <a href="/wiki/Segovia" title="Segovia">Segovia</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Motherhouse" title="Motherhouse">motherhouse</a> of the Order.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_Medieval_Europe">Western Medieval Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monastery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Western Medieval Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Monte_Cassino_Opactwo_1.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Monte_Cassino_Opactwo_1.JPG/220px-Monte_Cassino_Opactwo_1.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Monte_Cassino_Opactwo_1.JPG/330px-Monte_Cassino_Opactwo_1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Monte_Cassino_Opactwo_1.JPG/440px-Monte_Cassino_Opactwo_1.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="960" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Monte_Cassino_Opactwo_1.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Abbey" title="Abbey">Abbey</a> of <a href="/wiki/Monte_Cassino" title="Monte Cassino">Monte Cassino</a>, originally built by <a href="/wiki/Benedict_of_Nursia" title="Benedict of Nursia">Saint Benedict</a>, shown here as rebuilt after World War&#160;II</div></div></div>
<p>The life of prayer and communal living was one of rigorous schedules and self-sacrifice. Prayer was their work, and the Office prayers took up much of a monk's waking hours – <a href="/wiki/Matins" title="Matins">Matins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lauds" title="Lauds">Lauds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prime_(liturgy)" title="Prime (liturgy)">Prime</a>, <a href="/wiki/Terce" title="Terce">Terce</a>, daily Mass, <a href="/wiki/Sext" title="Sext">Sext</a>, <a href="/wiki/None_(liturgy)" class="mw-redirect" title="None (liturgy)">None</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vespers" title="Vespers">Vespers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Compline" title="Compline">Compline</a>. In between prayers, monks were allowed to sit in the cloister and work on their projects of writing, copying, or decorating books. These would have been assigned based on a monk's abilities and interests. The non-scholastic types were assigned to physical labour of varying degrees.
</p><p>The main meal of the day took place around noon, often taken at a <a href="/wiki/Refectory_table" title="Refectory table">refectory table</a>, and consisted of the most simple and bland foods i.e.,&#160;poached fish, boiled oats. While they ate, scripture would be read from a pulpit above them. Since no other words were allowed to be spoken, monks developed communicative gestures. Abbots and notable guests were honoured with a seat at the high table, while everyone else sat perpendicular to that in the order of seniority. This practice remained when some monasteries became universities after the first millennium, and can still be seen at <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxford University">Oxford University</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Cambridge University">Cambridge University</a>.
</p><p>Monasteries were important contributors to the surrounding community. They were centres of intellectual progression and education. They welcomed aspiring priests to come study and learn, allowing them even to challenge doctrine in dialogue with superiors. The earliest forms of <a href="/wiki/Musical_notation" title="Musical notation">musical notation</a> are attributed to a monk named <a href="/wiki/Notker_of_St_Gall" class="mw-redirect" title="Notker of St Gall">Notker of St Gall</a>, and was spread to musicians throughout <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> by way of the interconnected monasteries. Since monasteries offered respite for weary <a href="/wiki/Pilgrim" title="Pilgrim">pilgrim</a> travellers, monks were obligated also to care for their injuries or emotional needs. Over time, lay people started to make <a href="/wiki/Christian_pilgrimage" title="Christian pilgrimage">pilgrimages</a> <i>to</i> monasteries instead of just using them as a stopover. By this time, they had sizeable libraries that attracted learned tourists. Families would donate a son in return for blessings. During the <a href="/wiki/Plague_(disease)" title="Plague (disease)">plagues</a>, monks helped to till the fields and provide food for the sick.
</p><p>A Warming House is a common part of a <a href="/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">medieval</a> monastery, where monks went to warm themselves. It was often the only room in the monastery where a fire was lit.
</p>

<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Church</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Catholic Church">Eastern Catholic Church</a>, both monks and nuns follow a similar <a href="/wiki/Ascetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Ascetic">ascetic</a> discipline, and even their <a href="/wiki/Religious_habit" title="Religious habit">religious habit</a> is the same (though nuns wear an extra veil, called the <i><a href="/wiki/Apostolnik" title="Apostolnik">apostolnik</a></i>). Unlike <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Roman Catholic</a> monasticism, the Orthodox do not have separate religious orders, but a single monastic form throughout the Orthodox Church. Monastics, male or female, live away from the world, in order to pray for the world.
</p><p>Monasteries vary from the very large to the very small. There are three types of monastic houses in the Orthodox Church:
</p>
<ul><li>A <a href="/wiki/Cenobium" class="mw-redirect" title="Cenobium">cenobium</a> is a monastic community where monks live together, work together, and pray together, following the directions of an abbot and the elder monks. The concept of the cenobitic life is that when many men (or women) live together in a monastic context, like rocks with sharp edges, their "sharpness" becomes worn away and they become smooth and polished. The largest monasteries can hold many thousands of monks and are called <i><a href="/wiki/Lavra" title="Lavra">lavras</a></i>. In the cenobium the <a href="/wiki/Daily_office" class="mw-redirect" title="Daily office">daily office</a>, work and meals are all done in common.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Skete" title="Skete">skete</a> is a small monastic establishment that usually consist of one elder and two or three disciples. In the skete most prayer and work are done in private, coming together on Sundays and <a href="/wiki/Feast_day" class="mw-redirect" title="Feast day">feast days</a>. Thus, skete life has elements of both solitude and community, and for this reason is called the "middle way".</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Hermit" title="Hermit">hermit</a> is a monk who practises asceticism but lives in solitude rather than in a monastic community.</li></ul>
<p>One of the great centres of Orthodox monasticism is <a href="/wiki/Mount_Athos" title="Mount Athos">Mount Athos</a> in <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, which, like the <a href="/wiki/Vatican_State" class="mw-redirect" title="Vatican State">Vatican State</a>, is self-governing. It is located on an isolated peninsula approximately 20 miles (32&#160;km) long and 5 miles (8.0&#160;km) wide, and is administered by the heads of the 20 monasteries. Today the population of the Holy Mountain is around 2,200 men only and can only be visited by men with special permission granted by both the Greek government and the government of the Holy Mountain itself.
</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodoxy" class="mw-redirect" title="Oriental Orthodoxy">Oriental Orthodox</a> churches, distinguished by their <a href="/wiki/Miaphysitism" title="Miaphysitism">Miaphysite</a> beliefs, consist of the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Apostolic_Church" title="Armenian Apostolic Church">Armenian Apostolic Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coptic_Orthodox_Church_of_Alexandria" title="Coptic Orthodox Church of Alexandria">Coptic Orthodox Church of Alexandria</a> (whose Patriarch is considered first among equals for the following churches), <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Orthodox_Tewahedo_Church" title="Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church">Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eritrean_Orthodox_Tewahedo_Church" title="Eritrean Orthodox Tewahedo Church">Eritrean Orthodox Tewahedo Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malankara_Orthodox_Syrian_Church" title="Malankara Orthodox Syrian Church">Indian Orthodox Church</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Syriac_Orthodox_Church" title="Syriac Orthodox Church">Syriac Orthodox Church</a> of Antioch. The now extinct <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Caucasian_Albania" title="Church of Caucasian Albania">Church of Caucasian Albania</a> also fell under this group.
</p><p>The monasteries of St. Macarius (<i><a href="/wiki/Monastery_of_Saint_Macarius_the_Great" title="Monastery of Saint Macarius the Great">Deir Abu Makaria</a></i>) and St. Anthony (<i><a href="/wiki/Monastery_of_Saint_Anthony" title="Monastery of Saint Anthony">Deir Mar Antonios</a></i>) are the oldest monasteries in the world and under the patronage of the Patriarch of the Coptic Orthodox Church.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Others">Others</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monastery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Others">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The last years of the 18th&#160;century marked in the Christian Church the beginnings of growth of monasticism among <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> denominations. The center of this movement was in the United States and Canada beginning with the <a href="/wiki/Shaker" class="mw-redirect" title="Shaker">Shaker</a> Church, which was founded in England and then moved to the United States. In the 19th&#160;century many of these monastic societies were founded as Utopian communities based on the monastic model in many cases. Aside from the Shakers, there were the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amanna&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Amanna (page does not exist)">Amanna</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Anabaptist" class="mw-redirect" title="Anabaptist">Anabaptists</a>, and others. Many did allow marriage but most had a policy of <a href="/wiki/Celibacy" title="Celibacy">celibacy</a> and communal life in which members shared all things communally and disavowed personal ownership.
</p><p>In the 19th-century monasticism was revived in the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>, leading to the foundation of such institutions as the <a href="/wiki/House_of_the_Resurrection" class="mw-redirect" title="House of the Resurrection">House of the Resurrection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mirfield" title="Mirfield">Mirfield</a> (<a href="/wiki/Community_of_the_Resurrection" title="Community of the Resurrection">Community of the Resurrection</a>), <a href="/wiki/Nashdom_Abbey" class="mw-redirect" title="Nashdom Abbey">Nashdom Abbey</a> (<a href="/wiki/Benedictine" class="mw-redirect" title="Benedictine">Benedictine</a>), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cleeve_Priory&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cleeve Priory (page does not exist)">Cleeve Priory</a> (<a href="/wiki/Community_of_the_Glorious_Ascension" title="Community of the Glorious Ascension">Community of the Glorious Ascension</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Ewell_Monastery" class="mw-redirect" title="Ewell Monastery">Ewell Monastery</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cistercian" class="mw-redirect" title="Cistercian">Cistercian</a>), <a href="/wiki/Benedictine" class="mw-redirect" title="Benedictine">Benedictine</a> orders, <a href="/wiki/Franciscan" class="mw-redirect" title="Franciscan">Franciscan</a> orders and the Orders of the Holy Cross, Order of St.&#160;Helena. Other Protestant Christian denominations also engage in monasticism, particularly Lutherans in Europe and North America. For example, the Benedictine order of the Holy Cross at St Augustine's House in Michigan is a Lutheran order of monks and there are Lutheran religious communities in Sweden and Germany. In the 1960s, experimental monastic groups were formed in which both men and women were members of the same house and also were permitted to be married and have children—these were operated on a communal form.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Advaita_Vedanta">Advaita Vedanta</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monastery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Advaita Vedanta">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vidyashankara_Temple_at_Shringeri.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Vidyashankara_Temple_at_Shringeri.jpg/220px-Vidyashankara_Temple_at_Shringeri.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="162" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Vidyashankara_Temple_at_Shringeri.jpg/330px-Vidyashankara_Temple_at_Shringeri.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Vidyashankara_Temple_at_Shringeri.jpg/440px-Vidyashankara_Temple_at_Shringeri.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1178" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vidyashankara_Temple_at_Shringeri.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hindu <a href="/wiki/Matha" title="Matha">matha</a>, Vidyasankara Temple</div></div></div>
<p>From the times of the <a href="/wiki/Vedas" title="Vedas">Vedas</a> ( from circa 1700 BC ), people following monastic ways of life have existed in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>. In what is now called Hinduism, monks have existed for a long time, and with them, their respective monasteries, called <a href="/wiki/Matha" title="Matha">mathas</a>. Important among them are the chatur-amnaya mathas established by <a href="/wiki/Adi_Shankara" title="Adi Shankara">Adi Shankara</a> which formed the nodal centres of under whose guidance the ancient Order of Advaitin monks were re-organised under ten names of the <a href="/wiki/Dashanami_Sampradaya" title="Dashanami Sampradaya">Dashanami Sampradaya</a>. 
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Parakala_Mutt_-_as_it_stands_today.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Parakala_Mutt_-_as_it_stands_today.jpg/220px-Parakala_Mutt_-_as_it_stands_today.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Parakala_Mutt_-_as_it_stands_today.jpg/330px-Parakala_Mutt_-_as_it_stands_today.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Parakala_Mutt_-_as_it_stands_today.jpg/440px-Parakala_Mutt_-_as_it_stands_today.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="534" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Parakala_Mutt_-_as_it_stands_today.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Parakala Mutt - as it stands today</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ramanuja" title="Ramanuja">Ramanuja</a> heralded a new era in the world of Hinduism by reviving the lost faith in it and gave a firm doctrinal basis to the <a href="/wiki/Vishishtadvaita" title="Vishishtadvaita">Vishishtadvaita</a> philosophy which had existed since time immemorial. He ensured the establishment of a number of <a href="/wiki/Matha" title="Matha">mathas</a> of his <a href="/wiki/Sri_Vaishnava" class="mw-redirect" title="Sri Vaishnava">Sri Vaishnava</a> creed at different important centres of pilgrimage.

<li>Sriranga Narayana Jeeyar Mutt at <a href="/wiki/Srirangam" title="Srirangam">Srirangam</a></li>
<li>Tirumala Pedda Jeeyangar Mutt at <a href="/wiki/Tirupati" title="Tirupati">Tirupati</a></li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sufism">Sufism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monastery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Sufism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Islam discourages monasticism, which is referred to in the Quran as "an invention".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> However, the term "Sufi" is applied to Muslim mystics who, as a means of achieving union with Allah, adopted ascetic practices including wearing a garment made of coarse wool called "sf".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The term "<a href="/wiki/Sufism" title="Sufism">Sufism</a>" comes from "sf" meaning the person, who wears "sf".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> But in the course of time, Sufi has come to designate all Muslim believers in mystic union.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
