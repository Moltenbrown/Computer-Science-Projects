	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Phonaesthetics</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">&#160;&#160;(Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Euphony&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Euphony">Euphony</a>)</span></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Pleasantness to the ear</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Cacophony" and "Euphony" redirect here. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Cacophony_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Cacophony (disambiguation)">Cacophony (disambiguation)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Euphony_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Euphony (disambiguation)">Euphony (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Phonaesthetics</b> (also spelled <b>phonesthetics</b> in North America) is the study of beauty and pleasantness associated with the sounds of certain words or parts of words. The term was <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">first used</a>, perhaps by <a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">J. R. R. Tolkien</a> in this sense,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> during the mid-twentieth century, deriving from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">φωνή</span> <i>phōnē</i>, "voice-sound" plus <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">αἰσθητική</span> <i>aisthētikē</i>, "<a href="/wiki/Aesthetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Aesthetic">aesthetic</a>". Speech sounds have many aesthetic qualities, some of which are subjectively regarded as euphonious (pleasing) or cacophonous (displeasing).
</p><p>More broadly, phonaesthetics refers to the study of "phonaesthesia": <a href="/wiki/Sound_symbolism" title="Sound symbolism">sound symbolism</a>. For instance, the British linguist <a href="/wiki/David_Crystal" title="David Crystal">David Crystal</a>, who has compiled research on popular perceptions of beautiful-sounding English words, regards phonaesthetics as the "study of aesthetic properties of sounds, especially the sound symbolism attributable to individual sounds".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> An example is that English speakers tend to make an association of unpleasantness with the sound <i>sl-</i> in such words as <i>sleazy</i>, <i>slime</i>, <i>slug</i>, and <i>slush</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> or an association of formless repetition with <i>-tter</i> in such words as <i>chatter</i>, <i>glitter</i>, <i>flutter</i>, and <i>shatter</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Phonaesthetics remains a budding and often subjective field of study, with no formally established definition; today, it mostly exists as a marginal branch of <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phonetics" title="Phonetics">phonetics</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Poetics" title="Poetics">poetics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Euphony_and_cacophony"><span id="Euphony"></span><span id="Cacophy"></span> Euphony and cacophony</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phonaesthetics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Euphony and cacophony">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Euphony is the effect of sounds being perceived as pleasant, rhythmical, lyrical, or harmonious.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ElizabethPodhaizer2001_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ElizabethPodhaizer2001-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Cacophony is the effect of sounds being perceived as harsh, unpleasant, chaotic, and often discordant; these sounds are perhaps meaningless and jumbled together.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Compare with <a href="/wiki/Consonance_and_dissonance" title="Consonance and dissonance">consonance and dissonance</a> in music. In poetry, for example, euphony may be used deliberately to convey comfort, peace, or serenity, while cacophony may be used to convey discomfort, pain, or disorder. This is often furthered by the combined effect of the meaning beyond just the sounds themselves.
</p><p>The California Federation of <a href="/wiki/Chaparral" title="Chaparral">Chaparral</a> Poets, Inc. uses <a href="/wiki/Emily_Dickinson" title="Emily Dickinson">Emily Dickinson</a>'s "A Bird Came Down the Walk" as an example of euphonious poetry, one passage being "...Oars divide the Ocean, / Too silver for a seam" and <a href="/wiki/John_Updike" title="John Updike">John Updike</a>'s "Player Piano" as an example of cacophonous poetry, one passage being "My stick fingers click with a snicker / And, chuckling, they knuckle the keys".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phonaesthetics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/David_Crystal" title="David Crystal">David Crystal</a>'s 1995 paper "Phonaesthetically Speaking" explores lists, created by reader polls and individual writers, of English words that are commonly regarded as sounding beautiful, to search for any patterns within the words' phonetics. Frequently recurring example words in these lists include <i>gossamer</i>, <i>melody</i>, and <i>tranquil</i>. Crystal's findings, assuming a <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British</a> <a href="/wiki/Received_Pronunciation" title="Received Pronunciation">Received Pronunciation</a> accent, is that words perceived as pretty tend to have a majority of a wide array of criteria; here are some major ones:<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Three or more syllables (e.g., <i>goss·a·mer</i> and <i>mel·o·dy</i>)</li>
<li>Stress on the first syllable (e.g., <i>góssamer</i> and <i>mélody</i>)</li>
<li><span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span></span>/</a></span></span> is the most common consonant <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">phoneme</a>, followed by <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span></span>, <wbr /><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span></span>, <wbr /><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span></span>, <wbr /><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span></span>, <wbr /><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span></span>, <wbr /><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;d&#39; in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span></span>/</a></span></span>, then a huge drop-off before others consonants (e.g., <i><u>l</u>u<u>m</u>i<u>n</u>ou<u>s</u></i> contains the first four)</li>
<li>Short vowels (e.g., the <a href="/wiki/Schwa" title="Schwa">schwa</a>, followed in order by the vowels in <i>bid</i>, <i>bed</i>, and <i>bad</i>) are favored over long vowels and diphthongs (e.g., as in <i>bide</i>, <i>bode</i>, <i>bowed</i>)</li>
<li>Three or more <a href="/wiki/Manners_of_articulation" class="mw-redirect" title="Manners of articulation">manners of articulation</a> (with <a href="/wiki/Approximant_consonant" title="Approximant consonant">approximant consonants</a> the most common, followed by <a href="/wiki/Stop_consonant" title="Stop consonant">stop consonants</a>, and so on)</li></ul>
<p>A perfect example word, according to these findings, is <i>tremulous</i>. Crystal also suggests the invented words <i>ramelon</i> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;r&#39; in &#39;rye&#39;">r</span><span title="/æ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;bad&#39;">æ</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span><span title="/ɒ/: &#39;o&#39; in &#39;body&#39;">ɒ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span></span>/</a></span></span> and <i>drematol</i> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;d&#39; in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span><span title="&#39;r&#39; in &#39;rye&#39;">r</span><span title="/ɛ/: &#39;e&#39; in &#39;dress&#39;">ɛ</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span><span title="/ɒ/: &#39;o&#39; in &#39;body&#39;">ɒ</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span></span>/</a></span></span>, which he notes are similar to the types of names often employed in the marketing of pharmaceutical drugs.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cellar_door"><span id="Cellar_door"></span> <i>Cellar door</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phonaesthetics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cellar door">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Cellar door" redirects here. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Cellar_door_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Cellar door (disambiguation)">Cellar door (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/English_compound" title="English compound">English compound</a> <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">noun</a> <i>cellar door</i> has been widely cited as an example of a word or phrase that is beautiful purely in terms of its sound (i.e., euphony) without regard for its <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">meaning</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-barrett_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barrett-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The phenomenon of <i>cellar door</i> being regarded as euphonious appears to have begun in the very early twentieth century, first attested in the 1903 novel <i>Gee-Boy</i> by the <a href="/wiki/Shakespeare" class="mw-redirect" title="Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a> scholar Cyrus Lauron Hooper, and it has been promoted as beautiful-sounding by various writers; linguist <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Nunberg" title="Geoffrey Nunberg">Geoffrey Nunberg</a> specifically names the writers <a href="/wiki/H._L._Mencken" title="H. L. Mencken">H. L. Mencken</a> in 1920; <a href="/wiki/David_Allan_Robertson" title="David Allan Robertson">David Allan Robertson</a> in 1921; <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Parker" title="Dorothy Parker">Dorothy Parker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hendrik_Willem_van_Loon" title="Hendrik Willem van Loon">Hendrik Willem van Loon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Albert_Payson_Terhune" title="Albert Payson Terhune">Albert Payson Terhune</a> in the 1930s; <a href="/wiki/George_Jean_Nathan" title="George Jean Nathan">George Jean Nathan</a> in 1935; <a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">J. R. R. Tolkien</a> as early as a 1955 speech titled "<a href="/wiki/English_and_Welsh" title="English and Welsh">English and Welsh</a>"; and <a href="/wiki/C._S._Lewis" title="C. S. Lewis">C. S. Lewis</a> in 1963.<sup id="cite_ref-barrett_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barrett-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nunberg_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nunberg-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, the phenomenon itself is touched upon in many sources and media, including a 1905 issue of <i><a href="/wiki/Harper%27s_Magazine" title="Harper&#39;s Magazine">Harper's Magazine</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/William_Dean_Howells" title="William Dean Howells">William Dean Howells</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> the 1967 novel <i><a href="/wiki/Why_Are_We_in_Vietnam%3F" title="Why Are We in Vietnam?">Why Are We in Vietnam?</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Norman_Mailer" title="Norman Mailer">Norman Mailer</a>, a 1991 essay by <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Barzun" title="Jacques Barzun">Jacques Barzun</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and the 2001 <a href="/wiki/Psychological_drama" class="mw-redirect" title="Psychological drama">psychological drama</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Donnie_Darko" title="Donnie Darko">Donnie Darko</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The origin of <i>cellar door</i> being considered as an inherently beautiful or musical word is mysterious. However, in 2014, Nunberg speculated that the phenomenon might have arisen from Philip Wingate and <a href="/wiki/Henry_W._Petrie" title="Henry W. Petrie">Henry W. Petrie</a>'s 1894 hit song "I Don't Want to Play in Your Yard", which contains the lyric "You'll be sorry when you see me sliding down our cellar door". Following the song's success, "slide down my cellar door" became a popular catchphrase up until the 1930s or 1940s to mean engaging in a type of friendship or camaraderie reminiscent of childhood innocence.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;b&#93;</a></sup> A 1914 essay about <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe" title="Edgar Allan Poe">Edgar Allan Poe</a>'s choice of the word "Nevermore" in his 1845 poem "<a href="/wiki/The_Raven" title="The Raven">The Raven</a>" as being based on euphony may have spawned an unverified legend, propagated by syndicated columnists like Frank Colby in 1949<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> and  <a href="/wiki/L._M._Boyd" title="L. M. Boyd">L. M. Boyd</a> in 1979, that <i>cellar door</i> was Poe's favorite phrase.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Tolkien, Lewis, and others have suggested that <i>cellar door'</i>s auditory beauty becomes more apparent the more the word is dissociated from its literal meaning, for example, by using alternative spellings such as <i>Selador</i> or <i>Selladore</i>, which take on the quality of an enchanting name (and both of which suggest a specifically <a href="/wiki/British_accent" class="mw-redirect" title="British accent">British</a> <a href="/wiki/Rhoticity_in_English" title="Rhoticity in English">pronunciation</a> of the word: <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="/ɛ/: &#39;e&#39; in &#39;dress&#39;">ɛ</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="&#39;d&#39; in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span><span title="/ɔː/: &#39;au&#39; in &#39;fraud&#39;">ɔː</span></span>/</a></span></span>).<sup id="cite_ref-nunberg_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nunberg-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;c&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;d&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

Well then, in <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Language" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh Language">Welsh</a> for me <i>cellar doors</i> [i.e. such beautiful words] are extraordinarily frequent, and moving to the higher dimension, the words in which there is pleasure in the contemplation of the association of form and sense are abundant.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup></span>

Was it because they find l and r difficult to pronounce, and the word thus acquires remoteness and enchantment? I asked, and learned also that Tatsuo Sakuma, my friend, had never seen an American cellar door, either inside a house or outside&#160;— the usual two flaps on a sloping ledge. He also enjoyed going to restaurants and hearing the waiter ask if he would like salad or roast vegetables, because again the phrase 'salad or' could be heard. I concluded that its charmlessness to speakers of English lay simply in its meaning. It has the l and r sounds and d and long o dear to the analysts of verse music, but it is prosaic.
