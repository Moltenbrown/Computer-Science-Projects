	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Late antiquity</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">&#160;&#160;(Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_Antiquity&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Late Antiquity">Late Antiquity</a>)</span></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Period just before the Middle Ages</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Diptych_Barberini_Louvre_OA9063_whole.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Diptych_Barberini_Louvre_OA9063_whole.jpg/220px-Diptych_Barberini_Louvre_OA9063_whole.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="275" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Diptych_Barberini_Louvre_OA9063_whole.jpg/330px-Diptych_Barberini_Louvre_OA9063_whole.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Diptych_Barberini_Louvre_OA9063_whole.jpg/440px-Diptych_Barberini_Louvre_OA9063_whole.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2500" data-file-height="3125" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Diptych_Barberini_Louvre_OA9063_whole.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Barberini_ivory" title="Barberini ivory">Barberini ivory</a>, a late <a href="/wiki/House_of_Leo" title="House of Leo">Leonid</a>/<a href="/wiki/Justinian_dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Justinian dynasty">Justinian</a> <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine</a> <a href="/wiki/Ivory_carving" title="Ivory carving">ivory</a> leaf from an imperial <a href="/wiki/Diptych" title="Diptych">diptych</a>, from an imperial workshop in <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> in the first half of the sixth century (<a href="/wiki/Louvre_Museum" class="mw-redirect" title="Louvre Museum">Louvre Museum</a>)</div></div></div>
<p><b>Late antiquity</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Periodization" title="Periodization">periodization</a> used by historians to describe the time of transition from <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">classical antiquity</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> in mainland <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> world, and the <a href="/wiki/Near_East" title="Near East">Near East</a>. The popularization of this periodization in English has generally been accredited to historian <a href="/wiki/Peter_Brown_(historian)" title="Peter Brown (historian)">Peter Brown</a>, after the publication of his seminal work <i><a href="/wiki/The_World_of_Late_Antiquity_(1971)" title="The World of Late Antiquity (1971)">The World of Late Antiquity</a></i> (1971). Precise boundaries for the period are a continuing matter of debate, but Brown proposes a period between the 3rd and 8th centuries AD. Generally, it can be thought of as from the end of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Crisis_of_the_Third_Century" title="Crisis of the Third Century">Crisis of the Third Century</a> (c. 235–284) to, in the East, the <a href="/wiki/Early_Muslim_conquests" title="Early Muslim conquests">early Muslim conquests</a> in the mid-7th century. In the West the end was earlier, with the start of the <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">Early Middle Ages</a> typically placed in the 6th century, or earlier on the edges of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> underwent considerable social, cultural and organizational changes starting with the reign of <a href="/wiki/Diocletian" title="Diocletian">Diocletian</a>, who began the custom of splitting the Empire into Eastern and <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western</a> halves ruled by multiple emperors. Beginning with <a href="/wiki/Constantine_the_Great" title="Constantine the Great">Constantine the Great</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> was made legal in the Empire, and a new capital was founded at <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>. <a href="/wiki/Migration_Period" title="Migration Period">Migrations</a> of <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic</a> tribes disrupted Roman rule from the late 4th century onwards, culminating in the eventual <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_the_Western_Roman_Empire" title="Fall of the Western Roman Empire">collapse of the Empire in the West</a> in 476, replaced by the so-called <a href="/wiki/Barbarian_kingdoms" title="Barbarian kingdoms">barbarian kingdoms</a>. The resultant cultural fusion of <a href="/wiki/Greco-Roman_world" title="Greco-Roman world">Greco-Roman</a>, Germanic and Christian traditions formed the foundations of the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Europe" title="Culture of Europe">culture of Europe</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_antiquity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>Spätantike</i>, literally "late antiquity", has been used by German-speaking historians since its popularization by <a href="/wiki/Alois_Riegl" title="Alois Riegl">Alois Riegl</a> in the early 20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It was given currency in English partly by the writings of <a href="/wiki/Peter_Robert_Lamont_Brown" class="mw-redirect" title="Peter Robert Lamont Brown">Peter Brown</a>, whose survey <i>The World of Late Antiquity</i> (1971) revised the <a href="/wiki/Edward_Gibbon" title="Edward Gibbon">post-Gibbon</a> view of a stale and ossified Classical culture, in favour of a vibrant time of renewals and beginnings, and whose <i>The Making of Late Antiquity</i> offered a new paradigm of understanding the changes in Western culture of the time in order to confront Sir <a href="/wiki/Richard_Southern" class="mw-redirect" title="Richard Southern">Richard Southern</a>'s <i>The Making of the Middle Ages</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The continuities between the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Roman_Empire" title="History of the Roman Empire">later Roman Empire</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> as it was reorganized by <a href="/wiki/Diocletian" title="Diocletian">Diocletian</a> (r. 284–305), and the <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">Early Middle Ages</a> are stressed by writers who wish to emphasize that the seeds of medieval culture were already developing in the <a href="/wiki/Christianization" title="Christianization">Christianized</a> empire, and that they continued to do so in the Eastern Roman Empire or <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a> at least until the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_conquests" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic conquests">coming of Islam</a>. Concurrently, some migrating <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic tribes</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Ostrogoths" title="Ostrogoths">Ostrogoths</a> and <a href="/wiki/Visigoths" title="Visigoths">Visigoths</a> saw themselves as perpetuating the "Roman" tradition. While the usage "Late Antiquity" suggests that the social and cultural priorities of <a href="/wiki/Classical_Antiquity" class="mw-redirect" title="Classical Antiquity">Classical Antiquity</a> endured throughout <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, the usage of "Early Middle Ages" or "Early Byzantine" emphasizes a break with the classical past, and the term "<a href="/wiki/Migration_Period" title="Migration Period">Migration Period</a>" tends to de-emphasize the disruptions in the former Western Roman Empire caused by the creation of Germanic kingdoms within her borders beginning with the <i><a href="/wiki/Foederati" title="Foederati">foedus</a></i> with the <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Goths</a> in Aquitania in 418.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The general decline of population, technological knowledge and standards of living in Europe during this period became the archetypal example of <a href="/wiki/Societal_collapse" title="Societal collapse">societal collapse</a> for writers from the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>. As a result of this decline, and the relative scarcity of historical records from Europe in particular, the period from roughly the early fifth century until the <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_Renaissance" title="Carolingian Renaissance">Carolingian Renaissance</a> (or later still) was referred to as the "<a href="/wiki/Dark_Ages_(historiography)" title="Dark Ages (historiography)">Dark Ages</a>". This term has mostly been abandoned as a name for a historiographical epoch, being replaced by "Late Antiquity"
in the periodization of the late West Roman Empire, the early Byzantine empire and the <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">Early Middle Ages</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-clark_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clark-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_antiquity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of the most important transformations in Late Antiquity was the formation and evolution of the <a href="/wiki/Abrahamic_religions" title="Abrahamic religions">Abrahamic religions</a>: <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rabbinic_Judaism" title="Rabbinic Judaism">Rabbinic Judaism</a> and, eventually, <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Constantine_York_Minster.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Constantine_York_Minster.jpg/220px-Constantine_York_Minster.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="167" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Constantine_York_Minster.jpg/330px-Constantine_York_Minster.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Constantine_York_Minster.jpg/440px-Constantine_York_Minster.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2766" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Constantine_York_Minster.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Modern statue of <a href="/wiki/Constantine_the_Great" title="Constantine the Great">Constantine I</a> at <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a>, where he was proclaimed <a href="/wiki/Augustus_(honorific)" class="mw-redirect" title="Augustus (honorific)">Augustus</a> in 306.</div></div></div>
<p>A milestone in the rise of Christianity was the conversion of Emperor <a href="/wiki/Constantine_I_(emperor)" class="mw-redirect" title="Constantine I (emperor)">Constantine the Great</a> (r. 306–337) in 312, as claimed by his Christian panegyrist <a href="/wiki/Eusebius_of_Caesarea" class="mw-redirect" title="Eusebius of Caesarea">Eusebius of Caesarea</a>, although <a href="/wiki/Constantine_I_and_Christianity" class="mw-redirect" title="Constantine I and Christianity">the sincerity of his conversion is debated</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Constantine confirmed the legalization of the religion through the so-called <a href="/wiki/Edict_of_Milan" title="Edict of Milan">Edict of Milan</a> in 313, jointly issued with his rival in the East, <a href="/wiki/Licinius" title="Licinius">Licinius</a> (r. 308–324). By the late 4th century, Emperor <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_the_Great" class="mw-redirect" title="Theodosius the Great">Theodosius the Great</a> had made <a href="/wiki/State_church_of_the_Roman_Empire" title="State church of the Roman Empire">Christianity the State religion</a>, thereby transforming the Classical Roman world, which Peter Brown characterized as "rustling with the presence of many <a href="/wiki/Numen" title="Numen">divine spirits</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Constantine I was a key figure in many important events in Christian history, as he convened and attended the first ecumenical council of bishops at <a href="/wiki/First_Council_of_Nicaea" title="First Council of Nicaea">Nicaea</a> in 325, subsidized the building of churches and sanctuaries such as the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_Holy_Sepulchre" title="Church of the Holy Sepulchre">Church of the Holy Sepulchre</a> in <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem_in_Christianity" title="Jerusalem in Christianity">Jerusalem</a>, and involved himself in questions such as the timing of Christ's resurrection and its relation to the <a href="/wiki/Passover" title="Passover">Passover</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The birth of <a href="/wiki/Christian_monasticism" title="Christian monasticism">Christian monasticism</a> in the deserts of <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> in the 3rd century, which initially operated outside the episcopal authority of the Church, would become so successful that by the 8th century it penetrated the Church and became the primary Christian practice. <a href="/wiki/Monasticism" title="Monasticism">Monasticism</a> was not the only new Christian movement to appear in late antiquity, although it had perhaps the greatest influence. Other movements notable for their unconventional practices include the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grazers_(religion)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Grazers (religion) (page does not exist)">Grazers</a>, holy men who ate only grass and chained themselves up;<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Fool" class="mw-redirect" title="Holy Fool">Holy Fool</a> movement, in which acting like a fool was considered more divine than folly; and the <a href="/wiki/Stylites" class="mw-redirect" title="Stylites">Stylites</a> movement, where one practitioner lived atop a 50-foot pole for 40 years.
</p><p>Late Antiquity marks the decline of <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_ancient_Rome" title="Religion in ancient Rome">Roman state religion</a>, circumscribed in degrees by edicts likely inspired by Christian advisors such as Eusebius to 4th century emperors, and a period of dynamic religious experimentation and spirituality with many <a href="/wiki/Syncretism" title="Syncretism">syncretic</a> sects, some formed centuries earlier, such as <a href="/wiki/Gnosticism" title="Gnosticism">Gnosticism</a> or <a href="/wiki/Neoplatonism" title="Neoplatonism">Neoplatonism</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Chaldaean_oracles" class="mw-redirect" title="Chaldaean oracles">Chaldaean oracles</a>, some novel, such as <a href="/wiki/Hermetica" title="Hermetica">hermeticism</a>. Culminating in the reforms advocated by <a href="/wiki/Apollonius_of_Tyana" title="Apollonius of Tyana">Apollonius of Tyana</a> being adopted by <a href="/wiki/Aurelian" title="Aurelian">Aurelian</a> and formulised by <a href="/wiki/Flavius_Claudius_Julianus" class="mw-redirect" title="Flavius Claudius Julianus">Flavius Claudius Julianus</a> to create an organised but short-lived pagan state religion that ensured its underground survival into the Byzantine age and beyond.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many of the new religions relied on the emergence of the <a href="/wiki/Parchment" title="Parchment">parchment</a> <i>codex</i> (bound book) over the <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">papyrus</a> <i>volumen</i> (scroll), the former allowing for quicker access to key materials and easier portability than the fragile scroll, thus fueling the rise of synoptic <a href="/wiki/Exegesis" title="Exegesis">exegesis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Papyrology" title="Papyrology">papyrology</a>. Notable in this regard is the topic of the <a href="/wiki/Fifty_Bibles_of_Constantine" title="Fifty Bibles of Constantine">Fifty Bibles of Constantine</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Laity_vs_clergy">Laity vs clergy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_antiquity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Laity vs clergy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Within the recently legitimized Christian community of the 4th century, a division could be more distinctly seen between the <a href="/wiki/Laity" title="Laity">laity</a> and an increasingly <a href="/wiki/Celibacy" title="Celibacy">celibate</a> male leadership.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> These men presented themselves as removed from the traditional Roman motivations of <a href="/wiki/Public_sphere" title="Public sphere">public</a> and <a href="/wiki/Private_life" class="mw-redirect" title="Private life">private life</a> marked by pride, ambition and kinship solidarity, and differing from the married pagan leadership. Unlike later strictures on <a href="/wiki/Priestly_celibacy" class="mw-redirect" title="Priestly celibacy">priestly celibacy</a>, celibacy in Late Antique Christianity sometimes took the form of abstinence from sexual relations after marriage, and it came to be the expected norm for urban <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">clergy</a>. Celibate and detached, the upper clergy became an elite equal in prestige to urban notables, the <i>potentes</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Dynatoi" title="Dynatoi">dynatoi</a></i> (Brown (1987) p.&#160;270).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_rise_of_Islam">The rise of Islam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_antiquity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The rise of Islam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Byzantiumby650AD.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Byzantiumby650AD.svg/260px-Byzantiumby650AD.svg.png" decoding="async" width="260" height="119" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Byzantiumby650AD.svg/390px-Byzantiumby650AD.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Byzantiumby650AD.svg/520px-Byzantiumby650AD.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="924" data-file-height="423" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Byzantiumby650AD.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Byzantine Empire after the <a href="/wiki/Early_Muslim_conquests" title="Early Muslim conquests">Arabs conquered</a> the provinces of Syria and Egypt. At the same time the <a href="/wiki/Early_Slavs" title="Early Slavs">early Slavs</a> settled in the Balkans.</div></div></div>
<p>Islam appeared in the 7th century and spurred Arab peoples to invade the Eastern Roman Empire and the <a href="/wiki/Sassanian_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Sassanian Empire">Sassanian Empire</a> of <a href="/wiki/Persia" class="mw-redirect" title="Persia">Persia</a>, destroying the latter; and, after conquering all of <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Visigothic_Spain" class="mw-redirect" title="Visigothic Spain">Visigothic Spain</a>, to invade much of modern <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On the rise of Islam, two main theses prevail. On the one hand, there is the traditional view, as espoused by most historians prior to the second half of the twentieth century and by Muslim scholars. This view, the so-called "out of Arabia"-thesis, holds that Islam as a phenomenon was a new, alien element in the late antique world. Related to this is the <a href="/wiki/Pirenne_Thesis" class="mw-redirect" title="Pirenne Thesis">Pirenne Thesis</a>, according to which the <a href="/wiki/Arab" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab">Arab</a> invasions marked—through conquest and the disruption of Mediterranean trade routes—the cataclysmic end of Late Antiquity and the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>.
</p><p>On the other hand, there is the modern view, associated with scholars in the tradition of Peter Brown, in which Islam is seen to be a product of the Late Antique world, not foreign to it. This school suggests that its origin within the shared cultural horizon of the late antique world explains the character of Islam and its development. Such historians point to similarities with other late antique religions and philosophies—especially Christianity—in the prominent role and manifestations of piety in Islam, in Islamic asceticism and the role of "holy persons", in the pattern of universalist, homogeneous monotheism tied to worldly and military power, in early Islamic engagement with Greek schools of thought, in the apocalypticism of Islamic theology and in the way the <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a> seems to react to contemporary religious and cultural issues shared by the late antique world at large. Further indication that Arabia (and thus the environment in which Islam first developed) was a part of the late antique world is found in the close economic and military relations between Arabia, the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a> and the Sassanian Empire.<sup id="cite_ref-hoyland_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hoyland-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_transformations">Political transformations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_antiquity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Political transformations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:342px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_William_Waterhouse_-_The_Favorites_of_the_Emperor_Honorius_-_1883.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/John_William_Waterhouse_-_The_Favorites_of_the_Emperor_Honorius_-_1883.jpg/340px-John_William_Waterhouse_-_The_Favorites_of_the_Emperor_Honorius_-_1883.jpg" decoding="async" width="340" height="194" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/John_William_Waterhouse_-_The_Favorites_of_the_Emperor_Honorius_-_1883.jpg/510px-John_William_Waterhouse_-_The_Favorites_of_the_Emperor_Honorius_-_1883.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/John_William_Waterhouse_-_The_Favorites_of_the_Emperor_Honorius_-_1883.jpg/680px-John_William_Waterhouse_-_The_Favorites_of_the_Emperor_Honorius_-_1883.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1576" data-file-height="900" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:John_William_Waterhouse_-_The_Favorites_of_the_Emperor_Honorius_-_1883.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>The Favourites of the <a href="/wiki/Honorius_(emperor)" title="Honorius (emperor)">Emperor Honorius</a></i>, 1883: <a href="/wiki/John_William_Waterhouse" title="John William Waterhouse">John William Waterhouse</a> expresses the sense of moral decadence that coloured the 19th-century historical view of the 5th century.</div></div></div>
<p>The Late Antique period also saw a wholesale transformation of the <a href="/wiki/Political" class="mw-redirect" title="Political">political</a> and <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">social</a> basis of life in and around the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>.
</p><p>The Roman citizen elite in the 2nd and 3rd centuries, under the pressure of taxation and the ruinous cost of presenting spectacular public entertainments in the traditional <i><a href="/wiki/Cursus_honorum" title="Cursus honorum">cursus honorum</a></i>, had found under the <a href="/wiki/Antonines" class="mw-redirect" title="Antonines">Antonines</a> that security could only be obtained by combining their established roles in the local town with new ones as servants and representatives of a distant Emperor and his traveling court. After Constantine centralized the government in his new capital of <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> (dedicated in 330), the Late Antique upper classes were divided among those who had access to the far-away centralized administration (in concert with the <a href="/wiki/Latifundia" class="mw-redirect" title="Latifundia">great landowners</a>), and those who did not—though they were well-born and thoroughly educated, a classical education and the election by the Senate to magistracies was no longer the path to success. Room at the top of Late Antique society was more bureaucratic and involved increasingly intricate channels of access to the emperor: the plain toga that had identified all members of the <a href="/wiki/Nobiles" title="Nobiles">Republican senatorial class</a> was replaced with the silk court vestments and jewelry associated with Byzantine imperial iconography.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  Also indicative of the times is the fact that the imperial cabinet of advisors came to be known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Consistorium" title="Consistorium">consistorium</a></i>, or those who would stand in courtly attendance upon their seated emperor, as distinct from the informal set of friends and advisors surrounding the <i><a href="/wiki/Augustus_(honorific)" class="mw-redirect" title="Augustus (honorific)">Augustus</a></i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cities">Cities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_antiquity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The later Roman Empire was in a sense a network of cities. Archaeology now supplements literary sources to document the transformation followed by collapse of cities in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_basin" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean basin">Mediterranean basin</a>. Two diagnostic symptoms of decline—or as many historians prefer, 'transformation'—are subdivision, particularly of expansive formal spaces in both the <i><a href="/wiki/Domus" title="Domus">domus</a></i> and the public <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">basilica</a>, and encroachment, in which artisanal shops invade the public thoroughfare, a transformation that was to result in the <i><a href="/wiki/Souk" class="mw-redirect" title="Souk">souk</a></i> (marketplace).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Burials within the urban precincts mark another stage in dissolution of traditional urbanistic discipline, overpowered by the attraction of saintly shrines and relics. In Roman Britain, the typical 4th- and 5th-century layer of "black earth" within cities seems to be a result of increased gardening in formerly urban spaces.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The city of Rome went from a population of 800,000 in the beginning of the period to a population of 30,000 by the end of the period, the most precipitous drop coming with the breaking of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_aqueducts" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman aqueducts">aqueducts</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Gothic_War_(535%E2%80%93554)" title="Gothic War (535–554)">Gothic War</a>. A similar though less marked decline in urban population occurred later in Constantinople, which was gaining population until the outbreak of plague in 541. In Europe there was also a general decline in urban populations. As a whole, the period of late antiquity was accompanied by an overall population decline in almost all Europe, and a reversion to more of a subsistence economy. Long-distance markets disappeared, and there was a reversion to a greater degree of local production and consumption, rather than webs of commerce and specialized production.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ephesus_Curetes_street.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Ephesus_Curetes_street.jpg/220px-Ephesus_Curetes_street.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Ephesus_Curetes_street.jpg/330px-Ephesus_Curetes_street.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Ephesus_Curetes_street.jpg/440px-Ephesus_Curetes_street.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2816" data-file-height="1880" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ephesus_Curetes_street.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>View west along the Harbour Street towards the Library of Celsus in <a href="/wiki/Ephesus" title="Ephesus">Ephesus</a>, present-day <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>. The pillars on the left side of the street were part of the <a href="/wiki/Colonnade" title="Colonnade">colonnaded</a> walkway apparent in cities of Late Antique <a href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" class="mw-redirect" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Concurrently, the continuity of the Eastern Roman Empire at Constantinople meant that the turning-point for the <a href="/wiki/Greek_East" class="mw-redirect" title="Greek East">Greek East</a> came later, in the 7th century, as the Eastern Roman, or Byzantine Empire centered around the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a>, North Africa (<a href="/wiki/Egypt_(Roman_province)" title="Egypt (Roman province)">Egypt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Praetorian_prefecture_of_Africa" title="Praetorian prefecture of Africa">Carthage</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" class="mw-redirect" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a>. The degree and extent of discontinuity in the smaller cities of the Greek East is a moot subject among historians.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The urban continuity of Constantinople is the outstanding example of the Mediterranean world; of the two great cities of lesser rank, <a href="/wiki/Antioch" title="Antioch">Antioch</a> was devastated by the Persian sack of 540, followed by the <a href="/wiki/Plague_of_Justinian" title="Plague of Justinian">plague of Justinian</a> (542 onwards) and completed by earthquake, while <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a> survived its Islamic transformation, to suffer incremental decline in favour of <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a> in the medieval period.
</p><p>Justinian rebuilt his birthplace in <a href="/wiki/Praetorian_prefecture_of_Illyricum" title="Praetorian prefecture of Illyricum">Illyricum</a>, as <i>Justiniana Prima</i>, more in a gesture of <i>imperium</i> than out of an urbanistic necessity; another  "city", was reputed to have been founded, according to <a href="/wiki/Procopius" title="Procopius">Procopius</a>' panegyric on Justinian's buildings,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> precisely at the spot where the general <a href="/wiki/Belisarius" title="Belisarius">Belisarius</a> touched shore in North Africa: the miraculous spring that gushed forth to give them water and the rural population that straightway abandoned their ploughshares for civilised life within the new walls, lend a certain taste of unreality to the project.
</p><p>In mainland Greece, the inhabitants of <a href="/wiki/History_of_Sparta" title="History of Sparta">Sparta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argos" title="Argos">Argos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Corinth" title="Ancient Corinth">Corinth</a> abandoned their cities for fortified sites in nearby high places; the fortified heights of <a href="/wiki/Acrocorinth" title="Acrocorinth">Acrocorinth</a> are typical of Byzantine urban sites in Greece. In Italy, populations that had clustered within reach of <a href="/wiki/Roman_road" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman road">Roman roads</a> began to withdraw from them, as potential avenues of intrusion, and to rebuild in typically constricted fashion round an isolated fortified promontory, or <i><a href="/wiki/Rocca_(architecture)" title="Rocca (architecture)">rocca</a></i>; Cameron notes similar movement of populations in the Balkans, 'where inhabited centres contracted and regrouped around a defensible <a href="/wiki/Acropolis" title="Acropolis">acropolis</a>, or were abandoned in favour of such positions elsewhere."<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Roman_cavalry_-_Big_Game_Hunt_mosaic_-_Villa_Romana_del_Casale_-_Italy_2015.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Roman_cavalry_-_Big_Game_Hunt_mosaic_-_Villa_Romana_del_Casale_-_Italy_2015.JPG/300px-Roman_cavalry_-_Big_Game_Hunt_mosaic_-_Villa_Romana_del_Casale_-_Italy_2015.JPG" decoding="async" width="300" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Roman_cavalry_-_Big_Game_Hunt_mosaic_-_Villa_Romana_del_Casale_-_Italy_2015.JPG/450px-Roman_cavalry_-_Big_Game_Hunt_mosaic_-_Villa_Romana_del_Casale_-_Italy_2015.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Roman_cavalry_-_Big_Game_Hunt_mosaic_-_Villa_Romana_del_Casale_-_Italy_2015.JPG/600px-Roman_cavalry_-_Big_Game_Hunt_mosaic_-_Villa_Romana_del_Casale_-_Italy_2015.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2048" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Roman_cavalry_-_Big_Game_Hunt_mosaic_-_Villa_Romana_del_Casale_-_Italy_2015.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Roman_cavalry" title="Roman cavalry">Roman cavalry</a> from a <a href="/wiki/Mosaic" title="Mosaic">mosaic</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Villa_Romana_del_Casale" title="Villa Romana del Casale">Villa Romana del Casale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sicilia_(Roman_province)" title="Sicilia (Roman province)">Sicily</a>, 4th century AD</div></div></div>
<p>In the western Mediterranean, the only new cities known to be founded in Europe between the 5th and 8th centuries<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> were the four or five <a href="/wiki/Visigoth" class="mw-redirect" title="Visigoth">Visigothic</a> "victory cities".<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Reccopolis" title="Reccopolis">Reccopolis</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Guadalajara_(province)" class="mw-redirect" title="Guadalajara (province)">province of Guadalajara</a> is one: the others were   <i>Victoriacum</i>, founded by <a href="/wiki/Leovigild" class="mw-redirect" title="Leovigild">Leovigild</a>, which may survive as the city of <a href="/wiki/Vitoria-Gasteiz" title="Vitoria-Gasteiz">Vitoria</a>, though a 12th-century (re)foundation for this city is given in contemporary sources; <i>Lugo id est Luceo</i> in the <a href="/wiki/Asturias" title="Asturias">Asturias</a>, referred to by <a href="/wiki/Isidore_of_Seville" title="Isidore of Seville">Isidore of Seville</a>, and <i>Ologicus</i> (perhaps <i>Ologitis</i>), founded using <a href="/wiki/Basques" title="Basques">Basque</a> labour in 621 by <a href="/wiki/Suinthila" class="mw-redirect" title="Suinthila">Suinthila</a> as a fortification against the Basques, modern <a href="/wiki/Olite" title="Olite">Olite</a>. All of these cities were founded for military purposes and at least Reccopolis, Victoriacum, and Ologicus in celebration of victory. A possible fifth Visigothic foundation is <i>Baiyara</i> (perhaps modern <a href="/wiki/Montoro" title="Montoro">Montoro</a>), mentioned as founded by Reccared in the 15th-century geographical account, <i><a href="/wiki/Kitab_al-Rawd_al-Mitar" title="Kitab al-Rawd al-Mitar">Kitab al-Rawd al-Mitar</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The arrival of a highly urbanized Islamic culture in the decade following 711 ensured the survival of cities in the <i>Hispaniae</i> into the Middle Ages.
</p><p>Beyond the Mediterranean world, the cities of <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a> withdrew within a constricted line of defense around a citadel. Former imperial capitals such as <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trier" title="Trier">Trier</a> lived on in diminished form as administrative centres of the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Britain</a>, where the break with Late Antiquity comes earliest in the 5th and the 6th century, most towns cities had been in rapid decline during the 4th century during a time of prosperity until the very last decades of the century, well before the withdrawal of Roman governors and garrisons; historians emphasizing urban continuities with the Anglo-Saxon period depend largely on the post-Roman survival of Roman <a href="/wiki/Toponymy" title="Toponymy">toponymy</a>. Aside from a mere handful of its continuously inhabited sites, like York and London and possibly Canterbury, however, the rapidity and thoroughness with which its urban life collapsed with the dissolution of centralized bureaucracy calls into question the extent to which <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman Britain</a> had ever become authentically urbanized: "in Roman Britain towns appeared a shade exotic," observes <a href="/wiki/H._R._Loyn" title="H. R. Loyn">H. R. Loyn</a>, "owing their reason for being more to the military and administrative needs of Rome than to any economic virtue".<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The other institutional power centre,  the <a href="/wiki/Roman_villa" title="Roman villa">Roman villa</a>, did not survive in Britain either.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gildas" title="Gildas">Gildas</a> lamented the destruction of the twenty-eight cities of Britain; though not all in his list can be identified with known Roman sites, Loyn finds no reason to doubt the essential truth of his statement.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Classical_Antiquity" class="mw-redirect" title="Classical Antiquity">Classical Antiquity</a> can generally be defined as an age of cities; the Greek <a href="/wiki/Polis" title="Polis">polis</a> and Roman <a href="/wiki/Municipium" title="Municipium">municipium</a> were locally organised, self-governing bodies of citizens governed by written constitutions.  When Rome came to dominate the known world, local initiative and control were gradually subsumed by the ever-growing Imperial bureaucracy; by the <a href="/wiki/Crisis_of_the_Third_Century" title="Crisis of the Third Century">Crisis of the Third Century</a> the military, political and economic demands made by the Empire had crushed the civic spirit, and service in local government came to be an onerous duty, often imposed as punishment.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  Harassed urban dwellers fled to the walled estates of the wealthy to avoid taxes, military service, famine and disease. In the Western Roman Empire especially, many cities destroyed by invasion or civil war in the 3rd century could not be rebuilt.  Plague and famine hit the urban class in greater proportion, and thus the people who knew how to keep civic services running.  Perhaps the greatest blow came in the wake of the <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather_events_of_535%E2%80%93536" title="Extreme weather events of 535–536">extreme weather events of 535–536</a> and subsequent <a href="/wiki/Plague_of_Justinian" title="Plague of Justinian">Plague of Justinian</a>, when the remaining trade networks ensured the Plague spread to the remaining commercial cities.  The end of <a href="/wiki/Classical_Antiquity" class="mw-redirect" title="Classical Antiquity">Classical Antiquity</a> is the end of the Polis model, and the general decline of cities is a defining feature of Late Antiquity.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_building">Public building</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_antiquity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Public building">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the cities the strained economies of Roman over-expansion arrested growth. Almost all new public building in Late Antiquity came directly or indirectly from the emperors or imperial officials. Attempts were made to maintain what was already there. The supply of free grain and oil to 20% of the population of Rome remained intact the last decades of the 5th century. It was once thought that the elite and rich had withdrawn to the private luxuries of their numerous <a href="/wiki/Villa" title="Villa">villas</a> and town houses. Scholarly opinion has revised this. They monopolized the higher offices in the imperial administration, but they were removed from military command by the late 3rd century. Their focus turned to preserving their vast wealth rather than fighting for it.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">basilica</a>, which had functioned as a law court or for imperial reception of foreign dignitaries, became the primary public building in the 4th century. Due to the stress on civic finances, cities spent money on walls, maintaining baths and markets at the expense of amphitheaters, temples, libraries, porticoes, gymnasia, concert and lecture halls, theaters and other amenities of public life. In any case as Christianity took over many of these building which were associated with pagan cults were neglected in favor of building churches and donating to the poor. The Christian basilica was copied from the civic structure with variations. The bishop took the chair in the apse reserved in secular structures for the magistrate—or the Emperor himself—as the representative here and now of <a href="/wiki/Christ_Pantocrator" title="Christ Pantocrator">Christ Pantocrator</a>, the Ruler of All, his characteristic Late Antique <a href="/wiki/Icon" title="Icon">icon</a>.  These ecclesiastical basilicas (e.g., <a href="/wiki/St._John_Lateran" class="mw-redirect" title="St. John Lateran">St. John Lateran</a> and <a href="/wiki/St._Peter%27s_Basilica" title="St. Peter&#39;s Basilica">St. Peter's</a> in Rome) were themselves outdone by Justinian's <a href="/wiki/Hagia_Sophia" title="Hagia Sophia">Hagia Sophia</a>, a staggering display of later Roman/Byzantine power and architectural taste, though the building is not architecturally a basilica. In the former Western Roman Empire no great buildings were constructed from the 5th century. A most outstanding example is the Church of San Vitaly in Ravenna constructed circa 530 at a cost of 26,000 gold <a href="/wiki/Solidus_(coin)" title="Solidus (coin)">solidi</a> or 360 <a href="/wiki/Roman_pound" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman pound">Roman pounds</a> of gold.
</p><p>City life in the East, though negatively affected by the plague in the 6th–7th centuries, finally collapsed due to Slavic invasions in the Balkans and 
Persian destructions in Anatolia in the 620s. City life continued in Syria, Jordan and Palestine into the 8th. In the later 6th century street construction was still undertaken in <a href="/wiki/Caesarea_Maritima" title="Caesarea Maritima">Caesarea Maritima</a> in Palestine,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Edessa,_Mesopotamia" class="mw-redirect" title="Edessa, Mesopotamia">Edessa</a> was able to deflect <a href="/wiki/Chosroes_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Chosroes I">Chosroes I</a> with massive payments in gold in 540 and 544, before it was overrun in 609.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sculpture_and_art">Sculpture and art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_antiquity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sculpture and art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Venice_%E2%80%93_The_Tetrarchs_03.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Venice_%E2%80%93_The_Tetrarchs_03.jpg/220px-Venice_%E2%80%93_The_Tetrarchs_03.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Venice_%E2%80%93_The_Tetrarchs_03.jpg/330px-Venice_%E2%80%93_The_Tetrarchs_03.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Venice_%E2%80%93_The_Tetrarchs_03.jpg/440px-Venice_%E2%80%93_The_Tetrarchs_03.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="3072" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Venice_%E2%80%93_The_Tetrarchs_03.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Portrait_of_the_Four_Tetrarchs" title="Portrait of the Four Tetrarchs"><i>The Four Tetrarchs</i></a>, in <a href="/wiki/Porphyry_(geology)" title="Porphyry (geology)">porphyry</a>, later sacked from <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>, <a href="/wiki/St._Marks,_Venice" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Marks, Venice">St. Marks, Venice</a></div></div></div>
<p>As a complicated period bridging between <a href="/wiki/Roman_art" title="Roman art">Roman art</a> and <a href="/wiki/Medieval_art" title="Medieval art">medieval art</a> and <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_art" title="Byzantine art">Byzantine art</a>, the Late Antique period saw a transition from the classical idealized <a href="/wiki/Realism_(visual_arts)" class="mw-redirect" title="Realism (visual arts)">realism</a> tradition largely influenced by Ancient Greek art to the more iconic, stylized art of the Middle Ages.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKitzinger19772–21_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKitzinger19772–21-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Unlike classical art, Late Antique art does not emphasize the beauty and movement of the body, but rather, hints at the spiritual reality behind its subjects. Additionally, mirroring the rise of Christianity and the collapse of the western Roman Empire, painting and freestanding sculpture gradually fell from favor in the artistic community. Replacing them were greater interests in mosaics, architecture, and relief sculpture.
</p><p>As the soldier emperors such as <a href="/wiki/Maximinus_Thrax" title="Maximinus Thrax">Maximinus Thrax</a> (r. 235–238) emerged from the provinces in the 3rd century, they brought with them their own regional influences and artistic tastes. For example, artists jettisoned the classical portrayal of the human body for one that was more rigid and frontal. This is markedly evident in the combined <a href="/wiki/Porphyry_(geology)" title="Porphyry (geology)">porphyry</a> <a href="/wiki/Portrait_of_the_Four_Tetrarchs" title="Portrait of the Four Tetrarchs">Portrait of the Four Tetrarchs</a> in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>. With these stubby figures clutching each other and their swords, all <a href="/wiki/Individualism" title="Individualism">individualism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Naturalism_(art)" class="mw-redirect" title="Naturalism (art)">naturalism</a>, the verism or hyperrealism of Roman portraiture, and Greek <a href="/wiki/Idealism" title="Idealism">idealism</a> diminish.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKitzinger19779_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKitzinger19779-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKitzinger197712–13_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKitzinger197712–13-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Arch_of_Constantine" title="Arch of Constantine">Arch of Constantine</a> in Rome, which re-used earlier classicising <a href="/wiki/Relief" title="Relief">reliefs</a> together with ones in the new style, shows the contrast especially clearly.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKitzinger19777–8_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKitzinger19777–8-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> In nearly all artistic media, simpler shapes were adopted and once natural designs were abstracted. Additionally hierarchy of scale overtook the preeminence of perspective and other classical models for representing spatial organization.
</p><p>From around 300 <a href="/wiki/Early_Christian_art" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Christian art">Early Christian art</a> began to create new public forms, which now included <a href="/wiki/Sculpture" title="Sculpture">sculpture</a>, previously distrusted by Christians as it was so important in pagan worship. <a href="/wiki/Sarcophagi" class="mw-redirect" title="Sarcophagi">Sarcophagi</a> carved in relief had already become highly elaborate, and Christian versions adopted new styles, showing a series of different tightly packed scenes rather than one overall image (usually derived from Greek <a href="/wiki/History_painting" title="History painting">history painting</a>) as was the norm. Soon the scenes were split into two registers, as in the <a href="/wiki/Dogmatic_Sarcophagus" title="Dogmatic Sarcophagus">Dogmatic Sarcophagus</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Sarcophagus_of_Junius_Bassus" title="Sarcophagus of Junius Bassus">Sarcophagus of Junius Bassus</a> (the last of these exemplifying a partial revival of classicism).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKitzinger197715–28_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKitzinger197715–28-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Nearly all of these more abstracted conventions could be observed in the glittering mosaics of the era, which during this period moved from being decoration derivative from painting used on floors (and walls likely to become wet) to a major vehicle of religious art in churches.  The glazed surfaces of the <a href="/wiki/Tesserae" class="mw-redirect" title="Tesserae">tesserae</a> sparkled in the light and illuminated the basilica churches. Unlike their <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">fresco</a> predecessors, much more emphasis was placed on demonstrating a symbolic fact rather than on rendering a realistic scene. As time progressed during the Late Antique period, art become more concerned with biblical themes and influenced by interactions of Christianity with the Roman state. Within this Christian subcategory of Roman art, dramatic changes were also taking place in the <a href="/wiki/Depiction_of_Jesus" title="Depiction of Jesus">Depiction of Jesus</a>. Jesus Christ had been more commonly depicted as an itinerant philosopher, teacher or as the "Good Shepherd," resembling the traditional iconography of Hermes. He was increasingly given Roman elite status, and shrouded in purple robes like the emperors with orb and scepter in hand.
</p><p>As for luxury arts, manuscript illumination on vellum and parchment emerged from the 5th century, with a few manuscripts of Roman literary classics like the <a href="/wiki/Vergilius_Vaticanus" title="Vergilius Vaticanus">Vergilius Vaticanus</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Vergilius_Romanus" title="Vergilius Romanus">Vergilius Romanus</a>, but increasingly Christian texts, of which <a href="/wiki/Quedlinburg_Itala_fragment" title="Quedlinburg Itala fragment">Quedlinburg Itala fragment</a> (420–430) is the oldest survivor.  Carved ivory <a href="/wiki/Diptych" title="Diptych">diptychs</a> were used for secular subjects, as in the imperial and <a href="/wiki/Consular_diptych" title="Consular diptych">consular diptychs</a> presented to friends, as well as religious ones, both Christian and pagan – they seem to have been especially a vehicle for the last group of powerful pagans to resist Christianity, as in the late 4th century <a href="/wiki/Symmachi%E2%80%93Nicomachi_diptych" title="Symmachi–Nicomachi diptych">Symmachi–Nicomachi diptych</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKitzinger197729–34_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKitzinger197729–34-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Extravagant <a href="/wiki/Hoard" title="Hoard">hoards</a> of silver plate are especially common from the 4th century, including the <a href="/wiki/Mildenhall_Treasure" title="Mildenhall Treasure">Mildenhall Treasure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Esquiline_Treasure" title="Esquiline Treasure">Esquiline Treasure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hoxne_Hoard" title="Hoxne Hoard">Hoxne Hoard</a>, and the imperial <a href="/wiki/Missorium_of_Theodosius_I" title="Missorium of Theodosius I">Missorium of Theodosius I</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKitzinger1977_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKitzinger1977-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_antiquity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ViennaDioscoridesPlant.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/ViennaDioscoridesPlant.jpg/220px-ViennaDioscoridesPlant.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="275" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/ViennaDioscoridesPlant.jpg/330px-ViennaDioscoridesPlant.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/ViennaDioscoridesPlant.jpg/440px-ViennaDioscoridesPlant.jpg 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="639" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ViennaDioscoridesPlant.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Dioscurides" title="Vienna Dioscurides">Vienna Dioscurides</a>, an early 6th-century <a href="/wiki/Illuminated_manuscript" title="Illuminated manuscript">illuminated manuscript</a> of <i>De <a href="/wiki/Materia_Medica" class="mw-redirect" title="Materia Medica">Materia Medica</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Dioscorides" class="mw-redirect" title="Dioscorides">Dioscorides</a> in Greek, a rare example of a late antique scientific text.</div></div></div>
<p>In the field of literature, Late Antiquity is known for the declining use of classical Greek and Latin, and the rise of literary cultures in <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">Syriac</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armenian_language" title="Armenian language">Armenian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Old_Georgian_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Georgian language">Georgian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ge%27ez_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ge&#39;ez language">Ethiopic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Coptic_language" title="Coptic language">Coptic</a>. It also marks a shift in literary style, with a preference for encyclopedic works in a dense and allusive style, consisting of summaries of earlier works (anthologies, epitomes) often dressed up in elaborate allegorical garb (e.g., <i>De nuptiis Mercurii et Philologiae</i> [The Marriage of Mercury and Philology] of <a href="/wiki/Martianus_Capella" title="Martianus Capella">Martianus Capella</a> and the <i>De arithmetica</i>, <i>De musica</i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/De_consolatione_philosophiae" class="mw-redirect" title="De consolatione philosophiae">De consolatione philosophiae</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Anicius_Manlius_Severinus_Boethius" class="mw-redirect" title="Anicius Manlius Severinus Boethius">Boethius</a>—both later key works in medieval education). The 4th and 5th centuries also saw an explosion of <a href="/wiki/Christian_literature" title="Christian literature">Christian literature</a>, of which Greek writers such as <a href="/wiki/Eusebius_of_Caesarea" class="mw-redirect" title="Eusebius of Caesarea">Eusebius of Caesarea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basil_of_Caesarea" title="Basil of Caesarea">Basil of Caesarea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gregory_of_Nazianzus" title="Gregory of Nazianzus">Gregory of Nazianzus</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Chrysostom" title="John Chrysostom">John Chrysostom</a> and Latin writers such as <a href="/wiki/Ambrose_of_Milan" class="mw-redirect" title="Ambrose of Milan">Ambrose of Milan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jerome" title="Jerome">Jerome</a> and <a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo" title="Augustine of Hippo">Augustine of Hippo</a> are only among the most renowned representatives. On the other hand, authors such as <a href="/wiki/Ammianus_Marcellinus" title="Ammianus Marcellinus">Ammianus Marcellinus</a> (4th century) and <a href="/wiki/Procopius_of_Caesarea" class="mw-redirect" title="Procopius of Caesarea">Procopius of Caesarea</a> (6th century) were able to keep the tradition of classical <a href="/wiki/Historiography" title="Historiography">historiography</a> alive.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poetry">Poetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_antiquity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Poetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Greek poets of the Late Antique period included <a href="/wiki/Antoninus_Liberalis" title="Antoninus Liberalis">Antoninus Liberalis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quintus_Smyrnaeus" title="Quintus Smyrnaeus">Quintus Smyrnaeus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nonnus" title="Nonnus">Nonnus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romanus_the_Melodist" class="mw-redirect" title="Romanus the Melodist">Romanus the Melodist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_the_Silentiary" title="Paul the Silentiary">Paul the Silentiary</a>.
</p><p>Latin poets included <a href="/wiki/Ausonius" title="Ausonius">Ausonius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paulinus_of_Nola" title="Paulinus of Nola">Paulinus of Nola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Claudian" title="Claudian">Claudian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rutilius_Namatianus" class="mw-redirect" title="Rutilius Namatianus">Rutilius Namatianus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orientius" title="Orientius">Orientius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sidonius_Apollinaris" title="Sidonius Apollinaris">Sidonius Apollinaris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corippus" title="Corippus">Corippus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arator" title="Arator">Arator</a>.
</p><p>Jewish poets included <a href="/wiki/Yanai_(Payetan)" title="Yanai (Payetan)">Yannai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eleazar_ben_Killir" class="mw-redirect" title="Eleazar ben Killir">Eleazar ben Killir</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jose_b._Jose" class="mw-redirect" title="Jose b. Jose">Yose ben Yose</a>.
</p>

<li>486: In the   <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Soissons_(486)" title="Battle of Soissons (486)">Battle of Soissons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clovis_I" title="Clovis I">Clovis I</a> defeats the Roman <a href="/wiki/Rump_state" title="Rump state">rump state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Soissons" title="Kingdom of Soissons">Soissons</a>, establishing <a href="/wiki/Merovingian" class="mw-redirect" title="Merovingian">Merovingian</a> <a href="/wiki/Francia" title="Francia">Francia</a>.</li>
<li>529: The Eastern Roman Emperor <a href="/wiki/Justinian_I" title="Justinian I">Justinian I</a> ordered the prominent philosophical schools of antiquity throughout the Eastern Roman Empire (including the famous <a href="/wiki/Academy_in_Athens" class="mw-redirect" title="Academy in Athens">Academy in Athens</a>, among others) to close down—allegedly, because Justinian frowned upon the pagan nature of these schools</li>
<li>534: The <i><a href="/wiki/Corpus_Juris_Civilis" title="Corpus Juris Civilis">Corpus Juris Civilis</a></i>, otherwise known as the <i>Code of Justinian</i> is completed. The new law code will influence Medieval European Law and the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Code" title="Napoleonic Code">Napoleonic Code</a>.</li>
<li>537: The <a href="/wiki/Hagia_Sophia" title="Hagia Sophia">Hagia Sophia</a> the largest Christian building ever created is built in Constantinople, becoming a center of Byzantine society for the next millennium,</li>
<li>542: <a href="/wiki/Plague_of_Justinian" title="Plague of Justinian">Plague of Justinian</a>, a plague strikes Constantinople, and the rest of Europe, possibly killing half of Europe's population.</li>
<li>602: The beginning of the final <a href="/wiki/Byzantine%E2%80%93Sasanian_War_of_602%E2%80%93628" title="Byzantine–Sasanian War of 602–628">Byzantine-Sassanian War</a>,  lasting until 628. War encompasses entire Near East, exhausting both combatants.</li>
<li>634: The <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_al-Qaryatayn" title="Battle of al-Qaryatayn">Battle of al-Qaryatayn</a> marks the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Arab_conquest_of_Syria" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab conquest of Syria">Arab conquest of Syria</a>.</li></ul>
