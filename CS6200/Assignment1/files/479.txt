	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Disownment</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Disown" redirects here. For the band, see <a href="/wiki/Disown_(band)" title="Disown (band)">Disown (band)</a>. For the Unix command, see <a href="/wiki/Disown_(Unix)" title="Disown (Unix)">disown (Unix)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Disownment</b> occurs when a parent renounces or no longer accepts a <a href="/wiki/Offspring" title="Offspring">child</a> as a family member. It differs from giving a child up for <a href="/wiki/Adoption" title="Adoption">adoption</a>. It is a <a href="/wiki/Social" title="Social">social</a> and <a href="/wiki/Interpersonal" class="mw-redirect" title="Interpersonal">interpersonal</a> act and usually takes place later in the child's life. Among other things, it implies no responsibility for future care, making it similar to <a href="/wiki/Divorce" title="Divorce">divorce</a> or <a href="/wiki/Repudiation_(marriage)" title="Repudiation (marriage)">repudiation</a> (of a spouse). Disownment may entail <a href="/wiki/Disinherit" class="mw-redirect" title="Disinherit">disinheritance</a>, familial <a href="/wiki/Exile" title="Exile">exile</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Shunning" title="Shunning">shunning</a>, and often all three. 
</p><p>Disownment is often <a href="/wiki/Taboo" title="Taboo">taboo</a>. In many countries it is a form of <a href="/wiki/Child_abandonment" title="Child abandonment">child abandonment</a> and is illegal when the child is a minor. 
</p><p>In rare cases, a society and its institutions will accept an act of disownment.  For example, the British politician <a href="/wiki/Leo_Amery#Personal_life" title="Leo Amery">Leo Amery</a> had two adult sons, both young adults at the time of World War II; one fought in the British forces, while the other, <a href="/wiki/John_Amery" title="John Amery">John Amery</a>, cast his lot with <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> and beamed propaganda radio broadcasts to his homeland.  After the end of the war in 1945, young Amery was tried and executed for <a href="/wiki/Treason" title="Treason">treason</a>, whereupon the bereaved father asked, and received, permission from the editors of <i><a href="/wiki/Who%27s_Who_(UK)" title="Who&#39;s Who (UK)">Who's Who</a></i> to change the terms of his authorized biography from two sons to "one son".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
