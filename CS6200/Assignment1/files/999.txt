	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Las Meninas</i></h1>

<p><i><b>Las Meninas</b></i><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> (<small>pronounced&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">[laz meˈninas]</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>&#160;for "<i>The Ladies-in-waiting</i>") is a 1656 painting in the <a href="/wiki/Museo_del_Prado" title="Museo del Prado">Museo del Prado</a> in <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a>, by <a href="/wiki/Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez" title="Diego Velázquez">Diego Velázquez</a>, the leading artist of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Golden_Age" title="Spanish Golden Age">Spanish Golden Age</a>. Its complex and enigmatic composition raises questions about reality and illusion, and creates an uncertain relationship between the viewer and the figures depicted. Because of these complexities, <i>Las Meninas</i> has been one of the most widely analyzed works in <a href="/wiki/Western_painting" title="Western painting">Western painting</a>.
</p><p>The painting shows a large room in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Alcazar_of_Madrid" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Alcazar of Madrid">Royal Alcazar of Madrid</a> during the reign of King <a href="/wiki/Philip_IV_of_Spain" title="Philip IV of Spain">Philip IV of Spain</a>, and presents several figures, most identifiable from the Spanish court, captured, according to some commentators, in a particular moment as if in a <a href="/wiki/Snapshot_(photography)" title="Snapshot (photography)">snapshot</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Some look out of the canvas towards the viewer, while others interact among themselves. The young <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Theresa_of_Spain" title="Margaret Theresa of Spain">Infanta Margaret Theresa</a> is surrounded by her entourage of <a href="/wiki/Maid_of_honour" title="Maid of honour">maids of honour</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chaperone_(social)" title="Chaperone (social)">chaperone</a>, bodyguard, two dwarfs and a dog. Just behind them, Velázquez portrays himself working at a large canvas. Velázquez looks outwards, beyond the pictorial space to where a viewer of the painting would stand.<sup id="cite_ref-K225_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-K225-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In the background there is a mirror that reflects the upper bodies of the king and queen. They appear to be placed outside the picture space in a position similar to that of the viewer, although some scholars have speculated that their image is a reflection from the painting Velázquez is shown working on.
</p><p><i>Las Meninas</i> has long been recognised as one of the most important paintings in <a href="/wiki/Western_art_history" class="mw-redirect" title="Western art history">Western art history</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> painter <a href="/wiki/Luca_Giordano" title="Luca Giordano">Luca Giordano</a> said that it represents the "theology of painting" and in 1827 the president of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Arts" title="Royal Academy of Arts">Royal Academy of Arts</a> <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Lawrence" title="Thomas Lawrence">Sir Thomas Lawrence</a> described the work in a letter to his successor <a href="/wiki/David_Wilkie_(artist)" title="David Wilkie (artist)">David Wilkie</a> as "the true philosophy of the art".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> More recently, it has been described as "Velázquez's supreme achievement, a highly self-conscious, calculated demonstration of what painting could achieve, and perhaps the most searching comment ever made on the possibilities of the easel painting".<sup id="cite_ref-HF447_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HF447-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Las_Meninas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Court_of_Philip_IV">Court of Philip IV</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Las_Meninas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Court of Philip IV">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Margarita_Teresa_of_Spain_Mourningdress.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Margarita_Teresa_of_Spain_Mourningdress.jpg/170px-Margarita_Teresa_of_Spain_Mourningdress.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="246" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Margarita_Teresa_of_Spain_Mourningdress.jpg/255px-Margarita_Teresa_of_Spain_Mourningdress.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Margarita_Teresa_of_Spain_Mourningdress.jpg/340px-Margarita_Teresa_of_Spain_Mourningdress.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1875" data-file-height="2717" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Margarita_Teresa_of_Spain_Mourningdress.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Margarita_Teresa_of_Spain" class="mw-redirect" title="Margarita Teresa of Spain">Infanta Margaret Theresa</a> (1651–73), in mourning dress for her father in 1666, by <a href="/wiki/Juan_Bautista_Mart%C3%ADnez_del_Mazo" title="Juan Bautista Martínez del Mazo">Juan del Mazo</a>. The background figures include her young brother <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_Spain" title="Charles II of Spain">Charles&#160;II</a> and the dwarf Maribarbola, also in <i>Las Meninas</i>. She left Spain for her marriage in Vienna the same year.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>In 17th-century Spain, painters rarely enjoyed high social status. Painting was regarded as a craft, not an art such as poetry or music.<sup id="cite_ref-Dambe_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dambe-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Nonetheless, Velázquez worked his way up through the ranks of the court of <a href="/wiki/Philip_IV_of_Spain" title="Philip IV of Spain">Philip&#160;IV</a>, and in February 1651 was appointed <a href="/wiki/Chamberlain_(office)" title="Chamberlain (office)">palace chamberlain</a> (<i>aposentador mayor del palacio</i>). The post brought him status and material reward, but its duties made heavy demands on his time. During the remaining eight years of his life, he painted only a few works, mostly portraits of the royal family.<sup id="cite_ref-Carr46_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carr46-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> When he painted <i>Las Meninas</i>, he had been with the royal household for 33 years.
</p><p>Philip&#160;IV's first wife, <a href="/wiki/Elisabeth_of_France_(1602%E2%80%931644)" title="Elisabeth of France (1602–1644)">Elizabeth of France</a>, died in 1644; and their only son, <a href="/wiki/Balthasar_Charles,_Prince_of_Asturias" title="Balthasar Charles, Prince of Asturias">Balthasar Charles</a>, died two years later. Lacking an heir, Philip married <a href="/wiki/Mariana_of_Austria" title="Mariana of Austria">Mariana of Austria</a> in 1649,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and Margaret Theresa (1651–1673) was their first child, and their only one at the time of the painting. Subsequently, she had a short-lived brother <a href="/wiki/Philip_Prospero,_Prince_of_Asturias" title="Philip Prospero, Prince of Asturias">Philip Prospero</a> (1657–1661), and then <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_Spain" title="Charles II of Spain">Charles</a> (1661–1700) arrived, who succeeded to the throne as Charles&#160;II at the age of three. Velázquez painted portraits of Mariana and her children,<sup id="cite_ref-Carr46_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carr46-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and although Philip himself resisted being portrayed in his old age he did allow Velázquez to include him in <i>Las Meninas</i>. In the early 1650s he gave Velázquez the Pieza Principal ("main room") of the late Balthasar Charles's living quarters, by then serving as the palace museum, to use as his studio. It is here that <i>Las Meninas</i> is set. Philip had his own chair in the studio and would often sit and watch Velázquez at work. Although constrained by rigid etiquette, the art-loving king seems to have had a close relationship with the painter. After Velázquez's death, Philip wrote "I am crushed" in the margin of a memorandum on the choice of his successor.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the 1640s and 1650s, Velázquez served as both court painter and <a href="/wiki/Curator" title="Curator">curator</a> of Philip&#160;IV's expanding collection of European art. He seems to have been given an unusual degree of freedom in the role. He supervised the decoration and interior design of the rooms holding the most valued paintings, adding mirrors, statues and tapestries. He was also responsible for the sourcing, attribution, hanging and inventory of many of the Spanish king's paintings. By the early 1650s, Velázquez was widely respected in Spain as a connoisseur. Much of the collection of the Prado today—including works by <a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raphael" title="Raphael">Raphael</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Paul_Rubens" title="Peter Paul Rubens">Rubens</a>—were acquired and assembled under Velázquez's curatorship.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Provenance_and_condition">Provenance and condition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Las_Meninas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Provenance and condition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The painting was referred to in the earliest inventories as <i>La Familia</i> ("The Family").<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> A detailed description of <i>Las Meninas</i>, which provides the identification of several of the figures, was published by <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Palomino" title="Antonio Palomino">Antonio Palomino</a> ("the <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Vasari" title="Giorgio Vasari">Giorgio Vasari</a> of the Spanish Golden Age") in 1724.<sup id="cite_ref-K225_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-K225-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Examination under <a href="/wiki/Infrared#Art_history" title="Infrared">infrared</a> light reveals minor <a href="/wiki/Pentimento" title="Pentimento">pentimenti</a>, that is, there are traces of earlier working that the artist himself later altered. For example, at first Velázquez's own head inclined to his right rather than his left.<sup id="cite_ref-LR214_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LR214-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The painting has been cut down on both the left and right sides.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> It was damaged in the fire that destroyed the Alcázar in 1734, and was restored by court painter <a href="/wiki/Juan_Garc%C3%ADa_de_Miranda" title="Juan García de Miranda">Juan García de Miranda</a> (1677–1749). The left cheek of the Infanta was almost completely repainted to compensate for a substantial loss of pigment.<sup id="cite_ref-LR306_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LR306-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> After its rescue from the fire, the painting was inventoried as part of the royal collection in 1747–48, and the Infanta was misidentified as <a href="/wiki/Maria_Theresa_of_Spain" title="Maria Theresa of Spain">Maria Theresa</a>, Margaret Theresa's older half-sister, an error that was repeated when the painting was inventoried at the new Madrid Royal Palace in 1772.<sup id="cite_ref-LR310-311_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LR310-311-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> A 1794 inventory reverted to a version of the earlier title, <i>The Family of Philip&#160;IV</i>, which was repeated in the  records of 1814. The painting entered the collection of the Museo del Prado on its foundation in 1819.<sup id="cite_ref-LR306_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LR306-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In 1843, the Prado catalogue listed the work for the first time as <i>Las Meninas</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-LR310-311_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LR310-311-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In recent years, the picture has suffered a loss of texture and hue. Due to exposure to pollution and crowds of visitors, the once-vivid contrasts between blue and white pigments in the costumes of the <i>meninas</i> have faded.<sup id="cite_ref-LR306_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LR306-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> It was last cleaned in 1984 under the supervision of the American conservator John Brealey, to remove a "yellow veil" of dust that had gathered since the previous restoration in the 19th century. The cleaning provoked, according to the art historian <a href="/wiki/Federico_Zeri" title="Federico Zeri">Federico Zeri</a>, "furious protests, not because the picture had been damaged in any way, but because it looked different".<sup id="cite_ref-Burl_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burl-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> However, in the opinion of López-Rey, the "restoration was impeccable".<sup id="cite_ref-LR310-311_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LR310-311-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Due to its size, importance, and value, the painting is not lent out for exhibition.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;b&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Painting_materials">Painting materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Las_Meninas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Painting materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A thorough technical investigation including a pigment analysis of <i>Las Meninas</i> was conducted around 1981 in Museo Prado.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The analysis revealed the usual pigments of the baroque period frequently used by Velázquez in his other paintings. The main pigments used for this painting were <a href="/wiki/Lead_white" class="mw-redirect" title="Lead white">lead white</a>, <a href="/wiki/Azurite" title="Azurite">azurite</a> (for the skirt of the kneeling menina), <a href="/wiki/Vermilion" title="Vermilion">vermilion</a> and red lake, <a href="/wiki/Ochres" class="mw-redirect" title="Ochres">ochres</a> and carbon blacks.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Las_Meninas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subject_matter">Subject matter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Las_Meninas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Subject matter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Velazquez-Meninas-key3.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Velazquez-Meninas-key3.jpg/300px-Velazquez-Meninas-key3.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="209" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Velazquez-Meninas-key3.jpg/450px-Velazquez-Meninas-key3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Velazquez-Meninas-key3.jpg/600px-Velazquez-Meninas-key3.jpg 2x" data-file-width="767" data-file-height="534" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Velazquez-Meninas-key3.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Key to the people represented: see text</div></div></div>
<p><i>Las Meninas</i> is set in Velázquez's studio in Philip&#160;IV's <a href="/wiki/Alc%C3%A1zar" title="Alcázar">Alcázar palace</a> in Madrid.<sup id="cite_ref-Alpers185_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alpers185-25">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The high-ceilinged room is presented, in the words of Silvio Gaggi, as "a simple box that could be divided into a perspective grid with a single vanishing point".<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In the centre of the foreground stands the <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Theresa_of_Spain" title="Margaret Theresa of Spain">Infanta Margaret Theresa</a>&#160;(1). The five-year-old infanta, who later married <a href="/wiki/Leopold_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Leopold I, Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor Leopold&#160;I</a>, was at this point Philip and Mariana's only surviving child.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;c&#93;</a></sup> She is attended by two <a href="/wiki/Lady-in-waiting" title="Lady-in-waiting">ladies-in-waiting</a>, or <i>meninas</i>: doña Isabel de Velasco&#160;(2), who is poised to curtsy to the princess, and doña <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mar%C3%ADa_Agustina_Sarmiento_de_Sotomayor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="María Agustina Sarmiento de Sotomayor (page does not exist)">María Agustina Sarmiento de Sotomayor</a>&#160;(3), who kneels before Margaret Theresa, offering her a drink from a red cup, or <i>búcaro</i>, that she holds on a golden tray.<sup id="cite_ref-white143_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-white143-28">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> To the right of the Infanta are two dwarfs: the <a href="/wiki/Achondroplasia" title="Achondroplasia">achondroplastic</a> German, Maribarbola (4)<sup id="cite_ref-white143_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-white143-28">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Maria_Barbola" class="mw-redirect" title="Maria Barbola">Maria Barbola</a>), and the Italian, Nicolas Pertusato&#160;(5), who playfully tries to rouse a sleepy <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Mastiff" title="Spanish Mastiff">mastiff</a> with his foot. The dog is thought to be descended from two mastiffs from Lyme Hall in Cheshire, given to Phillip III in 1604 by James I of England.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Behind them stands doña Marcela de Ulloa&#160;(6), the princess's chaperone, dressed in mourning and talking to an unidentified bodyguard (or <i>guardadamas</i>)&#160;(7).<sup id="cite_ref-white143_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-white143-28">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Meninas_detail_Don_Jos%C3%A9_Nieto_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Meninas_detail_Don_Jos%C3%A9_Nieto_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg/170px-Meninas_detail_Don_Jos%C3%A9_Nieto_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="290" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fb/Meninas_detail_Don_Jos%C3%A9_Nieto_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="229" data-file-height="391" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Meninas_detail_Don_Jos%C3%A9_Nieto_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Detail showing Don <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Nieto_Vel%C3%A1zquez" title="José Nieto Velázquez">José Nieto Velázquez</a> at the door in the background of the painting</div></div></div>
<p>To the rear and at right stands Don <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Nieto_Vel%C3%A1zquez" title="José Nieto Velázquez">José Nieto Velázquez</a>&#160;(8)—the queen's chamberlain during the 1650s, and head of the royal <a href="/wiki/Tapestry" title="Tapestry">tapestry</a> works—who may have been a relative of the artist. Nieto is shown pausing, with his right knee bent and his feet on different steps. As the art critic Harriet Stone observes, it is uncertain whether he is "coming or going".<sup id="cite_ref-stone35_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stone35-30">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> He is rendered in silhouette and appears to hold open a curtain on a short flight of stairs, with an unclear wall or space behind. Both this backlight and the open doorway reveal space behind: in the words of the art historian <a href="/wiki/Analisa_Leppanen" title="Analisa Leppanen">Analisa Leppanen</a>, they lure "our eyes inescapably into the depths".<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The royal couple's reflection pushes in the opposite direction, forward into the picture space. The <a href="/wiki/Vanishing_point" title="Vanishing point">vanishing point</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Perspective_(graphical)" title="Perspective (graphical)">perspective</a> is in the doorway, as can be shown by extending the line of the meeting of wall and ceiling on the right. Nieto is seen only by the king and queen, who share the viewer's point of view, and not by the figures in the foreground. In the footnotes of Joel Snyder's article, the author recognizes that Nieto is the queen's attendant and was required to be at hand to open and close doors for her. Snyder suggests that Nieto appears in the doorway so that the king and queen might depart. In the context of the painting, Snyder argues that the scene is the end of the royal couple's sitting for Velázquez and they are preparing to exit, explaining that is "why the menina to the right of the Infanta begins to curtsy".<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Velázquez himself&#160;(9) is pictured to the left of the scene, looking outward past a large canvas supported by an <a href="/wiki/Easel" title="Easel">easel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Carr47_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carr47-33">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> On his chest is the red cross of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Santiago" title="Order of Santiago">Order of Santiago</a>, which he did not receive until 1659, three years after the painting was completed. According to Palomino, Philip ordered this to be added after Velázquez's death, "and some say that his Majesty himself painted it".<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> From the painter's belt hang the symbolic keys of his court offices.<sup id="cite_ref-HF449_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HF449-35">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A mirror on the back wall reflects the upper bodies and heads of two figures identified from other paintings, and by Palomino, as King Philip&#160;IV&#160;(10) and Queen Mariana&#160;(11). The most common assumption is that the reflection shows the couple in the pose they are holding for Velázquez as he paints them, while their daughter watches; and that the painting therefore shows their view of the scene.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Las_Meninas_mirror_detail.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Las_Meninas_mirror_detail.jpg/220px-Las_Meninas_mirror_detail.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="270" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Las_Meninas_mirror_detail.jpg/330px-Las_Meninas_mirror_detail.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Las_Meninas_mirror_detail.jpg/440px-Las_Meninas_mirror_detail.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2655" data-file-height="3253" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Las_Meninas_mirror_detail.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Detail of the mirror hung on the back wall, showing the reflected images of <a href="/wiki/Philip_IV_of_Spain" title="Philip IV of Spain">Philip&#160;IV</a> and his wife, <a href="/wiki/Mariana_of_Austria" title="Mariana of Austria">Mariana of Austria</a></div></div></div>
<p>Of the nine figures depicted, five are looking directly out at the royal couple or the viewer. Their glances, along with the king and queen's reflection, affirm the royal couple's presence outside the painted space.<sup id="cite_ref-stone35_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stone35-30">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Alternatively, art historians <a href="/wiki/H._W._Janson" title="H. W. Janson">H. W. Janson</a> and Joel Snyder suggest that the image of the king and queen is a reflection from Velázquez's canvas, the front of which is obscured from the viewer.<sup id="cite_ref-Janson_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Janson-36">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Other writers say the canvas Velázquez is painting is unusually large for a portrait by Velázquez, and is about the same size as <i>Las Meninas</i>. <i>Las Meninas</i> contains the only known double portrait of the royal couple painted by Velázquez.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The point of view of the picture is approximately that of the royal couple, though this has been widely debated. Many critics suppose that the scene is viewed by the king and queen as they pose for a double portrait, while the Infanta and her companions are present only to make the process more enjoyable.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Gombrich" title="Ernst Gombrich">Ernst Gombrich</a> suggested that the picture might have been the sitters' idea:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>"Perhaps the princess was brought into the royal presence to relieve the boredom of the sitting and the King or the Queen remarked to Velazquez that here was a worthy subject for his brush. The words spoken by the sovereign are always treated as a command and so we may owe this masterpiece to a passing wish which only Velazquez was able to turn into reality." <sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote><p> No single theory, however, has found universal agreement.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Leo_Steinberg" title="Leo Steinberg">Leo Steinberg</a> suggests that the King and Queen are to the left of the viewer and the reflection in the mirror is that of the canvas, a portrait of the king and queen.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Others speculate that Velázquez represents himself painting the Infanta Margaret Theresa.
</p><p>The back wall of the room, which is in shadow, is hung with rows of paintings, including one of a series of scenes from <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Metamorphoses" title="Metamorphoses">Metamorphoses</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Paul_Rubens" title="Peter Paul Rubens">Peter Paul Rubens</a>, and copies, by Velázquez's son-in-law and principal assistant <a href="/wiki/Juan_Bautista_Mart%C3%ADnez_del_Mazo" title="Juan Bautista Martínez del Mazo">Juan del Mazo</a>, of works by <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Jordaens" title="Jacob Jordaens">Jacob Jordaens</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Alpers185_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alpers185-25">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The paintings are shown in the exact positions recorded in an inventory taken around this time.<sup id="cite_ref-Carr47_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carr47-33">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The wall to the right is hung with a grid of eight smaller paintings, visible mainly as frames owing to their angle from the viewer.<sup id="cite_ref-stone35_30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stone35-30">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> They can be identified from the inventory as more Mazo copies of paintings from the Rubens Ovid series, though only two of the subjects can be seen.<sup id="cite_ref-Alpers185_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alpers185-25">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The paintings on the back wall are recognized as representing <a href="/wiki/Pallas_and_Arachne" title="Pallas and Arachne">Minerva Punishing Arachne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Apollo_as_Victor_over_Pan" title="Apollo as Victor over Pan">Apollo's Victory Over Marsyas</a>. Both stories involve Minerva, the Goddess of Wisdom and patron of the arts. These two legends are both stories of mortals challenging gods and the dreadful consequences. One scholar points out that the legend dealing with two women, Minerva and Arachne, is on the same side of the mirror as the queen's reflection while the male legend is on the side of the king.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Las_Meninas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The painted surface is divided into quarters horizontally and sevenths vertically; this grid is used to organise the elaborate grouping of characters, and was a common device at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-Clarke_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clarke-44">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Velázquez presents nine figures—eleven if the king and queen's reflected images are included—yet they occupy only the lower half of the canvas.<sup id="cite_ref-white140_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-white140-45">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to López-Rey, the painting has three focal points: the Infanta Margaret Theresa, the self-portrait and the half-length reflected images of King Philip&#160;IV and Queen Mariana. In 1960, the art historian <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Clark" title="Kenneth Clark">Kenneth Clark</a> made the point that the success of the composition is a result first and foremost of the accurate handling of light and shade:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Each focal point involves us in a new set of relations; and to paint a complex group like the Meninas, the painter must carry in his head a single consistent scale of relations which he can apply throughout. He may use all kinds of devices to help him do this—perspective is one of them—but ultimately the truth about a complete visual impression depends on one thing, truth of tone. Drawing may be summary, colours drab, but if the relations of tone are true, the picture will hold.<sup id="cite_ref-Clarke_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clarke-44">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>However, the focal point of the painting is widely debated. Leo Steinberg argues that the orthogonals in the work are intentionally disguised so that the picture's focal center shifts. Similar to Lopez-Rey, he describes three foci. The man in the doorway, however, is the vanishing point. More specifically, the crook of his arm is where the orthogonals of the windows and lights of the ceiling meet.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Depth and dimension are rendered by the use of linear perspective, by the overlapping of the layers of shapes, and in particular, as stated by Clark, through the use of tone. This compositional element operates within the picture in a number of ways. First, there is the appearance of natural light within the painted room and beyond it. The pictorial space in the midground and foreground is lit from two sources: by thin shafts of light from the open door, and by broad streams coming through the window to the right.<sup id="cite_ref-Carr47_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carr47-33">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The 20th-century <a href="/wiki/French_philosophy" title="French philosophy">French philosopher</a> and cultural critic <a href="/wiki/Michel_Foucault" title="Michel Foucault">Michel Foucault</a> observed that the light from the window illuminates both the studio foreground and the unrepresented area in front of it, in which the king, the queen, and the viewer are presumed to be situated.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> For <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Ortega_y_Gasset" title="José Ortega y Gasset">José Ortega y Gasset</a>, light divides the scene into three distinct parts, with foreground and background planes strongly illuminated, between which a darkened intermediate space includes silhouetted figures.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Velázquez uses this light not only to add volume and definition to each form but also to define the focal points of the painting. As the light streams in from the right it brightly glints on the braid and golden hair of the female dwarf, who is nearest the light source. But because her face is turned from the light, and in shadow, its tonality does not make it a point of particular interest. Similarly, the light glances obliquely on the cheek of the lady-in-waiting near her, but not on her facial features. Much of her lightly coloured dress is dimmed by shadow. The Infanta, however, stands in full illumination, and with her face turned towards the light source, even though her gaze is not. Her face is framed by the pale gossamer of her hair, setting her apart from everything else in the picture. The light models the volumetric geometry of her form, defining the conic nature of a small torso bound rigidly into a corset and stiffened bodice, and the panniered skirt extending around her like an oval candy-box, casting its own deep shadow which, by its sharp contrast with the bright brocade, both emphasises and locates the small figure as the main point of attention.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Meninas_detail.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Meninas_detail.jpg/220px-Meninas_detail.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="284" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Meninas_detail.jpg/330px-Meninas_detail.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Meninas_detail.jpg/440px-Meninas_detail.jpg 2x" data-file-width="900" data-file-height="1162" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Meninas_detail.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Detail of doña <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mar%C3%ADa_Agustina_Sarmiento_de_Sotomayor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="María Agustina Sarmiento de Sotomayor (page does not exist)">María de Sotomayor</a>, showing Velázquez's free brushwork on her dress</div></div></div>
<p>Velázquez further emphasises the Infanta by his positioning and lighting of her maids of honour, whom he sets opposing one another: to left and right, before and behind the Infanta. The maid to the left faces the light, her brightly lit profile and sleeve creating a diagonal. Her opposite number creates a broader but less defined reflection of her attention, making a diagonal space between them, in which their charge stands protected.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A further internal diagonal passes through the space occupied by the Infanta. There is a similar connection between the female dwarf and the figure of Velázquez himself, both of whom look towards the viewer from similar angles, creating a visual tension. The face of Velázquez is dimly lit by light that is reflected, rather than direct. For this reason his features, though not as sharply defined, are more visible than those of the dwarf who is much nearer the light source. This appearance of a total face, full-on to the viewer, draws the attention, and its importance is marked, tonally, by the contrasting frame of dark hair, the light on the hand and brush, and the skilfully placed triangle of light on the artist's sleeve, pointing directly to the face.
</p><p>From the figure of the artist, the viewer's eye leaps again diagonally into the pictorial space. Another man stands, echoing and opposing the form of the artist, outside rather than inside, made clearly defined and yet barely identifiable by the light and shade. The positioning of such an area of strong tonal contrast right at the rear of the pictorial space is a daring compositional tactic. The shapes of bright light are similar to the irregular light shapes of the foreground Maid of Honour, but the sharply defined door-frame repeats the border of the mirror.
</p><p>The mirror is a perfectly defined unbroken pale rectangle within a broad black rectangle. A clear geometric shape, like a lit face, draws the attention of the viewer more than a broken geometric shape such as the door, or a shadowed or oblique face such as that of the dwarf in the foreground or that of the man in the background. The viewer cannot distinguish the features of the king and queen, but in the opalescent sheen of the mirror's surface, the glowing ovals are plainly turned directly to the viewer. <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Miller" title="Jonathan Miller">Jonathan Miller</a> points out that apart from "adding suggestive gleams at the bevelled edges, the most important way the mirror betrays its identity is by disclosing imagery whose brightness is so inconsistent with the dimness of the surrounding wall that it can only have been borrowed, by reflection, from the strongly illuminated figures of the King and Queen".<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As the maids of honour are reflected in each other, so too do the king and queen have their doubles within the painting, in the dimly lit forms of the chaperone and guard, the two who serve and care for their daughter. The positioning of these figures sets up a pattern, one man, a couple, one man, a couple, and while the outer figures are nearer the viewer than the others, they all occupy the same horizontal band on the picture's surface.
</p><p>Adding to the inner complexities of the picture and creating further visual interactions is the male dwarf in the foreground, whose raised hand echoes the gesture of the figure in the background, while his playful demeanour, and distraction from the central action, are in complete contrast with it. The informality of his pose, his shadowed profile, and his dark hair all serve to make him a mirror image to the kneeling attendant of the Infanta. However, the painter has set him forward of the light streaming through the window, and so minimised the contrast of tone on this foreground figure.
</p><p>Despite certain spatial ambiguities this is the painter's most thoroughly rendered architectural space, and the only one in which a ceiling is shown. According to López-Rey, in no other composition did Velázquez so dramatically lead the eye to areas beyond the viewer's sight: both the canvas he is seen painting, and the space beyond the frame where the king and queen stand can only be imagined.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> The bareness of the dark ceiling, the back of Velázquez's canvas, and the strict geometry of framed paintings contrast with the animated, brilliantly lit and sumptuously painted foreground entourage.<sup id="cite_ref-LR216_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LR216-52">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Stone writes:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>We cannot take in all the figures of the painting in one glance. Not only do the life-size proportions of the painting preclude such an appreciation, but also the fact that the heads of the figures are turned in different directions means that our gaze is deflected. The painting communicates through images which, in order to be understood, must thus be considered in sequence, one after the other, in the context of a history that is still unfolding. It is a history that is still unframed, even in this painting composed of frames within frames.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>According to Kahr, the composition could have been influenced by the traditional Dutch Gallery Pictures such as those by <a href="/wiki/Frans_Francken_the_Younger" title="Frans Francken the Younger">Frans Francken the Younger</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Willem_van_Haecht" title="Willem van Haecht">Willem van Haecht</a>, or <a href="/wiki/David_Teniers_the_Younger" title="David Teniers the Younger">David Teniers the Younger</a>. Teniers' work was owned by Philip IV and would have been known by Velázquez. Like <i>Las Meninas</i>, they often depict formal visits by important collectors or rulers, a common occurrence,  and "show a room with a series of windows dominating one side wall and paintings hung between the windows as well as on the other walls". Gallery Portraits were also used to glorify the artist as well as royalty or members of the higher classes, as may have been Velázquez's intention with this work.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mirror_and_reflection">Mirror and reflection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Las_Meninas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mirror and reflection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Arnolfini_Portrait,_d%C3%A9tail_(2).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/The_Arnolfini_Portrait%2C_d%C3%A9tail_%282%29.jpg/220px-The_Arnolfini_Portrait%2C_d%C3%A9tail_%282%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="183" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/The_Arnolfini_Portrait%2C_d%C3%A9tail_%282%29.jpg/330px-The_Arnolfini_Portrait%2C_d%C3%A9tail_%282%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/The_Arnolfini_Portrait%2C_d%C3%A9tail_%282%29.jpg/440px-The_Arnolfini_Portrait%2C_d%C3%A9tail_%282%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="833" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Arnolfini_Portrait,_d%C3%A9tail_(2).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Detail of the mirror in van Eyck's <i><a href="/wiki/Arnolfini_Portrait" title="Arnolfini Portrait">Arnolfini Portrait</a></i>. Van Eyck's painting shows the pictorial space from "behind", and two further figures in front of the picture space, like those in the reflection in the mirror in <i>Las Meninas</i>.</div></div></div>
<p>The spatial structure and positioning of the mirror's reflection are such that Philip&#160;IV and Mariana appear to be standing on the viewer's side of the pictorial space, facing the Infanta and her entourage. According to Janson, not only is the gathering of figures in the foreground for Philip and Mariana's benefit, but the painter's attention is concentrated on the couple, as he appears to be working on their portrait.<sup id="cite_ref-Janson_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Janson-36">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Although they can only be seen in the mirror reflection, their distant image occupies a central position in the canvas, in terms of social hierarchy as well as composition. As spectators, our position in relation to the painting is uncertain. It has been debated whether the ruling couple are standing beside the viewer or have replaced the viewer, who sees the scene through their eyes. Lending weight to the latter idea are the gazes of three of the figures—Velázquez, the Infanta, and Maribarbola—who appear to be looking directly at the viewer.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The mirror on the back wall indicates what is not there: the king and queen, and in the words of Harriet Stone, "the generations of spectators who assume the couple's place before the painting".<sup id="cite_ref-stone35_30-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stone35-30">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Writing in 1980, the critics Snyder and Cohn observed:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Velázquez wanted the mirror to depend upon the useable (sic) painted canvas for its image. Why should he want that? The luminous image in the mirror appears to reflect the king and queen themselves, but it does more than just this: the mirror outdoes nature. The mirror image is only a reflection. A reflection of what? Of the real thing—of the art of Velázquez. In the presence of his divinely ordained monarchs&#160;... Velázquez exults in his artistry and counsels Philip and Maria not to look for the revelation of their image in the natural reflection of a looking glass but rather in the penetrating vision of their master painter. In the presence of Velázquez, a mirror image is a poor imitation of the real.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Van_Eyck_-_Arnolfini_Portrait.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Van_Eyck_-_Arnolfini_Portrait.jpg/220px-Van_Eyck_-_Arnolfini_Portrait.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="301" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Van_Eyck_-_Arnolfini_Portrait.jpg/330px-Van_Eyck_-_Arnolfini_Portrait.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Van_Eyck_-_Arnolfini_Portrait.jpg/440px-Van_Eyck_-_Arnolfini_Portrait.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3087" data-file-height="4226" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Van_Eyck_-_Arnolfini_Portrait.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In the <i><a href="/wiki/Arnolfini_Portrait" title="Arnolfini Portrait">Arnolfini Portrait</a></i> (1434), <a href="/wiki/Jan_van_Eyck" title="Jan van Eyck">Jan van Eyck</a> uses an image reflected in a mirror in a manner similar to Velázquez in <i>Las Meninas</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-LR214_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LR214-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>In <i>Las Meninas</i>, the king and queen are supposedly "outside" the painting, yet their reflection in the back wall mirror also places them "inside" the pictorial space.<sup id="cite_ref-Joyce_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Joyce-57">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Snyder proposes it is "a mirror of majesty" or an allusion to the <a href="/wiki/Mirror_for_princes" class="mw-redirect" title="Mirror for princes">mirror for princes</a>. While it is a literal reflection of the king and queen, Snyder writes "it is the image of exemplary monarchs, a reflection of ideal character" <sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Later he focuses his attention on the princess, writing that Velázquez's portrait is "the painted equivalent of a manual for the education of the princess—a mirror of the princess". <sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The painting is likely to have been influenced by <a href="/wiki/Jan_van_Eyck" title="Jan van Eyck">Jan van Eyck</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Arnolfini_Portrait" title="Arnolfini Portrait">Arnolfini Portrait</a></i>, of 1434. At the time, van Eyck's painting hung in Philip's palace, and would have been familiar to Velázquez.<sup id="cite_ref-LR214_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LR214-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> The <i>Arnolfini Portrait</i> also has a mirror positioned at the back of the pictorial space, reflecting two figures who would have the same angle of vision as does the viewer of Velázquez's painting; they are too small to identify, but it has been speculated that one may be intended as the artist himself, though he is not shown in the act of painting. According to Lucien Dällenbach:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The mirror [in <i>Las Meninas</i>] faces the observer as in Van Eyck's painting. But here the procedure is more realistic to the degree that the "rearview" mirror in which the royal couple appears is no longer convex but flat. Whereas the reflection in the Flemish painting recomposed objects and characters within a space that is condensed and deformed by the curve of the mirror, that of Velázquez refuses to play with the laws of perspective: it projects onto the canvas the perfect double of the king and queen positioned in front of the painting. Moreover, in showing the figures whom the painter observes, and also, through the mediation of the mirror, the figures who are observing him, the painter achieves a reciprocity of gazes that makes the interior oscillate with the exterior and which causes the image to "emerge from its frame" at the same time that it invites the visitors to enter the painting.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>Jonathan Miller asks: "What are we to make of the blurred features of the royal couple? It is unlikely that it has anything to do with the optical imperfection of the mirror, which would, in reality, have displayed a focused image of the King and Queen". He notes that "in addition to the <i>represented</i> mirror, he teasingly implies an unrepresented one, without which it is difficult to imagine how he could have shown himself painting the picture we now see".<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretation">Interpretation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Las_Meninas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Interpretation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The elusiveness of <i>Las Meninas</i>, according to Dawson Carr, "suggests that art, and life, are an illusion".<sup id="cite_ref-Carr_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carr-63">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>  The relationship between illusion and reality were central concerns in Spanish culture during the 17th century, figuring largely in <i><a href="/wiki/Don_Quixote" title="Don Quixote">Don Quixote</a></i>, the best-known work of <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Baroque_literature" title="Spanish Baroque literature">Spanish Baroque literature</a>. In this respect, <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Calder%C3%B3n_de_la_Barca" title="Pedro Calderón de la Barca">Calderón de la Barca's</a> play <i>Life is a Dream</i> is commonly seen as the literary equivalent of Velázquez's painting:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><div class="poem">
<p>What is a life? A frenzy. What is life?<br />
A shadow, an illusion, and a sham.<br />
The greatest good is small; all life, it seems<br />
Is just a dream, and even dreams are dreams.<sup id="cite_ref-Carr_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carr-63">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</div>
</blockquote>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Velazquez_figurando.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Velazquez_figurando.jpg/220px-Velazquez_figurando.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="251" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Velazquez_figurando.jpg/330px-Velazquez_figurando.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Velazquez_figurando.jpg/440px-Velazquez_figurando.jpg 2x" data-file-width="729" data-file-height="832" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Velazquez_figurando.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Detail showing the red cross of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Santiago" title="Order of Santiago">Order of Santiago</a> painted on the breast of Velázquez. Presumably this detail was added at a later date, as the painter was admitted to the order by the king's decree on November 28, 1659.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Jon Manchip White notes that the painting can be seen as a résumé of the whole of Velázquez's life and career, as well as a summary of his art to that point. He placed his only confirmed <a href="/wiki/Self-portrait" title="Self-portrait">self-portrait</a> in a room in the royal palace surrounded by an assembly of royalty, <a href="/wiki/Courtier" title="Courtier">courtiers</a>, and fine objects that represent his life at <a href="/wiki/Court_(royal)" class="mw-redirect" title="Court (royal)">court</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-white143_28-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-white143-28">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The art historian <a href="/wiki/Svetlana_Alpers" title="Svetlana Alpers">Svetlana Alpers</a> suggests that, by portraying the artist at work in the company of royalty and nobility, Velázquez was claiming high status for both the artist and his art,<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> and in particular to propose that painting is a <a href="/wiki/Liberal_arts" class="mw-redirect" title="Liberal arts">liberal</a> rather than a mechanical art. This distinction was a point of controversy at the time. It would have been significant to Velázquez, since the rules of the Order of Santiago excluded those whose occupations were mechanical.<sup id="cite_ref-HF447_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HF447-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Kahr asserts that this was the best way for Velázquez to show that he was "neither a craftsman or a tradesman, but an official of the court". Furthermore, this was a way to prove himself worthy of acceptance by the royal family.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Michel Foucault devoted the opening chapter of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Order_of_Things" title="The Order of Things">The Order of Things</a></i> (1966) to an analysis of <i>Las Meninas</i>. Foucault describes the painting in meticulous detail, but in a language that is "neither prescribed by, nor filtered through the various texts of art-historical investigation".<sup id="cite_ref-Gresle_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gresle-67">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> Foucault viewed the painting without regard to the subject matter, nor to the artist's biography, technical ability, sources and influences, social context, or relationship with his patrons. Instead he analyzes its conscious artifice, highlighting the complex network of visual relationships between painter, subject-model, and viewer:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>We are looking at a picture in which the painter is in turn looking out at us. A mere confrontation, eyes catching one another's glance, direct looks superimposing themselves upon one another as they cross. And yet this slender line of reciprocal visibility embraces a whole complex network of uncertainties, exchanges, and feints. The painter is turning his eyes towards us only in so far as we happen to occupy the same position as his subject.<sup id="cite_ref-Gresle_67-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gresle-67">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>For Foucault, <i>Las Meninas</i> illustrates the first signs of a new <i><a href="/wiki/Episteme" title="Episteme">episteme</a></i>, or way of thinking. It represents a midpoint between what he sees as the two "great discontinuities" in European thought, the classical and the modern: "Perhaps there exists, in this painting by Velázquez, the representation as it were of Classical representation, and the definition of the space it opens up to us&#160;... representation, freed finally from the relation that was impeding it, can offer itself as representation in its pure form."<sup id="cite_ref-Gresle_67-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gresle-67">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Now he (the painter) can be seen, caught in a moment of stillness, at the neutral centre of his oscillation. His dark torso and bright face are half-way between the visible and the invisible: emerging from the canvas beyond our view, he moves into our gaze; but when, in a moment, he makes a step to the right, removing himself from our gaze, he will be standing exactly in front of the canvas he is painting; he will enter that region where his painting, neglected for an instant, will, for him, become visible once more, free of shadow and free of reticence. As though the painter could not at the same time be seen on the picture where he is represented and also see that upon which he is representing something."<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>In the conclusion of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Order_of_Things" title="The Order of Things">The Order of Things</a></i> Foucault explained why he undertook such a forensic analysis of <i>Las Meninas</i>:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>let us, if we may, look for the previously existing law of that interplay [i.e., the law of representation] in the painting of Las Meninas… In Classical thought, the personage for whom the representation exists, and who represents himself within it, recognizing himself therein as an image or reflection, he who ties together all the interlacing threads of the 'representation in the form of a picture or table'—he is never to be found in that table himself. Before the end of the eighteenth century, man did not exist—any more than the potency of life, the fecundity of labour, or the historical density of language. He is a quite recent creature, which the demiurge of knowledge fabricated with its own hands less than two hundred years ago: but he has grown old so quickly that it has been only too easy to imagine that he had been waiting for thousands of years in the darkness for that moment of illumination in which he would finally be known.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>Foucault's analysis of <i>Las Meninas</i>, although on one level a contribution to art history, is more about <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemology</a>, specifically the 'cognitive status of the modern human sciences'.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Las_Meninas_as_culmination_of_themes_in_Vel.C3.A1zquez"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Las_Meninas_as_culmination_of_themes_in_Velázquez"><i>Las Meninas</i> as culmination of themes in Velázquez</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Las_Meninas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Las Meninas as culmination of themes in Velázquez">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cristo_en_casa_de_Marta_y_Mar%C3%ADa,_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Cristo_en_casa_de_Marta_y_Mar%C3%ADa%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg/220px-Cristo_en_casa_de_Marta_y_Mar%C3%ADa%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="128" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Cristo_en_casa_de_Marta_y_Mar%C3%ADa%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg/330px-Cristo_en_casa_de_Marta_y_Mar%C3%ADa%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Cristo_en_casa_de_Marta_y_Mar%C3%ADa%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg/440px-Cristo_en_casa_de_Marta_y_Mar%C3%ADa%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg 2x" data-file-width="7290" data-file-height="4226" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cristo_en_casa_de_Marta_y_Mar%C3%ADa,_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diego Velázquez's <i><a href="/wiki/Christ_in_the_House_of_Martha_and_Mary_(Vel%C3%A1zquez)" title="Christ in the House of Martha and Mary (Velázquez)">Christ in the House of Martha and Mary</a></i>, 1618. The smaller image may be a view to another room, a picture on the wall, or a reflection in a mirror.</div></div></div>
<p>Many aspects of <i>Las Meninas</i> relate to earlier works by Velázquez in which he plays with conventions of representation. In the <i><a href="/wiki/Rokeby_Venus" title="Rokeby Venus">Rokeby Venus</a></i>—his only surviving nude—the face of the subject is visible, blurred beyond any realism, in a mirror. The angle of the mirror is such that although "often described as looking at herself, [she] is more disconcertingly looking at us".<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> In the early <i><a href="/wiki/Christ_in_the_House_of_Martha_and_Mary_(Vel%C3%A1zquez)" title="Christ in the House of Martha and Mary (Velázquez)">Christ in the House of Martha and Mary</a></i> of 1618,<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> Christ and his companions are seen only through a serving hatch to a room behind, according to the <a href="/wiki/National_Gallery" title="National Gallery">National Gallery</a> (London), who are clear that this is the intention, although before restoration many art historians regarded this scene as either a painting hanging on the wall in the main scene, or a reflection in a mirror, and the debate has continued.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> The dress worn in the two scenes also differs: the main scene is in contemporary dress, while the scene with Christ uses conventional iconographic biblical dress. This is also a feature of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Triumph_of_Bacchus" title="The Triumph of Bacchus">Los Borrachos</a></i> of 1629, where contemporary peasants consort with the god <a href="/wiki/Dionysus" title="Dionysus">Bacchus</a> and his companions, who have the conventional undress of mythology. In this, as in some of his early <i><a href="/wiki/Bodeg%C3%B3n" title="Bodegón">bodegones</a></i>, the figures look directly at the viewer as if seeking a reaction.
</p><p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Las_Hilanderas_(Vel%C3%A1zquez)" title="Las Hilanderas (Velázquez)">Las Hilanderas</a></i>, probably painted the year after <i>Las Meninas</i>, two different scenes from Ovid are shown: one in contemporary dress in the foreground, and the other partly in antique dress, played before a tapestry on the back wall of a room behind the first. According to the critic Sira Dambe, "aspects of representation and power are addressed in this painting in ways closely connected with their treatment in <i>Las Meninas</i>".<sup id="cite_ref-Dambe_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dambe-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In a series of portraits of the late 1630s and 1640s—all now in the Prado—Velázquez painted clowns and other members of the royal household posing as gods, heroes, and philosophers; the intention is certainly partly comic, at least for those in the know, but in a highly ambiguous way.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Velázquez's portraits of the royal family themselves had until then been straightforward, if often unflatteringly direct and highly complex in expression. On the other hand, his royal portraits, designed to be seen across vast palace rooms, feature more strongly than his other works the bravura handling for which he is famous: "Velázquez's handling of paint is exceptionally free, and as one approaches <i>Las Meninas</i> there is a point at which the figures suddenly dissolve into smears and blobs of paint. The long-handled brushes he used enabled him to stand back and judge the total effect."<sup id="cite_ref-HF449_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HF449-35">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:La_familia_de_Carlos_IV,_por_Francisco_de_Goya.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/La_familia_de_Carlos_IV%2C_por_Francisco_de_Goya.jpg/220px-La_familia_de_Carlos_IV%2C_por_Francisco_de_Goya.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="184" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/La_familia_de_Carlos_IV%2C_por_Francisco_de_Goya.jpg/330px-La_familia_de_Carlos_IV%2C_por_Francisco_de_Goya.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/La_familia_de_Carlos_IV%2C_por_Francisco_de_Goya.jpg/440px-La_familia_de_Carlos_IV%2C_por_Francisco_de_Goya.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1911" data-file-height="1595" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:La_familia_de_Carlos_IV,_por_Francisco_de_Goya.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Francisco_Goya" title="Francisco Goya">Francisco Goya</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Charles_IV_of_Spain_and_His_Family" title="Charles IV of Spain and His Family">Charles IV of Spain and His Family</a></i> references <i>Las Meninas</i>, but is less sympathetic towards its subjects than Velázquez's portrait.<sup id="cite_ref-G69_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G69-78">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Las_Meninas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1692, the <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Neapolitan</a> painter <a href="/wiki/Luca_Giordano" title="Luca Giordano">Luca Giordano</a>  became one of the few allowed to view paintings held in Philip&#160;IV's private apartments, and was greatly impressed by <i>Las Meninas</i>. Giordano described the work as the "theology of painting",<sup id="cite_ref-white140_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-white140-45">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> and was inspired to paint <i>A Homage to Velázquez</i> (<a href="/wiki/National_Gallery,_London" class="mw-redirect" title="National Gallery, London">National Gallery, London</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Brady94_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brady94-79">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> By the early 18th century his oeuvre was gaining international recognition, and later in the century British collectors ventured to Spain in search of acquisitions. Since the popularity of <a href="/wiki/Italian_art" title="Italian art">Italian art</a> was then at its height among British connoisseurs, they concentrated on paintings that showed obvious Italian influence, largely ignoring others such as <i>Las Meninas</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An almost immediate influence can be seen in the two portraits by Mazo of subjects depicted in <i>Las Meninas</i>, which in some ways reverse the motif of that painting. Ten years later, in 1666, Mazo painted Infanta Margaret Theresa, who was then 15 and just about to leave Madrid to marry the Holy Roman Emperor. In the background are figures in two further receding doorways, one of which was the new King Charles (Margaret Theresa's brother), and another the dwarf Maribarbola. A Mazo portrait of the widowed Queen Mariana again shows, through a doorway in the Alcázar, the young king with dwarfs, possibly including Maribarbola, and attendants who offer him a drink.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup>  Mazo's painting of <i>The Family of the Artist</i> also shows a composition similar to that of <i>Las Meninas</i>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Francisco_Goya" title="Francisco Goya">Francisco Goya</a> <a href="/wiki/Etching" title="Etching">etched</a> a <a href="/wiki/Old_master_print" title="Old master print">print</a> of <i>Las Meninas</i> in 1778,<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> and later used Velázquez's painting as the model for his <i><a href="/wiki/Charles_IV_of_Spain_and_His_Family" title="Charles IV of Spain and His Family">Charles&#160;IV of Spain and His Family</a></i>. As in <i>Las Meninas</i>, the royal family in Goya's work is apparently visiting the artist's studio. In both paintings the artist is shown working on a canvas, of which only the rear is visible. Goya, however, replaces the atmospheric and warm perspective of <i>Las Meninas</i> with what Pierre Gassier calls a sense of "imminent suffocation". Goya's royal family is presented on a "stage facing the public, while in the shadow of the wings the painter, with a grim smile, points and says: 'Look at them and judge for yourself!'&#160;"<sup id="cite_ref-G69_78-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G69-78">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Daughters_of_Edward_Darley_Boit,_John_Singer_Sargent,_1882_(unfree_frame_crop).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/The_Daughters_of_Edward_Darley_Boit%2C_John_Singer_Sargent%2C_1882_%28unfree_frame_crop%29.jpg/220px-The_Daughters_of_Edward_Darley_Boit%2C_John_Singer_Sargent%2C_1882_%28unfree_frame_crop%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/The_Daughters_of_Edward_Darley_Boit%2C_John_Singer_Sargent%2C_1882_%28unfree_frame_crop%29.jpg/330px-The_Daughters_of_Edward_Darley_Boit%2C_John_Singer_Sargent%2C_1882_%28unfree_frame_crop%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/The_Daughters_of_Edward_Darley_Boit%2C_John_Singer_Sargent%2C_1882_%28unfree_frame_crop%29.jpg/440px-The_Daughters_of_Edward_Darley_Boit%2C_John_Singer_Sargent%2C_1882_%28unfree_frame_crop%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2002" data-file-height="2004" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Daughters_of_Edward_Darley_Boit,_John_Singer_Sargent,_1882_(unfree_frame_crop).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/John_Singer_Sargent" title="John Singer Sargent">John Singer Sargent</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daughters_of_Edward_Darley_Boit" title="The Daughters of Edward Darley Boit">The Daughters of Edward Darley Boit</a></i>, 1882, oil on canvas, 222.5 x 222.5&#160;cm, <a href="/wiki/Boston_Museum_of_Fine_Arts" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston Museum of Fine Arts">Boston Museum of Fine Arts</a></div></div></div>
<p>The 19th-century British art collector <a href="/wiki/William_John_Bankes" title="William John Bankes">William John Bankes</a> travelled to Spain during the <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_War" title="Peninsular War">Peninsular War</a> (1808–1814) and acquired a copy of <i>Las Meninas</i> painted by Mazo,<sup id="cite_ref-Brad100-101_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brad100-101-83">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> which he believed to be an original preparatory <a href="/wiki/Oil_sketch" title="Oil sketch">oil sketch</a> by Velázquez—although Velázquez did not usually paint studies. Bankes described his purchase as "the glory of my collection", noting that he had been "a long while in treaty for it and was obliged to pay a high price".<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> The copy was admired throughout the 19th century in Britain, and is now in <a href="/wiki/Kingston_Lacy" title="Kingston Lacy">Kingston Lacy</a>.  Recently there have been suggestions that it might be by Velázquez after all (see below).
</p><p>A new appreciation for Velázquez's less Italianate paintings developed after 1819, when <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_VII_of_Spain" title="Ferdinand VII of Spain">Ferdinand&#160;VII</a> opened the royal collection to the public.<sup id="cite_ref-Brad100-101_83-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brad100-101-83">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> In 1879 <a href="/wiki/John_Singer_Sargent" title="John Singer Sargent">John Singer Sargent</a>  painted a small-scale copy of <i>Las Meninas</i>, and in 1882 painted a homage to the painting in his <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daughters_of_Edward_Darley_Boit" title="The Daughters of Edward Darley Boit">The Daughters of Edward Darley Boit</a></i>, while the Irish artist <a href="/wiki/John_Lavery" title="John Lavery">Sir John Lavery</a> chose Velázquez's masterpiece as the basis for his portrait <i>The Royal Family at Buckingham Palace, 1913</i>. <a href="/wiki/George_V_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="George V of the United Kingdom">George&#160;V</a> visited Lavery's studio during the execution of the painting, and, perhaps remembering the legend that Philip&#160;IV had daubed the cross of the Knights of Santiago on the figure of Velázquez, asked Lavery if he could contribute to the portrait with his own hand. According to Lavery, "Thinking that royal blue might be an appropriate colour, I mixed it on the palette, and taking a brush he [George&#160;V] applied it to the Garter ribbon."<sup id="cite_ref-Brad100-101_83-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brad100-101-83">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PabloPicasso_Meninas.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e4/PabloPicasso_Meninas.jpg/250px-PabloPicasso_Meninas.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="185" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/e/e4/PabloPicasso_Meninas.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="222" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PabloPicasso_Meninas.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>During 1957 <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">Pablo Picasso</a> painted 58 recreations of <i><a href="/wiki/Las_Meninas_(Picasso)" title="Las Meninas (Picasso)">Las Meninas</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pablo_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pablo-85">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Between August and December 1957, <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">Pablo Picasso</a> painted a series of 58 interpretations of <i>Las Meninas</i>, and figures from it, which currently fill the <i><a href="/wiki/Las_Meninas_(Picasso)" title="Las Meninas (Picasso)">Las Meninas</a></i> room of the <a href="/wiki/Museu_Picasso" title="Museu Picasso">Museu Picasso</a> in Barcelona, Spain. Picasso did not vary the characters within the series, but largely retained the naturalness of the scene; according to the museum, his works constitute an "exhaustive study of form, rhythm, colour and movement".<sup id="cite_ref-Pablo_85-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pablo-85">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> A print of 1973 by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Hamilton_(artist)" title="Richard Hamilton (artist)">Richard Hamilton</a> called <i>Picasso's Meninas</i> draws on both Velázquez and Picasso.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> Photographer <a href="/wiki/Joel-Peter_Witkin" title="Joel-Peter Witkin">Joel-Peter Witkin</a> was commissioned by the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Culture_(Spain)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ministry of Culture (Spain)">Spanish Ministry of Culture</a>  to create a work titled <i>Las Meninas, New Mexico</i> (1987) which references Velázquez's painting as well as other works by Spanish artists.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2004, the video artist <a href="/wiki/Eve_Sussman" title="Eve Sussman">Eve Sussman</a> filmed <i>89 Seconds at <a href="/wiki/Alc%C3%A1zar" title="Alcázar">Alcázar</a></i>, a high-definition video tableau inspired by <i>Las Meninas</i>. The work is a recreation of the moments leading up to and directly following the approximately 89 seconds when the royal family and their courtiers would have come together in the exact configuration of Velázquez's painting. Sussman had assembled a team of 35, including an architect, a set designer, a choreographer, a costume designer, actors, actresses, and a film crew.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2008 exhibition at the Museu Picasso called "Forgetting Velázquez: <i>Las Meninas</i>" included art responding to Velázquez's painting by
<a href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferm%C3%ADn_Aguayo" class="extiw" title="es:Fermín Aguayo">Fermín Aguayo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avigdor_Arikha" title="Avigdor Arikha">Avigdor Arikha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Claudio_Bravo_(artist)" class="mw-redirect" title="Claudio Bravo (artist)">Claudio Bravo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juan_Carre%C3%B1o_de_Miranda" title="Juan Carreño de Miranda">Juan Carreño de Miranda</a>,
<a href="/wiki/Michael_Craig-Martin" title="Michael Craig-Martin">Michael Craig-Martin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Dal%C3%AD" title="Salvador Dalí">Salvador Dalí</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juan_Downey" title="Juan Downey">Juan Downey</a>, Goya, Hamilton, Mazo, <a href="/wiki/Vik_Muniz" title="Vik Muniz">Vik Muniz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jorge_Oteiza" title="Jorge Oteiza">Jorge Oteiza</a>, Picasso, <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Saura" title="Antonio Saura">Antonio Saura</a>, <a href="/wiki/Franz_von_Stuck" class="mw-redirect" title="Franz von Stuck">Franz von Stuck</a>, Sussman, <a href="/wiki/Manolo_Vald%C3%A9s" title="Manolo Valdés">Manolo Valdés</a>, and Witkin, among others.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup>  In 2009 the Museo del Prado launched a project facilitating access to <i>Las Meninas</i> in mega high resolution through the Internet.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2010 and 2011 <a href="/wiki/Felix_de_la_Concha" class="mw-redirect" title="Felix de la Concha">Felix de la Concha</a> created <i>Las Meninas Under An Artificial Light</i>. It is a meticulous copy made in <a href="/wiki/Iowa_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Iowa City">Iowa City</a>, painted in oil on 140 panels, which together reconstruct the actual size of the painting of 318 x 276&#160;cm. To this, 30&#160;cm on its left side were added to reflect the loss to the original from the fire at the Alcazar in 1734. This provides a new reading to the composition. His work also highlights, with its fragmentation, the artificiality of reproduction as a way of seeing works of art today. <i>Las Meninas under an artificial light</i> has been on public display since 2018 at the NH Hotel in <a href="/wiki/Zamora,_Spain" title="Zamora, Spain">Zamora, Spain</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kingston_Lacy_version">Kingston Lacy version</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Las_Meninas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Kingston Lacy version">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bankes' smaller version of the painting is now in the country house of Kingston Lacy in <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup>  Several experts, including the former Curator of the Department of Renaissance and Baroque Painting in the <a href="/wiki/Museo_del_Prado" title="Museo del Prado">Museo del Prado</a> and current Director of the Moll Institute of Studies of Flemish Paintings, in Madrid, Professor Matías Díaz Padrón, suggest that this "could be a model" painted by <a href="/wiki/Vel%C3%A1zquez" class="mw-redirect" title="Velázquez">Velázquez</a> before the completed work which hangs in the Museo del Prado, perhaps to be approved by the king.<sup id="cite_ref-elmundo_94-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-elmundo-94">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup>  Conflicting with this is the fact that the Kingston Lacy version represents the final state of <i>Las Meninas</i>, not the earlier state of the painting revealed by radiographs, suggesting that it was painted after the completed work, not before it.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The usual attribution since the 19th century has been that the Kingston Lacy painting is a copy by <a href="/wiki/Juan_Bautista_Mart%C3%ADnez_del_Mazo" title="Juan Bautista Martínez del Mazo">Juan Bautista Martínez del Mazo</a> (c. 1612-1667), son-in-law and close follower of Velázquez.<sup id="cite_ref-elmundo_94-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-elmundo-94">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> The version is missing some of the final work's details and nuances such as the royal couple's reflection in the mirror.  Its composition is almost identical to the original.  Although its colours are lighter, the light is less strong.  Pencil lines outlining the Infanta's face, eyes, and hair are also visible.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Kingston Lacy painting was previously owned by <a href="/wiki/Gaspar_Melchor_de_Jovellanos" title="Gaspar Melchor de Jovellanos">Gaspar Melchor de Jovellanos</a> and then by <a href="/wiki/Juan_Agust%C3%ADn_Ce%C3%A1n_Berm%C3%BAdez" title="Juan Agustín Ceán Bermúdez">Ceán Bermúdez</a>, who were both friends of <a href="/wiki/Goya" class="mw-redirect" title="Goya">Goya</a> whose portraits he painted.  Bermúdez's writings on the painting were published posthumously in 1885.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Las_Meninas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: lower-alpha;">
<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The name is sometimes given in print as <i>Las Meniñas</i>, but there is no word *<i>meniña</i> in Spanish. The word means "girl from a noble family brought up to serve at court" (<i>Oxford Concise Spanish Dictionary</i>) and comes from <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">menina</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a> word for "girl".
