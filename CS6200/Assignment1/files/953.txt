	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Water wheel</h1>

<p>A <b>water wheel</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Machine" title="Machine">machine</a> for converting the energy of flowing or falling water into useful forms of power, often in a <a href="/wiki/Watermill" title="Watermill">watermill</a>.  A water wheel consists of a wheel (usually constructed from wood or metal), with a number of <a href="/wiki/Blade" title="Blade">blades</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bucket" title="Bucket">buckets</a> arranged on the outside rim forming the driving surface.
</p><p>Water wheels were still in commercial use well into the 20th century but they are no longer in common use. Uses included milling flour in <a href="/wiki/Gristmill" title="Gristmill">gristmills</a>, grinding wood into pulp for <a href="/wiki/Papermaking" title="Papermaking">papermaking</a>, hammering <a href="/wiki/Wrought_iron" title="Wrought iron">wrought iron</a>, machining, ore crushing and pounding fiber for use in the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Cloth" class="mw-redirect" title="Cloth">cloth</a>.
</p><p>Some water wheels are fed by water from a mill pond, which is formed when a flowing stream is dammed. A channel for the water flowing to or from a water wheel is called a <a href="/wiki/Mill_race" title="Mill race">mill race</a>. The race bringing water from the mill pond to the water wheel is a <b>headrace</b>; the one carrying water after it has left the wheel is commonly referred to as a <b>tailrace</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ref-1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ref-1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the mid to late 18th century <a href="/wiki/John_Smeaton" title="John Smeaton">John Smeaton</a>'s scientific investigation  of the water wheel led to significant increases in efficiency supplying much needed power for the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson&amp;Musson_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson&amp;Musson-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Thomson_2009_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thomson_2009-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Water wheels began being displaced by the smaller, less expensive and more efficient <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">turbine</a>, developed by <a href="/wiki/Beno%C3%AEt_Fourneyron" title="Benoît Fourneyron">Benoît Fourneyron</a>, beginning with his first model in 1827.<sup id="cite_ref-Thomson_2009_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thomson_2009-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  Turbines are capable of handling high <i><a href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_head" title="Hydrostatic head">heads</a></i>, or <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">elevations</a>, that exceed the capability of practical-sized waterwheels.
</p><p>The main difficulty of water wheels is their dependence on flowing water, which limits where they can be located. Modern <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectric dams</a> can be viewed as the descendants of the water wheel, as they too take advantage of the movement of water downhill.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Water wheels come in two basic designs:<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>a horizontal wheel with a vertical axle; or</li>
<li>a vertical wheel with a horizontal axle.</li></ul>
<p>The latter can be subdivided according to where the water hits the wheel into backshot (pitch-back<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>) overshot, breastshot, undershot, and stream-wheels.<sup id="cite_ref-Stream_wheel_term_and_specifics_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stream_wheel_term_and_specifics-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
The term undershot can refer to any wheel where the water passes under the wheel<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> but it usually implies that the water entry is low on the wheel.
</p><p>Most water wheels in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> are (or were) vertical wheels rotating about a horizontal axle, but in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_highlands" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish highlands">Scottish highlands</a> and parts of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Europe" title="Southern Europe">Southern Europe</a> mills often had a horizontal wheel (with a vertical axle).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Summary_of_types">Summary of types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Summary of types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="wikitable">
<tbody><tr>
<td><b>Vertical axis</b> also known as tub or Norse mills.
<ul><li>Horizontal wheel with a vertical axis</li>
<li>A jet of water strikes blades mounted on the axle</li>
<li>Driving surfaces – blades</li>
<li>Water – low volume, high <a href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_head" title="Hydrostatic head">head</a></li>
<li>Efficiency – poor</li></ul>
</td>

<p><sup id="cite_ref-PITLtypes_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PITLtypes-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lewis_et_al_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis_et_al-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Overshot and backshot water wheels are typically used where the available height difference is more than a couple of meters. Breastshot wheels are more suited to large flows with a moderate <a href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_head" title="Hydrostatic head">head</a>. Undershot and stream wheel use large flows at little or no head.
</p><p>There is often an associated <a href="/wiki/Millpond" class="mw-redirect" title="Millpond">millpond</a>, a reservoir for storing water and hence energy until it is needed. Larger heads store more potential energy for the same amount of water so the reservoirs for overshot and backshot wheels tend to be smaller than for breast shot wheels.
</p><p>Overshot and pitchback water wheels are suitable where there is a small stream with a height difference of more than 2 metres (6.5&#160;ft), often in association with a small reservoir. Breastshot and undershot wheels can be used on rivers or high volume flows with large reservoirs.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vertical_axis">Vertical axis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Vertical axis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vertical_waterwheel_simple.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Vertical_waterwheel_simple.svg/220px-Vertical_waterwheel_simple.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="248" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Vertical_waterwheel_simple.svg/330px-Vertical_waterwheel_simple.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Vertical_waterwheel_simple.svg/440px-Vertical_waterwheel_simple.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="450" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vertical_waterwheel_simple.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diagram of vertical axis water mill.</div></div></div>
<p>A horizontal wheel with a vertical axle.
</p><p>Commonly called a <b>tub wheel</b>, <b>Norse mill</b> or <b>Greek mill</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> the horizontal wheel is a primitive and inefficient form of the modern turbine. However if it delivers the required power then the efficiency is of secondary importance. It is usually mounted inside a mill building below the working floor. A jet of water is directed on to the paddles of the water wheel, causing them to turn. This is a simple system usually without gearing so that the vertical axle of the water wheel becomes the drive spindle of the mill.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The earliest known reference to water wheels dates to about 400&#160;BCE,<sup id="cite_ref-ASMEnoria_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASMEnoria-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
and the earliest horizontal axis wheels date to about 200&#160;BCE, so vertical axis mills pre-date horizontal axis mills by about two centuries.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stream">Stream</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Stream">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stream_waterwheel_simple.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Stream_waterwheel_simple.svg/220px-Stream_waterwheel_simple.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="248" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Stream_waterwheel_simple.svg/330px-Stream_waterwheel_simple.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Stream_waterwheel_simple.svg/440px-Stream_waterwheel_simple.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="450" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stream_waterwheel_simple.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diagram of stream shot waterwheel.</div></div></div>
<p>A stream wheel<sup id="cite_ref-Stream_wheel_term_and_specifics_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stream_wheel_term_and_specifics-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PITLtypes_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PITLtypes-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> is a vertically mounted water wheel that is rotated by the water in a water course striking paddles or blades at the bottom of the wheel. This type of water wheel is the oldest type of horizontal axis wheel.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> They are also known as <a href="/wiki/Free_surface" title="Free surface">free surface</a> wheels because the water is not constrained by millraces or wheel pit.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Stream wheels are cheaper and simpler to build, and have less of an environmental impact, than other type of wheel. They do not constitute a major change of the river. Their disadvantages are their low efficiency, which means that they generate less power and can only be used where the flow rate is sufficient. A typical flat board undershot wheel uses about 20 percent of the energy in the flow of water striking the wheel as measured by English civil engineer <a href="/wiki/John_Smeaton" title="John Smeaton">John Smeaton</a> in the 18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> More modern wheels have higher efficiencies.
</p><p>Stream wheels gain little or no advantage from <a href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_head" title="Hydrostatic head">head</a>, a difference in water level.
</p><p>Stream wheels mounted on floating platforms are often referred to as <b>ship wheels</b> and the mill as a <b>ship mill</b>. The earliest were probably constructed by the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine" class="mw-redirect" title="Byzantine">Byzantine</a> general <a href="/wiki/Belisarius" title="Belisarius">Belisarius</a> during the siege of <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> in 537.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Later they were sometimes mounted immediately downstream from <a href="/wiki/Bridges" class="mw-redirect" title="Bridges">bridges</a> where the flow restriction of the bridge piers increased the speed of the current.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Historically they were very inefficient but major advances were made in the eighteenth century.<sup id="cite_ref-ASMEnoria_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASMEnoria-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Undershot_wheel">Undershot wheel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Undershot wheel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Undershot_waterwheel_simple.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Undershot_waterwheel_simple.svg/220px-Undershot_waterwheel_simple.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="248" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Undershot_waterwheel_simple.svg/330px-Undershot_waterwheel_simple.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Undershot_waterwheel_simple.svg/440px-Undershot_waterwheel_simple.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="450" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Undershot_waterwheel_simple.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diagram of undershot waterwheel showing headrace, tailrace, and water.</div></div></div>

<p>An undershot wheel is a vertically mounted water wheel with a horizontal axle that is rotated by the water from a low weir striking the wheel in the bottom quarter. Most of the energy gain is from the movement of the water and comparatively little from the head. They are similar in operation and design to stream wheels.
</p><p>The term undershot is sometimes used with related but different meanings:
</p>
<ul><li>all wheels where the water passes under the wheel<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>wheels where the water enters in the bottom quarter.</li>
<li>wheels where paddles are placed into the flow of a stream. See stream above.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PITLtypes_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PITLtypes-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>This is the oldest type of vertical water wheel.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breastshot_wheel">Breastshot wheel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breastshot wheel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Breastshot_waterwheel_simple.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Breastshot_waterwheel_simple.svg/220px-Breastshot_waterwheel_simple.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="248" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Breastshot_waterwheel_simple.svg/330px-Breastshot_waterwheel_simple.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Breastshot_waterwheel_simple.svg/440px-Breastshot_waterwheel_simple.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="450" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Breastshot_waterwheel_simple.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diagram of breastshot waterwheel showing headrace, tailrace, and water.</div></div></div>
<p>The word <b>breastshot</b> is used in a variety of ways. Some authors restrict the term to wheels where the water enters at about the 10 o’clock position, others 9 o’clock, and others for a range of heights.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In this article it is used for wheels where the water entry is significantly above the bottom and significantly below the top, typically the middle half.
</p><p>They are characterised by:
</p>
<ul><li>buckets carefully shaped to minimise turbulence as water enters</li>
<li>buckets ventilated with holes in the side to allow air to escape as the water enters</li>
<li>a masonry "apron" closely conforming to the wheel face, which helps contain the water in the buckets as they progress downwards</li></ul>
<p>Both <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic</a> (movement) and <a href="/wiki/Potential_energy" title="Potential energy">potential</a> (height and weight) energy are utilised.
</p><p>The small clearance between the wheel and the masonry requires that a breastshot wheel has a good <a href="/wiki/Trash_rack" title="Trash rack">trash rack</a> ('screen' in British English) to prevent debris from jamming between the wheel and the apron and potentially causing serious damage.
</p><p>Breastshot wheels are less efficient than overshot and backshot wheels but they can handle high flow rates and consequently high power. They are preferred for steady, high-volume flows such as are found on the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Seaboard_fall_line" title="Atlantic Seaboard fall line">Fall Line</a> of the North American East Coast. Breastshot wheels are the most common type in the United States of America<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and are said to have powered the industrial revolution.<sup id="cite_ref-ASMEnoria_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASMEnoria-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Backshot_waterwheel_simple.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Backshot_waterwheel_simple.svg/220px-Backshot_waterwheel_simple.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="248" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Backshot_waterwheel_simple.svg/330px-Backshot_waterwheel_simple.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Backshot_waterwheel_simple.svg/440px-Backshot_waterwheel_simple.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="450" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Backshot_waterwheel_simple.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diagram of backshot waterwheel showing headrace, tailrace, water, and spillage.</div></div></div>

<p>A backshot wheel (also called <b>pitchback</b>) is a variety of overshot wheel where the water is introduced just before the summit of the wheel. In many situations it has the advantage that the bottom of the wheel is moving in the same direction as the water in the tail race which makes it more efficient. It also performs better than an overshot wheel in flood conditions when the water level may submerge the bottom of the wheel. It will continue to rotate until the water in the wheel pit rises quite high on the wheel. This makes the technique particularly suitable for streams that experience significant variations in flow and reduces the size, complexity and hence cost of the tail race.
</p><p>The direction of rotation of a backshot wheel is the same as that of a breastshot wheel but in other respects it is very similar to the overshot wheel. See below.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overshot_wheel">Overshot wheel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Overshot wheel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Overshot_waterwheel_simple.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Overshot_waterwheel_simple.svg/220px-Overshot_waterwheel_simple.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="248" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Overshot_waterwheel_simple.svg/330px-Overshot_waterwheel_simple.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Overshot_waterwheel_simple.svg/440px-Overshot_waterwheel_simple.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="450" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Overshot_waterwheel_simple.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diagram of overshot waterwheel showing headrace, tailrace, water, and spillage.</div></div></div>
<p>A vertically mounted water wheel that is rotated by water entering buckets just past the top of the wheel is said to be overshot. The term is sometimes, erroneously, applied to backshot  wheels where the water goes down behind the wheel.
</p><p>A typical overshot wheel has the water channelled to the wheel at the top and slightly beyond the axle. The water collects in the buckets on that side of the wheel, making it heavier than the other "empty" side. The weight turns the wheel, and the water flows out into the tail-water when the wheel rotates enough to invert the buckets. The overshot design is very efficient, it can achieve 90%,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and does not require rapid flow.
</p><p>Nearly all of the energy is gained from the weight of water lowered to the tail race although a small contribution may be made by the kinetic energy of the water entering the wheel. They are suited to larger heads than the other type of wheel so they are ideally suited to hilly country. However even the largest water wheel, the <a href="/wiki/Laxey_Wheel" title="Laxey Wheel">Laxey Wheel</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>, only utilises a head of around 30&#160;m (100&#160;ft). The world's largest head turbines, <a href="/wiki/Bieudron_Hydroelectric_Power_Station" title="Bieudron Hydroelectric Power Station">Bieudron Hydroelectric Power Station</a> in <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, utilise about 1,869&#160;m (6,132&#160;ft).
</p><p>Overshot wheels require a large head compared to other types of wheel which usually means significant investment in constructing the head race. Sometimes the final approach of the water to the wheel is along a <a href="/wiki/Flume" title="Flume">flume</a> or <a href="/wiki/Penstock" title="Penstock">penstock</a>, which can be lengthy.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hybrid">Hybrid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Hybrid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overshot_and_backshot">Overshot and backshot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Overshot and backshot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Finch_Foundary_Water_Wheel,_Devon,_UK_-_Diliff.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Finch_Foundary_Water_Wheel%2C_Devon%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg/300px-Finch_Foundary_Water_Wheel%2C_Devon%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="185" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Finch_Foundary_Water_Wheel%2C_Devon%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg/450px-Finch_Foundary_Water_Wheel%2C_Devon%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Finch_Foundary_Water_Wheel%2C_Devon%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg/600px-Finch_Foundary_Water_Wheel%2C_Devon%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg 2x" data-file-width="6128" data-file-height="3787" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Finch_Foundary_Water_Wheel,_Devon,_UK_-_Diliff.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>One of Finch Foundry's water wheels.</div></div></div>
<p>Some wheels are overshot at the top and backshot at the bottom thereby potentially combining the best features of both types. The photograph shows an example at <a href="/wiki/Finch_Foundry" title="Finch Foundry">Finch Foundry</a> in Devon, UK. The head race is the overhead timber structure and a branch to the left supplies water to the wheel. The water exits from under the wheel back into the stream.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reversible">Reversible</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Reversible">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:AndersonMill.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/AndersonMill.jpg/220px-AndersonMill.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/AndersonMill.jpg/330px-AndersonMill.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/AndersonMill.jpg/440px-AndersonMill.jpg 2x" data-file-width="864" data-file-height="648" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:AndersonMill.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <b>Anderson Mill</b> of <a href="/wiki/Anderson_Mill,_Texas" class="mw-redirect" title="Anderson Mill, Texas">Texas</a> is undershot, backshot, and overshot using two sources of water. This allows the direction of the wheel to be reversed.</div></div></div>
<p>A special type of overshot/backshot wheel is the reversible water wheel. This has two sets of blades or buckets running in opposite directions, so that it can turn in either direction depending on which side the water is directed. Reversible wheels were used in the <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a> industry in order to power various means of ore conveyance. By changing the direction of the wheel, barrels or baskets of ore could be lifted up or lowered down a shaft or inclined plane. There was usually a cable drum or a chain basket (German: Kettenkorb) on the axle of the wheel. It is essential that the wheel have braking equipment to be able to stop the wheel (known as a braking wheel). The oldest known drawing of a reversible water wheel was by <a href="/wiki/Georgius_Agricola" title="Georgius Agricola">Georgius Agricola</a> and dates to 1556.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Suspension_wheels_and_rim-gears">Suspension wheels and rim-gears</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Suspension wheels and rim-gears">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Portland_Basin_waterwheel-_5185.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Portland_Basin_waterwheel-_5185.JPG/220px-Portland_Basin_waterwheel-_5185.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Portland_Basin_waterwheel-_5185.JPG/330px-Portland_Basin_waterwheel-_5185.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Portland_Basin_waterwheel-_5185.JPG/440px-Portland_Basin_waterwheel-_5185.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2848" data-file-height="2136" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Portland_Basin_waterwheel-_5185.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The suspension wheel with rim-gearing at the <a href="/wiki/Dukinfield_Junction" title="Dukinfield Junction">Portland Basin Canal Warehouse</a></div></div></div>
<p>Two early improvements were <a href="/wiki/Wire_wheel" title="Wire wheel">suspension wheels</a> and rim gearing. Suspension wheels are constructed in the same manner as a bicycle wheel, the rim being supported under tension from the hub- this led to larger lighter wheels than the former design where the heavy spokes were under compression. Rim-gearing entailed adding a notched wheel to the rim or shroud of the wheel. A stub gear engaged the rim-gear and took the power into the mill using an independent line shaft. This removed the rotative stress from the axle which could thus be lighter, and also allowed more flexibility in the location of the power train. The shaft rotation was geared up from that of the wheel which led to less power loss. An example of this design pioneered by Thomas Hewes and refined by <a href="/wiki/William_Fairburn" class="mw-redirect" title="William Fairburn">William Fairburn</a> can be seen at the 1849 restored wheel at the <a href="/wiki/Dukinfield_Junction" title="Dukinfield Junction">Portland Basin Canal Warehouse</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Watermill" title="Watermill">Watermill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Noria" title="Noria">Noria</a></div>
<p>The two main functions of water wheels were historically water-lifting for irrigation purposes and as a power source.  When used for water-lifting power can be supplied by either human or animal force or by the water current itself.
</p><p>It is not clear from the available historic texts and archaeology whether the watermill originates in Egypt, Persia, India, Greece, or regions in-between; usage within just a few decades of each other's cultures in the 4th to 3rd centuries&#160;BCE is documented over a large swath of Eurasia.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Near_East">Ancient Near East</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Ancient Near East">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the historian Helaine Selin, there is evidence indicating that the <a href="/wiki/Watermill" title="Watermill">watermill</a> originated from the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Empire" title="Persian Empire">Persian Empire</a> before 350&#160;BCE, likely in what are today <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> or <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>. There were <a href="/wiki/Quarries" class="mw-redirect" title="Quarries">quarries</a> known for their <a href="/wiki/Millstone" title="Millstone">millstones</a> in Iran and on the upper <a href="/wiki/Tigris" title="Tigris">Tigris</a> in what is today <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>. The mills invented at this date had horizontal, propeller-like water wheels that drove the millstones directly.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to Terry S. Reynolds and R. J. Forbes, the water wheel likely originated from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Near_East" title="Ancient Near East">ancient Near East</a> by the 3rd century&#160;BCE for use in moving <a href="/wiki/Millstone" title="Millstone">millstones</a> and small-scale <a href="/wiki/Gristmill" title="Gristmill">grain grinding</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Reynolds suggests that the first water wheels were <a href="/wiki/Noria" title="Noria">norias</a> and, by the 2nd century&#160;BCE, evolved into the vertical watermill in <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Asia Minor</a>, from where it spread to Greece and the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> S. Avitsur also supports a Near-Eastern origin for the watermill.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Egypt">Ancient Egypt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Ancient Egypt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Paddle-driven water-lifting wheels had appeared in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_technology" title="Ancient Egyptian technology">ancient Egypt</a> by the 4th century&#160;BCE.<sup id="cite_ref-Wikander_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wikander-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Egyptians" title="Egyptians">Egyptians</a> are credited with inventing the water wheel with attached pots, a water wheel with water compartments and a bucket chain, which ran over a pulley with buckets attached to it. The invention of the compartmentalized water wheel occurred in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egypt</a> around the 4th century&#160;BCE, in a rural context, away from the metropolis of <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">Hellenistic</a> <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a>, and then spread to other parts of <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/John_Peter_Oleson" title="John Peter Oleson">John Peter Oleson</a>, both the compartmented wheel and the hydraulic <a href="/wiki/Noria" title="Noria">noria</a> appeared in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> by the 4th century&#160;BCE, with the <a href="/wiki/Sakia" title="Sakia">Sakia</a> being invented there a century later. This is supported by archeological finds at <a href="/wiki/Faiyum" title="Faiyum">Faiyum</a>, where the oldest archeological evidence of a water-wheel has been found, in the form of a Sakia dating back to the 3rd century&#160;BCE. A <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">papyrus</a> dating to the 2nd century&#160;BCE also found in Faiyum mentions a water wheel used for irrigation, a 2nd-century&#160;BCE <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">fresco</a> found at Alexandria depicts a compartmented Sakia, and the writings of <a href="/wiki/Callixenus_of_Rhodes" title="Callixenus of Rhodes">Callixenus of Rhodes</a> mention the use of a Sakia in <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_Egypt" class="mw-redirect" title="Ptolemaic Egypt">Ptolemaic Egypt</a> during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy_IV_Philopator" title="Ptolemy IV Philopator">Ptolemy IV</a> in the late 3rd century&#160;BCE.<sup id="cite_ref-Miranda_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miranda-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greco-Roman_Mediterranean">Greco-Roman Mediterranean</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Greco-Roman Mediterranean">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_ancient_watermills" title="List of ancient watermills">List of ancient watermills</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Mediterranean_region" title="History of the Mediterranean region">Mediterranean</a> engineers of the <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">Hellenistic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> periods used the water wheel for both irrigation and as a power source.<sup id="cite_ref-Oleson_1984,_325ff._26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oleson_1984,_325ff.-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Its use in the <a href="/wiki/Greco-Roman_world" title="Greco-Roman world">Greco-Roman world</a> dates back to the technically advanced and scientifically minded <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">Hellenistic period</a> between the 3rd and 1st centuries&#160;BCE.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Drainage_wheels">Drainage wheels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Drainage wheels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:WaterwheelsSp.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/WaterwheelsSp.jpg/220px-WaterwheelsSp.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="216" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/WaterwheelsSp.jpg/330px-WaterwheelsSp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/WaterwheelsSp.jpg/440px-WaterwheelsSp.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1599" data-file-height="1571" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:WaterwheelsSp.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sequence of wheels found in <a href="/wiki/Rio_Tinto_(river)" title="Rio Tinto (river)">Rio Tinto</a> mines, southwestern Spain</div></div></div>
<p>The Romans used water wheels extensively in <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a> projects. Several such devices were described by <a href="/wiki/Vitruvius" title="Vitruvius">Vitruvius</a>. The one found during modern mining at the <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> mines at <a href="/wiki/Rio_Tinto_(river)" title="Rio Tinto (river)">Rio Tinto</a> in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>  involved 16 such wheels stacked above one another so as to lift water about 80 feet (24&#160;m) from the mine sump. Part of a similar wheel dated to about 90 CE, was found in the 1930s, at <a href="/wiki/Dolaucothi" class="mw-redirect" title="Dolaucothi">Dolaucothi</a>, a Roman <a href="/wiki/Gold_mine" class="mw-redirect" title="Gold mine">gold mine</a> in south <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_mills">Water mills</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Water mills">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Roda_de_Vitruvi.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Roda_de_Vitruvi.jpg/170px-Roda_de_Vitruvi.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="242" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Roda_de_Vitruvi.jpg/255px-Roda_de_Vitruvi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Roda_de_Vitruvi.jpg/340px-Roda_de_Vitruvi.jpg 2x" data-file-width="850" data-file-height="1212" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Roda_de_Vitruvi.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Reconstruction of <a href="/wiki/Vitruvius" title="Vitruvius">Vitruvius</a>' undershot-wheeled watermill</div></div></div>
<p>Taking indirect evidence into account from the work of the Greek technician <a href="/wiki/Apollonius_of_Perge" class="mw-redirect" title="Apollonius of Perge">Apollonius of Perge</a>, the British historian of technology M.J.T. Lewis dates the appearance of the vertical-axle watermill to the early 3rd century&#160;BCE, and the horizontal-axle watermill to around 240&#160;BCE, in <a href="/wiki/Byzantium" title="Byzantium">Byzantium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_Egypt" class="mw-redirect" title="Ptolemaic Egypt">Ptolemaic Egypt</a>), respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> A watermill is reported by the Greek geographer <a href="/wiki/Strabon" class="mw-redirect" title="Strabon">Strabon</a> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;64&#160;BCE–24&#160;CE</span>) to have existed sometime before 71&#160;BCE in the palace of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Pontus" title="Kingdom of Pontus">Pontian</a> king <a href="/wiki/Mithradates_VI_Eupator" class="mw-redirect" title="Mithradates VI Eupator">Mithradates VI Eupator</a>, but its exact construction cannot be gleaned from the text (XII, 3, 30 C 556).<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The first clear description of a geared watermill is from the 1st-century&#160;BCE Roman architect <a href="/wiki/Vitruvius" title="Vitruvius">Vitruvius</a>, who tells of the <a href="/wiki/Sakia" title="Sakia">sakia</a> gearing system as being applied to a watermill.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Vitruvius's account is particularly valuable in that it shows how the watermill came about, namely by the combination of the separate Greek inventions of the toothed gear and the water wheel into one effective mechanical system for harnessing water power.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Vitruvius's water wheel is described as being immersed with its lower end in the watercourse so that its paddles could be driven by the velocity of the running water (X, 5.2).<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:R%C3%B6mische_S%C3%A4gem%C3%BChle.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/R%C3%B6mische_S%C3%A4gem%C3%BChle.svg/220px-R%C3%B6mische_S%C3%A4gem%C3%BChle.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="139" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/R%C3%B6mische_S%C3%A4gem%C3%BChle.svg/330px-R%C3%B6mische_S%C3%A4gem%C3%BChle.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/R%C3%B6mische_S%C3%A4gem%C3%BChle.svg/440px-R%C3%B6mische_S%C3%A4gem%C3%BChle.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1050" data-file-height="663" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:R%C3%B6mische_S%C3%A4gem%C3%BChle.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Schematic of the Roman <a href="/wiki/Hierapolis_sawmill" title="Hierapolis sawmill">Hierapolis sawmill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" class="mw-redirect" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a>, powered by a breastshot wheel</div></div></div>
<p>About the same time, the overshot wheel appears for the first time in a poem by <a href="/wiki/Antipater_of_Thessalonica" title="Antipater of Thessalonica">Antipater of Thessalonica</a>, which praises it as a labour-saving device (IX, 418.4–6).<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The motif is also taken up by <a href="/wiki/Lucretius" title="Lucretius">Lucretius</a> (ca. 99-55 BC) who likens the rotation of the water wheel to the motion of the stars on the firmament (V 516).<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The third horizontal-axled type, the breastshot water wheel, comes into archaeological evidence by the late-2nd-century&#160;CE context in <a href="/wiki/Les_Martres-de-Veyre" title="Les Martres-de-Veyre">central Gaul</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Most excavated Roman watermills were equipped with one of these wheels which, although more complex to construct, were much more efficient than the vertical-axle water wheel.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> In the 2nd century&#160;CE, <a href="/wiki/Barbegal_aqueduct_and_mill" title="Barbegal aqueduct and mill">Barbegal watermill complex</a> a series of sixteen overshot wheels was fed by an artificial aqueduct, a proto-industrial grain factory which has been referred to as "the greatest known concentration of mechanical power in the ancient world".<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Africa_Province" class="mw-redirect" title="Africa Province">Roman North Africa</a>, several installations from around 300&#160;CE were found where vertical-axle water wheels fitted with angled blades were installed at the bottom of a water-filled, circular shaft. The water from the mill-race which entered the pit tangentially created a swirling water column that made the fully submerged wheel act like true <a href="/wiki/Water_turbine" title="Water turbine">water turbines</a>, the earliest known to date.<sup id="cite_ref-Roman_helix-turbine_mill_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roman_helix-turbine_mill-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Navigation">Navigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Navigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:De_Rebus_Bellicis,_XVth_Century_Miniature.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/De_Rebus_Bellicis%2C_XVth_Century_Miniature.JPG/220px-De_Rebus_Bellicis%2C_XVth_Century_Miniature.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/De_Rebus_Bellicis%2C_XVth_Century_Miniature.JPG/330px-De_Rebus_Bellicis%2C_XVth_Century_Miniature.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/De_Rebus_Bellicis%2C_XVth_Century_Miniature.JPG/440px-De_Rebus_Bellicis%2C_XVth_Century_Miniature.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1840" data-file-height="1232" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:De_Rebus_Bellicis,_XVth_Century_Miniature.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ox-powered Roman paddle wheel boat from a 15th-century copy of <i><a href="/wiki/De_Rebus_Bellicis" class="mw-redirect" title="De Rebus Bellicis">De Rebus Bellicis</a></i></div></div></div>
<p>Apart from its use in milling and water-raising, ancient engineers applied the paddled water wheel for <a href="/wiki/Automaton" title="Automaton">automatons</a> and in navigation. <a href="/wiki/Vitruvius" title="Vitruvius">Vitruvius</a> (X 9.5-7) describes multi-geared paddle wheels working as a ship <a href="/wiki/Odometer" title="Odometer">odometer</a>, the earliest of its kind. The first mention of paddle wheels as a means of propulsion comes from the  4th–5th-century military treatise <i><a href="/wiki/De_Rebus_Bellicis" class="mw-redirect" title="De Rebus Bellicis">De Rebus Bellicis</a></i> (chapter XVII), where the anonymous Roman author describes an ox-driven paddle-wheel warship.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tiangong_Kaiwu_Chain_Pumps.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Tiangong_Kaiwu_Chain_Pumps.jpg/170px-Tiangong_Kaiwu_Chain_Pumps.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="412" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Tiangong_Kaiwu_Chain_Pumps.jpg/255px-Tiangong_Kaiwu_Chain_Pumps.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/10/Tiangong_Kaiwu_Chain_Pumps.jpg 2x" data-file-width="320" data-file-height="775" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tiangong_Kaiwu_Chain_Pumps.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Two types of <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydraulic">hydraulic</a>-powered <a href="/wiki/Chain_pump" title="Chain pump">chain pumps</a> from the <i><a href="/wiki/Tiangong_Kaiwu" title="Tiangong Kaiwu">Tiangong Kaiwu</a></i> of 1637, written by the <a href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Ming Dynasty">Ming Dynasty</a> <a href="/wiki/Encyclopedist" class="mw-redirect" title="Encyclopedist">encyclopedist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Song_Yingxing" title="Song Yingxing">Song Yingxing</a> (1587–1666).</div></div></div>
<p>Chinese water wheels almost certainly have a separate origin, as early ones there were invariably horizontal water wheels. By at least the 1st century&#160;CE, the <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Han_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Han Dynasty">Eastern Han Dynasty</a> were using water wheels to crush grain in mills and to power the piston-<a href="/wiki/Bellows" title="Bellows">bellows</a> in forging <a href="/wiki/Iron_ore" title="Iron ore">iron ore</a> into <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast iron</a>.
</p><p>In the text known as the <i>Xin Lun</i> written by <a href="/wiki/Huan_Tan" title="Huan Tan">Huan Tan</a> about 20&#160;CE (during the usurpation of <a href="/wiki/Wang_Mang" title="Wang Mang">Wang Mang</a>), it states that the legendary mythological king known as <a href="/wiki/Fu_Xi" class="mw-redirect" title="Fu Xi">Fu Xi</a> was the one responsible for the pestle and mortar, which evolved into the tilt-hammer and then trip hammer device (see <a href="/wiki/Trip_hammer" title="Trip hammer">trip hammer</a>). Although the author speaks of the mythological Fu Xi, a passage of his writing gives hint that the water wheel was in widespread use by the 1st century&#160;CE in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> (<a href="/wiki/Wade-Giles" class="mw-redirect" title="Wade-Giles">Wade-Giles</a> spelling):
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Fu Hsi invented the pestle and mortar, which is so useful, and later on it was cleverly improved in such a way that the whole weight of the body could be used for treading on the tilt-hammer (<i>tui</i>), thus increasing the efficiency ten times. Afterwards the power of animals—donkeys, mules, oxen, and horses—was applied by means of machinery, and water-power too used for pounding, so that the benefit was increased a hundredfold.<sup id="cite_ref-needham_volume_4_part_2_392_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-needham_volume_4_part_2_392-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>In the year 31&#160;CE, the engineer and <a href="/wiki/Prefect" title="Prefect">Prefect</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nanyang_(region)" title="Nanyang (region)">Nanyang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Du_Shi" title="Du Shi">Du Shi</a> (d. 38), applied a complex use of the water wheel and machinery to power the <a href="/wiki/Bellows" title="Bellows">bellows</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnace</a> to create <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast iron</a>. Du Shi is mentioned briefly in the <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Later_Han" class="mw-redirect" title="Book of Later Han">Book of Later Han</a></i> (<i>Hou Han Shu</i>) as follows (in Wade-Giles spelling):
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>In the seventh year of the Chien-Wu reign period (31&#160;CE) Tu Shih was posted to be Prefect of Nanyang. He was a generous man and his policies were peaceful; he destroyed evil-doers and established the dignity (of his office). Good at planning, he loved the common people and wished to save their labor. He invented a water-power reciprocator (<i>shui phai</i>) for the casting of (iron) agricultural implements. Those who smelted and cast already had the push-bellows to blow up their charcoal fires, and now they were instructed to use the rushing of the water (<i>chi shui</i>) to operate it&#160;... Thus the people got great benefit for little labor. They found the 'water(-powered) bellows' convenient and adopted it widely.<sup id="cite_ref-needham_volume_4_part_2_370_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-needham_volume_4_part_2_370-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Water wheels in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> found practical uses such as this, as well as extraordinary use. The <a href="/wiki/List_of_Chinese_inventions" title="List of Chinese inventions">Chinese inventor</a> <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Heng" title="Zhang Heng">Zhang Heng</a> (78–139) was the first in history to apply motive power in rotating the astronomical instrument of an <a href="/wiki/Armillary_sphere" title="Armillary sphere">armillary sphere</a>, by use of a water wheel.<sup id="cite_ref-morton_70_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-morton_70-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_engineer" class="mw-redirect" title="Mechanical engineer">mechanical engineer</a> <a href="/wiki/Ma_Jun" title="Ma Jun">Ma Jun</a> (c. 200–265) from <a href="/wiki/Cao_Wei" title="Cao Wei">Cao Wei</a> once used a water wheel to power and operate a large mechanical puppet theater for the <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Ming_of_Wei" class="mw-redirect" title="Emperor Ming of Wei">Emperor Ming of Wei</a> (<abbr title="reigned">r.</abbr> 226–239).<sup id="cite_ref-needham_volume_4_part_2_158_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-needham_volume_4_part_2_158-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The early history of the watermill in <a href="/wiki/History_of_India" title="History of India">India</a> is obscure. Ancient Indian texts dating back to the 4th century&#160;BCE refer to the term <i>cakkavattaka</i> (turning wheel), which commentaries explain as <i>arahatta-ghati-yanta</i> (machine with wheel-pots attached). On this basis, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Needham" title="Joseph Needham">Joseph Needham</a> suggested that the machine was a <a href="/wiki/Noria" title="Noria">noria</a>. Terry S. Reynolds, however, argues that the "term used in Indian texts is ambiguous and does not clearly indicate a water-powered device". Thorkild Schiøler argued that it is "more likely that these passages refer to some type of tread- or hand-operated water-lifting device, instead of a water-powered water-lifting wheel".<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to Greek historical tradition, India received water-mills from the Roman Empire in the early 4th century&#160;CE when a certain Metrodoros introduced "water-mills and baths, unknown among them [the Brahmans] till then".<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Irrigation water for crops was provided by using water raising wheels, some driven by the force of the current in the river from which the water was being raised. This kind of water raising device was used in <a href="/wiki/History_of_India" title="History of India">ancient India</a>, predating, according to Pacey, its use in the later Roman Empire or China,<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> even though the first literary, archaeological and pictorial evidence of the water wheel appeared in the Hellenistic world.<sup id="cite_ref-Oleson_1984,_325ff._26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oleson_1984,_325ff.-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Around 1150, the astronomer <a href="/wiki/Bhaskara_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Bhaskara II">Bhaskara Achārya</a> observed water-raising wheels and imagined such a wheel lifting enough water to replenish the stream driving it, effectively, a <a href="/wiki/Perpetual_motion" title="Perpetual motion">perpetual motion</a> machine.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> The construction of water works and aspects of water technology in India is described in <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a> works. During medieval times, the diffusion of Indian and <a href="/wiki/Persia" class="mw-redirect" title="Persia">Persian</a> irrigation technologies gave rise to an advanced irrigation system which brought about economic growth and also helped in the growth of material culture.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Islamic_world">Islamic world</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Islamic world">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Muslim_Agricultural_Revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim Agricultural Revolution">Muslim Agricultural Revolution</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hama-3_norias.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Hama-3_norias.jpg/170px-Hama-3_norias.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Hama-3_norias.jpg/255px-Hama-3_norias.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Hama-3_norias.jpg/340px-Hama-3_norias.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1536" data-file-height="2048" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hama-3_norias.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Norias_of_Hama" title="Norias of Hama">norias of Hama</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Orontes_River" title="Orontes River">Orontes River</a></div></div></div>
<p>Arab engineers took over the water technology of the hydraulic societies of the ancient Near East; they adopted the water wheel as early as the 7th century, excavation of a canal in the Basra region discovered remains of a water wheel dating from this period. <a href="/wiki/Hama" title="Hama">Hama</a> in <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> still preserves <a href="/wiki/Norias_of_Hama" title="Norias of Hama">some of its large wheels</a>, on the river <a href="/wiki/Orontes_river" class="mw-redirect" title="Orontes river">Orontes</a>, although they are no longer in use.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>  One of the largest had a diameter of about 20 metres (66&#160;ft) and its rim was divided into 120 compartments. Another wheel that is still in operation is found at <a href="/wiki/Murcia" title="Murcia">Murcia</a> in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, La Nora, and although the original wheel has been replaced by a steel one, the <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moorish</a> system during <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">al-Andalus</a> is otherwise virtually unchanged. Some medieval Islamic compartmented water wheels could lift water as high as 30 metres (100&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_ibn_Zakariya_al-Razi" title="Muhammad ibn Zakariya al-Razi">Muhammad ibn Zakariya al-Razi</a>'s <i>Kitab al-Hawi</i> in the 10th century described a <a href="/wiki/Noria" title="Noria">noria</a> in Iraq that could lift as much as 153,000 litres per hour (34,000&#160;imp&#160;gal/h), or 2,550 litres per minute (560&#160;imp gal/min). This is comparable to the output of modern norias in <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a>, which can lift up to 288,000 litres per hour (63,000&#160;imp&#160;gal/h), or 4,800 litres per minute (1,100&#160;imp gal/min).<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:COLLECTIE_TROPENMUSEUM_Kintjir_of_waterschepwiel_in_Djambi_Sumatra_TMnr_10007886.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/COLLECTIE_TROPENMUSEUM_Kintjir_of_waterschepwiel_in_Djambi_Sumatra_TMnr_10007886.jpg/170px-COLLECTIE_TROPENMUSEUM_Kintjir_of_waterschepwiel_in_Djambi_Sumatra_TMnr_10007886.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="253" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/COLLECTIE_TROPENMUSEUM_Kintjir_of_waterschepwiel_in_Djambi_Sumatra_TMnr_10007886.jpg/255px-COLLECTIE_TROPENMUSEUM_Kintjir_of_waterschepwiel_in_Djambi_Sumatra_TMnr_10007886.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/COLLECTIE_TROPENMUSEUM_Kintjir_of_waterschepwiel_in_Djambi_Sumatra_TMnr_10007886.jpg/340px-COLLECTIE_TROPENMUSEUM_Kintjir_of_waterschepwiel_in_Djambi_Sumatra_TMnr_10007886.jpg 2x" data-file-width="471" data-file-height="700" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:COLLECTIE_TROPENMUSEUM_Kintjir_of_waterschepwiel_in_Djambi_Sumatra_TMnr_10007886.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Water wheel in <a href="/wiki/Djambi" title="Djambi">Djambi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a>, c. 1918</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industrial</a> uses of watermills in the Islamic world date back to the 7th century, while horizontal-wheeled and vertical-wheeled water mills were both in widespread use by the 9th century. A variety of industrial watermills were used in the Islamic world, including <a href="/wiki/Gristmill" title="Gristmill">gristmills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Huller" class="mw-redirect" title="Huller">hullers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sawmill" title="Sawmill">sawmills</a>, shipmills, <a href="/wiki/Stamp_mill" title="Stamp mill">stamp mills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steel_mill" title="Steel mill">steel mills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sugar_refinery" title="Sugar refinery">sugar mills</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tide_mill" title="Tide mill">tide mills</a>. By the 11th century, every province throughout the Islamic world had these industrial watermills in operation, from <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">al-Andalus</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Muslim and Christian engineers also used <a href="/wiki/Crankshaft" title="Crankshaft">crankshafts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_turbine" title="Water turbine">water turbines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gear" title="Gear">gears</a> in watermills and water-raising <a href="/wiki/Machine" title="Machine">machines</a>, and  <a href="/wiki/Dam" title="Dam">dams</a> as a source of water, used to provide additional power to watermills and water-raising machines.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> Fulling mills and steel mills may have spread from Islamic Spain to Christian Spain in the 12th century. Industrial water mills were also employed in large <a href="/wiki/Factory" title="Factory">factory</a> complexes built in <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">al-Andalus</a> between the 11th and 13th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The engineers of the Islamic world developed several solutions to achieve the maximum output from a water wheel. One solution was to mount them to <a href="/wiki/Pier" title="Pier">piers</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bridge" title="Bridge">bridges</a> to take advantage of the increased flow. Another solution was the shipmill, a type of <a href="/wiki/Water_mill" class="mw-redirect" title="Water mill">water mill</a> powered by water wheels mounted on the sides of <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">ships</a> <a href="/wiki/Mooring_(watercraft)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mooring (watercraft)">moored</a> in midstream. This technique was employed along the <a href="/wiki/Tigris" title="Tigris">Tigris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Euphrates" title="Euphrates">Euphrates</a> rivers in 10th-century <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>, where large shipmills made of <a href="/wiki/Teak" title="Teak">teak</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> could produce 10 <a href="/wiki/Ton" title="Ton">tons</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gristmill" title="Gristmill">flour from corn</a> every day for the <a href="/wiki/Granary" title="Granary">granary</a> in <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">Baghdad</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hill2_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hill2-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Flywheel" title="Flywheel">flywheel</a> mechanism, which is used to smooth out the delivery of power from a driving device to a driven machine, was invented by Ibn Bassal (<a href="/wiki/Floruit" title="Floruit">fl.</a> 1038-1075) of <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">Al-Andalus</a>; he pioneered the use of the flywheel in the <a href="/wiki/Sakia" title="Sakia">saqiya</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chain_pump" title="Chain pump">chain pump</a>) and noria.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> The engineers <a href="/wiki/Al-Jazari" class="mw-redirect" title="Al-Jazari">Al-Jazari</a> in the 13th century and <a href="/wiki/Taqi_al-Din_Muhammad_ibn_Ma%27ruf" class="mw-redirect" title="Taqi al-Din Muhammad ibn Ma&#39;ruf">Taqi al-Din</a> in the 16th century described many inventive water-raising machines in their technological treatises. They also employed water wheels to power a variety of devices, including various <a href="/wiki/Water_clock" title="Water clock">water clocks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Automaton" title="Automaton">automata</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_Europe">Medieval Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Medieval Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_early_medieval_watermills" title="List of early medieval watermills">List of early medieval watermills</a></div>
<p>Ancient water-wheel technology continued unabated in the early medieval period where the appearance of new documentary genres such as <a href="/wiki/Legal_code" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal code">legal codes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monastic</a> <a href="/wiki/Charter" title="Charter">charters</a>, but also <a href="/wiki/Hagiography" title="Hagiography">hagiography</a> was accompanied with a sharp increase in references to watermills and wheels.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The earliest vertical-wheel in a <a href="/wiki/Tide_mill" title="Tide mill">tide mill</a> is from 6th-century Killoteran near <a href="/wiki/Waterford" title="Waterford">Waterford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Murphy_2005_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Murphy_2005-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> while the first known horizontal-wheel in such a type of mill is from the Irish <a href="/wiki/Little_Island,_Cork" title="Little Island, Cork">Little Island</a> (c. 630).<sup id="cite_ref-Wikander_1985,_155–157_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wikander_1985,_155–157-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> As for the use in a common Norse or Greek mill, the oldest known horizontal-wheels were excavated in the Irish Ballykilleen, dating to c. 636.<sup id="cite_ref-Wikander_1985,_155–157_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wikander_1985,_155–157-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The earliest excavated water wheel driven by <a href="/wiki/Tidal_power" title="Tidal power">tidal power</a> was the <a href="/wiki/Nendrum_Monastery_mill" title="Nendrum Monastery mill">Nendrum Monastery mill</a> in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> which has been dated to 787, although a possible earlier mill dates to 619. <a href="/wiki/Tide_mill" title="Tide mill">Tide mills</a> became common in estuaries with a good tidal range in both Europe and America generally using undershot wheels.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Waterwheel-Uzhhorod.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Waterwheel-Uzhhorod.jpg/170px-Waterwheel-Uzhhorod.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="128" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Waterwheel-Uzhhorod.jpg/255px-Waterwheel-Uzhhorod.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Waterwheel-Uzhhorod.jpg/340px-Waterwheel-Uzhhorod.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2832" data-file-height="2128" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Waterwheel-Uzhhorod.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Water wheel powering a small village mill at the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Folk_Architecture_and_Life,_Uzhhorod" title="Museum of Folk Architecture and Life, Uzhhorod">Museum of Folk Architecture and Life, Uzhhorod</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cistercian" class="mw-redirect" title="Cistercian">Cistercian</a> <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monasteries</a>, in particular, made extensive use of water wheels to power watermills of many kinds. An early example of a very large water wheel is the still extant wheel at the early 13th century <a href="/wiki/Real_Monasterio_de_Nuestra_Senora_de_Rueda" class="mw-redirect" title="Real Monasterio de Nuestra Senora de Rueda">Real Monasterio de Nuestra Senora de Rueda</a>, a Cistercian monastery in the <a href="/wiki/Aragon" title="Aragon">Aragon</a> region of <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>. Grist mills (for corn) were undoubtedly the most common, but there were also sawmills, fulling mills and mills to fulfil many other labour-intensive tasks. The water wheel remained competitive with the <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engine</a> well into the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>. At around the 8th to 10th century, a number of irrigation technologies were brought into Spain and thus introduced to Europe. One of those technologies is the Noria, which is basically a wheel fitted with buckets on the peripherals for lifting water. It is similar to the undershot water wheel mentioned later in this article. It allowed peasants to power watermills more efficiently. According to Thomas Glick's book, <i>Irrigation and Society in Medieval Valencia</i>, the Noria probably originated from somewhere in <a href="/wiki/Persia" class="mw-redirect" title="Persia">Persia</a>. It has been used for centuries before the technology was brought into Spain by Arabs who had adopted it from the Romans. Thus the distribution of the Noria in the Iberian peninsula "conforms to the area of stabilized Islamic settlement".<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> This technology has a profound effect on the life of peasants. The Noria is relatively cheap to build. Thus it allowed peasants to cultivate land more efficiently in Europe. Together with the <a href="/wiki/Spaniards" title="Spaniards">Spaniards</a>, the technology spread to the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> following <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish expansion</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Domesday_inventory_of_English_mills_c._1086">Domesday inventory of English mills c. 1086</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Domesday inventory of English mills c. 1086">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The assembly convened by <a href="/wiki/William_of_Normandy" class="mw-redirect" title="William of Normandy">William of Normandy</a>, commonly referred to as the "<a href="/wiki/Domesday" class="mw-redirect" title="Domesday">Domesday</a>" or Doomsday survey, took an inventory of all potentially taxable property in England, which included over six thousand mills spread across three thousand different locations.<sup id="cite_ref-Friedel31_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Friedel31-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Locations">Locations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Locations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The type of water wheel selected  was dependent upon the location. Generally if only small volumes of water and high waterfalls were available a millwright would choose to use an <a href="/wiki/Overshot_wheel" class="mw-redirect" title="Overshot wheel">overshot wheel</a>. The decision was influenced by the fact that the buckets could catch and use even a small volume of water.<sup id="cite_ref-Howard_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Howard-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> For large volumes of water with small waterfalls the undershot wheel would have been used, since it was more adapted to such conditions and cheaper to construct. So long as these water supplies were abundant the question of efficiency remained irrelevant. By the 18th century, with increased demand for power coupled with limited water locales, an emphasis was made on efficiency scheme.<sup id="cite_ref-Howard_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Howard-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_influence">Economic influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Economic influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>By the 11th century there were parts of Europe where the exploitation of water was commonplace.<sup id="cite_ref-Friedel31_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Friedel31-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> The water wheel is understood to have actively shaped and forever changed the outlook of Westerners. Europe began to transit from human and animal muscle labor towards mechanical labor with the advent of the water wheel. Medievalist Lynn White Jr. contended that the spread of inanimate power sources was eloquent testimony to the emergence of the West of a new attitude toward, power, work, nature, and above all else technology.<sup id="cite_ref-Friedel31_61-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Friedel31-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Harnessing water-power enabled gains in agricultural productivity, food surpluses and the large scale urbanization starting in the 11th century. The usefulness of water power motivated European experiments with other power sources, such as wind and tidal mills.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> Waterwheels influenced the construction of cities, more specifically canals. The techniques that developed during this early period such as stream jamming and the building of <a href="/wiki/Canals" class="mw-redirect" title="Canals">canals</a>, put Europe on a <a href="/wiki/Hydraulically" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydraulically">hydraulically</a> focused path, for instance water supply and irrigation technology was combined to modify supply power of the wheel.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> Illustrating the extent to which there was a great degree of technological innovation that met the growing needs of the <a href="/wiki/Feudal_state" class="mw-redirect" title="Feudal state">feudal state</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications_of_the_water_wheel">Applications of the water wheel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Applications of the water wheel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gal_gromadzenie_urobku.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Gal_gromadzenie_urobku.jpg/220px-Gal_gromadzenie_urobku.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="230" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Gal_gromadzenie_urobku.jpg/330px-Gal_gromadzenie_urobku.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Gal_gromadzenie_urobku.jpg/440px-Gal_gromadzenie_urobku.jpg 2x" data-file-width="456" data-file-height="476" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gal_gromadzenie_urobku.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ore stamp mill (behind worker taking ore from chute). From <a href="/wiki/Georg_Agricola" class="mw-redirect" title="Georg Agricola">Georg Agricola</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/De_re_metallica" title="De re metallica">De re metallica</a></i> (1556)</div></div></div>
<p>The water mill was used for grinding grain, producing flour for bread, malt for beer, or coarse meal for porridge.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> Hammermills used the wheel to operate hammers. One type was <a href="/wiki/Fulling" title="Fulling">fulling mill</a>, which was used for cloth making.  The <a href="/wiki/Trip_hammer" title="Trip hammer">trip hammer</a> was also used for making <a href="/wiki/Wrought_iron" title="Wrought iron">wrought iron</a> and for working iron into useful shapes, an activity that was otherwise labour-intensive. The water wheel was also used in <a href="/wiki/Papermaking" title="Papermaking">papermaking</a>, beating material to a pulp. In the 13th century water mills used for hammering throughout Europe improved the productivity of early steel manufacturing. Along with the mastery of gunpowder, waterpower provided European countries worldwide military leadership from the 15th century.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="17th-_and_18th-century_Europe">17th- and 18th-century Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: 17th- and 18th-century Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Millwrights distinguished between the two forces, impulse and weight, at work in water wheels long before 18th-century Europe. Fitzherbert, a 16th-century agricultural writer, wrote "druieth the wheel as well as with the weight of the water as with strengthe [impulse]".<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a> also discussed water power, noting "the blow [of the water] is not weight, but excites a power of weight, almost equal to its own power".<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> However, even realisation of the two forces, weight and impulse, confusion remained over the advantages and disadvantages of the two, and there was no clear understanding of the superior efficiency of weight.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> Prior to 1750 it was unsure as to which force was dominant and was widely understood that both forces were operating with equal inspiration amongst one another.<sup id="cite_ref-Torricelli,_Evangelista_1919_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Torricelli,_Evangelista_1919-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> The waterwheel, sparked questions of the laws of nature, specifically the <a href="/wiki/Laws_of_force" class="mw-redirect" title="Laws of force">laws of force</a>. <a href="/wiki/Evangelista_Torricelli" title="Evangelista Torricelli">Evangelista Torricelli</a>'s work on water wheels used an analysis of Galileo's work on falling bodies, that the velocity of a water sprouting from an orifice under its <a href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_head" title="Hydrostatic head">head</a> was exactly equivalent to the velocity a drop of water acquired in falling freely from the same height.<sup id="cite_ref-Torricella,_Evangelica_1919_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Torricella,_Evangelica_1919-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_Europe">Industrial Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Industrial Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Laxey_Wheel_(1715237934).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Laxey_Wheel_%281715237934%29.jpg/170px-Laxey_Wheel_%281715237934%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="195" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Laxey_Wheel_%281715237934%29.jpg/255px-Laxey_Wheel_%281715237934%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Laxey_Wheel_%281715237934%29.jpg/340px-Laxey_Wheel_%281715237934%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2300" data-file-height="2644" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Laxey_Wheel_(1715237934).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Laxey_Wheel" title="Laxey Wheel">Lady Isabella Wheel</a>, Laxey, Isle of Man, used to drive mine pumps</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cromford_waterwheel.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Cromford_waterwheel.jpg/170px-Cromford_waterwheel.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="235" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Cromford_waterwheel.jpg/255px-Cromford_waterwheel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Cromford_waterwheel.jpg/340px-Cromford_waterwheel.jpg 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="966" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cromford_waterwheel.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A mid-19th-century water wheel at <a href="/wiki/Cromford" title="Cromford">Cromford</a> in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> used for grinding locally mined barytes.</div></div></div>
<p>The most powerful water wheel built in the United Kingdom was the 100&#160;hp <a href="/wiki/Quarry_Bank_Mill" title="Quarry Bank Mill">Quarry Bank Mill</a> water wheel near Manchester. A high breastshot design, it was retired in 1904 and replaced with several turbines. It has now been restored and is a museum open to the public.
</p><p>The biggest working water wheel in mainland Britain has a diameter of 15.4&#160;m (51&#160;ft) and was built by the De Winton company of Caernarfon. It is located within the <a href="/wiki/Dinorwic_Quarry" title="Dinorwic Quarry">Dinorwic workshops</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Slate_Museum" title="National Slate Museum">National Slate Museum in Llanberis</a>, North <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>.
</p><p>The largest working water wheel in the world is the <a href="/wiki/Laxey_Wheel" title="Laxey Wheel">Laxey Wheel</a> (also known as <i>Lady Isabella</i>) in the village of <a href="/wiki/Laxey" title="Laxey">Laxey</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>. It is 72&#160;feet 6&#160;inches (22.10&#160;m) in diameter and  6 feet (1.83&#160;m) wide and is maintained by <a href="/wiki/Manx_National_Heritage" title="Manx National Heritage">Manx National Heritage</a>.
</p><p>Development of <a href="/wiki/Water_turbine" title="Water turbine">water turbines</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> led to decreased popularity of water wheels. The main advantage of turbines is that its ability to harness <a href="/wiki/Head_(hydraulic)" class="mw-redirect" title="Head (hydraulic)">head</a> is much greater than the diameter of the turbine, whereas a water wheel cannot effectively harness head greater than its diameter. The migration from water wheels to modern turbines took about one  hundred years.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_developments">Modern developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Modern developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydraulic_wheel">Hydraulic wheel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Hydraulic wheel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A recent development of the breastshot wheel is a hydraulic wheel which effectively incorporates automatic regulation systems. The Aqualienne is one example. It generates between 37&#160;kW and 200&#160;kW of electricity from a 20&#160;m<sup>3</sup> (710&#160;cu&#160;ft) waterflow with a head of 1 to 3.5&#160;m (3 to 11&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-aqualienne_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aqualienne-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> It is designed to produce electricity at the sites of former watermills.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Efficiency">Efficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Efficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Overshot (and particularly backshot) wheels are the most efficient type; a backshot <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> wheel can be more efficient (about 60%) than all but the most advanced and well-constructed <a href="/wiki/Water_turbine" title="Water turbine">turbines</a>. In some situations an overshot wheel is preferable to a turbine.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The development of the <a href="/wiki/Water_turbine" title="Water turbine">hydraulic turbine</a> wheels with their improved efficiency (&gt;67%) opened up an alternative path for the installation of water wheels in existing mills, or redevelopment of abandoned mills.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_power_of_a_wheel">The power of a wheel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: The power of a wheel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The energy available to the wheel has two components:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">Kinetic energy</a> – depends on how fast the water is moving when it enters the wheel</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Potential_energy" title="Potential energy">Potential energy</a> – depends on the change in height of the water between entry to and exit from the wheel</li></ul>
<p>The kinetic energy can be accounted for by converting it into an equivalent head, the velocity head, and adding it to the actual head. For still water the velocity head is zero, and to a good approximation it is negligible for slowly moving water, and can be ignored. The velocity in the tail race is not taken into account because for a perfect wheel the water would leave with zero energy which requires zero velocity. That is impossible, the water has to move away from the wheel, and represents an unavoidable cause of inefficiency.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Power_(physics)" title="Power (physics)">power</a> is how fast that energy is delivered which is determined by the flow rate.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydraulic_wheel_part_reaction_turbine">Hydraulic wheel part reaction turbine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Hydraulic wheel part reaction turbine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A parallel development is the hydraulic wheel/part reaction turbine that also incorporates a weir into the centre of the wheel but uses blades angled to the water flow.
The WICON-Stem Pressure Machine (SPM) exploits this flow.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> Estimated efficiency 67%.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southampton" title="University of Southampton">University of Southampton</a> School of Civil Engineering and the Environment in the UK has investigated both types of Hydraulic wheel machines and has estimated their hydraulic efficiency and suggested improvements, i.e. The Rotary Hydraulic Pressure Machine. (Estimated maximum efficiency 85%).<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>These type of water wheels have high efficiency at part loads / variable flows and can operate at very low heads, &lt; 1&#160;m (3&#160;ft 3&#160;in). Combined with direct drive Axial Flux Permanent Magnet Alternators and power electronics they offer a viable alternative for <a href="/wiki/Low_head_hydro_power" title="Low head hydro power">low head hydroelectric power</a> generation.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water-lifting">Water-lifting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Water-lifting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Noria" title="Noria">Noria</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Diagram_of_waterwheel_or_azud_function.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Diagram_of_waterwheel_or_azud_function.jpg/220px-Diagram_of_waterwheel_or_azud_function.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="251" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Diagram_of_waterwheel_or_azud_function.jpg/330px-Diagram_of_waterwheel_or_azud_function.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Diagram_of_waterwheel_or_azud_function.jpg/440px-Diagram_of_waterwheel_or_azud_function.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1533" data-file-height="1748" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Diagram_of_waterwheel_or_azud_function.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Detail of <i>azud</i> at <a href="/wiki/Aranjuez" title="Aranjuez">Aranjuez</a>, Spain</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Archscrew2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Archscrew2.jpg/220px-Archscrew2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="283" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Archscrew2.jpg/330px-Archscrew2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Archscrew2.jpg/440px-Archscrew2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1526" data-file-height="1966" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Archscrew2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Drainage wheel from <a href="/wiki/Rio_Tinto_(river)" title="Rio Tinto (river)">Rio Tinto</a> mines</div></div></div>
<p>In water-raising devices rotary motion is typically more efficient than machines based on oscillating motion.<sup id="cite_ref-Oleson_2000,_229_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oleson_2000,_229-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The compartmented water wheel comes in two basic forms, the wheel with compartmented body (<a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>tympanum</i>) and the wheel with compartmented rim or a rim with separate, attached containers.<sup id="cite_ref-Oleson_2000,_229_81-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oleson_2000,_229-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> The wheels could be either turned by the flow of water, men treading on its outside or by animals by means of a <a href="/wiki/Sakia" title="Sakia">sakia</a> gear.<sup id="cite_ref-Oleson_2000,_230_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oleson_2000,_230-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> While the tympanum had a large discharge capacity, it could lift the water only to less than the height of its own radius and required a large torque for rotating.<sup id="cite_ref-Oleson_2000,_230_82-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oleson_2000,_230-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> These constructional deficiencies were overcome by the wheel with a compartmented rim which was a less heavy design with a higher lift.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Ptolemaic Egypt</dt></dl>
<p>The earliest literary reference to a water-driven, compartmented wheel appears in the technical treatise <i>Pneumatica</i> (chap. 61) of the Greek engineer <a href="/wiki/Philo_of_Byzantium" title="Philo of Byzantium">Philo of Byzantium</a> (ca. 280–220 BC).<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> In his <i>Parasceuastica</i> (91.43−44), Philo advises the use of such wheels for submerging siege mines as a defensive measure against enemy sapping.<sup id="cite_ref-Oleson_2000,_234_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oleson_2000,_234-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> Compartmented wheels appear to have been the means of choice for draining <a href="/wiki/Dry_dock" title="Dry dock">dry docks</a> in <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a> under the reign of <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy_IV" class="mw-redirect" title="Ptolemy IV">Ptolemy IV</a> (221−205 BC).<sup id="cite_ref-Oleson_2000,_234_85-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oleson_2000,_234-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> Several Greek <a href="/wiki/Papyri" class="mw-redirect" title="Papyri">papyri</a> of the 3rd to 2nd century&#160;BCE mention the use of these wheels, but don't give further details.<sup id="cite_ref-Oleson_2000,_234_85-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oleson_2000,_234-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> The non-existence of the device in the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Near_East" title="Ancient Near East">Ancient Near East</a> before <a href="/wiki/Wars_of_Alexander_the_Great" title="Wars of Alexander the Great">Alexander's conquest</a> can be deduced from its pronounced absence from the otherwise rich oriental iconography on irrigation practices.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> Unlike other water-lifting devices and pumps of the period though, the invention of the compartmented wheel cannot be traced to any particular Hellenistic engineer and may have been made in the late 4th century&#160;BCE in a rural context away from the metropolis of Alexandria.<sup id="cite_ref-Oleson_2000,_235_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oleson_2000,_235-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The earliest depiction of a compartmented wheel is from a tomb painting in <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_Egypt" class="mw-redirect" title="Ptolemaic Egypt">Ptolemaic Egypt</a> which dates to the 2nd century&#160;BCE. It shows a pair of yoked oxen driving the wheel via a <a href="/wiki/Sakia" title="Sakia">sakia</a> gear, which is here for the first time attested, too.<sup id="cite_ref-Oleson_2000,_234,_270_91-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oleson_2000,_234,_270-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> The Greek <a href="/wiki/Sakia" title="Sakia">sakia</a> gear system is already shown fully developed to the point that "modern Egyptian devices are virtually identical".<sup id="cite_ref-Oleson_2000,_234,_270_91-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oleson_2000,_234,_270-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> It is assumed that the scientists of the <a href="/wiki/Musaeum_at_Alexandria" class="mw-redirect" title="Musaeum at Alexandria">Museum of Alexandria</a>, at the time the most active Greek research center, may have been involved in its invention.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup> An episode from the <a href="/wiki/Alexandrian_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Alexandrian War">Alexandrian War</a> in 48&#160;BCE tells of how Caesar's enemies employed geared water wheels to pour sea water from elevated places on the position of the trapped Romans.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Around 300&#160;CE, the <a href="/wiki/Noria" title="Noria">noria</a> was finally introduced when the wooden compartments were replaced with inexpensive ceramic pots that were tied to the outside of an open-framed wheel.<sup id="cite_ref-Oleson_2000,_235_90-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oleson_2000,_235-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
