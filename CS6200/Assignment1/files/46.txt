<p>The <b>East Asian cultural sphere</b>, or the <b>Sinosphere</b>, consists of nations in <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> that were historically influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_culture" title="Chinese culture">Chinese culture</a>, including literary traditions and religions.<sup id="cite_ref-news.bbc.co.uk_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-news.bbc.co.uk-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Other names for the concept include the <b>Sinic world</b>, the <b>Confucian world</b>, the <b>Taoist world</b>, and the <b>ancient Chinese cultural sphere</b>, though the last name is also used to refer particularly to the <a href="/wiki/Sinophone" title="Sinophone">Sinophone</a> community (any place or neighbourhood inhabited by a significant minority of people who speak <a href="/wiki/Varieties_of_Chinese" title="Varieties of Chinese">varieties of Chinese</a>).
</p><p>However, the historical influence of ancient China has not just been confined to this narrow definition, because it has also spread to <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asian</a> countries like <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, through the establishment of significant <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Chinese" title="Overseas Chinese">overseas Chinese</a> populations and diaspora communities.
</p><p>The East Asian cultural sphere shares a <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucian</a> ethical philosophy, <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taoism" title="Taoism">Taoism</a>, and it historically has shared a <a href="/wiki/Adoption_of_Chinese_literary_culture" title="Adoption of Chinese literary culture">3,000-year-old ancient Han Chinese writing system</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-news.bbc.co.uk_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-news.bbc.co.uk-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The core regions of the East Asian cultural sphere are <a href="/wiki/Chinese_culture" title="Chinese culture">Greater China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Japan" title="Culture of Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Korea" title="Culture of Korea">Korea</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Vietnam" title="Culture of Vietnam">Vietnam</a> (CJKV, these are highlighted in dark blue in the image).
</p><p>The terms "East Asian cultural sphere" and "Chinese character (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_character" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese character">Hànzì</a>) cultural sphere" (see Chinese wikipedia) are used interchangeably with "Sinosphere" but they have different denotations and connotations.
</p><p>Also compare this with the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_world" title="Eastern world">Eastern world</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western world</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sino-Austronesian_languages" title="Sino-Austronesian languages">Sino-Austric languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Altaic_languages" title="Altaic languages">Altaic languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mongoloid" title="Mongoloid">Eastern Eurasian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austric_languages" title="Austric languages">Austric languages</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Asian_cultural_sphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>China has been regarded as one of the <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">centers of civilization</a>. The emergent cultures that arose from the migration of original Han settlers from the Yellow River is sometimes regarded as the starting point of the East Asian world. Nowadays, its population is around 2-2.5 billion (see <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_the_world" title="Demographics of the world">Demographics of the world</a>).
</p><p>The Japanese historian Nishijima Sadao (<a href="/w/index.php?title=%E8%A5%BF%E5%B6%8B%E5%AE%9A%E7%94%9F&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="西嶋定生 (page does not exist)">西嶋定生</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;&#91;<a href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E8%A5%BF%E5%B6%8B%E5%AE%9A%E7%94%9F" class="extiw" title="ja:西嶋定生">ja</a>&#93;</span>, 1919–1998), professor emeritus at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Tokyo" title="University of Tokyo">University of Tokyo</a>, originally coined the term 东亚文化圈 (later borrowed into Chinese, see Etymology). He conceived of a Chinese or East Asian cultural sphere distinct from cultures of the west. According to Nishijima, this cultural sphere shared the philosophy of Confucianism, the religion of Buddhism, and similar political and social structures. His cultural sphere includes China, Japan, Korea, Vietnam, stretching from areas between Mongolia and the Himalayas.<sup id="cite_ref-Fuchs_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fuchs-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cuisine">Cuisine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Asian_cultural_sphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cuisine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/East_Asian_cuisine" class="mw-redirect" title="East Asian cuisine">East Asian cuisine</a></div>
<p>The cuisine of East Asia shares many of the same ingredients and techniques. Chopsticks are used as an eating utensil in all of the core East Asian countries.<sup id="cite_ref-Davidson_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davidson-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The use of soy sauce, a sauce made from fermenting soy beans, is also widespread in East Asia. Rice is a main staple food in all of East Asia and is a major focus of <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">food security</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chern_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chern-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In East Asian countries, the word for 'cooked rice' can embody the meaning of food in general (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese" class="mw-redirect" title="Simplified Chinese">simplified Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans" title="Chinese language text">饭</span>; <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese" class="mw-redirect" title="Traditional Chinese">traditional Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant" title="Chinese language text">飯</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap;margin-right:.25em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Zh-f%C3%A0n.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" decoding="async" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a></span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2e/Zh-f%C3%A0n.ogg" class="internal" title="Zh-fàn.ogg">fàn</a></span></span>).<sup id="cite_ref-Davidson_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davidson-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Kimchi" title="Kimchi">Kimchi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sushi" title="Sushi">sushi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pho" title="Pho">phở</a>, sashimi, <a href="/wiki/Wasabi" title="Wasabi">wasabi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tea" title="Tea">tea</a>, noodles / ramen, udon, rice, <a href="/wiki/Hot_pot" title="Hot pot">hot pot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dumpling" title="Dumpling">dumplings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dim_sum" title="Dim sum">dimsum</a>, etc. are popular terms associated with East Asian cuisine (泡菜、寿司、面、米饭、火锅、茶、饺子、点心、等).
</p><p>Also see <a href="/wiki/Chinese_cuisine" title="Chinese cuisine">Chinese cuisine</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_Chinese_dishes" title="List of Chinese dishes">list of Chinese dishes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_cuisine" title="Vietnamese cuisine">Vietnamese cuisine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_cuisine" title="Japanese cuisine">Japanese cuisine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korean_cuisine" title="Korean cuisine">Korean cuisine</a>, etc.

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lion_Dance">Lion Dance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Asian_cultural_sphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Lion Dance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lion_dance" title="Lion dance">Lion Dance</a></div>
<p>The Lion Dance, is a form of traditional <a href="/wiki/Dance" title="Dance">dance</a> in <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Culture of China">Chinese culture</a> and other culturally East Asian countries in which performers mimic a lion's movements in a lion costume to bring good luck and fortune. Aside from China, versions of the lion dance are found in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>. Lion Dances are usually performed during <a href="/wiki/Chinese_New_Year" title="Chinese New Year">New Year</a>, religious and cultural celebrations.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Year">New Year</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Asian_cultural_sphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: New Year">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lunar_New_Year" title="Lunar New Year">Lunar New Year</a></div>
<p>Greater China, Japan, Korea, and Vietnam traditionally observe the same lunar new year. However, Japan has moved its New Year to fit the Western New Year since the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Restoration" title="Meiji Restoration">Meiji Restoration</a> while Korea later also moved to Western New Year since the 1970s.
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Shintoism">Shintoism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Asian_cultural_sphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Shintoism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Plum_trees_Kitano_Tenmangu.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Plum_trees_Kitano_Tenmangu.jpg/300px-Plum_trees_Kitano_Tenmangu.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="149" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Plum_trees_Kitano_Tenmangu.jpg/450px-Plum_trees_Kitano_Tenmangu.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Plum_trees_Kitano_Tenmangu.jpg/600px-Plum_trees_Kitano_Tenmangu.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4704" data-file-height="2340" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Plum_trees_Kitano_Tenmangu.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Two women praying in front of a Japanese Shinto shrine.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Shintoism" class="mw-redirect" title="Shintoism">Shintoism</a></div>
<p>Shintoism is the <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_religion" title="Ethnic religion">ethnic religion</a> of Japan. Shinto literally means "Way of the Gods". Shinto practitioners commonly affirm tradition, family, nature, cleanliness and ritual observation as core values.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Taoist influence is significant in their beliefs about nature and self-mastery. <a href="/wiki/Ritual_cleanliness" class="mw-redirect" title="Ritual cleanliness">Ritual cleanliness</a> is a central part of Shinto life.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Shinto_shrine" title="Shinto shrine">Shrines</a> have a significant place in Shinto, being places for the veneration of the <i><a href="/wiki/Kami" title="Kami">kami</a></i> (gods or spirits).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> "Folk", or "popular", Shinto features an emphasis on shamanism, particularly <a href="/wiki/Divination" title="Divination">divination</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spirit_possession" title="Spirit possession">spirit possession</a> and <a href="/wiki/Faith_healing" title="Faith healing">faith healing</a>. "Sect" Shinto is a diverse group including mountain-worshippers and Confucian Shinto schools.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Buddhism">Buddhism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Asian_cultural_sphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Buddhism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/East_Asian_Buddhism" title="East Asian Buddhism">East Asian Buddhism</a></div>
<p>The countries of China, Japan, Korea, and Vietnam share a history of Mahayana Buddhism. It is thought to have spread from India (perhaps Northeast India) via the Silk Road through Pakistan, Xinjiang, east as well as through SEA, Vietnam, then north through Guangzhou and Fujian. From China it proliferated to Korea and Japan, especially during the Tang dynasty (see Kukai). It could have also re-spread from China south to Vietnam. East Asia is now home to the largest Buddhist population in the world at around 200-400 million (see <a href="/wiki/Buddhism_by_country" title="Buddhism by country">Buddhism by country</a>; the top five are China, Thailand, Myanmar, Japan, Vietnam—three countries within the East Asian Cultural Sphere).
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Confucianism">Confucianism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Asian_cultural_sphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Confucianism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucianism</a></div>
<p>The countries of China, Japan, Korea, and Vietnam share a Confucian philosophical worldview.<sup id="cite_ref-Reischauer_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reischauer-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Confucianism is a <a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">humanistic</a><sup id="cite_ref-juergensmeyer_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-juergensmeyer-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> philosophy that believes that human beings are teachable, improvable and perfectible through personal and communal endeavour especially including self-cultivation and self-creation. Confucianism focuses on the cultivation of virtue and maintenance of ethics, the most basic of which are <i><a href="/wiki/Ren_(Confucianism)" title="Ren (Confucianism)">rén</a></i> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">仁</span>), <i><a href="/wiki/Yi_(Confucianism)" title="Yi (Confucianism)">yì</a></i> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">义</span>/<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">義</span>), and <i><a href="/wiki/Li_(Confucian)" class="mw-redirect" title="Li (Confucian)">lǐ</a></i> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">礼</span>/<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">禮</span>).<sup id="cite_ref-craig_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-craig-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> <i>Ren</i> is an obligation of <a href="/wiki/Altruism" title="Altruism">altruism</a> and humaneness for other individuals, <i>yi</i> is the upholding of righteousness and the moral disposition to do good, and <i>li</i> is a system of norms and propriety that determines how a person should properly act in everyday life.<sup id="cite_ref-craig_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-craig-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Neo-Confucianism">Neo-Confucianism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Asian_cultural_sphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Neo-Confucianism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Neo-Confucianism" title="Neo-Confucianism">Neo-Confucianism</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mid-Imperial_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Mid-Imperial China">Mid-Imperial Chinese</a> philosophy is primarily defined by the development of <a href="/wiki/Neo-Confucianism" title="Neo-Confucianism">Neo-Confucianism</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang dynasty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a> from <a href="/wiki/Buddhism_in_Nepal" title="Buddhism in Nepal">Nepal</a> also became a prominent philosophical and religious discipline. Neo-Confucianism has its origins in the <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang dynasty</a>; the Confucianist scholar <a href="/wiki/Han_Yu" title="Han Yu">Han Yu</a> is seen as a forebear of the Neo-Confucianists of the Song dynasty.<sup id="cite_ref-huang_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-huang-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The Song dynasty philosopher <a href="/wiki/Zhou_Dunyi" title="Zhou Dunyi">Zhou Dunyi</a> is seen as the first true "pioneer" of Neo-Confucianism, using Daoist metaphysics as a framework for his ethical philosophy.<sup id="cite_ref-chan_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chan-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Elsewhere in East Asia, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_philosophy" title="Japanese philosophy">Japanese philosophy</a> began to develop as indigenous <a href="/wiki/Shinto" title="Shinto">Shinto</a> beliefs fused with <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucianism</a> and other schools of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_philosophy" title="Chinese philosophy">Chinese philosophy</a>. Similar to Japan, in <a href="/wiki/Korean_philosophy" title="Korean philosophy">Korean philosophy</a> elements of <a href="/wiki/Korean_Shamanism" class="mw-redirect" title="Korean Shamanism">Shamanism</a> were integrated into the <a href="/wiki/Korean_Confucianism" title="Korean Confucianism">Neo-Confucianism</a> imported from China. In Vietnam, neo-Confucianism was developed into Vietnamese own Tam giáo as well, along with indigenous Vietnamese beliefs and <a href="/wiki/Mahayana_Buddhism" class="mw-redirect" title="Mahayana Buddhism">Mahayana Buddhism</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_religions">Other religions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Asian_cultural_sphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Other religions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<li>Islam, see Xinjiang, <a href="/wiki/Islam_in_China" title="Islam in China">Muslims in China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Islam_in_Hong_Kong" title="Islam in Hong Kong">Islam in Hong Kong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Islam_in_Japan" title="Islam in Japan">Islam in Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Islam_in_Korea" title="Islam in Korea">Islam in Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Islam_in_Vietnam" title="Islam in Vietnam">Islam in Vietnam</a>.</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Asian_cultural_sphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_Linguistics">Historical Linguistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Asian_cultural_sphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Historical Linguistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Various languages are thought to have originated in East Asia and have various degrees of influence on each other. These include:
</p>
<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Altaic_languages" title="Altaic languages">Altaic</a>: proposed to include the <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">Turkic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Mongolian languages">Mongolian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tungusic_languages" title="Tungusic languages">Tungusic</a> language families; and possibly also the <a href="/wiki/Japonic_languages" title="Japonic languages">Japonic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Koreanic_languages" title="Koreanic languages">Koreanic</a> families, and the <a href="/wiki/Ainu_language" title="Ainu language">Ainu language</a>. thought to have originated around Xinjiang or in the Eurasian steppe.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sino-Tibetan_languages" title="Sino-Tibetan languages">Sino-Tibetan</a>: thought to have originated around the <a href="/wiki/Yellow_River" title="Yellow River">Yellow River</a> north of the <a href="/wiki/Yangtze" title="Yangtze">Yangzi</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> These include the Chinese languages, Tibetan, <a href="/wiki/Burmese_language" title="Burmese language">Burmese</a>, etc. and are thought to have spread from north China to the southwest.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Austronesian_languages" title="Austronesian languages">Austronesian</a>: from Southern China then to Taiwan then throughout SEA, Madagascar, and Polynesia</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Austroasiatic_languages" title="Austroasiatic languages">Austroasiatic</a>: also from Southern China to Vietnam and Cambodia</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kra%E2%80%93Dai_languages" title="Kra–Dai languages">Kra-Dai</a>: from Southern China to Thailand and Laos</li></ol>
<p>The core Languages of the East Asian Cultural Sphere generally include the <a href="/wiki/Varieties_of_Chinese" title="Varieties of Chinese">Chinese languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_language" title="Vietnamese language">Vietnamese</a>. All of these languages have a well documented history of having historically used Chinese characters and Japanese, Korean, and Vietnamese all have roughly 60% of their vocabulary stemming from Chinese.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> There is a small set of minor languages that are comparable to the core East Asian languages such as <a href="/wiki/Zhuang_languages" title="Zhuang languages">Zhuang</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hmong%E2%80%93Mien_languages" title="Hmong–Mien languages">Hmong-Mien</a>. They are often overlooked since neither have their own country or heavily export their culture, but Zhuang has been written in hanzi inspired characters called <a href="/wiki/Sawndip" title="Sawndip">Sawndip</a> for over 1000 years. Hmong while having supposedly lacked a writing system until modern history is also suggested to have a similar percentage of Chinese loans to the core CJKV languages as well.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While other languages have been impacted by the Sinosphere such as the <a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a> with its <a href="/wiki/Thai_numerals" title="Thai numerals">Thai numeral system</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_language" title="Mongolian language">Mongolian</a> with its historical use of hanzi: the amount of Chinese vocabulary overall is not nearly as expansive in these languages as the core CJKV, or even Zhuang and Hmong.
</p><p>There are various hypotheses trying to unify various subsets of the above languages, including the <a href="/wiki/Sino-Austronesian_languages" title="Sino-Austronesian languages">Sino-Austronesian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Austric_languages" title="Austric languages">Austric</a> language groupings. An overview of these various language groups is discussed in <a href="/wiki/Jared_Diamond" title="Jared Diamond">Jared Diamond's</a> <a href="/wiki/Guns,_Germs,_and_Steel" title="Guns, Germs, and Steel"><i>Germs, Guns, and Steel</i></a>, among other places.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_Systems">Writing Systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Asian_cultural_sphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Writing Systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>East Asia is quite diverse in <a href="/wiki/Writing_system" title="Writing system">writing systems</a>, from the Brahmic inspired <a href="/wiki/Abugida" title="Abugida">abugidas</a> of SEA, the logographic hanzi of China, the syllabaries of Japan, and various alphabets and abjads used in Korea (Hangul), Mongolia (Cyrillic), Vietnam (Latin), Indonesia (Latin / Arabic), etc.
</p>

<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Chinese_characters">Chinese characters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Asian_cultural_sphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Chinese characters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chinese_characters" title="Chinese characters">Chinese characters</a></div>
<p>Hanzi (漢字 or 汉字) is considered the cultural glue that unifies the languages and cultures of many East Asian nations. Historically, Japan, Korea, and Vietnam have used Chinese characters. Today, they are mainly used in China, Japan, South Korea, and Singapore albeit in different forms.
</p><p>Mainland China and Singapore use <a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified characters</a>, whereas Taiwan, Hong Kong and Macau use <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional</a>.
</p><p>Japan still uses <a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">kanji</a> but has also invented <a href="/wiki/Kana" title="Kana">kana</a>, inspired by <a href="/wiki/Cursive_script_(East_Asia)" title="Cursive script (East Asia)">cursive forms</a> of Chinese.
</p><p>Korea used to write in <a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">hanja</a> but has invented an alphabetic system called <a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">hangul</a> (also inspired by Chinese and <a href="/wiki/%CA%BCPhags-pa_script" title="ʼPhags-pa script">phags-pa</a> during the Mongol Empire) that is nowadays the majority script. However hanja is a required subject in South Korea. Names are also written in hanja. Hanja is also studied and used in academia, newspapers, and law; areas where a lot of scholarly terms and <a href="/wiki/Sino-Korean_vocabulary" title="Sino-Korean vocabulary">Sino-Korean cognates</a> are used and necessary to distinguish between otherwise ambiguous homonyms.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:FlowRoot3824.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/FlowRoot3824.svg/220px-FlowRoot3824.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="133" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/FlowRoot3824.svg/330px-FlowRoot3824.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/FlowRoot3824.svg/440px-FlowRoot3824.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="2888" data-file-height="1743" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:FlowRoot3824.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Development of kana from Chinese characters</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Map-Chinese_Characters.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Map-Chinese_Characters.png/220px-Map-Chinese_Characters.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="142" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Map-Chinese_Characters.png/330px-Map-Chinese_Characters.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Map-Chinese_Characters.png/440px-Map-Chinese_Characters.png 2x" data-file-width="1971" data-file-height="1269" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Map-Chinese_Characters.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Country where Han character is/was used</div></div></div>
<p>Vietnam used to write in <a href="/wiki/History_of_writing_in_Vietnam" title="History of writing in Vietnam">chữ Hán</a> or <a href="/wiki/Classical_Chinese" title="Classical Chinese">Classical Chinese</a>. Since the 8th century they began inventing many of their own <a href="/wiki/Ch%E1%BB%AF_N%C3%B4m" title="Chữ Nôm">chữ Nôm</a>. Since French colonization, they have switched to using a modified version of the Latin alphabet called <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_alphabet" title="Vietnamese alphabet">chữ Quốc ngữ</a>. However, Chinese characters still hold a special place in the cultures as their history and literature have been greatly influenced by Chinese characters. In Vietnam (and North Korea), hanzi can be seen in temples, cemeteries, and monuments today, as well as serving as decorative motifs in art and design. And there are movements to restore Hán Nôm in Vietnam. (Also see <a href="/wiki/History_of_writing_in_Vietnam" title="History of writing in Vietnam">History of writing in Vietnam</a>.)
</p><p>Zhuang are similar to the Vietnamese in that they used to write in Sawgun (Chinese characters) and have invented many of their own characters called <a href="/wiki/Sawndip" title="Sawndip">Sawndip</a> (Immature characters or native characters). Sawndip is still used informally and in traditional settings, but in 1957, the People's Republic of China introduced an alphabetical script for the language, which is what it officially promotes.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Asian_cultural_sphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Adoption_of_Chinese_literary_culture" title="Adoption of Chinese literary culture">Adoption of Chinese literary culture</a></div>
<p>East Asian literary culture was based on the use of <a href="/wiki/Literary_Chinese" class="mw-redirect" title="Literary Chinese">Literary Chinese</a>, which became the medium of scholarship and government across the region. Although each of these countries developed vernacular writing systems and used them for popular literature, they continued to use Chinese for all formal writing until it was swept away by rising nationalism around the end of the 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-Kornicki_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kornicki-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Throughout East Asia, Literary Chinese was the language of administration and scholarship. Although Vietnam, Korea and Japan each developed writing systems for their own languages, these were limited to popular literature. Chinese remained the medium of formal writing until it was displaced by vernacular writing in the late 19th and early 20th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKornicki201166–67_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKornicki201166–67-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Though they did not use Chinese for spoken communication, each country had its own tradition of reading texts aloud, the so-called <a href="/wiki/Sino-Xenic_pronunciations" title="Sino-Xenic pronunciations">Sino-Xenic pronunciations</a>, which provide clues to the pronunciation of <a href="/wiki/Middle_Chinese" title="Middle Chinese">Middle Chinese</a>. Chinese words with these pronunciations were also borrowed extensively into the local vernaculars, and today comprise over half their vocabularies.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiyake200498–99_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiyake200498–99-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Books in Literary Chinese were widely distributed. By the 7th century and possibly earlier, <a href="/wiki/Woodblock_printing" title="Woodblock printing">woodblock printing</a> had been developed in China. At first, it was used only to copy the Buddhist scriptures, but later secular works were also printed. By the 13th century, metal <a href="/wiki/Movable_type" title="Movable type">movable type</a> was used by government printers in Korea, but seems to have not been extensively used in China, Vietnam, or Japan. At the same time manuscript reproduction remained important until the late 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKornicki201168_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKornicki201168-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy_and_trade">Economy and trade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Asian_cultural_sphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Economy and trade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Commerce" title="Commerce">business</a> cultures of Sinosphere countries in some ways are heavily influenced by Confucianism.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Important in China is the social concept of 关系 or <a href="/wiki/Guanxi" title="Guanxi">guanxi</a>. This has influenced the societies of Korea and Japan as well.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Japan features hierarchically-organized <a href="/wiki/Company" title="Company">companies</a> and the Japanese place a high value on <a href="/wiki/Interpersonal_relationship" title="Interpersonal relationship">relationships</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Japanese_work_environment" title="Japanese work environment">Japanese work environment</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Korean <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">businesses</a> also adhere to Confucian values, and are structured around a <a href="/wiki/Patriarchy" title="Patriarchy">patriarchal</a> family governed by <a href="/wiki/Filial_piety" title="Filial piety">filial piety</a> (孝顺) between management and a company's employees.<sup id="cite_ref-Luong_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Luong-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Before <a href="/wiki/European_imperialism" class="mw-redirect" title="European imperialism">European imperialism</a>, East Asia has always been one of the largest economies in the world, whose output had mostly been driven by China and the <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road" title="Silk Road">Silk Road</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, East Asia modernized and became an area of economic power starting with the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Restoration" title="Meiji Restoration">Meiji Restoration</a> in the late 19th century when Japan rapidly transformed itself into the only industrial power outside the North Atlantic area.<sup id="cite_ref-Ikeo_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ikeo-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Japan's early industrial economy reached its height in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> (1939-1945) when it expanded its empire and became a major world power.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Post_WW2_.28Tiger_economies.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Post_WW2_(Tiger_economies)">Post WW2 (Tiger economies)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Asian_cultural_sphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Post WW2 (Tiger economies)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Following Japanese defeat, economic collapse after the war, and <a href="/wiki/Occupation_of_Japan" title="Occupation of Japan">US military occupation</a>, Japan's economy recovered in the 1950s with the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_post-war_economic_miracle" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese post-war economic miracle">post-war economic miracle</a> in which rapid growth propelled the country to become the world's second largest economy by the 1980s.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Since the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a> and again under <a href="/wiki/Division_of_Korea#US_occupation_of_South_Korea" title="Division of Korea">US military occupation</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> has experienced its own postwar economic miracle called the <a href="/wiki/Miracle_on_the_Han_River" title="Miracle on the Han River">Miracle on the Han River</a>, with the rise of global tech industrial leaders like Samsung, LG, etc. As of 2019 its economy is 4th largest in Asia and 11th largest in the world.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/British_Hong_Kong" title="British Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> became one of the <a href="/wiki/Four_Asian_Tigers" title="Four Asian Tigers">Four Asian Tiger</a> economies, developing strong textile and manufacturing economies.<sup id="cite_ref-Harrington_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrington-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> South Korea followed a similar route, developing a textile industry.<sup id="cite_ref-Harrington_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrington-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Following in the footsteps of Hong Kong and Korea, Taiwan and <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> quickly industrialized through government policies. By 1997 the four Asian Tiger economies joined Japan as among East Asia's developed economies.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>As of 2019, South Korean and Japanese growth have stagnated (also see <a href="/wiki/Lost_Decade_(Japan)" title="Lost Decade (Japan)">Lost Decade</a>), and present growth in East Asia has now shifted to China and to the <a href="/wiki/Tiger_Cub_Economies" title="Tiger Cub Economies">Tiger Cub Economies</a> of Southeast Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Modern_era_.28China_and_SEA.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_era_(China_and_SEA)">Modern era (China and SEA)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Asian_cultural_sphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Modern era (China and SEA)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Since the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_economic_reform" title="Chinese economic reform">Reform and Opening Up</a> (改革开放) China has become the 2nd-largest economy in the world respectively by nominal GDP.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Nowadays the <a href="/wiki/Pearl_River_Delta" title="Pearl River Delta">Pearl River Delta</a> along with nearby Hong Kong and <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a>, is one of the top startup regions (comparable with <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai</a>) in East Asia, featuring some of the world's top <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">drone</a> companies like <a href="/wiki/DJI_(company)" title="DJI (company)">DJI</a>, among other things.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Up until the early 2010s, Vietnamese trade was heavily dependent on China, and many Chinese-Vietnamese speak both Cantonese and Vietnamese, which share many linguistic similarities. Vietnam, one of <a href="/wiki/Jim_O%27Neill,_Baron_O%27Neill_of_Gatley#Next_Eleven" title="Jim O&#39;Neill, Baron O&#39;Neill of Gatley">Next Eleven</a> countries as of 2005<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=East_Asian_cultural_sphere&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, is regarded as a rising economic <a href="/wiki/Power_(international_relations)" title="Power (international relations)">power</a> in Southeast Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>East Asia participates in numerous global economic organizations including:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>The <a href="/wiki/History_of_East_Asia" title="History of East Asia">History of East Asia</a> is also important in understanding the mutual interactions between various East Asian people and can also better point to what it means to be "East Asian."
</p><p>During WW2 and the Imperial Japanese Era, there were ideas of <a href="/wiki/Pan-Asianism" title="Pan-Asianism">Pan-Asianism</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Greater_East_Asia_Co-Prosperity_Sphere" title="Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere">Greater East Asian Co-prosperity sphere</a>. <h2><span id="Etymology_of_.27Sinosphere.27"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_of_'Sinosphere'">Etymology of 'Sinosphere'</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Asian_cultural_sphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Etymology of &#039;Sinosphere&#039;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>Sinosphere</i> is sometimes used as a synonym for the East Asian cultural sphere. The <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymology</a> of <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Sinosphere" class="extiw" title="wikt:Sinosphere">Sinosphere</a></i> is from <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Sino-" class="extiw" title="wikt:Sino-">Sino-</a></i> "China; Chinese" (cf. <a href="/wiki/Sinophone" title="Sinophone">Sinophone</a>) and <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-sphere" class="extiw" title="wikt:-sphere">-sphere</a></i> in the sense of "<a href="/wiki/Sphere_of_influence" title="Sphere of influence">sphere of influence</a>", "area influenced by a country".
</p><p>The "<a href="/wiki/CJK" class="mw-redirect" title="CJK">CJK</a>V" languages—Chinese, Japanese, Korean and Vietnamese—translate the English <i>-sphere</i> as Chinese <i>quān</i> <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%9C%88" class="extiw" title="wikt:圈">圈</a></span> "circle; ring; corral; pen", Japanese <i>ken</i> 圏けん "sphere; circle; range; radius", Korean <i>gwon</i> 권 and Vietnamese <i>quyển</i>, all of which are cognates.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Victor_H._Mair" title="Victor H. Mair">Victor H. Mair</a> discussed the origins of these "culture sphere" terms.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Chinese <i><i lang="zh-Latn" title="Chinese-language romanization">wénhuà quān</i></i> <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">文化圈</span> dates back to a 1941 translation for German <i><a href="/wiki/Kulturkreis" title="Kulturkreis">Kulturkreis</a></i> "culture circle/field", which the Austrian <a href="/wiki/Ethnologists" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethnologists">ethnologists</a> <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Graebner" title="Fritz Graebner">Fritz Graebner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Schmidt_(linguist)" title="Wilhelm Schmidt (linguist)">Wilhelm Schmidt</a> proposed. Japanese historian Nishijima Sadao coined the expressions <i>Kanji bunka ken</i><span style="font-weight: normal">&#32;(<span lang="ja">漢字文化圏</span>, "Chinese-character culture sphere")</span> and <i>Chuka bunka ken</i><span style="font-weight: normal">&#32;(<span lang="ja">中華文化圏</span>, "Chinese culture sphere")</span>, which China later re-borrowed as <a href="/wiki/Loanwords" class="mw-redirect" title="Loanwords">loanwords</a>. Nishijima devised these Sinitic "cultural spheres" within his "Theory of an East Asian World"<span style="font-weight: normal">&#32;(<span lang="ja">東アジア世界論</span><span style="display:none">,</span>&#32;<i>Higashi Ajia sekai-ron</i>)</span>.
</p><p>Chinese-English dictionaries give similar translations of this keyword <i><i lang="zh-Latn" title="Chinese-language romanization">wénhuà quān</i></i> <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">文化圈</span>: "the intellectual or literary circles" (<a href="/wiki/Liang_Shiqiu" class="mw-redirect" title="Liang Shiqiu">Liang Shiqiu</a> 1975) and "literary, educational circles" (<a href="/wiki/Lin_Yutang" title="Lin Yutang">Lin Yutang</a> 1972).
</p><p>The Sinosphere may be taken to be synonymous to <a href="/wiki/History_of_China#Ancient_China" title="History of China">Ancient China</a> and its descendant civilizations as well as the "Far Eastern civilizations" (the Mainland and the Japanese ones). In the 1930s in <i><a href="/wiki/A_Study_of_History" title="A Study of History">A Study of History</a></i>, the Sinosphere along with the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western</a>, Islamic, Eastern Orthodox, Indic, etc. civilizations is presented as among the major "units of study."<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparisons_with_the_West">Comparisons with the West</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Asian_cultural_sphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Comparisons with the West">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The British historian <a href="/wiki/Arnold_J._Toynbee" title="Arnold J. Toynbee">Arnold J. Toynbee</a> listed the Far Eastern civilization as one of the main civilizations outlined in his book, <i>A Study of History</i>. He included Japan and Korea in his definition of "Far Eastern civilization" and proposed that they grew out of the "Sinic civilization" that originated in the <a href="/wiki/Yellow_River" title="Yellow River">Yellow River</a> basin.<sup id="cite_ref-Sun_p.154_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sun_p.154-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Toynbee compared the relationship between the Sinic and Far Eastern civilization with that of the Hellenic and Western civilizations, which had an "apparentation-affiliation."<sup id="cite_ref-Sun_p.188_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sun_p.188-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The American <a href="/wiki/Sinologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Sinologist">Sinologist</a> and historian <a href="/wiki/Edwin_O._Reischauer" title="Edwin O. Reischauer">Edwin O. Reischauer</a> also grouped China, Korea, and Japan together into a cultural sphere that he called the <i>Sinic world</i>. These countries are centralized states that share a Confucian ethical philosophy. Reischauer states that this culture originated in Northern China, and compared the relationship between Northern China and East Asia to that of Greco-Roman civilization and Europe. The elites of East Asia were tied together through a common written language based on Chinese characters, much in the way that <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> had functioned in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-Reischauer_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reischauer-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The American political scientist <a href="/wiki/Samuel_P._Huntington" title="Samuel P. Huntington">Samuel P. Huntington</a> considered the Sinic world as one of many civilizations in his book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Clash_of_Civilizations" class="mw-redirect" title="The Clash of Civilizations">The Clash of Civilizations</a></i>. He notes that "all scholars recognize the existence of either a single distinct Chinese civilization dating back to at least 1500 B.C. and perhaps a thousand years earlier, or of two Chinese civilizations one succeeding the other in the early centuries of the Christian epoch."<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Huntington's Sinic civilization includes China, North Korea, South Korea, Mongolia, Vietnam, and Chinese communities in Southeast Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-Davis_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davis-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> Of the many civilizations that Huntington discusses, the Sinic world is the only one that is based on a cultural, rather than religious, identity.<sup id="cite_ref-Blinnikov_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blinnikov-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Huntington's theory was that in a post-<a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> world, humanity "identify with cultural groups: tribes, ethnic groups, religious communities, and at the broadest level, civilizations."<sup id="cite_ref-Sun_p.032_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sun_p.032-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gusterson_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gusterson-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
