	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Wright</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This article is about the surname. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Wright_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Wright (disambiguation)">Wright (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<table class="infobox" style="width:22em"><caption>Wright</caption><tbody><tr><th colspan="2" style="text-align:center;background:#ADD8E6">Origin</th></tr><tr><th scope="row">Meaning</th><td>Worker, maker</td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Region of origin</th><td>England</td></tr><tr><td colspan="2" style="text-align:center;border-top: #a2a9b1 1px solid;">Frequency Comparisons<sup id="cite_ref-BDF_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BDF-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><b>Wright</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Occupational_surname" class="mw-redirect" title="Occupational surname">occupational surname</a> originating in England.<sup id="cite_ref-BDF_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BDF-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The term 'Wright' comes from the circa 700 AD <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> word 'wryhta' or 'wyrhta', meaning worker or shaper of wood. Later it became any occupational worker<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> (for example, a <a href="/wiki/Shipwright" class="mw-redirect" title="Shipwright">shipwright</a> is a person who builds ships), and is used as a British <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family name</a>.
</p><p>The word's use as an occupational title continued until the mid-19th century, often combined with other words such as in shipwright, <a href="/wiki/Wheelwright" title="Wheelwright">wheelwright</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Wainwright" title="Wainwright">wainwright</a> and <a href="/wiki/Playwright" title="Playwright">playwright</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> As of 2014<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Wright&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup><i>, Wright</i> was the eleventh most common surname in England.<sup id="cite_ref-BDF_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BDF-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The word <a href="/wiki/Carpenter_(surname)" title="Carpenter (surname)"><i>carpentier</i>, now "carpenter"</a>, was introduced into England in the years after the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">Norman conquest</a> in 1066 and slowly replaced the traditional name and meaning of wright in most of England. 'Wright' is still used in Scottish English in the original meaning of 'skilled woodworker'. The Incorporation of Wrights of the Trades House of Glasgow,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and the Incorporation of Wrights and Masons of Edinburgh Trades<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> retain the word in its original meaning in their role of promoting the woodworking trade.
</p><p>Wright is also an <a href="/wiki/Anglicisation" title="Anglicisation">anglicised</a> version of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scots Gaelic</a> <a href="/wiki/Scottish_clan" title="Scottish clan">clan</a> name "<a href="/wiki/MacIntyre" title="MacIntyre">MacIntyre</a>" or "Mac an t-Saoir", meaning "son of the wright" (son of the carpenter).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
In Ireland, the native Gaelic Mac an Cheairt <a href="/wiki/Sept" title="Sept">sept</a> of <a href="/wiki/County_Mayo" title="County Mayo">County Mayo</a> occasionally changed their name to Wright. This is a literal translation meaning, "son of the right or righteous".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
