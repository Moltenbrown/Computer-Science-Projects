	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Latin translations of the 12th century</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Latin translations" redirects here. For contemporary translations of works into Latin, see <a href="/wiki/List_of_Latin_translations_of_modern_literature" title="List of Latin translations of modern literature">List of Latin translations of modern literature</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:De_Ludiciis_Natiuitatum_Albohali_Nuremberg_1546.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/De_Ludiciis_Natiuitatum_Albohali_Nuremberg_1546.jpg/220px-De_Ludiciis_Natiuitatum_Albohali_Nuremberg_1546.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="137" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/De_Ludiciis_Natiuitatum_Albohali_Nuremberg_1546.jpg/330px-De_Ludiciis_Natiuitatum_Albohali_Nuremberg_1546.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/De_Ludiciis_Natiuitatum_Albohali_Nuremberg_1546.jpg/440px-De_Ludiciis_Natiuitatum_Albohali_Nuremberg_1546.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="1917" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:De_Ludiciis_Natiuitatum_Albohali_Nuremberg_1546.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Albohali" class="mw-redirect" title="Albohali">Albohali</a>'s <i>De Iudiciis Natiuitatum</i> was translated into Latin by <a href="/wiki/Plato_of_Tivoli" class="mw-redirect" title="Plato of Tivoli">Plato of Tivoli</a> in 1136, and again by <a href="/wiki/John_of_Seville" title="John of Seville">John of Seville</a> in 1153.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Here is the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> edition of <a href="/wiki/John_of_Seville" title="John of Seville">John of Seville</a>'s translation, 1546.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Latin translations of the 12th century</b> were spurred by a major search by European scholars for new learning unavailable in western Europe <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_of_the_12th_century" title="Renaissance of the 12th century">at the time</a>; their search led them to areas of southern Europe, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Taifa_of_Toledo" title="Taifa of Toledo">central Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sicily#Arab_Sicily_(965–1072)" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>, which recently had come under Christian rule following their reconquest in the late 11th century. These areas had been under a <a href="/wiki/Muslim" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> rule for considerable time, and still had substantial Arabic-speaking populations to support their search.  The combination of Muslim accumulated knowledge, substantial numbers of Arabic-speaking scholars, and the new Christian rulers made these areas intellectually attractive, as well as culturally and politically accessible to <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> scholars.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> A typical story is that of <a href="/wiki/Gerard_of_Cremona" title="Gerard of Cremona">Gerard of Cremona</a> (c. 1114–87), who is said to have made his way to Toledo, well after its reconquest by Christians in 1085, because he<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>arrived at a knowledge of each part of [philosophy] according to the study of the Latins, nevertheless, because of his love for the <i><a href="/wiki/Almagest" title="Almagest">Almagest</a></i>, which he did not find at all amongst the Latins, he made his way to <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Spain" title="Toledo, Spain">Toledo</a>, where seeing an abundance of books in Arabic on every subject, and pitying the poverty he had experienced among the Latins concerning these subjects, out of his desire to translate he thoroughly learnt the Arabic language....
</p></blockquote>
<p>While Muslims were busy translating and adding their own ideas to Greek philosophies, the Latin West had been suspicious of pagan ideas. <a href="/wiki/St._Jerome" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Jerome">St. Jerome</a>, for example, was hostile to <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, and <a href="/wiki/St._Augustine" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Augustine">St. Augustine</a> had little interest in exploring philosophy, only applying logic to theology.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  For centuries, Greek ideas in Europe west were all but non-existent. Only a few monasteries had Greek works, and even fewer of them copied these works.<sup id="cite_ref-Laughlin_139_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Laughlin_139-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There was a brief period of revival, when the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon">Anglo-Saxon</a> monk <a href="/wiki/Alcuin" title="Alcuin">Alcuin</a> and others reintroduced some Greek ideas during the <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_Renaissance" title="Carolingian Renaissance">Carolingian Renaissance</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> After <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a>'s death, however, intellectual life again fell into decline.<sup id="cite_ref-char_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-char-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Excepting a few persons promoting <a href="/wiki/Boethius" title="Boethius">Boethius</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Gerbert_of_Aurillac" class="mw-redirect" title="Gerbert of Aurillac">Gerbert of Aurillac</a>, philosophical thought was developed little in Europe for about two centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-char_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-char-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> By the 12th century, however, <a href="/wiki/Scholasticism" title="Scholasticism">scholastic</a> thought was beginning to develop, leading to the rise of <a href="/wiki/Universities" class="mw-redirect" title="Universities">universities</a> throughout Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-schol_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-schol-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> These universities gathered what little Greek thought had been preserved over the centuries, including Boethius' commentaries on Aristotle. They also served as places of discussion for new ideas coming from new translations from Arabic throughout Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-schol_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-schol-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By the 12th century, European fear of Islam as a military threat had lessened somewhat. <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Spain" title="Toledo, Spain">Toledo</a>, in Spain, had fallen from Arab hands in 1085, Sicily in 1091, and Jerusalem in 1099.<sup id="cite_ref-watt_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-watt-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-conq_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-conq-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> These linguistic borderlands proved fertile ground for translators. These areas had been conquered by Arab, Greek and Latin-speaking peoples over the centuries and contained linguistic abilities from all these cultures.  The small and unscholarly population of the <a href="/wiki/Crusader_Kingdoms" class="mw-redirect" title="Crusader Kingdoms">Crusader Kingdoms</a>  contributed very little to the translation efforts, until the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Crusade" title="Fourth Crusade">Fourth Crusade</a> took most of the Byzantine Empire. Sicily, still largely Greek-speaking, was more productive; it had seen rule under Byzantines, Arabs, and Italians, and many were fluent in Greek, Arabic, and Latin. Sicilians, however, were less influenced by Arabs and instead are noted more for their translations directly from Greek to Latin.<sup id="cite_ref-conq_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-conq-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Spain, on the other hand, was an ideal place for translation from Arabic to Latin because of a combination of rich Latin and Arab cultures living side by side.<sup id="cite_ref-conq_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-conq-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Unlike the interest in the literature and history of <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">classical antiquity</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>, 12th century translators sought new <a href="/wiki/Islamic_science" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic science">scientific</a>, <a href="/wiki/Early_Islamic_philosophy" title="Early Islamic philosophy">philosophical</a> and, to a lesser extent, religious texts.  The latter concern was reflected in a renewed interest in translations of the Greek <a href="/wiki/Church_Fathers" title="Church Fathers">Church Fathers</a> into <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, a concern with translating Jewish teachings from <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>, an interest in the <a href="/wiki/Qur%27an" class="mw-redirect" title="Qur&#39;an">Qur'an</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islamic</a> religious texts.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In addition, some <a href="/wiki/Arabic_literature" title="Arabic literature">Arabic literature</a> was also translated into Latin.<sup id="cite_ref-Irwin_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Irwin-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Translators_in_Italy">Translators in Italy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Latin_translations_of_the_12th_century&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Translators in Italy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Just before the burst of translations in the 12th century, <a href="/wiki/Constantine_the_African" title="Constantine the African">Constantine the African</a>, a Christian from <a href="/wiki/Carthage" title="Carthage">Carthage</a> who studied medicine in <a href="/wiki/History_of_Arab_Egypt" class="mw-redirect" title="History of Arab Egypt">Egypt</a> and ultimately became a monk at the monastery of <a href="/wiki/Monte_Cassino" title="Monte Cassino">Monte Cassino</a> in Italy, translated <a href="/wiki/Islamic_medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic medicine">medical works</a> from Arabic. Constantine's many translations included <a href="/wiki/Ali_ibn_Abbas_al-Majusi" class="mw-redirect" title="Ali ibn Abbas al-Majusi">Ali ibn Abbas al-Majusi</a>'s medical encyclopedia <i><a href="/wiki/Liber_pantegni" title="Liber pantegni">The Complete Book of the Medical Art</a></i> (as <i>Liber pantegni</i>),<sup id="cite_ref-Bieber_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bieber-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
the ancient medicine of <a href="/wiki/Hippocrates" title="Hippocrates">Hippocrates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galen" title="Galen">Galen</a> as adapted by <a href="/wiki/Islamic_medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic medicine">Arabic physicians</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
and the <i>Isagoge ad Tegni Galeni</i><sup id="cite_ref-Danielle-981_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Danielle-981-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Hunayn_ibn_Ishaq" title="Hunayn ibn Ishaq">Hunayn ibn Ishaq</a> (Johannitius) and his nephew Hubaysh ibn al-Hasan.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
Other medical works he translated include <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Israeli_ben_Solomon" title="Isaac Israeli ben Solomon">Isaac Israeli ben Solomon</a>'s <i>Liber febribus, Liber de dietis universalibus et particularibus</i> and <i>Liber de urinis</i>; Ishaq ibn Imran's <a href="/wiki/Islamic_psychology" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic psychology">psychological</a> work <i>al-Maqala fi al-Malikhukiya</i> as <i>De melancolia</i>; and <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Al-Jazzar" class="mw-redirect" title="Ibn Al-Jazzar">Ibn Al-Jazzar</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/De_Gradibus" title="De Gradibus">De Gradibus</a>, Viaticum, Liber de stomacho, De elephantiasi, De coitu</i> and <i>De oblivione</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Danielle-981_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Danielle-981-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> had been part of the Byzantine Empire until 878, was under <a href="/wiki/Emirate_of_Sicily" title="Emirate of Sicily">Muslim control</a> from 878–1060, and came under Norman control between 1060 and 1090.  As a consequence the Norman <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Sicily" title="Kingdom of Sicily">Kingdom of Sicily</a> maintained a trilingual bureaucracy, which made it an ideal place for translations.  Sicily also maintained relations with the <a href="/wiki/Greek_East" class="mw-redirect" title="Greek East">Greek East</a>, which allowed for exchange of ideas and manuscripts.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ibn_Butlan_Receuil_de_Sante_Rhenanie_2nd_half_15th_century.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Ibn_Butlan_Receuil_de_Sante_Rhenanie_2nd_half_15th_century.jpg/220px-Ibn_Butlan_Receuil_de_Sante_Rhenanie_2nd_half_15th_century.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="184" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Ibn_Butlan_Receuil_de_Sante_Rhenanie_2nd_half_15th_century.jpg/330px-Ibn_Butlan_Receuil_de_Sante_Rhenanie_2nd_half_15th_century.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Ibn_Butlan_Receuil_de_Sante_Rhenanie_2nd_half_15th_century.jpg/440px-Ibn_Butlan_Receuil_de_Sante_Rhenanie_2nd_half_15th_century.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1286" data-file-height="1075" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ibn_Butlan_Receuil_de_Sante_Rhenanie_2nd_half_15th_century.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Ibn_Butlan" title="Ibn Butlan">Ibn Butlan</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Tacuinum_sanitatis" class="mw-redirect" title="Tacuinum sanitatis">Tacuinum sanitatis</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Rhineland" title="Rhineland">Rhineland</a>, 2nd half of the 15th century.</div></div></div>
<p>A copy of <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Almagest" title="Almagest">Almagest</a></i> was brought back to Sicily by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Aristippus" title="Henry Aristippus">Henry Aristippus</a>, as a gift from the Emperor to <a href="/wiki/William_I_of_Sicily" title="William I of Sicily">King William I</a>.  Aristippus, himself, translated <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Meno" title="Meno">Meno</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Phaedo" title="Phaedo">Phaedo</a></i> into Latin, but it was left to an anonymous student at Salerno to travel to Sicily and translate the <i>Almagest</i>, as well as several works by <a href="/wiki/Euclid" title="Euclid">Euclid</a>, from Greek to Latin.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>  Although the Sicilians generally translated directly from the Greek, when Greek texts were not available, they would translate from Arabic. <a href="/wiki/Admiral_Eugene_of_Sicily" class="mw-redirect" title="Admiral Eugene of Sicily">Admiral Eugene of Sicily</a> translated Ptolemy's <i><a href="/wiki/Ptolemy#Optics" title="Ptolemy">Optics</a></i> into Latin, drawing on his knowledge of all three languages in the task.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Accursius of <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Pistoia" title="Province of Pistoia">Pistoja</a>'s translations included the works of <a href="/wiki/Galen" title="Galen">Galen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hunayn_ibn_Ishaq" title="Hunayn ibn Ishaq">Hunayn ibn Ishaq</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Campbell-3_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell-3-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Gerard de <a href="/wiki/Sabbioneta" title="Sabbioneta">Sabloneta</a> translated <a href="/wiki/Avicenna" title="Avicenna">Avicenna</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Canon_of_Medicine" title="The Canon of Medicine">The Canon of Medicine</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Al-Razi" title="Al-Razi">al-Razi</a>'s <i>Almansor</i>. <a href="/wiki/Fibonacci" title="Fibonacci">Fibonacci</a> presented the first complete European account of the <a href="/wiki/Hindu-Arabic_numeral_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Hindu-Arabic numeral system">Hindu-Arabic numeral system</a> from <a href="/wiki/Arabic_numerals" title="Arabic numerals">Arabic sources</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Liber_Abaci" title="Liber Abaci">Liber Abaci</a></i> (1202).<sup id="cite_ref-Bieber_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bieber-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The <i>Aphorismi</i> by <a href="/wiki/Masawaiyh" title="Masawaiyh">Masawaiyh</a> (Mesue) was translated by an anonymous translator in late 11th or early 12th century Italy.<sup id="cite_ref-Danielle_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Danielle-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/James_of_Venice" title="James of Venice">James of Venice</a>, who probably spent some years in Constantinople, translated Aristotle's <i><a href="/wiki/Posterior_Analytics" title="Posterior Analytics">Posterior Analytics</a></i> from Greek into Latin in the mid-12th century,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> thus making the complete Aristotelian logical corpus, the <i><a href="/wiki/Organon" title="Organon">Organon</a></i>, available in Latin for the first time.
</p><p>In 13th century <a href="/wiki/Padua" title="Padua">Padua</a>, Bonacosa translated <a href="/wiki/Averroes" title="Averroes">Averroes</a>' medical work <i>Kitab al-Kulliyyat</i> as <i>Colliget</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Danielle-983_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Danielle-983-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/John_of_Capua" title="John of Capua">John of Capua</a> translated the <i>Kitab al-Taysir</i> by <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Zuhr" title="Ibn Zuhr">Ibn Zuhr</a> (Avenzoar) as <i>Theisir</i>. In 13th century <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>, <a href="/wiki/Faraj_ben_Salem" class="mw-redirect" title="Faraj ben Salem">Faraj ben Salem</a> translated <a href="/wiki/Rhazes" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhazes">Rhazes</a>' <i>al-Hawi</i> as <i>Continens</i> as well as <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Butlan" title="Ibn Butlan">Ibn Butlan</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Tacuinum_sanitatis" class="mw-redirect" title="Tacuinum sanitatis">Tacuinum sanitatis</a></i>. Also in 13th century Italy, Simon of <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a> and Abraham Tortuensis translated <a href="/wiki/Abu_al-Qasim_al-Zahrawi" class="mw-redirect" title="Abu al-Qasim al-Zahrawi">Abulcasis</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Al-Tasrif" title="Al-Tasrif">Al-Tasrif</a></i> as <i>Liber servitoris</i>, Alcoati's <i>Congregatio sive liber de oculis</i>, and the <i>Liber de simplicibus medicinis</i> by a <a href="/wiki/Serapion_the_Younger" title="Serapion the Younger">pseudo-Serapion</a><sup id="cite_ref-Danielle-984_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Danielle-984-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Translators_on_the_Spanish_frontier">Translators on the Spanish frontier</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Latin_translations_of_the_12th_century&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Translators on the Spanish frontier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As early as the end of the 10th century, European scholars travelled to Spain to study.  Most notable among these was <a href="/wiki/Gerbert_of_Aurillac" class="mw-redirect" title="Gerbert of Aurillac">Gerbert of Aurillac</a> (later Pope Sylvester II) who <a href="/wiki/Islamic_mathematics" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic mathematics">studied mathematics</a> in the region of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_March" class="mw-redirect" title="Spanish March">Spanish March</a> around <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a>.  Translations, however, did not begin in Spain until after 1085 when Toledo was reconquered by Christians.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The early translators in Spain focused heavily on <a href="/wiki/Islamic_science" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic science">scientific works</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Islamic_mathematics" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic mathematics">mathematics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Islamic_astronomy" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic astronomy">astronomy</a>, with a second area of interest including the <a href="/wiki/Qur%27an" class="mw-redirect" title="Qur&#39;an">Qur'an</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islamic</a> texts.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Spanish collections included many scholarly works written in Arabic, so translators worked almost exclusively from Arabic, rather than Greek texts, often in cooperation with a local speaker of Arabic.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>One of the more important translation projects was sponsored by <a href="/wiki/Peter_the_Venerable" title="Peter the Venerable">Peter the Venerable</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Abbot_of_Cluny" title="Abbot of Cluny">abbot</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cluny_Abbey" title="Cluny Abbey">Cluny</a>.  In 1142 he called upon <a href="/wiki/Robert_of_Ketton" title="Robert of Ketton">Robert of Ketton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herman_of_Carinthia" title="Herman of Carinthia">Herman of Carinthia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_of_Poitiers_(secretary)" title="Peter of Poitiers (secretary)">Peter of Poitiers</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Muslim" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> known only as "Mohammed" to produce the first Latin translation of the Qur'an (the <i><a href="/wiki/Lex_Mahumet_pseudoprophete" title="Lex Mahumet pseudoprophete">Lex Mahumet pseudoprophete</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Translations were produced throughout Spain and <a href="/wiki/Provence" title="Provence">Provence</a>. <a href="/wiki/Plato_Tiburtinus" title="Plato Tiburtinus">Plato of Tivoli</a> worked in <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">Catalonia</a>, Herman of Carinthia in Northern Spain and across the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a> in <a href="/wiki/Languedoc" title="Languedoc">Languedoc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hugh_of_Santalla" class="mw-redirect" title="Hugh of Santalla">Hugh of Santalla</a> in <a href="/wiki/Aragon" title="Aragon">Aragon</a>, Robert of Ketton in <a href="/wiki/Navarre" title="Navarre">Navarre</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_of_Chester" title="Robert of Chester">Robert of Chester</a> in <a href="/wiki/Segovia" title="Segovia">Segovia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>  The most important center of translation was the great cathedral library of <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Spain" title="Toledo, Spain">Toledo</a>.
</p><p>Plato of Tivoli's translations into Latin include <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_ibn_J%C4%81bir_al-Harr%C4%81n%C4%AB_al-Batt%C4%81n%C4%AB" class="mw-redirect" title="Muhammad ibn Jābir al-Harrānī al-Battānī">al-Battani</a>'s astronomical and <a href="/wiki/Trigonometry" title="Trigonometry">trigonometrical</a> work <i>De motu stellarum</i>, <a href="/wiki/Abraham_bar_Hiyya" title="Abraham bar Hiyya">Abraham bar Hiyya</a>'s <i>Liber embadorum</i>, <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_of_Bithynia" title="Theodosius of Bithynia">Theodosius of Bithynia</a>'s <i>Spherica</i>, and <a href="/wiki/Archimedes" title="Archimedes">Archimedes</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Measurement_of_a_Circle" title="Measurement of a Circle">Measurement of a Circle</a></i>. Robert of Chester's translations into Latin included <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_ibn_M%C5%ABs%C4%81_al-Khw%C4%81rizm%C4%AB" class="mw-redirect" title="Muhammad ibn Mūsā al-Khwārizmī">al-Khwarizmi</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Compendious_Book_on_Calculation_by_Completion_and_Balancing" title="The Compendious Book on Calculation by Completion and Balancing">Algebra</a></i> and astronomical tables (also containing trigonometric tables).<sup id="cite_ref-Katz_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Katz-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Abraham of <a href="/wiki/Tortosa" title="Tortosa">Tortosa</a>'s translations include Ibn Sarabi's (<a href="/wiki/Serapion_the_Younger" title="Serapion the Younger">Serapion Junior</a>) <i>De Simplicibus</i> and <a href="/wiki/Abu_al-Qasim_al-Zahrawi" class="mw-redirect" title="Abu al-Qasim al-Zahrawi">Abulcasis</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Al-Tasrif" title="Al-Tasrif">Al-Tasrif</a></i> as <i>Liber Servitoris</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Campbell-3_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell-3-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In 1126, <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_al-Fazari" class="mw-redirect" title="Muhammad al-Fazari">Muhammad al-Fazari</a>'s <i>Great Sindhind</i> (based on the <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> works of <i><a href="/wiki/Surya_Siddhanta" title="Surya Siddhanta">Surya Siddhanta</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Brahmagupta" title="Brahmagupta">Brahmagupta</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Brahmasphutasiddhanta" class="mw-redirect" title="Brahmasphutasiddhanta">Brahmasphutasiddhanta</a></i>) was translated into Latin.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to philosophical and scientific literature, the Jewish writer <a href="/wiki/Petrus_Alphonsi" title="Petrus Alphonsi">Petrus Alphonsi</a> translated a collection of 33 tales from <a href="/wiki/Arabic_literature" title="Arabic literature">Arabic literature</a> into <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>. Some of the tales he drew on were from the <i><a href="/wiki/Panchatantra" title="Panchatantra">Panchatantra</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/One_Thousand_and_One_Nights" title="One Thousand and One Nights">Arabian Nights</a></i>, such as the story cycle of "<a href="/wiki/Sinbad_the_Sailor" title="Sinbad the Sailor">Sinbad the Sailor</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Irwin_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Irwin-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Toledo_School_of_Translators">The Toledo School of Translators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Latin_translations_of_the_12th_century&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Toledo School of Translators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Toledo_School_of_Translators" title="Toledo School of Translators">Toledo School of Translators</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Las_Siete_Partidas.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Las_Siete_Partidas.jpg/250px-Las_Siete_Partidas.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="162" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Las_Siete_Partidas.jpg/375px-Las_Siete_Partidas.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/27/Las_Siete_Partidas.jpg 2x" data-file-width="452" data-file-height="293" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Las_Siete_Partidas.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>King <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_X" class="mw-redirect" title="Alfonso X">Alfonso X</a> (the Wise)</div></div></div>
<p>Toledo, with a large population of Arabic-speaking Christians (<a href="/wiki/Mozarabs" title="Mozarabs">Mozarabs</a>) had been an important center of learning since as early as the end of the 10th century, when European scholars traveled to Spain to study subjects that were not readily available in the rest of Europe. Among the early translators at Toledo were an Avendauth (who some have identified with <a href="/wiki/Abraham_ibn_Daud" title="Abraham ibn Daud">Abraham ibn Daud</a>), who translated <a href="/wiki/Avicenna" title="Avicenna">Avicenna</a>'s encyclopedia, the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Book_of_Healing" title="The Book of Healing">Kitāb al-Shifa</a></i> (<i>The Book of Healing</i>), in cooperation with <a href="/wiki/Domingo_Gundisalvo" class="mw-redirect" title="Domingo Gundisalvo">Domingo Gundisalvo</a>, Archdeacon of Cuéllar.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The translating efforts at Toledo are often overemphasized into a “school of translation,” however the representation of Toledo translating activity creates a false sense that a formal school arose around the Archbishop Raymond. Only one translation, by <a href="/wiki/John_of_Seville" title="John of Seville">John of Seville</a>, can be definitively dedicated to the archbishop. It is more accurate to consider Toledo as a geographically bilingual environment where local interests were favorable to translation efforts, making it a practical and appealing location for translators to work. As a result, many translators became active in the area and Toledo became the focus of translating activity.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However translating efforts were not properly organized until Toledo was reconquered by the Christian forces in 1085. <a href="/wiki/Raymond_de_Sauvet%C3%A2t" title="Raymond de Sauvetât">Raymond of Toledo</a> started the first translation efforts at the library of the <a href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_Toledo" class="mw-redirect" title="Cathedral of Toledo">Cathedral of Toledo</a>, where he led a team of translators that included Mozarabic Toledans, Jewish scholars, <a href="/wiki/Madrasah" class="mw-redirect" title="Madrasah">Madrasah</a> teachers and monks from the <a href="/wiki/Cluniac_Reforms" title="Cluniac Reforms">Order of Cluny</a>. They worked in the translation of many works from Arabic into Castilian, from Castilian into Latin, or directly from Arabic into Latin or Greek, and also made available important texts from Arabic and Hebrew philosophers who the Archbishop deemed important for an understanding of <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> As a result of their activities, the cathedral became a translations center known as the <i>Escuela de Traductores de Toledo</i> (<a href="/wiki/Toledo_School_of_Translators" title="Toledo School of Translators">Toledo School of Translators</a>), which was on a scale and importance not matched in the history of western culture.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Al_Razi_Receuil_de_traite_de_medecine_translated_by_Gerard_de_Cremone_Second_half_of_13th_century.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Al_Razi_Receuil_de_traite_de_medecine_translated_by_Gerard_de_Cremone_Second_half_of_13th_century.jpg/220px-Al_Razi_Receuil_de_traite_de_medecine_translated_by_Gerard_de_Cremone_Second_half_of_13th_century.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Al_Razi_Receuil_de_traite_de_medecine_translated_by_Gerard_de_Cremone_Second_half_of_13th_century.jpg/330px-Al_Razi_Receuil_de_traite_de_medecine_translated_by_Gerard_de_Cremone_Second_half_of_13th_century.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Al_Razi_Receuil_de_traite_de_medecine_translated_by_Gerard_de_Cremone_Second_half_of_13th_century.jpg/440px-Al_Razi_Receuil_de_traite_de_medecine_translated_by_Gerard_de_Cremone_Second_half_of_13th_century.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2964" data-file-height="1995" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Al_Razi_Receuil_de_traite_de_medecine_translated_by_Gerard_de_Cremone_Second_half_of_13th_century.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Al-Razi" title="Al-Razi">Al-Razi</a>'s <i>Recueil des traités de médecine</i> translated by <a href="/wiki/Gerard_of_Cremona" title="Gerard of Cremona">Gerard of Cremona</a>, second half of the 13th century.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Al-RaziInGerardusCremonensis1250.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Al-RaziInGerardusCremonensis1250.JPG/220px-Al-RaziInGerardusCremonensis1250.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="250" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Al-RaziInGerardusCremonensis1250.JPG/330px-Al-RaziInGerardusCremonensis1250.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Al-RaziInGerardusCremonensis1250.JPG/440px-Al-RaziInGerardusCremonensis1250.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1067" data-file-height="1211" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Al-RaziInGerardusCremonensis1250.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Depiction of the Persian physician <a href="/wiki/Al-Razi" title="Al-Razi">Al-Razi</a>, in Gerard of Cremona's "Recueil des traités de medecine" 1250–1260.</div></div></div>
<p>The most productive of the Toledo translators at that time was <a href="/wiki/Gerard_of_Cremona" title="Gerard of Cremona">Gerard of Cremona</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> who translated 87 books,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Almagest" title="Almagest">Almagest</a></i>, many of the works of <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, including his <a href="/wiki/Posterior_Analytics" title="Posterior Analytics">Posterior Analytics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physics_(Aristotle)" title="Physics (Aristotle)">Physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/On_the_Heavens" title="On the Heavens">On the Heavens and the World</a>, <a href="/wiki/On_Generation_and_Corruption" title="On Generation and Corruption">On Generation and Corruption</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Meteorology_(Aristotle)" title="Meteorology (Aristotle)">Meteorology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_ibn_M%C5%ABs%C4%81_al-Khw%C4%81rizm%C4%AB" class="mw-redirect" title="Muhammad ibn Mūsā al-Khwārizmī">al-Khwarizmi</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Compendious_Book_on_Calculation_by_Completion_and_Balancing" title="The Compendious Book on Calculation by Completion and Balancing">On Algebra and Almucabala</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Archimedes" title="Archimedes">Archimedes</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/On_the_Measurement_of_the_Circle" class="mw-redirect" title="On the Measurement of the Circle">On the Measurement of the Circle</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Euclid" title="Euclid">Euclid</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements" title="Euclid&#39;s Elements">Elements of Geometry</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Jabir_ibn_Aflah" title="Jabir ibn Aflah">Jabir ibn Aflah</a>'s <i>Elementa astronomica</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Katz_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Katz-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Al-Kindi" title="Al-Kindi">Al-Kindi</a>'s <i>On Optics</i>, <a href="/wiki/Ahmad_ibn_Muhammad_ibn_Kath%C4%ABr_al-Fargh%C4%81n%C4%AB" class="mw-redirect" title="Ahmad ibn Muhammad ibn Kathīr al-Farghānī">al-Farghani</a>'s <i>On Elements of Astronomy on the Celestial Motions</i>, <a href="/wiki/Al-Farabi" title="Al-Farabi">al-Farabi</a>'s <i>On the Classification of the Sciences</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Alchemy_(Islam)" class="mw-redirect" title="Alchemy (Islam)">chemical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Islamic_medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic medicine">medical</a> works of <a href="/wiki/Al-Razi" title="Al-Razi">al-Razi</a> (Rhazes),<sup id="cite_ref-Bieber_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bieber-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> the works of <a href="/wiki/Thabit_ibn_Qurra" class="mw-redirect" title="Thabit ibn Qurra">Thabit ibn Qurra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hunayn_ibn_Ishaq" title="Hunayn ibn Ishaq">Hunayn ibn Ishaq</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> and the works of <a href="/wiki/Al-Zarkali" class="mw-redirect" title="Al-Zarkali">al-Zarkali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jabir_ibn_Aflah" title="Jabir ibn Aflah">Jabir ibn Aflah</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ban%C5%AB_M%C5%ABs%C4%81" title="Banū Mūsā">Banu Musa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ab%C5%AB_K%C4%81mil_Shuj%C4%81_ibn_Aslam" class="mw-redirect" title="Abū Kāmil Shujā ibn Aslam">Abu Kamil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abu_al-Qasim_al-Zahrawi" class="mw-redirect" title="Abu al-Qasim al-Zahrawi">Abu al-Qasim al-Zahrawi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ibn_al-Haytham" title="Ibn al-Haytham">Ibn al-Haytham</a> (but not including the <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Optics" title="Book of Optics">Book of Optics</a></i>, because the catalog of the works of Gerard of Cremona does not list that title; however the Risner compilation of <i>Opticae Thesaurus Septem Libri</i> also includes a work by Witelo and also <i>de Crepusculis</i>, which Risner incorrectly attributed to Alhacen, and which <i>was</i> translated by Gerard of Cremona).<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> The medical works he translated include <a href="/wiki/Ali_ibn_Ridwan" title="Ali ibn Ridwan">Haly Abenrudian</a>'s <i>Expositio ad Tegni Galeni</i>; the <i>Practica, Brevarium medicine</i> by <a href="/wiki/Yahya_ibn_Sarafyun" title="Yahya ibn Sarafyun">Yuhanna ibn Sarabiyun</a> (Serapion); <a href="/wiki/Al-Kindi" title="Al-Kindi">Alkindus</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/De_Gradibus" title="De Gradibus">De Gradibus</a></i>; <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_ibn_Zakar%C4%ABya_R%C4%81zi" class="mw-redirect" title="Muhammad ibn Zakarīya Rāzi">Rhazes</a>' <i>Liber ad Almansorem, Liber divisionum, Introductio in medicinam, De egritudinibus iuncturarum, Antidotarium</i> and <i>Practica puerorum</i>; <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Israeli_ben_Solomon" title="Isaac Israeli ben Solomon">Isaac Israeli ben Solomon</a>'s <i>De elementis</i> and <i>De definitionibus</i>;<sup id="cite_ref-Danielle_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Danielle-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Abu_al-Qasim_al-Zahrawi" class="mw-redirect" title="Abu al-Qasim al-Zahrawi">Abulcasis</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Al-Tasrif" title="Al-Tasrif">Al-Tasrif</a></i> as <i>Chirurgia</i>; <a href="/wiki/Avicenna" title="Avicenna">Avicenna</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Canon_of_Medicine" title="The Canon of Medicine">The Canon of Medicine</a></i> as <i>Liber Canonis</i>; and the <i>Liber de medicamentis simplicus</i> by Ibn Wafid (<a href="/wiki/Abenguefit" class="mw-redirect" title="Abenguefit">Abenguefit</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Danielle-983_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Danielle-983-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
At the close of the 12th and the beginning of the 13th centuries, <a href="/wiki/Mark_of_Toledo" title="Mark of Toledo">Mark of Toledo</a> translated the <a href="/wiki/Qur%27an" class="mw-redirect" title="Qur&#39;an">Qur'an</a> (once again) and various <a href="/wiki/Islamic_medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic medicine">medical works</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> He also translated <a href="/wiki/Hunayn_ibn_Ishaq" title="Hunayn ibn Ishaq">Hunayn ibn Ishaq</a>'s medical work <i>Liber isagogarum</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Danielle-983_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Danielle-983-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Under King <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_X_of_Castile" title="Alfonso X of Castile">Alfonso X of Castile</a>, Toledo rose even higher in importance as a translation center. By insisting that the translated output was "llanos de entender" ("easy to understand"),<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> they reached a much wider audience both within Spain and in other European countries, as many scholars from places like Italy, Germany, England or the Netherlands, who had moved to Toledo in order to translate medical, religious, classical and philosophical texts, brought back to their countries the acquired knowledge. Others were selected and hired with very high salaries by the King himself from many places in Spain, like Seville or Córdoba and foreign places like Gascony or Paris.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Michael_Scot" title="Michael Scot">Michael Scot</a> (c. 1175–1232)<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> translated the works of <a href="/wiki/Nur_Ed-Din_Al_Betrugi" class="mw-redirect" title="Nur Ed-Din Al Betrugi">al-Betrugi</a> (Alpetragius) in 1217,<sup id="cite_ref-Bieber_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bieber-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Al-Bitruji" class="mw-redirect" title="Al-Bitruji">al-Bitruji</a>'s <i>On the Motions of the Heavens</i>, and <a href="/wiki/Averroes" title="Averroes">Averroes</a>' influential commentaries on the scientific works of <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_translators">Later translators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Latin_translations_of_the_12th_century&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later translators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>David the Jew (c. 1228–1245) translated the works of <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_ibn_Zakariya_al-Razi" title="Muhammad ibn Zakariya al-Razi">al-Razi</a> (Rhazes) into Latin. <a href="/wiki/Arnaldus_de_Villa_Nova" title="Arnaldus de Villa Nova">Arnaldus de Villa Nova</a>'s (1235–1313) translations include the works of <a href="/wiki/Galen" title="Galen">Galen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Avicenna" title="Avicenna">Avicenna</a><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> (including his <i>Maqala fi Ahkam al-adwiya al-qalbiya</i> as <i>De viribus cordis</i>), the <i>De medicinis simplicibus</i> by <a href="/wiki/Abu_al-Salt" title="Abu al-Salt">Abu al-Salt</a> (Albuzali),<sup id="cite_ref-Danielle-983_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Danielle-983-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Qusta_ibn_Luqa" title="Qusta ibn Luqa">Costa ben Luca</a>'s <i>De physicis ligaturis</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Danielle_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Danielle-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 13th century Portugal, <a href="/wiki/Giles_of_Santar%C3%A9m" title="Giles of Santarém">Giles of Santarém</a> translated <a href="/wiki/Rhazes" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhazes">Rhazes</a>' <i>De secretis medicine, Aphorismi Rasis</i> and <a href="/wiki/Masawaiyh" title="Masawaiyh">Mesue</a>'s <i>De secretis medicine</i>. In <a href="/wiki/Murcia" title="Murcia">Murcia</a>, Rufin of <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a> translated the <i>Liber questionum medicinalium discentium in medicina</i> by <a href="/wiki/Hunayn_ibn_Ishaq" title="Hunayn ibn Ishaq">Hunayn ibn Ishaq</a> (Hunen), and Dominicus Marrochinus translated the <i>Epistola de cognitione infirmatum oculorum</i> by <a href="/wiki/Ali_ibn_Isa_al-Asturlabi" title="Ali ibn Isa al-Asturlabi">Ali Ibn Isa</a> (Jesu Haly).<sup id="cite_ref-Danielle-983_23-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Danielle-983-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In 14th century <a href="/wiki/Lleida" title="Lleida">Lerida</a>, John Jacobi translated Alcoati's medical work <i>Liber de la figura del uyl</i> into <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">Catalan</a> and then Latin.<sup id="cite_ref-Danielle-984_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Danielle-984-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/William_of_Moerbeke" title="William of Moerbeke">Willem van Moerbeke</a>, known in the English speaking world as <a href="/wiki/William_of_Moerbeke" title="William of Moerbeke">William of Moerbeke</a> (c. 1215–1286) was a prolific medieval translator of philosophical, medical, and scientific texts from Greek into Latin. At the request of Aquinas, so it is assumed—the source document is not clear—he undertook a complete translation of the works of <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> or, for some portions, a revision of existing translations. He was the first translator of the <i>Politics</i> (c. 1260) from Greek into Latin. The reason for the request was that the many copies of Aristotle in Latin then in circulation had originated in Spain (see <a href="/wiki/Gerard_of_Cremona" title="Gerard of Cremona">Gerard of Cremona</a>). These earlier translations were assumed to have been influenced by the rationalist <a href="/wiki/Averroes" title="Averroes">Averroes</a>, who was suspected of being a source of philosophical and theological errors found in the earlier translations of Aristotle. Moerbeke's translations have had a long history; they were already standard classics by the 14th century, when <a href="/w/index.php?title=Henricus_Hervodius&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Henricus Hervodius (page does not exist)">Henricus Hervodius</a> put his finger on their enduring value: they were literal (<i>de verbo in verbo</i>), faithful to the spirit of Aristotle and <i>without elegance.</i> For several of William's translations, the Greek texts have since disappeared: without him the works would be lost. William also translated mathematical treatises by <a href="/wiki/Hero_of_Alexandria" title="Hero of Alexandria">Hero of Alexandria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Archimedes" title="Archimedes">Archimedes</a>. Especially important was his translation of the <i>Theological Elements</i> of <a href="/wiki/Proclus" title="Proclus">Proclus</a> (made in 1268), because the <i>Theological Elements</i> is one of the fundamental sources of the revived <a href="/wiki/Neo-Platonic" class="mw-redirect" title="Neo-Platonic">Neo-Platonic</a> philosophical currents of the 13th century. The <a href="/wiki/Vatican_Library" title="Vatican Library">Vatican</a> collection holds William's own copy of the translation he made of the greatest <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic" class="mw-redirect" title="Hellenistic">Hellenistic</a> mathematician, <a href="/wiki/Archimedes" title="Archimedes">Archimedes</a>, with commentaries of <a href="/wiki/Eutocius" class="mw-redirect" title="Eutocius">Eutocius</a>, which was made in 1269 at the papal court in Viterbo. William consulted two of the best Greek manuscripts of Archimedes, both of which have since disappeared.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_European_translators">Other European translators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Latin_translations_of_the_12th_century&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other European translators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Adelard_of_Bath" title="Adelard of Bath">Adelard of Bath</a>'s (<a href="/wiki/Floruit" title="Floruit">fl.</a> 1116–1142) translations into Latin included <a href="/wiki/Al-Khwarizmi" class="mw-redirect" title="Al-Khwarizmi">al-Khwarizmi</a>'s astronomical and trigonometrical work <i>Astronomical tables</i> and his <a href="/wiki/Arithmetic" title="Arithmetic">arithmetical</a> work <i>Liber ysagogarum Alchorismi</i>, the <i>Introduction to Astrology</i> of <a href="/wiki/Abu_Mashar" class="mw-redirect" title="Abu Mashar">Abū Ma'shar</a>, as well as Euclid's <i>Elements</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Adelard associated with other scholars in Western England such as <a href="/wiki/Petrus_Alphonsi" title="Petrus Alphonsi">Peter Alfonsi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Walcher_of_Malvern" title="Walcher of Malvern">Walcher of Malvern</a> who translated and developed the astronomical concepts brought from Spain.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ab%C5%AB_K%C4%81mil_Shuj%C4%81_ibn_Aslam" class="mw-redirect" title="Abū Kāmil Shujā ibn Aslam">Abu Kamil</a>'s <i>Algebra</i> was also translated into Latin during this period, but the translator of the work is unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-Katz_30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Katz-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Alfred_of_Sareshel" title="Alfred of Sareshel">Alfred of Sareshel</a>'s (c. 1200–1227) translations include the works of <a href="/wiki/Nicolaus_of_Damascus" title="Nicolaus of Damascus">Nicolaus of Damascus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hunayn_ibn_Ishaq" title="Hunayn ibn Ishaq">Hunayn ibn Ishaq</a>. Antonius Frachentius Vicentinus' translations include the works of <a href="/wiki/Avicenna" title="Avicenna">Ibn Sina</a> (Avicenna). Armenguad's translations include the works of Avicenna, <a href="/wiki/Averroes" title="Averroes">Averroes</a>, Hunayn ibn Ishaq, and <a href="/wiki/Maimonides" title="Maimonides">Maimonides</a>. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Berengarius_of_Valentia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Berengarius of Valentia (page does not exist)">Berengarius of Valentia</a> translated the works of <a href="/wiki/Abu_al-Qasim_al-Zahrawi" class="mw-redirect" title="Abu al-Qasim al-Zahrawi">Abu al-Qasim al-Zahrawi</a> (Abulcasis). Drogon (Azagont) translated the works of <a href="/wiki/Al-Kindi" title="Al-Kindi">al-Kindi</a>. Farragut (Faradj ben Salam) translated the works of Hunayn ibn Ishaq, Ibn Zezla (Byngezla), <a href="/wiki/Masawaiyh" title="Masawaiyh">Masawaiyh</a> (Mesue), and <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_ibn_Zakariya_al-Razi" title="Muhammad ibn Zakariya al-Razi">al-Razi</a> (Rhazes). Andreas Alphagus Bellnensis' translations include the works of Avicenna, Averroes, <a href="/wiki/Serapion_the_Younger" title="Serapion the Younger">Serapion</a>, al-Qifti, and Albe'thar.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 13th century <a href="/wiki/Montpellier" title="Montpellier">Montpellier</a>, Profatius and Bernardus Honofredi translated the <i>Kitab alaghdiya</i> by <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Zuhr" title="Ibn Zuhr">Ibn Zuhr</a> (Avenzoar) as <i>De regimine sanitatis</i>; and Armengaudus Blasius translated the <i>al-Urjuza fi al-tibb</i>, a work combining the medical writings of <a href="/wiki/Avicenna" title="Avicenna">Avicenna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Averroes" title="Averroes">Averroes</a>, as <i>Cantica cum commento</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Danielle-984_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Danielle-984-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Other texts translated during this period include the <a href="/wiki/Alchemy_and_chemistry_in_Islam" class="mw-redirect" title="Alchemy and chemistry in Islam">alchemical works</a> of <a href="/wiki/J%C4%81bir_ibn_Hayy%C4%81n" class="mw-redirect" title="Jābir ibn Hayyān">Jābir ibn Hayyān</a> (Geber), whose treatises became standard texts for European <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">alchemists</a>. These include the <i>Kitab al-Kimya</i> (titled <i>Book of the Composition of Alchemy</i> in Europe), translated by <a href="/wiki/Robert_of_Chester" title="Robert of Chester">Robert of Chester</a> (1144); the <i>Kitab al-Sab'een</i> translated by <a href="/wiki/Gerard_of_Cremona" title="Gerard of Cremona">Gerard of Cremona</a> (before 1187), and the <i>Book of the Kingdom</i>, <i>Book of the Balances</i> and <i>Book of Eastern Mercury</i> translated by <a href="/wiki/Marcelin_Berthelot" class="mw-redirect" title="Marcelin Berthelot">Marcelin Berthelot</a>. Another work translated during this period was <i><a href="/wiki/De_Proprietatibus_Elementorum" title="De Proprietatibus Elementorum">De Proprietatibus Elementorum</a></i>, an <a href="/wiki/Islamic_science" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic science">Arabic work</a> on <a href="/wiki/Islamic_geography" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic geography">geology</a> written by a <a href="/wiki/Pseudo-Aristotle" title="Pseudo-Aristotle">pseudo-Aristotle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bieber_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bieber-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> A pseudo-<a href="/wiki/Masawaiyh" title="Masawaiyh">Mesue</a>'s <i>De consolatione medicanarum simplicum, Antidotarium, Grabadin</i> was also translated into Latin by an anonymous translator.<sup id="cite_ref-Danielle-983_23-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Danielle-983-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vernacular_languages">Vernacular languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Latin_translations_of_the_12th_century&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Vernacular languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the  12th century in southern France and Italy, many Arabic scientific texts were translated into Hebrew. France and Italy had large Jewish communities where there was little knowledge of Arabic, requiring translations to provide access to Arabic science. The translation of Arabic texts into Hebrew was used by translators, such as <a href="/wiki/Profatius_Judaeus" class="mw-redirect" title="Profatius Judaeus">Profatius Judaeus</a>, as an intermediate step between translation from Arabic into Latin. This practice was most widely used from the thirteenth to the sixteenth centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
