<p><b>Western Christianity</b> is a religious category composed of the <a href="/wiki/Latin_Church" title="Latin Church">Latin Church</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a>, together with their offshoots such as <a href="/wiki/Independent_Catholicism" title="Independent Catholicism">Independent Catholicism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Restorationism" title="Restorationism">Restorationism</a>. The large majority of the world's 2.4 billion Christians are Western Christians (about 2 billion – 1.3 billion Latin Catholic and 800 million Protestant). The original and still major part, the Latin Church, developed under the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Rome" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishop of Rome">bishop of Rome</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Patriarch_of_the_West" title="Patriarch of the West">Patriarch of the West</a>) in the former <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a> in <a href="/wiki/Early_Christianity" title="Early Christianity">Antiquity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Out of the Latin Church emerged a wide variety of independent <a href="/wiki/Protestant_denominations" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant denominations">Protestant denominations</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutheranism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anglicanism" title="Anglicanism">Anglicanism</a>, starting from the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a> in the 16th century, as did Independent Catholicism in the 19th century. Thus, the term "Western Christianity" does not describe a single <a href="/wiki/Communion_(Christian)" class="mw-redirect" title="Communion (Christian)">communion</a> or <a href="/wiki/Religious_denomination" title="Religious denomination">religious denomination</a>, but is applied  to distinguish all these denominations collectively from <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Christianity" title="Eastern Christianity">Eastern Christianity</a>.
</p><p>The establishment of the distinct Latin Church, a <a href="/wiki/Particular_church" class="mw-redirect" title="Particular church">particular church</a> <a href="/wiki/Sui_iuris" title="Sui iuris">sui iuris</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a> (in contrast to the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholic_Churches" title="Eastern Catholic Churches">Eastern Catholic Churches</a>, also in <a href="/wiki/Full_communion" title="Full communion">full communion</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Pope</a> in <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Rome</a>) coincided with the consolidation of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, which claimed <a href="/wiki/Papal_primacy" title="Papal primacy">primacy</a> since Antiquity. The terms "Western" and "Eastern" in this regard originated with geographical divisions mirroring the cultural divide between the <a href="/wiki/Greek_East_and_Latin_West" title="Greek East and Latin West">Hellenistic east and Latin West</a>, and the political divide between the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western</a> and <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Eastern Roman empires</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> adherents of the Latin Church, irrespective of ethnicity, commonly referred to themselves as "Latins" to distinguish themselves from Eastern Christians.<sup id="cite_ref-Orbilat_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Orbilat-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>With the expansion of <a href="/wiki/European_colonialism" class="mw-redirect" title="European colonialism">European colonialism</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern_era" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Modern era">Early Modern era</a>, the Latin Church, in time along with its Protestant secessions, spread throughout the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>, much of the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southern_Africa" title="Southern Africa">Southern Africa</a>, pockets of <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a>, and throughout <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>. Thus, when used for historical periods after the 16th century, the term "Western Christianity" does not refer to a particular geographical area, but is rather used as a collective term for all these.
</p><p>Today, the geographical distinction between Western and Eastern Christianity is not nearly as absolute as in Antiquity or the Middle Ages, due to the spread of <a href="/wiki/Christian_missionaries" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian missionaries">Christian missionaries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human_migration" title="Human migration">migrations</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Globalisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Globalisation">globalisation</a>. As such, the adjectives "Western Christianity" and "Eastern Christianity" are typically used to refer to historical origins and differences in <a href="/wiki/Christian_theology" title="Christian theology">theology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christian_liturgy" title="Christian liturgy">liturgy</a>, rather than present geographical locations.
</p><p>While the Latin Church maintains the use of the <a href="/wiki/Latin_liturgical_rites" title="Latin liturgical rites">Latin liturgical rites</a>, Protestant denominations and Independent Catholicism use a wide variety of liturgical practices.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Christianity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gustav_Vasa_Bible_1541.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Gustav_Vasa_Bible_1541.jpg/220px-Gustav_Vasa_Bible_1541.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="338" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9f/Gustav_Vasa_Bible_1541.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="293" data-file-height="450" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gustav_Vasa_Bible_1541.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Title page of the Lutheran Swedish <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Vasa_Bible" title="Gustav Vasa Bible">Gustav Vasa Bible</a>, translated by the Petri brothers, along with <a href="/wiki/Laurentius_Andreae" title="Laurentius Andreae">Laurentius Andreae</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jesuites_en_chine.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Jesuites_en_chine.jpg/220px-Jesuites_en_chine.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="196" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Jesuites_en_chine.jpg/330px-Jesuites_en_chine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Jesuites_en_chine.jpg/440px-Jesuites_en_chine.jpg 2x" data-file-width="450" data-file-height="400" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jesuites_en_chine.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Jesuit_China_missions" title="Jesuit China missions">Jesuit</a> scholars in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. Top: <a href="/wiki/Matteo_Ricci" title="Matteo Ricci">Matteo Ricci</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adam_Schall_von_Bell" class="mw-redirect" title="Adam Schall von Bell">Adam Schaal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Verbiest" title="Ferdinand Verbiest">Ferdinand Verbiest</a> (1623–88); Bottom: <a href="/wiki/Xu_Guangqi" title="Xu Guangqi">Paul Siu (Xu Guangqi)</a>, <i>Colao</i> or Prime Minister of State, and his granddaughter Candide Hiu</div></div></div>
<p>For most of its history the church in Europe has been <a href="/wiki/Greek_East_and_Latin_West" title="Greek East and Latin West">culturally divided</a> between the Latin-speaking west, whose centre was <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, and the Greek-speaking east, whose centre was <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>. Cultural differences and political rivalry created tensions between the two churches, leading to disagreement over <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_%E2%80%93_Roman_Catholic_theological_differences" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Orthodox – Roman Catholic theological differences">doctrine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_%E2%80%93_Roman_Catholic_ecclesiastical_differences" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Orthodox – Roman Catholic ecclesiastical differences">ecclesiology</a> and ultimately to <a href="/wiki/East-West_schism" class="mw-redirect" title="East-West schism">schism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-west_christ_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-west_christ-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Like <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Christianity" title="Eastern Christianity">Eastern Christianity</a>, Western Christianity traces its roots directly to the <a href="/wiki/Twelve_Apostles" class="mw-redirect" title="Twelve Apostles">apostles</a> and other early preachers of the religion. In Western Christianity's  original area <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> was the principal language. Christian writers in Latin had more influence there than those who wrote in <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">Syriac</a>, or other Eastern languages. Though the first Christians in the West used Greek (such as <a href="/wiki/Clement_of_Rome" class="mw-redirect" title="Clement of Rome">Clement of Rome</a>), by the <a href="/wiki/Christianity_in_the_4th_century" title="Christianity in the 4th century">fourth century</a> Latin had superseded it even in the cosmopolitan city of <a href="/wiki/Early_centers_of_Christianity#Rome" title="Early centers of Christianity">Rome</a>, while there is evidence of a Latin translation of the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> in the 2nd century (see also <a href="/wiki/Vetus_Latina" title="Vetus Latina">Vetus Latina</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Early_centers_of_Christianity#Southern_Gaul" title="Early centers of Christianity">southern Gaul</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Early_centers_of_Christianity#Carthage" title="Early centers of Christianity">Roman province of Africa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>With the <a href="/wiki/Decline_of_the_Roman_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Decline of the Roman Empire">decline of the Roman Empire</a>, distinctions appeared also in organization, since the bishops in the West were not dependent on the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Emperor in Constantinople</a> and did not come under the influence of the <a href="/wiki/Caesaropapism#Caesaropapism_in_the_Eastern_Church" title="Caesaropapism">Caesaropapism in the Eastern Church</a>. While the <a href="/wiki/Ecumenical_Patriarch_of_Constantinople" title="Ecumenical Patriarch of Constantinople">see of Constantinople</a> became dominant throughout the Emperor's lands, the West looked exclusively to the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">see of Rome</a>, which in the East was seen as that of one of the five <a href="/wiki/Patriarch" title="Patriarch">patriarchs</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Pentarchy_(Christianity)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pentarchy (Christianity)">Pentarchy</a>, "the proposed government of universal <a href="/wiki/Christendom" title="Christendom">Christendom</a> by five <a href="/wiki/Patriarch" title="Patriarch">patriarchal</a> sees under the auspices of a single universal empire. Formulated in the legislation of the emperor <a href="/wiki/Justinian_I" title="Justinian I">Justinian I</a> (527–565), especially in his <a href="/wiki/Corpus_Juris_Civilis#Novellae" title="Corpus Juris Civilis">Novella</a> 131, the theory received formal ecclesiastical sanction at the <a href="/wiki/Quinisext_Council" title="Quinisext Council">Council in Trullo</a> (692), which ranked the five sees as Rome, Constantinople, Alexandria, Antioch, and Jerusalem."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Over the centuries, disagreements separated Western Christianity from the various forms of Eastern Christianity: first from <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_East" title="Church of the East">East Syriac Christianity</a> after the <a href="/wiki/First_Council_of_Ephesus" class="mw-redirect" title="First Council of Ephesus">Council of Ephesus (431)</a>, then from that of <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodoxy" class="mw-redirect" title="Oriental Orthodoxy">Oriental Orthodoxy</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Chalcedon" title="Council of Chalcedon">Council of Chalcedon</a> (451), and then from <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodoxy</a> with the <a href="/wiki/East-West_Schism" class="mw-redirect" title="East-West Schism">East-West Schism</a> of 1054. With the last-named form of Eastern Christianity, reunion agreements were signed at the <a href="/wiki/Second_Council_of_Lyon" title="Second Council of Lyon">Second Council of Lyon</a> (1274) and the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Florence" title="Council of Florence">Council of Florence</a> (1439), but these proved ineffective.
</p><p>The rise of <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a> led to major divisions within Western Christianity, which still persist, and wars&#8212;for example, the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Spanish_War_(1585%E2%80%931604)" title="Anglo-Spanish War (1585–1604)">Anglo-Spanish War</a> of 1585–1604 had religious as well as economic causes.
</p><p>In and after the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Discovery" title="Age of Discovery">Age of Discovery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europeans</a> spread Western Christianity to the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a> and elsewhere. Roman Catholicism came to the Americas (especially South America), Africa, Asia, Australia and the Pacific. Protestantism, including Anglicanism, came to North America, Australia-Pacific and some African locales.
</p><p>Today, the geographical distinction between Western and Eastern Christianity is now much less absolute, due to the great migrations of Europeans across the globe, as well as the work of <a href="/wiki/Missionary" title="Missionary">missionaries</a> worldwide over the past five centuries.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Original_sin">Original sin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Christianity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Original sin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although "<a href="/wiki/Original_sin" title="Original sin">original sin</a>" can be taken to mean the sin that Adam committed, it is usually understood as a consequence of the first sin, the hereditary stain with which we are born on account of our origin or descent from Adam.
With the exception of tendencies such as <a href="/wiki/Pelagianism" title="Pelagianism">Pelagianism</a>, Western Christianity is thought to hold this doctrine, which was championed especially by <a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo" title="Augustine of Hippo">Saint Augustine</a>, who wrote: "The deliberate sin of the first man is the cause of original sin" (De nupt. et concup., II, xxvi, 43).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filioque_clause">Filioque clause</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Christianity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Filioque clause">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Filioque" title="Filioque">Filioque</a></div>
<p>Most Western Christians use a version of the <a href="/wiki/Nicene_Creed" title="Nicene Creed">Nicene Creed</a> that states that the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Spirit_in_Christianity" title="Holy Spirit in Christianity">Holy Spirit</a> "<a href="/wiki/Filioque" title="Filioque">proceeds from the Father and the Son</a>", where the original text as adopted by the <a href="/wiki/First_Council_of_Constantinople" title="First Council of Constantinople">First Council of Constantinople</a> had "proceeds from the Father" without the addition of either "and the Son" or "alone". This Western version also has the additional phrase "God from God" (in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>Deum de Deo</i>), which was in the Creed as adopted by the <a href="/wiki/First_Council_of_Nicaea" title="First Council of Nicaea">First Council of Nicaea</a>, but which was dropped by the First Council of Constantinople.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Date_of_Easter">Date of Easter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Christianity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Date of Easter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Easter_controversy" title="Easter controversy">Easter controversy</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Easter#Date" title="Easter">date of Easter</a> usually differs between Eastern and Western Christianity, because the calculations are based on the <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">Julian calendar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a> respectively. However, before the Council of Nicea various dates including Jewish Passover were observed. Nicea "Romanized" the date for Easter and anathematized a "Judaized" (i.e. Passover date for) Easter. The date of observance of Easter has only differed in modern times since the promulgation of the Gregorian calendar in 1582; and further, the Western Church did not universally adopt the Gregorian calendar at once, so that for some time the dates of Easter differed as between the Eastern Church and the Roman Catholic Church, but not necessarily as between the Eastern Church and the Western Protestant churches. For example, the Church of England continued to observe Easter on the same date as the Eastern Church until 1753.
</p><p>Even the dates of other Christian holidays differ between Eastern and Western Christianity.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_denominations">Western denominations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Christianity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Western denominations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Today, Western Christianity makes up close to 90% of <a href="/wiki/Christianity_by_country" title="Christianity by country">Christians worldwide</a> with the Catholic Church accounting for over half and various Protestant denominations making up another 40%. 
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Hussite" class="mw-redirect" title="Hussite">Hussite</a> movements of 15th century <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a> preceded the main Protestant uprising by 100 years and evolved into several small Protestant churches, such as the <a href="/wiki/Moravian_Church" title="Moravian Church">Moravian Church</a>. <a href="/wiki/Waldensians" title="Waldensians">Waldensians</a> survived also, but blended into the <a href="/wiki/Reformed_tradition" class="mw-redirect" title="Reformed tradition">Reformed tradition</a>.
</p>
