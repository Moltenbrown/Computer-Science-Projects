<p><i><b>The School of Athens</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <i lang="it">Scuola di Atene</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">fresco</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance" title="Italian Renaissance">Italian Renaissance</a> artist <a href="/wiki/Raphael" title="Raphael">Raphael</a>. It was painted between 1509 and 1511 as a part of Raphael's commission to decorate the rooms now known as the <i lang="it" title="Italian language text"><a href="/wiki/Raphael_Rooms" title="Raphael Rooms">Stanze di Raffaello</a></i>, in the <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_Palace" title="Apostolic Palace">Apostolic Palace</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Vatican_City" title="Vatican City">Vatican</a>. The <i lang="it" title="Italian language text"><a href="/wiki/Stanza_della_Segnatura" class="mw-redirect" title="Stanza della Segnatura">Stanza della Segnatura</a></i> was the first of the rooms to be decorated, and <i>The School of Athens</i>, representing Philosophy, was probably the third painting to be finished there, after <i lang="it" title="Italian language text"><a href="/wiki/Disputation_of_the_Holy_Sacrament" title="Disputation of the Holy Sacrament">La Disputa</a></i> (Theology) on the opposite wall, and the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Parnassus" title="The Parnassus">Parnassus</a></i> (Literature).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The picture has long been seen as "Raphael's <a href="/wiki/Masterpiece" title="Masterpiece">masterpiece</a> and the perfect embodiment of the classical spirit of the Renaissance".<sup id="cite_ref-JJ_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JJ-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span id="Program.2C_subject.2C_figure_identifications_and_interpretations"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Program,_subject,_figure_identifications_and_interpretations">Program, subject, figure identifications and interpretations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_School_of_Athens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Program, subject, figure identifications and interpretations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Raffael_071.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Raffael_071.jpg/220px-Raffael_071.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="129" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Raffael_071.jpg/330px-Raffael_071.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Raffael_071.jpg/440px-Raffael_071.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1205" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Raffael_071.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Zoroaster" title="Zoroaster">Zoroaster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>, Raphael as <a href="/wiki/Apelles" title="Apelles">Apelles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Perugino" class="mw-redirect" title="Perugino">Perugino</a> or <a href="/wiki/Timoteo_Viti" title="Timoteo Viti">Timoteo Viti</a> as <a href="/wiki/Protogenes" title="Protogenes">Protogenes</a></div></div></div>
<p><i>The School of Athens</i> is one of a group of four main frescoes on the walls of the Stanza (those on either side centrally interrupted by windows) that depict distinct branches of knowledge. Each theme is identified above by a separate <a href="/wiki/Tondo_(art)" title="Tondo (art)">tondo</a> containing a majestic female figure seated in the clouds, with <a href="/wiki/Putto" title="Putto">putti</a> bearing the phrases: "Seek Knowledge of Causes,"  "Divine Inspiration,"  "Knowledge of Things Divine" (<i>Disputa</i>), "To Each What Is Due."  Accordingly, the figures on the walls below exemplify Philosophy, Poetry (including Music), Theology, and Law.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The traditional title is not Raphael's. The subject of the "School" is actually "Philosophy," or at least ancient Greek philosophy, and its overhead tondo-label, "Causarum Cognitio", tells us what kind, as it appears to echo <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>'s emphasis on wisdom as knowing why, hence knowing the causes, in <i>Metaphysics</i> Book I and <i>Physics</i> Book II. Indeed, <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> and Aristotle appear to be the central figures in the scene. However, all the philosophers depicted sought knowledge of first causes. Many lived before Plato and Aristotle, and hardly a third were Athenians. The architecture contains Roman elements, but the general semi-circular setting having Plato and Aristotle at its centre might be alluding to Pythagoras' <i><a href="/wiki/Monad_(philosophy)" title="Monad (philosophy)">circumpunct</a></i>.
</p><p>Commentators have suggested that nearly every great ancient Greek philosopher can be found in the painting, but determining which are depicted is difficult, since Raphael made no designations outside possible likenesses, and no contemporary documents explain the painting. Compounding the problem, Raphael had to invent a system of iconography to allude to various figures for whom there were no traditional visual types. For example, while the <a href="/wiki/Socrates" title="Socrates">Socrates</a> figure is immediately recognizable from Classical busts, the alleged <a href="/wiki/Epicurus" title="Epicurus">Epicurus</a> is far removed from his standard type. Aside from the identities of the figures depicted, many aspects of the fresco have been variously interpreted, but few such interpretations are unanimously accepted among scholars.
</p><p>The popular idea that the rhetorical gestures of Plato and Aristotle are kinds of pointing (to the heavens, and down to earth) is very likely. But Plato's <i><a href="/wiki/Timaeus_(dialogue)" title="Timaeus (dialogue)">Timaeus</a></i> – which is the book Raphael places in his hand – was a sophisticated treatment of space, time, and change, including the Earth, which guided mathematical sciences for over a millennium. Aristotle, with his four-elements theory, held that all change on Earth was owing to motions of the heavens. In the painting Aristotle carries his <i><a href="/wiki/Ethics_(Aristotle)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethics (Aristotle)">Ethics</a></i>, which he denied could be reduced to a mathematical science. It is not certain how much the young Raphael knew of ancient philosophy, what guidance he might have had from people such as <a href="/wiki/Donato_Bramante" title="Donato Bramante">Bramante</a>, or whether a detailed program was dictated by his sponsor, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Julius_II" title="Pope Julius II">Pope Julius II</a>.
</p><p>Nevertheless, the fresco has even recently been  interpreted as an exhortation to philosophy and, in a deeper way, as a visual representation of the role of Love in elevating people toward upper knowledge, largely in consonance with contemporary theories of Marsilio Ficino and other neo-Platonic thinkers linked to Raphael.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Finally, according to Vasari, the scene includes Raphael himself, the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Mantua" class="mw-redirect" title="Duke of Mantua">Duke of Mantua</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zoroaster" title="Zoroaster">Zoroaster</a> and some <a href="/wiki/Evangelism" title="Evangelism">Evangelists</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, to <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_W%C3%B6lfflin" title="Heinrich Wölfflin">Heinrich Wölfflin</a>, "it is quite wrong to attempt interpretations of the <i>School of Athens</i> as an esoteric treatise ... The all-important thing was the artistic motive which expressed a physical or spiritual state, and the name of the person was a matter of indifference" in Raphael's time.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Raphael's artistry then orchestrates a beautiful space, continuous with that of viewers in the Stanza, in which a great variety of human figures, each one expressing "mental states by physical actions," interact, in a "polyphony" unlike anything in earlier art, in the ongoing dialogue of Philosophy.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An interpretation of the fresco relating to hidden symmetries of the figures and the star constructed by Bramante was given by Guerino Mazzola and collaborators.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The main basis are two mirrored triangles on the drawing from Bramante (Euclid), which correspond to the feet positions of certain figures.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Figures">Figures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_School_of_Athens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Figures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The identities of some of the philosophers in the picture, such as Plato and Aristotle, are certain. Beyond that, identifications of Raphael's figures have always been hypothetical. To complicate matters, beginning from Vasari's efforts, some have received multiple identifications, not only as ancients but also as figures contemporary with Raphael. <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Vasari" title="Giorgio Vasari">Vasari</a> mentions portraits of the young <a href="/wiki/Federico_II_Gonzaga,_Duke_of_Mantua" title="Federico II Gonzaga, Duke of Mantua">Federico II Gonzaga, Duke of Mantua</a>, leaning over Bramante with his hands raised near the bottom right, and Raphael himself.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  He was writing over 40 years after the painting, and never knew Raphael, but no doubt reflects what was believed in his time.  Many other popular identifications of portraits are very dubious.
</p><p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luitpold_Dussler&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Luitpold Dussler (page does not exist)">Luitpold Dussler</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;&#91;<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luitpold_Dussler" class="extiw" title="de:Luitpold Dussler">de</a>&#93;</span> counts among those who can be identified with some certainty: Plato, Aristotle, Socrates, Pythagoras,<sup id="cite_ref-Raffael_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raffael-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Euclid,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Ptolemy, Zoroaster, Raphael, Sodoma and Diogenes of Sinope. Other identifications he holds to be "more or less speculative".<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A more comprehensive list of proposed identifications is given below:<sup id="cite_ref-Lahanas_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lahanas-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>

<h3><span id="Central_figures_.2814_and_15.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_figures_(14_and_15)">Central figures (14 and 15)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_School_of_Athens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Central figures (14 and 15)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sanzio_01_Plato_Aristotle.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Sanzio_01_Plato_Aristotle.jpg/180px-Sanzio_01_Plato_Aristotle.jpg" decoding="async" width="180" height="236" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Sanzio_01_Plato_Aristotle.jpg/270px-Sanzio_01_Plato_Aristotle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Sanzio_01_Plato_Aristotle.jpg/360px-Sanzio_01_Plato_Aristotle.jpg 2x" data-file-width="804" data-file-height="1052" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sanzio_01_Plato_Aristotle.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An elder <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> walks alongside <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>.</div></div></div>

<p>In the center of the fresco, at its architecture's central <a href="/wiki/Vanishing_point" title="Vanishing point">vanishing point</a>, are the two undisputed main subjects: <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> on the left and <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, his student, on the right. Both figures hold modern (of the time), bound copies of their books in their left hands, while gesturing with their right. Plato holds <i><a href="/wiki/Timaeus_(dialogue)" title="Timaeus (dialogue)">Timaeus</a></i>,  Aristotle his <i><a href="/wiki/Nicomachean_Ethics" title="Nicomachean Ethics">Nicomachean Ethics</a></i>. Plato is depicted as old, grey, and bare-foot.  By contrast, Aristotle, slightly ahead of him, is in mature manhood, wearing sandals and gold-trimmed robes, and the youth about them seem to look his way.  In addition, these two central figures gesture along different dimensions: Plato vertically, upward along the picture-plane, into the vault above; Aristotle on the horizontal plane at right-angles to the picture-plane (hence in strong <a href="/wiki/Foreshortening" class="mw-redirect" title="Foreshortening">foreshortening</a>), initiating a flow of space toward viewers.
</p><p>It is popularly thought that their gestures indicate central aspects of their philosophies, for <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>, his <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_Forms" class="mw-redirect" title="Theory of Forms">Theory of Forms</a>, and for Aristotle, with an emphasis on concrete <a href="/wiki/Particular" title="Particular">particulars</a>. Many interpret the painting to show a divergence of the two philosophical schools. Plato argues a sense of timelessness whilst Aristotle looks into the physicality of life and the present realm.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Setting">Setting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_School_of_Athens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Setting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Raffael_060.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Raffael_060.jpg/220px-Raffael_060.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="268" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Raffael_060.jpg/330px-Raffael_060.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Raffael_060.jpg/440px-Raffael_060.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2024" data-file-height="2467" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Raffael_060.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Detail of the architecture</div></div></div>
<p>The building is in the shape of a <a href="/wiki/St._Clement%27s_Cross" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Clement&#39;s Cross">Greek cross</a>, which some have suggested was intended to show a harmony between <a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">pagan</a> <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christian_theology" title="Christian theology">Christian theology</a><sup id="cite_ref-JJ_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JJ-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> (see <a href="/wiki/Christianity_and_Paganism" title="Christianity and Paganism">Christianity and Paganism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christian_philosophy" title="Christian philosophy">Christian philosophy</a>). The architecture of the building was inspired by the work of <a href="/wiki/Donato_Bramante" title="Donato Bramante">Bramante</a>, who, according to <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Vasari" title="Giorgio Vasari">Vasari</a>, helped Raphael with the architecture in the picture.<sup id="cite_ref-JJ_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JJ-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> (The resulting architecture was similar to the then new <a href="/wiki/St._Peter%27s_Basilica" title="St. Peter&#39;s Basilica">St. Peter's Basilica</a>.)<sup id="cite_ref-JJ_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JJ-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are two sculptures in the background. The one on the left is the god <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a>, god of light, archery and music, holding a <a href="/wiki/Lyre" title="Lyre">lyre</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-JJ_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JJ-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The sculpture on the right is <a href="/wiki/Athena" title="Athena">Athena</a>, goddess of <a href="/wiki/Wisdom" title="Wisdom">wisdom</a>, in her Roman guise as <a href="/wiki/Minerva" title="Minerva">Minerva</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-JJ_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JJ-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The main arch, above the characters, shows a <a href="/wiki/Meander_(art)" title="Meander (art)">meander</a> (also known as a Greek fret or Greek key design), a design using continuous lines that repeat in a "series of rectangular bends" which originated on pottery of the Greek Geometric period and then become widely used in ancient Greek architectural friezes.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Drawings_and_cartoon">Drawings and cartoon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_School_of_Athens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Drawings and cartoon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A number of drawings made by Raphael as studies for the School of Athens are extant.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> A study for the Diogenes is in the <a href="/wiki/St%C3%A4del" class="mw-redirect" title="Städel">Städel</a> in <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> while a study for the group around Pythagoras, in the lower left of the painting, is preserved in the <a href="/wiki/Albertina" title="Albertina">Albertina</a> Museum in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Several drawings, showing the two men talking while walking up the steps on the right and the <a href="/wiki/Medusa" title="Medusa">Medusa</a> on Athena's shield,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> the statue of Athena (Minerva) and three other statues,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> a study for the combat-scene in the relief below Apollo<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> and "Euclid" teaching his pupils<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> are in the <a href="/wiki/Ashmolean_Museum_of_Art_and_Archaeology" class="mw-redirect" title="Ashmolean Museum of Art and Archaeology">Ashmolean Museum of Art and Archaeology</a> at <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxford University">Oxford University</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Cartoon#Fine_art" title="Cartoon">cartoon</a> for the painting is in the <a href="/wiki/Pinacoteca_Ambrosiana" class="mw-redirect" title="Pinacoteca Ambrosiana">Pinacoteca Ambrosiana</a> in <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_School_of_Athens_by_Raphael_Copy_(5987367322).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/The_School_of_Athens_by_Raphael_Copy_%285987367322%29.jpg/220px-The_School_of_Athens_by_Raphael_Copy_%285987367322%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/The_School_of_Athens_by_Raphael_Copy_%285987367322%29.jpg/330px-The_School_of_Athens_by_Raphael_Copy_%285987367322%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/The_School_of_Athens_by_Raphael_Copy_%285987367322%29.jpg/440px-The_School_of_Athens_by_Raphael_Copy_%285987367322%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="719" data-file-height="485" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_School_of_Athens_by_Raphael_Copy_(5987367322).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The copy in the Victoria and Albert Museum</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Victoria_and_Albert_Museum" title="Victoria and Albert Museum">Victoria and Albert Museum</a> has a rectangular version over 4 metres by 8 metres in size, painted on canvas, dated 1755 by <a href="/wiki/Anton_Raphael_Mengs" title="Anton Raphael Mengs">Anton Raphael Mengs</a> on display in the eastern Cast Court.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Modern reproductions of the fresco abound.  For example, a full-size one can be seen in the auditorium of Old Cabell Hall at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Virginia" title="University of Virginia">University of Virginia</a>. Produced in 1902 by George W. Breck to replace an older reproduction that was destroyed in a fire in 1895, it is four inches off scale from the original, because the Vatican would not allow identical reproductions of its art works.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Other reproductions include: by Neide, in  <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsberg_Cathedral" title="Königsberg Cathedral">Königsberg Cathedral</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Kaliningrad" title="Kaliningrad">Kaliningrad</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> in the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Carolina_at_Asheville" title="University of North Carolina at Asheville">University of North Carolina at Asheville</a>'s Highsmith University Student Union, and a recent one in the seminar room at <a href="/wiki/Baylor_University" title="Baylor University">Baylor University</a>'s Brooks College. A copy of Raphael's School of Athens was painted on the wall of the ceremonial stairwell that leads to the famous, main-floor reading room of the <a href="/wiki/Sainte-Genevi%C3%A8ve_Library" title="Sainte-Geneviève Library">Bibliothèque Sainte-Geneviève</a> in Paris.
</p><p>The two figures at the left of Plotinus were used as part of the cover art of both <i><a href="/wiki/Use_Your_Illusion_I" title="Use Your Illusion I">Use Your Illusion I</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Use_Your_Illusion_II" title="Use Your Illusion II">II</a></i> albums of <a href="/wiki/Guns_N%27_Roses" title="Guns N&#39; Roses">Guns N' Roses</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theme">Theme</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_School_of_Athens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Theme">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MANNapoli_124545_plato%27s_academy_mosaic.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/MANNapoli_124545_plato%27s_academy_mosaic.jpg/220px-MANNapoli_124545_plato%27s_academy_mosaic.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="225" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/MANNapoli_124545_plato%27s_academy_mosaic.jpg/330px-MANNapoli_124545_plato%27s_academy_mosaic.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/MANNapoli_124545_plato%27s_academy_mosaic.jpg/440px-MANNapoli_124545_plato%27s_academy_mosaic.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2392" data-file-height="2441" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MANNapoli_124545_plato%27s_academy_mosaic.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Plato%27s_Academy_mosaic" title="Plato&#39;s Academy mosaic">Plato's Academy mosaic</a> from <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">Pompeii</a></div></div></div>
<p>A similar theme is known as <a href="/wiki/Plato%27s_Academy_mosaic" title="Plato&#39;s Academy mosaic">Plato's Academy mosaic</a>, and perhaps emerged in form of statues at the <a href="/wiki/Serapeum_of_Alexandria" title="Serapeum of Alexandria">Serapeum of Alexandria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Memphis,_Egypt" title="Memphis, Egypt">Memphis</a> <a href="/wiki/Saqqara" title="Saqqara">Saqqara</a>. Mimaut mentioned in the 19th century nine statues at Serapeum of Alexandria holding rolls. Eleven statues were found at <a href="/wiki/Saqqara" title="Saqqara">Saqqara</a>. A review of "Les Statues Ptolémaïques du Sarapieion de Memphis" noted they were probably sculpted in the 3rd century with limestone and stucco, some standing others sitting. <a href="/wiki/Alan_Rowe_(archaeologist)" title="Alan Rowe (archaeologist)">Rowe</a> and Rees 1956 suggested that both scenes in the Serapeum of Alexandria and Saqqara share a similar theme, such as with Plato's Academy mosaic, with Saqqara figures attributed to: "(1) Pindare, (2) Démétrios de Phalère, (3) x (?), (4) Orphée (?) aux oiseaux, (5) Hésiode, (6) Homère, (7) x (?), (8) Protagoras, (9) Thalès, (10) Héraclite, (11) Platon, (12) Aristote (?)."<sup id="cite_ref-RoweRees1956_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RoweRees1956-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> However, there have been other suggestions, see for instance Mattusch 2008, a common identification seems to be Plato as a central figure and Thales.<sup id="cite_ref-Joplin_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Joplin-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
