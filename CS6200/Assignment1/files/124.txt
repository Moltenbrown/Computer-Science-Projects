<p>The <b>Convention on the Elimination of all Forms of Discrimination Against Women</b> (<b>CEDAW</b>) is an international treaty adopted in 1979 by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations General Assembly</a>.
Described as an international <a href="/wiki/Bill_of_rights" title="Bill of rights">bill of rights</a> for women, it was instituted on 3 September 1981 and has been ratified by 189 states.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Over fifty countries that have ratified the Convention have done so subject to certain declarations, reservations, and objections, including 38 countries who rejected the enforcement article 29, which addresses means of settlement for disputes concerning the interpretation or application of the Convention.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Australia's declaration noted the limitations on central government power resulting from its federal constitutional system. The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palau" title="Palau">Palau</a> have signed, but not ratified the treaty. The <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">Tonga</a> are not signatories to CEDAW.
</p><p>The CEDAW Chairperson position is currently held by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hilary_Gbedemah&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hilary Gbedemah (page does not exist)">Hilary Gbedemah</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Convention">The Convention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_Against_Women&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The Convention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Summary">Summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_Against_Women&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Convention has a similar format to the <a href="/wiki/International_Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Racial_Discrimination" title="International Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Racial Discrimination">Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Racial Discrimination</a>, "both with regard to the scope of its substantive obligations and its international monitoring mechanisms".<sup id="cite_ref-:2_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The Convention is structured in six parts with 30 articles total.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Part I</b> (Articles 1-6) focuses on non-discrimination, sex stereotypes, and sex trafficking.</li>
<li><b>Part II</b> (Articles 7-9) outlines women's rights in the public sphere with an emphasis on political life, representation, and rights to nationality.</li>
<li><b>Part III</b> (Articles 10-14) describes the economic and social rights of women, particularly focusing on education, employment, and health. Part III also includes special protections for rural women and the problems they face.</li>
<li><b>Part IV</b> (Article 15 and 16) outlines women's right to equality in marriage and family life along with the right to equality before the law.</li>
<li><b>Part V</b> (Articles 17-22) establishes the Committee on the Elimination of Discrimination against Women as well as the states parties' reporting procedure.</li>
<li><b>Part VI</b> (Articles 23-30) describes the effects of the Convention on other treaties, the commitment of the states parties and the administration of the Convention.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Core_Provisions">Core Provisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_Against_Women&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Core Provisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Article 1</b> defines <a href="/wiki/Sexism" title="Sexism">discrimination against women</a> in the following terms:</p><blockquote><p>Any distinction, exclusion or restriction made on the basis of sex which has the effect or purpose of impairing or nullifying the recognition, enjoyment or exercise by women, irrespective of their marital status, on a basis of equality of men and <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_rights" title="Women&#39;s rights">women</a>, of human rights and fundamental freedoms in the political, economic, social, cultural, civil or any other field.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote><p><b>Article 2</b> mandates that states parties ratifying the Convention declare intent to enshrine <a href="/wiki/Gender_equality" title="Gender equality">gender equality</a> into their domestic legislation, repeal all discriminatory provisions in their laws, and enact new provisions to guard against discrimination against women.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> States ratifying the Convention must also establish tribunals and public institutions to guarantee women effective protection against discrimination, and take steps to eliminate all forms of discrimination practiced against women by individuals, organizations, and enterprises.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Article 3</b> requires states parties to guarantee basic human rights and fundamental freedoms to women "on a basis of equality with men" through the "political, social, economic, and cultural fields."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Article 4</b> notes that "[a]doption...of special measures aimed at accelerating <a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">de facto</a> equality between men and women shall not be considered discrimination." It adds that special protection for <a href="/wiki/Maternity" class="mw-redirect" title="Maternity">maternity</a> is not regarded as gender discrimination.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Article 5</b> requires states parties to take measures to seek to eliminate prejudices and customs based on the idea of the inferiority or the superiority of one sex or on <a href="/wiki/Stereotype" title="Stereotype">stereotyped</a> <a href="/wiki/Gender_role" title="Gender role">role for men and women</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It also mandates the states parties "[t]o ensure...the recognition of the common responsibility of men and women in the upbringing and development of their children."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Article 6</b> obliges states parties to "take all appropriate measures, including legislation, to suppress all forms of <a href="/wiki/Trafficking_in_women" class="mw-redirect" title="Trafficking in women">trafficking in women</a> and <a href="/wiki/Forced_prostitution" title="Forced prostitution">exploitation of prostitution</a> of women."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Article 7</b> guarantees women equality in political and public life with a focus on equality in <a href="/wiki/Suffrage" title="Suffrage">voting</a>, participation in government, and participation in "non-governmental organizations and associations concerned with the public and political life of the country."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Article 8</b> provides that states parties will guarantee women's equal "opportunity to represent their Government at the international level and to participate in the work of <a href="/wiki/International_organizations" class="mw-redirect" title="International organizations">international organizations</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Article 9</b> mandates state parties to "grant women equal rights with men to acquire, change or retain their <a href="/wiki/Nationality" title="Nationality">nationality</a>" and equal rights "with respect to the nationality of their children."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Article 10</b> necessitates equal opportunity in education for female students and encourages <a href="/wiki/Mixed-sex_education" title="Mixed-sex education">coeducation</a>. It also provides equal access to athletics, scholarships and grants as well as requires "reduction in female students' drop out rates."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Article 11</b> outlines the <a href="/wiki/Right_to_work" title="Right to work">right to work</a> for women as "an <a href="/wiki/Unalienable_rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Unalienable rights">unalienable right</a> of all human beings." It requires <a href="/wiki/Equal_pay_for_equal_work" title="Equal pay for equal work">equal pay for equal work</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Right_to_social_security" title="Right to social security">right to social security</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paid_leave" class="mw-redirect" title="Paid leave">paid leave</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maternity_leave" class="mw-redirect" title="Maternity leave">maternity leave</a> "with pay or with comparable <a href="/wiki/Social_benefits" class="mw-redirect" title="Social benefits">social benefits</a> without loss of former employment, seniority or social allowances." Dismissal on the grounds of maternity, <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnancy</a> or status of marriage shall be prohibited with sanction.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Article 12</b> creates the obligation of states parties to "take all appropriate measures to eliminate discrimination against women in the field of healthcare in order to ensure...access to health care services, including those related to <a href="/wiki/Family_planning" title="Family planning">family planning</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Article 13</b> guarantees equality to women "in economic and social life," especially with respect to "the right to family benefits, the right to bank loans, mortgages and other forms of financial credit, and the right to participate in recreational activities, sports and all aspects of cultural life."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Article 14</b> provides protections for rural women and their special problems, ensuring the right of women to participate in development programs, "to have access to adequate health care facilities," "to participate in all community activities," "to have access to agricultural credit" and "to enjoy adequate living conditions."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Article 15</b> obliges states parties to guarantee "women equality with men before the law," including "a <a href="/wiki/Capacity_(law)" title="Capacity (law)">legal capacity</a> identical to that of men." It also accords "to men and women the same rights with regard to the law relating to the <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_movement" title="Freedom of movement">movement of persons</a> and the freedom to choose their residence and domicile."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Article 16</b> prohibits "discrimination against women in all matters relating to marriage and family relations." In particular, it provides men and women with "the same right to enter into marriage, the same right freely to choose a spouse," "the same <a href="/wiki/Marriage_law" title="Marriage law">rights and responsibilities during marriage</a> and at its dissolution," "the same rights and responsibilities as parents," "the same rights to decide freely and responsibly on the number and spacing of their children," "the same personal rights as husband and wife, including the right to choose a family name, a profession and an occupation" "the same rights for both spouses in respect of the ownership, acquisition, management, administration, enjoyment and disposition of property, whether free of charge or for a valuable consideration."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Articles 17 - 24</b> These articles describe the composition and procedures of the CEDAW Committee,like the hierarchical structure and rules and regulations of systematic procedure of the relationship between CEDAW and national and international legislation and the obligation of States to take all steps necessary to implement CEDAW in full form.<sup id="cite_ref-gov.uk_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gov.uk-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Articles 25 - 30</b>  (Administration of CEDAW)
</p><p>These articles describe the general administrative procedures concerning enforcement of CEDAW, ratification and entering reservations of concerned states.<sup id="cite_ref-gov.uk_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gov.uk-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="CEDAW_with_UNSCR_1325_and_1820">CEDAW with UNSCR 1325 and 1820</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_Against_Women&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: CEDAW with UNSCR 1325 and 1820">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Map5.1Discrepant_Behavior_compressed.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Map5.1Discrepant_Behavior_compressed.jpg/400px-Map5.1Discrepant_Behavior_compressed.jpg" decoding="async" width="400" height="250" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Map5.1Discrepant_Behavior_compressed.jpg/600px-Map5.1Discrepant_Behavior_compressed.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Map5.1Discrepant_Behavior_compressed.jpg/800px-Map5.1Discrepant_Behavior_compressed.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2400" data-file-height="1502" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Map5.1Discrepant_Behavior_compressed.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A world map showing countries by CEDAW enforcement, 2010.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Resolutions 1325 10th anniversary events highlight use of CEDAW mechanisms<sup id="cite_ref-gnwp_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gnwp-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></b>
</p><p>The 10th anniversary of Resolution 1325 in October 2010 highlighted the increasing demand for accountability to UN Security Council Resolution 1325 on Women, Peace and Security. Many expressed concern about the fact that only 22 Member States out of 192 have adopted national action plans. Women are still underrepresented, if not totally absent, in most official peace negotiations and sexual violence in peacetime and in conflict continue to increase.
</p><p>These realities emphasized the need to use external legal mechanisms to strengthen the implementation of SCR 1325, particularly CEDAW. The well-established mechanisms of CEDAW – the Member States compliance report and the civil society shadow reporting process were cited as possible instruments to ensure accountability.
</p><p>Several regional and international meetings including the High Level Seminar "1325 in 2020: Looking Forward…Looking Back," organized by the African Center for the Constructive Resolution of Disputes, and the "Stockholm International Conference 10 years with 1325 – What now?" called for the use of CEDAW to improve 1325 implementation.
</p><p><b>Intersection between SCR 1325 and CEDAW</b>
<b><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></b>
</p><p>While CEDAW and UN Security Council Resolutions 1325 and 1820 on Women, Peace and Security are important international instruments on their own, there is also an intersection among the three standards that can be used to enhance their implementation and impact.
</p><p>Resolutions 1325 and 1820 broaden the scope of CEDAW application by clarifying its relevance to all parties in conflict, whereas CEDAW provides concrete strategic guidance for actions to be taken on the broad commitments outlined in the two Resolutions.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>CEDAW is a global human rights treaty that should be incorporated into national law as the highest standard for women's rights. It requires the UN Member States that have ratified it (185 to date) to set in place mechanisms to fully realize <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_rights" title="Women&#39;s rights">women's rights</a>.
</p><p>Resolution 1325 is an international law unanimously adopted by the Security Council that mandates the UN Member States to engage women in all aspects of peacebuilding including ensuring women's participation on all levels of decision–making on peace and security issues.
</p><p>Resolution 1820 links sexual violence as a tactic of war with the maintenance of international peace and security. It also demands a comprehensive report from the UN Secretary-General on implementation and strategies for improving information flow to the Security Council; and the adoption of concrete protection and prevention measures to end sexual violence.
</p><p>Resolutions 1325 and 1820, and CEDAW share the following agenda on women's human rights and <a href="/wiki/Gender_equality" title="Gender equality">gender equality</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-gnwp_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gnwp-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>Demand women’s participation in <a href="/wiki/Decision-making" title="Decision-making">decision-making</a> at all levels</li>
<li>Rejection of violence against women as it impedes the advancement of women and maintains their subordinate status</li>
<li>Equality of women and men under the law; protection of women and girls through the <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_law" title="Rule of law">rule of law</a></li>
<li>Demand security forces and systems to protect women and girls from <a href="/wiki/Gender-based_violence" class="mw-redirect" title="Gender-based violence">gender-based violence</a></li>
<li>Recognition of the fact that distinct experiences and burdens of women and girls come from systemic discrimination</li>
<li>Ensure that women’s experiences, needs and perspectives are incorporated into the political, legal and social decisions that determine the achievement of just and lasting peace</li></ol>
<p>A General Comment from the CEDAW committee could strengthen women’s advocacy for the full implementation of Resolutions 1325 and 1820 at the country and community levels. Conversely, CEDAW’s relevance to conflict-affected areas will be underscored further by the two Resolutions.  In other words, all three international instruments will reinforce each other and be much more effective if used together in leveraging women’s human rights.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Members_and_ratification">Members and ratification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_Against_Women&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Members and ratification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_parties_to_the_Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_Against_Women" title="List of parties to the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women">List of parties to the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women</a></div>
<p>The six UN member states that have not ratified or acceded to the convention are <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palau" title="Palau">Palau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">Tonga</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_Against_Women_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_Against_Women-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The one <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly_observers#Non-member_states" title="United Nations General Assembly observers">UN non-member state</a> that had not acceded to the convention is the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a>/<a href="/wiki/Vatican_City" title="Vatican City">Vatican City</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_Against_Women_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_Against_Women-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Republic of China (Taiwan)</a> in 2007 has also ratified the treaty in its legislature, but is unrecognized by the United Nations and is a party to the treaty only unofficially.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The latest state to have acceded the convention was <a href="/wiki/South_Sudan" title="South Sudan">South Sudan</a> on 30 April 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_Against_Women_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_Against_Women-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Within the United States, over 40 cities and local governments have adopted CEDAW ordinances or resolutions.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reservations">Reservations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_Against_Women&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reservations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Reservation_(law)" title="Reservation (law)">reservations</a> have been entered against certain articles of the Convention.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> There are also some reservations that are not specific to an article within the Convention but rather a general reservation to all aspects of the Convention that would violate a stated principle. For example, <a href="/wiki/Mauritania" title="Mauritania">Mauritania</a> made a reservation stating it approved the Convention "in each and every one of its parts which are not contrary to <a href="/wiki/Sharia" title="Sharia">Islamic Sharia</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-:4_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> A number these reservations, especially those entered by Islamic states parties, are subject to much debate.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Article 28 of the Convention states that "a <a href="/wiki/Reservation_(law)" title="Reservation (law)">reservation</a> incompatible with the object and purpose of the present Convention shall not be permitted."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> As a result, many states parties have entered objections to the reservations of other states parties.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Specifically, many Nordic states parties were concerned that some of the reservations were "undermining the integrity of the text."<sup id="cite_ref-:5_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Over the years, some states parties have withdrawn their reservations.
</p><p>As of May 2015, sixty-two states parties have entered reservations against some part of the Convention.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Twenty-four states parties have entered objections to at least one of these reservations.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The most reserved article is Article 29, concerning dispute resolution and interpretation of the Convention, with thirty-nine reservations.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Because reservations to Article 29 are expressly allowed by the Convention itself, these reservations were not very controversial.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Article 16, concerning the equality of women in marriage and family life is subject to twenty-three reservations.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_16-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The Committee, in General Recommendation No. 28, specifically stated that reservations to Article 2, concerning general non-discrimination, are impermissible.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> However, Article 2 has seventeen reservations.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_16-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Committee_on_the_Elimination_of_Discrimination_against_Women">Committee on the Elimination of Discrimination against Women</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_Against_Women&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Committee on the Elimination of Discrimination against Women">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Committee on the Elimination of Discrimination Against Women is the United Nations (U.N.) treaty body that oversees the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women (CEDAW). The formation of this committee was outlined in <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.un.org/womenwatch/daw/cedaw/text/econvention.htm#article17">Article 17</a> of the CEDAW, which also established the rules, purpose, and operating procedures of the committee.<sup id="cite_ref-Fact_Sheet_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fact_Sheet-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Throughout its years of operation the committee has held multiple sessions to ensure the rules outlined in the CEDAW are being followed. Over time the practices of the committee have evolved due to an increased focus on <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_rights" title="Women&#39;s rights">women's rights</a> issues.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_committee">History of the committee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_Against_Women&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History of the committee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Committee on the Elimination of Discrimination Against Women was formed on 3 September 1981 after the CEDAW received the 20 <a href="/wiki/Ratification" title="Ratification">ratifications</a> required for it to enter into force. Article 17 of the CEDAW established the committee in order to ensure that the provisions of the CEDAW were followed by the countries that had signed and agreed to be bound by it.<sup id="cite_ref-Fact_Sheet_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fact_Sheet-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The first regular session of the committee was held from 18–22 October 1982. In this session the first officers of the committee were elected by simple majority, with Ms. L. Ider of <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a> becoming chairperson.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Work_Of_CEDAW_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Work_Of_CEDAW-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Other officers elected were three vice-chairpersons: M.&#160;Caron of Canada, Z.&#160;Ilic of <a href="/wiki/Yugoslavia" title="Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a> and L.&#160;Mukayiranga of <a href="/wiki/Rwanda" title="Rwanda">Rwanda</a>. The final officer elected was D.&#160;P. Bernard of <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a> as <a href="/wiki/Rapporteur" title="Rapporteur">rapporteur</a> of the committee. During this session, the committee also unanimously approved to adopt its <a href="/wiki/Rules_of_procedure" class="mw-redirect" title="Rules of procedure">rules of procedure</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Work_Of_CEDAW_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Work_Of_CEDAW-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sessions">Sessions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_Against_Women&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sessions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Palais_des_nations.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Palais_des_nations.jpg/220px-Palais_des_nations.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Palais_des_nations.jpg/330px-Palais_des_nations.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Palais_des_nations.jpg/440px-Palais_des_nations.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1550" data-file-height="1160" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Palais_des_nations.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Palais_des_Nations" class="mw-redirect" title="Palais des Nations">Palais des Nations</a></div></div></div>
<p>The rules regarding where and when the committee can hold sessions are laid out in their rules of procedure.<sup id="cite_ref-Rules_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rules-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:UN_Headquarters.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/UN_Headquarters.jpg/220px-UN_Headquarters.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/UN_Headquarters.jpg/330px-UN_Headquarters.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/UN_Headquarters.jpg/440px-UN_Headquarters.jpg 2x" data-file-width="768" data-file-height="1024" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:UN_Headquarters.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/UN_Headquarters" class="mw-redirect" title="UN Headquarters">UN Headquarters</a></div></div></div>
<p>The committee is allowed to hold as many meetings as are required to perform their duties effectively, with the <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">states</a> party to the CEDAW and the <a href="/wiki/Secretary-General_of_the_United_Nations" title="Secretary-General of the United Nations">Secretary-General of the United Nations</a> authorizing the number of regular sessions held.<sup id="cite_ref-Rules_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rules-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> In addition, special sessions can be held at the request of either a state party to the convention or the majority of the members serving on the committee.<sup id="cite_ref-Rules_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rules-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Seventy-two sessions have been held to date, with the most recent taking place from 18 February to 9 March 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-Sessions_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sessions-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The first thirty-nine sessions were held at the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_headquarters" class="mw-redirect" title="United Nations headquarters">United Nations headquarters</a> building in New York City, with the fortieth and subsequent sessions held at the <a href="/wiki/Palais_des_Nations" class="mw-redirect" title="Palais des Nations">Palais des Nations</a> in Geneva.<sup id="cite_ref-Sessions_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sessions-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> At its regular sessions, the Committee hears reports from states party to the CEDAW on their progress in adhering to CEDAW and implementing its ideas in their countries.<sup id="cite_ref-Report_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Report-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The committee also holds pre-sessional work groups to discuss the issues and questions that the committee should deal with during the following session.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reports">Reports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_Against_Women&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Under <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.un.org/womenwatch/daw/cedaw/text/econvention.htm#article18">article 18</a> of the CEDAW states must report to the committee on the progress they have made in implementing the CEDAW within their state.<sup id="cite_ref-Rules_22-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rules-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> As most of the information the committee works with comes from these reports, guidelines have been developed to help states prepare accurate and useful reports.<sup id="cite_ref-Methods_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Methods-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Initial reports discussing the current picture of discrimination against women in the reporting states are required to specifically deal with each article of the CEDAW, and consist of no more than one-hundred pages.<sup id="cite_ref-Rules_22-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rules-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> States are required to prepare and present these initial reports within one year of ratifying the CEDAW.<sup id="cite_ref-Fact_Sheet_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fact_Sheet-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Periodic reports detailing the state's progress in adhering to the articles of the CEDAW should be no more than seventy-five pages in length and should focus on the specific period of time since the state's last report.<sup id="cite_ref-Rules_22-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rules-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> States party to the CEDAW are typically required to provide periodic reports every four years, but if the committee is concerned about the situation in that state they can request a report at any time.<sup id="cite_ref-Fact_Sheet_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fact_Sheet-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The committee chooses which reports addressing by considering factors such as the amount of time the report has been pending, whether the report is initial or periodic (with more priority given to initial reports), and from which region the report originates.<sup id="cite_ref-Rules_22-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rules-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Eight states are invited to give their reports during each session and it is required a representative from the state is in attendance when the report is presented.<sup id="cite_ref-Rules_22-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rules-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The committee focuses on constructive dialogue when a report is presented and appreciates careful time management on the part of the state presenting its report.<sup id="cite_ref-Rules_22-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rules-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Due to the high backlog of overdue reports the committee has encouraged states to combine all of their outstanding reports into one document and sends reminders to states who have reports that are five years overdue.<sup id="cite_ref-Rules_22-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rules-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The CEDAW also requires that the committee provide an annual report that includes its activities, comments relating to the reports provided by states, information relating to the Optional Protocol of the CEDAW, and any other general suggestions or recommendations the committee has made.<sup id="cite_ref-Rules_22-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rules-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> This report is given to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations General Assembly</a> through the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Economic_and_Social_Council" title="United Nations Economic and Social Council">Economic and Social Council</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rules_22-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rules-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> All reports, agendas and other official documents pertaining to the committee, including the reports provided by the states, are provided to the public unless otherwise decided by the committee.<sup id="cite_ref-Rules_22-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rules-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_Recommendations">General Recommendations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_Against_Women&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: General Recommendations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Along with issuing its annual report and offering advice to reporting states, the committee has the ability to issue general recommendations that elaborate on its views of the obligations imposed by CEDAW.<sup id="cite_ref-Methods_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Methods-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> To date, the committee has issued thirty-two general recommendations, the latest dealing with the gender related dimensions of refugee status, asylum, nationality and statelessness of women.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The recommendations issued by the committee in its first decade were short and dealt mainly with the content of states’ reports and reservations to the convention.<sup id="cite_ref-Methods_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Methods-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Since 1991, however, recommendations have been focused on guiding states’ application of the CEDAW in specific situations.<sup id="cite_ref-Methods_25-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Methods-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The formulation of a general recommendation begins with dialogue between the committee on the topic in the recommendation with various non-governmental organizations and other U.N. bodies.<sup id="cite_ref-Methods_25-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Methods-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The recommendation is then drafted by a member of the committee and discussed and revised in the next session, and finally adopted in the following session.<sup id="cite_ref-Methods_25-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Methods-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<li><b>General Recommendation No. 32</b> (2014) discusses "gender-related dimensions of refugee status, asylum, nationality and statelessness of women."<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Changes_in_the_committee">Changes in the committee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_Against_Women&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Changes in the committee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For the first ten years the committee operated significantly differently from now. The only form of <a href="/wiki/Censure" title="Censure">censure</a> given to the committee by the CEDAW was their general recommendations and concluding comments following a report.<sup id="cite_ref-Reilly_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reilly-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Due to the emergence of the Global Campaign for Women's Human Rights in 1991 more attention was given to the CEDAW, reviving the committee.<sup id="cite_ref-Reilly_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reilly-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The committee made changes to the CEDAW that allowed it to meet more than once a year, and have taken advantage of this by meeting at least twice a year since 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-Reilly_37-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reilly-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The committee originally only met for two weeks in its annual sessions, but that has now been changed to meeting multiple times a year in eighteen-day sessions.<sup id="cite_ref-Report_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Report-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> CEDAW also gained new complaint and inquiry proceedings allowing the committee to initiate inquiry proceedings if it believes a state is in severe violation of the articles of the CEDAW.<sup id="cite_ref-Reilly_37-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reilly-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recommendations_for_improvement">Recommendations for improvement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_Against_Women&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Recommendations for improvement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite evolving since the committee was first formed, members believe there are ways in which the committee can better meet the goals outlined in the CEDAW.<sup id="cite_ref-Fact_Sheet_20-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fact_Sheet-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> One of the committee's main goals moving forward is expanding its information base, allowing it to more effectively deal with issues that arise concerning the CEDAW.<sup id="cite_ref-Fact_Sheet_20-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fact_Sheet-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The committee is authorized in <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.un.org/womenwatch/daw/cedaw/text/econvention.htm#article22">Article 22</a> of the CEDAW to invite specialized U.N. agencies such as the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Development_Programme" title="United Nations Development Programme">United Nations Development Programme</a> to deliver reports discussing women's rights issues in the state under discussion.<sup id="cite_ref-Fact_Sheet_20-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fact_Sheet-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Another method for gathering information is requesting reports from non-governmental organizations dealing with discrimination against women that are operating in the country under discussion.<sup id="cite_ref-Fact_Sheet_20-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fact_Sheet-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> This is recommended to insure that the committee is receiving the full, unbiased picture of affairs within the reporting state.<sup id="cite_ref-Fact_Sheet_20-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fact_Sheet-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another recommendation for improvement involves interpreting and clarifying the language used in the CEDAW in order to make the document as useful as it can be.<sup id="cite_ref-Fact_Sheet_20-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fact_Sheet-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> A third improvement that has been suggested is improving the efficiency of the committee.<sup id="cite_ref-Fact_Sheet_20-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fact_Sheet-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Due to the backlog in reports faced by the committee it has been suggested that the government officials who prepare reports presented to the committee should be trained, in order to make all reports uniform and more easily processed.<sup id="cite_ref-Fact_Sheet_20-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fact_Sheet-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> A final suggestion for improvement is the implementation of a right of petition in the CEDAW, allowing the committee to hear complaints from citizens of a state against the state, increasing the committee's strength and direct impact on the problem of discrimination against women.<sup id="cite_ref-Fact_Sheet_20-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fact_Sheet-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Languages">Languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_Against_Women&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The official languages of the committee are <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>, with any statement made in one of the official languages translated into the other four.<sup id="cite_ref-Rules_22-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rules-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> A speaker who does not speak one of the official languages provides a translator.<sup id="cite_ref-Rules_22-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rules-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> All formal decisions and documents issued by the committee are provided in each of the official languages.<sup id="cite_ref-Rules_22-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rules-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The original rules of procedure adopted by the committee did not include Arabic as an official language, but the rule was amended in the committees second session to include Arabic.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Work_Of_CEDAW_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Work_Of_CEDAW-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Members_and_Officers_of_the_Committee">Members and Officers of the Committee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_Against_Women&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Members and Officers of the Committee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Twenty-three members serve on the committee, described as experts for their experience and expertise in women's issues.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The members are nominated by their national governments and elected through a secret ballot by states party to the convention.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Upon winning the election and taking up their responsibilities the members of the committee recite the following statement, known as the solemn declaration, "I solemnly declare that I shall perform my duties and exercise powers as a member of the Committee on the Elimination of Discrimination Against Women honourably, faithfully, impartially and conscientiously".<sup id="cite_ref-Rules_22-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rules-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The members come from a wide range of occupations including doctors, lawyers, diplomats and educators, providing various viewpoints to the committee due to their diversity.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_38-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Many members continue to hold full-time jobs outside the committee and receive little monetary payment for their work on the committee.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_38-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>To insure that the nationality of members encompasses all the diverse states who have signed the CEDAW, members are elected according to regions divided into <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_38-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The members of the committee differ from those of other treaty bodies of the United Nations in that they have all been women with only one exception.<sup id="cite_ref-Fact_Sheet_20-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fact_Sheet-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In the event a member of the committee is unable to continue serving on the committee before her term is up the state that had nominated the resigning member shall nominate another expert from their country to fill in her seat.<sup id="cite_ref-Rules_22-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rules-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Committee members and experts also attend an annual luncheon, hosted by the <a href="/wiki/NGO_Committee_on_the_Status_of_Women,_New_York" title="NGO Committee on the Status of Women, New York">NGO Committee on the Status of Women, NY</a> (NGO CSW/NY), where key issues are discusses and the efforts of the committee are honored.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Officers of the Committee</b>
</p><p>The officers of the committee are composed of a chairperson, three vice-chairpersons and a rapporteur.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Officers of the committee are nominated by another member of the committee, as opposed to a government which nominates members for the committee.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Work_Of_CEDAW_21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Work_Of_CEDAW-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> All officers are elected by majority vote to a two-year term of office, and remain eligible for re-election after their term expires.<sup id="cite_ref-Rules_22-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rules-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The chairperson's duties include declaring a meeting to be open or closed, directing the discussion in a session, announcing decisions made by the committee, preparing agendas in consultation with the secretary-general, designating the members of pre-sessional working groups and representing the committee at United Nations meetings which the committee is invited to participate in.<sup id="cite_ref-Rules_22-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rules-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> In the case the chairperson is unable to perform any her duties she designates one of the three vice-chairpersons to take over her role. If the chairperson fails to designate a vice-chairperson prior to her absence then the vice-chairperson with the first name in English alphabetical order takes over.<sup id="cite_ref-Rules_22-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rules-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> In the event an officer is unable to continue serving on the committee before her term expires a new officer from the same region as the original officer shall be nominated, elected and will take over the vacated office.<sup id="cite_ref-Rules_22-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rules-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Optional_Protocol">Optional Protocol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_Against_Women&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Optional Protocol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Optional_Protocol_to_the_Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_against_Women" title="Optional Protocol to the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination against Women">Optional Protocol to the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination against Women</a> is a side-agreement to the Convention which allows its parties to recognise the competence of the Committee on the Elimination of Discrimination Against Women to consider complaints from individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Optional Protocol was adopted by the UN General Assembly on 6 October 1999 and entered into force on 22 December 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Currently it has 80 signatories and 109 parties.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_Against_Women&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Controversy around CEDAW comes from two opposite directions: social and religious <a href="/wiki/Conservative" class="mw-redirect" title="Conservative">conservatives</a> which claim that CEDAW is seeking to impose a <a href="/wiki/Liberalism" title="Liberalism">liberal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Progressivism" title="Progressivism">progressive</a>, <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminist</a> standard on countries, in detriment of <a href="/wiki/Traditional_values" class="mw-redirect" title="Traditional values">traditional values</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Radical_feminists" class="mw-redirect" title="Radical feminists">radical feminists</a>, who are skeptical of the power, or even desire, of CEDAW to radically transform societies and truly liberate women, and claim that CEDAW adheres to a form of weak <a href="/wiki/Liberal_feminism" title="Liberal feminism">liberal feminism</a> similar to other mainstream organizations. They also claim that UN members cannot create goals which represent values of the poor, conservative, religious, or weak, because few, if any, UN officials/staff are actually from this group for which they claim to represent. <sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
