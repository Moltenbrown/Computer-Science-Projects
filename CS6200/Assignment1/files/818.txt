<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Holbein-erasmus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Holbein-erasmus.jpg/150px-Holbein-erasmus.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="212" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Holbein-erasmus.jpg/225px-Holbein-erasmus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Holbein-erasmus.jpg/300px-Holbein-erasmus.jpg 2x" data-file-width="809" data-file-height="1145" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Holbein-erasmus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Erasmus" title="Erasmus">Erasmus of Rotterdam</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Because of the great number of perspectives which can be taken on the subject, it is impossible to form a single, all-embracing conception of European culture.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Nonetheless, there are core elements which are generally agreed upon as forming the cultural foundation of modern Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> One list of these elements given by K. Bochmann includes:<sup id="cite_ref-K.Bochmann(1990)_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-K.Bochmann(1990)-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>A common cultural and spiritual heritage derived from <a href="/wiki/Greco-Roman" class="mw-redirect" title="Greco-Roman">Greco-Roman</a> antiquity, <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">Humanism</a>, the political thinking of the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>, and the developments of <a href="/wiki/Modernity" title="Modernity">Modernity</a>, including all types of <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-Berting_2006_page=52_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berting_2006_page=52-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-K.Bochmann(1990)_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-K.Bochmann(1990)-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>A rich and dynamic material culture that has been extended to the other continents as the result of <a href="/wiki/Industrialization" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrialization">industrialization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">colonialism</a> during the "<a href="/wiki/Great_Divergence" title="Great Divergence">Great Divergence</a>";<sup id="cite_ref-Berting_2006_page=52_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berting_2006_page=52-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>A specific conception of the individual expressed by the existence of, and respect for, a legality that guarantees <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Political_freedom" title="Political freedom">liberty of the individual</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-Berting_2006_page=52_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berting_2006_page=52-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>A plurality of states with different political orders, which are feeding each other with new ideas;<sup id="cite_ref-Berting_2006_page=52_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berting_2006_page=52-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Respect for peoples, states and nations outside Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-Berting_2006_page=52_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berting_2006_page=52-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Berting says that these points fit with "Europe's most positive realisations".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
The concept of European culture is generally linked to the classical definition of the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western world</a>. In this definition, Western culture is the set of <a href="/wiki/Western_literature" title="Western literature">literary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">scientific</a>, <a href="/wiki/Political_modernity" class="mw-redirect" title="Political modernity">political</a>, <a href="/wiki/European_art" class="mw-redirect" title="European art">artistic</a> and <a href="/wiki/European_philosophy" class="mw-redirect" title="European philosophy">philosophical</a> principles which set it apart from other civilizations. Much of this set of traditions and knowledge is collected in the <a href="/wiki/Western_canon" title="Western canon">Western canon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The term has come to apply to countries whose history has been strongly marked by European immigration or settlement during the 18th and 19th centuries, such as <a href="/wiki/The_Americas" class="mw-redirect" title="The Americas">the Americas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a>, and is not restricted to Europe..
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prize</a> laureate in Literature <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Stearns_Eliot" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Stearns Eliot">Thomas Stearns Eliot</a> in his 1948 book <i>Notes Towards the Definition of Culture</i>, credited the prominent <a href="/wiki/Role_of_the_Christian_Church_in_civilization" class="mw-redirect" title="Role of the Christian Church in civilization">Christian influence</a> upon the European culture:<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> "It is in Christianity that our arts have developed; it is in Christianity that the laws of Europe have--until recently--been rooted."
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Art">Art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Venus_of_Willendorf_frontview_retouched.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Venus_of_Willendorf_frontview_retouched.jpg/150px-Venus_of_Willendorf_frontview_retouched.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="282" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Venus_of_Willendorf_frontview_retouched.jpg/225px-Venus_of_Willendorf_frontview_retouched.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Venus_of_Willendorf_frontview_retouched.jpg/300px-Venus_of_Willendorf_frontview_retouched.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1322" data-file-height="2486" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Venus_of_Willendorf_frontview_retouched.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Venus_of_Willendorf" title="Venus of Willendorf">Venus of Willendorf</a>, figure from between 28,000 and 25,000 BC. Now in the <a href="/wiki/Naturhistorisches_Museum" class="mw-redirect" title="Naturhistorisches Museum">Naturhistorisches Museum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>. An example of prehistoric art.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/European_art" class="mw-redirect" title="European art">European art</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_painting" title="Western painting">Western painting</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistoric_art">Prehistoric art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Prehistoric art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_art" title="Prehistoric art">Prehistoric art</a></div>
<p>Surviving European <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_art" title="Prehistoric art">prehistoric art</a> mainly comprises sculpture and rock art. It includes the oldest known representation of the human body, the <a href="/wiki/Venus_of_Hohle_Fels" title="Venus of Hohle Fels">Venus of Hohle Fel</a>, dating from 40,000-35,000 BC, found in <a href="/wiki/Schelklingen" title="Schelklingen">Schelklingen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and the <a href="/wiki/L%C3%B6wenmensch_figurine" class="mw-redirect" title="Löwenmensch figurine">Löwenmensch figurine</a>, from about 30,000 BC, the oldest undisputed piece of figurative art. The <a href="/wiki/Swimming_Reindeer" title="Swimming Reindeer">Swimming Reindeer</a> of about 11,000 BCE is among the finest <a href="/wiki/Magdalenian" title="Magdalenian">Magdalenian</a> carvings in bone or antler of animals in the <a href="/wiki/Art_of_the_Upper_Paleolithic" title="Art of the Upper Paleolithic">art of the Upper Paleolithic</a>. At the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a> in Europe figurative sculpture greatly reduced, and remained a less common element in art than relief decoration of practical objects until the Roman period, despite some works such as the <a href="/wiki/Gundestrup_cauldron" title="Gundestrup cauldron">Gundestrup cauldron</a> from the <a href="/wiki/European_Iron_Age" class="mw-redirect" title="European Iron Age">European Iron Age</a> and the Bronze Age <a href="/wiki/Trundholm_sun_chariot" title="Trundholm sun chariot">Trundholm sun chariot</a>. The oldest European cave art dates back 40,800<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and can be found in the <a href="/wiki/El_Castillo_Cave" class="mw-redirect" title="El Castillo Cave">El Castillo Cave</a> in Spain, but cave art exists across the continent. Rock painting was also performed on cliff faces, but fewer of those paintings have survived because of erosion. One well-known example is the rock paintings of <a href="/wiki/Astuvansalmi_rock_paintings" title="Astuvansalmi rock paintings">Astuvansalmi</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Saimaa" title="Saimaa">Saimaa</a> area of Finland.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Rock_art_of_the_Iberian_Mediterranean_Basin" title="Rock art of the Iberian Mediterranean Basin">Rock art of the Iberian Mediterranean Basin</a> forms a distinct group with the human figure the main focus, often seen in large groups, with battles, dancing and hunting all represented, as well as other activities and details such as clothing. The figures are generally rather sketchily depicted in thin paint, with the relationships between the groups of humans and animals more carefully depicted than individual figures. Prehistoric <a href="/wiki/Celtic_art" title="Celtic art">Celtic art</a> is another distinct grouping from much of <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age_Europe" title="Iron Age Europe">Iron Age Europe</a> and survives mainly in the form of high-status metalwork skillfully decorated with complex, elegant and mostly abstract designs, often using curving and spiral forms. Full-length human figures of any size are so rare that their absence may represent a religious taboo. As the Romans conquered Celtic territories the style vanished, except in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, where it influenced the <a href="/wiki/Insular_style" class="mw-redirect" title="Insular style">Insular style</a> of the Early Middle Ages.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_art">Classical art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Classical art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Statue-Augustus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Statue-Augustus.jpg/150px-Statue-Augustus.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="225" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Statue-Augustus.jpg/225px-Statue-Augustus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Statue-Augustus.jpg/300px-Statue-Augustus.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1500" data-file-height="2250" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Statue-Augustus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Augustus_of_Prima_Porta" title="Augustus of Prima Porta">Augustus of Prima Porta</a></i>, statue of the emperor <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a>, 1st century AD, <a href="/wiki/Vatican_Museums" title="Vatican Museums">Vatican Museums</a>. An example of Roman art.</div></div></div>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_art" title="Ancient Greek art">Ancient Greek art</a></b> stands out among that of other ancient cultures for its development of naturalistic but idealized depictions of the human body, in which largely nude male figures were generally the focus of innovation. The rate of stylistic development between about 750 and 300 BC was remarkable by ancient standards, and in surviving works is best seen in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_sculpture" title="Ancient Greek sculpture">Ancient Greek sculpture</a>. There were important innovations in painting, which have to be essentially reconstructed due to the lack of original survivals of quality, other than the distinct field of painted pottery. <a href="/wiki/Black-figure_pottery" title="Black-figure pottery">Black-figure pottery</a> and the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Red-figure_pottery" title="Red-figure pottery">red-figure pottery</a> are famous and influential examples of the Ancient Greek decorative arts. <b><a href="/wiki/Roman_art" title="Roman art">Roman art</a></b> was influenced by Greece and can in part be taken as a descendant of ancient Greek painting and sculpture, but was also strongly influenced by the more local <a href="/wiki/Etruscan_art" title="Etruscan art">Etruscan art</a> of Italy. Sculpture was perhaps considered as the highest form of art by Romans, but figure painting was also very highly regarded. <a href="/wiki/Roman_sculpture" title="Roman sculpture">Roman sculpture</a> is primarily portraiture derived from the upper classes of society as well as depictions of the gods. However, Roman painting does have important unique characteristics. Among surviving Roman paintings are wall paintings, many from villas in <a href="/wiki/Campania" title="Campania">Campania</a>, in Southern Italy, especially at <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">Pompeii</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herculaneum" title="Herculaneum">Herculaneum</a>. Such painting can be grouped into four main "styles" or periods and may contain the first examples of <a href="/wiki/Trompe-l%27oeil" class="mw-redirect" title="Trompe-l&#39;oeil">trompe-l'oeil</a>, pseudo-perspective, and pure landscape. <b><a href="/wiki/Early_Christian_art" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Christian art">Early Christian art</a></b> grew out of Roman popular, and later Imperial, art and adapted its <a href="/wiki/Iconography" title="Iconography">iconography</a> from these sources.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_art">Medieval art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Medieval art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><p>
Medieval art can be broadly categorised into the Byzantine art of the Eastern Roman Empire, and the Gothic art that emerged in Western Europe over the same period.</p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Emperor_Justinian_and_Members_of_His_Court_MET_LC_25_100_1a-e_s01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Emperor_Justinian_and_Members_of_His_Court_MET_LC_25_100_1a-e_s01.jpg/220px-Emperor_Justinian_and_Members_of_His_Court_MET_LC_25_100_1a-e_s01.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Emperor_Justinian_and_Members_of_His_Court_MET_LC_25_100_1a-e_s01.jpg/330px-Emperor_Justinian_and_Members_of_His_Court_MET_LC_25_100_1a-e_s01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Emperor_Justinian_and_Members_of_His_Court_MET_LC_25_100_1a-e_s01.jpg/440px-Emperor_Justinian_and_Members_of_His_Court_MET_LC_25_100_1a-e_s01.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="2896" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Emperor_Justinian_and_Members_of_His_Court_MET_LC_25_100_1a-e_s01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mosaic of <a href="/wiki/Justinian_I" title="Justinian I">Emperor Justinian</a> and his court, from the church of <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_San_Vitale" title="Basilica of San Vitale">San Vitale, Ravenna</a>. An example of Byzantine art.</div></div></div>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Byzantine_art" title="Byzantine art">Byzantine art</a></b> was strongly influenced by its classical heritage, but distinguished itself by the development of a new, abstract, aesthetic, marked by anti-naturalism and a favour for symbolism. The subject matter of monumental Byzantine art was primarily religious and imperial: the two themes are often combined, as in the portraits of later Byzantine emperors that decorated the interior of the sixth-century church of <a href="/wiki/Hagia_Sophia" title="Hagia Sophia">Hagia Sophia</a> in Constantinople. However, the Byzantines inherited the <a href="/wiki/Early_Christian_art" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Christian art">Early Christian</a> distrust of <a href="/wiki/Monumental_sculpture" title="Monumental sculpture">monumental sculpture</a> in religious art, and produced only <a href="/wiki/Relief" title="Relief">reliefs</a>, of which very few survivals are anything like life-size, in sharp contrast to the medieval art of the West, where monumental sculpture revived from <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_art" title="Carolingian art">Carolingian art</a> onwards. Small ivories were also mostly in relief. The so-called "minor arts" were very important in Byzantine art and luxury items, including ivories carved in relief as formal presentation <a href="/wiki/Consular_diptych" title="Consular diptych">Consular diptychs</a> or caskets such as the <a href="/wiki/Veroli_casket" class="mw-redirect" title="Veroli casket">Veroli casket</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hardstone_carving" title="Hardstone carving">hardstone carvings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_enamel" title="Byzantine enamel">enamels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_glass" title="Byzantine glass">glass</a>, jewelry, metalwork, and <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_silk" title="Byzantine silk">figured silks</a> were produced in large quantities throughout the Byzantine era.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:El_nacimiento_de_Venus,_por_Sandro_Botticelli.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/El_nacimiento_de_Venus%2C_por_Sandro_Botticelli.jpg/220px-El_nacimiento_de_Venus%2C_por_Sandro_Botticelli.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="137" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/El_nacimiento_de_Venus%2C_por_Sandro_Botticelli.jpg/330px-El_nacimiento_de_Venus%2C_por_Sandro_Botticelli.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/El_nacimiento_de_Venus%2C_por_Sandro_Botticelli.jpg/440px-El_nacimiento_de_Venus%2C_por_Sandro_Botticelli.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2500" data-file-height="1560" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:El_nacimiento_de_Venus,_por_Sandro_Botticelli.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/The_Birth_of_Venus" title="The Birth of Venus">The Birth of Venus</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Sandro_Botticelli" title="Sandro Botticelli">Sandro Botticelli</a>, c. 1485. Now in the <a href="/wiki/Uffizi" title="Uffizi">Uffizi Gallery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>. An example of Renaissance art.</div></div></div>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Migration_Period_art" title="Migration Period art">Migration Period art</a></b> includes the art of the Germanic tribes on the continent, as well the start of the distinct <b><a href="/wiki/Insular_art" title="Insular art">Insular art</a></b> or Hiberno-Saxon art of the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon">Anglo-Saxon</a> and Celtic fusion in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>. It covers many different styles of art including the polychrome style and the Scythian and Germanic <a href="/wiki/Animal_style" title="Animal style">animal style</a>. After <a href="/wiki/Christianization" title="Christianization">Christianization</a>, Migration Period art developed into various schools of Early <a href="/wiki/Medieval_art" title="Medieval art">Medieval art</a> in Western Europe which are normally classified by region, such as <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_art" title="Anglo-Saxon art">Anglo-Saxon art</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_art" title="Carolingian art">Carolingian art</a>, before the continent-wide styles of <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_art" title="Romanesque art">Romanesque art</a> and finally <a href="/wiki/Gothic_art" title="Gothic art">Gothic art</a> developed.
</p><p><b><a href="/wiki/Romanesque_art" title="Romanesque art">Romanesque art</a></b> and <b><a href="/wiki/Gothic_art" title="Gothic art">Gothic art</a></b> dominated Western and Central Europe from approximately 1000 AD to the rise of the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_art" title="Renaissance art">Renaissance style</a> in the 15th century or later, depending on region. The Romanesque style was greatly influenced by Byzantine and Insular art. Religious art, such as church sculpture and decorated manuscripts, was particularly prominent. Art of the period was characterised by a very vigorous style in both sculpture and painting. Colours tended to be very striking and mostly primary. Compositions usually had little depth, and needed to be flexible to be squeezed into the shapes of <a href="/wiki/Historiated_initial" title="Historiated initial">historiated initials</a>, column capitals, and church <a href="/wiki/Tympanum_(architecture)" title="Tympanum (architecture)">tympanums</a>. Figures often varied in size in relation to their importance, and landscape backgrounds, if attempted at all, were closer to abstract decorations than realism.
</p><p>Gothic art developed from Romanesque art in Northern France the 12th century AD, led by the concurrent development of <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic architecture</a>. It spread to all of <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a>, and much of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Europe" title="Southern Europe">Southern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a>. In the late 14th century, the sophisticated court style of <a href="/wiki/International_Gothic" title="International Gothic">International Gothic</a> developed, which continued to evolve until the late 15th century. In many areas, especially England and Germany, Late Gothic art continued well into the 16th century.  Gothic art was often <a href="/wiki/Typology_(theology)" title="Typology (theology)">typological</a> in nature, showing the stories of the New Testament and the Old Testament side by side. Saints' lives were often depicted. Images of the <a href="/wiki/Mary,_the_mother_of_Jesus" class="mw-redirect" title="Mary, the mother of Jesus">Virgin Mary</a> changed from the Byzantine iconic form to a more human and affectionate mother, often showing the refined manners of a courtly lady.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Secular" class="mw-redirect" title="Secular">Secular</a> art came into its own during the gothic period alongside the creation of a <a href="/wiki/Bourgeois" class="mw-redirect" title="Bourgeois">bourgeois</a> class who could afford to patronize the arts and commission works. Increased literacy and a growing body of <a href="/wiki/Medieval_literature" title="Medieval literature">secular vernacular literature</a> encouraged the representation of secular themes in art. With the growth of cities, trade <a href="/wiki/Guild" title="Guild">guilds</a> were formed and artists were often required to be members of a <a href="/wiki/Painters%27_guild" class="mw-redirect" title="Painters&#39; guild">painters' guild</a>—as a result, because of better record keeping, more artists are known to us by name in this period than any previous.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renaissance_art">Renaissance art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Renaissance art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:%27David%27_by_Michelangelo_Fir_JBU002.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/%27David%27_by_Michelangelo_Fir_JBU002.jpg/220px-%27David%27_by_Michelangelo_Fir_JBU002.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/%27David%27_by_Michelangelo_Fir_JBU002.jpg/330px-%27David%27_by_Michelangelo_Fir_JBU002.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/%27David%27_by_Michelangelo_Fir_JBU002.jpg/440px-%27David%27_by_Michelangelo_Fir_JBU002.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3138" data-file-height="4707" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:%27David%27_by_Michelangelo_Fir_JBU002.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/David_(Michelangelo)" title="David (Michelangelo)">David (Michelangelo)</a>.</div></div></div>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Renaissance_art" title="Renaissance art">Renaissance art</a></b> emerged as a distinct style in northern Italy from around 1420, in parallel with developments which occurred in <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_philosophy" title="Renaissance philosophy">philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_literature" title="Renaissance literature">literature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_music" title="Renaissance music">music</a> and <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_science" class="mw-redirect" title="Renaissance science">science</a>. It took as its foundation the art of <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">Classical antiquity</a>, but was also influenced by the art of Northern Europe and contemporary scientific knowledge. Renaissance artists painted a wide variety of <a href="/wiki/Themes_in_Italian_Renaissance_painting" title="Themes in Italian Renaissance painting">themes</a>. Religious <a href="/wiki/Altarpiece" title="Altarpiece">altarpieces</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">fresco</a> cycles, and small works for private devotion were very popular. Painters in both Italy and northern Europe frequently turned to <a href="/wiki/Jacobus_de_Voragine" class="mw-redirect" title="Jacobus de Voragine">Jacobus de Voragine</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Golden_Legend" title="Golden Legend">Golden Legend</a></i> (1260), a highly influential source book for the lives of <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saints</a> that had already had a strong influence on Medieval artists. Interest in classical antiquity and <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_humanism" title="Renaissance humanism">Renaissance humanism</a> also resulted in many <a href="/wiki/Mythology" class="mw-redirect" title="Mythology">Mythological</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_painting" title="History painting">history paintings</a>. Decorative <a href="/wiki/Ornament_(architecture)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ornament (architecture)">ornament</a>, often used in painted architectural elements, was especially influenced by classical Roman motifs.</p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ecstasy_of_Saint_Teresa_September_2015-2a.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Ecstasy_of_Saint_Teresa_September_2015-2a.jpg/220px-Ecstasy_of_Saint_Teresa_September_2015-2a.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="349" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Ecstasy_of_Saint_Teresa_September_2015-2a.jpg/330px-Ecstasy_of_Saint_Teresa_September_2015-2a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Ecstasy_of_Saint_Teresa_September_2015-2a.jpg/440px-Ecstasy_of_Saint_Teresa_September_2015-2a.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4242" data-file-height="6722" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ecstasy_of_Saint_Teresa_September_2015-2a.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <i><a href="/wiki/Ecstasy_of_Saint_Teresa" title="Ecstasy of Saint Teresa">Ecstasy of Saint Teresa</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Gian_Lorenzo_Bernini" title="Gian Lorenzo Bernini">Gian Lorenzo Bernini</a>, 1647-52, in <a href="/wiki/Santa_Maria_della_Vittoria,_Rome" title="Santa Maria della Vittoria, Rome">Santa Maria della Vittoria, Rome</a>. A Baroque sculpture.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pierre-Auguste_Renoir,_Le_Moulin_de_la_Galette.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Pierre-Auguste_Renoir%2C_Le_Moulin_de_la_Galette.jpg/220px-Pierre-Auguste_Renoir%2C_Le_Moulin_de_la_Galette.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Pierre-Auguste_Renoir%2C_Le_Moulin_de_la_Galette.jpg/330px-Pierre-Auguste_Renoir%2C_Le_Moulin_de_la_Galette.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Pierre-Auguste_Renoir%2C_Le_Moulin_de_la_Galette.jpg/440px-Pierre-Auguste_Renoir%2C_Le_Moulin_de_la_Galette.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2200" data-file-height="1639" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pierre-Auguste_Renoir,_Le_Moulin_de_la_Galette.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Bal_du_moulin_de_la_Galette" title="Bal du moulin de la Galette">Dance at Le moulin de la Galette</a></i>, by <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Auguste_Renoir" title="Pierre-Auguste Renoir">Pierre-Auguste Renoir</a>, 1876, oil on canvas, height: 131&#160;cm, <a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_d%27Orsay" title="Musée d&#39;Orsay">Musée d'Orsay</a> (Paris).</div></div></div>
<p>Techniques characteristic of Renaissance art include the use of <a href="/wiki/Proportion_(architecture)" title="Proportion (architecture)">proportion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Perspective_(graphical)" title="Perspective (graphical)">linear perspective</a>; <a href="/wiki/Foreshortening" class="mw-redirect" title="Foreshortening">foreshortening</a>, to create an illusion of depth; <a href="/wiki/Sfumato" title="Sfumato">sfumato</a>, a technique of softening of sharp outlines by subtle blending of tones to give the illusion of depth or three-dimensionality; and <a href="/wiki/Chiaroscuro" title="Chiaroscuro">chiaroscuro</a>, the effect of using a strong contrast between light and dark to give the illusion of depth or three-dimensionality.
</p>
<h3><span id="Mannerism.2C_Baroque_and_Rococo"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Mannerism,_Baroque_and_Rococo">Mannerism, Baroque and Rococo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mannerism, Baroque and Rococo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Renaissance Classicism spawned two different movements—<b><a href="/wiki/Mannerism" title="Mannerism">Mannerism</a></b> and the <b><a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a></b>. Mannerism, a reaction against the idealist perfection of Classicism, employed distortion of light and spatial frameworks in order to emphasize the emotional content of a painting and the emotions of the painter. Where High Renaissance art emphasizes proportion, balance, and ideal beauty, Mannerism exaggerates such qualities, often resulting in compositions that are asymmetrical or unnaturally elegant. The style is notable for its intellectual sophistication as well as its artificial (as opposed to naturalistic) qualities. It favors compositional tension and instability rather than the balance and clarity of earlier Renaissance painting.
</p><p>In contrast, Baroque art took the representationalism of the Renaissance to new heights, emphasizing detail, movement, lighting, and drama. Perhaps the best known Baroque painters are <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo_Merisi" class="mw-redirect" title="Michelangelo Merisi"><b>Caravaggio</b></a>, <a href="/wiki/Rembrandt_Harmenszoon_van_Rijn" class="mw-redirect" title="Rembrandt Harmenszoon van Rijn"><b>Rembrandt</b></a>, <b><a href="/wiki/Peter_Paul_Rubens" title="Peter Paul Rubens">Peter Paul Rubens</a></b>, and <b><a href="/wiki/Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez" title="Diego Velázquez">Diego Velázquez</a></b>. Baroque art is often seen as part of the <a href="/wiki/Counter-Reformation" title="Counter-Reformation">Counter-Reformation</a>— the revival of spiritual life in the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>. Religious and political themes are widely explored within the Baroque artistic context, and both paintings and sculptures are characterised by a strong element of drama, emotion and theatricality. Baroque art was particularly ornate and elaborate in nature, often using rich, warm colours with dark undertones. <b><a href="/wiki/Dutch_Golden_Age_painting" title="Dutch Golden Age painting">Dutch Golden Age painting</a></b> is a distinct subset of Baroque, leading to the development of secular genres such as <a href="/wiki/Still_life" title="Still life">still life</a>, <a href="/wiki/Genre_painting" title="Genre painting">genre paintings</a> of everyday scenes, and <a href="/wiki/Landscape_painting" title="Landscape painting">landscape painting</a>.
</p><p>By the 18th century Baroque art had developed into the <b><a href="/wiki/Rococo" title="Rococo">Rococo</a></b> in France. Rococo art was even more elaborate than the Baroque, but it was less serious and more playful. The artistic movement no longer placed an emphasis on politics and religion, focusing instead on lighter themes such as romance, celebration, and appreciation of nature. Furthermore, it sought inspiration from the artistic forms and ornamentation of <a href="/wiki/Far_East" title="Far East">Far Eastern Asia</a>, resulting in the rise in favour of <a href="/wiki/Porcelain" title="Porcelain">porcelain</a> figurines and <a href="/wiki/Chinoiserie" title="Chinoiserie">chinoiserie</a> in general. Rococo soon fell out of favor, being seen by many as a gaudy and superficial movement emphasizing aesthetics over meaning.
</p>
<h3><span id="Neoclassical.2C_Romanticism.2C_and_Realism"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Neoclassical,_Romanticism,_and_Realism">Neoclassical, Romanticism, and Realism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Neoclassical, Romanticism, and Realism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Neoclassicism" title="Neoclassicism">Neoclassicism</a></b> began in the 18th century as counter movement opposing the Rococo. It desired for a return to the simplicity, order and 'purism' of classical antiquity, especially ancient Greece and Rome. Neoclassicism was the artistic component of the intellectual movement known as <a href="/wiki/The_Enlightenment" class="mw-redirect" title="The Enlightenment">the Enlightenment</a>. Neoclassicism had become widespread in Europe throughout the 18th century, especially in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. In many ways Neoclassicism can be seen as a political movement as well as an artistic and cultural one. Neoclassical art places an emphasis on order, symmetry and classical simplicity; common themes in Neoclassical art include courage and war, as were commonly explored in ancient Greek and Roman art. <a href="/wiki/Jean_Auguste_Dominique_Ingres" class="mw-redirect" title="Jean Auguste Dominique Ingres"><b>Ingres</b></a>, <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Canova" title="Antonio Canova"><b>Canova</b></a>, and <b><a href="/wiki/Jacques-Louis_David" title="Jacques-Louis David">Jacques-Louis David</a></b> are among the best-known neoclassicists.
</p><p>Just as Mannerism rejected Classicism, <b><a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romanticism</a></b> rejected the aesthetic of the Neoclassicists, specifically the highly objective and ordered nature of Neoclassicism, favouring instead a more individual and emotional approach to the arts. Emphasis was placed on nature, especially when aiming to portray the power and beauty of the natural world, and emotions. Romantic art often used colours in order to express feelings and emotion. Romantic art was inspitred by ancient Greek and Roman art and mythology, but also takes much of its aesthetic qualities from <a href="/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">medievalism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gothic_art" title="Gothic art">Gothicism</a>, as well as later mythology and <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a>. Among the greatest Romantic artists were <b><a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix" title="Eugène Delacroix">Eugène Delacroix</a></b>, <b><a href="/wiki/Francisco_Goya" title="Francisco Goya">Francisco Goya</a></b>, <b><a href="/wiki/J.M.W._Turner" class="mw-redirect" title="J.M.W. Turner">J.M.W. Turner</a></b>, <b><a href="/wiki/John_Constable" title="John Constable">John Constable</a></b>, <b><a href="/wiki/Caspar_David_Friedrich" title="Caspar David Friedrich">Caspar David Friedrich</a></b>, and <b><a href="/wiki/William_Blake" title="William Blake">William Blake</a></b>.
</p><p>In response to these changes caused by <a href="/wiki/Industrialisation" title="Industrialisation">Industrialisation</a>, the movement of <a href="/wiki/Realism_(arts)" title="Realism (arts)"><b>Realism</b></a> emerged, which sought to accurately portray the conditions and hardships of the poor in the hopes of changing society. In contrast with Romanticism, which was essentially optimistic about mankind, Realism offered a stark vision of poverty and despair. While Romanticism glorified nature, Realism portrayed life in the depths of an urban wasteland. Like Romanticism, <a href="/wiki/Realism_(arts)" title="Realism (arts)">Realism</a> was a literary as well as an artistic movement. Other contemporary movements were more <a href="/wiki/Historicism_(art)" title="Historicism (art)">Historicist</a> in nature, such as the <b><a href="/wiki/Pre-Raphaelite_Brotherhood" title="Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood">Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood</a></b>, who attempted to return art to its state of "purity" prior to <a href="/wiki/Raphael" title="Raphael">Raphael</a>, and the <b><a href="/wiki/Arts_and_Crafts_Movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Arts and Crafts Movement">Arts and Crafts Movement</a></b>, which reacted against the impersonality of mass-produced goods and advocated a return to medieval craftsmanship.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_Music_pre-1600">Classical Music pre-1600</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Classical Music pre-1600">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This broad era encompasses <b><a href="/wiki/Early_music" title="Early music">early music</a></b>, which generally comprises <b><a href="/wiki/Medieval_music" title="Medieval music">Medieval music</a></b> (500–1400) and <b><a href="/wiki/Renaissance_music" title="Renaissance music">Renaissance music</a></b> (1400–1600), but sometimes includes <b><a href="/wiki/Baroque_music" title="Baroque music">Baroque music</a></b> (1600–1760).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_Music_post-1600">Classical Music post-1600</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Classical Music post-1600">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This era includes the <b><a href="/wiki/Common_practice_period" title="Common practice period">common practice period</a></b> from approximately 1600 to 1900, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Modernism_(music)" title="Modernism (music)"><b>modernist</b></a> and <b><a href="/wiki/Postmodernism" title="Postmodernism">postmodernist</a></b> styles that emerged after 1900 and which continue to the present day.
</p><p><br />
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Folk_and_Popular_Music">Folk and Popular Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Folk and Popular Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">Folk music</a></b>: Europe has a wide and diverse range of indigenous music, sharing common features in rural, travelling or maritime communities. Folk music is embedded in an unwritten, aural tradition, but was increasingly transcribed from the nineteenth century onwards. Many classical composers used folk melodies, and folk has influenced some popular music in Europe. See the <a href="/wiki/List_of_European_folk_music_traditions" title="List of European folk music traditions">list of European folk musics</a>.
</p><p><b><a href="/wiki/Popular_music" title="Popular music">Popular music</a></b>: Europe has also imported many different genres of music, mainly from the United States, ranging from Blues, Jazz, Soul, Pop, Electronic, Hip-Hop, R'n'B or Dance and creating its own genres like <a href="/wiki/Britpop" title="Britpop">Britpop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Europop" title="Europop">Europop</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_hip-hop" class="mw-redirect" title="French hip-hop">French hip-hop</a>, <a href="/wiki/German_hip_hop" title="German hip hop">German hip hop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italian_hip_hop" title="Italian hip hop">Italian hip hop</a> or <a href="/wiki/Russian_hip_hop" title="Russian hip hop">Russian hip hop</a> and many others.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistoric_architecture">Prehistoric architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Prehistoric architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <b>Neolithic long house</b> was a long, narrow timber dwelling built by the first farmers in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> beginning at least as early as the period 5000 to 6000 <a href="/wiki/Anno_Domini" title="Anno Domini">BC</a>. <a href="/wiki/Knap_of_Howar" title="Knap of Howar">Knap of Howar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skara_Brae" title="Skara Brae">Skara Brae</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Orkney_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Orkney Islands">Orkney Islands</a>, Scotland, are stone-built Neolithic settlement dating from 3,500 BC. <a href="/wiki/Megaliths" class="mw-redirect" title="Megaliths">Megaliths</a> found in Europe and the Mediterranean were also erected in the Neolithic period. See <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_architecture" title="Neolithic architecture">Neolithic architecture</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Classical_architecture">Ancient Classical architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Ancient Classical architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Parthenon-2008_entzerrt.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Parthenon-2008_entzerrt.jpg/220px-Parthenon-2008_entzerrt.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="161" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Parthenon-2008_entzerrt.jpg/330px-Parthenon-2008_entzerrt.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Parthenon-2008_entzerrt.jpg/440px-Parthenon-2008_entzerrt.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3906" data-file-height="2865" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Parthenon-2008_entzerrt.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Parthenon" title="Parthenon">Parthenon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, is an example of Ancient Greek architecture.</div></div></div><p><b><a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_architecture" title="Ancient Greek architecture">Ancient Greek architecture</a></b> was produced by the <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greek-speaking people</a> whose <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">culture</a> flourished on the Greek mainland, the <a href="/wiki/Peloponnese" title="Peloponnese">Peloponnese</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Islands" title="Aegean Islands">Aegean Islands</a>, and in colonies in <a href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" class="mw-redirect" title="Asia Minor">Anatolia</a> and Italy for a period from about 900 BC until the 1st century AD. Ancient Greek architecture is distinguished by its highly formalised characteristics, both of structure and decoration. The formal vocabulary of ancient Greek architecture, in particular the division of architectural style into three defined orders: the <a href="/wiki/Doric_Order" class="mw-redirect" title="Doric Order">Doric Order</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ionic_Order" class="mw-redirect" title="Ionic Order">Ionic Order</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Corinthian_Order" class="mw-redirect" title="Corinthian Order">Corinthian Order</a>, was to have profound effect on <a href="/wiki/History_of_architecture" title="History of architecture">Western architecture</a> of later periods.
</p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rome_Pantheon_front.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Rome_Pantheon_front.jpg/220px-Rome_Pantheon_front.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="136" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Rome_Pantheon_front.jpg/330px-Rome_Pantheon_front.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Rome_Pantheon_front.jpg/440px-Rome_Pantheon_front.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3455" data-file-height="2133" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rome_Pantheon_front.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Pantheon,_Rome" title="Pantheon, Rome">Rome Pantheon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, cylindrical <a href="/wiki/Roman_temple" title="Roman temple">roman temple</a> with a portico</div></div></div><p><b><a href="/wiki/Ancient_Roman_architecture" title="Ancient Roman architecture">Ancient Roman architecture</a></b> adopted the external language of classical <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_Architecture" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Greek Architecture">Greek architecture</a> for the purposes of the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Romans" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Romans">ancient Romans</a>, but differed from Greek buildings, becoming a new <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architectural</a> style. The two styles are often considered one body of <a href="/wiki/Classical_architecture" title="Classical architecture">classical architecture</a>. Roman architecture flourished in the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a> and even more so under the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Empire</a>, when the great majority of surviving buildings were constructed. It used new materials, particularly concrete, and newer technologies such as the <a href="/wiki/Arch" title="Arch">arch</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Dome" title="Dome">dome</a> to make buildings that were typically strong and well-engineered. Large numbers remain in some form across the empire, sometimes complete and still in use.
</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_architecture">Medieval architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Medieval architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Catedral_G%C3%B3tica_de_Le%C3%B3n.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Catedral_G%C3%B3tica_de_Le%C3%B3n.jpg/150px-Catedral_G%C3%B3tica_de_Le%C3%B3n.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="221" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Catedral_G%C3%B3tica_de_Le%C3%B3n.jpg/225px-Catedral_G%C3%B3tica_de_Le%C3%B3n.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Catedral_G%C3%B3tica_de_Le%C3%B3n.jpg/300px-Catedral_G%C3%B3tica_de_Le%C3%B3n.jpg 2x" data-file-width="813" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Catedral_G%C3%B3tica_de_Le%C3%B3n.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Le%C3%B3n_Cathedral" title="León Cathedral">León Cathedral</a>, <a href="/wiki/Castile_and_Leon" class="mw-redirect" title="Castile and Leon">Castile and Leon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, built in the <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> style.</div></div></div><p><b><a href="/wiki/Romanesque_architecture" title="Romanesque architecture">Romanesque architecture</a></b> combines features of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Roman_architecture" title="Ancient Roman architecture">ancient Roman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_architecture" title="Byzantine architecture">Byzantine</a> buildings and other local traditions. It is known by its massive quality, thick walls, round arches, sturdy <a href="/wiki/Pier_(architecture)" title="Pier (architecture)">pillars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Groin_vault" title="Groin vault">groin vaults</a>, large towers and decorative <a href="/wiki/Arcade_(architecture)" title="Arcade (architecture)">arcading</a>. Each building has clearly defined forms, frequently of very regular, <a href="/wiki/Symmetrical" class="mw-redirect" title="Symmetrical">symmetrical</a> plan; the overall appearance is one of simplicity when compared with the Gothic buildings that were to follow. The style can be identified right across Europe, despite regional characteristics and different materials, and is most frequently seen in churches. Plenty of examples of this architecture are found alongside the <a href="/wiki/Camino_de_Santiago" title="Camino de Santiago">Camino de Santiago</a>.
</p><p><b><a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic architecture</a></b> flourished in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> during the <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">High</a> and <a href="/wiki/Late_Middle_Ages" title="Late Middle Ages">Late</a> <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. It evolved from <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_architecture" title="Romanesque architecture">Romanesque architecture</a> and was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_architecture" title="Renaissance architecture">Renaissance architecture</a>. Originating in 12th century <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and lasting into the 16th century, Gothic architecture was known during the period as <i>Opus Francigenum</i> ("French work") with the term <i>Gothic</i> first appearing during the later part of the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>. Its characteristics include the <a href="/wiki/Ogive" title="Ogive">pointed arch</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ribbed_vault" class="mw-redirect" title="Ribbed vault">ribbed vault</a> (which evolved from the joint vaulting of Romanesque architecture) and the <a href="/wiki/Flying_buttress" title="Flying buttress">flying buttress</a>. Gothic architecture is most familiar as the architecture of many of the great <a href="/wiki/Cathedral" title="Cathedral">cathedrals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abbey" title="Abbey">abbeys</a> and churches of Europe.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renaissance_and_Neoclassical_architecture">Renaissance and Neoclassical architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Renaissance and Neoclassical architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Florence_Italy_Santa-Maria-Novella-01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Florence_Italy_Santa-Maria-Novella-01.jpg/220px-Florence_Italy_Santa-Maria-Novella-01.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Florence_Italy_Santa-Maria-Novella-01.jpg/330px-Florence_Italy_Santa-Maria-Novella-01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Florence_Italy_Santa-Maria-Novella-01.jpg/440px-Florence_Italy_Santa-Maria-Novella-01.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3715" data-file-height="2654" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Florence_Italy_Santa-Maria-Novella-01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Santa_Maria_Novella" title="Santa Maria Novella">Santa Maria Novella</a>, Florence, Italy, an example of Renaissance architecture.</div></div></div><p><b><a href="/wiki/Renaissance_architecture" title="Renaissance architecture">Renaissance architecture</a></b> began in the early 14th and lasted until the early 17th century. It demonstrates a conscious revival and development of certain elements of ancient Greek and Roman architectural thought and material culture, particularly the <a href="/wiki/Symmetry" title="Symmetry">symmetry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Proportion_(architecture)" title="Proportion (architecture)">proportion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a> and the regularity of parts of ancient buildings. Developed first in <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Brunelleschi" title="Filippo Brunelleschi">Filippo Brunelleschi</a> as one of its innovators, the Renaissance style quickly spread to other Italian cities. The style was carried to France, Germany, England, Russia and other parts of Europe at different dates and with varying degrees of impact
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Palladian_architecture" title="Palladian architecture"><b>Palladian architecture</b></a> was derived from and inspired by the designs of the Renaissance architect <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Palladio" title="Andrea Palladio">Andrea Palladio</a> (1508–1580). Palladio's work was strongly based on the symmetry, perspective and values of the formal classical temple architecture of the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greeks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a>. From the 17th century Palladio's interpretation of this classical architecture was adapted as the style known as Palladianism. It continued to develop until the end of the 18th century, and continued to be popular in Europe throughout the 19th and early 20th centuries, where it was frequently employed in the design of public and municipal buildings.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Neptune%27s_glory_(29838154532).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Neptune%27s_glory_%2829838154532%29.jpg/220px-Neptune%27s_glory_%2829838154532%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Neptune%27s_glory_%2829838154532%29.jpg/330px-Neptune%27s_glory_%2829838154532%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Neptune%27s_glory_%2829838154532%29.jpg/440px-Neptune%27s_glory_%2829838154532%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3696" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Neptune%27s_glory_(29838154532).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Queluz" title="Palace of Queluz">Palace of Queluz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lisbon_District" title="Lisbon District">Lisbon District</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, an example of Baroque architecture.</div></div></div><p><b><a href="/wiki/Baroque_architecture" title="Baroque architecture">Baroque architecture</a></b> began in 16th-century Italy. It took the <a href="/wiki/Roman_architecture" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman architecture">Roman</a> vocabulary of <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_architecture" title="Renaissance architecture">Renaissance architecture</a> and used it in a new rhetorical and theatrical fashion. It was, initially at least, directly linked to the <a href="/wiki/Counter-Reformation" title="Counter-Reformation">Counter-Reformation</a>, a movement within the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a> to reform itself in response to the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a>. Baroque was characterised by new explorations of form, light and shadow, and a freer treatment of classical elements. It reached its extreme form in the <a href="/wiki/Rococo" title="Rococo">Rococo</a> style.
</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th-century_architecture">19th-century architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: 19th-century architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Budapest_Parliament_4604.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Budapest_Parliament_4604.JPG/220px-Budapest_Parliament_4604.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="134" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Budapest_Parliament_4604.JPG/330px-Budapest_Parliament_4604.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Budapest_Parliament_4604.JPG/440px-Budapest_Parliament_4604.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1250" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Budapest_Parliament_4604.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Hungarian Parliament Building in Budapest, an example of Gothic revival architecture.</div></div></div><p><a href="/wiki/Revivalism_(architecture)" title="Revivalism (architecture)"><b>Revivalism</b></a> was a hallmark of nineteenth-century European architecture. Revivals of the <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_Revival_architecture" title="Romanesque Revival architecture">Romanesque</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gothic_Revival_architecture" title="Gothic Revival architecture">Gothic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_Revival_architecture" title="Renaissance Revival architecture">Renaissance</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Baroque_Revival_architecture" title="Baroque Revival architecture">Baroque</a> styles all took place, alongside revivals of the Classical styles. Regional styles, such as English <a href="/wiki/Tudor_Revival_architecture" title="Tudor Revival architecture">Tudor</a> were also revived, as well as non-European styles, such as Chinese (<a href="/wiki/Chinoiserie" title="Chinoiserie">Chinoiserie</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_Revival_architecture" title="Egyptian Revival architecture">Egyptian</a>. These revivals often used elements of the original style in a freer way than original examples, sometimes borrowing from multiple styles at once. At Alnwick Castle, for example, Gothic revival elements were added to the exterior of the original medieval castle, while the interiors were designed in a Renaissance style.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Art_Nouveau" title="Art Nouveau"><b>Art Nouveau architecture</b></a> was a reaction against the eclectic styles which dominated European architecture in the second half of the 19th century. It was expressed through decoration. The buildings were covered with ornament in curving forms, based on flowers, plants or animals: butterflies, peacocks, swans, irises, cyclamens, orchids and water lilies. Façades were asymmetrical, and often decorated with polychrome ceramic tiles. The decoration usually suggested movement; there was no distinction between the structure and the ornament.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th-century_and_modern_architecture">20th-century and modern architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: 20th-century and modern architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Art_Deco" title="Art Deco"><b>Art Deco architecture</b></a> began in Brussels in 1903-4. Early buildings had clean lines, rectangular forms, and no decoration on the facades; they marked a clean break with the art nouveau style. After the First World War, art deco buildings of steel and reinforced concrete began to appear in large cities across Europe and the United States. Buildings became more decorated, and interiors were extremely colorful and dynamic, combining sculpture, murals, and ornate geometric design in marble, glass, ceramics and stainless steel.
</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Modern_architecture" title="Modern architecture"><b>Modernist architecture</b></a> is a term applied to a group of styles of <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a> which emerged in the first half of the 20th century and became dominant after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. It was based upon new technologies of construction, particularly the use of <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>and <a href="/wiki/Reinforced_concrete" title="Reinforced concrete">reinforced concrete</a>; and upon a rejection of the traditional <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_architecture" title="Neoclassical architecture">neoclassical architecture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beaux-Arts_architecture" title="Beaux-Arts architecture">Beaux-Arts</a> styles that were popular in the 19th century. Modernist architecture continued to be the dominant architectural style for institutional and corporate buildings into 1980s, when it was challenged by <a href="/wiki/Postmodern_architecture" title="Postmodern architecture">postmodernism</a>.</p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Modernisme" title="Modernisme">modernisme</a> building.</div></div></div><p><b><a href="/wiki/Expressionist_architecture" title="Expressionist architecture">Expressionist architecture</a></b> is a form of modern architecture that began during the first decades of the 20th century, in parallel with the <a href="/wiki/Expressionism" title="Expressionism">expressionist</a> visual and performing arts that especially developed and dominated in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. In the 1950s, a second movement of expressionist architecture developed, initiated by the <a href="/wiki/Notre_Dame_du_Haut" title="Notre Dame du Haut">Ronchamp Chapel Notre-Dame-du-Haut</a> (1950–1955) by <a href="/wiki/Le_Corbusier" title="Le Corbusier">Le Corbusier</a>. The style was individualistic, but tendencies include Distortion of form for an emotional effect, efforts at achieving the new, original, and visionary, and a conception of architecture as a work of art.
</p><p><b><a href="/wiki/Postmodern_architecture" title="Postmodern architecture">Postmodern architecture</a></b> emerged in the 1960s as a reaction against the austerity, formality, and lack of variety of <a href="/wiki/Modern_architecture" title="Modern architecture">modern architecture</a>, particularly in the <a href="/wiki/International_Style_(architecture)" title="International Style (architecture)">international style</a> advocated by <a href="/wiki/Le_Corbusier" title="Le Corbusier">Le Corbusier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Mies_van_der_Rohe" title="Ludwig Mies van der Rohe">Ludwig Mies van der Rohe</a>. Embraced in the USA first, it spread to Europe. In contrast to Modernist buildings, Postmodern buildings have curved forms, decorative elements, asymmetry, bright colors, and features often borrowed from earlier periods. Colors and textures unrelated to the structure of function of the building. While rejecting the "puritanism" of modernism, it called for a return to ornament, and an accumulation of citations and collages borrowed from past styles. It borrowed freely from classical architecture, <a href="/wiki/Rococo" title="Rococo">rococo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_architecture" title="Neoclassical architecture">neoclassical architecture</a>, the Viennese <a href="/wiki/Secession" title="Secession">secession</a>, the British <a href="/wiki/Arts_and_crafts_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Arts and crafts movement">arts and crafts movement</a>, the German <a href="/wiki/Jugendstil" title="Jugendstil">Jugendstil</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Deconstructivism" title="Deconstructivism"><b>Deconstructivist architecture</b></a> is a movement of <a href="/wiki/Postmodern_architecture" title="Postmodern architecture">postmodern architecture</a> which appeared in the 1980s, which gives the impression of the fragmentation of the constructed building. It is characterized by an absence of harmony, continuity, or symmetry. Its name comes from the idea of "<a href="/wiki/Deconstruction" title="Deconstruction">Deconstruction</a>", a form of <a href="/wiki/Semiotic" class="mw-redirect" title="Semiotic">semiotic</a> analysis developed by the French philosopher <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Derrida" title="Jacques Derrida">Jacques Derrida</a>. Besides fragmentation, Deconstructivism often manipulates the structure's surface skin and creates by non-<a href="/wiki/Rectilinear_polygon" title="Rectilinear polygon">rectilinear</a> shapes which appear to distort and dislocate <a href="/wiki/Design_principles_and_elements" class="mw-redirect" title="Design principles and elements">elements of architecture</a>. The finished visual appearance is characterized by unpredictability and controlled chaos.
</p>

<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sartre_and_de_Beauvoir_at_Balzac_Memorial.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Sartre_and_de_Beauvoir_at_Balzac_Memorial.jpg/170px-Sartre_and_de_Beauvoir_at_Balzac_Memorial.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="265" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Sartre_and_de_Beauvoir_at_Balzac_Memorial.jpg/255px-Sartre_and_de_Beauvoir_at_Balzac_Memorial.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Sartre_and_de_Beauvoir_at_Balzac_Memorial.jpg/340px-Sartre_and_de_Beauvoir_at_Balzac_Memorial.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1366" data-file-height="2127" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sartre_and_de_Beauvoir_at_Balzac_Memorial.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Jean-Paul Sartre and Simone de Beauvoir at the Balzac Memorial</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Western_philosophy" title="Western philosophy">Western philosophy</a></div>
<p>European <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> is a predominant strand of philosophy globally, and is central to philosophical enquiry in the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a> and most other parts of the world which have fallen under its influence. The Greek schools of philosophy in <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">antiquity</a> provide the basis of philosophical discourse that extends to today. <a href="/wiki/Christian_thought" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian thought">Christian thought</a> had a huge influence on many fields of European philosophy (as European philosophy has been on Christian thought too), sometimes as a reaction. Many political ideologies were theorised in Europe such as <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fascism" title="Fascism">fascism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a> or <a href="/wiki/Anarchism" title="Anarchism">anarchism</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=43" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_Europe" title="Religion in Europe">Religion in Europe</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=44" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Eurobarometer Poll 2005<sup id="cite_ref-eurobarometer_2005_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eurobarometer_2005-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> found that, on average, 52% of the citizens of <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a> member states state that they "believe in God", 27% believe there is some sort of spirit or life force while 18% do not believe there is any sort of spirit, God or life force. 3% declined to answer. According to new polls about Religiosity in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> in 2012 by <a href="/wiki/Eurobarometer" title="Eurobarometer">Eurobarometer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> is the largest religion in the European Union accounting 72% of EU citizens.<sup id="cite_ref-euroreligion2012_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-euroreligion2012-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Agnostic" class="mw-redirect" title="Agnostic">Non believer/Agnostic</a> account 16%,<sup id="cite_ref-euroreligion2012_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-euroreligion2012-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Atheist" class="mw-redirect" title="Atheist">Atheist</a> account's 7%,<sup id="cite_ref-euroreligion2012_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-euroreligion2012-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Muslim" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> 2%.<sup id="cite_ref-euroreligion2012_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-euroreligion2012-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bosanska_Krupa_Churches.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Bosanska_Krupa_Churches.JPG/250px-Bosanska_Krupa_Churches.JPG" decoding="async" width="250" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Bosanska_Krupa_Churches.JPG/375px-Bosanska_Krupa_Churches.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Bosanska_Krupa_Churches.JPG/500px-Bosanska_Krupa_Churches.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2056" data-file-height="1648" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bosanska_Krupa_Churches.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Catholic church (left), a Serbian Orthodox church (right), and a mosque (center background) in <a href="/wiki/Bosanska_Krupa" title="Bosanska Krupa">Bosanska Krupa</a>.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> has been the dominant religion shaping European culture for at least the last 1700 years.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cambridge_University_Historical_Series_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge_University_Historical_Series-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Caltron_J.H_Hayas_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Caltron_J.H_Hayas-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Horst_Hutter_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Horst_Hutter-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Fred_Reinhard_Dallmayr_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fred_Reinhard_Dallmayr-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Modern philosophical thought has very much been influenced by Christian philosophers such as St Thomas Aquinas and Erasmus. And throughout most of its history, Europe has been nearly equivalent to <a href="/wiki/Christian_culture" title="Christian culture">Christian culture</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Christian_culture" title="Christian culture">Christian culture</a> was the predominant force in <a href="/wiki/Western_civilization" class="mw-redirect" title="Western civilization">western civilization</a>, guiding the course of <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">art</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1994_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1994-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The notion of "<a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>" and the "<a href="/wiki/Western_World" class="mw-redirect" title="Western World">Western World</a>" has been intimately connected with the concept of "<a href="/wiki/Christendom" title="Christendom">Christianity and Christendom</a>" many even attribute Christianity for being the link that created a unified <a href="/wiki/European_identity" class="mw-redirect" title="European identity">European identity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cuisine">Cuisine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=53" title="Edit section: Cuisine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/European_cuisine" title="European cuisine">European cuisine</a></div>
<p>The cuisines of European countries are diverse by themselves, although there are common characteristics that distinguishes European cooking from cuisines of <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asian countries</a> and others. Compared with traditional cooking of Asian countries, for example, meat is more prominent and substantial in serving-size. <a href="/wiki/Steak" title="Steak">Steak</a> in particular is a common dish across Europe. European cuisines also put substantial emphasis on <a href="/wiki/Sauce" title="Sauce">sauces</a> as <a href="/wiki/Condiment" title="Condiment">condiments</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seasoning" title="Seasoning">seasonings</a>, or accompaniments (in part due to the difficulty of seasonings penetrating the often larger pieces of meat used in European cooking). Many <a href="/wiki/Dairy_products" class="mw-redirect" title="Dairy products">dairy products</a> are utilized in the cooking process, except in <a href="/wiki/Nouvelle_cuisine" title="Nouvelle cuisine">nouvelle cuisine</a>. Wheat-flour <a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">bread</a> has long been the most common sources of <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a> in this cuisine, along with <a href="/wiki/Pasta" title="Pasta">pasta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dumpling" title="Dumpling">dumplings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pastries" class="mw-redirect" title="Pastries">pastries</a>, although the <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">potato</a> has become a major starch plant in the diet of Europeans and their diaspora since the <a href="/wiki/European_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="European colonization of the Americas">European colonization of the Americas</a>. The common way to eat a beef or pork chop is with a knife in the right hand and a fork in the left way. To begin a such meal by first chopping the meat up, and then just use a fork with the right hand, is generally considered to be <i>bad manners</i>, especially at restaurants.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_Sport">Contemporary Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=58" title="Edit section: Contemporary Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Football">Football</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=59" title="Edit section: Football">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">Association football</a>, which has contested origins between <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> (where <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a> insisted the game be called by the name <a href="/wiki/Football_in_Italy" title="Football in Italy">Calcio</a>). What is uncontestable is that the oldest association is <a href="/wiki/The_Football_Association" title="The Football Association">The Football Association</a> of England (1863) and the first international match was between <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> (1872). It is now the world's most popular sport and is played throughout Europe.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">Cricket</a> has its origins in south eastern <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a>. It's popular throughout <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, and parts of <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>. It is also popular in other areas in <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="Northwest Europe">Northwest Europe</a>. It is however very popular worldwide, especially in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cycling" title="Cycling">Cycling</a>, which is immensely popular as a means of <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a>, has most of its sporting adherents in Europe. <a href="/wiki/Tour_de_France" title="Tour de France">Tour de France</a> is the world's most watched live annual sporting event. The <a href="/wiki/Bicycle" title="Bicycle">bicycle</a> itself is probably from <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> (see <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_bicycle" title="History of the bicycle">History of the bicycle</a>).</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Discus_throw" title="Discus throw">discus throw</a>, <a href="/wiki/Javelin_throw" title="Javelin throw">javelin throw</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shot_put" title="Shot put">shot put</a> have their origins in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greece</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympics</a>, both ancient and modern, have their origins too in Europe, and have a massive influence globally.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Field_Hockey" class="mw-redirect" title="Field Hockey">Field Hockey</a> as a modern game, began in 18th Century <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a> with <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a> having the oldest federation. It is popular in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey" title="Ice hockey">Ice hockey</a>, popular in Europe and <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> may derive from this sport.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">Golf</a>, one of the most popular sports in Europe, <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, has its origins in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, with the oldest course being at <a href="/wiki/Musselburgh" title="Musselburgh">Musselburgh</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Team_handball" class="mw-redirect" title="Team handball">Handball</a>, which is popular in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and elsewhere, has its origins in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_history" title="Ancient history">antiquity</a>. The modern game is from <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> with Germany having been involved in both the first women's and men's internationals.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rugby_league" title="Rugby league">Rugby league</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rugby_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="Rugby Union">Rugby Union</a> which both have similar origins to football. Rugby Union is the older of the two codes and has rules that date from 1845 (see articles: <a href="/wiki/History_of_rugby_league" title="History of rugby league">History of rugby league</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_rugby_union" title="History of rugby union">History of rugby union</a>). They <a href="/wiki/History_of_rugby_league#The_schism_in_England" title="History of rugby league">acrimoniously split</a> in the late 19th century over the treatment of injured players. Rugby league gradually changed its laws over the next century with the end result that today both sports have little in common, apart from the basics. They have both been carried abroad by colonization, particularly to many former British colonies. <a href="/wiki/American_Football" class="mw-redirect" title="American Football">American Football</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Football" class="mw-redirect" title="Canadian Football">Canadian Football</a> are derivatives of rugby.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tennis" title="Tennis">Tennis</a> which originates from <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and related games such as <a href="/wiki/Table_Tennis" class="mw-redirect" title="Table Tennis">Table Tennis</a> derive from the game <a href="/wiki/Real_Tennis" class="mw-redirect" title="Real Tennis">Real Tennis</a> which is from <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. It is popular throughout the world.</li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_Sports">Regional Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=60" title="Edit section: Regional Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition, Europe has numerous national or regional sports which do not command a large international following outside of emigrant groups. These include:
</p>
<ul><li>Alpine Wrestling in <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bandy" title="Bandy">Bandy</a> in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Basque_Pelota" class="mw-redirect" title="Basque Pelota">Basque Pelota</a> in parts of <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, and which has been brought to the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a> by emigrants.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bullfighting" title="Bullfighting">Bullfighting</a> in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, and parts of southern <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> near the <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spanish</a> Border.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gaelic_Football" class="mw-redirect" title="Gaelic Football">Gaelic Football</a> in <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a>, which influenced <a href="/wiki/Australian_rules_football" title="Australian rules football">Australian rules football</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gaelic_Handball" class="mw-redirect" title="Gaelic Handball">Gaelic Handball</a> (<a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a>) which was taken to the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> in the form of <a href="/wiki/American_Handball" class="mw-redirect" title="American Handball">American Handball</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hurling" title="Hurling">Hurling</a> in <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Korfbal" class="mw-redirect" title="Korfbal">Korfbal</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pes%C3%A4pallo" title="Pesäpallo">Pesäpallo</a> <i>(Boboll)</i> in <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/P%C3%A9tanque" title="Pétanque">Pétanque</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boules" title="Boules">Boules</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irish_Road_Bowling" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Road Bowling">Irish Road Bowling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skittles_(sport)" title="Skittles (sport)">Skittles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bocce" title="Bocce">Bocce</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bowls" title="Bowls">Bowls</a> and others are variations of bowling games which are popular throughout Europe and have been spread around the world.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rounders" title="Rounders">Rounders</a> from Britain<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> now popular in northwest Europe from which <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">Baseball</a> derives.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Shinty" title="Shinty">Shinty</a> in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, which influenced <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey" title="Ice hockey">ice hockey</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> (<i>see also <a href="/wiki/Shinny" title="Shinny">Shinny</a></i>).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Trotting" class="mw-redirect" title="Trotting">Trotting</a> in <a href="/wiki/Southern_Europe" title="Southern Europe">southern Europe</a>.</li></ul>
<p>Some sport competitions feature a <a href="/wiki/Team_Europe" title="Team Europe">European team</a> gathering athletes from different European countries. These teams use the European flag as an emblem. The most famous of these competitions is the <a href="/wiki/Ryder_Cup" title="Ryder Cup">Ryder Cup</a> in golf. Some sporting organisations hold European Championships like <a href="/wiki/European_Cricket_Council" title="European Cricket Council">European Cricket Council</a>, the <a href="/wiki/European_Games" title="European Games">European Games</a>, the <a href="/wiki/European_Rugby_Cup" title="European Rugby Cup">European Rugby Cup</a> (Club/Regional competition), the <a href="/wiki/European_SC_Championships" class="mw-redirect" title="European SC Championships">European SC Championships</a>, the <a href="/wiki/FIRA_-_Association_of_European_Rugby" class="mw-redirect" title="FIRA - Association of European Rugby">FIRA - Association of European Rugby</a>, the <a href="/wiki/IIHF" class="mw-redirect" title="IIHF">IIHF</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mitropa_Cup" title="Mitropa Cup">Mitropa Cup</a>, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rugby_league_European_Federation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rugby league European Federation (page does not exist)">Rugby league European Federation</a> - <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rugby_league_European_Nations_Cup&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rugby league European Nations Cup (page does not exist)">European Nations Cup</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sport_in_the_European_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="Sport in the European Union">Sport in the European Union</a> and the <a href="/wiki/UEFA" title="UEFA">UEFA</a>.
</p>
