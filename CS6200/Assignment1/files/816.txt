<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hellenistic_philosophy_and_early_Christian_philosophy">Hellenistic philosophy and early Christian philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Hellenistic philosophy and early Christian philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Christianity_and_Hellenistic_philosophy" title="Christianity and Hellenistic philosophy">Christianity and Hellenistic philosophy</a></div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_philosophy" title="Hellenistic philosophy">Hellenism</a> is the traditional designation for the Greek culture of the Roman Empire in the days of Jesus, Paul, and for centuries after. Classical philosophies of the Greeks had already expired and diluted beyond recognition except for small bands of continuators of the traditions of the Pythagoreans, of Plato, and Aristotle (whose library was lost for centuries). The new philosophies of the Hellenistic world were those of the Cynics, Skeptics, and increasingly the Stoics. Gradually a more integral and rounded tendency emerged within Hellenism, but also in certain respects in opposition at times to it in regard to one philosophical problem or another, or an ensemble of problems. Thinkers most closely associated with Hellenistic Christian philosophies are:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Justin_Martyr" title="Justin Martyr">Justin Martyr</a>: Christian apologist and philosopher whose work often focused on the doctrine of the <a href="/wiki/Logos" title="Logos">Logos</a> and argued that many <a href="/wiki/Stoicism" title="Stoicism">Stoic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Platonism" title="Platonism">Platonic</a> philosophical ideas were similar to ideas in the Old Testament</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tertullian" title="Tertullian">Tertullian</a>: Tertullian was a philosopher before he converted to Christianity; after that change of direction he remained a prolific writer in the second century A.D., and is commonly called the "Father of the Western Church." He was the first church father to use the term <i><a href="/wiki/Trinity" title="Trinity">Trinitas</a></i> in reference to the Godhead and developed the doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Traducianism" title="Traducianism">traducianism</a>, or the idea that the soul was inherited from the parents, the idea that God had corporeal (although not fleshly) existence, and the doctrine of the authority of the gospels. He fought voraciously against <a href="/wiki/Marcionism" title="Marcionism">Marcionism</a>, and considered Greek philosophy to be incompatible with Christian wisdom. Toward the end of his life, he joined the heterodox sect of <a href="/wiki/Montanism" title="Montanism">Montanism</a>, and thus has not been canonized by the Catholic Church.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Irenaeus_of_Lyons" class="mw-redirect" title="Irenaeus of Lyons">Irenaeus of Lyons</a>: Irenaeus is best known for his writings arguing for the unity of God, and against <a href="/wiki/Gnosticism" title="Gnosticism">Gnosticism</a>. He argued that <a href="/wiki/Original_sin" title="Original sin">original sin</a> is latent in humanity, and that it was by Jesus' incarnation as a man that he "undid" the original sin of Adam, thus sanctifying life for all mankind. Irenaeus maintained the view that Christ is the Teacher of the human race through whom wisdom would be made accessible to all.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Clement_of_Alexandria" title="Clement of Alexandria">Clement of Alexandria</a>: Theologian and apologist who wrote on Greek philosophy, using ideas from pagan literature, Stoic and Platonic philosophy, and Gnosticism to argue for Christianity</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Origen" title="Origen">Origen</a>: Origen was influential in integrating elements of <a href="/wiki/Platonism" title="Platonism">Platonism</a> into <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>. He incorporated Platonic <a href="/wiki/Idealism" title="Idealism">idealism</a> into his conceptions of the Logos, and the two churches, one ideal and one real. He also held a strongly Platonic view of God, describing him as the perfect, incorporeal ideal. He was later declared a heretic for subscribing to the "too Platonistic" doctrine of the preexistence of the soul. Despite this, Origen is a <a href="/wiki/Church_Fathers" title="Church Fathers">Church Father</a><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrafton2011222_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrafton2011222-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPope_Benedict_XVI200724–27_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPope_Benedict_XVI200724–27-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and is widely regarded as one of the most important Christian theologians of all time.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOlson199999_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOlson199999-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo" title="Augustine of Hippo">Augustine of Hippo</a>: Augustine developed classical Christian philosophy, and the whole of Western thought, largely by <a href="/wiki/Influence_of_Hellenic_philosophy_on_Christianity" class="mw-redirect" title="Influence of Hellenic philosophy on Christianity">synthesizing Hebrew and Greek thought</a>. He drew particularly from <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Neoplatonism" title="Neoplatonism">Neoplatonism</a> of <a href="/wiki/Plotinus" title="Plotinus">Plotinus</a>, and Stoicism, which he altered and refined in light of <a href="/wiki/Divine_revelation" class="mw-redirect" title="Divine revelation">divine revelation</a> of Christian teaching and the Scriptures. Augustine wrote extensively on many religious and philosophical topics; he employed an allegorical method of reading the Bible, further developed the doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Hell" title="Hell">hell</a> as endless punishment, original sin as inherited guilt, <a href="/wiki/Divine_grace" title="Divine grace">divine grace</a> as the necessary remedy for original sin, <a href="/wiki/Baptismal_regeneration" title="Baptismal regeneration">baptismal regeneration</a> and consequently <a href="/wiki/Infant_baptism" title="Infant baptism">infant baptism</a>, inner experience and the concept of "<a href="/wiki/Self_(spirituality)" class="mw-redirect" title="Self (spirituality)">self</a>", the moral necessity of human <a href="/wiki/Free_will" title="Free will">free will</a>, and individual <a href="/wiki/Doctrine_of_election" class="mw-redirect" title="Doctrine of election">election</a> to salvation by eternal <a href="/wiki/Predestination" title="Predestination">predestination</a>. He has been a major influence in the development of Western theology and his thought, and in particular his works, <i><a href="/wiki/City_of_God_(book)" class="mw-redirect" title="City of God (book)">City of God</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Confessions_(Augustine)" title="Confessions (Augustine)">Confessions</a></i>,  laid the foundations for <a href="/wiki/Western_Philosophy" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Philosophy">Western Philosophy</a>, influencing many of philosophers and making him one of the most influential figures in the history of philosophy.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Athanasius_of_Alexandria" title="Athanasius of Alexandria">Athanasius of Alexandria</a>: father of trinitarian orthodoxy involved in the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Nicene_Creed" title="Nicene Creed">Nicene Creed</a>, who vehemently opposed <a href="/wiki/Arius" title="Arius">Arius</a>, the bishop of Alexandria who held that Christ was a created being, and his following.</li>

<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Peter_Abelard" title="Peter Abelard">Peter Abelard</a> (1079-1143): Abelard was a leading 12th-century philosopher and theologian, best known for his association with <a href="/wiki/Conceptualism" title="Conceptualism">conceptualism</a> and his development of the <a href="/wiki/Moral_influence_theory_of_atonement" title="Moral influence theory of atonement">moral influence theory of atonement</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Anselm_of_Canterbury" title="Anselm of Canterbury">Anselm of Canterbury</a> (1033-1109): Anselm is best known for the <a href="/wiki/Ontological_argument" title="Ontological argument">ontological argument</a> for God's existence, i.e.: God is that than which nothing greater can be conceived. But to exist is greater than not to exist. If God does not exist then he wouldn't be "that than which nothing greater can be conceived." Therefore, God exists. Anselm's argumentation was used as a theological directive for conceptualizing divine perfection. He was one of the first Western thinkers to directly engage the reintroduction of Aristotle to the West. However, he didn't have all of Aristotle's works and those he had access to were from Arabic translations and Islamic commentaries. Also developed the <a href="/wiki/Satisfaction_theory_of_atonement" title="Satisfaction theory of atonement">satisfaction theory of atonement</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas" title="Thomas Aquinas">Thomas Aquinas</a> (1225-1274): Aquinas was the student of <a href="/wiki/Albert_the_Great" class="mw-redirect" title="Albert the Great">Albert the Great</a>, a brilliant Dominican experimentalist, much like the Franciscan, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Bacon" title="Roger Bacon">Roger Bacon</a>, of Oxford in the 13th century. Aquinas synthesized <a href="/wiki/Aristotelianism" title="Aristotelianism">Aristotelian philosophy</a> with Christianity. He believed that there was no contradiction between faith and secular reason, but that they complemented each other epistemically. He thought Aristotle had achieved the pinnacle of human striving for truth apart from divine revelation and thus adopted Aristotle's philosophy as a framework in constructing his theological and philosophical outlook. Thomas Aquinas was a professor at the prestigious <a href="/wiki/University_of_Paris" title="University of Paris">University of Paris</a>, a contemporary of <a href="/wiki/Bonaventure" title="Bonaventure">Bonaventure</a>, a Franciscan Professor at the University of Paris whose approach differed significantly from Aquinas' in favor of the more traditional Augustinian Platonism. Widely accepted as one of the most influential figures in the history of philosophy, his philosophy is the foundation for <a href="/wiki/Thomism" title="Thomism">Thomism</a>. His most famous work is <i><a href="/wiki/Summa_Theologica" title="Summa Theologica">Summa Theologica</a></i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/William_of_Ockham" title="William of Ockham">William of Ockham</a> (1287-1347): philosopher and theologian who developed <a href="/wiki/Ockham%27s_razor" class="mw-redirect" title="Ockham&#39;s razor">Ockham's razor</a> and wrote extensively on metaphysics, ontology, epistemology, theology, logic, and politics</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/John_Duns_Scotus" class="mw-redirect" title="John Duns Scotus">John Duns Scotus</a> (1266-1308): John Duns Scotus is known as the "subtle doctor" whose hair-splitting distinctions were important contributions in scholastic thought and the modern development of logic. Scotus was also a Professor at the University of Paris, but not at the same time as Aquinas. Along with Aquinas, he is one of the two giants of <a href="/wiki/Scholastic_philosophy" class="mw-redirect" title="Scholastic philosophy">Scholastic philosophy</a></li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Renaissance_and_Reformation_Christian_philosophy">Renaissance and Reformation Christian philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Renaissance and Reformation Christian philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_philosophy" title="Renaissance philosophy">Renaissance philosophy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a></div>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes" title="René Descartes">René Descartes</a> (1596 – 1650) French philosopher, mathematician, and scientist. He was a member of the Catholic church and Christian ideals can be found in his ideas, like the <a href="/wiki/Evil_demon" title="Evil demon">Evil demon</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jacobus_Arminius" title="Jacobus Arminius">Jacobus Arminius</a> (1560–1609) preacher, theologian, and church court operative.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Francis_Bacon" title="Francis Bacon">Francis Bacon</a> English philosopher, statesman, scientist, jurist, orator, and author.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jean_Bodin" title="Jean Bodin">Jean Bodin</a> (1530–1596) French legal scholar and political philosopher, he wrote widely in a number of areas</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Desiderius_Erasmus" class="mw-redirect" title="Desiderius Erasmus">Desiderius Erasmus</a> (1466–1536) not a philosopher strictly speaking; indeed, he wrote excoriatingly about philosophers. He consolidated the space of <a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">Humanism</a> in the late Medieval scholarship of letters, and came to represent its acme. He was a leader of the development of the humanities into a department of European scholarly activities. He bent his studies to recovery and exegesis of the Hebrew Bible's ancient languages and began building the first critical text, and the New Testament became a formal scholarly text. He wrote about issues relevant to the Catholic Church. He spent six years in an Augustinian monastery; he was a joyful satirist; and became most famous for his book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Praise_of_Folly" class="mw-redirect" title="The Praise of Folly">The Praise of Folly</a></i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hugo_Grotius" title="Hugo Grotius">Hugo Grotius</a> (1583–1645) his early work on the law of the seas was outdistanced by <i>On the law of war and peace</i> (1625).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a> (1483–1546) not strictly a philosopher, he knew something of <a href="/wiki/William_of_Occam" class="mw-redirect" title="William of Occam">William of Occam</a> and nominalist epistemology from an earlier era of European thought. He had also studied some philosophical materials of Augustine of Hippo, and did not follow Thomas Aquinas. Luther followed Erasmus in developing a critical text of the Biblical manuscripts.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Marsilio_Ficino" title="Marsilio Ficino">Marsilio Ficino</a> (1433–1499) influential Italian humanist philosopher who revived Neoplatonism and was a leader in the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>; translated all of Plato's and Plotinus' works into Latin, as well as many Neoplatonic authors and the Corpus Hermeticum. He also wrote many commentaries on Plato and Christian authors as Pseudo Dyonisius.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Pico_della_Mirandola" title="Giovanni Pico della Mirandola">Giovanni Pico della Mirandola</a> (1463–1494) Italian philosopher who was a major figure in the Renaissance; at the age of 23 he proposed 900 theses on religion, natural philosophy and magic, writing the <i><a href="/wiki/Oration_on_the_Dignity_of_Man" title="Oration on the Dignity of Man">Oration on the Dignity of Man</a></i>, which was a central text in <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_humanism" title="Renaissance humanism">Renaissance humanism</a> and has been called the movement's manifesto</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Huldrych_Zwingli" title="Huldrych Zwingli">Huldrych Zwingli</a> (1484–1531) leading Reformer who was influenced by a party in his church congregation to de-metaphorize the understanding of the Lord's Supper into a memorial only (no real presence, and no communion of saints, therefore no eschatological community of saints composed of the believers at the Communion Table).</li></ul>
<p>In most cases, these writers reference something in an earlier philosopher, without adding to the ongoing problem-historical shape of Western philosophical knowledge. Between Calvin, and Arminius, born four years before Calvin's death, a <a href="/wiki/Protestant_scholasticism" title="Protestant scholasticism">Protestant scholasticism</a> took from various loci and authorities of the Western Middle Ages. It begins already with Luther's colleague <a href="/wiki/Philip_Melanchthon" title="Philip Melanchthon">Philip Melanchthon</a>, who turned from Luther's <i>sola Scriptura</i> to  philosophical theology; but Protestant Scholasticism's Reformed variants are diverse. There were no real alternatives until <a href="/wiki/Herman_Dooyeweerd" title="Herman Dooyeweerd">Herman Dooyeweerd</a> and <a href="/wiki/D._H._Th._Vollenhoven" title="D. H. Th. Vollenhoven">D. H. Th. Vollenhoven</a> in the last century.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="17th_century">17th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 17th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/17th-century_philosophy" title="17th-century philosophy">17th-century philosophy</a></div>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Browne" title="Thomas Browne">Thomas Browne</a> (1605–1682) English philosopher and scientist who also made contributions to the field of medicine</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Joseph_Butler" title="Joseph Butler">Joseph Butler</a> (1692–1752) English bishop, theologian, apologist and philosopher who offered critiques of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hobbes" title="Thomas Hobbes">Thomas Hobbes</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">John Locke</a> and influenced figures such as <a href="/wiki/David_Hume" title="David Hume">David Hume</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Reid" title="Thomas Reid">Thomas Reid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a> (1564–1642) Italian philosopher, physicist, mathematician, and astronomer who played a central role in the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_Revolution" title="Scientific Revolution">Scientific Revolution</a>, controversially advocating <a href="/wiki/Heliocentrism" title="Heliocentrism">heliocentrism</a>, leading to the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Galileo_Affair&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Galileo Affair (page does not exist)">Galileo Affair</a>, he also wrote about the <a href="/wiki/Relationship_between_science_and_religion" class="mw-redirect" title="Relationship between science and religion">relationship between science and religion</a>; often labelled "The Father of Modern Science"</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Joseph_Glanvill" title="Joseph Glanvill">Joseph Glanvill</a> (1636–1680) English philosopher, writer, and clergyman who was a major apologist for <a href="/wiki/Natural_philosophy" title="Natural philosophy">natural philosophy</a>, although he was not himself a scientist</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">John Locke</a> (1632–1704) Extremely influential political philosopher often dubbed "The Father of Classical Liberalism"; many of his philosophical concepts were developed from his religious beliefs, which included his development of the <a href="/wiki/Social_contract" title="Social contract">social contract</a> theory. He also wrote an apology entitled <i>The Reasonableness of Christianity</i> (1695).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Malebranche" title="Nicolas Malebranche">Nicolas Malebranche</a> (1638–1715) French rationalist philosopher best known for his ideas of <a href="/wiki/Occasionalism" title="Occasionalism">occasionalism</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vision_in_God&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Vision in God (page does not exist)">Vision in God</a>; he drew heavily from the work of Augustine and Thomas Aquinas</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a> (1642–1727) English physicist, mathematician, astronomer, natural philosopher, alchemist, and theologian who was one of the leading figures of the Scientific Revolution, he wrote often about religious and theological issues; authored <i><a href="/wiki/Philosophi%C3%A6_Naturalis_Principia_Mathematica" title="Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica">Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica</a></i>; considered by some to be the most influential scientist of all-time.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Blaise_Pascal" title="Blaise Pascal">Blaise Pascal</a> (1623–1662) French mathematician, physicist, inventor, and philosopher who wrote widely on religion and Catholic theology. <i><a href="/wiki/Pens%C3%A9es" title="Pensées">Pensées</a></i> Considered a masterpiece of theological thought and <a href="/wiki/Will_Durant" title="Will Durant">Will Durant</a> hailed it as "the most eloquent book in French prose." Also developed <a href="/wiki/Pascal%27s_Wager" class="mw-redirect" title="Pascal&#39;s Wager">Pascal's Wager</a> to argue for belief in Christianity.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Antoine_Augustin_Calmet" title="Antoine Augustin Calmet">Antoine Augustin Calmet</a> 1672–1757) French <a href="/wiki/Black_Monk" class="mw-redirect" title="Black Monk">Black Monk</a> scholar of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Saint_Benedict" class="mw-redirect" title="Order of Saint Benedict">Order of Saint Benedict</a> who wrote many religious works including the famous <a href="/wiki/Treatise_on_the_Apparitions_of_Spirits_and_on_Vampires_or_Revenants" class="mw-redirect" title="Treatise on the Apparitions of Spirits and on Vampires or Revenants">Treatise on the Apparitions of Spirits and on Vampires or Revenants</a> that used a scientific approach when looking into pre-modern cases of witchcraft, vampires, superstitious beliefs and various other topics of the occult. He delved into the use of the scientific method, biology, psychology, chemistry, etymology and investigated the history of various legends of folklore to determine whether a claim of hauntings, apparitions or magic were truth or fraud.</li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_and_early_20th_century">19th and early 20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 19th and early 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/19th-century_philosophy" title="19th-century philosophy">19th-century philosophy</a></div>

<li><a href="/wiki/Egbert_Schuurman" title="Egbert Schuurman">Egbert Schuurman</a>, leading philosopher of technology who actively espouses a Christian philosophical approach</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Robert_Spaemann" title="Robert Spaemann">Robert Spaemann</a>, German Roman Catholic philosopher</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Holmes_Rolston_III" title="Holmes Rolston III">Holmes Rolston III</a>, American philosopher dealing with environmental ethics and the relationship between science and religion</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Eugen_Rosenstock-Huessy" title="Eugen Rosenstock-Huessy">Eugen Rosenstock-Huessy</a>, German historian and social philosopher</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Roger_Scruton" title="Roger Scruton">Roger Scruton</a>, English philosopher and professor</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pope_Shenouda_III" class="mw-redirect" title="Pope Shenouda III">Pope Shenouda III</a>, (b. Nazeer Gayed, 1923) <a href="/wiki/Pope_of_Alexandria" class="mw-redirect" title="Pope of Alexandria">Pope of Alexandria</a> (1971–2012), has written on almost every aspect of <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodoxy" class="mw-redirect" title="Oriental Orthodoxy">Oriental Orthodox</a> Christianity. Has pioneered Christian ecumenism and written over 150 books on many topics including theology, dogma, comparative theology, spiritual theology, and church history.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Melville_Y._Stewart" title="Melville Y. Stewart">Melville Y. Stewart</a>, editor, author of books in philosophy of religion, and a Series on Science and Religion including a 2-volume set in English, <i>Science and Religion in Dialogue</i>, 科学与宗教 (5-volume Series in Chinese), Наука и Религия в Диалоге (4-volume Series in Russian). Visiting Philosopher at various universities in China.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/James_K.A._Smith" class="mw-redirect" title="James K.A. Smith">James K.A. Smith</a>, Canadian-American philosopher who draws on three different traditions of Christian thought (<a href="/wiki/Pentecostalism" title="Pentecostalism">Pentecostalism</a>, Calvinism, and <a href="/wiki/Radical_Orthodoxy" class="mw-redirect" title="Radical Orthodoxy">Radical Orthodoxy</a>) in dialogue with deconstruction and phenomenology to create practical works for broad, general audiences</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Brendan_Sweetman" title="Brendan Sweetman">Brendan Sweetman</a>, Irish philosopher of religion</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Richard_Swinburne" title="Richard Swinburne">Richard Swinburne</a>, British philosopher of religion</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aleksandr_Solzhenitsyn" title="Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn">Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn</a>, Russian writer and philosopher; won the 1970 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Literature" title="Nobel Prize in Literature">Nobel Prize in Literature</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Peter_van_Inwagen" title="Peter van Inwagen">Peter van Inwagen</a>, metaphysician who is one of the leading figures in contemporary philosophy of religion, teaching at the University of Notre Dame</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Charles_Taylor_(philosopher)" title="Charles Taylor (philosopher)">Charles Taylor</a>, Canadian political philosopher, philosopher of social science and social theorist</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Charles_Taliaferro" title="Charles Taliaferro">Charles Taliaferro</a>, an expert in the philosophy of religion and the philosophy of mind. He is a professor of philosophy at St. Olaf College and a senior research fellow at <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20120115012149/http://www.faithfulresearch.com/">the Institute for Faithful Research</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Paul_Tillich" title="Paul Tillich">Paul Tillich</a>, Rather than beginning his philosophical work with questions of God or gods, Tillich began with a "phenomenology of the Holy." His basic thesis is that religion is <i>Ultimate Concern</i>. What a person is Ultimately Concerned with in regard to their Ultimate meaning and being can be understood as religion because, "There is nobody to whom nothing is sacred because no one can rid themselves of their humanity no matter how desperately they may try" (Young-Ho Chun, <i>Tillich and Religion</i>, 1998, pg. 14.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Denys_Turner" title="Denys Turner">Denys Turner</a>, British philosopher and theologian teaching at Yale University whose work focuses on political philosophy, social theory, and mystical theology</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nick_Trakakis" title="Nick Trakakis">Nick Trakakis</a>, Australian philosopher who specializes in the philosophy of religion and theodicy</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bas_Van_Fraassen" class="mw-redirect" title="Bas Van Fraassen">Bas Van Fraassen</a>, world-renowned philosopher of science, who is also a Catholic</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cornelius_Van_Til" title="Cornelius Van Til">Cornelius Van Til</a>, Dutch-American Calvinist philosopher, who contributed especially in epistemology and developed one variety of philosophical apologetics known as presuppositional apologetics.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gregory_Vlastos" title="Gregory Vlastos">Gregory Vlastos</a>, philosopher specializes in ancient philosophy</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/D._H._Th._Vollenhoven" title="D. H. Th. Vollenhoven">D. H. Th. Vollenhoven</a>, Vollenhoven's <i>Calvinism and the Reformation of Philosophy</i> (Dutch, 1933) launched a philosophical movement that, after the massive re-inforcing effect of his brother-in-law Herman Dooyeweerd's first trilogy, <i>Philosophy of the Law-Idea</i> (1935–36), led to the formation of the Association for Calvinist Philosophy in 1936. For decades, Vollenhoven served as president of the aforementioned association, which has become the Association for Reformational Philosophy/ Vereniging voor Reformatorische Wijsbegeerte (VRW), still based in the Netherlands but with ever-enlarging interest in the rest of the world. It is disputed whether Vollenhoven's, his colleague Herman Dooyeweerd's, and many among the subsequent generations of philosophers in the Reformational philosophy movement are best described as "modern" or "postmodern," since they anticipated numerous themes that resurfaced in <a href="/wiki/Postmodernism" title="Postmodernism">postmodernism</a>, yet remain steadfastly and would-be distinctively Christian and non-Roman.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Keith_Ward" title="Keith Ward">Keith Ward</a>, British philosopher, theologian, and pastor who has written widely in the areas of the philosophy of religion and <a href="/wiki/Comparative_theology" title="Comparative theology">comparative theology</a>, has also made major contributions related to the relationship between science and religion; advocates for open theism</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Simone_Weil" title="Simone Weil">Simone Weil</a>, French philosopher, mystic, and social activist</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cornel_West" title="Cornel West">Cornel West</a>, philosopher, writer, public speaker and political activist who argues for <a href="/wiki/Christian_Socialism" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian Socialism">Christian Socialism</a>; has taught at Harvard, Princeton, Yale, and Union Theological Seminary in New York</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Westphal" title="Jonathan Westphal">Jonathan Westphal</a>, analytic philosopher of time and color, member of the <a href="/wiki/Epiphany_Philosophers" title="Epiphany Philosophers">Epiphany Philosophers</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dallas_Willard" title="Dallas Willard">Dallas Willard</a>, notable Christian philosopher at the University of Southern California. Willard has written extensively in philosophy but also in practical Christian theology with an emphasis in Christian spiritual formation.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Wolterstorff" title="Nicholas Wolterstorff">Nicholas Wolterstorff</a>, American philosopher at Yale University associated with Reformed epistemology who has written on epistemology, metaphysics, aesthetics, political philosophy, and the philosophy of religion</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Christos_Yannaras" title="Christos Yannaras">Christos Yannaras</a>, Greek philosopher</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Linda_Trinkaus_Zagzebski" title="Linda Trinkaus Zagzebski">Linda Trinkaus Zagzebski</a>, American philosopher specializing in the philosophy of religion, epistemology and ethics; pioneer in the field of <a href="/wiki/Virtue_epistemology" title="Virtue epistemology">virtue epistemology</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dean_Zimmerman" title="Dean Zimmerman">Dean Zimmerman</a>, American philosopher whose work deals with metaphysics and the philosophy of religion</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ravi_Zacharias" title="Ravi Zacharias">Ravi Zacharias</a>, American Christian philosopher and apologist who specializes in philosophy of religion and worldview.</li></ul>
