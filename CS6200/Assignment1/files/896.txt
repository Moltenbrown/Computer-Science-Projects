	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Renaissance of the 12th century</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Canterburyintr.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Canterburyintr.JPG/220px-Canterburyintr.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="332" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Canterburyintr.JPG/330px-Canterburyintr.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Canterburyintr.JPG/440px-Canterburyintr.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1678" data-file-height="2532" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Canterburyintr.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>New technological discoveries allowed the development of <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic architecture</a></div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Renaissance of the 12th century </b> was a period of many changes at the outset of the <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">High Middle Ages</a>. It included <a href="/wiki/Social" title="Social">social</a>, <a href="/wiki/Political" class="mw-redirect" title="Political">political</a> and <a href="/wiki/Economic" class="mw-redirect" title="Economic">economic</a> transformations, and an <a href="/wiki/Intellectual" title="Intellectual">intellectual</a> revitalization of <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a> with strong <a href="/wiki/Philosophical" class="mw-redirect" title="Philosophical">philosophical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scientific" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific">scientific</a> roots. These changes paved the way for later achievements such as the literary and artistic movement of the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance" title="Italian Renaissance">Italian Renaissance</a> in the 15th century and the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_Revolution" title="Scientific Revolution">scientific developments of the 17th century</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_renaissances">Medieval renaissances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renaissance_of_the_12th_century&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Medieval renaissances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Medieval_renaissances" title="Medieval renaissances">Medieval renaissances</a></div>
<p>The groundwork for the rebirth of learning was laid by the process of political consolidation and centralization of the monarchies of Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  This process of centralization began with <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a> (768–814) King of the Franks and later (800–814), Holy Roman Emperor.  Charlemagne's inclination towards education, which led to the creation of many new churches and schools where students were required to learn Latin and Greek, has been called the <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_Renaissance" title="Carolingian Renaissance">Carolingian Renaissance</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  
</p><p>A second "renaissance" occurred during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Otto_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Otto I">Otto I</a> (The Great) (936–973) King of the Saxons<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and from 962 onwards Emperor of the Holy Roman Empire.  Otto was successful in unifying his kingdom and asserting his right to appoint bishops and archbishops throughout his kingdom.  Otto's assumption of this ecclesiastical power brought him into close contact with the best educated and ablest class of men in his kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  Because of this close contact many new reforms were introduced in the Saxon Kingdom and in the Holy Roman Empire.  Thus, Otto's reign has been called the <a href="/wiki/Ottonian_Renaissance" title="Ottonian Renaissance">Ottonian Renaissance</a>. 
</p><p>Therefore, the Renaissance of the 12th century has been identified as the third and final of the <a href="/wiki/Medieval_renaissances" title="Medieval renaissances">medieval renaissances</a>.  Yet the renaissance of the twelfth century was far more thoroughgoing than those renaissances that preceded in the Carolingian or in the Ottonian periods.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  Indeed, Charlemagne's Carolingian Renaissance was really more particular to Charlemagne himself, and was really more of a "veneer on a changing society"<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> than a true renaissance springing up from society, and the same might be said of the Ottonian Renaissance.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historiography">Historiography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renaissance_of_the_12th_century&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historiography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Charles_H._Haskins" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles H. Haskins">Charles H. Haskins</a> was the first historian to write extensively about a renaissance that ushered in the <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">High Middle Ages</a> starting about 1070. In 1927, he wrote that:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>[The 12th century in Europe] was in many respects an age of fresh and vigorous life. The epoch of the <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">Crusades</a>, of the <a href="/wiki/Commune_(medieval)" class="mw-redirect" title="Commune (medieval)">rise of towns</a>, and of the earliest <a href="/wiki/Nation_state" title="Nation state">bureaucratic states</a> of the West, it saw the culmination of <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_art" title="Romanesque art">Romanesque art</a> and the beginnings of <a href="/wiki/Gothic_art" title="Gothic art">Gothic</a>; the emergence of the <a href="/wiki/Vernacular_literature" title="Vernacular literature">vernacular literatures</a>; the revival of the Latin <a href="/wiki/Classics" title="Classics">classics</a> and of Latin poetry and <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">Roman law</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Latin_translations_of_the_12th_century" title="Latin translations of the 12th century">recovery</a> of <a href="/wiki/History_of_science_and_technology" title="History of science and technology">Greek science</a>, with its <a href="/wiki/Islamic_science" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic science">Arabic additions</a>, and of much of <a href="/wiki/Greek_philosophy" class="mw-redirect" title="Greek philosophy">Greek philosophy</a>; and the origin of the first <a href="/wiki/Medieval_university" title="Medieval university">European universities</a>. The 12th century left its signature on higher education, on the <a href="/wiki/Scholasticism" title="Scholasticism">scholastic philosophy</a>, on European systems of law, on architecture and sculpture, on the <a href="/wiki/Liturgical_drama" title="Liturgical drama">liturgical drama</a>, on Latin and vernacular poetry...<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>British art historian <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Clark" title="Kenneth Clark">Kenneth Clark</a> wrote that Western Europe's first "great age of civilisation" was ready to begin around the year 1000. From 1100, he wrote, monumental abbeys and cathedrals were constructed and decorated with sculptures, hangings, mosaics and works belonging to one of the greatest epochs of art and providing stark contrast to the monotonous and cramped conditions of ordinary living during the period. <a href="/wiki/Abbot_Suger" class="mw-redirect" title="Abbot Suger">Abbot Suger</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_St_Denis" class="mw-redirect" title="Basilica of St Denis">Abbey of St. Denis</a> is considered an influential early patron of Gothic architecture and believed that love of beauty brought people closer to God: "The dull mind rises to truth through that which is material". Clark calls this "the intellectual background of all the sublime works of art of the next century and in fact has remained the basis of our belief of the value of art until today".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Legal historian Vanja Hamzić noted:
<br />
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The eventful twelfth century was, in many ways, a veritable paradox. On the one hand, it saw a sudden surge in academic works and universities in western and southern Europe that sought to bridge the worlds previously thought entirely incommensurable and usher in an age of scholasticism that would eventually lead to the fourteenth- to seventeenth-century Renaissance. For this reason, it has been a staple of mediaevalist scholarship to describe those thorough-going changes as the 'renaissance of the twelfth century'. On the other hand, the same century also reads as a striking catalogue of most violent acts and disasters: from the rise of inquisition and merciless Christian infighting, over the first expulsions of Jews and the intensification of the <i>Reconquista</i> on Muslim Spain to the blood and gore of the Second, Third and German Crusades. Might it not be more appropriate, then, to characterise this period as an age of profound crisis, in which the true contours of a 'persecuting society' were drawn?<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote> 
<p>Interested in gender and sexual diversity in this period, Hamzić considers "an unlikely rise of neo-Roman European civil law and <a href="/wiki/Seljuq_dynasty" title="Seljuq dynasty">Seljuk</a> proto-civil legality and its formidable effect on two paradigmatic twelfth-century intellectual debates on the public, legal and theological standing of '<a href="/wiki/Sodomy" title="Sodomy">sodomy</a>' (<i>peccatum sodomiticum</i>, <i>liwāṭ</i>): one in amongst prominent <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Saint_Benedict" class="mw-redirect" title="Order of Saint Benedict">Benedictines</a> and the other between the leading <a href="/wiki/Hanafi" title="Hanafi">Ḥanafī</a> scholars". <sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> He argues that these debates, "led in the distinct spirit of <i>concordia discors</i> (discordant harmony) or <i>ikhtilāf</i> (permissible scholarly disagreement), are indispensable for our understanding of legal and social aspects of sexual and gender diversity in the twelfth century and, in turn, the way in which certain rapturous pluralities were continued and ruptured—concomitantly".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> This complicates the story of the twelfth-century Renaissance as a site of both progressive and deeply troubled legal, religious and social developments.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Translation_movement">Translation movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renaissance_of_the_12th_century&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Translation movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Al_Razi_Receuil_de_traite_de_medecine_translated_by_Gerard_de_Cremone_Second_half_of_13th_century.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Al_Razi_Receuil_de_traite_de_medecine_translated_by_Gerard_de_Cremone_Second_half_of_13th_century.jpg/220px-Al_Razi_Receuil_de_traite_de_medecine_translated_by_Gerard_de_Cremone_Second_half_of_13th_century.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Al_Razi_Receuil_de_traite_de_medecine_translated_by_Gerard_de_Cremone_Second_half_of_13th_century.jpg/330px-Al_Razi_Receuil_de_traite_de_medecine_translated_by_Gerard_de_Cremone_Second_half_of_13th_century.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Al_Razi_Receuil_de_traite_de_medecine_translated_by_Gerard_de_Cremone_Second_half_of_13th_century.jpg/440px-Al_Razi_Receuil_de_traite_de_medecine_translated_by_Gerard_de_Cremone_Second_half_of_13th_century.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2964" data-file-height="1995" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Al_Razi_Receuil_de_traite_de_medecine_translated_by_Gerard_de_Cremone_Second_half_of_13th_century.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Al-Razi" title="Al-Razi">Al-Razi</a>'s <i>Recueil des traités de médecine</i> translated by <a href="/wiki/Gerard_of_Cremona" title="Gerard of Cremona">Gerard of Cremona</a>, second half of 13th century.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Latin_translations_of_the_12th_century" title="Latin translations of the 12th century">Latin translations of the 12th century</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transmission_of_the_Greek_Classics" title="Transmission of the Greek Classics">Transmission of the Greek Classics</a></div>
<p>The translation of texts from other cultures, especially ancient Greek works, was an important aspect of both this Twelfth-Century Renaissance and the latter Renaissance (of the 15th century), the relevant difference being that Latin scholars of this earlier period focused almost entirely on translating and studying Greek and Arabic works of <a href="/wiki/Natural_science" title="Natural science">natural science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, while the later Renaissance focus was on <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literary</a> and <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">historical</a> texts.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trade_and_commerce">Trade and commerce</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renaissance_of_the_12th_century&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Trade and commerce">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Haupthandelsroute_Hanse.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Haupthandelsroute_Hanse.png/220px-Haupthandelsroute_Hanse.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="168" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Haupthandelsroute_Hanse.png/330px-Haupthandelsroute_Hanse.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Haupthandelsroute_Hanse.png/440px-Haupthandelsroute_Hanse.png 2x" data-file-width="789" data-file-height="603" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Haupthandelsroute_Hanse.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Main trading routes of the Hanseatic League</div></div></div>
<p>In Northern Europe, the <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic League</a> was founded in the 12th century, with the foundation of the city of <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BCbeck" title="Lübeck">Lübeck</a> in 1158–1159. Many northern cities of the Holy Roman Empire became Hanseatic cities, including <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stettin" class="mw-redirect" title="Stettin">Stettin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bremen_(city)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bremen (city)">Bremen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rostock" title="Rostock">Rostock</a>. Hanseatic cities outside the Holy Roman Empire were, for instance, <a href="/wiki/Bruges" title="Bruges">Bruges</a>, <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> and the Polish city of Danzig (<a href="/wiki/Gda%C5%84sk" title="Gdańsk">Gdańsk</a>). In <a href="/wiki/Bergen,_Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="Bergen, Norway">Bergen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Novgorod,_Russia" class="mw-redirect" title="Novgorod, Russia">Novgorod</a> the league had factories and middlemen. In this period the Germans started colonizing Eastern Europe beyond the Empire, into <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Silesia" title="Silesia">Silesia</a>.
</p><p>The era of the <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">Crusades</a> brought large groups of Europeans into contact with the technologies and luxuries of Byzantium for the first time in many centuries. Crusaders returning to Europe brought numerous small luxuries and souvenirs with them, stimulating a new appetite for trade, carried out by both the Hanseatic League/Rus via the Black Sea routes, as well as rising Italian maritime powers such as <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Genoa" title="Republic of Genoa">Genoa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Venice</a>. 
</p><p>In the mid 13th century, the "<a href="/wiki/Pax_Mongolica" title="Pax Mongolica">Pax Mongolica</a>" re-invigorated the land-based trade routes between China and West Asia that had fallen dormant in the 9th and 10th centuries. Following the Mongol incursion into Europe in 1241, the Pope and some European rulers sent clerics as emissaries and/or missionaries to the Mongol court; these included <a href="/wiki/William_of_Rubruck" title="William of Rubruck">William of Rubruck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_da_Pian_del_Carpini" class="mw-redirect" title="Giovanni da Pian del Carpini">Giovanni da Pian del Carpini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andrew_of_Longjumeau" class="mw-redirect" title="Andrew of Longjumeau">Andrew of Longjumeau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Odoric_of_Pordenone" title="Odoric of Pordenone">Odoric of Pordenone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_de_Marignolli" class="mw-redirect" title="Giovanni de Marignolli">Giovanni de Marignolli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_di_Monte_Corvino" class="mw-redirect" title="Giovanni di Monte Corvino">Giovanni di Monte Corvino</a>, and other travelers such as <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_da_Conti" class="mw-redirect" title="Niccolò da Conti">Niccolò da Conti</a>. While the accounts of Carpini et al were written in Latin as letters to their sponsors, the account of the later Italian traveller <a href="/wiki/Marco_Polo" title="Marco Polo">Marco Polo</a>, who followed his father and uncle as far as China, was written first in French c.1300 and later in other popular languages, making it relatively accessible to larger groups of Europeans.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Science">Science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renaissance_of_the_12th_century&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Science_in_the_Middle_Ages" class="mw-redirect" title="Science in the Middle Ages">Science in the Middle Ages</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Islamic_contributions_to_Medieval_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic contributions to Medieval Europe">Islamic contributions to Medieval Europe</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:God_the_Geometer.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/God_the_Geometer.jpg/170px-God_the_Geometer.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="233" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/God_the_Geometer.jpg/255px-God_the_Geometer.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/God_the_Geometer.jpg/340px-God_the_Geometer.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1244" data-file-height="1705" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:God_the_Geometer.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Medieval scholars sought to understand the <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometric</a> and harmonic principles by which God created the universe.<sup id="cite_ref-Geometer_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Geometer-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BL_Royal_Vincent_of_Beauvais.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/BL_Royal_Vincent_of_Beauvais.jpg/220px-BL_Royal_Vincent_of_Beauvais.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="311" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/BL_Royal_Vincent_of_Beauvais.jpg/330px-BL_Royal_Vincent_of_Beauvais.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/BL_Royal_Vincent_of_Beauvais.jpg/440px-BL_Royal_Vincent_of_Beauvais.jpg 2x" data-file-width="6204" data-file-height="8779" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BL_Royal_Vincent_of_Beauvais.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Miniature copying of a manuscript in a scriptorium</div></div></div>
<p>After the collapse of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a> had entered the Middle Ages with great difficulties. Apart from depopulation and other factors, most classical scientific treatises of <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">classical antiquity</a>, written in <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> or <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, had become unavailable. Philosophical and scientific teaching of the <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">Early Middle Ages</a> was based upon the few Latin translations and commentaries on ancient Greek scientific and philosophical texts that remained in the <a href="/wiki/Latin_West" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin West">Latin West</a>, the study of which remained at minimal levels. Only the Christian church maintained copies of these written works, and they were periodically replaced and distributed to other churches.
</p><p>This scenario changed during the renaissance of the 12th century. For several centuries, popes had been sending clerics to the various kings of Europe. Kings of Europe were typically illiterate. Literate clerics would be specialists of some subject or other, such as music, medicine or history etc., otherwise known as Roman <a href="/wiki/Cohors_amicorum" title="Cohors amicorum">cohors amicorum</a>, the root of the Italian word corte 'court'. As such, these clerics would become part of a king's retinue or court, educating the king and his children, paid for by the pope, whilst facilitating the spread of knowledge into the Middle Ages. The church maintained classic scriptures in scrolls and books in numerous scriptoriums across Europe, thus preserving the classic knowledge and allowing access to this important information to the European kings. In return, kings were encouraged to build monasteries that would act as orphanages, hospitals and schools, benefiting societies and eventually smoothing the transition from the Middle Ages.
</p><p>The increased contact with the <a href="/wiki/Divisions_of_the_world_in_Islam#Dar_al-Islam_(House_of_Islam)" title="Divisions of the world in Islam">Islamic world</a> in <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">Muslim-dominated Iberia</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_Islam_in_southern_Italy" class="mw-redirect" title="History of Islam in southern Italy">Southern Italy</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">Crusades</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a>, as well as increased contact with <a href="/wiki/Byzantium" title="Byzantium">Byzantium</a>, allowed Western Europeans to seek and translate the works of <a href="/wiki/Greek_philosophy" class="mw-redirect" title="Greek philosophy">Hellenic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Islamic_philosophy" title="Islamic philosophy">Islamic philosophers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Islamic_science" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic science">scientists</a>, especially the works of <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>. Several translations were made of Euclid but no extensive commentary was written until the middle of the 13th century.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The development of <a href="/wiki/Medieval_university" title="Medieval university">medieval universities</a> allowed them to aid materially in the translation and propagation of these texts and started a new infrastructure which was needed for scientific communities. In fact, the European university put many of these texts at the center of its curriculum,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> with the result that the "medieval university laid far greater emphasis on science than does its modern counterpart and descendent."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At the beginning of the 13th century, there were reasonably accurate Latin translations of the main ancient Greek scientific works. From then on, these texts were studied and elaborated, leading to new insights into the phenomena of the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a>. The influence of this revival is evident in the scientific work of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Grosseteste" title="Robert Grosseteste">Robert Grosseteste</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology">Technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renaissance_of_the_12th_century&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Medieval_technology" title="Medieval technology">Medieval technology</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:164px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hugh_specs.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4c/Hugh_specs.jpg" decoding="async" width="162" height="186" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="162" data-file-height="186" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption">Detail of a portrait of Hugh de Provence, painted by Tommaso da Modena in 1352</div></div></div>
<p>During the High Middle Ages in Europe, there was increased innovation in means of production, leading to economic growth.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Alfred_Crosby" class="mw-redirect" title="Alfred Crosby">Alfred Crosby</a> described some of this technological revolution in <i>The Measure of Reality&#160;: Quantification in Western Europe, 1250-1600</i> and other major historians of technology have also noted it.
</p>
<ul><li>The earliest written record of a <a href="/wiki/Windmill" title="Windmill">windmill</a> is from <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>,  dated 1185.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">Paper</a> manufacture began in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> around 1100, and from there it spread to <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> during the 12th century.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Magnetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnetic">magnetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Compass" title="Compass">compass</a> aided navigation, attested in Europe in the late 12th century.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Astrolabe" title="Astrolabe">astrolabe</a> returned to Europe via Islamic Spain.</li>
<li>The West's oldest known depiction of a stern-mounted <a href="/wiki/Rudder" title="Rudder">rudder</a> can be found on church carvings dating to around 1180.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Latin_literature">Latin literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renaissance_of_the_12th_century&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Latin literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The early 12th century saw a revival of the study of Latin classics, prose, and verse before and independent of the revival of Greek philosophy in Latin translation. The <a href="/wiki/Cathedral_schools" class="mw-redirect" title="Cathedral schools">Cathedral schools</a> at <a href="/wiki/Chartres_Cathedral" title="Chartres Cathedral">Chartres</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orl%C3%A9ans_Cathedral" title="Orléans Cathedral">Orleans</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Canterbury" title="Canterbury">Canterbury</a> were centers of Latin literature staffed by notable scholars. <a href="/wiki/John_of_Salisbury" title="John of Salisbury">John of Salisbury</a>, secretary at Canterbury, became the bishop of Chartres. He held <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a> in the highest regard in philosophy, language, and the humanities. Latin humanists possessed and read virtually all the Latin authors we have today—Ovid, Virgil, <a href="/wiki/Terence" title="Terence">Terence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horace" title="Horace">Horace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seneca_the_younger" class="mw-redirect" title="Seneca the younger">Seneca</a>, Cicero. The exceptions were few—Tacitus, Livy, Lucretius. In poetry, <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a> was universally admired, followed by <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Like the earlier <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_Renaissance" title="Carolingian Renaissance">Carolingian revival</a>, the 12th-century Latin revival would not be permanent. While religious opposition to pagan Roman literature existed, Haskins argues that “it was not religion but logic” in particular “Aristotle’s <a href="/wiki/Organon" title="Organon">New Logic</a> toward the middle of [the 12th] century [that] threw a heavy weight on the side of dialectic ...” at the expense of the letters, literature, oratory, and poetry of the Latin authors. The nascent universities would become Aristotelean centers displacing the Latin humanist heritage<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> until its final revival by <a href="/wiki/Petrarch" title="Petrarch">Petrarch</a> in the 14th century.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_law">Roman law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renaissance_of_the_12th_century&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Roman law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="box-Expand_section plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><a href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg" class="image"><img alt="[icon]" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" decoding="async" width="20" height="14" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="44" data-file-height="31" /></a></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs expansion</b>. <small>You can help by <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Renaissance_of_the_12th_century&amp;action=edit&amp;section=">adding to it</a>.</small>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">July 2013</span>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The study of the <a href="/wiki/Digest_(Roman_law)" title="Digest (Roman law)">Digest</a> was the first step to the revival of Roman legal jurisprudence and the establishment of Roman law as the basis of <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(legal_system)" title="Civil law (legal system)">civil law</a> in continental Europe. The <a href="/wiki/Bologna_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Bologna University">Bologna University</a> was Europe's center of legal scholarship during this period.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scholasticism">Scholasticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renaissance_of_the_12th_century&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Scholasticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Scholasticism" title="Scholasticism">Scholasticism</a></div>
<p>A new method of learning called scholasticism developed in the late 12th century from the rediscovery of the works of <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>; the works of <a href="/wiki/Judeo-Islamic_philosophies_(800_-_1400)" class="mw-redirect" title="Judeo-Islamic philosophies (800 - 1400)">medieval Muslims and Jews</a> influenced by him, notably <a href="/wiki/Maimonides" title="Maimonides">Maimonides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avicenna" title="Avicenna">Avicenna</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Avicennism" title="Avicennism">Avicennism</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Averroes" title="Averroes">Averroes</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Averroism" title="Averroism">Averroism</a>). The great scholastic scholars of the 13th century were <a href="/wiki/Albertus_Magnus" title="Albertus Magnus">Albertus Magnus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bonaventure" title="Bonaventure">Bonaventure</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas" title="Thomas Aquinas">Thomas Aquinas</a>. Those who practiced the scholastic method defended Roman Catholic doctrines through secular study and logic. Other notable scholastics ("schoolmen") included <a href="/wiki/Roscelin" class="mw-redirect" title="Roscelin">Roscelin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Lombard" title="Peter Lombard">Peter Lombard</a>. One of the main questions during this time was the <a href="/wiki/Problem_of_universals" title="Problem of universals">problem of the universals</a>. Prominent non-scholastics of the time included <a href="/wiki/Anselm_of_Canterbury" title="Anselm of Canterbury">Anselm of Canterbury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Damian" title="Peter Damian">Peter Damian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bernard_of_Clairvaux" title="Bernard of Clairvaux">Bernard of Clairvaux</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Victorines" class="mw-redirect" title="Victorines">Victorines</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-GilsonP_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GilsonP-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arts">Arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renaissance_of_the_12th_century&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Arts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Islamic_influences_on_Western_art" title="Islamic influences on Western art">Islamic influences on Western art</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Ars_antiqua" title="Ars antiqua">Ars antiqua</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_art" title="Romanesque art">Romanesque art</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gothic_Art" class="mw-redirect" title="Gothic Art">Gothic Art</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gothic_Architecture" class="mw-redirect" title="Gothic Architecture">Gothic Architecture</a></div>
<p>The 12th-century renaissance saw a revival of interest in poetry. Writing mostly in their own native languages, contemporary poets produced significantly more work than those of the <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_Renaissance" title="Carolingian Renaissance">Carolingian Renaissance</a>. The subject matter varied wildly across epic, lyric, and dramatic. Meter was no longer confined to the classical forms and began to diverge into newer schemes. Additionally, the division between religious and secular poetry became smaller.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In particular, the <a href="/wiki/Goliard" title="Goliard">Goliards</a> were noted for profane parodies of religious texts.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>These expansions of poetic form contributed to the rise of vernacular literature, which tended to prefer the newer rhythms and structures.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
