<p>A <b>husband</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Male" title="Male">male</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">marital relationship</a>, who may also be referred to as a <a href="/wiki/Spouse" title="Spouse">spouse</a> or partner. The rights and obligations of a husband regarding his spouse and others, and his status in the community and in law, vary between societies, cultures and have varied over time.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamous</a> cultures, there are only two parties to a marriage, which is enforced by laws against <a href="/wiki/Bigamy" title="Bigamy">bigamy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polygamy" title="Polygamy">polygamy</a>. Traditionally, the husband was regarded as the <a href="/wiki/Household" title="Household">head of the household</a> and was expected to be the sole provider or <a href="/wiki/Breadwinner_model" title="Breadwinner model">breadwinner</a>, a role that continues in some cultures (sometimes described as <a href="/wiki/Paternalistic" class="mw-redirect" title="Paternalistic">paternalistic</a>). Today, a husband is not necessarily considered the <a href="/wiki/Breadwinner_model" title="Breadwinner model">breadwinner</a> of the family,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (December 2017)">by whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> especially if his spouse has a more financially rewarding occupation or career. In such cases, it is not uncommon for a husband to be considered a stay-at-home father if the married couple have children.
</p><p>The term continues to be applied to such a man who has <a href="/wiki/Legal_separation" title="Legal separation">separated</a> from his spouse and ceases to be applied to him only when his marriage has come to an end following a legally recognized <a href="/wiki/Divorce" title="Divorce">divorce</a> or the death of his spouse. On the death of his spouse, a husband is referred to as a <a href="/wiki/Widower" class="mw-redirect" title="Widower">widower</a>; after a divorce a man may be referred to as the "ex-husband" of his former spouse.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_and_etymology">Origin and etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Husband&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin and etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>husband</i> refers to <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> <i>huseband</i>, from <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>hūsbōnda</i>, from <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> <i>hūsbōndi</i> (<i>hūs</i>, "house" + <i>bōndi</i>, <i>būandi</i>, present participle of <i>būa</i>, "to dwell", so, etymologically, "a householder").
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_terms">Related terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Husband&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Related terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the conclusion of a valid wedding, the marrying parties acquire the <a href="/wiki/Social_status" title="Social status">status</a> of married persons and, while the marriage persists, a man is called a husband. In heterosexual marriages the woman is called a <a href="/wiki/Wife" title="Wife">wife</a>; in same-sex marriages between males, each male is called a husband. 
</p><p>Although "husband" is a close term to <a href="/wiki/Bridegroom" title="Bridegroom">groom</a>, the latter is a male participant in a wedding ceremony, while a husband is a married man after the wedding and for the duration of the marriage. The term husband refers to the institutionalized role of the married male, while the term father refers to the male in context of his offspring, a state which may or may not indicate that a marriage ceremony has taken place.
</p><p>In some cases of heterosexual marriage, before the marriage, the forthcoming husband or his family may have received a <a href="/wiki/Dowry" title="Dowry">dowry</a>, or have had to pay a <a href="/wiki/Bride_price" title="Bride price">bride price</a>, or both were exchanged. The dowry not only supported the establishment of a household, but also served as a condition that if the husband committed grave offenses upon his wife, he had to return the dowry to the wife or her family. For the time of the marriage, they were made inalienable by the husband.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  He might leave his wife (or wives), then widow (or widows), a dower (often a third or a half of his estate) to support her as <a href="/wiki/Dowager" title="Dowager">dowager</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As an external symbol of the fact that they are married, each spouse commonly wears a <a href="/wiki/Wedding_ring" title="Wedding ring">wedding ring</a> on the ring finger; whether this is on the left or right hand depends on the country's tradition.
</p><p>"Husband" further refers to the institutionalized form in relation to the spouse and offspring, unlike father, a term that puts a man into the context of his children. Also compare the similar <a href="/wiki/Husbandry" class="mw-redirect" title="Husbandry">husbandry</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> which in the 14th century referred to the care of the household, but today means the "control or judicious use of resources", conservation, and in agriculture, the cultivation of plants and animals, and the science about its <a href="/wiki/Profession" title="Profession">profession</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_culture">Western culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Husband&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Western culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_status">Historical status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Husband&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Historical status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Seuso_and_his_wife_at_Lake_Balaton.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Seuso_and_his_wife_at_Lake_Balaton.jpg/220px-Seuso_and_his_wife_at_Lake_Balaton.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="291" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Seuso_and_his_wife_at_Lake_Balaton.jpg/330px-Seuso_and_his_wife_at_Lake_Balaton.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/27/Seuso_and_his_wife_at_Lake_Balaton.jpg 2x" data-file-width="382" data-file-height="506" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Seuso_and_his_wife_at_Lake_Balaton.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Seuso and his wife</div></div></div> 
<p>In premodern heterosexual unions (<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Roman" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Roman">ancient Roman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">medieval</a>, and early modern history), a husband was obliged to protect and support not only his wife and children, but servants and animals of his domain. The  father (as the "<a href="/wiki/Patron" class="mw-redirect" title="Patron">patron</a>") was awarded with much <a href="/wiki/Authority" title="Authority">authority</a>, differing from that of his wife (in these cultures, no polygamy existed).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> and <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Modern">Early Modern</a> European history, it was unusual to marry out of love, but then doing so became an influential ideal.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> During this period, a husband in a heterosexual marriage had more opportunities in society than his wife, who was not recognized as legally independent.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_status">Contemporary status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Husband&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Contemporary status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In contemporary <a href="/wiki/Secularized" class="mw-redirect" title="Secularized">secularized</a> Western culture, the rights of the spouses have been made equal. The <a href="/wiki/Civil_marriage" title="Civil marriage">civil marriage</a> generally forces the wealthier spouse "<i>breadwinner</i>" to provide <a href="/wiki/Alimony" title="Alimony">alimony</a> to the former spouse, even after separation and also after a divorce (see also <a href="/wiki/Law_and_divorce_around_the_world" class="mw-redirect" title="Law and divorce around the world">Law and divorce around the world</a>).
</p><p>The legal status of marriage allows each spouse to speak on the other's behalf when one is incapacitated (e.g., in a coma); a husband is also responsible for his spouse's child(ren) in states where he is automatically assumed to be the <a href="/wiki/Biological_father" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological father">biological father</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Husband&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Islam">Islam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Husband&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Islam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Islamic_marital_jurisprudence" title="Islamic marital jurisprudence">Islamic marital jurisprudence</a>, husbands are considered protectors of the household and their wives. As protector, the husband has <a href="/wiki/Rights_and_obligations_of_spouses_in_Islam" class="mw-redirect" title="Rights and obligations of spouses in Islam">various rights and obligations</a> that he is expected to fulfill and thus is offered opportunities different from that of his wife or wives, not only in legal and economical affairs of the family but within the family as well. As in most cases in Islam law and culture, everything is being related to the <a href="/wiki/Qur%27an" class="mw-redirect" title="Qur&#39;an">Qur'an</a>.
</p><p>Many Muslims may agree on a perfectly equal relationship.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Islam is the only major religion that puts a cap on <a href="/wiki/Polygyny_in_Islam" title="Polygyny in Islam">polygamy</a>, limiting the number of a man's wives to four—provided the husband can do justice to all of them. Although some religions, such as Catholicism for instance, puts a cap on polygamy all together, or even serial monogamy, allowing one spouse until death does them apart, not even accepting divorce. According to the teachings of Islam a Muslim man should have a valid reason and have to get permission from his existing wife (without any force) if he requires to marry again. Islam vehemently abhors any intimate relationship outside the bond of marriage.
</p><p>There is no external sign to show his status as a husband, unless he adopted the tradition of wearing a wedding ring.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hinduism">Hinduism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Husband&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hinduism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Marriage_in_Hinduism" title="Marriage in Hinduism">Marriage in Hinduism</a></div>
<p>A Hindu husband traditionally takes his wife to his home. He is expected to provide for her and to prove his abilities to do so. The marriage in Hinduism is a relationship for Seven births (सात जन्मों का रिश्ता). Before 1951 there was no divorce allowed in Hindu marriage.
</p><p>In modern times once again after 1951, equal rights for women through society and law jurisdiction is given. In Hinduism, based on the different regions, marriage process is observed differently with the same Saat Pheras around agni kund (light pyre) to be taken to become a husband and wife.
</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica" title="Encyclopædia Britannica">Encyclopædia Britannica</a></i> mentions that "In Hindu law, the male members of a joint family, together with their wives, widows, and children, are entitled to support out of the joint property."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Buddhism_and_Chinese_folk_religions">Buddhism and Chinese folk religions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Husband&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Buddhism and Chinese folk religions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>China's family laws were changed by the Communist revolution; and in 1950, the People's Republic of China enacted a comprehensive marriage law including provisions giving the spouses equal rights with regard to ownership and management of marital property.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Christianity">Christianity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Husband&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Christianity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Christianity, according to the Bible, a husband in a heterosexual marriage has a number of honors:
</p>
<ul><li>To present his bride to God throughout their lives as perfectly holy and virtuous as can be.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>To protect his wife with his own life, physically, emotionally and "spiritually".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>To "lay down" his life, counting her more important than himself.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>To lead his wife and his family into the best things for them.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>To be the best that he can be in God's power.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>To not withhold his body from her.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_cultures">Other cultures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Husband&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other cultures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Japan, before enactment of the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Constitution" title="Meiji Constitution">Meiji</a> Civil Code of 1898, all of the woman's property such as land or money passed to her husband except for personal clothing and a mirror stand.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Expectation_of_fidelity">Expectation of fidelity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Husband&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Expectation of fidelity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Adultery" title="Adultery">Adultery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Open_marriage" title="Open marriage">Open marriage</a></div>
<p>Although there is generally an expectation for a spouse not to have <a href="/wiki/Sexual_relation" class="mw-redirect" title="Sexual relation">sexual relations</a> with anyone other than his spouse(s), historically, in most cultures, this expectation was not as strong as in the case of wives, a situation which was evident in legal codes which prohibited adultery, with male adultery often being criminalized only if "aggravating" circumstances existed, such as if he brought his mistress in the conjugal home, or if there was public scandal.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The double standard was also evident in divorce laws of many countries, such as the UK or Australia, which differentiated between female adultery, which was a ground of adultery by itself, and male adultery, which was a ground only under certain circumstances.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> This double standard continues to be seen today in many parts of the world. For instance, in the Philippines, a wife can be charged with the crime of a<i>dultery</i> (for merely having one act of sexual intercourse with a man other than her husband), while a husband can only be charged with the related crime of c<i>oncubinage</i>, which is more loosely defined (it requires either keeping the mistress in the family home, or cohabiting with her, or having sexual relations under scandalous circumstances).<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A breach of this expectation of <a href="/wiki/Fidelity" title="Fidelity">fidelity</a> is commonly referred to as <a href="/wiki/Adultery" title="Adultery">adultery</a> or <a href="/wiki/Extramarital_sex" title="Extramarital sex">extramarital sex</a>. Historically, adultery has been considered a serious <a href="/wiki/Offense_(law)" class="mw-redirect" title="Offense (law)">offense</a>, sometimes a crime. Even if that is not so, it may still have legal consequences, particularly a divorce. Adultery may be a factor to consider in a <a href="/wiki/Property_settlement" class="mw-redirect" title="Property settlement">property settlement</a>, it may affect the status of children, the <a href="/wiki/Custody_of_children" class="mw-redirect" title="Custody of children">custody of children</a>, etc.
</p>
