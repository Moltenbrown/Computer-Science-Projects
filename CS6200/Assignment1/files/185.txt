<p><b>Rogers</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Patronymic_surname" title="Patronymic surname">patronymic surname</a> of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> origin, deriving from the given name of <a href="/wiki/Roger" title="Roger">Roger</a> commonly used by the <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Normans</a> and meaning "son of Roger".  Variants include <a href="/wiki/Rodgers" title="Rodgers">Rodgers</a>.
</p><p>Most genealogists believe that the name Roger is derived from the pre-7th century Anglo-Saxon name <a href="/wiki/Hrothgar" title="Hrothgar">Hrothgar</a>, which means 'fame spear' ("hroð" fame or renown, "gari" spear), the first reference to which is in <a href="/wiki/Beowulf" title="Beowulf">Beowulf</a>, the Anglo-Saxon epic poem.
</p><p>The surname was probably first introduced into England during the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_settlement_of_Britain" title="Anglo-Saxon settlement of Britain">Anglo-Saxon settlement of Britain</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (May 2019)">dubious</span></a>&#32;<span class="metadata"> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Talk:Rogers_(surname)#Dubious" title="Talk:Rogers (surname)">discuss</a></span></i>&#93;</sup> The <a href="/wiki/Rogers_(given_name)" title="Rogers (given name)">"Rogers" given name</a> was probably first introduced to England after the Norman Conquest of 1066, and is first recorded as "Rogerus" in the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a> of 1086. It was introduced to Ireland when the Anglo-Normans invaded in the 1170s.
</p><p>The first recorded mention of the surname is in mid-13th-century England. Examples include William Rogger in the <a href="/wiki/Subsidy_roll" title="Subsidy roll">subsidy tax rolls</a> of the <a href="/wiki/County_of_Sussex" class="mw-redirect" title="County of Sussex">county of Sussex</a> in 1296, and Henry Rogeres in similar records for <a href="/wiki/Worcestershire" title="Worcestershire">Worcestershire</a> of 1327.
</p><p>The first recorded spelling of the surname is shown to be that of Richard Roger from 1263.  This is found in the "Archaeological Records" of the county of Kent during the reign of King Henry III (1216–1272).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The surname is now found commonly throughout Britain (often spelled "Rodgers"), particularly in southern and western <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and also occasionally in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>. The surname was also taken from <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> to <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> in Cromwellian times. 
</p><p>However many occurrences of it in Ireland represent an Anglicisation of Mac Ruaidhrí and Mac Ruairí in the newer and current standard spelling.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In England and Wales it ranks as the 77th most common surname.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  According to the 1990 United States Census, 'Rogers' ranked fifty-fourth in frequency among all reported surnames, accounting for 0.12% of the population.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
