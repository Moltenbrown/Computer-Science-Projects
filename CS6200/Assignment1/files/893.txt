	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Seminary</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p><b>Seminary</b>, <b>school of theology</b>, <b>theological seminary</b>, and <b>divinity school</b> are educational institutions for educating students (sometimes called <i>seminarians</i>) in <a href="/wiki/Scripture" class="mw-redirect" title="Scripture">scripture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theology</a>, generally to prepare them for <a href="/wiki/Ordination" title="Ordination">ordination</a> to serve as clergy, in academics, or in <a href="/wiki/Christian_ministry" title="Christian ministry">Christian ministry</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The English word is taken from the Latin <i>seminarium</i>, translated as <i>seed-bed</i>, an image taken from the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Trent" title="Council of Trent">Council of Trent</a> document <i>Cum adolescentium aetas</i> which called for the first modern seminaries.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In the West, the term now refers to <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic</a> educational institutes and has widened to include other Christian denominations and <a href="/wiki/American_Jews" title="American Jews">American Jewish</a> institutions.<sup id="cite_ref-oed_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oed-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>In the US, the term is currently used for graduate-level institutions, but historically it was used for high schools.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seminary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The establishment of modern seminaries resulted from Roman Catholic reforms of the <a href="/wiki/Counter-Reformation" title="Counter-Reformation">Counter-Reformation</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Trent" title="Council of Trent">Council of Trent</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The Tridentine seminaries placed great emphasis on personal discipline as well as the teaching of philosophy as a preparation for theology.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_Catholic_practice">Current Catholic practice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seminary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Current Catholic practice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Seminaries in the Catholic Church are divided into <a href="/wiki/Minor_seminary" title="Minor seminary">minor seminaries</a> for teenagers and <a href="/wiki/Major_seminary" class="mw-redirect" title="Major seminary">major seminaries</a> for young adults, including both <a href="/wiki/College_seminary" class="mw-redirect" title="College seminary">college seminaries</a> (though in the U.S. these are often called minor seminaries) for undergraduate students and post-graduate seminaries for those who already have a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor&#39;s degree">bachelor's degree</a>.  There are also seminaries for older adults who are well out of school, such as the <a href="/wiki/Pope_St._John_XXIII_National_Seminary" title="Pope St. John XXIII National Seminary">Pope St. John XXIII National Seminary</a> in Massachusetts, and for other more specialized purposes.
</p><p>All seminaries are run either by <a href="/wiki/Religious_order" title="Religious order">religious orders</a> or by <a href="/wiki/Diocese" title="Diocese">dioceses</a> or other similar structures.  Often a seminary will train both that particular order's or diocese's priests and the priests of other orders or dioceses that select that particular seminary for its priests.  For instance, <a href="/wiki/Saint_John%27s_Seminary_(Massachusetts)" title="Saint John&#39;s Seminary (Massachusetts)">Saint John's Seminary</a> in Boston, Massachusetts trains priests for many of the other dioceses in New England who are <a href="/wiki/Suffragan_diocese" title="Suffragan diocese">suffragan dioceses</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Boston" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Boston">Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Boston</a>. Either way, a man who seeks to enter a seminary to become a priest must be sponsored by either a diocese or by a religious order.
</p><p>Often a diocese might be attached to or affiliated with a larger Catholic college or university so that the larger college and its faculty provides more general education in history or theology while the seminary focuses on topics specific to the needs of future priests, such as training in <a href="/wiki/Canon_law" title="Canon law">canon law</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sacrament" title="Sacrament">sacraments</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Preaching" class="mw-redirect" title="Preaching">preaching</a>, or specific to the particular order or diocese.  For instance the <a href="/wiki/Theological_College_(The_Catholic_University_of_America)" title="Theological College (The Catholic University of America)">Theological College</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> is part of <a href="/wiki/The_Catholic_University_of_America" class="mw-redirect" title="The Catholic University of America">The Catholic University of America</a>.
</p><p>Further, in Rome there are several seminaries who educate seminarians or already ordained priests and bishops and which are maintained by orders or dioceses from outside of Italy.  For instance, the <a href="/wiki/Pontifical_North_American_College" title="Pontifical North American College">Pontifical North American College</a>, which trains priests from the United States and elsewhere, is supported by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Conference_of_Catholic_Bishops" title="United States Conference of Catholic Bishops">United States Conference of Catholic Bishops</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accreditation_and_recognition">Accreditation and recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seminary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Accreditation and recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In North America, four entities that accredit religious schools in particular are recognized by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Education" title="United States Department of Education">United States Department of Education</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Council_for_Higher_Education_Accreditation" title="Council for Higher Education Accreditation">Council for Higher Education Accreditation</a>: <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Advanced_Rabbinical_and_Talmudic_Schools" title="Association of Advanced Rabbinical and Talmudic Schools">Association of Advanced Rabbinical and Talmudic Schools</a>, <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Biblical_Higher_Education" title="Association for Biblical Higher Education">Association for Biblical Higher Education</a>, <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Theological_Schools_in_the_United_States_and_Canada" title="Association of Theological Schools in the United States and Canada">Association of Theological Schools in the United States and Canada</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Transnational_Association_of_Christian_Colleges_and_Schools" title="Transnational Association of Christian Colleges and Schools">Transnational Association of Christian Colleges and Schools</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses_of_the_term">Other uses of the term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seminary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other uses of the term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In general use, a seminary can be a secular institution, or part of an institution, designated for specialized training, e.g. a graduate course.<sup id="cite_ref-oed_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oed-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It has occasionally been used for <a href="/wiki/Military_academy" title="Military academy">military academies</a>, though this use is not well attested after the nineteenth century.<sup id="cite_ref-oed_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oed-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In some countries, the term <i>seminary</i> is also used for secular schools of higher education that train teachers; in the nineteenth century, many <a href="/wiki/Female_seminary" title="Female seminary">female seminaries</a> were established in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints</a> (LDS Church or the Mormons) hosts seminary classes for <a href="/wiki/High_school" class="mw-redirect" title="High school">high school</a> students ages 14 to 18, as part of the <a href="/wiki/Church_Educational_System" title="Church Educational System">Church Educational System</a>. Unlike use in other religious contexts, the word "seminary", in an LDS Church context, does not refer to a higher education program designed to train students that they may obtain a church-based career.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> LDS seminary students do not get high school credit for their seminary studies.
</p>
