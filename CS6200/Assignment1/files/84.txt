<p><b>Brown</b> is an English-language <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a> in origin chiefly descriptive of a person with brown hair, complexion or clothing. It is one of the most common surnames in English-speaking countries. It is the second most common surname in Canada and Scotland, third most common in Australia and United Kingdom<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and fourth most common in England and the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-fb_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fb-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It is particularly clustered in southern Scotland.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_history_of_the_surname">Etymology and history of the surname</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brown_(surname)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and history of the surname">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most occurrences   of the name are derived from a <a href="/wiki/Nickname" title="Nickname">nickname</a> concerning the <a href="/wiki/Complexion" title="Complexion">complexion</a> of an individual, the colour of their hair or the clothing worn. This nickname is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>brun</i>, <i>brūn</i>; <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> <i>brun</i>, <i>broun</i>; or <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> <i>brun</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Reaney_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reaney-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ancestry_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ancestry-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The root word is also sometimes found in Old English and Old Norse bynames,<sup id="cite_ref-Ancestry_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ancestry-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> such as the Old Norse <i>Brúnn</i>; however these names were not common after the <a href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman Conquest">Norman Conquest</a> (in 1066).<sup id="cite_ref-Reaney_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reaney-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In some cases, the Old English personal name <i>Brun</i> may be a short form of one of several compound names, such as <i>Brungar</i> and <i>Brunwine</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ancestry_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ancestry-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Early recordings of the English name/surname are: <i>Brun</i>, <i>Brunus</i> in 1066; Conan <i>filius Brun</i> in 1209; Richard <i>Brun</i>, <i>le Brun</i> le mercer in 1111–38; William <i>le Brun</i> in 1169; William <i>Brun</i> 1182–1205; Hugh <i>Bron</i> in 1274; Agnes <i>Broun</i> in 1296; and John <i>le Browne</i> in 1318.<sup id="cite_ref-Reaney_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reaney-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Another of the earliest recorded Browns is John Brown of <a href="/wiki/Stamford,_Lincolnshire" title="Stamford, Lincolnshire">Stamford, Lincolnshire</a> in 1312.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In Scotland, <i>The People of Medieval Scotland<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></i> academic project surveys over 8600 extant records from between the years 1093 to 1314 and lists 26 Brouns and 14 Browns on its database<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The name also originates independently in the United States, as an <a href="/wiki/Anglicisation_of_names" title="Anglicisation of names">Anglicization</a> of other surnames, such as the German <a href="/wiki/Braun_(surname)" class="mw-redirect" title="Braun (surname)">Braun</a>, or other surnames with similar meanings.<sup id="cite_ref-Ancestry_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ancestry-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It can also arise as a translation from the <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Gaelic language">Gaelic</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Donn_(given_name)" title="Donn (given name)">Donn</a></i> ("brown").<sup id="cite_ref-Ancestry_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ancestry-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The <i>Mac A Brehon</i> clan of <a href="/wiki/County_Donegal" title="County Donegal">County Donegal</a> have anglicized as Brown or <a href="/wiki/Browne" title="Browne">Browne</a> since about 1800.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a>, the name Brown is translated Mac-a-Bhruithainn (pronounced "mac-avroon") from the root word "Bruithainn", which is roughly pronounced "bro-an" and is similar to the word for judge (just as in the Irish).  Its sound is very similar to the Scots surname Broun/Broon/Brown, which are all pronounced similarly.
</p><p>The German cognates are associated with the much more common Continental personal name <a href="/wiki/Bruno_(name)" title="Bruno (name)">Bruno</a>, which was borne by the <a href="/wiki/Dukes_of_Saxony" class="mw-redirect" title="Dukes of Saxony">Dukes of Saxony</a>, among others, from the Tenth century or before. It was also the name of several medieval German and Italian saints, such as Saint <a href="/wiki/Bruno_of_Cologne" title="Bruno of Cologne">Bruno of Cologne</a> (1030–1101), founder of the <a href="/wiki/Carthusian_Order" class="mw-redirect" title="Carthusian Order">Carthusian Order</a>.
</p>
