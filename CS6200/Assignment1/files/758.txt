<p><b>Hellenistic philosophy</b> is the period of <a href="/wiki/Western_philosophy" title="Western philosophy">Western philosophy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Middle_Eastern_philosophy" title="Middle Eastern philosophy">Middle Eastern philosophy</a> that was developed in the <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">Hellenistic period</a> following <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> and ending with the beginning of <a href="/wiki/Neoplatonism" title="Neoplatonism">Neoplatonism</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hellenistic_schools_of_thought">Hellenistic schools of thought</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hellenistic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Hellenistic schools of thought">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pythagoreanism">Pythagoreanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hellenistic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pythagoreanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pythagoreanism" title="Pythagoreanism">Pythagoreanism</a> is the name given to the system of philosophy and science developed by <a href="/wiki/Pythagoras" title="Pythagoras">Pythagoras</a>, which influenced nearly all the systems of Hellenistic philosophy that followed. Two schools of Pythagorean thought eventually developed; one based largely on mathematics and continuing his line of scientific work, while the other focused on his metaphysical teachings, though each shared a part of the other.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Pythagoras" title="Pythagoras">Pythagoras</a> of Croton (570–495 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hippasus" title="Hippasus">Hippasus</a> (5th century BC)</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sophism">Sophism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hellenistic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sophism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Ancient Greece, the <a href="/wiki/Sophist" title="Sophist">sophists</a> were a category of teachers who specialized in using the tools of philosophy and rhetoric for the purpose of teaching <a href="/wiki/Arete" title="Arete">arete</a> (excellence, virtue) predominantly to young statesmen and nobility.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Protagoras" title="Protagoras">Protagoras</a> (490–420 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gorgias" title="Gorgias">Gorgias</a> (485–380 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Antiphon_(orator)" title="Antiphon (orator)">Antiphon</a> (480–411 BC)</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cynicism">Cynicism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hellenistic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cynicism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cynicism_(philosophy)" title="Cynicism (philosophy)">Cynics</a> were an ascetic sect of philosophers beginning with <a href="/wiki/Antisthenes" title="Antisthenes">Antisthenes</a> in the 4th century BC and continuing until the 5th century AD. They believed that one should live a life of <a href="/wiki/Virtue" title="Virtue">Virtue</a> in agreement with <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">Nature</a>. This meant rejecting all conventional desires for <a href="/wiki/Wealth" title="Wealth">wealth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Power_(social_and_political)" title="Power (social and political)">power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Health" title="Health">health</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Celebrity" title="Celebrity">celebrity</a>, and living a life free from possessions.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Antisthenes" title="Antisthenes">Antisthenes</a> (445–365 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Diogenes" title="Diogenes">Diogenes</a> (412–323 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Crates_of_Thebes" title="Crates of Thebes">Crates of Thebes</a> (365–285 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Menippus" title="Menippus">Menippus</a> (c. 275 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Demetrius_the_Cynic" title="Demetrius the Cynic">Demetrius</a> (10–80 AD)</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cyrenaicism">Cyrenaicism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hellenistic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cyrenaicism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cyrenaics" title="Cyrenaics">Cyrenaics</a> were a <a href="/wiki/Hedonism" title="Hedonism">hedonist</a> school of philosophy founded in the fourth century BC by <a href="/wiki/Aristippus" title="Aristippus">Aristippus</a>, who was a student of <a href="/wiki/Socrates" title="Socrates">Socrates</a>. They held that pleasure was the supreme good, especially immediate gratifications; and that people could only know their own experiences, beyond that truth was unknowable. 
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Aristippus" title="Aristippus">Aristippus</a> (435–360 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Anniceris" title="Anniceris">Anniceris</a> (flourished 300 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hegesias_of_Cyrene" title="Hegesias of Cyrene">Hegesias of Cyrene</a> (flourished 290 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Theodorus_the_Atheist" title="Theodorus the Atheist"> Theodorus</a> (c. 340 – c. 250 BC)</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Platonism">Platonism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hellenistic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Platonism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Platonism" title="Platonism">Platonism</a> is the name given to the philosophy of <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>, which was maintained and developed by his followers. The central concept was the <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_forms" title="Theory of forms">theory of forms</a>: the transcendent, perfect archetypes, of which objects in the everyday world are imperfect copies. The highest form was the <a href="/wiki/Form_of_the_Good" title="Form of the Good">Form of the Good</a>, the source of being, which could be known by <a href="/wiki/Reason" title="Reason">reason</a>. In the 3rd century BC, <a href="/wiki/Arcesilaus" title="Arcesilaus">Arcesilaus</a> adopted <a href="/wiki/Academic_skepticism" title="Academic skepticism">Academic skepticism</a>, which became a central tenet of the school until 90 BC when <a href="/wiki/Antiochus_of_Ascalon" title="Antiochus of Ascalon">Antiochus</a> added <a href="/wiki/Stoicism" title="Stoicism">Stoic</a> elements, rejecting skepticism. With the adoption of oriental <a href="/wiki/Mysticism" title="Mysticism">mysticism</a> in the third century AD, Platonism evolved into <a href="/wiki/Neoplatonism" title="Neoplatonism">Neoplatonism</a>.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Speusippus" title="Speusippus">Speusippus</a> (407–339 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Xenocrates" title="Xenocrates">Xenocrates</a> (396–314 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Antiochus_of_Ascalon" title="Antiochus of Ascalon">Antiochus of Ascalon</a> (130–68 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a> (46–120 AD)</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Peripateticism">Peripateticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hellenistic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Peripateticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Peripatetic_school" title="Peripatetic school">Peripatetic school</a> was the name given to the philosophers who maintained and developed the philosophy of <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>. They advocated examination of the world to understand the ultimate foundation of things. The goal of life was the <a href="/wiki/Happiness" title="Happiness">happiness</a> which originated from <a href="/wiki/Virtue" title="Virtue">virtuous</a> actions, which consisted in keeping the <a href="/wiki/Golden_mean_(philosophy)" title="Golden mean (philosophy)">mean</a> between the two extremes of the too much and the too little.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> (384–322 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Theophrastus" title="Theophrastus">Theophrastus</a> (371–287 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Strato_of_Lampsacus" title="Strato of Lampsacus">Strato of Lampsacus</a> (335–269 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alexander_of_Aphrodisias" title="Alexander of Aphrodisias">Alexander of Aphrodisias</a> (c. 200 AD)</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pyrrhonism">Pyrrhonism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hellenistic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pyrrhonism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pyrrhonism" title="Pyrrhonism">Pyrrhonism</a> is a school of <a href="/wiki/Philosophical_skepticism" title="Philosophical skepticism">philosophical skepticism</a> that originated with <a href="/wiki/Pyrrho" title="Pyrrho">Pyrrho</a> in the 3rd century BC, and was further advanced by <a href="/wiki/Aenesidemus" title="Aenesidemus">Aenesidemus</a> in the 1st century BC. Its objective is <a href="/wiki/Ataraxia" title="Ataraxia">ataraxia</a> (being mentally unperturbed), which is achieved through <a href="/wiki/Epoch%C3%A9" title="Epoché">epoché</a> (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Suspension_of_judgment" title="Suspension of judgment">suspension of judgment</a>) about non-evident matters (i.e., matters of <a href="/wiki/Belief" title="Belief">belief</a>). 
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Pyrrho" title="Pyrrho">Pyrrho</a> (365–275 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Timon_of_Phlius" title="Timon of Phlius">Timon of Phlius</a> (320–230 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aenesidemus" title="Aenesidemus">Aenesidemus</a> (1st century BC)</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Epicureanism">Epicureanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hellenistic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Epicureanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Epicureanism" title="Epicureanism">Epicureanism</a> was founded by Epicurus in the 3rd century BC. It viewed the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> as being ruled by <a href="/wiki/Chance_(philosophy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Chance (philosophy)">chance</a>, with no interference from <a href="/wiki/Gods" class="mw-redirect" title="Gods">gods</a>. It regarded absence of pain as the greatest pleasure, and advocated a simple life. It was the main rival to <a href="/wiki/Stoicism" title="Stoicism">Stoicism</a> until both philosophies died out in the 3rd century AD.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Epicurus" title="Epicurus">Epicurus</a> (341–270 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Metrodorus_of_Lampsacus_(the_younger)" title="Metrodorus of Lampsacus (the younger)">Metrodorus</a> (331–278 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hermarchus" title="Hermarchus">Hermarchus</a> (325-250 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Zeno_of_Sidon" title="Zeno of Sidon">Zeno of Sidon</a> (1st century BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Philodemus" title="Philodemus">Philodemus</a> (110–40 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lucretius" title="Lucretius">Lucretius</a> (99–55 BC)</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stoicism">Stoicism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hellenistic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Stoicism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Paolo_Monti_-_Servizio_fotografico_(Napoli,_1969)_-_BEIC_6353768.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Paolo_Monti_-_Servizio_fotografico_%28Napoli%2C_1969%29_-_BEIC_6353768.jpg/220px-Paolo_Monti_-_Servizio_fotografico_%28Napoli%2C_1969%29_-_BEIC_6353768.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="325" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Paolo_Monti_-_Servizio_fotografico_%28Napoli%2C_1969%29_-_BEIC_6353768.jpg/330px-Paolo_Monti_-_Servizio_fotografico_%28Napoli%2C_1969%29_-_BEIC_6353768.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Paolo_Monti_-_Servizio_fotografico_%28Napoli%2C_1969%29_-_BEIC_6353768.jpg/440px-Paolo_Monti_-_Servizio_fotografico_%28Napoli%2C_1969%29_-_BEIC_6353768.jpg 2x" data-file-width="867" data-file-height="1280" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Paolo_Monti_-_Servizio_fotografico_(Napoli,_1969)_-_BEIC_6353768.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Zeno_of_Citium" title="Zeno of Citium">Zeno of Citium</a> (333–263 BC), the founder of <a href="/wiki/Stoicism" title="Stoicism">Stoicism</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Stoicism" title="Stoicism">Stoicism</a> was founded by <a href="/wiki/Zeno_of_Citium" title="Zeno of Citium">Zeno of Citium</a> in the 3rd century BC. Based on the ethical ideas of the <a href="/wiki/Cynicism_(philosophy)" title="Cynicism (philosophy)">Cynics</a>, it taught that the goal of life was to live in accordance with <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">Nature</a>. It advocated the development of self-control and fortitude as a means of overcoming destructive <a href="/wiki/Emotions" class="mw-redirect" title="Emotions">emotions</a>. 
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Zeno_of_Citium" title="Zeno of Citium">Zeno of Citium</a> (333–263 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cleanthes" title="Cleanthes">Cleanthes</a> (331–232 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chrysippus" title="Chrysippus">Chrysippus</a> (280–207 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Panaetius" title="Panaetius">Panaetius</a> (185–110 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Posidonius" title="Posidonius">Posidonius</a> (135–51 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Seneca_the_Younger" title="Seneca the Younger">Seneca</a> (4 BC – 65 AD)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Epictetus" title="Epictetus">Epictetus</a> (55–135 AD)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Marcus_Aurelius" title="Marcus Aurelius">Marcus Aurelius</a> (121–180 AD)</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_Skepticism">Academic Skepticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hellenistic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Academic Skepticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Academic_skepticism" title="Academic skepticism">Academic skepticism</a> is the period of ancient <a href="/wiki/Platonism" title="Platonism">Platonism</a> dating from around 266 BC, when <a href="/wiki/Arcesilaus" title="Arcesilaus">Arcesilaus</a> became head of the <a href="/wiki/Platonic_Academy" title="Platonic Academy">Platonic Academy</a>, until around 90 BC, when <a href="/wiki/Antiochus_of_Ascalon" title="Antiochus of Ascalon">Antiochus of Ascalon</a> rejected skepticism, although individual philosophers, such as <a href="/wiki/Favorinus" title="Favorinus">Favorinus</a> and his teacher <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a> continued to defend Academic skepticism after this date. The Academic skeptics maintained that knowledge of things is impossible. Ideas or notions are never true; nevertheless, there are degrees of truth-likeness, and hence degrees of belief, which allow one to act. The school was characterized by its attacks on the <a href="/wiki/Stoics" class="mw-redirect" title="Stoics">Stoics</a> and on the Stoic <a href="/wiki/Dogma" title="Dogma">dogma</a> that <a href="/wiki/Katalepsis" title="Katalepsis">convincing impressions</a> led to true <a href="/wiki/Episteme" title="Episteme">knowledge</a>. 
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Arcesilaus" title="Arcesilaus">Arcesilaus</a> (316–232 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Carneades" title="Carneades">Carneades</a> (214–129 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a> (106–43 BC)</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eclecticism">Eclecticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hellenistic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Eclecticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Eclecticism" title="Eclecticism">Eclecticism</a> was a system of philosophy which adopted no single set of doctrines but selected from existing philosophical beliefs those doctrines that seemed most reasonable. Its most notable advocate was <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a>.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Marcus_Terentius_Varro" title="Marcus Terentius Varro">Varro Reatinus</a> (116–27 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a> (106–43 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Seneca_the_Younger" title="Seneca the Younger">Seneca the Younger</a> (4 BC – 65 AD)</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hellenistic_Judaism">Hellenistic Judaism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hellenistic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Hellenistic Judaism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_Judaism" title="Hellenistic Judaism">Hellenistic Judaism</a> was an attempt to establish the <a href="/wiki/Jewish" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> religious tradition within the culture and language of <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_civilization" class="mw-redirect" title="Hellenistic civilization">Hellenism</a>. Its principal representative was Philo of Alexandria.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Philo_of_Alexandria" class="mw-redirect" title="Philo of Alexandria">Philo of Alexandria</a> (30 BC – 45 AD)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Josephus" title="Josephus">Josephus</a> (37–100 AD)</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neopythagoreanism">Neopythagoreanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hellenistic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Neopythagoreanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Neopythagoreanism" title="Neopythagoreanism">Neopythagoreanism</a> was a school of philosophy reviving <a href="/wiki/Pythagoreanism" title="Pythagoreanism">Pythagorean</a> doctrines, which was prominent in the 1st and 2nd centuries AD. It was an attempt to introduce a <a href="/wiki/Religious" class="mw-redirect" title="Religious">religious</a> element into Greek philosophy, worshipping God by living an <a href="/wiki/Ascetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Ascetic">ascetic</a> life, ignoring bodily pleasures and all sensuous impulses, to purify the <a href="/wiki/Soul" title="Soul">soul</a>.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Nigidius_Figulus" title="Nigidius Figulus">Nigidius Figulus</a> (98–45 BC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Apollonius_of_Tyana" title="Apollonius of Tyana">Apollonius of Tyana</a> (15/40–100/120 AD)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Numenius_of_Apamea" title="Numenius of Apamea">Numenius of Apamea</a> (2nd century AD)</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hellenistic_Christianity">Hellenistic Christianity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hellenistic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Hellenistic Christianity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_philosophy_and_Christianity" class="mw-redirect" title="Hellenistic philosophy and Christianity">Hellenistic Christianity</a> was the attempt to reconcile <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> with Greek philosophy, beginning in the late 2nd century. Drawing particularly on <a href="/wiki/Platonism" title="Platonism">Platonism</a> and the newly emerging Neoplatonism, figures such as <a href="/wiki/Clement_of_Alexandria" title="Clement of Alexandria">Clement of Alexandria</a> sought to provide Christianity with a philosophical framework.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Clement_of_Alexandria" title="Clement of Alexandria">Clement of Alexandria</a> (150–215 AD)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Origen" title="Origen">Origen</a> (185–254 AD)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo" title="Augustine of Hippo">Augustine of Hippo</a> (354–430 AD)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aelia_Eudocia" title="Aelia Eudocia">Aelia Eudocia</a> (401–460 AD)</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neoplatonism">Neoplatonism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hellenistic_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Neoplatonism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Neoplatonism" title="Neoplatonism">Neoplatonism</a>, or <i>Plotinism</i>, is a school of religious and mystical philosophy founded by <a href="/wiki/Plotinus" title="Plotinus">Plotinus</a> in the 3rd century AD and based on the teachings of Plato and the other Platonists. The summit of existence was <a href="/wiki/Absolute_(philosophy)" title="Absolute (philosophy)">the One</a> or the Good, the source of all things. In <a href="/wiki/Virtue" title="Virtue">virtue</a> and <a href="/wiki/Meditation" title="Meditation">meditation</a> the soul had the power to elevate itself to attain union with the One, the true function of human beings. Non-Christian Neoplatonists used to attack Christianity until Christians such as <a href="/wiki/Augustine" class="mw-redirect" title="Augustine">Augustine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boethius" title="Boethius">Boethius</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Eriugena" class="mw-redirect" title="Eriugena">Eriugena</a> adopt Neoplatonism.  
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Plotinus" title="Plotinus">Plotinus</a> (205–270 AD)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Porphyry_(philosopher)" title="Porphyry (philosopher)">Porphyry</a> (233–309 AD)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Iamblichus_of_Chalcis" class="mw-redirect" title="Iamblichus of Chalcis">Iamblichus of Chalcis</a> (245–325 AD)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Proclus" title="Proclus">Proclus</a> (412–485 AD)</li></ul>
