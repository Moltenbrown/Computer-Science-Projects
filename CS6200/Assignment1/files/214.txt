	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Vocation</h1>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Vocation_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Vocation (disambiguation)">Vocation (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>A <b>vocation</b> (from&#32;<a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>&#32;<i> <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vocatio#Latin" class="extiw" title="wikt:vocatio">vocātiō</a></i>, meaning 'a call, summons'<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>) is an <a href="/wiki/Job" title="Job">occupation</a> to which a person is specially drawn or for which they are suited, trained, or qualified.  Though now often used in non-religious contexts, the meanings of the term originated in <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Senses">Senses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vocation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Senses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Use of the word "vocation" before the sixteenth century referred firstly to the "call" by God<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> to an individual, or <a href="/wiki/Universal_call_to_holiness" title="Universal call to holiness">calling of all humankind to salvation</a>, particularly in the <a href="/wiki/Vulgate" title="Vulgate">Vulgate</a>, and more specifically to the "vocation" to the <a href="/wiki/Priesthood" class="mw-redirect" title="Priesthood">priesthood</a>, or to the <a href="/wiki/Religious_(Catholicism)" class="mw-redirect" title="Religious (Catholicism)">religious life</a>, which is still the usual sense in Roman Catholicism. Roman Catholicism recognizes marriage, religious, and ordained life as the three vocations.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (November 2014)">failed verification</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> followed by <a href="/wiki/John_Calvin" title="John Calvin">John Calvin</a>, placed a particular emphasis on vocations, or divine callings, as potentially including most secular occupations, though this idea was by no means new.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Calvinism" title="Calvinism">Calvinism</a> developed complex ideas about different types of vocations of the first type, connected with the concepts of <a href="/wiki/Predestination" title="Predestination">Predestination</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irresistible_grace" title="Irresistible grace">Irresistible grace</a>, and the elect. There are the <i>vocatio universalis</i>, the <i>vocatio specialis</i>, only extended to some. There were also complex distinctions between internal and external, and the "vocatio efficax" and "inefficax" types of callings.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Hyper-Calvinism" title="Hyper-Calvinism">Hyper-Calvinism</a>, unusually, rejects the idea of a "universal call", a vocation, to repent and believe, held by virtually all other Christian groups.
</p><p>In Protestantism the call from God to devote one's life to him by joining the <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">clergy</a> is often covered by the English equivalent term "<a href="/wiki/Calling_(religious)" class="mw-redirect" title="Calling (religious)">call</a>", whereas in Roman Catholicism "vocation" is still used.
</p><p>Both senses of the word "call" are used in <a href="/wiki/1_Corinthians" class="mw-redirect" title="1 Corinthians">1 Corinthians</a> 7:20, where Paul says "Let every man abide in the same calling wherein he was called" (<a href="/wiki/KJV" class="mw-redirect" title="KJV">KJV</a>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Concept">Concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vocation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The idea of vocation is central to the Christian belief that God has created each person with gifts and talents oriented toward specific purposes and a way of life.  In the broadest sense, as stated in the Catechism of the Catholic Church, "<i>Love</i> is the fundamental and innate vocation of every human being" (CCC 2392).  More specifically, in the <a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Orthodox Church">Orthodox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Churches</a>, this idea of vocation is especially associated with a divine call to service to the Church and humanity through particular vocational  life commitments such as marriage to a particular person, consecration as a religious, ordination to priestly ministry in the Church and even a holy life as a single person.  In the broader sense, Christian vocation includes the use of one's gifts in their profession, family life, church and civic commitments for the sake of the greater common good.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Christian_views_on_work">Christian views on work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vocation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Christian views on work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many Christian theologians appeal to the Old Testment <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">Book of Genesis</a> in regards to work. According to <a href="/wiki/Genesis_creation_narrative" title="Genesis creation narrative">Genesis 1</a>, human beings were created in the <a href="/wiki/Image_of_God" title="Image of God">image of God</a>, and according to <a href="/wiki/Genesis_creation_narrative" title="Genesis creation narrative">Genesis 2</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adam" title="Adam">Adam</a> was placed in the <a href="/wiki/Garden_of_Eden" title="Garden of Eden">Garden of Eden</a> to "work it and keep it" (2:15, <a href="/wiki/English_Standard_Version" title="English Standard Version">ESV</a>). <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_L._Sayers" title="Dorothy L. Sayers">Dorothy L. Sayers</a> has argued that "work is the natural exercise and function of man – the creature who is made in the image of his Creator."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  Likewise, <a href="/wiki/John_Paul_II" class="mw-redirect" title="John Paul II">John Paul II</a> said in <i><a href="/wiki/Laborem_exercens" title="Laborem exercens">Laborem exercens</a></i> that by his work, man shares in the image of his Creator.
</p><p>Christian theologians see the <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_man" title="Fall of man">Fall of man</a> profoundly affecting human work. In Genesis 3:17, God said to Adam, "cursed is the ground because of you; in pain you shall eat of it all the days of your life" (<a href="/wiki/English_Standard_Version" title="English Standard Version">ESV</a>). <a href="/wiki/Leland_Ryken" title="Leland Ryken">Leland Ryken</a> points out that, because of the Fall, "many of the tasks we perform in a fallen world are inherently distasteful and wearisome."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Through the Fall, work has become toil, but John Paul II says that work is a good thing for man in spite of this toil, and "perhaps, in a sense, because of it" because work is something that corresponds to man's dignity and through it he achieves fulfilment as a human being.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The Fall also means that a <a href="/wiki/Work_ethic" title="Work ethic">work ethic</a> is needed. As a result of the Fall work has become subject to the abuses of idleness on the one hand, and overwork on the other. Drawing on <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, Ryken suggests that the moral ideal is the <a href="/wiki/Golden_mean_(philosophy)" title="Golden mean (philosophy)">golden mean</a> between the two extremes of being lazy and being a workaholic.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some Christian theologians also draw on the doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Redemption_(theology)" title="Redemption (theology)">redemption</a> to discuss the concept of work. <a href="/wiki/Oliver_O%27Donovan" title="Oliver O&#39;Donovan">Oliver O'Donovan</a> points out that although work is a gift of creation, it is "ennobled into mutual service in the fellowship of Christ."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Leland_Ryken" title="Leland Ryken">Leland Ryken</a> argues for seeing the call of God to a particular occupation as a reflection of the gospel call, and suggests that this implies vocational loyalty – "modern notions of job become deficient" and "the element of arbitrariness of one's choice of work" is removed.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_vocation">Modern vocation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vocation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Modern vocation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Avocation" title="Avocation">Avocation</a></div>
<p>Since the establishment of Vocational Guidance in 1908 by the engineer <a href="/wiki/Frank_Parsons_(school_counselor)" class="mw-redirect" title="Frank Parsons (school counselor)">Frank Parsons</a>, the use of the term “vocation” has evolved, with emphasis shifting to an individual's development of talents and abilities in the choice and enjoyment of a career. This semantic expansion has meant some diminution of reference to the term's religious meanings in everyday usage.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
