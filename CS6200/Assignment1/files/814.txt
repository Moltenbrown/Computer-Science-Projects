<p><b>20th-century philosophy</b> saw the development of a number of new philosophical schools—including <a href="/wiki/Logical_positivism" title="Logical positivism">logical positivism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Analytic_philosophy" title="Analytic philosophy">analytic philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phenomenology_(philosophy)" title="Phenomenology (philosophy)">phenomenology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Existentialism" title="Existentialism">existentialism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Poststructuralism" class="mw-redirect" title="Poststructuralism">poststructuralism</a>. In terms of the eras of philosophy, it is usually labelled as <i><a href="/wiki/Contemporary_philosophy" title="Contemporary philosophy">contemporary philosophy</a></i> (succeeding <a href="/wiki/Modern_philosophy" title="Modern philosophy">modern philosophy</a>, which runs roughly from the time of <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes" title="René Descartes">René Descartes</a> until the late 19th to early 20th centuries).
</p><p>As with other academic disciplines, philosophy increasingly became <a href="/wiki/Professionalized" class="mw-redirect" title="Professionalized">professionalized</a> in the twentieth century, and a split emerged between philosophers who considered themselves part of either the "<a href="/wiki/Analytic_philosophy" title="Analytic philosophy">analytic</a>" or "<a href="/wiki/Continental_philosophy" title="Continental philosophy">Continental</a>" traditions. However, there have been disputes regarding both the terminology and the reasons behind the divide, as well as philosophers who see themselves as bridging the divide, such as <a href="/wiki/Process_philosophy" title="Process philosophy">process philosophy</a> advocates<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Neopragmatist" class="mw-redirect" title="Neopragmatist">neopragmatists</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In addition, philosophy in the twentieth century became increasingly technical and harder for lay people to read.
</p><p>The publication of <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Husserl" title="Edmund Husserl">Edmund Husserl</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Logical_Investigations_(Husserl)" title="Logical Investigations (Husserl)">Logical Investigations</a></i> (1900–1) and <a href="/wiki/Bertrand_Russell" title="Bertrand Russell">Bertrand Russell</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Principles_of_Mathematics" title="The Principles of Mathematics">The Principles of Mathematics</a></i> (1903) is considered to mark the beginning of 20th-century philosophy.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Analytic_philosophy">Analytic philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=20th-century_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Analytic philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Analytic_philosophy" title="Analytic philosophy">Analytic philosophy</a></div>
<p>Analytic philosophy is a generic term for a style of <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> that came to dominate <a href="/wiki/English-speaking_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="English-speaking countries">English-speaking countries</a> in the 20th century. In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, the overwhelming majority of university philosophy departments identify themselves as "analytic" departments.<sup id="cite_ref-Searle03P1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Searle03P1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Epistemology">Epistemology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=20th-century_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Epistemology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">Epistemology</a></div>
<p>Epistemology in the Anglo-American tradition was radically shaken up by the publication of <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Gettier" title="Edmund Gettier">Edmund Gettier</a>'s 1963 paper "Is Justified True Belief Knowledge?" This paper provided counter-examples to the traditional formulation of knowledge going back to Plato. A huge number of responses to the <a href="/wiki/Gettier_problem" title="Gettier problem">Gettier problem</a> were formulated, generally falling into <a href="/wiki/Internalism" class="mw-redirect" title="Internalism">internalist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Externalism" title="Externalism">externalist</a> camps, the latter including work by philosophers like <a href="/wiki/Alvin_Goldman" title="Alvin Goldman">Alvin Goldman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fred_Dretske" title="Fred Dretske">Fred Dretske</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Malet_Armstrong" title="David Malet Armstrong">David Malet Armstrong</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alvin_Plantinga" title="Alvin Plantinga">Alvin Plantinga</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Logical_positivism">Logical positivism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=20th-century_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Logical positivism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Logical_positivism" title="Logical positivism">Logical positivism</a></div>
<p>Logical positivism (also known as logical empiricism, scientific philosophy, and neo-positivism) is a philosophy that combines <a href="/wiki/Empiricism" title="Empiricism">empiricism</a>—the idea that observational evidence is indispensable for knowledge—with a version of <a href="/wiki/Rationalism" title="Rationalism">rationalism</a> that incorporates mathematical and logico-linguistic constructs and deductions of <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemology</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Circle" title="Vienna Circle">Vienna Circle</a> was a group that promoted this philosophy.<sup id="cite_ref-ViennaCircleSEP_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ViennaCircleSEP-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neopragmatism">Neopragmatism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=20th-century_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Neopragmatism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Neopragmatism" title="Neopragmatism">Neopragmatism</a></div>
<p>Neopragmatism, sometimes called linguistic pragmatism is a recent philosophical term for philosophy that reintroduces many concepts from <a href="/wiki/Pragmatism" title="Pragmatism">pragmatism</a>. The Blackwell dictionary of Western philosophy (2004) defines "Neo-pragmatism" as follows: "A <a href="/wiki/Postmodernism" title="Postmodernism">postmodern</a> version of pragmatism developed by the American philosopher <a href="/wiki/Richard_Rorty" title="Richard Rorty">Richard Rorty</a> and drawing inspiration from authors such as <a href="/wiki/John_Dewey" title="John Dewey">John Dewey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martin_Heidegger" title="Martin Heidegger">Martin Heidegger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wilfrid_Sellars" title="Wilfrid Sellars">Wilfrid Sellars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Willard_Van_Orman_Quine" title="Willard Van Orman Quine">W.V.O. Quine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Derrida" title="Jacques Derrida">Jacques Derrida</a>. It repudiates the notion of universal truth, epistemological foundationalism, representationalism, and the notion of epistemic objectivity. It is a nominalist approach that denies that natural kinds and linguistic entities have substantive ontological implications.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ordinary_language_philosophy">Ordinary language philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=20th-century_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ordinary language philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ordinary_language_philosophy" title="Ordinary language philosophy">Ordinary language philosophy</a></div>
<p>Ordinary language philosophy is a philosophical school that approaches traditional philosophical problems as rooted in misunderstandings philosophers develop by distorting or forgetting what words actually mean in everyday use. This approach typically involves eschewing philosophical "theories" in favour of close attention to the details of the use of everyday, "ordinary" language. Sometimes called "Oxford philosophy", it is generally associated with the work of a number of mid-century Oxford professors: mainly <a href="/wiki/J._L._Austin" title="J. L. Austin">J. L. Austin</a>, but also <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Ryle" title="Gilbert Ryle">Gilbert Ryle</a>, <a href="/wiki/H._L._A._Hart" title="H. L. A. Hart">H. L. A. Hart</a>, and <a href="/wiki/P._F._Strawson" title="P. F. Strawson">Peter Strawson</a>. The later <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Wittgenstein" title="Ludwig Wittgenstein">Ludwig Wittgenstein</a> is ordinary language philosophy's most celebrated proponent outside the Oxford circle. Second generation figures include <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Cavell" title="Stanley Cavell">Stanley Cavell</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Searle" title="John Searle">John Searle</a>.
</p>
<ul><li><b>Ludwig Wittgenstein</b>: Ludwig Wittgenstein was a philosopher of language accredited with a number of works, including <i>Tractus Logico-Philosophicus</i>, <i>Philosophical Investigations</i>, and <i>On Certainty</i>. These texts explored notions of meaning, language, and epistemology. It was in his <i>Philosophical Investigations</i> that Wittgenstein introduced his language-game theory, which was one of Wittgenstein’s most significant philosophical contributions. According to this philosophy, language functions similar to any given game where there are rules that guide the game and teach the players how to play. However, the rules of Language, for Wittgenstein, are much less explicit and most commonly unnamable.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Saul Kripke</b>: Saul A. Kripke was a philosopher of language who wrote texts such as <i>Identity and Necessity,</i> as well as others. These two texts introduce Kripke's possible worlds theory and how he understands that which is necessary to the actual world in terms of identification and naming.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> He also authored <i>Wittgenstein on Rules and Private Language</i>, which introduced a new reading of Wittgenstein's <i>Philosophical Investigations</i>. This reading elaborated on the Skeptic's Paradox, which asks about the warrant and reason that people interpret the rules of any given language game. Kripke famously identified this problem by asking why one might interpret '68+57' as a problem requiring the function 'plus' rather than the made-up function of 'quus' which says:                          x⊕y=xty, if x, y&lt;57  &#160; &#160; &#160;   = 5    &#160;otherwise<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Willard Van Quine</b>: Willard Van Orman Quine was a philosopher of language who contributed significantly to the concepts of naming and the relationships between name, property, and that which is being names. He famously provides the example of “gavagai” in order to illustrate the ambiguity of naming; if one says “gavagai!”, it is not sure that another person who is unfamiliar with a rabbit will know what the first person is naming. Even with the assistance of ostension, the person who is unfamiliar with the rabbit may think that “gavagai!” is pointing out the rabbits paws, ears, etc. He makes this argument to say that there is no unambiguous naming or way to translate accurately from one language to another. Quine also presents us with his concept of a Museum of Ideas, which is to say that each person has access to a collection of concepts that contributes to the process of naming and the truth relations that we have determined about the world. His two dogmas of empiricism, Analytic and Synthetic, are presented by Quine only for his relativism to undercut ideas of empiricism.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Continental_philosophy">Continental philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=20th-century_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Continental philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Continental_philosophy" title="Continental philosophy">Continental philosophy</a></div>
<p>Continental philosophy, in contemporary usage, refers to a set of traditions of 19th and 20th century <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> from mainland <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  This sense of the term originated among English-speaking philosophers in the second half of the 20th century, who used it to refer to a range of thinkers and traditions outside the <a href="/wiki/Analytic_philosophy" title="Analytic philosophy">analytic movement</a>. Continental philosophy includes the following movements: <a href="/wiki/German_idealism" title="German idealism">German idealism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phenomenology_(philosophy)" title="Phenomenology (philosophy)">phenomenology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Existentialism" title="Existentialism">existentialism</a> (and its antecedents, such as the thought of <a href="/wiki/Kierkegaard" class="mw-redirect" title="Kierkegaard">Kierkegaard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nietzsche" class="mw-redirect" title="Nietzsche">Nietzsche</a>), <a href="/wiki/Hermeneutics" title="Hermeneutics">hermeneutics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Structuralism" title="Structuralism">structuralism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Post-structuralism" title="Post-structuralism">post-structuralism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Feminism#French_feminism" title="Feminism">French feminism</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Critical_theory" title="Critical theory">critical theory</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_School" title="Frankfurt School">Frankfurt School</a> and related branches of <a href="/wiki/Western_Marxism" title="Western Marxism">Western Marxism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Psychoanalytic_theory" title="Psychoanalytic theory">psychoanalytic theory</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Existentialism">Existentialism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=20th-century_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Existentialism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Existentialism" title="Existentialism">Existentialism</a></div>
<p>Existentialism is generally considered a philosophical and cultural movement that holds that the starting point of philosophical thinking must be the individual and the experiences of the individual. For Existentialists, religious and ethical imperatives may not satisfy the desire for individual identity, and both theistic and atheistic existentialism tend to resist mainstream religious movements. Sometimes coined the Father of existentialism, Soren Kierkegaard introduced the concerns of the existentialist from a theistic perspective as a Christian philosopher concerned with the individual's understanding of God and the resulting implications for the human condition. The individual's life gains significance only in relation to the love of God.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Common themes are the primacy of experience, <a href="/wiki/Angst" title="Angst">Angst</a>, <a href="/wiki/Absurdism" title="Absurdism">the Absurd</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Authenticity_(philosophy)" title="Authenticity (philosophy)">authenticity</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marxism">Marxism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=20th-century_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Marxism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Western_Marxism" title="Western Marxism">Western Marxism</a></div>
<p>Western Marxism, in terms of 20th-century philosophy, generally describes the writings of <a href="/wiki/Marxist_philosophy" title="Marxist philosophy">Marxist theoreticians</a>, mainly based in <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a>; this stands in contrast with the Marxist <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_in_the_Soviet_Union" title="Philosophy in the Soviet Union">philosophy in the Soviet Union</a>. While <a href="/wiki/Gy%C3%B6rgy_Luk%C3%A1cs" title="György Lukács">György Lukács</a>'s <i>History and Class Consciousness</i> and <a href="/wiki/Karl_Korsch" title="Karl Korsch">Karl Korsch</a>'s <i>Marxism and Philosophy</i>, first published in 1923, are often seen as the works that inaugurated this current.  <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Merleau-Ponty" title="Maurice Merleau-Ponty">Maurice Merleau-Ponty</a> coined the phrase <i>Western Marxism</i> much later.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phenomenology">Phenomenology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=20th-century_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Phenomenology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Phenomenology_(philosophy)" title="Phenomenology (philosophy)">Phenomenology (philosophy)</a></div>
<p>Phenomenology is the study of the phenomena of experience. It is a broad <a href="/wiki/Philosophical_movement" title="Philosophical movement">philosophical movement</a> founded in the early years of the 20th century by <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Husserl" title="Edmund Husserl">Edmund Husserl</a>. Phenomenology, in Husserl's conception, is primarily concerned with the systematic reflection on, and study of, the structures of <a href="/wiki/Consciousness" title="Consciousness">consciousness</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Phenomena" class="mw-redirect" title="Phenomena">phenomena</a> that appear in acts of consciousness. This phenomenological ontology can be clearly differentiated from the Cartesian method of analysis, which sees the world as objects, sets of objects, and objects that act and react upon one another.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Martin Heidegger</b>: Martin Heidegger was a continental philosopher who is accredited with <i>Being and Time</i> which explores the concept of being itself (partly in contrast to questions being asked about beings). He introduces his <i>dasein</i> to discuss being as deeply rooted in the world and to deny any claims of metaphysical dualism. He is known for his ideas about particularity and existential claims about human nature in terms of dasein being with other dasien.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Hortense Spillers</b>: Hortense Spillers is an academic whose work functions significantly in the intersections of race theory, philosophy, and literature. One of her most significant texts is “Mama’s Baby, Papa’s Maybe”, which discusses the philosophy of language in terms of naming and labeling black people, women in particular. The sustained argument that she is making in the paper is about the historicity of any given name. In fact, in order to make any determinations about contemporary names, one must look at the <i>history</i> of this name. Because of this, black women in the contemporary period are suffering under harmful names, inaccurate assumptions, etc., because of the history of race. She argues that race has been turned into a mere negation as people are described within a particular binary that asserts either “white” or “not white.” While harm has been done to the black body, especially the black female body, at the hands of racism, Spillers makes the case that there is power in the reclamation of harmful words to allow them to, instead, describe the righteous anger of the black woman.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-structuralism">Post-structuralism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=20th-century_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Post-structuralism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Post-structuralism" title="Post-structuralism">Post-structuralism</a></div>
<p>Post-structuralism is a label formulated by American academics to denote the heterogeneous works of a series of French intellectuals who came to international prominence in the 1960s and  '70s.<sup id="cite_ref-Bensmaia05_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bensmaia05-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Poster88_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Poster88-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The label primarily encompasses the intellectual developments of prominent mid-<a href="/wiki/20th-century_French_philosophy" title="20th-century French philosophy">20th-century French</a> and <a href="/wiki/Continental_philosophy" title="Continental philosophy">Continental philosophers</a> and theorists.<sup id="cite_ref-Merquior1987_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Merquior1987-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structuralism">Structuralism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=20th-century_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Structuralism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Structuralism" title="Structuralism">Structuralism</a></div>
<p>Structuralism is a theoretical paradigm that emphasizes that elements of culture must be understood in terms of their relationship to a larger, overarching system or "structure." Alternately, as summarized by philosopher <a href="/wiki/Simon_Blackburn" title="Simon Blackburn">Simon Blackburn</a>, Structuralism is "the belief that phenomena of human life are not intelligible except through their interrelations. These relations constitute a structure, and behind local variations in the surface phenomena there are constant laws of abstract culture".<sup id="cite_ref-Blackburn_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blackburn-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Michel Foucault: Michel Foucault was an academic whose work spanned a number of fields including sociology, psychology, philosopher, and others. Some of his most well-known works are <i>The History of Sexuality</i> and <i>Discipline and Punish</i>. His philosophy concerning power structures is one of his greatest contributions to a number of fields, including philosophy. In his <i>The Subject and Power</i>, Foucault asserts that power is a structure that commonly manifests itself in discourse and happens at the site of the human body. He explains that humans do not wield power but, instead, mediate power. Power is something that happens in action and, while we cannot escape the structure of power, we can choose to deny particular ways that it might manifest itself.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
