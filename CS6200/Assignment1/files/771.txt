<p>The <b>Italian Renaissance</b> (<a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <i lang="it">Rinascimento</i> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Italian" title="Help:IPA/Italian">[rinaʃʃiˈmento]</a></span>) was a period of <a href="/wiki/Italian_history" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian history">Italian history</a> that began in the 14th century (<i><a href="/wiki/Trecento" title="Trecento">Trecento</a></i>) and lasted until the 17th century (<i><a href="/wiki/Seicento" title="Seicento">Seicento</a></i>). It peaked during the 15th (<i><a href="/wiki/Quattrocento" title="Quattrocento">Quattrocento</a></i>) and 16th (<i><a href="/wiki/Cinquecento" title="Cinquecento">Cinquecento</a></i>) centuries, spreading across <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and marking the transition from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> to <a href="/wiki/Modernity" title="Modernity">Modernity</a>. The French word <i>renaissance</i> (<i>rinascimento</i> in Italian) means "rebirth" and defines the period as one of cultural revival and renewed interest in <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">classical antiquity</a> after the centuries labeled the <a href="/wiki/Dark_Ages_(historiography)" title="Dark Ages (historiography)">Dark Ages</a> by <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_humanism" title="Renaissance humanism">Renaissance humanists</a>. The Renaissance author <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Vasari" title="Giorgio Vasari">Giorgio Vasari</a> used the term "Rebirth" in his <i><a href="/wiki/Lives_of_the_Most_Excellent_Painters,_Sculptors,_and_Architects" title="Lives of the Most Excellent Painters, Sculptors, and Architects">Lives of the Most Excellent Painters, Sculptors, and Architects</a></i> but the concept became widespread only in the 19th century, after the works of scholars such as <a href="/wiki/Jules_Michelet" title="Jules Michelet">Jules Michelet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Burckhardt" title="Jacob Burckhardt">Jacob Burckhardt</a>.
</p><p>The Renaissance began in <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">Tuscany</a> (<a href="/wiki/Central_Italy" title="Central Italy">Central Italy</a>), and was centred in the city of <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Florence, one of the several <a href="/wiki/Italian_city-states" title="Italian city-states">city-states</a> of the peninsula, rose to economic prominence by providing credit for European monarchs and laying down the groundwork for <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Banking" class="mw-redirect" title="Banking">banking</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The Renaissance later spread to <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, heart of a <a href="/wiki/Venetian_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Venetian Empire">mediterranean empire</a> and in control of the trade routes with the east since the participation in the <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">crusades</a> and the voyages of <a href="/wiki/Marco_Polo" title="Marco Polo">Marco Polo</a>, where the remains of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greek culture</a> were brought together and provided <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_humanism" title="Renaissance humanism">humanist scholars</a> with new texts. Finally the Renaissance had a significant effect on the <a href="/wiki/Papal_States" title="Papal States">Papal States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, largely rebuilt by Humanist and <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_pope" class="mw-redirect" title="Renaissance pope">Renaissance popes</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Alexander_VI" class="mw-redirect" title="Alexander VI">Alexander VI</a> and <a href="/wiki/Julius_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Julius II">Julius II</a>), who were frequently involved in <a href="/wiki/Guelphs_and_Ghibellines" title="Guelphs and Ghibellines">Italian politics</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Tordesillas" title="Treaty of Tordesillas">arbitrating disputes</a> between competing colonial powers and in <a href="/wiki/Counter-Reformation" title="Counter-Reformation">opposing</a> the <a href="/wiki/Reformation" title="Reformation">Reformation</a>.
</p><p>The Italian Renaissance is best known for its achievements in <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance_painting" title="Italian Renaissance painting">painting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance_architecture" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian Renaissance architecture">architecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance_sculpture" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian Renaissance sculpture">sculpture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance_literature" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian Renaissance literature">literature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_music" title="Renaissance music">music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_philosophy" title="Renaissance philosophy">philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/History_of_science_in_the_Renaissance" title="History of science in the Renaissance">science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_technology" title="Renaissance technology">technology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_exploration" class="mw-redirect" title="Renaissance exploration">exploration</a>. Italy became the recognized European leader in all these areas by the late 15th century, during the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Lodi" class="mw-redirect" title="Peace of Lodi">Peace of Lodi</a> (1454–1494) agreed between Italian states. The Italian Renaissance peaked in the mid-16th century as domestic disputes and foreign invasions plunged the region into the turmoil of the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Wars" title="Italian Wars">Italian Wars</a> (1494–1559). However, the ideas and ideals of the Italian Renaissance spread into the rest of Europe, setting off the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Renaissance" title="Northern Renaissance">Northern Renaissance</a>. Italian explorers from the <a href="/wiki/Maritime_republics" title="Maritime republics">maritime republics</a> served under the auspices of European monarchs, ushering the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_discovery" class="mw-redirect" title="Age of discovery">Age of discovery</a>. The most famous among them are <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> who sailed for Spain, <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_da_Verrazzano" title="Giovanni da Verrazzano">Giovanni da Verrazzano</a> for France, <a href="/wiki/Amerigo_Vespucci" title="Amerigo Vespucci">Amerigo Vespucci</a> for Portugal, and <a href="/wiki/John_Cabot" title="John Cabot">John Cabot</a> for England. Italian scientists such as <a href="/wiki/Falloppio" class="mw-redirect" title="Falloppio">Falloppio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Fontana_Tartaglia" title="Niccolò Fontana Tartaglia">Tartaglia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galileo" class="mw-redirect" title="Galileo">Galileo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Evangelista_Torricelli" title="Evangelista Torricelli">Torricelli</a>, played a key role in the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific revolution">scientific revolution</a> and foreigners such as <a href="/wiki/Copernicus" class="mw-redirect" title="Copernicus">Copernicus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vesalius" class="mw-redirect" title="Vesalius">Vesalius</a> worked in Italian universities. Various events and dates of the 17th century, such as the conclusion of the <a href="/wiki/European_Wars_of_Religion" class="mw-redirect" title="European Wars of Religion">European Wars of Religion</a> in 1648, have been proposed for the end of the Renaissance.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Accounts of <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_literature" title="Renaissance literature">Renaissance literature</a> usually begin with the three great poets of the 14th century: <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante Alighieri</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Divine_Comedy" title="Divine Comedy">Divine Comedy</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Petrarch" title="Petrarch">Petrarch</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Canzoniere" class="mw-redirect" title="Canzoniere">Canzoniere</a></i>) and <a href="/wiki/Boccaccio" class="mw-redirect" title="Boccaccio">Boccaccio</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Decameron" class="mw-redirect" title="Decameron">Decameron</a></i>). Famous vernacular poets of the Renaissance include the epic authors <a href="/wiki/Luigi_Pulci" title="Luigi Pulci">Luigi Pulci</a> (author of <i><a href="/wiki/Morgante" title="Morgante">Morgante</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Matteo_Maria_Boiardo" title="Matteo Maria Boiardo">Matteo Maria Boiardo</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Orlando_Innamorato" title="Orlando Innamorato">Orlando Innamorato</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Ludovico_Ariosto" title="Ludovico Ariosto">Ludovico Ariosto</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Orlando_Furioso" title="Orlando Furioso">Orlando Furioso</a></i>) and <a href="/wiki/Torquato_Tasso" title="Torquato Tasso">Torquato Tasso</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Jerusalem_Delivered" title="Jerusalem Delivered">Jerusalem Delivered</a></i>). 15th-century writers such as the poet <a href="/wiki/Poliziano" title="Poliziano">Poliziano</a> and the Platonist philosopher <a href="/wiki/Marsilio_Ficino" title="Marsilio Ficino">Marsilio Ficino</a> made extensive translations from both Latin and Greek. In the early 16th century, <a href="/wiki/Baldassare_Castiglione" title="Baldassare Castiglione">Baldassare Castiglione</a> laid out his vision of the ideal gentleman and lady in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Book_of_the_Courtier" title="The Book of the Courtier">The Book of the Courtier</a></i>, while <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Machiavelli" title="Niccolò Machiavelli">Niccolò Machiavelli</a> cast a jaundiced eye on "<i>la verità effettuale della cosa</i>"—the effectual truth of things—in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Prince" title="The Prince">The Prince</a></i>, composed, in humanistic style, chiefly of parallel ancient and modern examples of <a href="/wiki/Virt%C3%B9" title="Virtù">Virtù</a>. Historians of the period include Machiavelli himself, his friend and critic <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Guicciardini" title="Francesco Guicciardini">Francesco Guicciardini</a> and <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Botero" title="Giovanni Botero">Giovanni Botero</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/The_Reason_of_State" title="The Reason of State">The Reason of State</a></i>). The Aldine Press, founded by the printer <a href="/wiki/Aldo_Manuzio" class="mw-redirect" title="Aldo Manuzio">Aldo Manuzio</a>, active in Venice, developed <a href="/wiki/Italic_type" title="Italic type">Italic type</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pocket_edition" title="Pocket edition">pocket editions</a> that could be carried in one's pocket, as well as being the first to publish editions of books in Ancient Greek. Venice also became the birthplace of the <i><a href="/wiki/Commedia_dell%27Arte" class="mw-redirect" title="Commedia dell&#39;Arte">Commedia dell'Arte</a></i>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance_art" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian Renaissance art">Italian Renaissance art</a> exercised a dominant influence on subsequent European painting and sculpture for centuries afterwards, with artists such as <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raphael" title="Raphael">Raphael</a>, <a href="/wiki/Donatello" title="Donatello">Donatello</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giotto_di_Bondone" class="mw-redirect" title="Giotto di Bondone">Giotto di Bondone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Masaccio" title="Masaccio">Masaccio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fra_Angelico" title="Fra Angelico">Fra Angelico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Piero_della_Francesca" title="Piero della Francesca">Piero della Francesca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Domenico_Ghirlandaio" title="Domenico Ghirlandaio">Domenico Ghirlandaio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perugino" class="mw-redirect" title="Perugino">Perugino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Botticelli" class="mw-redirect" title="Botticelli">Botticelli</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titian</a>. The same is true for <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_architecture" title="Renaissance architecture">architecture</a>, as practiced by <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Brunelleschi" title="Filippo Brunelleschi">Brunelleschi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leon_Battista_Alberti" title="Leon Battista Alberti">Leon Battista Alberti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Palladio" title="Andrea Palladio">Andrea Palladio</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bramante" class="mw-redirect" title="Bramante">Bramante</a>. Their works include, to name only a few, the <a href="/wiki/Florence_Cathedral" title="Florence Cathedral">Florence Cathedral</a>, <a href="/wiki/St._Peter%27s_Basilica" title="St. Peter&#39;s Basilica">St. Peter's Basilica</a> in Rome, and the <a href="/wiki/Tempio_Malatestiano" title="Tempio Malatestiano">Tempio Malatestiano</a> in Rimini, as well as <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_architecture" title="Renaissance architecture">several private residences</a>. The musical era of the Italian Renaissance was defined by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_School" title="Roman School">Roman School</a> and later by the <a href="/wiki/Venetian_School_(music)" title="Venetian School (music)">Venetian School</a> and the birth of <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">Opera</a> in Florence. In <a href="/wiki/Modern_philosophy#Modern_Western_philosophy" title="Modern philosophy">philosophy</a>, thinkers such as Galileo, Machiavelli, <a href="/wiki/Giordano_Bruno" title="Giordano Bruno">Giordano Bruno</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pico_della_Mirandola" class="mw-redirect" title="Pico della Mirandola">Pico della Mirandola</a>, emphasized <a href="/wiki/Naturalism_(philosophy)" title="Naturalism (philosophy)">naturalism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">humanism</a>, thus rejecting dogma and <a href="/wiki/Scholasticism" title="Scholasticism">scholasticism</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_background">Origins and background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Italian_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins and background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_and_Central_Italy_in_the_Late_Middle_Ages">Northern and Central Italy in the Late Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Italian_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Northern and Central Italy in the Late Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By the <a href="/wiki/Late_Middle_Ages" title="Late Middle Ages">Late Middle Ages</a> (circa 1300 onward), <a href="/wiki/Latium" title="Latium">Latium</a>, the former heartland of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Italy" title="Southern Italy">southern Italy</a> were generally poorer than the North. <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> was a city of ancient ruins, and the <a href="/wiki/Papal_States" title="Papal States">Papal States</a> were loosely administered, and vulnerable to external interference, particularly by France, and later Spain. The Papacy was affronted when the <a href="/wiki/Avignon_Papacy" title="Avignon Papacy">Avignon Papacy</a> was created in southern France as a consequence of pressure from <a href="/wiki/Philip_IV_of_France" title="Philip IV of France">King Philip the Fair</a> of France. In the south, <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> had for some time been under foreign domination, <a href="/wiki/History_of_Islam_in_southern_Italy" class="mw-redirect" title="History of Islam in southern Italy">by the Arabs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_southern_Italy" title="Norman conquest of southern Italy">then the Normans</a>. Sicily had prospered for 150 years during the <a href="/wiki/Emirate_of_Sicily" title="Emirate of Sicily">Emirate of Sicily</a> and later for two centuries during the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Sicily" title="Kingdom of Sicily">Norman Kingdom</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Sicily" title="Kingdom of Sicily">Hohenstaufen Kingdom</a>, but had declined by the late Middle Ages.
</p><p>In contrast, Northern and Central Italy had become far more prosperous, and it has been calculated that the region was among the richest of Europe. The <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">Crusades</a> had built lasting trade links to the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Crusade" title="Fourth Crusade">Fourth Crusade</a> had done much to destroy the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Roman Empire</a> as a commercial rival to the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Venetians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Genoa" title="Republic of Genoa">Genoese</a>. The main trade routes from the east passed through the Byzantine Empire or the <a href="/wiki/Arab_World" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab World">Arab lands</a> and onward to the ports of <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">Pisa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>. Luxury goods bought in the Levant, such as spices, dyes, and silks were imported to Italy and then resold throughout Europe. Moreover, the inland <a href="/wiki/City-state" title="City-state">city-states</a> profited from the rich agricultural land of the <a href="/wiki/Po_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Po River">Po</a> valley. From France, Germany, and the Low Countries, through the medium of the <a href="/wiki/Champagne_fairs" title="Champagne fairs">Champagne fairs</a>, land and river trade routes brought goods such as <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Precious_metal" title="Precious metal">precious metals</a> into the region. The extensive trade that stretched from Egypt to the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_region" title="Baltic region">Baltic</a> generated substantial surpluses that allowed significant investment in mining and agriculture. Thus, while northern Italy was not richer in resources than many other parts of Europe, the level of development, stimulated by trade, allowed it to prosper. In particular, Florence became one of the wealthiest of the cities of Northern Italy,  mainly due to its woolen textile production, developed under the supervision of its dominant trade <a href="/wiki/Guild" title="Guild">guild</a>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Arte_della_Lana" title="Arte della Lana">Arte della Lana</a></i>. Wool was imported from Northern Europe (and in the 16th century from Spain)<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and together with <a href="/wiki/Dye" title="Dye">dyes</a> from the east were used to make high quality textiles.
</p><p>The Italian trade routes that covered the Mediterranean and beyond were also major conduits of culture and knowledge. The
<a href="/wiki/Transmission_of_the_Classics" class="mw-redirect" title="Transmission of the Classics">recovery of lost Greek classics</a> (and, to a lesser extent, Arab advancements on them) following the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Crusade" title="Fourth Crusade">Crusader</a> <a href="/wiki/Sack_of_Constantinople" title="Sack of Constantinople">conquest of the Byzantine heartlands</a>, revitalized <a href="/wiki/Medieval_philosophy" title="Medieval philosophy">medieval philosophy</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_of_the_12th_century" title="Renaissance of the 12th century">Renaissance of the 12th century</a>, just as the <a href="/wiki/Greek_scholars_in_the_Renaissance" title="Greek scholars in the Renaissance">refugee Byzantine scholars</a> who migrated to Italy during and following the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman-Byzantine_Wars" class="mw-redirect" title="Ottoman-Byzantine Wars">Turkish conquest of the Byzantines</a> between the 12th and 15th centuries were important in sparking the new linguistic studies of the Renaissance, in newly created <a href="/wiki/Academy" title="Academy">academies</a> in Florence and Venice. Humanist scholars searched monastic libraries for ancient manuscripts and recovered <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a> and other Latin authors. The rediscovery of <a href="/wiki/Vitruvius" title="Vitruvius">Vitruvius</a> meant that the architectural principles of Antiquity could be observed once more, and Renaissance artists were encouraged, in the atmosphere of humanist optimism, to excel the achievements of the Ancients, like <a href="/wiki/Apelles" title="Apelles">Apelles</a>, of whom they read.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thirteenth-century">Thirteenth-century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Italian_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Thirteenth-century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 13th century, much of Europe experienced strong economic growth. The trade routes of the Italian states linked with those of established Mediterranean ports and eventually the <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic League</a> of the Baltic and northern regions of Europe to create a network economy in Europe for the first time since the 4th century. The  <a href="/wiki/Italian_city-states" title="Italian city-states">city-states of Italy</a> expanded greatly during this period and grew in power to become de facto fully independent of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>; apart from the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Naples" title="Kingdom of Naples">Kingdom of Naples</a>, outside powers kept their armies out of Italy. During this period, the modern commercial infrastructure developed, with <a href="/wiki/Double-entry_book-keeping" class="mw-redirect" title="Double-entry book-keeping">double-entry book-keeping</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joint_stock_companies" class="mw-redirect" title="Joint stock companies">joint stock companies</a>, an international <a href="/wiki/Banking" class="mw-redirect" title="Banking">banking</a> system, a systematized <a href="/wiki/Foreign_exchange_market" title="Foreign exchange market">foreign exchange market</a>, <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Government_debt" title="Government debt">government debt</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Florence became the centre of this financial industry and the gold <a href="/wiki/Italian_coin_florin" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian coin florin">florin</a> became the main currency of international trade.
</p><p>The new mercantile governing class, who gained their position through financial skill, adapted to their purposes the <a href="/wiki/Feudal" class="mw-redirect" title="Feudal">feudal</a> aristocratic model that had dominated Europe in the Middle Ages. A feature of the High Middle Ages in Northern Italy was the rise of the <a href="/wiki/Medieval_commune" title="Medieval commune">urban communes</a> which had broken from the control by bishops and local counts. In much of the region, the <a href="/wiki/Landed_nobility" title="Landed nobility">landed nobility</a> was poorer than the urban patriarchs in the High Medieval money economy whose inflationary rise left land-holding aristocrats impoverished. The increase in trade during the early Renaissance enhanced these characteristics. The decline of feudalism and the rise of cities influenced each other; for example, the demand for luxury goods led to an increase in trade, which led to greater numbers of tradesmen becoming wealthy, who, in turn, demanded more luxury goods. This atmosphere of assumed luxury of the time created a need for the creation of visual symbols of wealth, an important way to show a family’s affluence and taste.
</p><p>This change also gave the merchants almost complete control of the governments of the Italian city-states, again enhancing trade. One of the most important effects of this political control was security. Those that grew extremely wealthy in a feudal state ran constant risk of running afoul of the monarchy and having their lands confiscated, as famously occurred to <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Coeur" class="mw-redirect" title="Jacques Coeur">Jacques Coeur</a> in France. The northern states also kept many medieval laws that severely hampered commerce, such as those against <a href="/wiki/Usury" title="Usury">usury</a>, and prohibitions on trading with non-Christians. In the city-states of Italy, these laws were repealed or rewritten.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fourteenth-century_collapse">Fourteenth-century collapse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Italian_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fourteenth-century collapse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 14th century saw a series of catastrophes that caused the European economy to go into recession. The <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Warm_Period" title="Medieval Warm Period">Medieval Warm Period</a> was ending as the transition to the <a href="/wiki/Little_Ice_Age" title="Little Ice Age">Little Ice Age</a> began.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> This change in climate saw agricultural output decline significantly, leading to repeated <a href="/wiki/Famine" title="Famine">famines</a>, exacerbated by the rapid population growth of the earlier era. The <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Years%27_War" title="Hundred Years&#39; War">Hundred Years' War</a> between England and France disrupted trade throughout northwest Europe, most notably when, in 1345, King <a href="/wiki/Edward_III_of_England" title="Edward III of England">Edward III of England</a> repudiated his debts, contributing to the collapse of the two largest Florentine banks, those of the <a href="/wiki/Bardi_family" title="Bardi family">Bardi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peruzzi" title="Peruzzi">Peruzzi</a>. In the east, war was also disrupting trade routes, as the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> began to expand throughout the region. Most devastating, though, was the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a> that decimated the populations of the densely populated cities of Northern Italy and returned at intervals thereafter. Florence, for instance, which had a pre-plague population of 45,000 decreased over the next 47 years by 25–50%.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Widespread disorder followed, including a revolt of Florentine textile workers, the <i><a href="/wiki/Ciompi" class="mw-redirect" title="Ciompi">ciompi</a></i>, in 1378.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dante_Alighieri_-_Serie_Gioviana.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Dante_Alighieri_-_Serie_Gioviana.jpg/220px-Dante_Alighieri_-_Serie_Gioviana.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="303" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Dante_Alighieri_-_Serie_Gioviana.jpg/330px-Dante_Alighieri_-_Serie_Gioviana.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Dante_Alighieri_-_Serie_Gioviana.jpg/440px-Dante_Alighieri_-_Serie_Gioviana.jpg 2x" data-file-width="743" data-file-height="1024" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dante_Alighieri_-_Serie_Gioviana.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Portrait of <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante Alighieri</a> by <a href="/wiki/Cristofano_dell%27Altissimo" title="Cristofano dell&#39;Altissimo">Cristofano dell'Altissimo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uffizi_Gallery" class="mw-redirect" title="Uffizi Gallery">Uffizi Gallery</a>  Florence</div></div></div> 
<p>It was during this period of instability that authors such as <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante</a> and <a href="/wiki/Petrarch" title="Petrarch">Petrarch</a> lived, and the first stirrings of Renaissance art were to be seen, notably in the realism of <a href="/wiki/Giotto" title="Giotto">Giotto</a>. Paradoxically, some of these disasters would help establish the Renaissance. The <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a> wiped out a third of Europe's population. The resulting labour shortage increased wages and the reduced population was therefore much wealthier, better fed, and, significantly, had more surplus money to spend on luxury goods.  As incidences of the <a href="/wiki/Bubonic_plague" title="Bubonic plague">plague</a> began to decline in the early 15th century, Europe's devastated population once again began to grow.  The new demand for products and services also helped create a growing class of <a href="/wiki/Bankers" class="mw-redirect" title="Bankers">bankers</a>, merchants, and skilled <a href="/wiki/Artisan" title="Artisan">artisans</a>.  The horrors of the Black Death and the seeming inability of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Church</a> to provide relief would contribute to a decline of church influence. Additionally, the collapse of the <a href="/wiki/Compagnia_dei_Bardi" title="Compagnia dei Bardi">Bardi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peruzzi_Bank" class="mw-redirect" title="Peruzzi Bank">Peruzzi</a> banks would open the way for the <a href="/wiki/Medici" class="mw-redirect" title="Medici">Medici</a> to rise to prominence in Florence. <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Sabatino_Lopez" class="mw-redirect" title="Roberto Sabatino Lopez">Roberto Sabatino Lopez</a> argues that the economic collapse was a crucial cause of the Renaissance.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> According to this view, in a more prosperous era, businessmen would have quickly reinvested their earnings in order to make more money in a climate favourable to investment. However, in the leaner years of the 14th century, the wealthy found few promising investment opportunities for their earnings and instead chose to spend more on culture and art.
</p><p>Another popular explanation for the Italian Renaissance is the thesis, first advanced by historian <a href="/wiki/Hans_Baron" title="Hans Baron">Hans Baron</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> that states that the primary impetus of the early Renaissance was the long-running series of <a href="/wiki/Italian_Wars" title="Italian Wars">wars</a> between Florence and Milan. By the late 14th century, Milan had become a centralized monarchy under the control of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Visconti" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Visconti">Visconti</a> family. <a href="/wiki/Giangaleazzo_Visconti" class="mw-redirect" title="Giangaleazzo Visconti">Giangaleazzo Visconti</a>, who ruled the city from 1378 to 1402, was renowned both for his cruelty and for his abilities, and set about building an empire in Northern Italy. He launched a long series of wars, with Milan steadily conquering neighbouring states and defeating the various coalitions led by Florence that sought in vain to halt the advance. This culminated in the 1402 siege of Florence, when it looked as though the city was doomed to fall, before Giangaleazzo suddenly died and his empire collapsed.
</p><p>Baron's thesis suggests that during these long wars, the leading figures of Florence rallied the people by presenting the war as one between the free republic and a despotic monarchy, between the ideals of the Greek and Roman Republics and those of the Roman Empire and Medieval kingdoms. For Baron, the most important figure in crafting this ideology was <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_Bruni" title="Leonardo Bruni">Leonardo Bruni</a>. This time of crisis in Florence was the period when the most influential figures of the early Renaissance were coming of age, such as <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_Ghiberti" title="Lorenzo Ghiberti">Ghiberti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Donatello" title="Donatello">Donatello</a>, <a href="/wiki/Masolino" class="mw-redirect" title="Masolino">Masolino</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Brunelleschi" title="Filippo Brunelleschi">Brunelleschi</a>. Inculcated with this republican ideology they later went on to advocate republican ideas that were to have an enormous impact on the Renaissance.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Italian_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_relationships">International relationships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Italian_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: International relationships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Italian_Wars" title="Italian Wars">Italian Wars</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Piero,_ritratto_di_sigismondo_malatesta.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Piero%2C_ritratto_di_sigismondo_malatesta.jpg/220px-Piero%2C_ritratto_di_sigismondo_malatesta.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="288" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Piero%2C_ritratto_di_sigismondo_malatesta.jpg/330px-Piero%2C_ritratto_di_sigismondo_malatesta.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Piero%2C_ritratto_di_sigismondo_malatesta.jpg/440px-Piero%2C_ritratto_di_sigismondo_malatesta.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2906" data-file-height="3804" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Piero,_ritratto_di_sigismondo_malatesta.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Sigismondo_Pandolfo_Malatesta" title="Sigismondo Pandolfo Malatesta">Pandolfo Malatesta</a> (1417–1468), lord of <a href="/wiki/Rimini" title="Rimini">Rimini</a>, by <a href="/wiki/Piero_della_Francesca" title="Piero della Francesca">Piero della Francesca</a>. Malatesta was a capable <i><a href="/wiki/Condottiere" class="mw-redirect" title="Condottiere">condottiere</a></i>, following the tradition of his family. He was hired by the <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venetians</a> to fight against the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Turks</a> (unsuccessfully) in 1465, and was patron of <a href="/wiki/Leone_Battista_Alberti" class="mw-redirect" title="Leone Battista Alberti">Leone Battista Alberti</a>, whose <i><a href="/wiki/Tempio_Malatestiano" title="Tempio Malatestiano">Tempio Malatestiano</a></i> at Rimini is one of the first entirely classical buildings of the Renaissance.</div></div></div>
<p>Northern Italy and upper Central Italy were divided into a number of warring <a href="/wiki/Italian_city-states" title="Italian city-states">city-states</a>, the most powerful being <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">Pisa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siena" title="Siena">Siena</a>, <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ferrara" title="Ferrara">Ferrara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mantua" title="Mantua">Mantua</a>, <a href="/wiki/Verona" title="Verona">Verona</a> and <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>.  High Medieval Northern Italy was further divided by the long-running battle for supremacy between the forces of the Papacy and of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>: each city aligned itself with one faction or the other, yet was divided internally between the two warring parties, <a href="/wiki/Guelf" class="mw-redirect" title="Guelf">Guelfs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ghibelline" class="mw-redirect" title="Ghibelline">Ghibellines</a>.  Warfare between the states was common, invasion from outside Italy confined to intermittent sorties of <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperors" class="mw-redirect" title="Holy Roman Emperors">Holy Roman Emperors</a>. Renaissance politics developed from this background. Since the 13th century, as armies became primarily composed of <a href="/wiki/Mercenaries" class="mw-redirect" title="Mercenaries">mercenaries</a>, prosperous city-states could field considerable forces, despite their low populations. In the course of the 15th century, the most powerful city-states annexed their smaller neighbors. Florence took <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">Pisa</a> in 1406, Venice captured <a href="/wiki/Padua" title="Padua">Padua</a> and <a href="/wiki/Verona" title="Verona">Verona</a>, while the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Milan" title="Duchy of Milan">Duchy of Milan</a> annexed a number of nearby areas including <a href="/wiki/Pavia" title="Pavia">Pavia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parma" title="Parma">Parma</a>.
</p><p>The first part of the Renaissance saw almost constant warfare on land and sea as the city-states vied for preeminence.  On land, these wars were primarily fought by armies of mercenaries known as <i><a href="/wiki/Condottiere" class="mw-redirect" title="Condottiere">condottieri</a></i>, bands of soldiers drawn from around Europe, but especially Germany and Switzerland, led largely by Italian captains.  The mercenaries were not willing to risk their lives unduly, and war became one largely of sieges and maneuvering, occasioning few pitched battles. It was also in the interest of mercenaries on both sides to prolong any conflict, to continue their employment.  Mercenaries were also a constant threat to their employers; if not paid, they often turned on their patron.  If it became obvious that a state was entirely dependent on mercenaries, the temptation was great for the mercenaries to take over the running of it themselves—this occurred on a number of occasions.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Neutrality was maintained with France, which found itself surrounded by enemies when Spain disputed <a href="/wiki/Charles_VIII_of_France" title="Charles VIII of France">Charles VIII</a>'s claim to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Naples" title="Kingdom of Naples">Kingdom of Naples</a>. Peace with France ended when Charles VIII invaded Italy to take Naples.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At sea, Italian city-states sent many fleets out to do battle. The main contenders were Pisa, Genoa, and Venice, but after a long conflict the Genoese succeeded in reducing Pisa.  Venice proved to be a more powerful adversary, and with the decline of Genoese power during the 15th century Venice became pre-eminent on the seas. In response to threats from the landward side, from the early 15th century Venice developed an increased interest in controlling the <i>terrafirma</i> as the <a href="/wiki/Venetian_Renaissance" title="Venetian Renaissance">Venetian Renaissance</a> opened.
</p><p>On land, decades of fighting saw Florence, Milan, and Venice emerge as the dominant players, and these three powers finally set aside their differences and agreed to the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Lodi" class="mw-redirect" title="Peace of Lodi">Peace of Lodi</a> in 1454, which saw relative calm brought to the region for the first time in centuries.  This peace would hold for the next forty years, and Venice's unquestioned hegemony over the sea also led to unprecedented peace for much of the rest of the 15th century. In the beginning of the 15th century, adventurer and traders such as <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Da_Conti" class="mw-redirect" title="Niccolò Da Conti">Niccolò Da Conti</a> (1395–1469) traveled as far as <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> and back, bringing fresh knowledge on the state of the world, presaging further European voyages of exploration in the years to come.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Florence_under_the_Medici">Florence under the Medici</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Italian_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Florence under the Medici">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/House_of_Medici" title="House of Medici">House of Medici</a></div>
<p>Until the late 14th century, prior to the Medici, Florence's leading family were the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Albizzi" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Albizzi">House of Albizzi</a>. In 1293 the Ordinances of Justice were enacted which effectively became the constitution of the republic of Florence throughout the Italian Renaissance.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The city's numerous luxurious <a href="/wiki/Palace#Italy" title="Palace">palazzi</a> were becoming surrounded by <a href="/wiki/Townhouses" class="mw-redirect" title="Townhouses">townhouses</a>, built by the ever prospering merchant class.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In 1298, one of the leading banking families of Europe, the Bonsignoris, were bankrupted and so the city of Siena lost her status as the banking center of Europe to Florence.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pontormo_-_Ritratto_di_Cosimo_il_Vecchio_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Pontormo_-_Ritratto_di_Cosimo_il_Vecchio_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/220px-Pontormo_-_Ritratto_di_Cosimo_il_Vecchio_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="297" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Pontormo_-_Ritratto_di_Cosimo_il_Vecchio_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/330px-Pontormo_-_Ritratto_di_Cosimo_il_Vecchio_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Pontormo_-_Ritratto_di_Cosimo_il_Vecchio_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/440px-Pontormo_-_Ritratto_di_Cosimo_il_Vecchio_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2437" data-file-height="3285" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pontormo_-_Ritratto_di_Cosimo_il_Vecchio_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Portrait of <a href="/wiki/Cosimo_de%27_Medici" title="Cosimo de&#39; Medici">Cosimo de' Medici</a> by <a href="/wiki/Jacopo_Pontormo" class="mw-redirect" title="Jacopo Pontormo">Jacopo Pontormo</a></div></div></div>
<p>The main challengers of the Albizzi family were the <a href="/wiki/Medici" class="mw-redirect" title="Medici">Medicis</a>, first under <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_di_Bicci_de%27_Medici" title="Giovanni di Bicci de&#39; Medici">Giovanni de' Medici</a>, later under his son <a href="/wiki/Cosimo_de%27_Medici" title="Cosimo de&#39; Medici">Cosimo de' Medici</a>. The Medici controlled the <a href="/wiki/Medici_bank" class="mw-redirect" title="Medici bank">Medici bank</a>—then Europe's largest bank—and an array of other enterprises in Florence and elsewhere. In 1433, the Albizzi managed to have Cosimo exiled.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The next year, however, saw a pro-Medici <a href="/wiki/Signoria_of_Florence" title="Signoria of Florence">Signoria</a> elected and Cosimo returned. The Medici became the town's leading family, a position they would hold for the next three centuries. Florence organized the trade routes for commodities between England and the Netherlands, France, and Italy. By the middle of the century, the city had become the banking capital of Europe, and thereby obtained vast riches.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In 1439, <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Emperor" class="mw-redirect" title="Byzantine Emperor">Byzantine Emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/John_VIII_Palaiologos" title="John VIII Palaiologos">John VIII Palaiologos</a> attended a <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Florence" title="Council of Florence">council in Florence</a> in an attempt to unify the Eastern and Western Churches. This brought books and, especially after the <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Constantinople" title="Fall of Constantinople">fall of the Byzantine Empire</a> in 1453, an influx of scholars to the city.<sup id="cite_ref-princes_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-princes-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greece</a> began to be studied with renewed interest, especially the <a href="/wiki/Platonism_in_the_Renaissance" title="Platonism in the Renaissance">Neoplatonic</a> school of thought,<sup id="cite_ref-princes_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-princes-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> which was the subject of <a href="/wiki/Platonic_Academy_(Florence)" title="Platonic Academy (Florence)">an academy</a> established by the Medici.
</p><p>Florence remained a republic until 1537, traditionally marking the end of the <a href="/wiki/High_Renaissance" title="High Renaissance">High Renaissance</a> in Florence, but the instruments of republican government were firmly under the control of the Medici and their allies, save during the intervals after 1494 and 1527. Cosimo and <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_de%27_Medici" title="Lorenzo de&#39; Medici">Lorenzo de' Medici</a> rarely held official posts, but were the unquestioned leaders. Cosimo was highly popular among the citizenry, mainly for bringing an era of stability and prosperity to the town. One of his most important accomplishments was negotiating the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Lodi" class="mw-redirect" title="Peace of Lodi">Peace of Lodi</a> with <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Sforza" class="mw-redirect" title="Francesco Sforza">Francesco Sforza</a> ending the decades of war with Milan and bringing stability to much of Northern Italy. Cosimo was also an important patron of the arts, directly and indirectly, by the influential example he set.
</p><p>Cosimo was succeeded by his sickly son <a href="/wiki/Piero_di_Cosimo_de%27_Medici" title="Piero di Cosimo de&#39; Medici">Piero de' Medici</a>, who died after five years in charge of the city. In 1469 the reins of power passed to Cosimo's 21-year-old grandson <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_de%27_Medici" title="Lorenzo de&#39; Medici">Lorenzo</a>, who would become known as "Lorenzo the Magnificent." Lorenzo was the first of the family to be educated from an early age in the humanist tradition and is best known as one of the Renaissance's most important patrons of the arts. Under Lorenzo, the Medici rule was formalized with the creation of a new <a href="/w/index.php?title=Council_of_Seventy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Council of Seventy (page does not exist)">Council of Seventy</a>, which Lorenzo headed. The republican institutions continued, but they lost all power. Lorenzo was less successful than his illustrious forebears in business, and the Medici commercial empire was slowly eroded. Lorenzo continued the alliance with Milan, but relations with the papacy soured, and in 1478, Papal agents allied with the <a href="/wiki/Pazzi" title="Pazzi">Pazzi</a> family in an attempt to assassinate Lorenzo. Although the <a href="/wiki/Pazzi_conspiracy" title="Pazzi conspiracy">Pazzi conspiracy</a> failed, Lorenzo's young brother, <a href="/wiki/Giuliano_di_Piero_de%27_Medici" class="mw-redirect" title="Giuliano di Piero de&#39; Medici">Giuliano</a>, was killed, and the failed assassination led to a war with the Papacy and was used as justification to further centralize power in Lorenzo's hands.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spread">Spread</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Italian_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Spread">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Leonardo_da_Vinci_-_presumed_self-portrait_-_WGA12798.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Leonardo_da_Vinci_-_presumed_self-portrait_-_WGA12798.jpg/220px-Leonardo_da_Vinci_-_presumed_self-portrait_-_WGA12798.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="343" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Leonardo_da_Vinci_-_presumed_self-portrait_-_WGA12798.jpg/330px-Leonardo_da_Vinci_-_presumed_self-portrait_-_WGA12798.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Leonardo_da_Vinci_-_presumed_self-portrait_-_WGA12798.jpg/440px-Leonardo_da_Vinci_-_presumed_self-portrait_-_WGA12798.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2408" data-file-height="3754" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Leonardo_da_Vinci_-_presumed_self-portrait_-_WGA12798.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>, Italian <a href="/wiki/Polymath" title="Polymath">Renaissance Man</a></div></div></div>
<p>Renaissance ideals first spread from Florence to the neighbouring states of <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">Tuscany</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Siena" title="Siena">Siena</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lucca" title="Lucca">Lucca</a>. The Tuscan culture soon became the model for all the states of Northern Italy, and the <a href="/wiki/Tuscan_dialect" title="Tuscan dialect">Tuscan dialect</a> came to predominate throughout the region, especially in literature. In 1447 <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Sforza" class="mw-redirect" title="Francesco Sforza">Francesco Sforza</a> came to power in <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a> and rapidly transformed that still medieval city into a major centre of art and learning that drew <a href="/wiki/Leone_Battista_Alberti" class="mw-redirect" title="Leone Battista Alberti">Leone Battista Alberti</a>. <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, one of the wealthiest cities due to its control of the Adriatic Sea, also became a centre for Renaissance culture, especially <a href="/wiki/Venetian_Renaissance_architecture" title="Venetian Renaissance architecture">Venetian Renaissance architecture</a>. Smaller courts brought Renaissance patronage to lesser cities, which developed their characteristic arts: <a href="/wiki/Ferrara" title="Ferrara">Ferrara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mantua" title="Mantua">Mantua</a> under the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Gonzaga" title="House of Gonzaga">Gonzaga</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Urbino" title="Urbino">Urbino</a> under <a href="/wiki/Federico_da_Montefeltro" title="Federico da Montefeltro">Federico da Montefeltro</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a>, the Renaissance was ushered in under the patronage of <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_V_of_Aragon" title="Alfonso V of Aragon">Alfonso I</a>, who conquered Naples in 1443 and encouraged artists like <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Laurana" title="Francesco Laurana">Francesco Laurana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antonello_da_Messina" title="Antonello da Messina">Antonello da Messina</a> and writers like the poet <a href="/wiki/Jacopo_Sannazaro" title="Jacopo Sannazaro">Jacopo Sannazaro</a> and the humanist scholar <a href="/wiki/Angelo_Poliziano" class="mw-redirect" title="Angelo Poliziano">Angelo Poliziano</a>.
</p><p>In 1417 the Papacy returned to Rome, but that once imperial city remained poor and largely in ruins through the first years of the Renaissance.<sup id="cite_ref-rome_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rome-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The great transformation began under <a href="/wiki/Pope_Nicholas_V" title="Pope Nicholas V">Pope Nicholas V</a>, who became pontiff in 1447.  He launched a dramatic rebuilding effort that would eventually see much of the city renewed.  The humanist scholar Aeneas Silvius Piccolomini became <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_II" title="Pope Pius II">Pope Pius II</a> in 1458. As the papacy fell under the control of the wealthy families, such as the <a href="/wiki/Medici" class="mw-redirect" title="Medici">Medici</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Borgia" class="mw-redirect" title="Borgia">Borgias</a> the spirit of Renaissance art and philosophy came to dominate the Vatican.  <a href="/wiki/Pope_Sixtus_IV" title="Pope Sixtus IV">Pope Sixtus IV</a> continued Nicholas' work, most famously ordering the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Sistine_Chapel" title="Sistine Chapel">Sistine Chapel</a>.  The popes also became increasingly secular rulers as the <a href="/wiki/Papal_States" title="Papal States">Papal States</a> were forged into a centralized power by a series of "warrior popes".
</p><p>The nature of the Renaissance also changed in the late 15th century. The Renaissance ideal was fully adopted by the ruling classes and the aristocracy.  In the early Renaissance artists were seen as craftsmen with little prestige or recognition.  By the later Renaissance the top figures wielded great influence and could charge great fees.  A flourishing trade in <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_art" title="Renaissance art">Renaissance art</a> developed.  While in the early Renaissance many of the leading artists were of lower- or middle-class origins, increasingly they became aristocrats.<sup id="cite_ref-rome_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rome-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wider_population">Wider population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Italian_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Wider population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As a cultural movement, the Italian Renaissance affected only a small part of the population. Italy was the most urbanized region of Europe, but three quarters of the people were still rural peasants.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> For this section of the population, life remained essentially unchanged from the Middle Ages.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Classic <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudalism</a> had never been prominent in Northern Italy, and most peasants worked on private farms or as <a href="/wiki/Sharecropper" class="mw-redirect" title="Sharecropper">sharecroppers</a>.  Some scholars see a trend towards <a href="/wiki/Refeudalization" title="Refeudalization">refeudalization</a> in the later Renaissance as the urban elites turned themselves into landed aristocrats.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The situation differed in the  cities. These were dominated by a commercial elite; as exclusive as the aristocracy of any Medieval kingdom.  This group became the main patrons of and audience for Renaissance culture.  Below them there was a large class of artisans and guild members who lived comfortable lives and had significant power in the republican governments.  This was in sharp contrast to the rest of Europe where artisans were firmly in the lower class.  Literate and educated, this group did participate in the Renaissance culture.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The largest section of the urban population was the urban poor of semi-skilled workers and the unemployed.  Like the peasants, the Renaissance had little effect on them. Historians debate how easy it was to move between these groups during the Italian Renaissance. Examples of individuals who rose from humble beginnings can be instanced, but Burke notes two major studies in this area that have found that the data do not clearly demonstrate an increase in <a href="/wiki/Social_mobility" title="Social mobility">social mobility</a>. Most historians feel that early in the Renaissance social mobility was quite high, but that it faded over the course of the 15th century.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Inequality in society was very high.  An upper-class figure would control hundreds of times more income than a servant or labourer.  Some historians see this unequal distribution of wealth as important to the Renaissance, as art patronage relies on the very wealthy.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Renaissance was not a period of great social or economic change, only of cultural and ideological development.  It only touched a small fraction of the population, and in modern times this has led many historians, such as any that follow <a href="/wiki/Historical_materialism" title="Historical materialism">historical materialism</a>, to reduce the importance of the Renaissance in human history. These historians tend to think in terms of "<a href="/wiki/Early_Modern_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Modern Europe">Early Modern Europe</a>" instead. Roger Osborne<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> argues that "The Renaissance is a difficult concept for historians because the history of Europe quite suddenly turns into a history of Italian painting, sculpture and architecture."
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renaissance_end">Renaissance end</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Italian_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Renaissance end">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Mannerism" title="Mannerism">Mannerism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Renaissance" title="Northern Renaissance">Northern Renaissance</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_in_the_Low_Countries" title="Renaissance in the Low Countries">Renaissance in the Low Countries</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Clovio_magi.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Clovio_magi.jpg/220px-Clovio_magi.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="178" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Clovio_magi.jpg/330px-Clovio_magi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Clovio_magi.jpg/440px-Clovio_magi.jpg 2x" data-file-width="580" data-file-height="468" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Clovio_magi.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Giulio_Clovio" title="Giulio Clovio">Giulio Clovio</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Adoration_of_the_Magi" title="Adoration of the Magi">Adoration of the Magi</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Solomon" title="Solomon">Solomon</a> Adored by the <a href="/wiki/Queen_of_Sheba" title="Queen of Sheba">Queen of Sheba</a></i> from the <i>Farnese Hours,</i> 1546</div></div></div>
<p>The end of the Italian Renaissance is as imprecisely marked as its starting point.  For many, the rise to power in Florence of the austere monk <a href="/wiki/Girolamo_Savonarola" title="Girolamo Savonarola">Girolamo Savonarola</a> in 1494–1498 marks the end of the city's flourishing; for others, the triumphant return of the Medici family to power in 1512 marks the beginning of the late phase in the Renaissance arts called <a href="/wiki/Mannerism" title="Mannerism">Mannerism</a>.  Other accounts trace the end of the Italian Renaissance to the French invasions of the early 16th century and the subsequent conflict between France and Spanish rulers for control of Italian territory.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Savonarola rode to power on a widespread backlash over the secularism and indulgence of the Renaissance<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> his brief rule saw many works of art destroyed in the "<a href="/wiki/Bonfire_of_the_Vanities" class="mw-redirect" title="Bonfire of the Vanities">Bonfire of the Vanities</a>" in the centre of Florence.  With the Medici returned to power, now as <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duke_of_Tuscany" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Duke of Tuscany">Grand Dukes of Tuscany</a>, the counter movement in the church continued.  In 1542 the <a href="/wiki/Sacred_Congregation_of_the_Inquisition" class="mw-redirect" title="Sacred Congregation of the Inquisition">Sacred Congregation of the Inquisition</a> was formed and a few years later the <i><a href="/wiki/Index_Librorum_Prohibitorum" title="Index Librorum Prohibitorum">Index Librorum Prohibitorum</a></i> banned a wide array of Renaissance works of literature, which marks the end of the <a href="/wiki/Illuminated_manuscript" title="Illuminated manuscript">illuminated manuscript</a> together with <a href="/wiki/Giulio_Clovio" title="Giulio Clovio">Giulio Clovio</a>, who is considered the greatest illuminator of the Italian High Renaissance, and arguably the last very notable artist in the long tradition of the illuminated manuscript, before some modern revivals.
</p><p>Equally important was the end of stability with a series of foreign invasions of Italy known as the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Wars" title="Italian Wars">Italian Wars</a> that would continue for several decades.  These began with the 1494 invasion by France that wreaked widespread devastation on Northern Italy and ended the independence of many of the city-states.  Most damaging was the 6 May 1527, Spanish and German troops' <a href="/wiki/Sack_of_Rome_(1527)" title="Sack of Rome (1527)">sacking Rome</a> that for two decades all but ended the role of the Papacy as the largest patron of Renaissance art and architecture.<sup id="cite_ref-rome_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rome-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While the Italian Renaissance was fading, the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Renaissance" title="Northern Renaissance">Northern Renaissance</a> adopted many of its ideals and transformed its styles. A number of Italy's greatest artists chose to emigrate. The most notable example was <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>, who left for France in 1516, but teams of lesser artists invited to transform the <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_de_Fontainebleau" class="mw-redirect" title="Château de Fontainebleau">Château de Fontainebleau</a> created the <a href="/wiki/School_of_Fontainebleau" title="School of Fontainebleau">School of Fontainebleau</a> that infused the style of the Italian Renaissance in France. From Fontainebleau, the new styles, transformed by <a href="/wiki/Mannerism" title="Mannerism">Mannerism</a>, brought the Renaissance to the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_in_the_Low_Countries" title="Renaissance in the Low Countries">Low Countries</a> and thence throughout Northern Europe.
</p><p>This spread north was also representative of a larger trend.  No longer was the Mediterranean Europe's most important trade route.  In 1498, <a href="/wiki/Vasco_da_Gama" title="Vasco da Gama">Vasco da Gama</a> reached India, and from that date the primary route of goods from the Orient was through the Atlantic ports of Lisbon, Seville, Nantes, Bristol, and London.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Italian_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature_and_poetry">Literature and poetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Italian_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Literature and poetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance_literature" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian Renaissance literature">Italian Renaissance literature</a></div>
<p>The thirteenth-century Italian literary revolution helped set the stage for the Renaissance. Prior to the Renaissance, the <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian language</a> was not the <a href="/wiki/Literary_language" title="Literary language">literary language</a> in Italy.  It was only in the 13th century that Italian authors began writing in their native language rather than <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Proven%C3%A7al_dialect" title="Provençal dialect">Provençal</a>.  The 1250s saw a major change in Italian poetry as the <i><a href="/wiki/Dolce_Stil_Novo" title="Dolce Stil Novo">Dolce Stil Novo</a></i> (<i>Sweet New Style</i>, which emphasized <a href="/wiki/Platonic_love" title="Platonic love">Platonic</a> rather than <a href="/wiki/Courtly_love" title="Courtly love">courtly love</a>) came into its own, pioneered by poets like <a href="/wiki/Guittone_d%27Arezzo" title="Guittone d&#39;Arezzo">Guittone d'Arezzo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guido_Guinizelli" title="Guido Guinizelli">Guido Guinizelli</a>.  Especially in <a href="/wiki/Poetry" title="Poetry">poetry</a>, major changes in <a href="/wiki/Italian_literature" title="Italian literature">Italian literature</a> had been taking place decades before the Renaissance truly began.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Santi_di_Tito_-_Niccolo_Machiavelli%27s_portrait_headcrop.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Santi_di_Tito_-_Niccolo_Machiavelli%27s_portrait_headcrop.jpg/220px-Santi_di_Tito_-_Niccolo_Machiavelli%27s_portrait_headcrop.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="282" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Santi_di_Tito_-_Niccolo_Machiavelli%27s_portrait_headcrop.jpg/330px-Santi_di_Tito_-_Niccolo_Machiavelli%27s_portrait_headcrop.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/27/Santi_di_Tito_-_Niccolo_Machiavelli%27s_portrait_headcrop.jpg 2x" data-file-width="435" data-file-height="557" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Santi_di_Tito_-_Niccolo_Machiavelli%27s_portrait_headcrop.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Machiavelli" title="Niccolò Machiavelli">Niccolò Machiavelli</a> (1469–1527), the author of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Prince" title="The Prince">The Prince</a></i> and prototypical <a href="/wiki/Polymath" title="Polymath">Renaissance man</a>. Detail from a portrait by <a href="/wiki/Santi_di_Tito" title="Santi di Tito">Santi di Tito</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>With the printing of books initiated in Venice by <a href="/wiki/Aldus_Manutius" title="Aldus Manutius">Aldus Manutius</a>, an increasing number of works began to be published in the Italian language in addition to the flood of Latin and Greek texts that constituted the mainstream of the Italian Renaissance.  The source for these works expanded beyond works of <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theology</a> and towards the pre-Christian eras of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Imperial Rome</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greece</a>.  This is not to say that no religious works were published in this period:  <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante Alighieri</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Divine_Comedy" class="mw-redirect" title="The Divine Comedy">The Divine Comedy</a></i> reflects a distinctly medieval world view.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> remained a major influence for artists and authors, with the <a href="/wiki/Classics" title="Classics">classics</a> coming into their own as a second primary influence.
</p><p>In the early Italian Renaissance, much of the focus was on translating and studying classic works from Latin and Greek. Renaissance authors were not content to rest on the laurels of ancient authors, however.  Many authors attempted to integrate the methods and styles of the ancient Greeks into their own works.  Among the most emulated Romans are <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horace" title="Horace">Horace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sallust" title="Sallust">Sallust</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a>.  Among the Greeks, <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> were now being read in the original for the first time since the 4th century, though Greek compositions were few.
</p><p>The literature and poetry of the Renaissance was largely influenced by the developing science and philosophy.  The humanist <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Petrarch" class="mw-redirect" title="Francesco Petrarch">Francesco Petrarch</a>, a key figure in the renewed sense of scholarship, was also an accomplished poet, publishing several important works of poetry.  He wrote poetry in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, notably the <a href="/wiki/Punic_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Punic War">Punic War</a> epic <i><a href="/wiki/Africa_(epic_poem)" class="mw-redirect" title="Africa (epic poem)">Africa</a></i>, but is today remembered for his works in the Italian <a href="/wiki/Vernacular" title="Vernacular">vernacular</a>, especially the <i><a href="/wiki/Canzoniere" class="mw-redirect" title="Canzoniere">Canzoniere</a></i>, a collection of love <a href="/wiki/Sonnet" title="Sonnet">sonnets</a> dedicated to his unrequited love Laura.  He was the foremost writer of <a href="/wiki/Petrarchan_sonnet" title="Petrarchan sonnet">Petrarchan sonnets</a>, and translations of his work into English by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Wyatt_(poet)" title="Thomas Wyatt (poet)">Thomas Wyatt</a> established the sonnet form in that country, where it was employed by <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a> and countless other poets.
</p><p>Petrarch's disciple, <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Boccaccio" title="Giovanni Boccaccio">Giovanni Boccaccio</a>, became a major author in his own right.  His major work was the <i><a href="/wiki/Decameron" class="mw-redirect" title="Decameron">Decameron</a></i>, a collection of 100 stories told by ten storytellers who have fled to the outskirts of Florence to escape the <a href="/wiki/Black_plague" class="mw-redirect" title="Black plague">black plague</a> over ten nights. The <i>Decameron</i> in particular and Boccaccio's work in general were a major source of inspiration and plots for many English authors in the Renaissance, including <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Chaucer" title="Geoffrey Chaucer">Geoffrey Chaucer</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>.
</p><p>Aside from Christianity, <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">classical antiquity</a>, and scholarship, a fourth influence on Renaissance literature was politics. The political philosopher <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Machiavelli" title="Niccolò Machiavelli">Niccolò Machiavelli</a>'s most famous works are <i><a href="/wiki/Discourses_on_Livy" title="Discourses on Livy">Discourses on Livy</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Florentine_Histories" title="Florentine Histories">Florentine Histories</a></i> and finally <i><a href="/wiki/The_Prince" title="The Prince">The Prince</a></i>, which has become so well known in modern societies that the word <i>Machiavellian</i> has come to refer to the cunning and ruthless actions advocated by the book.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Along with many other Renaissance works, <i>The Prince</i> remains a relevant and influential work of literature today.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy">Philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Italian_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_humanism" title="Renaissance humanism">Renaissance humanism</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Petrarch_by_Bargilla.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Petrarch_by_Bargilla.jpg/170px-Petrarch_by_Bargilla.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="264" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Petrarch_by_Bargilla.jpg/255px-Petrarch_by_Bargilla.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Petrarch_by_Bargilla.jpg/340px-Petrarch_by_Bargilla.jpg 2x" data-file-width="773" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Petrarch_by_Bargilla.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Petrarch" title="Petrarch">Petrarch</a>, from the <i>Cycle of Famous Men and Women.</i> ca. 1450.  Detached fresco.  247&#160;cm ×&#160;153&#160;cm (97.24&#160;in ×&#160;60.24&#160;in). Galleria degli <a href="/wiki/Uffizi" title="Uffizi">Uffizi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>.  Artist: <a href="/wiki/Andrea_di_Bartolo_di_Bargilla" class="mw-redirect" title="Andrea di Bartolo di Bargilla">Andrea di Bartolo di Bargilla</a> (ca. 1423–1457).</div></div></div>
<p>One role of <a href="/wiki/Petrarch" title="Petrarch">Petrarch</a> is as the founder of a new method of scholarship,  <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_humanism" title="Renaissance humanism">Renaissance humanism</a>.
</p><p>Petrarch encouraged the study of the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> classics and carried his copy of Homer about, at a loss to find someone to teach him to read Greek. An essential step in the humanist education being propounded by scholars like <a href="/wiki/Pico_della_Mirandola" class="mw-redirect" title="Pico della Mirandola">Pico della Mirandola</a> was the hunting down of lost or forgotten manuscripts that were known only by reputation.  These endeavors were greatly aided by the wealth of Italian patricians, merchant-princes and despots, who would spend substantial sums building <a href="/wiki/Libraries" class="mw-redirect" title="Libraries">libraries</a>. Discovering the past had become fashionable and it was a passionate affair pervading the upper reaches of society. <i>I go</i>, said <a href="/wiki/Cyriac_of_Ancona" class="mw-redirect" title="Cyriac of Ancona">Cyriac of Ancona</a>, <i>I go to awake the dead</i>. As the Greek works were acquired, manuscripts found, libraries and museums formed, the age of the <a href="/wiki/Printing_press" title="Printing press">printing press</a> was dawning.  The works of Antiquity were translated from Greek and Latin into the contemporary modern languages throughout Europe, finding a receptive middle-class audience, which might be, like Shakespeare, "with little Latin and less Greek".
</p><p>While concern for <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, art, and literature all increased greatly in the Renaissance, the period is usually seen as one of scientific backwardness.  The reverence for classical sources further enshrined the <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotelian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemaic</a> views of the universe. Humanism stressed that nature came to be viewed as an animate spiritual creation that was not governed by laws or mathematics.  At the same time philosophy lost much of its rigour as the rules of <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a> and deduction were seen as secondary to intuition and emotion.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_and_technology">Science and technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Italian_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Science and technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/History_of_science_in_the_Renaissance" title="History of science in the Renaissance">History of science in the Renaissance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_technology" title="Renaissance technology">Renaissance technology</a></div>
<p>According to some recent scholarship, the 'father of modern science' is <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>, whose experiments and clear scientific method earn him this title, Italian universities such as Padua, Bologna and Pisa were scientific centres of renown and with many northern European students, the science of the Renaissance moved to Northern Europe and flourished there, with such figures as <a href="/wiki/Copernicus" class="mw-redirect" title="Copernicus">Copernicus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Francis_Bacon" title="Francis Bacon">Francis Bacon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Descartes" class="mw-redirect" title="Descartes">Descartes</a>. <a href="/wiki/Galileo" class="mw-redirect" title="Galileo">Galileo</a>, a contemporary of Bacon and Descartes, made an immense contribution to scientific thought and experimentation, paving the way for the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific revolution">scientific revolution</a> that later flourished in Northern Europe. Bodies were also stolen from gallows and examined by many like <a href="/wiki/Andreas_Vesalius" title="Andreas Vesalius">Andreas Vesalius</a>, a professor of anatomy. This allowed them to create accurate skeleton models and correct previously believed theories. For example, many thought that the <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> <a href="/wiki/Mandible" title="Mandible">jawbone</a> was made up of two bones, as they had seen this on <a href="/wiki/Animals" class="mw-redirect" title="Animals">animals</a>. However, through examining human corpses they were able to understand that humans actually have only one.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Painting_and_sculpture">Painting and sculpture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Italian_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Painting and sculpture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance_painting" title="Italian Renaissance painting">Italian Renaissance painting</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Last_judgement.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Last_judgement.jpg/170px-Last_judgement.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="291" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Last_judgement.jpg/255px-Last_judgement.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Last_judgement.jpg/340px-Last_judgement.jpg 2x" data-file-width="606" data-file-height="1039" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Last_judgement.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Detail of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Judgment_(Michelangelo)" title="The Last Judgment (Michelangelo)">The Last Judgment</a></i>, 1536–1541, by <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a></div></div></div>
<p>In painting, the Late Medieval painter Giotto di Bondone, or <a href="/wiki/Giotto" title="Giotto">Giotto</a>, helped shape the artistic concepts that later defined much of the Renaissance art. The key ideas that he explored – <a href="/wiki/Classicism" title="Classicism">classicism</a>, the illusion of three-dimensional space and a realistic emotional context – inspired other artists such as <a href="/wiki/Masaccio" title="Masaccio">Masaccio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> He was not the only  Medieval artist to develop these ideas, however; the artists <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Cavallini" title="Pietro Cavallini">Pietro Cavallini</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cimabue" title="Cimabue">Cimabue</a> both influenced Giotto’s use of statuesque figures and expressive storylines.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">frescos</a> of Florentine artist <a href="/wiki/Masaccio" title="Masaccio">Masaccio</a> are generally considered to be among the earliest examples of Italian Renaissance art. Masaccio incorporated the ideas of Giotto, <a href="/wiki/Donatello" title="Donatello">Donatello</a> and <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Brunelleschi" title="Filippo Brunelleschi">Brunelleschi</a> into his paintings, creating mathematically precise scenes that give the impression of three-dimensional space.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Holy_Trinity_(Masaccio)" title="Holy Trinity (Masaccio)">Holy Trinity</a></i> fresco in the Florentine church of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Maria_Novella" title="Santa Maria Novella">Santa Maria Novella</a>, for example, looks as if it is receding at a dramatic angle into the dark background, while single-source lighting and <a href="/wiki/Foreshortening" class="mw-redirect" title="Foreshortening">foreshortening</a> appear to push the figure of Christ into the viewer's space.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>While mathematical precision and classical idealism fascinated painters in Rome and Florence, many Northern artists in the regions of Venice, Milan and Parma preferred highly <a href="/wiki/Illusionism_(art)" title="Illusionism (art)">illusionistic</a> scenes of the natural world.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The period also saw the first secular (non-religious) themes. There has been much debate as to the degree of <a href="/wiki/Secularism" title="Secularism">secularism</a> in the Renaissance, which had been emphasized by early 20th-century writers like Jacob Burckhardt based on, among other things, the presence of a relatively small number of mythological paintings. Those of <a href="/wiki/Sandro_Botticelli" title="Sandro Botticelli">Botticelli</a>, notably <i><a href="/wiki/The_Birth_of_Venus_(Botticelli)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Birth of Venus (Botticelli)">The Birth of Venus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Primavera_(painting)" class="mw-redirect" title="Primavera (painting)">Primavera</a></i>, are now among the best known, although he was deeply religious (becoming a follower of Savonarola) and the great majority of his output was of traditional religious paintings or portraits.<sup id="cite_ref-Frederick_Hartt_2003_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Frederick_Hartt_2003-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Donatello_-_David_-_Floren%C3%A7a.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Donatello_-_David_-_Floren%C3%A7a.jpg/170px-Donatello_-_David_-_Floren%C3%A7a.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="274" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Donatello_-_David_-_Floren%C3%A7a.jpg/255px-Donatello_-_David_-_Floren%C3%A7a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Donatello_-_David_-_Floren%C3%A7a.jpg/340px-Donatello_-_David_-_Floren%C3%A7a.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1066" data-file-height="1719" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Donatello_-_David_-_Floren%C3%A7a.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/David_(Donatello)" title="David (Donatello)">David</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Donatello" title="Donatello">Donatello</a></div></div></div> 
<p>In sculpture, the Florentine artist Donato di Niccolò di Betto Bardi, or <a href="/wiki/Donatello" title="Donatello">Donatello</a>, was among the earliest sculptors to translate classical references into marble and bronze.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> His second sculpture of <i><a href="/wiki/Donatello%27s_David" class="mw-redirect" title="Donatello&#39;s David">David</a></i> was the first free-standing bronze nude created in Europe since the Roman Empire.  
</p><p>The period known as the High Renaissance of painting was the culmination of the varied means of expression<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> and various advances in painting technique, such as <a href="/wiki/Perspective_(graphical)" title="Perspective (graphical)">linear perspective</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> the realistic depiction of both physical<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>  and psychological features,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> and the manipulation of light and darkness, including tone contrast, <i><a href="/wiki/Sfumato" title="Sfumato">sfumato</a></i> (softening the transition between colours) and <i><a href="/wiki/Chiaroscuro" title="Chiaroscuro">chiaroscuro</a></i> (contrast between light and dark),<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> in a single unifying style<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> which expressed total compositional order, balance and harmony.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> In particular, the individual parts of the painting had a complex but balanced and well-knit relationship to the whole.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> The most famous painters from this phase are Leonardo da Vinci, <a href="/wiki/Raphael" title="Raphael">Raphael</a>, and Michelangelo and their images, including Leonardo's the <a href="/wiki/Last_Supper_(da_Vinci)" class="mw-redirect" title="Last Supper (da Vinci)"><i>Last Supper</i></a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Mona_Lisa" title="Mona Lisa">Mona Lisa</a></i>, Raphael's <i><a href="/wiki/The_School_of_Athens" title="The School of Athens">The School of Athens</a></i> and Michelangelo's <i><a href="/wiki/Sistine_Chapel_Ceiling" class="mw-redirect" title="Sistine Chapel Ceiling">Sistine Chapel Ceiling</a></i> are the masterpieces of the period and among the most widely known works of art in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-Frederick_Hartt_2003_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Frederick_Hartt_2003-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>High Renaissance painting evolved into <a href="/wiki/Mannerism" title="Mannerism">Mannerism</a>, especially in Florence.  Mannerist artists, who consciously rebelled against the principles of High Renaissance, tend to represent elongated figures in illogical spaces.   Modern scholarship has recognized the capacity of Mannerist art to convey strong (often religious) emotion where the High Renaissance failed to do so. Some of the main artists of this period are <a href="/wiki/Pontormo" title="Pontormo">Pontormo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bronzino" title="Bronzino">Bronzino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rosso_Fiorentino" title="Rosso Fiorentino">Rosso Fiorentino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parmigianino" title="Parmigianino">Parmigianino</a> and Raphael's pupil <a href="/wiki/Giulio_Romano" title="Giulio Romano">Giulio Romano</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Italian_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance_architecture" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian Renaissance architecture">Italian Renaissance architecture</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PalladioBramanteTempietto1570.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/PalladioBramanteTempietto1570.jpg/220px-PalladioBramanteTempietto1570.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="304" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/PalladioBramanteTempietto1570.jpg/330px-PalladioBramanteTempietto1570.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/PalladioBramanteTempietto1570.jpg/440px-PalladioBramanteTempietto1570.jpg 2x" data-file-width="984" data-file-height="1359" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PalladioBramanteTempietto1570.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Donato_Bramante" title="Donato Bramante">Bramante</a>'s <i>Tempietto</i> in San Pietro in Montorio, Rome, 1502</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>, the Renaissance style was introduced with a revolutionary but incomplete monument by <a href="/wiki/Leone_Battista_Alberti" class="mw-redirect" title="Leone Battista Alberti">Leone Battista Alberti</a>.  Some of the earliest buildings showing Renaissance characteristics are <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Brunelleschi" title="Filippo Brunelleschi">Filippo Brunelleschi</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Basilica_di_San_Lorenzo_di_Firenze" class="mw-redirect" title="Basilica di San Lorenzo di Firenze">church of San Lorenzo</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pazzi_Chapel" title="Pazzi Chapel">Pazzi Chapel</a>. The interior of <i>Santo Spirito</i> expresses a new sense of light, clarity and spaciousness, which is typical of the early Italian Renaissance.  Its architecture reflects the philosophy of <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_humanism" title="Renaissance humanism">Renaissance humanism</a>, the enlightenment and clarity of mind as opposed to the darkness and spirituality of the Middle Ages.  The revival of classical antiquity can best be illustrated by the <a href="/wiki/Palazzo_Rucellai" title="Palazzo Rucellai">Palazzo Rucellai</a>.  Here the <a href="/wiki/Pilaster" title="Pilaster">pilasters</a> follow the superposition of <a href="/wiki/Classical_orders" class="mw-redirect" title="Classical orders">classical orders</a>, with  <a href="/wiki/Doric_order" title="Doric order">Doric</a> <a href="/wiki/Capital_(architecture)" title="Capital (architecture)">capitals</a> on the ground floor, <a href="/wiki/Ionic_order" title="Ionic order">Ionic</a> capitals on the <i><a href="/wiki/Piano_nobile" title="Piano nobile">piano nobile</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Corinthian_order" title="Corinthian order">Corinthian</a> capitals on the uppermost floor. Soon, Renaissance architects favored grand, large <a href="/wiki/Dome" title="Dome">domes</a> over tall and imposing spires, doing away with the Gothic style of the predating ages.
</p><p>In Mantua, Alberti ushered in the new antique style, though his culminating work, <a href="/wiki/Basilica_di_Sant%27Andrea_di_Mantova" class="mw-redirect" title="Basilica di Sant&#39;Andrea di Mantova">Sant'Andrea</a>, was not begun until 1472, after the architect's death.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/High_Renaissance" title="High Renaissance">High Renaissance</a>, as we call the style today, was introduced to <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> with <a href="/wiki/Donato_Bramante" title="Donato Bramante">Donato Bramante</a>'s <a href="/wiki/San_Pietro_in_Montorio" title="San Pietro in Montorio">Tempietto at San Pietro in Montorio</a> (1502) and his original centrally planned <a href="/wiki/St._Peter%27s_Basilica" title="St. Peter&#39;s Basilica">St. Peter's Basilica</a> (1506), which was the most notable architectural commission of the era, influenced by almost all notable Renaissance artists, including <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Giacomo_della_Porta" title="Giacomo della Porta">Giacomo della Porta</a>.  The beginning of the late Renaissance in 1550 was marked by the development of a new column order by <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Palladio" title="Andrea Palladio">Andrea Palladio</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Giant_order" title="Giant order">Giant order</a> columns that were two or more stories tall decorated the <a href="/wiki/Facade" title="Facade">facades</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Italian_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_music" title="Renaissance music">Renaissance music</a></div>
<p>In Italy during the 14th century there was an explosion of musical activity that corresponded in scope and level of innovation to the activity in the other arts.  Although <a href="/wiki/Musicologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Musicologist">musicologists</a> typically group the music of the <a href="/wiki/Trecento" title="Trecento">Trecento</a> (music of the 14th century) with the late <a href="/wiki/Medieval_music" title="Medieval music">medieval</a> period, it included features which align with the early Renaissance in important ways:  an increasing emphasis on secular sources, styles and forms; a spreading of culture away from ecclesiastical institutions to the nobility, and even to the common people; and a quick development of entirely new techniques.  The principal forms were the <a href="/wiki/Madrigal_(Trecento)" title="Madrigal (Trecento)">Trecento madrigal</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Caccia_(music)" class="mw-redirect" title="Caccia (music)">caccia</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Ballata" title="Ballata">ballata</a>. Overall, the musical style of the period is sometimes labelled as the "Italian <a href="/wiki/Ars_nova" title="Ars nova">ars nova</a>." From the early 15th century to the middle of the 16th century, the center of innovation in <a href="/wiki/Religious_music" title="Religious music">religious music</a> was in the <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">Low Countries</a>, and a flood of talented composers came to Italy from this region. Many of them sang in either the papal choir in Rome or the choirs at the numerous chapels of the aristocracy, in Rome, Venice, Florence, Milan, Ferrara and elsewhere; and they brought their <a href="/wiki/Polyphony" title="Polyphony">polyphonic</a> style with them, influencing many native Italian composers during their stay.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bernardo_Strozzi_-_Claudio_Monteverdi_(c.1630).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Bernardo_Strozzi_-_Claudio_Monteverdi_%28c.1630%29.jpg/220px-Bernardo_Strozzi_-_Claudio_Monteverdi_%28c.1630%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="275" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Bernardo_Strozzi_-_Claudio_Monteverdi_%28c.1630%29.jpg/330px-Bernardo_Strozzi_-_Claudio_Monteverdi_%28c.1630%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Bernardo_Strozzi_-_Claudio_Monteverdi_%28c.1630%29.jpg/440px-Bernardo_Strozzi_-_Claudio_Monteverdi_%28c.1630%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1648" data-file-height="2060" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bernardo_Strozzi_-_Claudio_Monteverdi_(c.1630).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Claudio_Monteverdi" title="Claudio Monteverdi">Claudio Monteverdi</a> by <a href="/wiki/Bernardo_Strozzi" title="Bernardo Strozzi">Bernardo Strozzi</a> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;1630</span>)</div></div></div>
<p>The predominant forms of <a href="/wiki/Sacred_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Sacred music">sacred music</a> during the period were the <a href="/wiki/Mass_(music)" title="Mass (music)">mass</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Motet" title="Motet">motet</a>.  By far the most famous composer of church music in 16th-century Italy was <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Pierluigi_da_Palestrina" title="Giovanni Pierluigi da Palestrina">Palestrina</a>, the most prominent member of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_School" title="Roman School">Roman School</a>, whose style of smooth, emotionally cool polyphony was to become the defining sound of the late 16th century, at least for generations of 19th- and 20th-century musicologists.  Other Italian composers of the late 16th century focused on composing the main <a href="/wiki/Secular_music" title="Secular music">secular form</a> of the era, the <a href="/wiki/Madrigal_(music)" class="mw-redirect" title="Madrigal (music)">madrigal</a>; for almost a hundred years these secular songs for multiple singers were distributed all over Europe.  Composers of madrigals included <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Arcadelt" title="Jacques Arcadelt">Jacques Arcadelt</a>, at the beginning of the age, <a href="/wiki/Cipriano_de_Rore" title="Cipriano de Rore">Cipriano de Rore</a>, in the middle of the century, and <a href="/wiki/Luca_Marenzio" title="Luca Marenzio">Luca Marenzio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philippe_de_Monte" title="Philippe de Monte">Philippe de Monte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Gesualdo" title="Carlo Gesualdo">Carlo Gesualdo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Claudio_Monteverdi" title="Claudio Monteverdi">Claudio Monteverdi</a> at the end of the era. Italy was also a centre of innovation in <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Instrumental music">instrumental music</a>.  By the early 16th century keyboard improvisation came to be greatly valued, and numerous composers of virtuoso keyboard music appeared.  Many familiar instruments were invented and perfected in late Renaissance Italy, such as the <a href="/wiki/Violin" title="Violin">violin</a>, the earliest forms of which came into use in the 1550s.
</p><p>By the late 16th century Italy was the musical centre of Europe. Almost all of the innovations which were to define the transition to the <a href="/wiki/Baroque_music" title="Baroque music">Baroque</a> period originated in northern Italy in the last few decades of the century.  In Venice, the <a href="/wiki/Venetian_polychoral_style" title="Venetian polychoral style">polychoral</a> productions of the <a href="/wiki/Venetian_School_(music)" title="Venetian School (music)">Venetian School</a>, and associated instrumental music, moved north into Germany; in Florence, the <a href="/wiki/Florentine_Camerata" title="Florentine Camerata">Florentine Camerata</a> developed <a href="/wiki/Monody" title="Monody">monody</a>, the important precursor to <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">opera</a>, which itself first appeared around 1600; and the avant-garde, <a href="/wiki/Mannerism" title="Mannerism">manneristic</a> style of the <a href="/wiki/Ferrara_school" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferrara school">Ferrara school</a>, which migrated to <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a> and elsewhere through the music of <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Gesualdo" title="Carlo Gesualdo">Carlo Gesualdo</a>, was to be the final statement of the polyphonic vocal music of the Renaissance.
</p>
