<p>A <b>family tree</b>, or pedigree chart, is a chart representing family relationships in a conventional <a href="/wiki/Tree_structure" title="Tree structure">tree structure</a>. The more detailed family trees used in <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_work" title="Social work">social work</a> are known as <a href="/wiki/Genograms" class="mw-redirect" title="Genograms">genograms</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_history_representations">Family history representations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Family_tree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Family history representations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Genealogical data can be represented in several formats, for example as a <a href="/wiki/Pedigree_chart" title="Pedigree chart">pedigree</a> or <b><span id="ancestry_chart">ancestry chart</span></b>. Family trees are often presented with the oldest generations at the top and the newer generations at the bottom. An ancestry chart, which is a tree showing the ancestors of an individual, will more closely resemble a tree in shape, being wider at the top than the bottom. In some ancestry charts, an individual appears on the left and his or her ancestors appear to the right. A descendancy chart, which depicts all the descendants of an individual will be narrowest at the top.
</p><p>Family trees can have many themes. One might encompass all direct descendants of a single figure, or all known ancestors of a living person. Another might include all members of a particular surname (e.g. male-line descendants). Yet another approach is to construct a tree including all holders of a certain office, such as <a href="/wiki/Kings_of_Germany_family_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Kings of Germany family tree">kings of Germany</a>. This relies on <a href="/wiki/Royal_intermarriage" title="Royal intermarriage">dynastic marriage</a> to hold together the links between dynasties.
</p><p>The image of the tree probably originated with one in medieval art of the <a href="/wiki/Tree_of_Jesse" title="Tree of Jesse">Tree of Jesse</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> used to illustrate the <a href="/wiki/Genealogy_of_Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Genealogy of Christ">Genealogy of Christ</a> in terms of a prophecy of <a href="/wiki/Isaiah" title="Isaiah">Isaiah</a> (Isaiah 11:1). Possibly the first non-Biblical use, and the first to show full family relationships rather than a purely patrilineal scheme, was that involving family trees of the classical gods in <a href="/wiki/Boccaccio" class="mw-redirect" title="Boccaccio">Boccaccio</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Genealogia_deorum_gentilium" class="mw-redirect" title="Genealogia deorum gentilium">Genealogia deorum gentilium</a></i> ("On the Genealogy of the Gods of the Gentiles"), whose first version dates to 1360.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ahnentafel">Ahnentafel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Family_tree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ahnentafel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Waldburg_Ahnentafel.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Waldburg_Ahnentafel.jpg/220px-Waldburg_Ahnentafel.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="169" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Waldburg_Ahnentafel.jpg/330px-Waldburg_Ahnentafel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Waldburg_Ahnentafel.jpg/440px-Waldburg_Ahnentafel.jpg 2x" data-file-width="985" data-file-height="756" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Waldburg_Ahnentafel.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An <a href="/wiki/Ahnentafel" title="Ahnentafel">ahnentafel</a> family tree displaying an ancestor chart of <a href="/wiki/House_of_Waldburg" title="House of Waldburg">Sigmund Christoph, Graf von Zeil und Trauchburg</a></div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ahnentafel" title="Ahnentafel">Ahnentafel</a></div>
<p>An <i><a href="/wiki/Ahnentafel" title="Ahnentafel">ahnentafel</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> for "ancestor table") is a <a href="/wiki/Genealogical_numbering_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Genealogical numbering system">genealogical numbering system</a> for listing a person's direct ancestors in a fixed sequence of ascent: 
</p>
<ol><li>Subject (or <a href="/wiki/Proband" title="Proband">proband</a>)</li>
<li>Father</li>
<li>Mother</li>
<li>Paternal grandfather</li>
<li>Paternal grandmother</li>
<li>Maternal grandfather</li>
<li>Maternal grandmother</li></ol>
<p>and so on, back through the generations. Apart from No.&#8239;1, who can be male or female, all even-numbered persons are male, and all odd-numbered persons are female. In this <a href="/wiki/Conceptual_model" title="Conceptual model">scheme*</a>, the number of any person's father is double the person's number, and a person's mother is double the person's number plus one. This system can be displayed as a tree:

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fan_chart">Fan chart</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Family_tree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fan chart">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Fan-chart-example-gramps5.0.1win10.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Fan-chart-example-gramps5.0.1win10.png/220px-Fan-chart-example-gramps5.0.1win10.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Fan-chart-example-gramps5.0.1win10.png/330px-Fan-chart-example-gramps5.0.1win10.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Fan-chart-example-gramps5.0.1win10.png/440px-Fan-chart-example-gramps5.0.1win10.png 2x" data-file-width="1159" data-file-height="791" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Fan-chart-example-gramps5.0.1win10.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Screenshot of <a href="/wiki/Gramps" title="Gramps">Gramps</a> (v. 5.0.1) displaying a fan chart and the Given name cloud gramplet on the bottom.</div></div></div>
<p>A "fan chart" features a half circle chart with concentric rings: the person of interest is the inner circle, the second circle is divided in two (each side is one parent), the third circle is divided in four, and so forth. Fan charts depict paternal and maternal ancestors.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Graph_theory">Graph theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Family_tree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Graph theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While family trees are depicted as trees, family relations do not in general form a <a href="/wiki/Tree_(graph_theory)" title="Tree (graph theory)">tree</a> in the sense of <a href="/wiki/Graph_theory" title="Graph theory">graph theory</a>, since distant relatives can mate, so a person can have a common ancestor on their mother's and father's side. However, because a parent must be born before their child is born, a person cannot be their own ancestor, and thus there are no loops, so ancestry forms a <a href="/wiki/Directed_acyclic_graph" title="Directed acyclic graph">directed acyclic graph</a>.
</p><p>The graphs of <a href="/wiki/Matrilineal" class="mw-redirect" title="Matrilineal">matrilineal</a> descent ("mother" relationships between women) and <a href="/wiki/Patrilineal" class="mw-redirect" title="Patrilineal">patrilineal</a> descent ("father" relationships between men) are trees however.
</p><p>Assuming no common ancestor, an ancestry chart is a <a href="/wiki/Perfect_binary_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Perfect binary tree">perfect binary tree</a>, as each person has exactly one mother and one father, for two parents; these thus have a regular structure. A descendancy chart, on the other hand, does not in general have a regular structure, as a person can have any number of children, or none at all.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_examples">Notable examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Family_tree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Notable examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The longest family tree in the world <a href="/wiki/Family_tree_of_Confucius_in_the_main_line_of_descent" title="Family tree of Confucius in the main line of descent">is that of</a> the Chinese philosopher and educator <a href="/wiki/Confucius" title="Confucius">Confucius</a> (551–479 BC), and he is the descendant of <a href="/wiki/Tang_of_Shang" title="Tang of Shang">King Tang</a> (1675–1646 BC). The tree spans more than 80 generations from him, and includes more than 2 million members. An international effort involving more than 450 branches around the world was started in 1998 to retrace and revise this family tree. A new edition of the Confucius genealogy was printed in September 2009 by the <a href="/wiki/Confucius_Genealogy_Compilation_Committee" title="Confucius Genealogy Compilation Committee">Confucius Genealogy Compilation Committee</a>, to coincide with the 2560th anniversary of the birth of the Chinese thinker. This latest edition is expected to include some 1.3 million living members who are scattered around the world today.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are extensive genealogies for the ruling dynasties of China, but these do not form a single, unified family tree; and it is unclear at which point(s) the most ancient historical figures named become mythological.
</p><p>In Japan, the ancestry of the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_family_of_Japan" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial family of Japan">Imperial Family</a> is traced back to the mythological origins of Japan. The connection to persons from the established historical record begins in the mid-first millennium AD.
</p><p>Another very old and extensive tree is that of the <a href="/wiki/Lurie" title="Lurie">Lurie</a> lineage—which includes <a href="/wiki/Sigmund_Freud" title="Sigmund Freud">Sigmund Freud</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martin_Buber" title="Martin Buber">Martin Buber</a>—and traces back to Jehiel Lurie, a 13th-century rabbi in Brest-Litovsk, and from there to <a href="/wiki/Rashi" title="Rashi">Rashi</a> and purportedly back to the legendary <a href="/wiki/King_David" class="mw-redirect" title="King David">King David</a>, as documented by Neil Rosenstein in his book <i>The Lurie Legacy</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The 1999 edition of the <i><a href="/wiki/Guinness_Book_of_Records" class="mw-redirect" title="Guinness Book of Records">Guinness Book of Records</a></i> recorded the Lurie family in the "longest lineage" category as oldest-known living family in the world today.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Biblical genealogies of Jesus also claim descent from the House of David; covering a period of approximately 1000 years. 
</p><p>In the Torah and Old Testament, genealogies are provided for many biblical persons, including a record of the descendants of Adam. 
</p><p>According to the Torah, the <a href="/wiki/Kohanim" class="mw-redirect" title="Kohanim">Kohanim</a> are descended from <a href="/wiki/Aaron" title="Aaron">Aaron</a>. Genetic testing performed at the <a href="/wiki/Technion" class="mw-redirect" title="Technion">Technion</a> has shown that most modern Kohanim share common Y-chromosome origins, although there is no complete family tree of the Kohanim.
</p><p>In the Islamic world, claimed descent from the prophet <a href="/wiki/Mohammed" class="mw-redirect" title="Mohammed">Mohammed</a> greatly enhanced the status of political and religious leaders. New dynasties often used claims of such descent to help establish their legitimacy. 
</p><p>In Europe, the pedigree of <a href="/wiki/Niall_No%C3%ADg%C3%ADallach" class="mw-redirect" title="Niall Noígíallach">Niall Noígíallach</a> would be a contender for the longest, through <a href="/wiki/Conn_of_the_Hundred_Battles" title="Conn of the Hundred Battles">Conn of the Hundred Battles</a> (<a href="/wiki/Floruit" title="Floruit">fl.</a> 123 AD)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>; in the legendary history of Ireland he is further descended from <a href="/wiki/Breog%C3%A1n" title="Breogán">Breogán</a>, and ultimately from Adam, through the sons of Noah. Many noble and aristocratic families of European and West-Asian origin can reliably trace their ancestry back as far as the mid to late first millennium AD; some claiming undocumented descent from Classical Antiquity or mythological ancestors.
</p><p>Before the <a href="/wiki/Dark_Ages_(historiography)" title="Dark Ages (historiography)">Dark Ages</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Greco-Roman" class="mw-redirect" title="Greco-Roman">Greco-Roman</a> world, some reliable pedigrees dated back perhaps at least as far as the first half of the first millennium BC; with claimed or mythological origins reaching back further. <a href="/wiki/Ancient_rome" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient rome">Roman</a> clan and family lineages played an important part in the structure of their society, and were the basis of their intricate system of personal names. However, there was a break in the continuity of records-keeping at the end of <a href="/wiki/Classical_Antiquity" class="mw-redirect" title="Classical Antiquity">Classical Antiquity</a>. Records of the lines of succession of the <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Popes</a> and the Eastern Roman Emperors through this transitional period have survived, but these are not continuous genealogical histories of single families. See: <a href="/wiki/Descent_from_antiquity" title="Descent from antiquity">descent from antiquity</a>.
</p><p>In Africa, the <a href="/wiki/Solomonic_dynasty" title="Solomonic dynasty">ruling dynasty</a> of Ethiopia claimed descent from <a href="/wiki/King_Solomon" class="mw-redirect" title="King Solomon">King Solomon</a> via the <a href="/wiki/Queen_of_Sheba" title="Queen of Sheba">Queen of Sheba</a>. Through this claim, the family traced their descent back to the aforementioned House of David.
</p><p>The genealogy of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egyptian</a> ruling dynasties was recorded from the beginnings of the Pharaonic era circa 3000 BC, to the end of the <a href="/wiki/Ptolomaic_dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Ptolomaic dynasty">Ptolomaic Kingdom</a>; although this is not a record of one continuously-linked family lineage, and surviving records are incomplete.
</p><p>Elsewhere in Africa, <a href="/wiki/Oral_traditions" class="mw-redirect" title="Oral traditions">oral traditions</a> of genealogical recording predominate. Members of the <a href="/wiki/Keita_dynasty" title="Keita dynasty">Keita dynasty</a> of Mali, for example, have had their pedigrees sung by <a href="/wiki/Griot" title="Griot">griots</a> during annual ceremonies since the 14th century. Meanwhile, in Nigeria, many ruling clans - most notably those descended from <a href="/wiki/Oduduwa" title="Oduduwa">Oduduwa</a> - claim descent from the legendary <a href="/wiki/Kisra_legend" title="Kisra legend">King Kisra</a>. Here too, pedigrees are recited by griots attached to the royal courts.
</p><p>In some pre-contact <a href="/wiki/Native_American_civilizations" class="mw-redirect" title="Native American civilizations">Native American civilizations</a>, genealogical records of ruling and priestly families were kept, some of which extended over at least several centuries. 
</p><p>In many human cultures, <a href="/wiki/Clan" title="Clan">clan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tribal" class="mw-redirect" title="Tribal">tribal</a> associations are based on claims of common ancestry; although detailed documentation of those origins is often very limited.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Family_tree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The author <a href="/wiki/Pete_Frame" title="Pete Frame">Pete Frame</a> is notable for having produced "family trees" of <a href="/wiki/Rock_band" class="mw-redirect" title="Rock band">rock bands</a>. In this instance, the entries represent membership of certain groups, and personnel changes within them, rather than family relationships. Several books have been produced with his family trees,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> which in turn have led to a <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> television series about them, including interviews from the bands depicted in the trees.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
