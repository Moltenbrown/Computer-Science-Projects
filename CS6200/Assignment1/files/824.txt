<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tigris" title="Tigris">Tigris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Euphrates" title="Euphrates">Euphrates</a>, with their <a href="/wiki/Tributary" title="Tributary">tributaries</a>, form a major river system in <a href="/wiki/Western_Asia" title="Western Asia">Western Asia</a>. From sources originating in eastern <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, they flow by/through <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> through <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EB1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  The system is part of the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> Tigris–Euphrates <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion" title="Ecoregion">ecoregion</a>, which includes Iraq and parts of Turkey, Syria, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwait</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a>.
</p><p>From their sources and upper courses in the mountains of <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Anatolia_Region" title="Eastern Anatolia Region">eastern Anatolia</a>, the rivers descend through valleys and gorges to the uplands of Syria and northern Iraq and then to the <a href="/wiki/Alluvial_plain" title="Alluvial plain">alluvial plain</a> of central Iraq. The rivers flow in a south-easterly direction through the central plain and combine at <a href="/wiki/Al-Qurnah" title="Al-Qurnah">Al-Qurnah</a> to form the <a href="/wiki/Shatt_al-Arab" title="Shatt al-Arab">Shatt al-Arab</a> and discharge into the Persian Gulf.<sup id="cite_ref-EB_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The region has historical importance as part of the <a href="/wiki/Fertile_Crescent" title="Fertile Crescent">Fertile Crescent region</a>, in which civilization is believed to have first emerged.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tigris%E2%80%93Euphrates_river_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ecoregion is characterized by two large rivers, the <a href="/wiki/Tigris" title="Tigris">Tigris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Euphrates" title="Euphrates">Euphrates</a>. The rivers have several small tributaries which feed into the system from shallow freshwater lakes, <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamps</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a>, all surrounded by desert.  The <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrology</a> of these vast marshes is extremely important to the ecology of the entire upper <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a>. Historically, the area is known as <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>. As part of the larger <a href="/wiki/Fertile_Crescent" title="Fertile Crescent">Fertile Crescent</a>, it saw the earliest emergence of literate urban <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilization</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Uruk_period" title="Uruk period">Uruk period</a>, for which reason it is often described as a "<a href="/wiki/Cradle_of_Civilization" class="mw-redirect" title="Cradle of Civilization">Cradle of Civilization</a>".
</p><p>In the 1980s, this ecoregion was put in grave danger as the <a href="/wiki/Iran%E2%80%93Iraq_War" title="Iran–Iraq War">Iran–Iraq War</a> raged within its boundaries.  The wetlands of Iraq, which were inhabited by the <a href="/wiki/Marsh_Arabs" title="Marsh Arabs">Marsh Arabs</a>, were almost completely dried out, and have only recently<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (December 2012)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> shown signs of recovery.
</p><p>The Tigris–Euphrates Basin is shared by <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>, Iraq, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwait</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-eu_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eu-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Many Tigris tributaries originate in Iran and a Tigris–Euphrates <a href="/wiki/Confluence" title="Confluence">confluence</a> forms part of the Kuwait–Iraq border.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Since the 1960s and in the 1970s, when Turkey began the <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_Anatolia_Project" title="Southeastern Anatolia Project">GAP project</a> in earnest, water disputes have regularly occurred in addition to the associated dam's effects on the environment.  In addition, Syrian and Iranian dam construction has also contributed to political tension within the basin, particularly during drought.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General_description">General description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tigris%E2%80%93Euphrates_river_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: General description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Marsh_Arabs_in_a_mashoof.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Marsh_Arabs_in_a_mashoof.jpg/300px-Marsh_Arabs_in_a_mashoof.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="219" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Marsh_Arabs_in_a_mashoof.jpg/450px-Marsh_Arabs_in_a_mashoof.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Marsh_Arabs_in_a_mashoof.jpg/600px-Marsh_Arabs_in_a_mashoof.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1472" data-file-height="1077" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Marsh_Arabs_in_a_mashoof.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Marsh_Arabs" title="Marsh Arabs">Marsh Arabs</a> poling a <i><a href="/wiki/Mashoof" title="Mashoof">mashoof</a></i> in the marshes of southern Iraq</div></div></div><p>The general climate of the region is subtropical, hot and arid.  At the northern end of the Persian Gulf is the vast <a href="/wiki/Floodplain" title="Floodplain">floodplain</a> of the Euphrates, Tigris, and <a href="/wiki/Karun" title="Karun">Karun</a> Rivers, featuring huge permanent lakes, marshes, and forest. The aquatic vegetation includes <a href="/wiki/Reed_(plant)" title="Reed (plant)">reeds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juncaceae" title="Juncaceae">rushes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">papyrus</a>, which support numerous species. Areas around the Tigris and the Euphrates are very fertile. Marshy land is home to water birds, some stopping here while migrating, and some spending the winter in these marshes living off the lizards, snakes, frogs, and fish. Other animals found in these marshes are <a href="/wiki/Domestic_buffalo" class="mw-redirect" title="Domestic buffalo">water buffalo</a>, two <a href="/wiki/Endemic_(ecology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Endemic (ecology)">endemic</a> <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodent</a> species, <a href="/wiki/Antelope" title="Antelope">antelopes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gazelle" title="Gazelle">gazelles</a> and small animals such as the <a href="/wiki/Jerboa" title="Jerboa">jerboa</a> and several other mammals.
</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_threats">Ecological threats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tigris%E2%80%93Euphrates_river_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecological threats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

Reds represent drier conditions, while blues represent wetter conditions. The effects of the seasons are evident, as is the major drought that hit the region in 2007. The majority of the water lost was due to reductions in groundwater caused by human activities.</div></div></div><p>Iraq suffers from <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soil_salination" class="mw-redirect" title="Soil salination">soil salination</a> due in large part to thousands of years of agricultural activity. Water and plant life are sparse. <a href="/wiki/Saddam_Hussein" title="Saddam Hussein">Saddam Hussein</a>'s government water-control projects <a href="/wiki/Draining_of_the_Qurna_Marshes" class="mw-redirect" title="Draining of the Qurna Marshes">drained the inhabited marsh areas</a> east of An Nasiriyah by drying up or diverting streams and rivers. <a href="/wiki/Shi%27a_Muslim" class="mw-redirect" title="Shi&#39;a Muslim">Shi'a Muslims</a> were displaced under the <a href="/wiki/Ba%27athist" class="mw-redirect" title="Ba&#39;athist">Ba'athist</a> regime. The destruction of the natural habitat poses serious threats to the area's <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">wildlife</a> populations. There are also inadequate supplies of potable water.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Marshland" class="mw-redirect" title="Marshland">marshlands</a> were an extensive natural wetlands <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> which developed over thousands of years in the Tigris–Euphrates basin and once covered 15–20,000 square kilometers. According to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Environmental_Program" class="mw-redirect" title="United Nations Environmental Program">United Nations Environmental Program</a> and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=AMAR_Charitable_Foundation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="AMAR Charitable Foundation (page does not exist)">AMAR Charitable Foundation</a>, between 84% and 90% of the marshes have been destroyed since the 1970s. In 1994, 60 percent of the wetlands were destroyed by Hussein's regime – drained to permit military access and greater political control of the native <a href="/wiki/Marsh_Arabs" title="Marsh Arabs">Marsh Arabs</a>. Canals, dykes and dams were built routing the water of the Tigris and Euphrates Rivers around the marshes, instead of allowing water to move slowly through the marshland. After part of the Euphrates was dried up due to re-routing its water to the sea, a dam was built so water could not back up from the Tigris and sustain the former marshland. Some marshlands were burned and pipes buried underground helped to carry away water for quicker drying.
</p><p>The drying of the marshes led to the disappearance of the <a href="/wiki/Halophyte" title="Halophyte">salt-tolerant vegetation</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a> rich waters that fertilized surrounding soils; 52 native fish species; the <a href="/wiki/Wild_boar" title="Wild boar">wild boar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Red_fox" title="Red fox">red fox</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bubalus_bubalis" class="mw-redirect" title="Bubalus bubalis">buffalo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_bird" title="Water bird">water birds</a> of the marsh habitat.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_dispute">Water dispute</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tigris%E2%80%93Euphrates_river_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Water dispute">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The issue of water rights became a point of contention for Iraq, Turkey and Syria beginning in the 1960s when Turkey implemented a public-works project (the <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_Anatolia_Project" title="Southeastern Anatolia Project">GAP project</a>) aimed at harvesting the water from the Tigris and Euphrates rivers through the construction of  22 dams, for <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectric energy</a> purposes. Although the <a href="/wiki/Water_dispute" class="mw-redirect" title="Water dispute">water dispute</a> between Turkey and Syria was more problematic, the GAP project was also perceived as a threat by Iraq. The tension between Turkey and Iraq about the issue was increased by the effect of Syria and Turkey's participation in the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_sanctions" class="mw-redirect" title="Iraq sanctions">UN embargo against Iraq</a> following the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Gulf War</a>. However, the issue had never become as significant as the water dispute between Turkey and Syria.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 2008 drought in Iraq sparked new negotiations between Iraq and Turkey over <a href="/wiki/Trans-boundary_river" class="mw-redirect" title="Trans-boundary river">trans-boundary river</a> flows.  Although the drought affected Turkey, Syria and Iran as well, Iraq complained regularly about reduced water flows.  Iraq particularly complained about the Euphrates River because of the large amount of dams on the river.  Turkey agreed to increase the flow several times, beyond its means in order to supply Iraq with extra water.  Iraq has seen significant declines in water storage and crop yields because of the drought.  To make matters worse, Iraq's water infrastructure has suffered from years of conflict and neglect.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2008, Turkey, Iraq and Syria agreed to restart the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Joint_Trilateral_Committee&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Joint Trilateral Committee (page does not exist)">Joint Trilateral Committee</a> on water for the three nations for better water resources management. Turkey, Iraq and Syria signed a memorandum of understanding on September 3, 2009, in order to strengthen communication within the Tigris–Euphrates Basin and to develop joint water-flow-monitoring stations.  On September 19, 2009, Turkey formally agreed to increase the flow of the Euphrates River to 450 to 500 <a href="/wiki/Cubic_metres_per_second" class="mw-redirect" title="Cubic metres per second">m³/s</a>, but only until October 20, 2009.  In exchange, Iraq agreed to trade petroleum with Turkey and help curb <a href="/wiki/Kurd" class="mw-redirect" title="Kurd">Kurdish</a> militant activity in their border region.  One of Turkey's last large GAP dams on the Tigris – the <a href="/wiki/Ilisu_Dam" class="mw-redirect" title="Ilisu Dam">Ilisu Dam</a> – is strongly opposed by Iraq and is the source of political strife.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tigris%E2%80%93Euphrates_river_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamian_Marshes" title="Mesopotamian Marshes">Mesopotamian Marshes</a> in southern Iraq were historically the largest wetland <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> of Western <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>. <a href="/wiki/Draining_of_the_Mesopotamian_Marshes" title="Draining of the Mesopotamian Marshes">Their drainage</a> began in the 1950s, to reclaim land for agriculture and oil exploration. <a href="/wiki/Saddam_Hussein" title="Saddam Hussein">Saddam Hussein</a> extended this work in the late 1980s and early 1990s, as part of ecological warfare against the Marsh Arabs, a rebellious group of people in Baathist Iraq. However, with the breaching of the dikes by local communities after the <a href="/wiki/2003_invasion_of_Iraq" title="2003 invasion of Iraq">2003 invasion of Iraq</a> and the ending of a four-year drought that same year, the process has been reversed and the marshes have experienced a substantial rate of recovery. The permanent wetlands now cover more than 50% of pre-1970s levels, with a remarkable regrowth of the <a href="/wiki/Hammar_Marshes" title="Hammar Marshes">Hammar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hawizeh_Marshes_(Iraq/Iran)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hawizeh Marshes (Iraq/Iran)">Hawizeh Marshes</a> and some recovery of the <a href="/wiki/Central_Marshes_(Iraq)" class="mw-redirect" title="Central Marshes (Iraq)">Central Marshes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
