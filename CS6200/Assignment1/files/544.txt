<p><b>Bilateral descent</b> is a system of family <a href="/wiki/Kinship_and_descent" class="mw-redirect" title="Kinship and descent">lineage</a> in which the relatives on the mother's side and father's side are equally important for emotional ties or for transfer of property or wealth. It is a family arrangement where descent and inheritance are passed equally through both parents.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Families who use this system trace descent through both parents simultaneously and recognize multiple ancestors, but unlike with <a href="/wiki/Cognatic" class="mw-redirect" title="Cognatic">cognatic</a> descent it is not used to form descent groups.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While bilateral descent is increasingly the norm in <a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">Western culture</a>, traditionally it is only found among relatively few groups in <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Melanesia" title="Melanesia">Melanesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, the Philippines, and <a href="/wiki/Polynesia" title="Polynesia">Polynesia</a>. Anthropologists believe that a tribal structure based on bilateral descent helps members live in extreme environments because it allows individuals to rely on two sets of families dispersed over a wide area.<sup id="cite_ref-scientificamerican2001_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scientificamerican2001-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Under bilateral descent, every tribe member belongs to two clans, one through the father (a patriclan) and another through the mother (a matriclan). For example, among the <a href="/wiki/Himba_people" title="Himba people">Himba</a>, clans are led by the eldest male in the clan. Sons live with their father's clan and when daughters marry they go to live with the clan of their husband. However inheritance of wealth does not follow the patriclan but is determined by the matriclan i.e. a son does not inherit his father's cattle but his maternal uncle's instead.<sup id="cite_ref-scientificamerican2001_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scientificamerican2001-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title="&#160;Dead link since January 2019">dead link</span></a></i>&#93;</span></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Javanese_people" title="Javanese people">Javanese people</a>, the largest ethnic group in <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, also adopt a bilateral kinship system .<sup id="cite_ref-women_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-women-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-beyond_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beyond-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Dimasa Kachari people of Northeast India has a system of dual family clan. The <a href="/wiki/Urapmin_people" title="Urapmin people">Urapmin people</a>, a small tribe in <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a>, have a system of kinship classes known as <i>tanum miit</i>. The classes are inherited bilaterally from both parents. Since they also practice strict <a href="/wiki/Endogamy" title="Endogamy">endogamy</a>, most Urapmin belong to all of the major classes, creating great fluidity and doing little to differentiate individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
