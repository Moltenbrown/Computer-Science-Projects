<p><b>Tikrit</b> (<a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl">تكريت</span>&#8206; <i>Tikrīt</i> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Arabic" title="Help:IPA/Arabic">[ˈtɪkriːt]</a></span>, <a href="/wiki/Classical_Syriac_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Classical Syriac language">Classical Syriac</a>: <span lang="syc" dir="rtl">ܬܓܪܝܬ</span>&#8206; <i lang="syc-Latn" title="Classical Syriac-language romanization">Tagriṯ</i>) sometimes transliterated as <b>Takrit</b> or <b>Tekrit</b>, is a city in <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>, located 140 kilometres (87&#160;mi) northwest of <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">Baghdad</a> and 220 kilometres (140&#160;mi) southeast of <a href="/wiki/Mosul" title="Mosul">Mosul</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Tigris_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Tigris River">Tigris River</a>. It is the administrative center of the <a href="/wiki/Saladin_Governorate" title="Saladin Governorate">Saladin Governorate</a>. As of 2012<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tikrit&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, it had a population of 160,000.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Under <a href="/wiki/Arab_Socialist_Ba%27ath_Party_-_Iraq_Region" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab Socialist Ba&#39;ath Party - Iraq Region">Ba'athism</a>, Tikrit was notable as the birthplace of <a href="/wiki/Saddam_Hussein" title="Saddam Hussein">Saddam Hussein</a>, and early on as a power base for the regime.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In recent years the city has been the site of conflict culminating in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Battle_of_Tikrit" title="Second Battle of Tikrit">Second Battle of Tikrit</a> from March through April 2015, resulting in the displacement of 28,000 civilians.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The Iraqi government regained control of the city from the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_State_of_Iraq_and_the_Levant" title="Islamic State of Iraq and the Levant">Islamic State</a> on March 31, 2015. But the city was recaptured at the end of 2015 and now is in peace.<sup id="cite_ref-timesunion.com_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timesunion.com-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tikrit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_times">Ancient times</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tikrit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancient times">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As a fort along the <a href="/wiki/Tigris" title="Tigris">Tigris</a> (<a href="/wiki/Akkadian_language" title="Akkadian language">Akkadian</a>: <i>Idiqlat</i>), the city is first mentioned in the <i>Fall of Assyria Chronicle</i> as being a refuge for the <a href="/wiki/Babylonia" title="Babylonia">Babylonian</a> king <a href="/wiki/Nabopolassar" title="Nabopolassar">Nabopolassar</a> during his attack on the city of <a href="/wiki/Assur" title="Assur">Assur</a> in 615 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Tikrit is usually identified as the <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic" class="mw-redirect" title="Hellenistic">Hellenistic</a> settlement <a href="/wiki/Birtha_(Mesopotamia)" title="Birtha (Mesopotamia)">Birtha</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Christian_Tikrit">Christian Tikrit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tikrit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Christian Tikrit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Until the 6th century, Christianity within the <a href="/wiki/Sasanian_Empire" title="Sasanian Empire">Sasanian Empire</a> was predominantly <a href="/wiki/Dyophysitism" title="Dyophysitism">dyophysite</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_East" title="Church of the East">Church of the East</a>, however, as a result of <a href="/wiki/Miaphysitism" title="Miaphysitism">Miaphysite</a> missionary work, Tikrit became a major Miaphysite (Orthodox Christian) centre under its first bishop, <a href="/wiki/Ahudemmeh" title="Ahudemmeh">Ahudemmeh</a>, in 559.<sup id="cite_ref-Maas2005_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maas2005-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Under <a href="/wiki/Marutha_of_Tikrit" title="Marutha of Tikrit">Marutha of Tikrit</a>, the bishopric was elevated into a <a href="/wiki/Maphrian" title="Maphrian">maphrianate</a> and the city's ecclesiastical jurisdiction extended as far as Central Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-syriacstudies_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-syriacstudies-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The city remained predominantly <a href="/wiki/Syriac_Orthodox_Christians_(Middle_East)" class="mw-redirect" title="Syriac Orthodox Christians (Middle East)">Syriac Orthodox Christian</a> in the early centuries of <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islamic</a> rule and gained fame as an important centre of <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_people" title="Assyrian people">Syriac</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arab_Christians" title="Arab Christians">Christian Arab</a> literature. Some famous Christians from the city include its <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishop</a> <a href="/wiki/Quriaqos_of_Tagrit" title="Quriaqos of Tagrit">Quriaqos of Tagrit</a> who ascended to become the patriarch of the <a href="/wiki/Syriac_Orthodox_Church" title="Syriac Orthodox Church">Syriac Orthodox Church</a>, theologians Abu Zakariya Denha and <a href="/wiki/Abu_Raita_al-Takriti" title="Abu Raita al-Takriti">Abu Raita</a>, and translator <a href="/wiki/Yahya_ibn_Adi" title="Yahya ibn Adi">Yahya ibn Adi</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EOI_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EOI-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From the ninth century Christians of Tikrit began to migrate northwards due to restrictive measures taken by some Muslim governors. Many settled in <a href="/wiki/Mosul" title="Mosul">Mosul</a> and villages in the <a href="/wiki/Nineveh_plains" class="mw-redirect" title="Nineveh plains">Nineveh Plains</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Bakhdida" title="Bakhdida">Bakhdida</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Tur_Abdin" title="Tur Abdin">Tur Abdin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rassam2005_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rassam2005-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The Christian community received a setback when the governor ordered the destruction of the main cathedral known popularly as the "<a href="/wiki/Saint_Ahoadamah_Church,_Tikrit" title="Saint Ahoadamah Church, Tikrit">Green Church</a>" in 1089, the maphrian and some of the Christians of Tikrit had to relocate to the <a href="/wiki/Mar_Mattai_monastery" class="mw-redirect" title="Mar Mattai monastery">Mar Mattai Monastery</a>, where a village named <a href="/wiki/Merki,_Iraq" title="Merki, Iraq">Merki</a> was established in the valley below the monastery. Another governor permitted the reconstruction of the cathedral. However, instability returned and the maphrian moved indefinitely to Mosul in 1156.<sup id="cite_ref-Rassam2005_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rassam2005-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Regardless, the city remained an important center of the <a href="/wiki/Syriac_Orthodox_Church" title="Syriac Orthodox Church">Syriac Orthodox Church</a> until its destruction by <a href="/wiki/Timur" title="Timur">Timur</a> in the late 14th century. A Christian presence has not existed in the city since the 17th century.<sup id="cite_ref-EOI_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EOI-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_Tikrit">Medieval Tikrit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tikrit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Medieval Tikrit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The town was also home to the <a href="/wiki/Arab_Christian" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab Christian">Arab Christian</a> tribe of Iyad. The Arabs of the town secretly assisted the Muslims when they besieged the town. The Muslims entered Tikrit in 640, it was from then considered as part of the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Mesopotamia" title="Upper Mesopotamia">Jazira</a> province, it was later regarded as belonging to Iraq by Arab geographers.<sup id="cite_ref-EOI_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EOI-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Arab <a href="/wiki/Uqaylid_dynasty" title="Uqaylid dynasty">Uqaylid Dynasty</a> took hold of Tikrit in 1036.
Around 1138, <a href="/wiki/Saladin" title="Saladin">Saladin</a> was born there.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The modern province of which Tikrit is the capital is named after him.
</p><p>The city was devastated in the 14th century by <a href="/wiki/Timur" title="Timur">Timur</a>. During Ottoman period Tikrit existed as a small settlement that belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Rakka_Eyalet" title="Rakka Eyalet">Rakka Eyalet</a> and whose population never exceeded 4,000-5,000.<sup id="cite_ref-EOI_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EOI-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_Tikrit">Contemporary Tikrit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tikrit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Contemporary Tikrit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tikrit.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Tikrit.jpg/220px-Tikrit.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="284" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Tikrit.jpg/330px-Tikrit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Tikrit.jpg/440px-Tikrit.jpg 2x" data-file-width="540" data-file-height="696" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tikrit.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>View of Tikrit ca. 1914</div></div></div>
<p>In September 1917, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> forces captured the city during a major advance against the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>.
</p><p>The Tikriti Jewish community was mostly gone by 1948. By the time Saddam Hussein rose to power there were only two Jewish families in the city.
</p><p>The city is the birthplace of <a href="/wiki/Saddam_Hussein" title="Saddam Hussein">Saddam Hussein</a>. Many senior members of the Iraqi government during his rule were drawn from Saddam's own Tikriti tribe, the <a href="/wiki/Al-Bu_Nasir_(Iraqi_tribe)" title="Al-Bu Nasir (Iraqi tribe)">Al-Bu Nasir,</a> as were members of his <a href="/wiki/Iraqi_Republican_Guard" class="mw-redirect" title="Iraqi Republican Guard">Iraqi Republican Guard</a>, chiefly because Saddam apparently felt that he was most able to rely on relatives and allies of his family. The Tikriti domination of the Iraqi government became something of an embarrassment to Hussein and, in 1977, he abolished the use of surnames in Iraq to conceal the fact that so many of his key supporters bore the same surname, <i>al-Tikriti</i> (as did Saddam himself).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Saddam Hussein was buried near Tikrit in his hometown of Owja following his hanging on December 30, 2006.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iraq_War">Iraq War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tikrit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Iraq War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the opening weeks of the <a href="/wiki/2003_invasion_of_Iraq" title="2003 invasion of Iraq">2003 US-led invasion</a>, many observers speculated that Saddam would return to Tikrit as his "last stronghold". The city was subjected to intense aerial bombardment meant to throw Saddam's elite Republican Guard troops out of the city. On April 13, 2003 several thousand <a href="/wiki/U.S._Marines" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Marines">U.S. Marines</a> and other coalition members aboard 300 armored vehicles converged on the town, meeting little or no resistance. With the fall of Tikrit, U.S. Army Major General <a href="/wiki/Stanley_McChrystal" class="mw-redirect" title="Stanley McChrystal">Stanley McChrystal</a> said, "I would anticipate that the major combat operations are over."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, during the subsequent <a href="/wiki/History_of_Iraq_(2003%E2%80%932011)" title="History of Iraq (2003–2011)">occupation</a> Tikrit became the scene of a number of <a href="/wiki/Iraqi_insurgency_(Iraq_War)" class="mw-redirect" title="Iraqi insurgency (Iraq War)">insurgent</a> attacks against the occupation forces. It is commonly regarded as being the northern angle of the "<a href="/wiki/Sunni_Triangle" title="Sunni Triangle">Sunni Triangle</a>" within which the insurgency was at its most intense. In June 2003, <a href="/wiki/Abid_Hamid_Mahmud" title="Abid Hamid Mahmud">Abid Hamid Mahmud</a>, Saddam Hussein's Presidential Secretary and the Ace of Diamonds on the <a href="/wiki/Most-wanted_Iraqi_playing_cards" title="Most-wanted Iraqi playing cards">most wanted 'Deck of Cards,'</a> was captured in a joint raid by special operations forces and the 1st Battalion, <a href="/wiki/22nd_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="22nd Infantry Regiment (United States)">22nd Infantry Regiment</a> of 1st Brigade, <a href="/wiki/4th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="4th Infantry Division (United States)">4th Infantry Division</a>.
</p><p>After the fall of <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">Baghdad</a>, Saddam Hussein was in and around Tikrit. He was hidden by relatives and supporters for about six months. During his final period in hiding, he lived in a small hole just outside the town of <a href="/wiki/Ad-Dawr" title="Ad-Dawr">ad-Dawr</a>, fifteen kilometres south of Tikrit on the eastern bank of the Tigris, a few kilometers southeast of his hometown of Al-Awja. The missions which resulted in the capture of Saddam Hussein were assigned to the 1st Brigade Combat Teams of the 4th Infantry Division, commanded by Colonel <a href="/wiki/James_Hickey_(soldier)" title="James Hickey (soldier)">James Hickey</a> of the 4th Infantry Division. The U.S. Army finally captured Saddam Hussein on December 13, 2003 during <a href="/wiki/Operation_Red_Dawn" title="Operation Red Dawn">Operation Red Dawn</a>.
</p><p>During the 2003 invasion of Iraq, <a href="/wiki/AFN_Iraq" title="AFN Iraq">AFN Iraq</a> ("Freedom Radio") broadcast news and entertainment within Tikrit, among other locations.
</p><p>On November 22, 2005, HHC <a href="/wiki/42nd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="42nd Infantry Division (United States)">42nd Infantry Division</a> New York Army National Guard, handed over control of Saddam Hussein's primary palace complex in Tikrit to the governor of Salah Ah Din Province, who represented the Iraqi government. Discontinuing the existence of what once was FOB Danger. The palace complex had served as a headquarters for <a href="/wiki/4th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="4th Infantry Division (United States)">U.S. 4th Infantry Division</a>, <a href="/wiki/1st_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="1st Infantry Division (United States)">U.S. 1st Infantry Division</a>, and 42nd Infantry Division. The palace complex now serves several purposes for the Iraqi police and army, including headquarters and jails. The U.S. military subsequently moved their operations to al Sahra Airfield, later known as <a href="/wiki/COB_Speicher" class="mw-redirect" title="COB Speicher">COB Speicher</a>, northwest of Tikrit.
</p><p>Saddam Hussein's primary palace complex contained his own palace, one built for his mother and his sons and also included a man made lake, all enclosed with a wall and towers. Plans for the palace grounds when originally returned to the Iraqi people included turning it into an exclusive and lush resort. However, within weeks of turning over the palace, it was ravaged, and its contents, (furniture, columns, even light switches), were stolen and sold on the streets of Tikrit.
</p><p>The 402nd Civil Affairs Detachment of the U.S. Army, and the government of Salah Ah Din province, began plans to improve local economic conditions. One of the many projects they are working on is building an industrial vocational school in the Tikrit area. The school will teach local people skills in different fields of technology, which will help to build and improve Iraq's economic stability.<sup id="cite_ref-defendamerica.mil_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-defendamerica.mil-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The curriculum will educate men and women in multiple occupational fields such as the production of high-tech products, plastic production technology, masonry, carpentry, petroleum equipment maintenance and repair, farm machinery and automotive repair. This self-supporting educational institution owns a textile mill where many of the graduates will work producing uniforms. The mill is scheduled to begin producing and selling products within the year, with the profits from the mill going to fund the school. The vocational school's operation, support and funding are modeled after a system South Korea used in another part of Iraq.<sup id="cite_ref-defendamerica.mil_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-defendamerica.mil-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On April 18, 2010, <a href="/wiki/Abu_Ayyub_al-Masri" title="Abu Ayyub al-Masri">Abu Ayyub al-Masri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Abu_Abdullah_al-Rashid_al-Baghdadi" class="mw-redirect" title="Abu Abdullah al-Rashid al-Baghdadi">Abu Abdullah al-Rashid al-Baghdadi</a> were killed in a raid 10&#160;km (6&#160;mi) of Tikrit in a safe house.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ISIL_insurgents">ISIL insurgents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tikrit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: ISIL insurgents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Second_Battle_of_Tikrit" title="Second Battle of Tikrit">Second Battle of Tikrit</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Fobspeicheriraq2.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Fobspeicheriraq2.png/220px-Fobspeicheriraq2.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Fobspeicheriraq2.png/330px-Fobspeicheriraq2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Fobspeicheriraq2.png/440px-Fobspeicheriraq2.png 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="398" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Fobspeicheriraq2.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tikrit Air Academy (formerly <a href="/wiki/COB_Speicher" class="mw-redirect" title="COB Speicher">COB Speicher</a>), site of the <a href="/wiki/Camp_Speicher_massacre" title="Camp Speicher massacre">Camp Speicher massacre</a> by ISIL, was recaptured and used as an offensive launching platform for the second battle of Tikrit where Gen. <a href="/wiki/Qasem_Soleimani" title="Qasem Soleimani">Qasem Soleimani</a> coordinated the Badr brigade &amp; Kata’ib Imam Ali efforts.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Islamic_State_of_Iraq" title="Islamic State of Iraq">Islamic State of Iraq</a> launched <a href="/wiki/2011_Tikrit_assault" title="2011 Tikrit assault">an attack on March 29, 2011</a> that killed 65 people and wounded over 100.<sup id="cite_ref-IFEX1_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IFEX1-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Reuters news agency included the attack in its list of deadliest attacks in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-Reuters_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On June 11, 2014, during the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Iraq_offensive_(June_2014)" title="Northern Iraq offensive (June 2014)">Northern Iraq offensive</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_State_of_Iraq_and_the_Levant" title="Islamic State of Iraq and the Levant">Islamic State of Iraq and the Levant</a> <a href="/wiki/First_Battle_of_Tikrit" title="First Battle of Tikrit">took control of the city</a>. Hours later, the Iraqi Army made an attempt to recapture the city, which resulted in heavy fighting.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> On June 12, <a href="/wiki/Camp_Speicher_massacre" title="Camp Speicher massacre">ISIL executed</a> at least 1,566<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Iraqi_Air_Force" title="Iraqi Air Force">Iraqi Air Force</a> cadets from <a href="/wiki/Camp_Speicher" title="Camp Speicher">Camp Speicher</a> at Tikrit. At the time of the attack there were between 4,000 and 11,000 unarmed cadets in the camp.<sup id="cite_ref-Buratha_News_Agency:_4000_soldiers_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Buratha_News_Agency:_4000_soldiers-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Iraq" class="mw-redirect" title="Government of Iraq">Iraqi government</a> blamed the massacre on both ISIL and members of the <a href="/wiki/Arab_Socialist_Ba%27ath_Party_%E2%80%93_Iraq_Region" title="Arab Socialist Ba&#39;ath Party – Iraq Region">Arab Socialist Ba'ath Party – Iraq Region</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CNN_Arabic_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNN_Arabic-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> By July 2014, government forces had withdrawn from Tikrit.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On September 25, 2014, <a href="/wiki/Destruction_of_cultural_heritage_by_ISIL" title="Destruction of cultural heritage by ISIL">Islamist militants destroyed</a> the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Ahoadamah_Church,_Tikrit" title="Saint Ahoadamah Church, Tikrit">Assyrian Church</a> there that dated back to 700 AD.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2015, the <a href="/wiki/Iraqi_Army" title="Iraqi Army">Iraqi Army</a> along with the <a href="/wiki/Popular_Mobilization_Forces_(Iraq)" class="mw-redirect" title="Popular Mobilization Forces (Iraq)"><i>Hashd Shaabi</i> popular forces</a> launched <a href="/wiki/Second_Battle_of_Tikrit" title="Second Battle of Tikrit">an operation to retake Tikrit</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> On March 31, the Iraqi government claimed the city had been recaptured.<sup id="cite_ref-timesunion.com_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timesunion.com-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tikrit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is about 160 kilometres (99&#160;mi) north of <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">Baghdad</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Tigris_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Tigris River">Tigris River</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tikrit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture_and_community">Culture and community</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tikrit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Culture and community">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tikrit_Museum" title="Tikrit Museum">Tikrit Museum</a> was damaged during 2003 <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Tikrit" title="University of Tikrit">University of Tikrit</a> was established in 1987 and is one of the largest universities in Iraq.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Tikrit_Stadium" title="Tikrit Stadium">Tikrit Stadium</a> is a multi-use facility used mostly for <a href="/wiki/Football_(soccer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Football (soccer)">football</a> matches and serves as the home stadium of <a href="/wiki/Salahaddin_FC" title="Salahaddin FC">Salah ad Din FC</a>. It holds 10,000 people.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_facilities">Military facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tikrit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Military facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Iraqi_Air_Force" title="Iraqi Air Force">Iraqi Air Force</a> has had several air bases at Tikrit: the <a href="/wiki/Tikrit_South_Air_Base" title="Tikrit South Air Base">Tikrit South Air Base</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tikrit_East_Air_Base" title="Tikrit East Air Base">Tikrit East Air Base</a> and Al Sahra Airfield (Tikrit Air Academy, formerly <a href="/wiki/COB_Speicher" class="mw-redirect" title="COB Speicher">COB Speicher</a>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tikrit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The city of Tikrit has two small airports; <a href="/wiki/Tikrit_East_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Tikrit East Airport">Tikrit East Airport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tikrit_South_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Tikrit South Airport">Tikrit South Airport</a>.
</p>
