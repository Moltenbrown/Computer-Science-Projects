<p>Commonly, "<b>cousin</b>" refers to a "first cousin", people whose <a href="/wiki/Most_recent_common_ancestor" title="Most recent common ancestor">most recent common ancestor</a> is a grandparent.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> A first cousin is a <a href="/wiki/Third-degree_relative" title="Third-degree relative">third-degree relative</a> and used to be known as a <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cousin-german" class="extiw" title="wikt:cousin-german"><i>cousin-german</i></a>, though this term is rarely used today.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>More generally, cousin is a type of <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">familial</a> relationship in which people with a known common ancestor are both two or more generations away from their most recent common ancestor. This distinguishes a cousin from an ancestor, descendant, sibling, aunt, uncle, niece, or nephew.<sup id="cite_ref-DictWebster_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DictWebster-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Systems of "degrees" and "removals" are used in the English-speaking world to describe the exact relationship between two cousins (in the broad sense) and the ancestor they have in common.<sup id="cite_ref-DictGen_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DictGen-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Various governmental entities have established systems for legal use that can precisely specify kinship with common ancestors any number of generations in the past.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Common usage often eliminates the degrees and removals, and refers to people with common ancestry as simply "distant cousins" or "relatives".<sup id="cite_ref-DictOxford_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DictOxford-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_definitions"><span id="Removed"></span><span id="First_cousins_once_removed"></span>Basic definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cousin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Basic definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CousinTree.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/CousinTree.svg/300px-CousinTree.svg.png" decoding="async" width="300" height="261" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/CousinTree.svg/450px-CousinTree.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/CousinTree.svg/600px-CousinTree.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="548" data-file-height="477" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CousinTree.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Family tree. Cousins are colored green. Generations are shown by alternating stripes of gray and white.</div></div></div>
<p>People are related with a type of cousin relationship if they share a common ancestor and the most recent common ancestor is two or more generations away from both people. This means neither person is an <a href="/wiki/Lineal_descendant" title="Lineal descendant">ancestor</a> of the other, they do not share a parent (<a href="/wiki/Sibling" title="Sibling">siblings</a>), and neither is a sibling of a common ancestor (<a href="/wiki/Aunt" title="Aunt">aunts</a>/<a href="/wiki/Uncle" title="Uncle">uncles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nephew_and_niece" class="mw-redirect" title="Nephew and niece">nieces/nephews</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-DictWebster_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DictWebster-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the English system the cousin relationship is further detailed by <i>degree</i> and <i>removal</i>. For example, the "second cousin once removed" relationship is a "second-degree cousin" with one removal. When the degree is not specified first cousin is assumed. When the removal is not specified no removal is assumed.
</p><p>Removal in the cousin relationship is the number of generations the cousins are separated by. When the cousins are separated by a different number of generations from the most recent common ancestor, the cousin relationship is "removed". The difference between the number of generations for each cousin is the removal.<sup id="cite_ref-DictGen_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DictGen-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> For example, if the most recent common ancestor is 2 generations prior for one person and 3 generations prior for the other (one person's grandfather is the other person's great-grandfather) or the most recent common ancestor is 3 generations prior for one person and 4 generations prior for the other (one person's great-grandfather is the other person's great-great-grandfather) the cousins are separated by one generation and therefore once removed. Two people can be removed but be around the same age due to differences in birth dates of parents children and other relevant ancestors.<sup id="cite_ref-DictGen_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DictGen-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GG_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GG-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The degree of the cousin relationship is the number of generations prior to the parents before a most recent common ancestor is found. If the cousins are removed, the smaller number of generations to the most recent common ancestor is used to determine the degree of the cousin relationship.<sup id="cite_ref-DictGen_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DictGen-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> For example, if one of the cousins has to go back one generation beyond their parents (the grandparents) before finding the most recent common ancestor and the other has to go back one or more they are first cousins. If one had to go back two generations beyond the parents (great grandparents) and the other had to go back two or more they would be second cousins.<sup id="cite_ref-GG_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GG-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DictGen_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DictGen-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Multiplicities">Multiplicities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cousin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Multiplicities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Double cousins arise when two <a href="/wiki/Sibling" title="Sibling">siblings</a> of one family mate with two siblings of another family.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> This may also be referred to as 'cousins on both sides.' The resulting children are related to each other through both of their <a href="/wiki/Parent" title="Parent">parents</a> and are thus doubly related. Double first cousins share both sets of <a href="/wiki/Grandparent" title="Grandparent">grandparents</a> and have twice the degree of <a href="/wiki/Consanguinity" title="Consanguinity">consanguinity</a> of ordinary first cousins.
</p><p>Half cousins are descended from half siblings.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The children of two half siblings are first half cousins. If half siblings have children with another pair of half siblings, the resulting children would be double first half cousins.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Paternal_and_maternal_cousins">Paternal and maternal cousins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cousin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Paternal and maternal cousins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>A maternal cousin is a cousin that is related to the mother's side of the family, while a paternal cousin is a cousin that is related to the father's side of the family. Unlike all the other cousin relationships discussed thus far, this relationship is not necessarily reciprocal, as the maternal cousin of one person could be the paternal cousin of the other.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Parallel_and_cross_cousins" title="Parallel and cross cousins">Parallel and cross cousins</a> on the other hand are reciprocal relationships. Parallel cousins are descended from same-sex siblings. Cousins that are related to same-sex siblings of their most recent common ancestor are parallel cousins. A parallel first cousin is either the paternal cousin on the father's side of the family or the maternal cousin on the mother's side of the family. Cross cousins are descendants from opposite-sex siblings. A cross first cousin is either the maternal cousin on the father's side of the family or the paternal cousin on the mother's side of the family.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-blood_relations">Non-blood relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cousin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Non-blood relations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Stepcousins are either stepchildren of an individual's aunt or uncle, nieces and nephews of one's stepparent, or the children of one's parent's stepsibling.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Cousins in law are the cousins of a person's spouse or the spouse of a person's cousin.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Neither of these relationships has consanguinity.
</p>

<dl><dt><span id="First_cousin"></span><b>First cousins</b></dt>
<dd>A person shares a first cousin or cousin relationship with the children of their parent's full siblings. Cousins share two grandparents.<sup id="cite_ref-GG_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GG-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In the example family tree, Joseph and Julie are first cousins.</dd></dl>
<ul><li><b>Maternal first cousins</b>: a person's maternal first cousins are the children of their mother's full siblings. In the example family tree, Julie is Joseph's maternal first cousin.</li>
<li><b>Paternal first cousins</b>: a person's paternal first cousins are the children of their father's full siblings. In the example family tree, Joseph is Julie's paternal first cousin.</li></ul>
<dl><dt>Second cousins</dt>
<dd>A person shares a second cousin relationship with the children of their parent's first cousins. Second cousins share two great-grandparents.<sup id="cite_ref-GG_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GG-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In the example family tree, Gordon and Matt are second cousins.</dd></dl>
<ul><li><b>Maternal second cousins - number one</b>: a person shares a second cousin relationship with the children of their mother's maternal first cousins.</li>
<li><b>Maternal second cousins - number two</b>: a person shares a second cousin relationship with the children of their mother's paternal first cousins.</li>
<li><b>Paternal second cousins - number one</b>: a person shares a second cousin relationship with the children of their father's maternal first cousins.</li>
<li><b>Paternal second cousins - number two</b>: a person shares a second cousin relationship with the children of their father's paternal first cousins.</li></ul>
<dl><dt>Third cousins</dt>
<dd>A person shares a third cousin relationship with the children of their parent's second cousins. Third cousins share two great-great-grandparents.<sup id="cite_ref-GG_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GG-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In the example family tree, Sam and Lyla are third cousins.</dd>
<dt><span id="1stOnceRem"></span>First cousins once removed</dt>
<dd>A person shares a first cousins once removed relationship with their parent's first cousins and their first cousin's children. At least one set of one person's grandparents are the great-grandparents of the other person.<sup id="cite_ref-GG_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GG-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In the example family tree, Gordon and Julie, as well as Joseph and Matt, are first cousins once removed.</dd>
<dd>There exist numerous terms for first cousins once removed that describe the relationship more specifically:</dd></dl>
<ul><li>For a male in the higher generation, "cousin-uncle" or "first cousin-uncle"
<ul><li><b>Maternal first cousin-uncle - number one</b>: mother's maternal first cousin.</li>
<li><b>Maternal first cousin-uncle - number two</b>: mother's paternal first cousin.</li>
<li><b>Paternal first cousin-uncle - number one</b>: father's maternal first cousin.</li>
<li><b>Paternal first cousin-uncle - number two</b>: father's paternal first cousin.</li></ul></li>
<li>For a female in the higher generation, "cousin-aunt" or "first cousin-aunt"
<ul><li><b>Maternal first cousin-aunt - number one</b>: mother's maternal first cousin.</li>
<li><b>Maternal first cousin-aunt - number two</b>: mother's paternal first cousin.</li>
<li><b>Paternal first cousin-aunt - number one</b>: father's maternal first cousin.</li>
<li><b>Paternal first cousin-aunt - number two</b>: father's paternal first cousin.</li></ul></li>
<li>For a male in the lower generation, "cousin-nephew" or "first cousin-nephew"</li>
<li>For a female in the lower generation, "cousin-niece" or "first cousin-niece"</li></ul>
<dl><dt><span id="1stTwiceRem"></span>First cousins twice removed</dt>
<dd>A person shares a first cousins twice removed relationship with their grandparent's first cousins and their first cousin's grandchildren. At least one set of one person's grandparents are the great-great-grandparents of the other person.<sup id="cite_ref-GG_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GG-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In the example family tree, Sam and Julie, as well as Joseph and Lyla, are first cousins twice removed.</dd>
<dd>There exist numerous addition terms to describe the relationship of first cousin twice removed more specifically:</dd></dl>
<ul><li>For either sexes, higher generation or lower generation, “grandcousin”</li>
<li>For a male in a higher generation, “great-cousin-uncle” or “first great-cousin-uncle”</li>
<li>For a female in a higher generation, “great-cousin-aunt” or “first great-cousin-aunt”</li>
<li>For a male in a lower generation, “great-cousin-nephew” or “first great-cousin-nephew”</li>
<li>For a female in a lower generation, “great-cousin-niece” or “first great-cousin-niece”</li></ul>
<dl><dt><span id="2ndOnceRem"></span>Second cousins once removed</dt>
<dd>A person shares a second cousin once removed relationship with their parent's second cousins and their second cousin's children. At least one set of one person's great-grandparents are the great-great-grandparents of the other person.<sup id="cite_ref-GG_6-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GG-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In the example family tree, Sam and Matt, as well as Gordon and Lyla, are second cousins once removed.</dd>
<dd>There are more ways in which this type of relationship can be described more specifically:</dd></dl>
<ul><li>For a male in a higher generation, "second cousin-uncle"</li>
<li>For a female in a higher generation, "second cousin-aunt"</li>
<li>For a male in a lower generation, "second cousin-nephew"</li>
<li>For a female in a lower generation, "second cousin-niece"</li></ul>
<dl><dt><span id="2ndTwiceRem"></span>Second cousins twice removed</dt>
<dd>A person shares a second cousins twice removed relationship with their grandparent's second cousins and their second cousin's grandchildren. In the example family tree, Sam and Julie, as well as Joseph and Lyla, are second cousins twice removed.</dd>
<dd>There exist numerous addition terms to describe the relationship of second cousin twice removed more specifically:</dd></dl>
<p>For either sexes, higher generation or lower generation, “second grandcousin”
</p>
<ul><li>For a male in a higher generation, "second great-cousin-uncle"</li>
<li>For a female in a higher generation, "second great-cousin-aunt"</li>
<li>For a male in a lower generation, "second great-cousin-nephew"</li>
<li>For a female in a lower generation, "second great-cousin-niece"</li></ul>
<dl><dt>Cross first cousins</dt>
<dd>A person shares a cross cousin relationship with their first cousin when the children of the most recent common ancestor are of different sexes. For cross first cousins the parents of each cousin are siblings and of different sexes.<sup id="cite_ref-GG_6-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GG-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In the example family tree, Joseph and Julie are cross first cousins.</dd>
<dt>Parallel first cousins</dt>
<dd>A person shares a parallel cousin relationship with their first cousin when the children of the most recent common ancestor are of same sex. For parallel first cousins the parents of each cousin are siblings and of same sex.<sup id="cite_ref-GG_6-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GG-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></dd>
<dt>First cousin-in-law</dt>
<dd>A person shares a cousin-in-law relationship with their spouse's cousin or the cousin of one's spouse. In the example family tree, Joseph and Roger are first cousins-in-law to each other.</dd></dl>
<ul><li><b>Maternal first cousins-in-law</b>: the children-in-law of their mother's full siblings.</li>
<li><b>Paternal first cousins-in-law</b>: the children-in-law of their father's full siblings.</li></ul>
<dl><dt>First co-cousin-in-law</dt>
<dd>A person shares a co-cousin-in-law relationship with the spouse of the first cousin of one's spouse.</dd>
<dt>Second cousin-in-law</dt>
<dd>A person shares a second cousin-in-law relationship with their spouse's second cousin or the second cousin of one's spouse.</dd>
<dt>Second co-cousin-in-law</dt>
<dd>A person shares a second co-cousin-in-law relationship with the spouse of the second cousin of one's spouse.</dd>
<dt>Half-first cousins</dt>
<dd>Half-first cousins are the children of their parent's half-siblings. Half-cousins share one grandparent.</dd></dl>
<ul><li><b>Maternal half-first cousins - number one</b>: the children of their mother's maternal half-siblings.</li>
<li><b>Maternal half-first cousins - number two</b>: the children of their mother's paternal half-siblings.</li>
<li><b>Paternal half-first cousins - number one</b>: the children of their father's maternal half-siblings.</li>
<li><b>Paternal half-first cousins - number two</b>: the children of their father's paternal half-siblings.</li></ul>
<dl><dt>Sesqui-first cousins</dt>
<dd>Sesqui-first cousins are the children of their parent’s half-sibling with the other parent’s full sibling. Sesqui-cousins share three grandparents.</dd>
<dt>Double-first cousins</dt>
<dd>Double-first cousins are the children of their mother's full sister with their father's full brother, or the children of their mother's full brother with their father's full sister. Double cousins share four grandparents.</dd>
<dt>Double-half-first cousins</dt>
<dd>Double-half-first cousins are the children of their parent’s half-sibling with their other parent’s half-sibling. Double-half-cousins share two maternal grandparents, or two paternal grandparents, or two grandparents on both sides.</dd>
<dt>Half-second cousins</dt>
<dd>Half-second cousins are the children of their parent's half-first cousins. Half-second cousins share one great-grandparent.</dd>
<dt>Sesqui-second cousins</dt>
<dd>Sesqui-second cousins are the children of their parent's sesqui-first cousins or are the children of their parent’s half-first cousin with their other parent’s first cousin. Sesqui-second cousins share three great-grandparents.</dd>
<dt>Double-second cousins</dt>
<dd>Double-second cousins are the children of their parent's double-first cousins are the children of their parent’s first cousin with their other parent’s first cousin. Double-second cousins share four great-grandparents.</dd>
<dt>2.5-tuple-second cousins</dt>
<dd>2.5-tuple-second cousins are the children of their parent’s first cousin with their other parent’s sesqui-first cousin or are the children of their parent’s double-first cousin with their other parent’s half-first cousin. 2.5-tuple-second cousins share five great-grandparents.</dd>
<dt>Triple-second cousins</dt>
<dd>Triple-second cousins are the children of their parent’s double-first cousin with their other parent’s first cousin or are the children of their parent’s sesqui-first cousin with their other parent’s sesqui-first cousin. Triple-second cousins share six great-grandparents.</dd>
<dt>3.5-tuple-second cousins</dt>
<dd>3.5-tuple-second cousins are the children of their parent’s double-first cousin with their other parent’s sesqui-first cousin. 3.5-tuple-second cousins share seven great-grandparents.</dd>
<dt>Quadruple-second cousins</dt>
<dd>Quadruple-second cousins are the children of their mother’s double-first cousin with their father’s double-first cousin. Quadruple-second cousins share eight great-grandparents.</dd>
<dt>First cousins-in-law once removed</dt>
<dd>A person shares a first cousins-in-law once removed relationship with their parent's first cousins' spouses, their first cousin's children's spouses, their spouse's parent's first cousins or their spouse's first cousins' children.</dd>
<dd>There exist numerous terms for first cousins-in-law once removed that describe the relationship more specifically:</dd></dl>
<ul><li>For a male in the higher generation, "cousin-uncle-in-law" or "first cousin-uncle-in-law"</li>
<li>For a female in the higher generation, "cousin-aunt-in-law" or "first cousin-aunt-in-law"</li>
<li>For a male in the lower generation, "cousin-nephew-in-law" or "first cousin-nephew-in-law"</li>
<li>For a female in the lower generation, "cousin-niece-in-law" or "first cousin-niece-in-law"</li></ul>
<dl><dt>First cousins-in-law twice removed</dt>
<dd>A person shares a first cousins-in-law twice removed relationship with their grandparent's first cousins' spouses, their first cousin's grandchildren's spouses, their spouse's grandparent's first cousins or their spouse's first cousins' grandchildren.</dd>
<dd>There exist numerous terms for first cousins-in-law twice removed that describe the relationship more specifically:</dd></dl>
<ul><li>For either sexes, higher generation or lower generation, “grandcousin-in-law”</li>
<li>For a male in the higher generation, "great-cousin-uncle-in-law” or "first great-cousin-uncle-in-law”</li>
<li>For a female in the higher generation, "great-cousin-aunt-in-law” or "first great-cousin-aunt-in-law”</li>
<li>For a male in the lower generation, "great-cousin-nephew-in-law" or "first great-cousin-nephew-in-law”</li>
<li>For a female in the lower generation, "great-cousin-niece-in-law" or "first great-cousin-niece-in-law”</li></ul>
<dl><dt>Second cousins-in-law once removed</dt>
<dd>A person shares a second cousins-in-law once removed relationship with their parent's second cousins' spouses, their second cousin's children's spouses, their spouse's parent's second cousins or their spouse's second cousins' children.</dd>
<dd>There exist numerous terms for second cousins-in-law once removed that describe the relationship more specifically:</dd></dl>
<ul><li>For a male in the higher generation, "second cousin-uncle-in-law"</li>
<li>For a female in the higher generation, "second cousin-aunt-in-law"</li>
<li>For a male in the lower generation, "second cousin-nephew-in-law"</li>
<li>For a female in the lower generation, "second cousin-niece-in-law"</li></ul>
<dl><dt>Second cousins-in-law twice removed</dt>
<dd>A person shares a second cousins-in-law twice removed relationship with their grandparent's second cousins’ spouses, their second cousin's grandchildren's spouses, their spouse's grandparent's second cousins or their spouse's second cousins' grandchildren.</dd>
<dd>There exist numerous terms for second cousins-in-law twice removed that describe the relationship more specifically:</dd></dl>
<p>For either sexes, higher generation or lower in generation, “second grandcousin-in-law”
</p>
<ul><li>For a male in the higher generation, "second great-cousin-uncle-in-law"</li>
<li>For a female in the higher generation, "second great-cousin-aunt-in-law"</li>
<li>For a male in the lower generation, "second great-cousin-nephew-in-law"</li>
<li>For a female in the lower generation, "second great-cousin-niece-in-law"</li></ul>
<dl><dt>First co-cousins-in-law once removed</dt>
<dd>A person shares a first co-cousins-in-law once removed relationship with their spouse's parent's first cousins's spouse or their spouse's first cousins' children's spouse.</dd>
<dd>There exist numerous terms for first co-cousins-in-law once removed that describe the relationship more specifically:</dd></dl>

<li>For a female in the higher generation, "co-cousin-aunt-in-law" or "first co-cousin-aunt-in-law"</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cousin_marriage">Cousin marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cousin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cousin marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cousin_marriage" title="Cousin marriage">Cousin marriage</a></div>
<p>Cousin marriage is important in several anthropological theories which often differentiate between matriarchal and patriarchal parallel and cross cousins.
</p><p>Currently about 10% and historically as high as 80% of all marriages are between first or second cousins.<sup id="cite_ref-kershaw_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kershaw-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-kissyourcousin_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kissyourcousin-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Cousin marriages are often <a href="/wiki/Arranged_marriage" title="Arranged marriage">arranged</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kershaw_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kershaw-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-kissyourcousin_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kissyourcousin-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bittles1_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bittles1-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bittles_1994,_p._567_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bittles_1994,_p._567-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Anthropologists believe it is used as a tool to strengthen the family, conserve its wealth, protect its cultural heritage, and retain the power structure of the family and its place in the community. 
Some groups encourage cousin marriage while others attach a strong <a href="/wiki/Social_stigma" title="Social stigma">social stigma</a> to it. In some regions in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> over half of all marriages are between first and second cousins. In some of the countries in this region this may exceed 70%.<sup id="cite_ref-map_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-map-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Just outside this region it is often legal but infrequent. In other places it is <a href="/wiki/Prohibited_degree_of_kinship" title="Prohibited degree of kinship">legally prohibited</a> and culturally equivalent to <a href="/wiki/Incest" title="Incest">incest</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-truth_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-truth-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-plos_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-plos-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Supporters of cousin marriage often view the prohibition as <a href="/wiki/Discrimination" title="Discrimination">discrimination</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-finalthoughts_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-finalthoughts-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-okbyscience_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-okbyscience-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> while opponents cite the potential <a href="/wiki/Immorality" title="Immorality">immorality</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-slate_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-slate-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetics">Genetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cousin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Genetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Married couples that possess higher than normal <a href="/wiki/Consanguinity" title="Consanguinity">consanguinity</a>, shared identical <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> and <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetic</a> material, have an increased chance of sharing genes for recessive traits.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  The percentage of consanguinity between any two individuals decreases fourfold as the <a href="/wiki/Most_recent_common_ancestor" title="Most recent common ancestor">most recent common ancestor</a> recedes one generation. First cousins have four times the consanguinity of second cousins, while first cousins once removed have half that of first cousins. Double first cousins share both sets of <a href="/wiki/Grandparent" title="Grandparent">grandparents</a> and have twice the degree of <a href="/wiki/Consanguinity" title="Consanguinity">consanguinity</a> of ordinary first cousins; genetically, they are as related as half-siblings.
Children of these marriages may have an increased risk of <a href="/wiki/Genetic_disorder" title="Genetic disorder">genetic disorders</a>, particularly if their parents both carry a harmful <a href="/wiki/Recessive" class="mw-redirect" title="Recessive">recessive</a> mutation. In a scenario where two monozygotic (identical) twins mate with another pair of monozygotic twins, the resulting double cousins would test as genetically similar as siblings.
</p><p>Scientists through multiple studies have established a substantial and consistent positive correlation between the kinship of couples and the number of children and grandchildren they have. The 2008 deCODE study results show that couples related at the level of third cousins have the greatest number of offspring, with the greatest reproductive success observed for couples related at the level of third and fourth cousins.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>  This study provides the most comprehensive answer yet to the longstanding question of how kinship affects human <a href="/wiki/Fertility" title="Fertility">fertility</a>. The study result was somewhat counterintuitive from an evolutionary perspective because closely related parents have a higher probability of having offspring homozygous for deleterious recessive mutations, although closer parental kinship can also decrease the likelihood of immunological incompatibility between mother and offspring, for example in rhesus factor blood type.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The study confirmed that the offspring of first and second cousins died younger and reproduced less.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

</td>
