It is the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Lucca" title="Province of Lucca">Province of Lucca</a>. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucca&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_Lucca" title="Timeline of Lucca">Timeline of Lucca</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_and_medieval_city">Ancient and medieval city</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucca&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancient and medieval city">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lucca was founded by the <a href="/wiki/Etruscan_civilization" title="Etruscan civilization">Etruscans</a> (there are traces of an earlier <a href="/wiki/Liguri" class="mw-redirect" title="Liguri">Ligurian</a> settlement in the 3rd century BC called <i>Luk</i> meaning marsh in which the name Lucca originated) and became a <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> colony in 180 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-h.f.ullmann_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-h.f.ullmann-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The rectangular grid of its historical centre preserves the Roman street plan, and the Piazza San Michele occupies the site of the ancient <a href="/wiki/Forum_(Roman)" title="Forum (Roman)">forum</a>. Traces of the <a href="/wiki/Amphitheatre" title="Amphitheatre">amphitheatre</a> may still be seen in the <a href="/wiki/Piazza_dell%27Anfiteatro,_Lucca" class="mw-redirect" title="Piazza dell&#39;Anfiteatro, Lucca">Piazza dell'Anfiteatro</a>.
</p><p>At the <a href="/wiki/Lucca_Conference" title="Lucca Conference">Lucca Conference</a>, in 56 BC, <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pompey" title="Pompey">Pompey</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Crassus" class="mw-redirect" title="Crassus">Crassus</a> reaffirmed their political alliance known as the <a href="/wiki/First_Triumvirate" title="First Triumvirate">First Triumvirate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-h.f.ullmann_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-h.f.ullmann-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Torre_guinigi,_view_11,_piazza_dell%27anfiteatro.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Torre_guinigi%2C_view_11%2C_piazza_dell%27anfiteatro.JPG/220px-Torre_guinigi%2C_view_11%2C_piazza_dell%27anfiteatro.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Torre_guinigi%2C_view_11%2C_piazza_dell%27anfiteatro.JPG/330px-Torre_guinigi%2C_view_11%2C_piazza_dell%27anfiteatro.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Torre_guinigi%2C_view_11%2C_piazza_dell%27anfiteatro.JPG/440px-Torre_guinigi%2C_view_11%2C_piazza_dell%27anfiteatro.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Torre_guinigi,_view_11,_piazza_dell%27anfiteatro.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Piazza_dell%27Anfiteatro" title="Piazza dell&#39;Anfiteatro">Piazza dell'Anfiteatro</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_San_Frediano" title="Basilica of San Frediano">Basilica of San Frediano</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Frediano" class="mw-redirect" title="Frediano">Frediano</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Irish</a> <a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">monk</a>, was <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Lucca" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishop of Lucca">bishop of Lucca</a> in the early sixth century.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> At one point, Lucca was plundered by <a href="/wiki/Odoacer" title="Odoacer">Odoacer</a>, the first Germanic King of Italy. Lucca was an important city and fortress even in the sixth century, when <a href="/wiki/Narses" title="Narses">Narses</a> besieged it for several months in 553. Under the <a href="/wiki/Lombards" title="Lombards">Lombards</a>, it was the seat of a duke who minted his own coins. The <a href="/wiki/Holy_Face_of_Lucca" title="Holy Face of Lucca">Holy Face of Lucca</a> (or Volto Santo), a major relic supposedly carved by <a href="/wiki/Nicodemus" title="Nicodemus">Nicodemus</a>, arrived in 742. During the eighth-tenth centuries Lucca was a center of <a href="/wiki/Jewish" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> life, the community being led by the <a href="/wiki/Kalonymos_family" title="Kalonymos family">Kalonymos family</a> (which at some point during this time migrated to Germany to become a major component of proto-<a href="/wiki/Ashkenazi_Jews" title="Ashkenazi Jews">Ashkenazic Jewry</a>). Lucca became prosperous through the <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a> trade that began in the eleventh century, and came to rival the silks of <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantium</a>. During the tenth–eleventh centuries Lucca was the capital of the feudal <a href="/wiki/Margraviate_of_Tuscany" class="mw-redirect" title="Margraviate of Tuscany">margraviate of Tuscany</a>, more or less independent but owing nominal allegiance to the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_republic">First republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucca&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: First republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Lucca" title="Republic of Lucca">Republic of Lucca</a></div>
<p>After the death of <a href="/wiki/Matilda_of_Tuscany" title="Matilda of Tuscany">Matilda of Tuscany</a>, the city began to constitute itself an independent <a href="/wiki/Medieval_commune" title="Medieval commune">commune</a> with a charter in 1160. For almost 500 years, Lucca remained an independent republic. There were many minor provinces in the region between southern <a href="/wiki/Liguria" title="Liguria">Liguria</a> and northern Tuscany dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Malaspina_family" title="Malaspina family">Malaspina</a>; Tuscany in this time was a part of feudal Europe. <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante</a>’s <i>Divine Comedy</i> includes many references to the great feudal families who had huge jurisdictions with administrative and judicial rights. Dante spent some of his exile in Lucca.
</p><p>In 1273 and again in 1277, Lucca was ruled by a <a href="/wiki/Guelphs_and_Ghibellines" title="Guelphs and Ghibellines">Guelph</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Capitano_del_popolo" title="Capitano del popolo">capitano del popolo</a></i> (captain of the people) named <a href="/wiki/Luchetto_Gattilusio" title="Luchetto Gattilusio">Luchetto Gattilusio</a>. In 1314, internal discord allowed <a href="/wiki/Uguccione_della_Faggiuola" title="Uguccione della Faggiuola">Uguccione della Faggiuola</a> of Pisa to make himself lord of Lucca. The Lucchesi expelled him two years later, and handed over the city to another  <i><a href="/wiki/Condottiero" class="mw-redirect" title="Condottiero">condottiero</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Castruccio_Castracani" title="Castruccio Castracani">Castruccio Castracani</a>, under whose rule it became a leading state in central Italy. Lucca rivalled <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a> until Castracani's death in 1328. On 22 and 23 September 1325, in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Altopascio" title="Battle of Altopascio">battle of Altopascio</a>, Castracani defeated <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>'s Guelphs. For this he was nominated by <a href="/wiki/Louis_IV_the_Bavarian" class="mw-redirect" title="Louis IV the Bavarian">Louis IV the Bavarian</a> to become duke of Lucca. Castracani's tomb is in the church of San Francesco. His biography is <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Machiavelli" title="Niccolò Machiavelli">Machiavelli</a>'s third famous book on political rule.
</p><p>In 1408, Lucca hosted the <a href="/wiki/Convocation" title="Convocation">convocation</a> intended to end the schism in the papacy. Occupied by the troops of Louis of Bavaria, the city was sold to a rich Genoese, Gherardino Spinola, then seized by John, king of Bohemia. Pawned to the Rossi of Parma, by them it was ceded to <a href="/wiki/Mastino_II_della_Scala" title="Mastino II della Scala">Mastino II della Scala</a> of <a href="/wiki/Verona" title="Verona">Verona</a>, sold to the Florentines, surrendered to the Pisans, and then nominally liberated by the emperor <a href="/wiki/Charles_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles IV, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles IV</a> and governed by his vicar. Lucca managed, at first as a <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democracy</a>, and after 1628 as an <a href="/wiki/Oligarchy" title="Oligarchy">oligarchy</a>, to maintain its independence alongside of <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a>, and painted the word <i>Libertas</i> on its banner until the French Revolution in 1789.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_Napoleonic_conquest">After Napoleonic conquest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucca&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: After Napoleonic conquest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Palazzo_pfanner,_giardini_03.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Palazzo_pfanner%2C_giardini_03.jpg/220px-Palazzo_pfanner%2C_giardini_03.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="151" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Palazzo_pfanner%2C_giardini_03.jpg/330px-Palazzo_pfanner%2C_giardini_03.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Palazzo_pfanner%2C_giardini_03.jpg/440px-Palazzo_pfanner%2C_giardini_03.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3548" data-file-height="2432" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Palazzo_pfanner,_giardini_03.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Palazzo_Pfanner" title="Palazzo Pfanner">Palazzo Pfanner</a>, garden view</div></div></div>
<p>Lucca had been the second largest Italian city state (after <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>) with a republican constitution ("comune") to remain independent over the centuries.
</p><p>In 1805, Lucca was conquered by <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_I_of_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleon I of France">Napoleon</a>, who installed his sister <a href="/wiki/Elisa_Bonaparte" title="Elisa Bonaparte">Elisa Bonaparte Baciocchi</a> as "Princess of Lucca".
</p><p>From 1815 to 1847 it was a <a href="/wiki/Bourbon_house" class="mw-redirect" title="Bourbon house">Bourbon-Parma</a> <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Lucca" title="Duchy of Lucca">duchy</a>. The only reigning dukes of Lucca were <a href="/wiki/Maria_Luisa_of_Spain,_Duchess_of_Lucca" class="mw-redirect" title="Maria Luisa of Spain, Duchess of Lucca">Maria Luisa of Spain</a>, who was succeeded by her son <a href="/wiki/Charles_II,_Duke_of_Parma" title="Charles II, Duke of Parma">Charles II, Duke of Parma</a> in 1824. Meanwhile, the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Parma" title="Duchy of Parma">Duchy of Parma</a> had been assigned for life to <a href="/wiki/Marie_Louise,_Duchess_of_Parma" title="Marie Louise, Duchess of Parma">Marie Louise, Duchess of Parma</a>, the second wife of <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>. In accordance with the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Vienna_(1815)" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaty of Vienna (1815)">Treaty of Vienna (1815)</a>, upon the death of <a href="/wiki/Marie_Louise,_Duchess_of_Parma" title="Marie Louise, Duchess of Parma">Marie Louise, Duchess of Parma</a> in 1847, Parma reverted to <a href="/wiki/Charles_II,_Duke_of_Parma" title="Charles II, Duke of Parma">Charles II, Duke of Parma</a>, while Lucca lost independence and was annexed to the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Tuscany" title="Grand Duchy of Tuscany">Grand Duchy of Tuscany</a>. As part of Tuscany, it became part of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Sardinia" title="Kingdom of Sardinia">Kingdom of Sardinia</a> in 1860 and finally part of the <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian State</a> in 1861.
</p>

<h3><span id="Walls.2C_streets.2C_and_squares"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Walls,_streets,_and_squares">Walls, streets, and squares</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucca&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Walls, streets, and squares">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The walls encircling the old town remain intact, even as the city expanded and modernized, unusual for cities in the region. Initially built as a defensive <a href="/wiki/Rampart_(fortification)" title="Rampart (fortification)">rampart</a>, once the walls lost their military importance they became a pedestrian promenade, the Passeggiata delle Mura Urbane, a street atop the walls linking the bastions. It passes through the Bastions of Santa Croce, San Frediano, San Martino, San Pietro/Battisti, San Salvatore, La Libertà/Cairoli, San Regolo, San Colombano, Santa Maria, San Paolino/Catalani, and San Donato; and over the gates (Porte): San Donato, Santa Maria, San Jocopo, Elisa, San Pietro, and Sant'Anna. Each of the four principal sides of the structure is lined with a different tree species than the others.
</p><p>The walled city is encircled by Piazzale Boccherini, Viale Lazzaro Papi, Viale Carlo Del Prete, Piazzale Martiri della Libertà, Via Batoni, Viale Agostino Marti, Viale G. Marconi (<i>vide</i> <a href="/wiki/Guglielmo_Marconi" title="Guglielmo Marconi">Guglielmo Marconi</a>), Piazza Don A. Mei, Viale <a href="/wiki/Alfredo_Pacini" title="Alfredo Pacini">Pacini</a>, Viale Giusti, Piazza Curtatone, Piazzale Ricasoli, Viale Ricasoli, Piazza Risorgimento (<i>vide</i> <a href="/wiki/Risorgimento" class="mw-redirect" title="Risorgimento">Risorgimento</a>), and Viale <a href="/wiki/Giosu%C3%A8_Carducci" title="Giosuè Carducci">Giosuè Carducci</a>.
</p><p>The town includes a number of public squares, most notably the <a href="/wiki/Piazza_dell%27Anfiteatro,_Lucca" class="mw-redirect" title="Piazza dell&#39;Anfiteatro, Lucca">Piazza dell'Anfiteatro</a>, site of ancient Roman amphitheater; but also Piazzale Verdi; Piazza Napoleone'; and Piazza San Michele.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucca&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lucca is the <a href="/wiki/Birthplace" class="mw-redirect" title="Birthplace">birthplace</a> of <a href="/wiki/Composer" title="Composer">composers</a> <a href="/wiki/Giacomo_Puccini" title="Giacomo Puccini">Giacomo Puccini</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/La_Boh%C3%A8me" class="mw-redirect" title="La Bohème">La Bohème</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Madama_Butterfly" title="Madama Butterfly">Madama Butterfly</a>)</i>, <a href="/wiki/Nicolao_Dorati" title="Nicolao Dorati">Nicolao Dorati</a>, <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Geminiani" title="Francesco Geminiani">Francesco Geminiani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gioseffo_Guami" title="Gioseffo Guami">Gioseffo Guami</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luigi_Boccherini" title="Luigi Boccherini">Luigi Boccherini</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alfredo_Catalani" title="Alfredo Catalani">Alfredo Catalani</a>. It is also the birthplace of artist <a href="/wiki/Benedetto_Brandimarte" title="Benedetto Brandimarte">Benedetto Brandimarte</a>. Since 2004, Lucca is home to <a href="/wiki/IMT_Institute_for_Advanced_Studies_Lucca" class="mw-redirect" title="IMT Institute for Advanced Studies Lucca">IMT Lucca</a>, a public research institution and a selective graduate school and part of the <a href="/wiki/Superior_Graduate_Schools_in_Italy" title="Superior Graduate Schools in Italy">Superior Graduate Schools in Italy</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Grandes_%C3%A9coles" title="Grandes écoles">Grandes écoles</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
