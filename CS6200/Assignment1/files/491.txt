<p>An <b>ancestor</b>  is a <a href="/wiki/Parent" title="Parent">parent</a> or (<a href="/wiki/Recursion" title="Recursion">recursively</a>) the parent of an antecedent (i.e., a <a href="/wiki/Grandparent" title="Grandparent">grandparent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great-grandparent" class="mw-redirect" title="Great-grandparent">great-grandparent</a>, great-great-grandparent, and so forth). <i>Ancestor</i> is "any person from whom one is descended. In law the person from whom an estate has been inherited."<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Two individuals have a <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetic</a> relationship if one is the ancestor of the other, or if they share a common ancestor. In <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolutionary</a> theory, species which share an evolutionary ancestor are said to be of <a href="/wiki/Common_descent" title="Common descent">common descent</a>. However, this concept of ancestry does not apply to some <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> and other organisms capable of <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_gene_transfer" title="Horizontal gene transfer">horizontal gene transfer</a>. Some research suggests that the average person has twice as many female ancestors as male ancestors. This might have been due to the past prevalence of polygynous relations and female hypergamy.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Assuming that all of an individual's ancestors are otherwise unrelated to each other, that individual has 2<sup><i>n</i></sup> ancestors in the <i>n</i>th generation before him and a total of 2<sup><i>g</i>+1</sup>&#160;−&#160;2 ancestors in the <i>g</i> generations before him.  In practice, however, it is clear that most ancestors of humans (and any other species) are multiply related (see <a href="/wiki/Pedigree_collapse" title="Pedigree collapse">pedigree collapse</a>).  Consider <i>n</i>&#160;=&#160;40: the human species is mors, both living and dead; in contrast, some more youth-oriented cultural contexts display less veneration of elders. In other cultural contexts, some people seek <a href="/wiki/Divine_providence" title="Divine providence">providence</a> from their deceased ancestors; this practice is sometimes known as <i><a href="/wiki/Ancestor_worship" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancestor worship">ancestor worship</a></i> or, more accurately, <i><a href="/wiki/Ancestor_veneration" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancestor veneration">ancestor veneration</a></i>.
</p>
