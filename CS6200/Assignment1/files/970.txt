<p>A <b>symphony</b> is an extended <a href="/wiki/Musical_composition" title="Musical composition">musical composition</a> in Western <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">classical music</a>, most often written by <a href="/wiki/Composer" title="Composer">composers</a> for <a href="/wiki/Orchestra" title="Orchestra">orchestra</a>. Although the term has had many meanings from its origins in the ancient Greek era, by the late 18th century the word had taken on the meaning common today: a work usually consisting of multiple distinct sections or <a href="/wiki/Movement_(music)" title="Movement (music)">movements</a>, often four, with the first movement in <a href="/wiki/Sonata_form" title="Sonata form">sonata form</a>. Symphonies are almost always scored for an orchestra consisting of a <a href="/wiki/String_section" title="String section">string section</a> (<a href="/wiki/Violin" title="Violin">violin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Viola" title="Viola">viola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cello" title="Cello">cello</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Double_bass" title="Double bass">double bass</a>), <a href="/wiki/Brass_instrument" title="Brass instrument">brass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Woodwind_instrument" title="Woodwind instrument">woodwind</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Percussion_instrument" title="Percussion instrument">percussion</a> <a href="/wiki/Musical_instrument" title="Musical instrument">instruments</a> which altogether number about 30 to 100 musicians. Symphonies are notated in a <a href="/wiki/Full_score" class="mw-redirect" title="Full score">musical score</a>, which contains all the instrument parts. Orchestral musicians play from parts which contain just the notated music for their own instrument. Some symphonies also contain vocal parts (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Beethoven</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._9_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 9 (Beethoven)">Ninth Symphony</a>).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Symphony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <i>symphony</i> is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> word <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">συμφωνία</span> (<i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek-language romanization">symphonia</i>), meaning "agreement or concord of sound", "concert of vocal or instrumental music", from <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">σύμφωνος</span> (<i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek-language romanization">symphōnos</i>), "harmonious".<sup id="cite_ref-OED_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The word referred to a variety of different concepts before ultimately settling on its current meaning designating a musical form.
</p><p>In late Greek and medieval theory, the word was used for <a href="/wiki/Consonance_and_dissonance" title="Consonance and dissonance">consonance</a>, as opposed to <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">διαφωνία</span> (<i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek-language romanization">diaphōnia</i>), which was the word for "dissonance".<sup id="cite_ref-Brown2001_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2001-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In the Middle Ages and later, the Latin form <i>symphonia</i> was used to describe various instruments, especially those capable of producing more than one sound simultaneously.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown2001_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2001-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Isidore_of_Seville" title="Isidore of Seville">Isidore of Seville</a> was the first to use the word symphonia as the name of a two-headed drum, and from c. 1155 to 1377 the French form <i>symphonie</i> was the name of the <i>organistrum</i> or <a href="/wiki/Hurdy-gurdy" title="Hurdy-gurdy">hurdy-gurdy</a>. In late medieval England, <i>symphony</i> was used in both of these senses, whereas by the 16th century it was equated with the <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_dulcimer" title="Appalachian dulcimer">dulcimer</a>. In German, <i>Symphonie</i> was a generic term for <a href="/wiki/Spinet" title="Spinet">spinets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Virginals" title="Virginals">virginals</a> from the late 16th century to the 18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarcuse1975501_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarcuse1975501-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the sense of "sounding together," the word begins to appear in the titles of some works by 16th- and 17th-century composers including <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Gabrieli" title="Giovanni Gabrieli">Giovanni Gabrieli</a>'s <i>Sacrae symphoniae</i>, and <i>Symphoniae sacrae, liber secundus</i>, published in 1597 and 1615, respectively; <a href="/wiki/Adriano_Banchieri" title="Adriano Banchieri">Adriano Banchieri</a>'s <i>Eclesiastiche sinfonie, dette canzoni in aria francese, per sonare, et cantare</i>, op. 16, published in 1607; <a href="/wiki/Lodovico_Grossi_da_Viadana" title="Lodovico Grossi da Viadana">Lodovico Grossi da Viadana</a>'s <i>Sinfonie musicali</i>, op. 18, published in 1610; and <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Sch%C3%BCtz" title="Heinrich Schütz">Heinrich Schütz</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Symphoniae_sacrae_I" title="Symphoniae sacrae I">Symphoniae sacrae</a></i>, op. 6, and <i>Symphoniarum sacrarum secunda pars</i>, op. 10, published in 1629 and 1647, respectively. Except for Viadana's collection, which contained purely instrumental and secular music, these were all collections of sacred vocal works, some with instrumental accompaniment.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBowman19717_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBowman19717-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELaRue,_Bonds,_Walsh,_and_Wilson2001_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELaRue,_Bonds,_Walsh,_and_Wilson2001-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 17th century, for most of the Baroque period, the terms <i>symphony</i> and <i>sinfonia</i> were used for a range of different compositions, including instrumental pieces used in <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">operas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sonata" title="Sonata">sonatas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Concerto" title="Concerto">concertos</a>—usually part of a larger work. The <i>opera sinfonia</i>, or <i>Italian <a href="/wiki/Overture" title="Overture">overture</a></i> had, by the 18th century, a standard structure of three contrasting movements: fast, slow, fast and dance-like. It is this form that is often considered as the direct forerunner of the orchestral symphony. The terms "overture", "symphony" and "sinfonia" were widely regarded as interchangeable for much of the 18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELaRue,_Bonds,_Walsh,_and_Wilson2001_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELaRue,_Bonds,_Walsh,_and_Wilson2001-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 17th century, pieces scored for large instrumental ensemble did not precisely designate which instruments were to play which parts, as is the practice from the 19th century to the current period. When composers from the 17th century wrote pieces, they expected that these works would be performed by whatever group of musicians were available. To give one example, whereas the <a href="/wiki/Bassline" title="Bassline">bassline</a> in a 19th-century work is scored for <a href="/wiki/Cello" title="Cello">cellos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Double_bass" title="Double bass">double basses</a> and other specific instruments, in a 17th-century work, a <a href="/wiki/Basso_continuo" class="mw-redirect" title="Basso continuo">basso continuo</a> part for a sinfonia would not specify which instruments would play the part. A performance of the piece might be done with a basso continuo group as small as a single cello and <a href="/wiki/Harpsichord" title="Harpsichord">harpsichord</a>. However, if a bigger budget was available for a performance and a larger sound was required, a basso continuo group might include multiple chord-playing instruments (harpsichord, <a href="/wiki/Lute" title="Lute">lute</a>, etc.) and a range of bass instruments, including cello, double bass, bass viol or even a <a href="/wiki/Serpent_(instrument)" title="Serpent (instrument)">serpent</a>, an early bass woodwind instrument.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="18th_century">18th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Symphony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 18th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>LaRue, Bonds, Walsh, and Wilson write in the second edition of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Grove_Dictionary_of_Music_and_Musicians" title="The New Grove Dictionary of Music and Musicians">The New Grove Dictionary of Music and Musicians</a></i> that "the symphony was cultivated with extraordinary intensity" in the 18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELaRue,_Bonds,_Walsh,_and_Wilson2001§I.2,_citing_two_scholarly_catalogs_listing_over_13,000_distinct_works:_&#91;&#91;#CITEREFLaRue1959&#124;LaRue_1959&#93;&#93;_and_&#91;&#91;#CITEREFLaRue1988&#124;LaRue_1988&#93;&#93;_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELaRue,_Bonds,_Walsh,_and_Wilson2001§I.2,_citing_two_scholarly_catalogs_listing_over_13,000_distinct_works:_[[#CITEREFLaRue1959|LaRue_1959]]_and_[[#CITEREFLaRue1988|LaRue_1988]]-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It played a role in many areas of public life, including church services,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELaRue,_Bonds,_Walsh,_and_Wilson2001§I.2_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELaRue,_Bonds,_Walsh,_and_Wilson2001§I.2-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> but a particularly strong area of support for symphonic performances was the aristocracy. In Vienna, perhaps the most important location in Europe for the composition of symphonies, "literally hundreds of noble families supported musical establishments, generally dividing their time between Vienna and their ancestral estate [elsewhere in the Empire]". <sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELaRue,_Bonds,_Walsh,_and_Wilson2001§I.10_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELaRue,_Bonds,_Walsh,_and_Wilson2001§I.10-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Since the normal size of the orchestra at the time was quite small, many of these courtly establishments were capable of performing symphonies. The young <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Haydn" title="Joseph Haydn">Joseph Haydn</a>, taking up his first job as a music director in 1757 for the <a href="/wiki/Count_Morzin" title="Count Morzin">Morzin family</a>, found that when the Morzin household was in Vienna, his own orchestra was only part of a lively and competitive musical scene, with multiple aristocrats sponsoring concerts with their own ensembles.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>LaRue, Bonds, Walsh, and Wilson's article traces the gradual expansion of the symphonic orchestra through the 18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELaRue,_Bonds,_Walsh,_and_Wilson2001§I.4_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELaRue,_Bonds,_Walsh,_and_Wilson2001§I.4-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> At first, symphonies were string symphonies, written in just four parts: first violin, second violin, viola, and bass (the bass line was taken by cello(s), double bass(es) playing the part an octave below, and perhaps also a bassoon). Occasionally the early symphonists even dispensed with the viola part, thus creating three-part symphonies. A basso continuo part including a bassoon together with a <a href="/wiki/Harpsichord" title="Harpsichord">harpsichord</a> or other chording instrument was also possible.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELaRue,_Bonds,_Walsh,_and_Wilson2001§I.4_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELaRue,_Bonds,_Walsh,_and_Wilson2001§I.4-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The first additions to this simple ensemble were a pair of horns, occasionally a pair of oboes, and then both horns and oboes together. Over the century, other instruments were added to the classical orchestra: flutes (sometimes replacing the oboes), separate parts for bassoons, clarinets, and trumpets and timpani. Works varied in their scoring concerning which of these additional instruments were to appear. The full-scale classical orchestra, deployed at the end of the century for the largest-scale symphonies, has the standard string ensemble mentioned above, pairs of winds (flutes, oboes, clarinets, bassoons), a pair of horns, and timpani. A keyboard continuo instrument (harpsichord or <a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a>) remained an option.
</p><p>The "Italian" style of symphony, often used as overture and <a href="/wiki/Entr%27acte" title="Entr&#39;acte">entr'acte</a> in <a href="/wiki/Opera_house" title="Opera house">opera houses</a>, became a standard three-movement form: a fast movement, a slow movement, and another fast movement. Over the course of the 18th century it became the custom to write four-movement symphonies,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> along the lines described in the next paragraph. The three-movement symphony died out slowly; about half of <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Haydn" title="Joseph Haydn">Haydn</a>'s first thirty symphonies are in three movements;<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and for the young <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Mozart</a>, the three-movement symphony was the norm, perhaps under the influence of his friend <a href="/wiki/Johann_Christian_Bach" title="Johann Christian Bach">Johann Christian Bach</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> An outstanding late example of the three-movement Classical symphony is Mozart's <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._38_(Mozart)" title="Symphony No. 38 (Mozart)">"Prague" Symphony</a>, from 1787.
</p><p>The four-movement form that emerged from this evolution was as follows:<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJackson199926_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJackson199926-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStein1979106_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStein1979106-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>an opening <a href="/wiki/Sonata_form" title="Sonata form">sonata</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tempo#Basic_tempo_markings" title="Tempo">allegro</a></li>
<li>a slow movement, such as <a href="/wiki/Tempo#Basic_tempo_markings" title="Tempo">adagio</a></li>
<li>a <a href="/wiki/Minuet" title="Minuet">minuet</a> or <a href="/wiki/Scherzo" title="Scherzo">scherzo</a> with trio</li>
<li>an allegro, <a href="/wiki/Rondo" title="Rondo">rondo</a>, or sonata</li></ol>
<p>Variations on this layout, like changing the order of the middle movements or adding a slow introduction to the first movement, were common. Haydn, Mozart and their contemporaries restricted their use of the four-movement form to orchestral or multi-instrument chamber music such as quartets, though since Beethoven solo sonatas are as often written in four as in three movements.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEProut1895249_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEProut1895249-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The composition of early symphonies was centred on Milan, Vienna, and <a href="/wiki/Mannheim" title="Mannheim">Mannheim</a>. The Milanese school centred around <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Sammartini" title="Giovanni Battista Sammartini">Giovanni Battista Sammartini</a> and included <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Brioschi" title="Antonio Brioschi">Antonio Brioschi</a>, Ferdinando Galimberti and <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Lampugnani" title="Giovanni Battista Lampugnani">Giovanni Battista Lampugnani</a>. Early exponents of the form in Vienna included <a href="/wiki/Georg_Christoph_Wagenseil" title="Georg Christoph Wagenseil">Georg Christoph Wagenseil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wenzel_Raimund_Birck" title="Wenzel Raimund Birck">Wenzel Raimund Birck</a> and <a href="/wiki/Georg_Matthias_Monn" title="Georg Matthias Monn">Georg Matthias Monn</a>, while later significant Viennese composers of symphonies included <a href="/wiki/Johann_Baptist_Wanhal" title="Johann Baptist Wanhal">Johann Baptist Wanhal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Ditters_von_Dittersdorf" title="Carl Ditters von Dittersdorf">Carl Ditters von Dittersdorf</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leopold_Hofmann" title="Leopold Hofmann">Leopold Hofmann</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Mannheim_school" title="Mannheim school">Mannheim school</a> included <a href="/wiki/Johann_Stamitz" title="Johann Stamitz">Johann Stamitz</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnon.n.d._17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnon.n.d.-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The most important symphonists of the latter part of the 18th century are Haydn, who wrote at least 107 symphonies over the course of 36 years,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWebster2001_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWebster2001-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and Mozart, with at least 47 symphonies in 24 years.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEisenSadie2001_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEisenSadie2001-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>At the beginning of the 19th century, <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Beethoven</a> elevated the symphony from an everyday genre produced in large quantities to a supreme form in which composers strove to reach the highest potential of music in just a few works.<sup id="cite_ref-Dahlhaus1989_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dahlhaus1989-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Beethoven began with two works directly emulating his models Mozart and Haydn, then seven more symphonies, starting with the <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._3_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 3 (Beethoven)">Third Symphony</a> ("Eroica") that expanded the scope and ambition of the genre. His <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._5_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 5 (Beethoven)">Symphony No. 5</a> is perhaps the most famous symphony ever written; its transition from the emotionally stormy <a href="/wiki/Beethoven_and_C_minor" title="Beethoven and C minor">C minor</a> opening movement to a triumphant major-key finale provided a model adopted by later symphonists such as <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Brahms" title="Johannes Brahms">Brahms</a><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELibbey199940_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELibbey199940-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Mahler" title="Gustav Mahler">Mahler</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> His <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._6_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 6 (Beethoven)">Symphony No. 6</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Program_music" title="Program music">programmatic</a> work, featuring instrumental imitations of bird calls and a storm; and, unconventionally, a fifth movement (symphonies usually had at most four movements). His <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._9_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 9 (Beethoven)">Symphony No. 9</a> includes parts for vocal soloists and choir in the last movement, making it a <a href="/wiki/Choral_symphony" title="Choral symphony">choral symphony</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Of the symphonies of <a href="/wiki/Franz_Schubert" title="Franz Schubert">Franz Schubert</a>, two are core repertory items and are frequently performed. Of the <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._8_(Schubert)" title="Symphony No. 8 (Schubert)">Eighth Symphony</a> (1822), Schubert completed only the first two movements; this highly Romantic work is usually called by its nickname "The Unfinished". His last completed symphony, the <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._9_(Schubert)" title="Symphony No. 9 (Schubert)">Ninth</a> (1826) is a massive work in the Classical idiom.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Of the early Romantics, <a href="/wiki/Felix_Mendelssohn" title="Felix Mendelssohn">Felix Mendelssohn</a> (five symphonies) and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Schumann" title="Robert Schumann">Robert Schumann</a> (four) continued to write symphonies in the classical mold, though using their own musical language. In contrast, <a href="/wiki/Hector_Berlioz" title="Hector Berlioz">Hector Berlioz</a> favored programmatic works, including his "dramatic symphony" <i><a href="/wiki/Rom%C3%A9o_et_Juliette_(Berlioz)" title="Roméo et Juliette (Berlioz)">Roméo et Juliette</a></i> and the highly original <i><a href="/wiki/Symphonie_fantastique" title="Symphonie fantastique">Symphonie fantastique</a></i>. The latter is also a programme work and has both a march and a <a href="/wiki/Waltz" title="Waltz">waltz</a> and five movements instead of the customary four. His fourth and last symphony, the <i><a href="/wiki/Grande_symphonie_fun%C3%A8bre_et_triomphale" title="Grande symphonie funèbre et triomphale">Grande symphonie funèbre et triomphale</a></i> (originally titled <i>Symphonie militaire</i>) was composed in 1840 for a 200-piece <a href="/wiki/Marching_band" title="Marching band">marching</a> <a href="/wiki/Military_band" title="Military band">military band</a>, to be performed out of doors, and is an early example of a band symphony. Berlioz later added optional string parts and a choral finale.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacdonald2001§3:_1831–42_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacdonald2001§3:_1831–42-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In 1851, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a> declared that all of these post-Beethoven symphonies were no more than an epilogue, offering nothing substantially new. Indeed, after Schumann's last symphony, the "Rhenish" composed in 1850, for two decades the <a href="/wiki/Franz_Liszt" title="Franz Liszt">Lisztian</a> <a href="/wiki/Symphonic_poem" title="Symphonic poem">symphonic poem</a> appeared to have displaced the symphony as the leading form of large-scale instrumental music. If the symphony had been eclipsed, it was not long before it re-emerged in a "second age" in the 1870s and 1880s, with the symphonies of <a href="/wiki/Anton_Bruckner" title="Anton Bruckner">Anton Bruckner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Brahms" title="Johannes Brahms">Johannes Brahms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pyotr_Ilyich_Tchaikovsky" title="Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky">Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Camille_Saint-Sa%C3%ABns" title="Camille Saint-Saëns">Camille Saint-Saëns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Borodin" title="Alexander Borodin">Alexander Borodin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anton%C3%ADn_Dvo%C5%99%C3%A1k" title="Antonín Dvořák">Antonín Dvořák</a>, and <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_Franck" title="César Franck">César Franck</a>—works which continued to dominate the concert repertory for at least a century.<sup id="cite_ref-Dahlhaus1989_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dahlhaus1989-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Over the course of the 19th century, composers continued to add to the size of the symphonic orchestra. Around the beginning of the century, a full-scale orchestra would consist of the string section plus pairs of flutes, oboes, clarinets, bassoons, horns, trumpets, and lastly a set of timpani.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELaRue,_Bonds,_Walsh,_and_Wilson2001II.1_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELaRue,_Bonds,_Walsh,_and_Wilson2001II.1-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> This is, for instance, the scoring used in Beethoven's symphonies <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._1_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 1 (Beethoven)">numbered 1</a>, <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._2_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 2 (Beethoven)">2</a>, <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._4_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 4 (Beethoven)">4</a>, <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._7_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 7 (Beethoven)">7</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._8_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 8 (Beethoven)">8</a>. Trombones, which had previously been confined to church and theater music, came to be added to the symphonic orchestra, notably in Beethoven's <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._5_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 5 (Beethoven)">5th</a>, <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._6_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 6 (Beethoven)">6th</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._9_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 9 (Beethoven)">9th</a> symphonies. The combination of bass drum, triangle, and cymbals (sometimes also: piccolo), which 18th century composers employed as a coloristic effect in so-called "<a href="/wiki/Turkish_music_(style)" title="Turkish music (style)">Turkish music</a>", came to be increasingly used during the second half of the 19th century without any such connotations of genre.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELaRue,_Bonds,_Walsh,_and_Wilson2001II.1_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELaRue,_Bonds,_Walsh,_and_Wilson2001II.1-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> By the time of Mahler (see below), it was possible for a composer to write a symphony scored for "a veritable compendium of orchestral instruments".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELaRue,_Bonds,_Walsh,_and_Wilson2001II.1_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELaRue,_Bonds,_Walsh,_and_Wilson2001II.1-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In addition to increasing in variety of instruments, 19th century symphonies were gradually augmented with more string players and more wind parts, so that the orchestra grew substantially in sheer numbers, as concert halls likewise grew.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELaRue,_Bonds,_Walsh,_and_Wilson2001II.1_25-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELaRue,_Bonds,_Walsh,_and_Wilson2001II.1-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_and_21st_centuries">20th and 21st centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Symphony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 20th and 21st centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the beginning of the 20th century, <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Mahler" title="Gustav Mahler">Gustav Mahler</a> wrote long, large-scale symphonies. His <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._8_(Mahler)" title="Symphony No. 8 (Mahler)">Eighth Symphony</a>, for example, was composed in 1906 and is nicknamed the "Symphony of a Thousand" because of the large number of voices required to perform the work. Additionally, his <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._3_(Mahler)" title="Symphony No. 3 (Mahler)">Third Symphony</a> is one of the longest regularly performed symphonies at around 100 minutes in length for most performances. The 20th century also saw further diversification in the style and content of works that composers labeled <i>symphonies</i> (Anon. 2008). Some composers, including Dmitri Shostakovich, <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Rachmaninoff" title="Sergei Rachmaninoff">Sergei Rachmaninoff</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carl_Nielsen" title="Carl Nielsen">Carl Nielsen</a>, continued to write in the traditional four-movement form, while other composers took different approaches: <a href="/wiki/Jean_Sibelius" title="Jean Sibelius">Jean Sibelius</a>' <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._7_(Sibelius)" title="Symphony No. 7 (Sibelius)">Symphony No. 7</a>, his last, is in one movement, whereas <a href="/wiki/Alan_Hovhaness" title="Alan Hovhaness">Alan Hovhaness</a>'s Symphony No. 9, <i>Saint Vartan</i>—originally op. 80, changed to op. 180—composed in 1949–50, is in twenty-four.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETawa2001352_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETawa2001352-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A concern with unification of the traditional four-movement symphony into a single, subsuming formal conception had emerged in the late 19th century. This has been called a "two-dimensional symphonic form", and finds its key turning point in <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Schoenberg" title="Arnold Schoenberg">Arnold Schoenberg</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Chamber_Symphony_No._1" class="mw-redirect" title="Chamber Symphony No. 1">Chamber Symphony No. 1</a>, Op. 9 (1909), which was followed in the 1920s by other notable single-movement German symphonies, including <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Weill" title="Kurt Weill">Kurt Weill</a>'s First Symphony (1921), <a href="/wiki/Max_Butting" title="Max Butting">Max Butting</a>'s Chamber Symphony, Op. 25 (1923), and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Dessau" title="Paul Dessau">Paul Dessau</a>'s 1926 Symphony.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVande_Moortele2013269,_284n9_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVande_Moortele2013269,_284n9-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There remained, however, certain tendencies. Designating a work a "symphony" still implied a degree of sophistication and seriousness of purpose. The word <i><a href="/wiki/Sinfonietta_(symphony)" title="Sinfonietta (symphony)">sinfonietta</a></i> came into use to designate a work that is shorter, of more modest aims, or "lighter" than a symphony, such as <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Prokofiev" title="Sergei Prokofiev">Sergei Prokofiev</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Sinfonietta_(Prokofiev)" title="Sinfonietta (Prokofiev)">Sinfonietta</a> for <a href="/wiki/Orchestra" title="Orchestra">orchestra</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy2006a_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy2006a-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETemperley2001_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETemperley2001-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the first half of the century, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Elgar" title="Edward Elgar">Edward Elgar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Mahler" title="Gustav Mahler">Gustav Mahler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean_Sibelius" title="Jean Sibelius">Jean Sibelius</a>, Carl Nielsen, <a href="/wiki/Igor_Stravinsky" title="Igor Stravinsky">Igor Stravinsky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bohuslav_Martin%C5%AF" title="Bohuslav Martinů">Bohuslav Martinů</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Sessions" title="Roger Sessions">Roger Sessions</a>, Sergei Prokofiev, <a href="/wiki/Rued_Langgaard" title="Rued Langgaard">Rued Langgaard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Shostakovich" title="Dmitri Shostakovich">Dmitri Shostakovich</a> composed symphonies "extraordinary in scope, richness, originality, and urgency of expression".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESteinberg1995404_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESteinberg1995404-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> One measure of the significance of a symphony is the degree to which it reflects conceptions of temporal form particular to the age in which it was created. Five composers from across the span of the 20th century who fulfil this measure are Jean Sibelius, Igor Stravinsky, <a href="/wiki/Luciano_Berio" title="Luciano Berio">Luciano Berio</a> (in his <a href="/wiki/Sinfonia_(Berio)" title="Sinfonia (Berio)">Sinfonia</a>, 1968–69), <a href="/wiki/Elliott_Carter" title="Elliott Carter">Elliott Carter</a> (in his <i>Symphony of Three Orchestras</i>, 1976), and <a href="/wiki/Pelle_Gudmundsen-Holmgreen" title="Pelle Gudmundsen-Holmgreen">Pelle Gudmundsen-Holmgreen</a> (in <i>Symphony/Antiphony</i>, 1980).<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From the mid-20th century into the 21st there has been a resurgence of interest in the symphony with many composers adding substantially to the canon, not least in the United Kingdom: <a href="/wiki/Peter_Maxwell_Davies" title="Peter Maxwell Davies">Peter Maxwell Davies</a> (10),<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robin_Holloway" title="Robin Holloway">Robin Holloway</a> (1),<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/David_Matthews_(composer)" title="David Matthews (composer)">David Matthews</a> (9),<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/James_MacMillan" title="James MacMillan">James MacMillan</a> (4),<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Seabourne" title="Peter Seabourne">Peter Seabourne</a> (4),<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> and Philip Sawyers (3).<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>.
</p><p>Beginning in the 20th century, more symphonies have been written for <a href="/wiki/Concert_band" title="Concert band">concert band</a> than in past centuries. Although examples exist from as early as 1932, the first such symphony of importance since <a href="/wiki/Hector_Berlioz" title="Hector Berlioz">Hector Berlioz</a> wrote the <i><a href="/wiki/Grande_symphonie_fun%C3%A8bre_et_triomphale" title="Grande symphonie funèbre et triomphale">Grande symphonie funèbre et triomphale</a></i> in 1840 is <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Myaskovsky" title="Nikolai Myaskovsky">Nikolai Myaskovsky</a>'s Symphony No. 19, Op. 46, composed in 1939 (Battisti 2002, 42). Some further examples are <a href="/wiki/Paul_Hindemith" title="Paul Hindemith">Paul Hindemith</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Symphony_in_B-flat_for_Band_(Hindemith)" title="Symphony in B-flat for Band (Hindemith)">Symphony in B-flat for Band</a>, composed in 1951<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>; <a href="/wiki/Morton_Gould" title="Morton Gould">Morton Gould</a>'s Symphony No. 4 "West Point", composed in 1952; <a href="/wiki/Vincent_Persichetti" title="Vincent Persichetti">Vincent Persichetti</a>'s Symphony No. 6, Op. 69, composed in 1956; <a href="/wiki/Vittorio_Giannini" title="Vittorio Giannini">Vittorio Giannini</a>'s Symphony No.3, composed in 1959<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>; Alan Hovhaness's Symphonies No. 4, op.&#160;165, No.&#160;7, "Nanga Parvat", op.&#160;175, No.&#160;14, "Ararat", op.&#160;194, and No.&#160;23, "Ani", op.&#160;249, composed in 1958, 1959, 1961, and 1972 respectively<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>; <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Reed" title="Alfred Reed">Alfred Reed</a>'s 2nd, 3rd, 4th, and 5th symphonies, composed in 1979, 1988, 1992, and 1994 respectively<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>; and <a href="/wiki/Johan_de_Meij" title="Johan de Meij">Johan de Meij</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._1_%22The_Lord_of_the_Rings%22" title="Symphony No. 1 &quot;The Lord of the Rings&quot;">Symphony No. 1 "The Lord of the Rings"</a>, composed in 1988, and his Symphony No. 2 "The Big Apple", composed in 1993<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>.
</p>
<h2><span id="Other_modern_usages_of_.22symphony.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_modern_usages_of_&quot;symphony&quot;">Other modern usages of "symphony"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Symphony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other modern usages of &quot;symphony&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In some forms of English, for example <a href="/wiki/U.S._English" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. English">U.S. English</a> the word "symphony" is also used to refer to the <a href="/wiki/Orchestra" title="Orchestra">orchestra</a>, the large ensemble that often performs these works. The word "symphony" appears in the name of many orchestras, for example, the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Symphony_Orchestra" title="Boston Symphony Orchestra">Boston Symphony Orchestra</a>, the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Symphony" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Louis Symphony">St. Louis Symphony</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Symphony" title="Houston Symphony">Houston Symphony</a>, or Miami's <a href="/wiki/New_World_Symphony_(orchestra)" title="New World Symphony (orchestra)">New World Symphony</a>. For some orchestras, "(city name) Symphony" provides a shorter version of the full name; for instance, the <a href="/wiki/OED" class="mw-redirect" title="OED">OED</a> gives "Vancouver Symphony" as a possible abbreviated form of <a href="/wiki/Vancouver_Symphony_Orchestra" title="Vancouver Symphony Orchestra">Vancouver Symphony Orchestra</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOEDdefinition_5d_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOEDdefinition_5d-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, in common usage, a person may say they are going out to hear a symphony perform, a reference to the orchestra and not the works on the program. These usages are not common in <a href="/wiki/U.K._English" class="mw-redirect" title="U.K. English">U.K. English</a>.
</p>
