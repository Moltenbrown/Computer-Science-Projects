	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">String quartet</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Musical ensemble of four string players</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:272px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ensemble_layout_-_string_quartet.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Ensemble_layout_-_string_quartet.png/270px-Ensemble_layout_-_string_quartet.png" decoding="async" width="270" height="102" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Ensemble_layout_-_string_quartet.png/405px-Ensemble_layout_-_string_quartet.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Ensemble_layout_-_string_quartet.png/540px-Ensemble_layout_-_string_quartet.png 2x" data-file-width="696" data-file-height="264" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ensemble_layout_-_string_quartet.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Typical Ensemble layout</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>string quartet</b> refers to (a) a <a href="/wiki/Musical_ensemble" title="Musical ensemble">musical ensemble</a> consisting of four <a href="/wiki/String_instrument" title="String instrument">string</a> players – two <a href="/wiki/Violin" title="Violin">violin</a> players, a <a href="/wiki/Viola" title="Viola">viola</a> player and a <a href="/wiki/Cellist" class="mw-redirect" title="Cellist">cellist</a> – or (b) a <a href="/wiki/Musical_composition" title="Musical composition">piece</a> written to be performed by such a group. The string quartet is one of the most prominent <a href="/wiki/Chamber_music" title="Chamber music">chamber</a> ensembles in <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">classical music</a>, with most major composers, from the mid 18th century onwards, writing string quartets.
</p><p>The string quartet was developed into its current form by the Austrian composer <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Haydn" title="Joseph Haydn">Joseph Haydn</a>, with his works in the 1750s establishing the genre. Ever since Haydn's day the string quartet has been considered a prestigious form and represents one of the true tests of the composer's art. With four parts to play with, a composer working in anything like the classical key system has enough lines to fashion a full argument, but none to spare for padding. The closely related characters of the four instruments, moreover, while they cover in combination an ample compass of pitch, do not lend themselves to indulgence in purely colouristic effects. Thus, where composers of symphonies command the means for textural enrichment beyond the call of their harmonic discourse, and where the concerto medium offers the further resource of personal characterization and drama in the individual-pitted-against-the-mass vein, writers of string quartets must perforce concentrate on the bare bones of musical logic. Thus, in many ways the string quartet is pre-eminently the dialectical form of instrumental music, the one most naturally suited to the activity of logical disputation and philosophical enquiry.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Quartet composition flourished in the <a href="/wiki/Classical_music_era" class="mw-redirect" title="Classical music era">Classical</a> era, with <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Ludwig van Beethoven</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franz_Schubert" title="Franz Schubert">Franz Schubert</a> following Haydn in each writing a number of quartets. A slight slackening in the pace of quartet composition occurred in the later 19th century, in part due to a movement away from classical forms by composers such as <a href="/wiki/Franz_Liszt" title="Franz Liszt">Franz Liszt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Strauss" title="Richard Strauss">Richard Strauss</a>.  Then it received a resurgence in the 20th Century with the <a href="/wiki/Second_Viennese_School" title="Second Viennese School">Second Viennese School</a>, <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9la_Bart%C3%B3k" title="Béla Bartók">Béla Bartók</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Shostakovich" title="Dmitri Shostakovich">Dmitri Shostakovich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milton_Babbitt" title="Milton Babbitt">Milton Babbitt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elliott_Carter" title="Elliott Carter">Elliott Carter</a> producing highly regarded examples of the genre. In the 21st century it remains an important and refined musical form.
</p><p>The standard structure for a string quartet as established in the Classical era is four movements, with the first movement in <a href="/wiki/Sonata_form" title="Sonata form">Sonata form</a>, Allegro, in the <a href="/wiki/Tonic_(music)" title="Tonic (music)">tonic</a> key; second movement is a slow movement, in a related key; third movement is a Minuet and Trio, in the tonic key; and the fourth movement is often in <a href="/wiki/Rondo_form" class="mw-redirect" title="Rondo form">Rondo form</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sonata_rondo_form" title="Sonata rondo form">Sonata rondo form</a>, in the tonic key.
</p><p>Some quartets play together for many years in ensembles which may be named after the first violinist (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Tak%C3%A1cs_Quartet" title="Takács Quartet">Takács Quartet</a>), a composer (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Borodin_Quartet" title="Borodin Quartet">Borodin Quartet</a>) or a location (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Budapest_String_Quartet" title="Budapest String Quartet">Budapest Quartet</a>). Some have fanciful names such as the <a href="/wiki/Kronos_Quartet" title="Kronos Quartet">Kronos Quartet</a> or the <a href="/wiki/JACK_Quartet" title="JACK Quartet">JACK Quartet</a>. Well-known string quartets can be found in the <a href="/wiki/List_of_string_quartet_ensembles" title="List of string quartet ensembles">list of string quartet ensembles</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_development">History and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=String_quartet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The early history of the string quartet is in many ways the history of Haydn's journey with the genre. Not that he composed the first quartet of all: before Haydn alighted on the genre there had been several spasmodic examples of divertimenti for two solo violins, viola and cello by Viennese composers such as <a href="/wiki/Georg_Christoph_Wagenseil" title="Georg Christoph Wagenseil">Georg Christoph Wagenseil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ignaz_Holzbauer" title="Ignaz Holzbauer">Ignaz Holzbauer</a>; and there had long been a tradition of performing orchestral works with one instrument to a part. <a href="/wiki/David_Wyn_Jones" title="David Wyn Jones">David Wyn Jones</a> cites the widespread practice of playing works written for <a href="/wiki/String_orchestra" title="String orchestra">string orchestra</a>, such as divertimenti and serenades, with just four players, one to a part, there being no separate (fifth) contrabass part in string scoring before the 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWyn_Jones2003179_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWyn_Jones2003179-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> However, these composers showed no interest in exploring the development of the string quartet as a medium.
</p><p>The origins of the string quartet can be further traced back to the <a href="/wiki/Baroque_music" title="Baroque music">Baroque</a> <a href="/wiki/Trio_sonata" title="Trio sonata">trio sonata</a>, in which two <a href="/wiki/Solo_(music)" title="Solo (music)">solo</a> instruments performed with a <a href="/wiki/Basso_continuo" class="mw-redirect" title="Basso continuo">continuo</a> section consisting of a <a href="/wiki/Bass_instrument" class="mw-redirect" title="Bass instrument">bass instrument</a> (such as the cello) and <a href="/wiki/Keyboard_instrument" title="Keyboard instrument">keyboard</a>. A very early example is a four-part sonata for string ensemble by <a href="/wiki/Gregorio_Allegri" title="Gregorio Allegri">Gregorio Allegri</a> (1582–1652) that might be considered an important prototype.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  By the early 18th century, composers were often adding a third soloist; and moreover it became common to omit the keyboard part, letting the cello support the bass line alone. Thus when <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Scarlatti" title="Alessandro Scarlatti">Alessandro Scarlatti</a> wrote a set of six works entitled "Sonata à Quattro per due Violini, Violetta [viola], e Violoncello senza Cembalo" (Sonata for four instruments: two violins, viola, and cello without harpsichord), this was a natural evolution from the existing tradition.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWyn_Jones2003178_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWyn_Jones2003178-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The string quartet in its now accepted form came about with Haydn. If the combination of two violins, viola and cello was not unknown before Haydn, when it occurred in chamber music it was more likely through circumstance than conscious design; certainly the string quartet enjoyed no recognized status as an ensemble in the way that two violins with basso continuo – the so-called 'trio sonata' – had for more than a hundred years. Even the composition of Haydn's earliest string quartets owed more to chance than artistic imperative.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFinscher2000398_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFinscher2000398-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> During the 1750s, when the young composer was still working mainly as a teacher and violinist in Vienna, he would occasionally be invited to spend time at the nearby <a href="/wiki/Weinzierl_Castle" title="Weinzierl Castle">castle at Weinzierl</a> of the music-loving Austrian nobleman  <a href="/w/index.php?title=Karl_Joseph_Weber,_Edler_von_F%C3%BCrnberg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Karl Joseph Weber, Edler von Fürnberg (page does not exist)">Karl Joseph Weber, Edler von Fürnberg</a>. There he would play chamber music in an <i>ad hoc</i> ensemble consisting of Fürnberg's steward, a priest and a local cellist, and when the Baron asked for some new music for the group to play, Haydn's first string quartets were born. It is not clear whether any of these works ended up in the two sets published in the mid-1760s and known as Haydn's Opp.1 and 2 ('Op.0' is a quartet included in some early editions of Op.1, and only rediscovered in the 1930s), but it seems reasonable to assume that they were at least similar in character.
</p><p>Haydn's early biographer <a href="/wiki/Georg_August_Griesinger" title="Georg August Griesinger">Georg August Griesinger</a> tells the story thus:
</p>
<blockquote><p>The following purely chance circumstance had led him to try his luck at the composition of quartets. A Baron Fürnberg had a place in <a href="/wiki/Weinzierl_Castle" title="Weinzierl Castle">Weinzierl</a>, several stages from Vienna, and he invited from time to time his pastor, his manager, Haydn, and Albrechtsberger (a brother of the celebrated <a href="/wiki/Contrapuntist" class="mw-redirect" title="Contrapuntist">contrapuntist</a> <a href="/wiki/Johann_Georg_Albrechtsberger" title="Johann Georg Albrechtsberger">Albrechtsberger</a>) in order to have a little music. Fürnberg requested Haydn to compose something that could be performed by these four amateurs. Haydn, then eighteen years old,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> took up this proposal, and so originated his first quartet which, immediately it appeared, received such general approval that Haydn took courage to work further in this form.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGriesinger1810/196313_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGriesinger1810/196313-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Haydn went on to write nine other quartets around this time. These works were published as his Op. 1 and Op. 2; one quartet went unpublished, and some of the early "quartets" are actually symphonies missing their wind parts. They have five movements and take the form: fast movement, <a href="/wiki/Minuet_and_trio" class="mw-redirect" title="Minuet and trio">minuet and trio</a> I, slow movement, minuet and trio II, and fast <a href="/wiki/Finale_(music)" title="Finale (music)">finale</a>. As Ludwig Finscher notes, they draw stylistically on the Austrian <a href="/wiki/Divertimento" title="Divertimento">divertimento</a> tradition.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFinscher2000398_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFinscher2000398-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After these early efforts Haydn did not return to the string quartet for several years, but when he did so, it was to make a significant step in the genre's development. The intervening years saw Haydn begin his lifelong employment as <a href="/wiki/Kapellmeister" title="Kapellmeister">Kapellmeister</a> to the Esterhazy princes, for whom he was required to compose numerous symphonies and dozens of trios for violin, viola and the curious bass instrument called the <a href="/wiki/Baryton" title="Baryton">baryton</a> (played by Prince <a href="/wiki/Nikolaus_Esterhazy" class="mw-redirect" title="Nikolaus Esterhazy">Nikolaus Esterhazy</a> himself). The opportunities for experiment which both these genres offered Haydn perhaps helped him in the pursuit of the more advanced quartet style found in the eighteen worked published in the early 1770s as Opp.9, 17 and 20. These are written in a form that became established as standard both for Haydn and for other composers. Clearly composed as sets, these quartets feature a four-movement layout with more broadly conceived, moderately paced first movements and, in increasing measure, a democratic and conversational interplay of parts, close-knit thematic development, and skilful though often self-effacing use of counterpoint. The convincing realizations of the progressive aims of the <a href="/wiki/String_Quartets,_Op._20_(Haydn)" title="String Quartets, Op. 20 (Haydn)">Op.20</a> set of 1772, in particular, makes them the first major peak in the history of the string quartet.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Certainly they offered to their own time state-of-the art models to follow for the best part of a decade; the teenage <a href="/wiki/Mozart" class="mw-redirect" title="Mozart">Mozart</a>, in his early quartets, was among the composers moved to imitate many of their characteristics, right down to the vital <a href="/wiki/Fugue" title="Fugue">fugues</a> with which Haydn sought to bring greater architectural weight to the finales of nos. 2, 5 and 6.
</p><p>After Op.20 it becomes harder to point to similar major jumps in the string quartet's development in Haydn's hands, though not due to any lack of invention or application on the composer's part. As <a href="/wiki/Donald_Tovey" title="Donald Tovey">Donald Tovey</a> put it: "with Op.20 the historical development of Haydn's quartets reaches its goal; and further progress is not progress in any historical sense, but simply the difference between one masterpiece and the next."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETovey&#91;&#91;Category:Wikipedia_articles_needing_page_number_citations_from_January_2017&#93;&#93;&lt;sup_class=&quot;noprint_Inline-Template_&quot;_style=&quot;white-space:nowrap;&quot;&gt;&amp;#91;&lt;i&gt;&#91;&#91;Wikipedia:Citing_sources&#124;&lt;span_title=&quot;This_citation_requires_a_reference_to_the_specific_page_or_range_of_pages_in_which_the_material_appears.&amp;#32;(January_2017)&quot;&gt;page&amp;nbsp;needed&lt;/span&gt;&#93;&#93;&lt;/i&gt;&amp;#93;&lt;/sup&gt;_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETovey[[Category:Wikipedia_articles_needing_page_number_citations_from_January_2017]]&lt;sup_class=&quot;noprint_Inline-Template_&quot;_style=&quot;white-space:nowrap;&quot;&gt;&amp;#91;&lt;i&gt;[[Wikipedia:Citing_sources|&lt;span_title=&quot;This_citation_requires_a_reference_to_the_specific_page_or_range_of_pages_in_which_the_material_appears.&amp;#32;(January_2017)&quot;&gt;page&amp;nbsp;needed&lt;/span&gt;]]&lt;/i&gt;&amp;#93;&lt;/sup&gt;-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>That Haydn's string quartets were already "classics" that defined the genre by 1801 can be judged by <a href="/wiki/Ignaz_Pleyel" title="Ignaz Pleyel">Ignaz Pleyel</a>'s publication in Paris of a "complete" series that year, and the quartet's evolution as vehicle for public performance can be judged by Pleyel's ten-volume set of <a href="/wiki/Miniature_score" class="mw-redirect" title="Miniature score">miniature scores</a> intended for hearers rather than players, early examples of ths genre of <a href="/wiki/Music_publisher_(popular_music)" title="Music publisher (popular music)">music publishing</a>. Since Haydn's day the string quartet has been prestigious and considered one of the true tests of a composer's art. This may be partly because the palette of sound is more restricted than with <a href="/wiki/Orchestra" title="Orchestra">orchestral</a> music, forcing the music to stand more on its own rather than relying on <a href="/wiki/Timbre" title="Timbre">tonal color</a>; or from the inherently <a href="/wiki/Counterpoint" title="Counterpoint">contrapuntal</a> tendency in music written for four equal instruments.
</p><p>Quartet composition flourished in the <a href="/wiki/Classical_music_era" class="mw-redirect" title="Classical music era">Classical</a> era, with <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Mozart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Beethoven</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franz_Schubert" title="Franz Schubert">Schubert</a> each writing a number of quartets to set alongside Haydn's. "Beethoven in particular is credited with developing the genre in an experimental and dynamic fashion, especially in his later series of quartets written in the 1820s up until his death. Their forms and ideas inspired and continue to inspire musicians and composers, such as <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a> and <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9la_Bart%C3%B3k" title="Béla Bartók">Béla Bartók</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Schubert's last musical wish was to hear <a href="/wiki/String_Quartet_No._14_(Beethoven)" title="String Quartet No. 14 (Beethoven)">Beethoven's Quartet in C<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-sharp">&#x266f;</span></span> minor, Op. 131</a>, which he did on 14 November 1828, just five days before his death. Upon listening to an earlier performance of this quartet, Schubert had remarked, "After this, what is left for us to write?" Wagner, when reflecting on Op. 131's first movement, said that it "reveals the most melancholy sentiment expressed in music". Of the late quartets, Beethoven cited his own favorite as Op. 131, which he saw as his most perfect single work.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/String_Quartets_(Mendelssohn)" title="String Quartets (Mendelssohn)">Mendelssohn's six string quartets</a> span the full range of his career, from 1828 to 1847, but otherwise a slight slackening in the pace of quartet composition occurred after the 30s of the 19th century; <a href="/wiki/String_Quartets_(Schumann)" title="String Quartets (Schumann)">Schumann's three string quartets</a> were all written in 1842 and dedicated to Mendelssohn, whose quartets Schumann had been studying in preparation, along with those of Haydn, Mozart and Beethoven. Other Romantic-era composers often wrote only one quartet, perhaps to show that they could fully command this hallowed genre. <a href="/wiki/Anton%C3%ADn_Dvo%C5%99%C3%A1k" title="Antonín Dvořák">Antonín Dvořák</a> was a notable exception as he wrote a series of 14. With the onset of the Modern era of classical music, the quartet returned to full popularity among composers, and played a key role in the development of <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Schoenberg" title="Arnold Schoenberg">Arnold Schoenberg</a> (who was the first to add a soprano in his <a href="/wiki/String_Quartet_No._2_(Schoenberg)" class="mw-redirect" title="String Quartet No. 2 (Schoenberg)">String Quartet No. 2</a>), <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9la_Bart%C3%B3k" title="Béla Bartók">Béla Bartók</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Shostakovich" title="Dmitri Shostakovich">Dmitri Shostakovich</a> especially. After the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, some composers, such as <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Boulez" title="Pierre Boulez">Pierre Boulez</a> and <a href="/wiki/Olivier_Messiaen" title="Olivier Messiaen">Olivier Messiaen</a> questioned the relevance of the string quartet and avoided writing them. However, from the 1960s onwards, many composers have shown a renewed interest in the genre. During his tenure as <a href="/wiki/Master_of_the_Queen%27s_Music" title="Master of the Queen&#39;s Music">Master of the Queen's Music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Maxwell_Davies" title="Peter Maxwell Davies">Peter Maxwell Davies</a> produced a set of ten entitled the <a href="/wiki/Naxos_Quartets" title="Naxos Quartets">Naxos Quartets</a> (to a commission from <a href="/wiki/Naxos_Records" title="Naxos Records">Naxos Records</a>) from 2001–2007. <a href="/wiki/David_Matthews_(composer)" title="David Matthews (composer)">David Matthews</a> has written eleven, and <a href="/wiki/Robin_Holloway" title="Robin Holloway">Robin Holloway</a> both five quartets and six "quartettini".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="String_quartet_traditional_form">String quartet traditional form</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=String_quartet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: String quartet traditional form">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A composition for four players of stringed instruments may be in any form. Quartets written in the classical period usually have four movements with a large-scale structure similar to that of a <a href="/wiki/Symphony" title="Symphony">symphony</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>:
</p>
<ul><li>First movement: <a href="/wiki/Sonata_form" title="Sonata form">Sonata form</a>, Allegro, in the <a href="/wiki/Tonic_(music)" title="Tonic (music)">tonic</a> key;</li>
<li>Second movement: <a href="/wiki/Slow_movement_(music)" title="Slow movement (music)">Slow movement</a>, in a related key;</li>
<li>Third movement: <a href="/wiki/Minuet" title="Minuet">Minuet</a> and Trio, in the tonic key;</li>
<li>Fourth movement: <a href="/wiki/Rondo_form" class="mw-redirect" title="Rondo form">Rondo form</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sonata_rondo_form" title="Sonata rondo form">Sonata rondo form</a>, in the tonic key.</li></ul>
<p>Substantial modifications to the typical structure were already achieved in <a href="/wiki/Late_String_Quartets_(Beethoven)" class="mw-redirect" title="Late String Quartets (Beethoven)">Beethoven's later quartets</a>, and despite some notable examples to the contrary, composers writing in the twentieth century increasingly abandoned this structure.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations_of_string_quartet">Variations of string quartet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=String_quartet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Variations of string quartet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many other chamber groups can be seen as modifications of the string quartet:
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/String_quintet" title="String quintet">string quintet</a> is a string quartet augmented by a fifth string instrument. <a href="/wiki/Mozart" class="mw-redirect" title="Mozart">Mozart</a> employed two violas in his string quintets, while Schubert's <a href="/wiki/String_Quintet_(Schubert)" title="String Quintet (Schubert)">string quintet</a> utilized two cellos. <a href="/wiki/Luigi_Boccherini" title="Luigi Boccherini">Boccherini</a> wrote a few quintets with a <a href="/wiki/Double_bass" title="Double bass">double bass</a> as the fifth instrument.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/String_trio" title="String trio">string trio</a> has one violin, a viola, and a cello.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Piano_quintet" title="Piano quintet">piano quintet</a> is a string quartet with an added <a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Piano_quartet" title="Piano quartet">piano quartet</a> is a string quartet with one of the violins replaced by a piano.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Clarinet_quintet" title="Clarinet quintet">clarinet quintet</a> is a string quartet with an added <a href="/wiki/Clarinet" title="Clarinet">clarinet</a>, such as those by <a href="/wiki/Clarinet_Quintet_(Mozart)" title="Clarinet Quintet (Mozart)">Mozart</a> and <a href="/wiki/Clarinet_Quintet_(Brahms)" title="Clarinet Quintet (Brahms)">Brahms</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/String_sextet" title="String sextet">string sextet</a> contains two each of violins, violas and cellos.  Brahms, for example, wrote two string sextets.</li></ul>
<p>Further expansions have also been produced such as the <a href="/wiki/Octet_(Mendelssohn)" title="Octet (Mendelssohn)">String octet</a> by <a href="/wiki/Mendelssohn" class="mw-redirect" title="Mendelssohn">Mendelssohn</a>. Notably, <a href="/wiki/Schoenberg" title="Schoenberg">Schoenberg</a> included a <a href="/wiki/Soprano" title="Soprano">soprano</a> in the last two movements of his <a href="/wiki/String_Quartets_(Schoenberg)#String_Quartet_No._2" title="String Quartets (Schoenberg)">second string quartet</a>, composed in 1908. This instrumentation of adding a voice is succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Ginastera" class="mw-redirect" title="Ginastera">Ginastera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Maxwell_Davies" title="Peter Maxwell Davies">Davies</a> and many others. Another variation on the traditional string quartet is the electric string quartet with players performing on <a href="/wiki/Electric_violin" title="Electric violin">electric instruments</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span id="String_quartets_.28ensembles.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="String_quartets_(ensembles)">String quartets (ensembles)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=String_quartet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: String quartets (ensembles)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_string_quartet_ensembles" title="List of string quartet ensembles">List of string quartet ensembles</a></div>
<p>Whereas individual string players often group together to make ad hoc string quartets, others continue to play together for many years in ensembles which may be named after the first violinist (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Tak%C3%A1cs_Quartet" title="Takács Quartet">Takács Quartet</a>), a composer (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Borodin_Quartet" title="Borodin Quartet">Borodin Quartet</a>) or a location (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Budapest_String_Quartet" title="Budapest String Quartet">Budapest Quartet</a>). Established quartets may undergo changes in membership whilst retaining their original name.
</p>
