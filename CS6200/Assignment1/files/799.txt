<p>Beginning from the late eighteenth century, the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Old_Regime" title="Ottoman Old Regime">Ottoman Old Regime</a>) faced challenges defending itself against foreign invasion and occupation. In response to foreign threats, the empire initiated a period of tremendous internal reform which came to be known as the <a href="/wiki/Tanzimat" title="Tanzimat">Tanzimat</a>, which succeeded in significantly strengthening the Ottoman central state, despite the empire's precarious international position. Over the course of the nineteenth century, the Ottoman state became increasingly powerful and rationalized, exercising a greater degree of influence over its population than in any previous era.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The process of reform and modernization in the empire began with the declaration of the <a href="/wiki/Nizam-I_Cedid" title="Nizam-I Cedid">Nizam-I Cedid</a> (New Order) during the reign of Sultan <a href="/wiki/Selim_III" title="Selim III">Selim III</a>  and was punctuated by several reform decrees, such as the <a href="/wiki/Edict_of_G%C3%BClhane" title="Edict of Gülhane">Hatt-ı Şerif of Gülhane</a> in 1839 and the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Reform_Edict_of_1856" title="Ottoman Reform Edict of 1856">Hatt-ı Hümayun</a> in 1856. At the end of this period, marked with 1908, to a degree the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Modern_Army" class="mw-redirect" title="Ottoman Modern Army">Ottoman military became modernized and professionalized</a> according to the model of Western European Armies. The period was followed by the <a href="/wiki/Defeat_and_dissolution_of_the_Ottoman_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Defeat and dissolution of the Ottoman Empire">defeat and dissolution of the Ottoman Empire</a> (1908–1922).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_issues_of_the_period">Main issues of the period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Main issues of the period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <p>The <a href="/wiki/Rise_of_nationalism_under_the_Ottoman_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Rise of nationalism under the Ottoman Empire">rise of nationalism</a> swept through many countries during the 19th century, and it affected territories within the Ottoman Empire. A burgeoning <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">national consciousness</a>, together with a growing sense of <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_nationalism" title="Ethnic nationalism">ethnic nationalism</a>, made nationalistic thought one of the most significant Western ideas imported to the Ottoman Empire. The empire was forced to deal with nationalism from both within and beyond its borders. The number of revolutionary, secret societies which turned into <a href="/wiki/List_of_parties_in_Ottoman_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="List of parties in Ottoman Empire">political parties</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Defeat_and_dissolution_of_the_Ottoman_Empire_(1908%E2%80%931922)" class="mw-redirect" title="Defeat and dissolution of the Ottoman Empire (1908–1922)">next period</a> rose dramatically. Uprisings in Ottoman territory had many far-reaching consequences during the 19th century and determined much of the Ottoman policy during the early 20th century. Many Ottoman ruling elite questioned whether the policies of the state were to blame: some felt that the sources of <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_conflict" title="Ethnic conflict">ethnic conflict</a> were external, and unrelated to issues of governance. While this era was not without some successes, the ability of the Ottoman state to have any effect on ethnic uprisings was seriously called into question.
</p><p>The Russian extension in this century developed with the main theme of supporting independence of Ottomans' former provinces and then bringing all of the Slav peoples of the Balkans under Bulgaria or using Armenians in the east sets the stage. At the end of the century from Russian perspective; Romania, Serbia and Montenegro and autonomy of Bulgaria was achieved. That alarmed the Great Powers. After the Congress of Berlin the Russian expansion was controlled through stopping the expansion of Bulgaria. The Russian public felt that at the end of Congress of Berlin thousands of Russian soldiers had died for nothing.
</p><p>The military of the Ottoman Empire remained an effective fighting force until the second half of the eighteenth century, when it suffered a catastrophic defeat against Russia in the <a href="/wiki/Russo-Turkish_War_(1768%E2%80%9374)" class="mw-redirect" title="Russo-Turkish War (1768–74)">1768-74 war</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Selim_III" title="Selim III">Selim III</a> came to the throne with an ambitious effort in military reforms in 1789. He failed. Selim III was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Mahmud_II" title="Mahmud II">Mahmud II</a> in 1808 who established martial law of <a href="/wiki/Alemdar_Mustafa_Pasha" title="Alemdar Mustafa Pasha">Alemdar Mustafa Pasha</a>. His first task was to ally with the Janissaries in order to break the power of the provincial governors. He then turned on the Janissaries and removed them from power during <a href="/wiki/Auspicious_Incident" title="Auspicious Incident">Auspicious Incident</a> in 1826. Efforts for a new system (1826–1858) began following the Auspicious incident.
</p><p>Economic historian <a href="/wiki/Paul_Bairoch" title="Paul Bairoch">Paul Bairoch</a> argues that <a href="/wiki/Free_trade" title="Free trade">free trade</a> contributed to <a href="/wiki/Deindustrialization" title="Deindustrialization">deindustrialization</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>. In contrast to the <a href="/wiki/Protectionism" title="Protectionism">protectionism</a> of China, Japan, and <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, the Ottoman Empire had a <a href="/wiki/Economic_liberalism" title="Economic liberalism">liberal trade</a> policy, open to foreign imports. This has origins in <a href="/wiki/Capitulations_of_the_Ottoman_Empire" title="Capitulations of the Ottoman Empire">capitulations of the Ottoman Empire</a>, dating back to the first commercial treaties signed with France in 1536 and taken further with <a href="/wiki/Capitulation_(treaty)" title="Capitulation (treaty)">capitulations</a> in 1673 and 1740, which lowered <a href="/wiki/Duty_(economics)" title="Duty (economics)">duties</a> to 3% for imports and exports. The liberal Ottoman policies were praised by British economists such as <a href="/wiki/John_Ramsay_McCulloch" title="John Ramsay McCulloch">John Ramsay McCulloch</a> in his <i>Dictionary of Commerce</i> (1834), but later criticized by British politicians such as <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Disraeli" title="Benjamin Disraeli">Benjamin Disraeli</a>, who cited the Ottoman Empire as "an instance of the injury done by unrestrained competition" in the 1846 <a href="/wiki/Corn_Laws" title="Corn Laws">Corn Laws</a> debate:<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>There has been free trade in Turkey, and what has it produced? It has destroyed some of the finest manufactures of the world. As late as 1812 these manufactures existed; but they have been destroyed. That was the consequences of competition in Turkey, and its effects have been as pernicious as the effects of the contrary principle in Spain.
</p></blockquote>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Stagnation_and_reform_of_the_Ottoman_Empire_(1683%E2%80%931827)" class="mw-redirect" title="Stagnation and reform of the Ottoman Empire (1683–1827)">stagnation and reform of the Ottoman Empire (1683–1827)</a> ended with the dismemberment of <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Classical_Army" class="mw-redirect" title="Ottoman Classical Army">Ottoman Classical Army</a>. The issue during the decline and modernization of the Ottoman Empire (1828–1908) was to create a military (a security apparatus) that could win wars and bring security to its subjects. That goal took multiple Sultans with multiple reorganizations during this period. At the end of this period, with the <a href="/wiki/Second_Constitutional_Era" title="Second Constitutional Era">Second Constitutional Era</a> in 1908, -with a degree of- <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Modern_Army" class="mw-redirect" title="Ottoman Modern Army">Ottoman military became modernized and professionalized</a> in the form of European Armies.
</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Mahmud_II" title="Mahmud II">Mahmud II</a> had to deal with multiple issues inherited from generations past. These issues lasted all through his reign. Shortly, the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Question" title="Eastern Question">Eastern Question</a> with Russia, England, and France, and military problems arising from mutinous Janissaries and factious Ulemas. He also faced numerous internal conflicts with Egyptians, Wahabbis, Serbians, Albanians, Greeks and Syrians, and had administrative problems from rebellious Pashas, who would fain have founded new kingdoms on the ruins of the House of Osman.
</p><p>Mahmud understood the growing problems of the state and the approaching overthrow of the monarchy, and began to deal with the problems as he saw them. For example, he closed the Court of Confiscations, and took away much of the power of the pashas. He personally set an example of reform by regularly attending the <a href="/wiki/Divan" title="Divan">Divan</a>, or state council. The practice of the sultan's avoiding the Divan had been introduced two centuries prior, during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Suleiman_the_Magnificent" title="Suleiman the Magnificent">Suleiman I</a>, and was considered to be one of the causes of the decline of the Empire. Mahmud II also addressed some of the worst abuses connected with the <a href="/wiki/Vakif" class="mw-redirect" title="Vakif">Vakifs</a>, by placing their revenues under state administration. However, he did not venture to apply this vast mass of property to the general purposes of the government.
</p>
<h4><span id="Serbs.2C_1810s"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Serbs,_1810s">Serbs, 1810s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Serbs, 1810s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1804 the <a href="/wiki/Serbian_Revolution" title="Serbian Revolution">Serbian Revolution</a> against Ottoman rule erupted in the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a>, running in parallel with the <a href="/wiki/Illyrian_provinces" class="mw-redirect" title="Illyrian provinces">Napoleonic invasion</a>. By 1817, when the revolution ended, Serbia was raised to the status of <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Serbia" title="Principality of Serbia">self-governing monarchy</a> under nominal Ottoman <a href="/wiki/Suzerainty" title="Suzerainty">suzerainty</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In 1821 the <a href="/wiki/First_Hellenic_Republic" title="First Hellenic Republic">First Hellenic Republic</a> became the first <a href="/wiki/Balkan" class="mw-redirect" title="Balkan">Balkan</a> country to achieve its independence from the Ottoman Empire. It was officially recognised by the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Porte" class="mw-redirect" title="Ottoman Porte">Porte</a> in 1829, after the end of the <a href="/wiki/Greek_War_of_Independence" title="Greek War of Independence">Greek War of Independence</a>.
</p>
<h4><span id="Greeks.2C_1820s"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Greeks,_1820s">Greeks, 1820s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Greeks, 1820s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>In 1814, a secret organization called the <a href="/wiki/Filiki_Eteria" title="Filiki Eteria">Filiki Eteria</a> was founded with the aim of liberating Greece. The Filiki Eteria planned to launch revolts in the <a href="/wiki/Peloponnese" title="Peloponnese">Peloponnese</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Danubian_Principalities" title="Danubian Principalities">Danubian Principalities</a>, and capital with its surrounding areas. The first of these revolts began on 6 March 1821 in the Danubian Principalities which was put down by the Ottomans. On 17 March 1821, the <a href="/wiki/Maniots" title="Maniots">Maniots</a> declared war which was the start of revolutionary actions from other controlled states. In October 1821, <a href="/wiki/Theodoros_Kolokotronis" title="Theodoros Kolokotronis">Theodoros Kolokotronis</a> had captured <a href="/wiki/Tripoli,_Greece" title="Tripoli, Greece">Tripolitsa</a>, followed by other revolts in <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a>, <a href="/wiki/Macedonia_(Greece)" title="Macedonia (Greece)">Macedonia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Central_Greece" title="Central Greece">Central Greece</a>. Tensions soon developed among different Greek factions, leading to two consecutive civil wars. <a href="/wiki/Mehmet_Ali_of_Egypt" class="mw-redirect" title="Mehmet Ali of Egypt">Mehmet Ali of Egypt</a> agreed to send his son <a href="/wiki/Ibrahim_Pasha_of_Egypt" title="Ibrahim Pasha of Egypt">Ibrahim Pasha</a> to Greece with an army to suppress the revolt in return for territorial gain. By the end of 1825, most of the Peloponnese was under Egyptian control, and the city of <a href="/wiki/Missolonghi" title="Missolonghi">Missolonghi</a> was put under siege and fell in April 1826. Ibrahim had succeeded in suppressing most of the revolt in the Peloponnese and <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a> had been retaken. <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russia</a>, Britain and <a href="/wiki/Bourbon_Restoration" title="Bourbon Restoration">France</a> decided to intervene in the conflict and each nation sent a navy to Greece. Following news that combined Ottoman–Egyptian fleets were going to attack the Greek island of <a href="/wiki/Hydra,_Saronic_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydra, Saronic Islands">Hydra</a>, the allied fleet intercepted the Ottoman–Egyptian fleet in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Navarino" title="Battle of Navarino">battle of Navarino</a>. Following a week long standoff, a <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Navarino" title="Battle of Navarino">battle</a> began which resulted in the destruction of the Ottoman–Egyptian fleet. With the help of a <a href="/wiki/Morea_expedition" title="Morea expedition">French expeditionary force</a> proceeded to the captured part of Central Greece by 1828.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Greek_War_of_Independence" title="Greek War of Independence">Greek War of Independence</a> saw the beginning of the spread of the Western notion of nationalism, stimulated the <a href="/wiki/Rise_of_nationalism_under_the_Ottoman_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Rise of nationalism under the Ottoman Empire">rise of nationalism under the Ottoman Empire</a>, and eventually caused the breakdown of the Ottoman <i><a href="/wiki/Millet_(Ottoman_Empire)" title="Millet (Ottoman Empire)">millet</a></i> concept. Unquestionably, the concept of nationhood prevalent in the Ottoman Empire was different from the current one as it was centered on religion.
</p>
<h4><span id="The_Auspicious_Incident.2C_1826"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Auspicious_Incident,_1826">The Auspicious Incident, 1826</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Auspicious Incident, 1826">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_military_reforms" title="Ottoman military reforms">Ottoman military reforms</a></div>
<p>Mahmud II's most notable achievements include the <a href="/wiki/Auspicious_Incident" title="Auspicious Incident">abolition of the Janissary corps</a> in 1826, the establishment of a modern Ottoman army, and the preparation of the <a href="/wiki/Tanzimat" title="Tanzimat">Tanzimat</a> reforms in 1839. By 1826, the sultan was ready to move against the Janissary in favor of a more modern military. Mahmud II incited them to revolt on purpose, describing it as the sultan's "coup against the Janissaries". The sultan informed them, through a <a href="/wiki/Fatwa" title="Fatwa">fatwa</a>, that he was forming a new army, organised and trained along modern European lines. As predicted, they mutinied, advancing on the sultan's palace. In the ensuing fight, the Janissary barracks were set in flames by artillery fire resulting in 4,000 Janissary fatalities. The survivors were either exiled or executed, and their possessions were confiscated by the Sultan. This event is now called <a href="/wiki/The_Auspicious_Incident" class="mw-redirect" title="The Auspicious Incident">the Auspicious Incident</a>. The last of the Janissaries were then put to death by decapitation in what was later called the blood tower, in <a href="/wiki/Thessaloniki" title="Thessaloniki">Thessaloniki</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kinross_1977_457-457_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kinross_1977_457-457-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>These marked the beginning of the modernization and had immediate effects such as introducing European-style clothing, architecture, legislation, institutional organization and land reform.
</p>
<h4><span id="Russia.2C_1828.E2.80.931829"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Russia,_1828–1829">Russia, 1828–1829</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Russia, 1828–1829">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Russo-Turkish_War_(1828%E2%80%931829)" class="mw-redirect" title="Russo-Turkish War (1828–1829)">Russo-Turkish War</a> of 1828–1829 did not give him time to organize a new army, and the Sultan was forced to use these young and undisciplined recruits in the fight against the veterans of the Tsar. The war was brought to a close by the disastrous <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Adrianople_(1829)" title="Treaty of Adrianople (1829)">Treaty of Adrianople</a>. While the reforms in question were mainly implemented to improve the military, the most notable development that arose out of these efforts was a series of schools teaching everything from math to medicine to train new officers.
</p>
<h4><span id="Egypt.2C_1830s"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Egypt,_1830s">Egypt, 1830s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Egypt, 1830s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Egypt_Eyalet#War_with_the_Sultan_(1831–1841)" title="Egypt Eyalet">Egypt Eyalet §&#160;War with the Sultan (1831–1841)</a></div>
<p>Later in his reign, Mahmud became involved in disputes with the <a href="/wiki/W%C4%81li" title="Wāli">Wāli</a> of <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Ali_of_Egypt" title="Muhammad Ali of Egypt">Muhammad Ali</a>, who was technically Mahmud's vassal. The Sultan had asked for Muhammad Ali's help in suppressing a rebellion in Greece, but had not paid the promised price for his services. In 1831, Muhammad Ali declared war and managed to take control of <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arabia" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabia">Arabia</a> by the war's end in 1833. In 1839, Mahmud resumed the war, hoping to recover his losses, but he died at the time news was on its way to <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> that the Empire's army had been signally defeated at <a href="/wiki/Nezib" class="mw-redirect" title="Nezib">Nezib</a> by an Egyptian army led by Muhammad Ali's son, <a href="/wiki/Ibrahim_Pasha_of_Egypt" title="Ibrahim Pasha of Egypt">Ibrahim Pasha</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">September 2016</span>)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>In his time the financial situation of the Empire was dire, and certain social classes had long been oppressed by burdensome taxes. In dealing with the complicated questions that arose, Mahmud II is considered to have demonstrated the best spirit of the best of the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6pr%C3%BCl%C3%BC_family" title="Köprülü family">Köprülüs</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Firman" title="Firman">Firman</a> of 22 February 1834 abolished the vexatious charges which public functionaries, when traversing the provinces, had long been accustomed to take from the inhabitants. By the same edict all collection of money, except for the two regular half-yearly periods, was denounced as an abuse. "No one is ignorant," said Sultan Mahmud II in this document, "that I am bound to afford support to all my subjects against vexatious proceedings; to endeavour unceasingly to lighten, instead of increasing their burdens, and to ensure peace and tranquility. Therefore, those acts of oppression are at once contrary to the will of God, and to my imperial orders."
</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Hara%C3%A7" title="Haraç">haraç</a></i>, or capitation tax, though moderate and exempting those who paid it from military service, had long been made an engine of gross tyranny through the insolence and misconduct of government collectors. The Firman of 1834 abolished the old mode of levying it, and ordained that it should be raised by a commission composed of the <i>Kadı</i>, the Muslim governors, and the <i>Ayans</i>, or municipal chiefs of <i>Rayas</i> in each district. Many other financial improvements were effected. By another important series of measures, the administrative government was simplified and strengthened, and a large number of sinecure offices were abolished. Sultan Mahmud II gave a valuable personal example of good sense and economy, organised the imperial household, suppressed all titles without duties and eliminated all the positions of salaried officials without functions.
</p>
<h2><span id="Tanzimat_Era_1839.E2.80.931876"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Tanzimat_Era_1839–1876">Tanzimat Era 1839–1876</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Tanzimat Era 1839–1876">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tanzimat" title="Tanzimat">Tanzimat</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mustafa_reshid_pasha.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Mustafa_reshid_pasha.jpg/220px-Mustafa_reshid_pasha.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="241" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Mustafa_reshid_pasha.jpg/330px-Mustafa_reshid_pasha.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Mustafa_reshid_pasha.jpg/440px-Mustafa_reshid_pasha.jpg 2x" data-file-width="493" data-file-height="541" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mustafa_reshid_pasha.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Mustafa_Re%C5%9Fid_Pasha" title="Mustafa Reşid Pasha">Mustafa Reşid Pasha</a>, the principal architect of the <a href="/wiki/Edict_of_G%C3%BClhane" title="Edict of Gülhane">Edict of Gülhane</a></div></div></div>
<p>In 1839, the <a href="/wiki/Hatt-i_Sharif" class="mw-redirect" title="Hatt-i Sharif">Hatt-i Sharif</a> proclamation launched the <a href="/wiki/Tanzimat" title="Tanzimat">Tanzimat</a> (from Arabic: تنظيم <i>tanẓīm</i>, meaning "organisation") (1839–76), period. Previous to the first of the firmans, the property of all persons banished or condemned to death was forfeited to the crown, which kept a sordid motive for acts of cruelty in perpetual operation, besides encouraging a host of vile <a href="/wiki/Delator" title="Delator">Delators</a>. The second firman removed the ancient rights of Turkish governors to condemn men to instant death at will; the <i>Paşas</i>, the <i>Ağas</i>, and other officers were enjoined that "they should not presume to inflict, themselves, the punishment of death on any man, whether Raya or Turk, unless authorized by a legal sentence pronounced by the Kadi, and regularly signed by the judge."
</p><p>The Tanzimat reforms did not halt the rise of nationalism in the <a href="/wiki/Danubian_Principalities" title="Danubian Principalities">Danubian Principalities</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Serbia" title="Principality of Serbia">Principality of Serbia</a>, which had been semi-independent for almost six decades. In 1875, the tributary principalities of Serbia and <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Montenegro" title="Principality of Montenegro">Montenegro</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_Principalities" class="mw-redirect" title="United Principalities">United Principalities</a> of <a href="/wiki/Wallachia" title="Wallachia">Wallachia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moldavia" title="Moldavia">Moldavia</a>, unilaterally declared their independence from the empire. Following the <a href="/wiki/Russo-Turkish_War_(1877%E2%80%931878)" title="Russo-Turkish War (1877–1878)">Russo-Turkish War (1877–1878)</a>, the empire granted independence to all three belligerent nations. <a href="/wiki/History_of_Bulgaria" title="History of Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a> also achieved virtual independence<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> (as the <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Bulgaria" title="Principality of Bulgaria">Principality of Bulgaria</a>); its volunteers had participated in the Russo-Turkish War on the side of the rebelling nations.
</p><p>The government's series of constitutional reforms led to a fairly modern conscripted army, banking system reforms, the decriminalisation of homosexuality, the replacement of religious law with secular law<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Guild" title="Guild">guilds</a> with modern factories.
</p>
<h3><span id="1839.E2.80.931861_Abd.C3.BClmecit_I"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1839–1861_Abdülmecit_I">1839–1861 Abdülmecit I</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 1839–1861 Abdülmecit I">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1840s">1840s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 1840s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Ottoman Ministry of Post was established in Istanbul on 23 October 1840.<sup id="cite_ref-PTT_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PTT-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PTT2_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PTT2-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The first post office was the <i>Postahane-i Amire</i> near the courtyard of the <a href="/wiki/New_Mosque_(Istanbul)" title="New Mosque (Istanbul)">Yeni Mosque</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-PTT_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PTT-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The introduction of the first Ottoman paper <a href="/wiki/Banknotes" class="mw-redirect" title="Banknotes">banknotes</a> (1840) and opening of the first post offices (1840); the reorganization of the finance system according to the French model (1840); the reorganization of the Civil and Criminal Code according to the French model (1840); the establishment of the <i>Meclis-i Maarif-i Umumiye</i> (1841) which was the prototype of the <a href="/wiki/First_Constitutional_Era_(Ottoman_Empire)" class="mw-redirect" title="First Constitutional Era (Ottoman Empire)">First Ottoman Parliament</a> (1876); the reorganisation of the army and a regular method of recruiting, levying the army and fixing the duration of military service (1843–44); the adoption of an <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_imperial_anthem" class="mw-redirect" title="Ottoman imperial anthem">Ottoman national anthem</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_flag" class="mw-redirect" title="Ottoman flag">Ottoman national flag</a> (1844); the institution of a Council of Public Instruction (1845) and the Ministry of Education (<i>Mekatib-i Umumiye Nezareti</i>, 1847, which later became the <i>Maarif Nezareti</i>, 1857); the abolition of slavery and slave trade (1847); the establishment of the first modern universities (<i>darülfünun</i>, 1848), academies (1848) and teacher schools (<i>darülmuallimin</i>, 1848).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Ottoman Ministry of Post was established in Istanbul on 23 October 1840.<sup id="cite_ref-PTT_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PTT-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PTT2_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PTT2-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The first post office was the <i>Postahane-i Amire</i> near the courtyard of the <a href="/wiki/New_Mosque_(Istanbul)" title="New Mosque (Istanbul)">Yeni Mosque</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-PTT_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PTT-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Samuel_Morse" title="Samuel Morse">Samuel Morse</a> received his first ever patent for the <a href="/wiki/Telegraph" class="mw-redirect" title="Telegraph">telegraph</a> in 1847, at the old Beylerbeyi Palace (the present <a href="/wiki/Beylerbeyi_Palace" title="Beylerbeyi Palace">Beylerbeyi Palace</a> was built in 1861–1865 on the same location) in Istanbul, which was issued by Sultan <a href="/wiki/Abd%C3%BClmecid_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Abdülmecid I">Abdülmecid</a> who personally tested the new invention.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Following this successful test, installation works of the first telegraph line (Istanbul-<a href="/wiki/Adrianople" class="mw-redirect" title="Adrianople">Adrianople</a>&#8211;<a href="/wiki/%C5%9Eumnu" class="mw-redirect" title="Şumnu">Şumnu</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-NTVtarih2_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NTVtarih2-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> began on 9 August 1847.<sup id="cite_ref-telekomhistory_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-telekomhistory-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>

<h4><span id="Identity_Card_and_Ottoman_Census.2C_1844"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Identity_Card_and_Ottoman_Census,_1844">Identity Card and Ottoman Census, 1844</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Identity Card and Ottoman Census, 1844">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>While the Ottoman Empire had population records prior to the 1830s, it was only in 1831 that the Office of Population Registers fund (Ceride-i Nüfus Nezareti) was founded. The Office decentralized in 1839 to draw more accurate data. Registrars, inspectors, and population officials were appointed to the provinces and smaller administrative districts. They recorded births and deaths periodically and compared lists indicating the population in each district. These records were not a total count of population. Rather, they were based on what is known as “head of household”. Only the ages, occupation, and property of the male family members only were counted.
</p><p>The first nationwide Ottoman census was in 1844. The first national identity cards which officially named the <i>Mecidiye</i> identity papers, or informally <i>kafa kağıdı</i> (<i>head paper</i>) documents.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1850s">1850s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: 1850s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1856, the <i><a href="/wiki/Hatt-%C4%B1_H%C3%BCmayun" class="mw-redirect" title="Hatt-ı Hümayun">Hatt-ı Hümayun</a></i> promised equality for all Ottoman citizens regardless of their ethnicity and religious confession; which thus widened the scope of the 1839 <a href="/wiki/Hatt-i_Sharif" class="mw-redirect" title="Hatt-i Sharif"><i>Hatt-ı Şerif</i> of Gülhane</a>. Overall, the Tanzimat reforms had far-reaching effects. Those educated in the schools established during the Tanzimat period included <a href="/wiki/Mustafa_Kemal_Atat%C3%BCrk" title="Mustafa Kemal Atatürk">Mustafa Kemal Atatürk</a> and other progressive leaders and thinkers of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Turkey" class="mw-redirect" title="Republic of Turkey">Republic of Turkey</a> and of many other former Ottoman states in the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a>, the Middle East and North Africa. These reforms included<sup id="cite_ref-NTVtarih_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NTVtarih-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> guarantees to ensure the Ottoman subjects perfect security for their lives, honour and property;
</p><p>Establishment of the Ministry of Healthcare (<i>Tıbbiye Nezareti</i>, 1850); the Commerce and Trade Code (1850); establishment of the Academy of Sciences (<i>Encümen-i Daniş</i>, 1851); establishment of the <i>Şirket-i Hayriye</i> which operated the first steam-powered commuter ferries (1851); the first European style courts (<i>Meclis-i Ahkam-ı Adliye</i>, 1853) and supreme judiciary council (<i>Meclis-i Ali-yi Tanzimat</i>, 1853); establishment of the modern Municipality of Istanbul (<i>Şehremaneti</i>, 1854) and the City Planning Council (<i>İntizam-ı Şehir Komisyonu</i>, 1855); the abolition of the <a href="/wiki/Jizya" title="Jizya">capitation (Jizya) tax</a> on non-Muslims, with a regular method of establishing and collecting taxes (1856); non-Muslims were allowed to become soldiers (1856); various provisions for the better administration of the public service and advancement of commerce; the establishment of the first <a href="/wiki/Telegraph" class="mw-redirect" title="Telegraph">telegraph</a> networks (1847–1855) and <a href="/wiki/Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Railway">railways</a> (1856); the replacement of guilds with factories; the establishment of the Ottoman Central Bank (originally established as the <i>Bank-ı Osmanî</i> in 1856, and later reorganised as the <i>Bank-ı Osmanî-i Şahane</i> in 1863)<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and the Ottoman Stock Exchange (<i>Dersaadet Tahvilat Borsası</i>, established in 1866);<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> the Land Code (<i>Arazi Kanunnamesi</i>, 1857); permission for private sector publishers and printing firms with the <i>Serbesti-i Kürşad Nizamnamesi</i> (1857); establishment of the School of Economical and Political Sciences (<i>Mekteb-i Mülkiye</i>, 1859).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1855 the Ottoman telegraph network became operational and the Telegraph Administration was established.<sup id="cite_ref-PTT_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PTT-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PTT2_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PTT2-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NTVtarih2_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NTVtarih2-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Crimean_War.2C_1853.E2.80.931856"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Crimean_War,_1853–1856">Crimean War, 1853–1856</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Crimean War, 1853–1856">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Crimean_War" title="Crimean War">Crimean War</a> (1853–1856) was part of a long-running contest between the major European powers for influence over territories of the declining Ottoman Empire. Britain and France successfully defended the Ottoman Empire against Russia.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most of the fighting took place when the allies landed on Russia's <a href="/wiki/Crimean_Peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Crimean Peninsula">Crimean Peninsula</a> to gain control of the Black Sea. There were smaller campaigns in western <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>, the Pacific Ocean and the <a href="/wiki/White_Sea" title="White Sea">White Sea</a>. It was one of the first "modern" wars, as it introduced new technologies to warfare, such as the first tactical use of railways and the telegraph.<sup id="cite_ref-royle1_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-royle1-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>  The subsequent <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_(1856)" title="Treaty of Paris (1856)">Treaty of Paris (1856)</a> secured Ottoman control over the <a href="/wiki/Balkan_Peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Balkan Peninsula">Balkan Peninsula</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> basin. That lasted until defeat in the <a href="/wiki/Russo-Turkish_War_(1877%E2%80%931878)" title="Russo-Turkish War (1877–1878)">Russo-Turkish War of 1877–1878</a>.
</p><p>The Ottoman Empire took its <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_public_debt" title="Ottoman public debt">first foreign loans</a> on 4 August 1854,<sup id="cite_ref-Ottomandebthistory_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ottomandebthistory-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> shortly after the beginning of the Crimean War.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The war caused an exodus of the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_Tatars" title="Crimean Tatars">Crimean Tatars</a>. From the total Tatar population of 300,000 in the <a href="/wiki/Tauride_Province" class="mw-redirect" title="Tauride Province">Tauride Province</a>, about 200,000 Crimean Tatars moved to the Ottoman Empire in continuing waves of emigration.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Toward the end of the <a href="/wiki/Caucasian_Wars" class="mw-redirect" title="Caucasian Wars">Caucasian Wars</a>, 90% of the <a href="/wiki/Circassians" title="Circassians">Circassians</a> were exiled from their homelands in the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> and settled in the Ottoman Empire.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> During the 19th century, there was an exodus to present-day Turkey by a large portion of Muslim peoples from the Balkans, Caucasus, <a href="/wiki/Crimea" title="Crimea">Crimea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a>, By the early 19th century, as many as 45% of the islanders may have been Muslim, had great influence in molding the country's fundamental features. These people were called <i><a href="/wiki/Muhajir_(Turkey)" class="mw-redirect" title="Muhajir (Turkey)">Muhacir</a></i> under a general definition.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> By the time the Ottoman Empire came to an end in 1922, half of the urban population of Turkey was descended from Muslim refugees from Russia.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Crimean Tatar refugees in the late 19th century played an especially notable role in seeking to modernise Turkish education.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span id="Armenians.2C_1860s"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Armenians,_1860s">Armenians, 1860s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Armenians, 1860s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Armenian_national_liberation_movement.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Armenian_national_liberation_movement.jpg/220px-Armenian_national_liberation_movement.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="162" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Armenian_national_liberation_movement.jpg/330px-Armenian_national_liberation_movement.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Armenian_national_liberation_movement.jpg/440px-Armenian_national_liberation_movement.jpg 2x" data-file-width="979" data-file-height="723" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Armenian_national_liberation_movement.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Armenian_national_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Armenian national movement">Armenian national movement</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Armenian_national_awakening" title="Armenian national awakening">Armenian national awakening</a> is influenced by the Age of Enlightenment and the rise of nationalism under the Ottoman Empire, the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_national_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Armenian national movement">Armenian national movement</a> developed in the early 1860s. The factors contributing to its emergence made the movement similar to that of the Balkan nations, especially the Greeks. The Armenian élite and various militant groups sought to improve and defend the mostly rural Armenian population of the eastern Ottoman Empire from the Muslims, but the ultimate goal was the creation of an Armenian state in the Armenian-populated areas controlled at the time by the Ottoman Empire and the Russian Empire.
</p>
<h3><span id="1861.E2.80.931876_Abd.C3.BClaziz"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1861–1876_Abdülaziz">1861–1876 Abdülaziz</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: 1861–1876 Abdülaziz">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Abdülaziz continued the Tanzimat and Islahat reforms. New administrative districts (<i>vilayets</i>) were set up in 1864 and a Council of State was established in 1868. Public education was organized on the French model and <a href="/wiki/Istanbul_University" title="Istanbul University">Istanbul University</a> was reorganized as a modern institution in 1861. Abdülaziz was also the first sultan who traveled outside his empire. His 1867 trip included a visit to the United Kingdom. The Press and Journalism Regulation Code (<i>Matbuat Nizamnamesi</i>, 1864); among others.<sup id="cite_ref-NTVtarih_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NTVtarih-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  1876 the first international mailing network between Istanbul and the lands beyond the vast Ottoman Empire was established.<sup id="cite_ref-PTT_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PTT-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In 1901 the first money transfers were made through the post offices and the first cargo services became operational.<sup id="cite_ref-PTT_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PTT-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In 1868 homosexuality was decriminalised<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Christian <a href="/wiki/Millet_(Ottoman_Empire)" title="Millet (Ottoman Empire)">millets</a> gained privileges, such as in the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_National_Constitution" title="Armenian National Constitution">Armenian National Constitution</a> of 1863. This <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Porte" class="mw-redirect" title="Ottoman Porte">Divan</a>-approved form of the <i>Code of Regulations</i> consisted of 150 articles drafted by the Armenian intelligentsia. Another institution was the newly formed <a href="/wiki/Armenian_National_Assembly_(Ottoman_Empire)" class="mw-redirect" title="Armenian National Assembly (Ottoman Empire)">Armenian National Assembly</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The Christian population of the empire, owing to their higher educational levels, started to pull ahead of the Muslim majority, leading to much resentment on the part of the latter.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In 1861, there were 571 primary and 94 secondary schools for Ottoman Christians with 140,000 pupils in total, a figure that vastly exceeded the number of Muslim children in school at the same time, who were further hindered by the amount of time spent learning Arabic and Islamic theology.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_24-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In turn, the higher educational levels of the Christians allowed them to play a large role in the economy.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_24-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In 1911, of the 654 wholesale companies in Istanbul, 528 were owned by ethnic Greeks.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_24-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1871 the Ministry of Post and the Telegraph Administration were merged, becoming the Ministry of Post and Telegraph.<sup id="cite_ref-PTT2_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PTT2-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In July 1881 the first telephone circuit in Istanbul was established between the Ministry of Post and Telegraph in the Soğukçeşme quarter and the Postahane-i Amire in the Yenicami quarter.<sup id="cite_ref-telekomhistory_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-telekomhistory-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> On 23 May 1909, the first manual <a href="/wiki/Telephone_exchange" title="Telephone exchange">telephone exchange</a> with a 50 line capacity entered service in the <a href="/wiki/Istanbul_Main_Post_Office" class="mw-redirect" title="Istanbul Main Post Office">Büyük Postane</a> (Grand Post Office) in <a href="/wiki/Sirkeci" title="Sirkeci">Sirkeci</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-telekomhistory_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-telekomhistory-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Bulgaria.2C_1870s"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Bulgaria,_1870s">Bulgaria, 1870s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Bulgaria, 1870s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>The rise of <a href="/wiki/National_awakening_of_Bulgaria" title="National awakening of Bulgaria">national awakening of Bulgaria</a> led to the <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_revival" class="mw-redirect" title="Bulgarian revival">Bulgarian revival</a> movement. Unlike <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> and <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>, the nationalist movement in Bulgaria did not concentrate initially on armed resistance against the Ottoman Empire. After the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_Exarchate" title="Bulgarian Exarchate">Bulgarian Exarchate</a> on 28 February 1870 a large-scale armed struggle started to develop as late as the beginning of the 1870s with the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Internal_Revolutionary_Organisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Internal Revolutionary Organisation">Internal Revolutionary Organisation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_Revolutionary_Central_Committee" title="Bulgarian Revolutionary Central Committee">Bulgarian Revolutionary Central Committee</a>, as well as the active involvement of <a href="/wiki/Vasil_Levski" title="Vasil Levski">Vasil Levski</a> in both organisations. The struggle reached its peak with the <a href="/wiki/April_Uprising" class="mw-redirect" title="April Uprising">April Uprising</a> of 1876 in several Bulgarian districts in Moesia, Thrace and Macedonia. The suppression of the uprising and the atrocities committed by Ottoman soldiers against the civilian population increased the Bulgarian desire for independence.
</p>

<h4><span id="Albanians.2C_1870s"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Albanians,_1870s">Albanians, 1870s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Albanians, 1870s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>Because of religious ties of the Albanian majority of the population with the ruling Ottomans and the lack of an Albanian state in past, nationalism was less developed among Albanians in the 19th century than among other southeast European nations. Only from the 1870s and onwards did a movement of ‘national awakening‘ evolve among them - greatly delayed, compared to the Greeks and the Serbs. The 1877–1878 Russo-Turkish War dealt a decisive blow to Ottoman power in the Balkan Peninsula. The Albanians' fear that the lands they inhabited would be partitioned among Montenegro, Serbia, Bulgaria, and Greece fueled the rise of Albanian nationalism.
</p>
<h2><span id="Ottoman_Constitution.2C_1876"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Ottoman_Constitution,_1876">Ottoman Constitution, 1876</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Ottoman Constitution, 1876">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_constitution_of_1876" title="Ottoman constitution of 1876">Ottoman constitution of 1876</a></div>
<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <p>The reformist period peaked with the Constitution, called the <i>Kanûn-u Esâsî</i> (meaning "<a href="/wiki/Basic_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="Basic Law">Basic Law</a>" in Ottoman Turkish), written by members of the <a href="/wiki/Young_Ottomans" title="Young Ottomans">Young Ottomans</a>, which was promulgated on 23 November 1876. It established the freedom of belief and equality of all citizens before the law. The empire's <a href="/wiki/First_Constitutional_Era_(Ottoman_Empire)" class="mw-redirect" title="First Constitutional Era (Ottoman Empire)">First Constitutional era</a>, was short-lived. But the idea of <a href="/wiki/Ottomanism" title="Ottomanism">Ottomanism</a> proved influential. A group of reformers known as the <a href="/wiki/Young_Ottomans" title="Young Ottomans">Young Ottomans</a>, primarily educated in <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western</a> universities, believed that a <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a> would give an answer to the empire's growing social unrest. Through a <a href="/wiki/Military_coup" class="mw-redirect" title="Military coup">military coup</a> in 1876, they forced Sultan <a href="/wiki/Abd%C3%BClaziz" title="Abdülaziz">Abdülaziz</a> (1861–1876) to abdicate in favour of <a href="/wiki/Murad_V" title="Murad V">Murad V</a>. However, Murad V was mentally ill and was deposed within a few months. His heir-apparent, <a href="/wiki/Abd%C3%BClhamid_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Abdülhamid II">Abdülhamid II</a> (1876–1909), was invited to assume power on the condition that he would declare a constitutional monarchy, which he did on 23 November 1876. The parliament survived for only two years before the sultan suspended it. When forced to reconvene it, he abolished the representative body instead. This ended the effectiveness of the <i><a href="/wiki/Kan%C3%BBn-%C4%B1_Es%C3%A2s%C3%AE" class="mw-redirect" title="Kanûn-ı Esâsî">Kanûn-ı Esâsî</a></i>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1876_Murat_V">1876 Murat V</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: 1876 Murat V">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After Abdülaziz's dethronement Murat was enthroned. It was hoped that he would sign the constitution. However, due to health problems Murat was also dethroned after 93 days; he was the shortest reigning sultan of the Empire.
</p>
<h2><span id="First_Constitutional_Era.2C_1876.E2.80.931878"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="First_Constitutional_Era,_1876–1878">First Constitutional Era, 1876–1878</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: First Constitutional Era, 1876–1878">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/First_Constitutional_Era_(Ottoman_Empire)" class="mw-redirect" title="First Constitutional Era (Ottoman Empire)">First Constitutional Era</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> was the period of <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a> from the promulgation of the <i><a href="/wiki/Kan%C3%BBn-%C4%B1_Es%C3%A2s%C3%AE" class="mw-redirect" title="Kanûn-ı Esâsî">Kanûn-ı Esâsî</a></i> (meaning "<a href="/wiki/Basic_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="Basic Law">Basic Law</a>" in Ottoman Turkish), written by members of the <a href="/wiki/Young_Ottomans" title="Young Ottomans">Young Ottomans</a>, on 23 November 1876 until 13 February 1878. The era ended with the suspension of the Ottoman parliament by <a href="/wiki/Abd%C3%BClhamid_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Abdülhamid II">Abdülhamid II</a>.
</p>
<h3><span id="1876.E2.80.931879_Abdul_Hamid_II"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1876–1879_Abdul_Hamid_II">1876–1879 Abdul Hamid II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: 1876–1879 Abdul Hamid II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span id="Russo-Turkish_War_1877.E2.80.931878"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Russo-Turkish_War_1877–1878">Russo-Turkish War 1877–1878</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Russo-Turkish War 1877–1878">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r889293853/mw-parser-output/.tmulti"/><div class="thumb tmulti tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:204px;max-width:204px"><div class="trow"><div class="theader" style="text-align:center;background-color:transparent">Multiple Fronts of Russo-Turkish War</div></div><div class="trow"><div class="tsingle" style="width:202px;max-width:202px"><div class="thumbimage"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Russo-Turkish_War_in_Caucasia,_1877.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/The_Russo-Turkish_War_in_Caucasia%2C_1877.gif/200px-The_Russo-Turkish_War_in_Caucasia%2C_1877.gif" decoding="async" width="200" height="138" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/The_Russo-Turkish_War_in_Caucasia%2C_1877.gif/300px-The_Russo-Turkish_War_in_Caucasia%2C_1877.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/The_Russo-Turkish_War_in_Caucasia%2C_1877.gif/400px-The_Russo-Turkish_War_in_Caucasia%2C_1877.gif 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="482" /></a></div><div class="thumbcaption"><a href="/wiki/Russo-Turkish_War_(1877%E2%80%9378)" class="mw-redirect" title="Russo-Turkish War (1877–78)">Russo-Turkish War (1877–78)</a></div></div></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Russo-Turkish_War_of_1877%E2%80%931878" class="mw-redirect" title="Russo-Turkish War of 1877–1878">Russo-Turkish War of 1877–1878</a> had its origins in a rise in nationalism in the Balkans as well as in the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian</a> goal of recovering territorial losses it had suffered during the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_War" title="Crimean War">Crimean War</a>, reestablishing itself in the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> and following the political movement attempting to free Balkan nations from the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>. As a result of the war, the principalities of <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Montenegro" title="Montenegro">Montenegro</a>, each of which had <i>de facto</i> sovereignty for some time, formally proclaimed independence from the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>. After almost half a millennium of Ottoman domination (1396–1878), the Bulgarian state was reestablished as the <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Bulgaria" title="Principality of Bulgaria">Principality of Bulgaria</a>, covering the land between the <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a> River and the <a href="/wiki/Balkan_Mountains" title="Balkan Mountains">Balkan Mountains</a> (except Northern <a href="/wiki/Dobrudja" class="mw-redirect" title="Dobrudja">Dobrudja</a> which was given to Romania) and the region of <a href="/wiki/Sofia" title="Sofia">Sofia</a>, which became the new state's capital. The <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Berlin" title="Congress of Berlin">Congress of Berlin</a> also allowed <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a> to occupy <a href="/wiki/Bosnia_and_Herzegovina" title="Bosnia and Herzegovina">Bosnia and Herzegovina</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">Great Britain</a> to take over <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, while the Russian Empire annexed Southern <a href="/wiki/Bessarabia" title="Bessarabia">Bessarabia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kars" title="Kars">Kars</a> region.
</p>
<h4><span id="Congress_of_Berlin.2C_1878"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Congress_of_Berlin,_1878">Congress of Berlin, 1878</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Congress of Berlin, 1878">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r889293853/mw-parser-output/.tmulti"/><div class="thumb tmulti tnone center"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:408px;max-width:408px"><div class="trow"><div class="theader" style="text-align:center;background-color:transparent">Political negotiations</div></div><div class="trow"><div class="tsingle" style="width:202px;max-width:202px"><div class="thumbimage"><a href="/wiki/File:Treaty_of_San_Stefano.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" "Article 61" which was the "Article 16")</div></div></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Berlin" title="Congress of Berlin">Congress of Berlin</a> (13 June – 13 July 1878) was a meeting of the leading statesmen of Europe's Great Powers and the Ottoman Empire. In the wake of the <a href="/wiki/Russo-Turkish_War_(1877%E2%80%931878)" title="Russo-Turkish War (1877–1878)">Russo-Turkish War (1877–1878)</a> that ended with a decisive victory for Russia and her Orthodox Christian allies (subjects of the Ottoman Empire before the war) in the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkan Peninsula</a>, the urgent need was to stabilise and reorganise the Balkans, and set up new nations. German Chancellor <a href="/wiki/Otto_von_Bismarck" title="Otto von Bismarck">Otto von Bismarck</a>, who led the Congress, undertook to adjust boundaries to minimise the risks of major war, while recognising the reduced power of the Ottomans, and balance the distinct interests of the great powers.
</p><p>As a result, Ottoman holdings in Europe declined sharply; <a href="/wiki/History_of_Bulgaria" title="History of Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a> was established as an independent principality inside the Ottoman Empire, but was not allowed to keep all its previous territory. Bulgaria lost <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Rumelia" title="Eastern Rumelia">Eastern Rumelia</a>, which was restored to the Turks under a special administration; and Macedonia, which was returned outright to the Turks, who promised reform. <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> achieved full independence, but had to turn over part of <a href="/wiki/Bessarabia" title="Bessarabia">Bessarabia</a> to Russia. <a href="/wiki/History_of_Serbia" title="History of Serbia">Serbia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Montenegro" title="Montenegro">Montenegro</a> finally gained complete independence, but with smaller territories.
</p><p>In 1878, <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a> unilaterally occupied the Ottoman provinces of <a href="/wiki/Bosnia_Vilayet" title="Bosnia Vilayet">Bosnia-Herzegovina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sanjak_of_Novi_Pazar" title="Sanjak of Novi Pazar">Novi Pazar</a>, but the Ottoman government contested this move and maintained its troops in both provinces. The stalemate lasted for 30 years (Austrian and Ottoman forces coexisted in Bosnia and Novi Pazar for three decades) until 1908, when the Austrians took advantage of the political turmoil in the Ottoman Empire that stemmed from the <a href="/wiki/Young_Turk_Revolution" title="Young Turk Revolution">Young Turk Revolution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bosnian_crisis" class="mw-redirect" title="Bosnian crisis">annexed Bosnia-Herzegovina</a>, but pulled their troops out of Novi Pazar in order to reach a compromise and avoid a war with the Turks.
</p><p>In return for British Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Disraeli" title="Benjamin Disraeli">Benjamin Disraeli</a>'s advocacy for restoring the Ottoman territories on the Balkan Peninsula during the Congress of Berlin, Britain assumed the administration of <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a> in 1878<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> and later sent troops to <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> in 1882 with the pretext of helping the Ottoman government to put down the <a href="/wiki/Urabi_Revolt" class="mw-redirect" title="Urabi Revolt">Urabi Revolt</a>; effectively gaining control in both territories (Britain formally annexed the still nominally Ottoman territories of Cyprus and Egypt on 5 November 1914, in response to the Ottoman Empire's decision to enter World War I on the side of the <a href="/wiki/Central_Powers" title="Central Powers">Central Powers</a>.) France, on its part, occupied <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a> in 1881.
</p><p>The results were first hailed as a great achievement in peacemaking and stabilisation. However, most of the participants were not fully satisfied, and grievances regarding the results festered until they exploded into <a href="/wiki/World_war" title="World war">world war</a> in 1914. Serbia, Bulgaria and Greece made gains, but far less than they thought they deserved. The Ottoman Empire, called at the time the "sick man of Europe", was humiliated and significantly weakened, rendering it more liable to domestic unrest and more vulnerable to attack. Although Russia had been victorious in the war that occasioned the conference, it was humiliated at Berlin, and resented its treatment. Austria gained a great deal of territory, which angered the South Slavs, and led to decades of tensions in Bosnia and Herzegovina. Bismarck became the target of hatred of Russian nationalists and Pan-Slavists, and found that he had tied Germany too closely to Austria in the Balkans.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the long-run, tensions between Russia and Austria-Hungary intensified, as did the nationality question in the Balkans. The Congress succeeded in keeping Istanbul in Ottoman hands. It effectively disavowed Russia's victory. The Congress of Berlin returned to the Ottoman Empire territories that the previous treaty had given to the <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Bulgaria" title="Principality of Bulgaria">Principality of Bulgaria</a>, most notably <a href="/wiki/Macedonia_(region)" title="Macedonia (region)">Macedonia</a>, thus setting up a strong revanchist demand in Bulgaria that in 1912 led to the <a href="/wiki/First_Balkan_War" title="First Balkan War">First Balkan War</a> in which the Turks were defeated and lost nearly all of Europe. As the Ottoman Empire gradually shrank in size, military power and wealth, many Balkan Muslims migrated to the empire's remaining territory in Balkans or to the heartland in Anatolia.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Muslims had been the majority in some parts of Ottoman Empire such as the Crimea, the Balkans and the Caucasus as well as a plurality in southern Russia and also in some parts of Romania. Most of these lands were lost with time by the Ottoman Empire between 19th and 20th centuries. By 1923, only Anatolia and eastern <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thrace</a> remained as the Muslim land.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Istibdat_1879-1908">Istibdat 1879-1908</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Istibdat 1879-1908">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Map-of-Ottoman-Empire-1900.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Map-of-Ottoman-Empire-1900.png/220px-Map-of-Ottoman-Empire-1900.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="139" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Map-of-Ottoman-Empire-1900.png/330px-Map-of-Ottoman-Empire-1900.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Map-of-Ottoman-Empire-1900.png/440px-Map-of-Ottoman-Empire-1900.png 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="806" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Map-of-Ottoman-Empire-1900.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of the Ottoman Empire in 1900, with the names of the <a href="/wiki/Vilayet" title="Vilayet">Ottoman provinces</a>.</div></div></div>
<h3><span id="1879.E2.80.931908_Abdul_Hamid_II"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1879–1908_Abdul_Hamid_II">1879–1908 Abdul Hamid II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: 1879–1908 Abdul Hamid II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Abdul Hamid is also considered one of the last sultans to have full control. His reign struggled with the culmination of 75 years of change throughout the empire and an opposing reaction to that change.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_24-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> He was particularly concerned with the centralization of the empire.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> His efforts to centralize the Sublime Porte were not unheard of among other sultans. The Ottoman Empire’s local provinces had more control over their areas than the central government. Abdul Hamid II's foreign relations came from a “policy of non-commitment."<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The sultan understood the fragility of the Ottoman military, and the Empire’s weaknesses of its domestic control.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Pan-Islamism became Abdülhamid’s solution to the empire’s loss of identity and power.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> His efforts to promote Pan-Islamism were for the most part unsuccessful because of the large non-Muslim population, and the European influence onto the empire.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> His policies essentially isolated the Empire, which further aided in its decline. Several of the elite who sought a new constitution and reform for the empire were forced to flee to Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> New groups of radicals began to threaten the power of the Ottoman Empire.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Egypt_1880s">Egypt 1880s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Egypt 1880s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Egypt" title="Ottoman Egypt">Ottoman Egypt</a></div>
<p>After gaining some amount of autonomy during the early 1800s, Egypt had entered into a period of political turmoil by the 1880s. In April 1882, British and French warships appeared in <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a> to support the <a href="/wiki/Khedive" title="Khedive">khedive</a> and prevent the country from falling into the hands of anti-European nationals.
</p><p>In August 1882 British forces invaded and occupied <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> on the pretext of bringing order. The British supported <a href="/wiki/Tewfik_Pasha" title="Tewfik Pasha">Khedive Tewfiq</a> and restored stability with was especially beneficial to British and French financial interests. <a href="/wiki/List_of_monarchs_of_the_Muhammad_Ali_Dynasty#Khedivate_(1867–1914)" class="mw-redirect" title="List of monarchs of the Muhammad Ali Dynasty">Egypt and Sudan</a> remained as Ottoman provinces <i><a href="/wiki/De_jure" title="De jure">de jure</a></i> until 1914, when the Ottoman Empire joined the <a href="/wiki/Central_Powers" title="Central Powers">Central Powers</a> of World War I. Great Britain officially annexed these two provinces and <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a> in response.
</p>
<h4><span id="1893.E2.80.9396_Ottoman_Census"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1893–96_Ottoman_Census">1893–96 Ottoman Census</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: 1893–96 Ottoman Census">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1867, the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_Council_of_State" class="mw-redirect" title="Turkish Council of State">Council of States</a> took charge of drawing population tables, increasing the precision of population records. They introduced new measures of recording population counts in 1874. This led to the establishment of a General Population Administration, attached to the Ministry of Interior in 1881-1882.
</p><p>The first official census (1881–93) took 10 years to finish. In 1893, the results were compiled and presented. This census is the first modern, general and standardized census accomplished not for taxation nor for military purposes, but to acquire demographic data. The population was divided into ethno-religious and gender characteristics. Numbers of both male and female subjects are given in ethno-religious categories including Muslims, Greeks, Armenians, Bulgarians, Catholics, Jews, Protestants, Latins, Syriacs and Gypsies<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span id="Armenians.2C_1890s"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Armenians,_1890s">Armenians, 1890s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Armenians, 1890s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Armenians_in_the_Ottoman_Empire" title="Armenians in the Ottoman Empire">Armenians in the Ottoman Empire</a></div>
<p>Although granted their own <a href="/wiki/Armenian_National_Constitution" title="Armenian National Constitution">constitution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Armenian_National_Assembly_(Ottoman_Empire)" class="mw-redirect" title="Armenian National Assembly (Ottoman Empire)">national assembly</a> with the Tanzimat reforms, the Armenians attempted to demand implementation of Article 61 from the Ottoman government as agreed upon at the Congress of Berlin in 1878.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Autonomists">Autonomists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Autonomists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r889293853/mw-parser-output/.tmulti"/><div class="thumb tmulti tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:204px;max-width:204px"><div class="trow"><div class="theader" style="text-align:center;background-color:transparent">Kurds, principalities, forces</div></div><div class="trow"><div class="tsingle" style="width:202px;max-width:202px"><div class="thumbimage"><a href="/wiki/File:Kurdish_states_1835.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Kurdish_states_1835.png/200px-Kurdish_states_1835.png" decoding="async" width="200" height="200" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Kurdish_states_1835.png/300px-Kurdish_states_1835.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Kurdish_states_1835.png/400px-Kurdish_states_1835.png 2x" data-file-width="1566" data-file-height="1569" /></a></div><div class="thumbcaption">Circa 1835, <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Kurdish_people#Shaikh_Ubaidullah.27s_Revolt_and_Armenians" class="mw-redirect" title="History of the Kurdish people">Kurdish organization</a></div></div></div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Armenian%E2%80%93Kurdish_relations" title="Armenian–Kurdish relations">Armenian–Kurdish relations</a></div>
<p>During 1880 - 1881, while the Armenian national liberation movement was in its early stage; lack of outside support and inability to maintain a trained, organized Kurdish force diminished Kurdish aspirations. However, two prominent Kurdish families (tribes) mounted opposition to the empire, based more from an ethno-nationalistic stand point. The Badr Khans were successionists while the Sayyids of Nihiri were autonomists. The Russo-Turkish War of 1877-78 was followed in 1880 - 1881 by the attempt of Shaykh Ubayd Allah of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nihri&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nihri (page does not exist)">Nihri</a> to found an "independent Kurd principality" around Ottoman-Persian border (including the Van Vilayet) where Armenian population was significant. Shaykh Ubayd Allah of Nihri gathered 20,000 fighters.<sup id="cite_ref-McDowall4247_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McDowall4247-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Lacking discipline, his man left the ranks after pillaging and acquiring riches from the villages in the region (indiscriminately, including Armenian villages). Shaykh Ubayd Allah of Nihri captured by the Ottoman forces in 1882 and this movement ended.<sup id="cite_ref-McDowall4247_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McDowall4247-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/1889_Bashkale_clash" title="1889 Bashkale clash">Bashkaleh clash</a> was the bloody encounter between the <a href="/wiki/Armenakan_Party" title="Armenakan Party">Armenakan Party</a> and the Ottoman Empire in May 1889. Its name comes from <a href="/wiki/Ba%C5%9Fkale" title="Başkale">Başkale</a>, a border town of <a href="/wiki/Van_Eyalet" class="mw-redirect" title="Van Eyalet">Van Eyalet</a> of the Ottoman Empire. The event was important, as it was reflected on main Armenian newspapers as the recovered documents on the Armenakans showed an extensive plot for a national movement.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Ottoman officials believed that the men were members of a large revolutionary apparatus and the discussion was reflected on newspapers, (Eastern Express, Oriental Advertiser, Saadet, and Tarik) and the responses were on the Armenian papers. In some Armenian circles, this event was considered as a martyrdom and brought other armed conflicts.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The Bashkaleh Resistance was on the Persian border, which the Armenakans were in communication with <a href="/wiki/Armenians_in_the_Persian_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Armenians in the Persian Empire">Armenians in the Persian Empire</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Gugunian_Expedition" title="Gugunian Expedition">Gugunian Expedition</a>, which followed within the couple months, was an attempt by a small group of Armenian nationalists from the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Armenia" title="Russian Armenia">Russian Armenia</a> to launch an armed expedition across the border into the Ottoman Empire in 1890 in support of local Armenians.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Kum_Kapu_demonstration" title="Kum Kapu demonstration">Kum Kapu demonstration</a> occurred at the Armenian quarter of Kum Kapu, the seat of the Armenian Patriarch, was spared through the prompt action of the commandant, Hassan Aga.<sup id="cite_ref-kum_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kum-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> On 27 July 1890, <a href="/wiki/Harutiun_Jang%C3%BClian" title="Harutiun Jangülian">Harutiun Jangülian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mihran_Damadian" title="Mihran Damadian">Mihran Damadian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hambartsum_Boyajian" class="mw-redirect" title="Hambartsum Boyajian">Hambartsum Boyajian</a> interrupted the Armenian mass to read a <a href="/wiki/Manifesto" title="Manifesto">manifesto</a> and denounce the indifference of the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Patriarch_of_Constantinople" title="Armenian Patriarch of Constantinople">Armenian patriarch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Armenian_National_Assembly_(Ottoman_Empire)" class="mw-redirect" title="Armenian National Assembly (Ottoman Empire)">Armenian National Assembly</a>. Harutiun Jangülian (member from <a href="/wiki/Van,_Turkey" title="Van, Turkey">Van</a>) tried to assassinate the Patriarch of Istanbul. The goal was to persuade the Armenian clerics to bring their policies into alignment with the national politics. They soon forced the patriarch to join the procession heading to the <a href="/wiki/Yildiz_Palace" class="mw-redirect" title="Yildiz Palace">Yildiz Palace</a> to demand implementation of Article 61 of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Berlin_(1878)" title="Treaty of Berlin (1878)">Treaty of Berlin</a>. It is significant that this massacre, in which 6000 Armenians are said to have perished, was not the result of a general rising of the Muslim population.<sup id="cite_ref-kum_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kum-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> The Softas took no part in it, and many Armenians found refuge in the Muslim sections of the city.<sup id="cite_ref-kum_43-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kum-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Reform_program">Reform program</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Reform program">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Armenian_reform_program" title="Armenian reform program">Armenian reform program</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kurdish_chiefdoms" title="Kurdish chiefdoms">Kurdish chiefdoms</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hamidiye_(cavalry)" title="Hamidiye (cavalry)">Hamidiye (cavalry)</a></div>
<p>The Kurdish (force, rebels, bandits) sacked neighboring towns and villages with impunity.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The central assumption of the Hamidiye system—Kurdish tribes (<a href="/wiki/Kurdish_chiefdoms" title="Kurdish chiefdoms">Kurdish chiefdoms</a> cited among Armenian security concerns) could be brought under military discipline—proved to be "Utopian". The <a href="/wiki/Persian_Cossack_Brigade" title="Persian Cossack Brigade">Persian Cossack Brigade</a> later proved that it can function as independent unit, but Ottoman example, which was modeled after, never replaced the tribal loyalty to Ottoman Sultan or even to its establishing unit.
</p><p>In 1892, first time a trained and organized Kurdish force encouraged by the Sultan Abdul Hamid II. There are several reasons advanced as to why the Hamidiye light cavalry was created. The establishment of the Hamidiye was in one part a response to the Russian threat, but scholars believe that the central reason was to suppress <a href="/wiki/Armenian_national_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Armenian national movement">Armenian socialist/nationalist revolutionaries</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-newperspectivesonturkey.net_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newperspectivesonturkey.net-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> The Armenian revolutionaries posed a threat because they were seen as disruptive, and they could work with the Russians against the Ottoman Empire.<sup id="cite_ref-newperspectivesonturkey.net_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newperspectivesonturkey.net-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> The Hamidiye corps or Hamidiye Light Cavalry Regiments were well-armed, irregular, majority <a href="/wiki/Kurdish_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Kurdish people">Kurdish</a> cavalry (minor amounts of other nationalities, such as Turcoman) formations that operated in the eastern provinces of the Ottoman Empire.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> They were intended to be modeled after the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Russian_Cossack&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Russian Cossack (page does not exist)">Caucasian Cossack Regiments</a> (example Persian Cossack Brigade) and were firstly tasked to patrol the Russo-Ottoman frontier<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> and secondly, to reduce the potential of Kurdish-Armenian cooperation.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> The Hamidiye Cavalry was in no way a cross-tribal force, despite their military appearance, organization, and potential.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Hamidiye quickly find out that they could only be tried through a military court martial<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> They became immune to civil administration. Realizing their immunity, they turned their tribes into “legalized robber brigades” as they steal grain, reap fields not of their possession, drive off herds, and openly steal from shopkeepers.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>  Some argue that the creation of the Hamidiye “further antagonized the Armenian population” and it worsened the very conflict they were created to prevent.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Kurdish chieftain also taxed the population of the region in sustaining these units, which Armenian's perceived this Kurdish taxation as an exploitation. When Armenian spokesmen confronted the Kurdish chieftain (issue of double taxation), it brought about enmity between both populations. The Hamidiye cavalry harassed and assaulted Armenians.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1908, after the overthrow of Sultan, the Hamidiye Cavalry was disbanded as an organized force, but as they were “tribal forces” before official recognition, they stayed as  “tribal forces” after dismemberment. The Hamidiye Cavalry is described as a military disappointment and a failure because of its contribution to tribal feuds.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Armenians">Armenians</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decline_and_modernization_of_the_Ottoman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Armenians">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Hamidian_massacres" title="Hamidian massacres">Hamidian massacres</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muzuri_Kurds" title="Muzuri Kurds">Muzuri Kurds</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Db_Bank_Ottomani_kravman_masnagtsadz_fedayiner_Marseille_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Db_Bank_Ottomani_kravman_masnagtsadz_fedayiner_Marseille_1.jpg/220px-Db_Bank_Ottomani_kravman_masnagtsadz_fedayiner_Marseille_1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="160" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Db_Bank_Ottomani_kravman_masnagtsadz_fedayiner_Marseille_1.jpg/330px-Db_Bank_Ottomani_kravman_masnagtsadz_fedayiner_Marseille_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Db_Bank_Ottomani_kravman_masnagtsadz_fedayiner_Marseille_1.jpg/440px-Db_Bank_Ottomani_kravman_masnagtsadz_fedayiner_Marseille_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="436" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Db_Bank_Ottomani_kravman_masnagtsadz_fedayiner_Marseille_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Surviving members of the takeover after they arrived in Marseille.</div></div></div>
<p>A major role in the <a href="/wiki/Hamidian_massacres" title="Hamidian massacres">Hamidian massacres</a> of 1894-96 has been often ascribed to the <i>Hamidiye</i> regiments, particularly during the bloody suppression of Sasun (1894). On July 25, 1897 the Khanasor Expedition was against the Kurdish Mazrik tribe (<a href="/wiki/Muzuri_Kurds" title="Muzuri Kurds">Muzuri Kurds</a>) who owned a significant portion of this cavalry. The first notable battle in the Armenian resistance movement took place in <a href="/wiki/Sason" title="Sason">Sassoun</a>, where nationalist ideals were proliferated by <a href="/wiki/Hunchak" class="mw-redirect" title="Hunchak">Hunchak</a> activists, such as <a href="/wiki/Mihran_Damadian" title="Mihran Damadian">Mihran Damadian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hampartsoum_Boyadjian" title="Hampartsoum Boyadjian">Hampartsoum Boyadjian</a>, and Hrayr. The <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Revolutionary_Federation" title="Armenian Revolutionary Federation">Armenian Revolutionary Federation</a> also played a significant role in arming the people of the region. The Armenians of Sassoun confronted the Ottoman army and <a href="/wiki/Hamidieh_soldier" class="mw-redirect" title="Hamidieh soldier">Kurdish irregulars</a> at Sassoun, succumbing to superior numbers.<sup id="cite_ref-Hayots_p.42-44_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hayots_p.42-44-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> This was followed by <a href="/wiki/Zeitun_Rebellion_(1895%E2%80%931896)" class="mw-redirect" title="Zeitun Rebellion (1895–1896)">Zeitun Rebellion (1895–1896)</a>, which between the years 1891 and 1895, Hunchak activists toured various regions of <a href="/wiki/Cilicia" title="Cilicia">Cilicia</a> and <a href="/wiki/S%C3%BCleymanl%C4%B1" title="Süleymanlı">Zeitun</a> to encourage resistance, and established new branches of the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democrat_Hunchakian_Party" title="Social Democrat Hunchakian Party">Social Democrat Hunchakian Party</a>.
</p><p>In this area, something resembling a civil war between Armenians and Muslims (involving Hamidiye (cavalry)) raged for months before being brought to an end through mediation by the Great Powers. However instead of Armenian autonomy in these regions, Kurds (Kurdish tribal chiefs) retained much of their autonomy and power.<sup id="cite_ref-Denise_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Denise-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> The Abdulhamid made little attempt to alter the traditional power structure of “segmented, agrarian Kurish societies” – agha, shayk, and tribal chief.<sup id="cite_ref-Denise_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Denise-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Because of their geographical position at the southern and eastern fringe of the empire and mountainous topography, and limited transportation and communication system.<sup id="cite_ref-Denise_56-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Denise-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> The state had little access to these provinces and were forced to make informal agreements with tribal chiefs, for instance the Ottoman qadi and mufti did not have jurisdiction over religious law which bolstered Kurdish authority and autonomy.<sup id="cite_ref-Denise_56-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Denise-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/1896_Ottoman_Bank_takeover" class="mw-redirect" title="1896 Ottoman Bank takeover">1896 Ottoman Bank takeover</a> was perpetrated by an Armenian group armed with <a href="/wiki/Pistols" class="mw-redirect" title="Pistols">pistols</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grenades" class="mw-redirect" title="Grenades">grenades</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dynamite" title="Dynamite">dynamite</a> and hand-held bombs against the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Bank" title="Ottoman Bank">Ottoman Bank</a> in <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>. The seizure of the bank lasted 14 hours, resulting in the deaths of 10 of the Armenian men and Ottoman soldiers. The Ottoman reaction to takeover saw further massacres and pogroms of the several thousand Armenians living in Constantinople and Sultan <a href="/wiki/Abdul_Hamid_II" title="Abdul Hamid II">Abdul Hamid II</a> threatening to level the entire building itself. However, intervention on part of the European diplomats in the city managed to persuade the men to give, assigning safe passage to the survivors to France. Despite the level of violence the incident had wrought, the takeover was reported positively in the European press, praising the men for their courage and the objectives they attempted to accomplish.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Economically, the empire had difficulty in repaying the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_public_debt" title="Ottoman public debt">Ottoman public debt</a> to European banks, which caused the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Public_Debt_Administration" title="Ottoman Public Debt Administration">Council of Administration of the Ottoman Public Debt</a>. By the end of the 19th century, the main reason the empire was not overrun by Western powers was their attempt to maintain a <a href="/wiki/Balance_of_power_in_international_relations" class="mw-redirect" title="Balance of power in international relations">balance of power</a> in the area. Both Austria and Russia wanted to increase their spheres of influence and territory at the expense of the Ottoman Empire, but were kept in check mostly by Britain, which feared Russian dominance in the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Mediterranean" title="Eastern Mediterranean">Eastern Mediterranean</a>.
</p>
