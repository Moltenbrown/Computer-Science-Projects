	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">History of anatomy</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p>The <b>history of anatomy</b> extends from the earliest examinations of <a href="/wiki/Sacrifice" title="Sacrifice">sacrificial</a> victims to the sophisticated analyses of the body performed by modern scientists. The study of human anatomy can be traced back thousands of years, at least to the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_anatomical_studies" title="Ancient Egyptian anatomical studies">Egyptians</a>, but the science of anatomy, as we know it today, did not develop until far later. The development of the study of anatomy gradually built upon concepts that were understood during the time of Galen and slowly became a part of the traditional medical curriculum.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It has been characterized, over time, by a continually developing understanding of the functions of <a href="/wiki/Organ_(anatomy)" title="Organ (anatomy)">organs</a> and structures in the body.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_anatomy">Ancient anatomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_anatomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ancient anatomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Ayurvedic_Man.,_c.18th_century_Wellcome_L0017592.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/The_Ayurvedic_Man.%2C_c.18th_century_Wellcome_L0017592.jpg/220px-The_Ayurvedic_Man.%2C_c.18th_century_Wellcome_L0017592.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="340" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/The_Ayurvedic_Man.%2C_c.18th_century_Wellcome_L0017592.jpg/330px-The_Ayurvedic_Man.%2C_c.18th_century_Wellcome_L0017592.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/The_Ayurvedic_Man.%2C_c.18th_century_Wellcome_L0017592.jpg/440px-The_Ayurvedic_Man.%2C_c.18th_century_Wellcome_L0017592.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2686" data-file-height="4151" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Ayurvedic_Man.,_c.18th_century_Wellcome_L0017592.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Ayurvedic" class="mw-redirect" title="Ayurvedic">Ayurvedic</a> Man, Nepal c.18th century</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Egypt">Egypt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_anatomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Egypt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_anatomical_studies" title="Ancient Egyptian anatomical studies">Ancient Egyptian anatomical studies</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Cardiocentric_hypothesis" title="Cardiocentric hypothesis">Cardiocentric hypothesis</a></div>
<p>The study of <a href="/wiki/Anatomy" title="Anatomy">anatomy</a> begins at least as early as <a href="/wiki/1600_BC" class="mw-redirect" title="1600 BC">1600 BC</a>, the date of the <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Smith_Surgical_Papyrus" class="mw-redirect" title="Edwin Smith Surgical Papyrus">Edwin Smith Surgical Papyrus</a>. This treatise shows that the <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a>, its vessels, <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spleen" title="Spleen">spleen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kidneys" class="mw-redirect" title="Kidneys">kidneys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hypothalamus" title="Hypothalamus">hypothalamus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uterus" title="Uterus">uterus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urinary_bladder" title="Urinary bladder">bladder</a> were recognized, and that the <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessel" title="Blood vessel">blood vessels</a> were known to emanate from the heart. Other vessels are described, some carrying air, some <a href="/wiki/Mucus" title="Mucus">mucus</a>, and two to the right <a href="/wiki/Ear" title="Ear">ear</a> are said to carry the "breath of life",<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> while two to the left ear the "breath of death".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>The <a href="/wiki/Ebers_Papyrus" title="Ebers Papyrus">Ebers Papyrus</a> (c. 1550 BC) features a treatise on the heart. It notes that the heart is the center of blood supply, and attached to it are vessels for every member of the body. The Egyptians seem to have known little about the function of the kidneys and the brain and made the heart the meeting point of a number of vessels which carried all the fluids of the body – <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tears" title="Tears">tears</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urine" title="Urine">urine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Semen" title="Semen">semen</a>. However, they did not have a theory as to where <a href="/wiki/Saliva" title="Saliva">saliva</a> and sweat came from.<sup id="cite_ref-Porter1999_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Porter1999-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greek_advances_in_anatomy">Greek advances in anatomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_anatomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Greek advances in anatomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nomenclature, methods and applications for the study of anatomy all date back to the Greeks.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The early scientist Alcmaeon began to construct a background for medical and anatomical science with the dissection of animals. He identified the optic nerves and the tubes later termed the Eustachius.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Others such as <a href="/wiki/Acron" title="Acron">Acron</a> (480 BC), <a href="/wiki/Pausanias_of_Sicily" title="Pausanias of Sicily">Pausanias</a> (480 BC), and <a href="/wiki/Philistion_of_Locri" title="Philistion of Locri">Philistion of Locri</a> made investigations into anatomy. One important figure during this time was <a href="/wiki/Empedocles" title="Empedocles">Empedocles</a> (480 BC) who viewed the blood as the <i>innate heat</i> which he acquired from previous folklore. He also argued that the heart was the chief organ of both the vascular system and the pneuma (this could refer to either breath or soul; it was considered to be distributed by the blood vessels).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many medical texts by various authors are collected in the <i><a href="/wiki/Hippocratic_Corpus" title="Hippocratic Corpus">Hippocratic Corpus</a></i>, none of which can definitely be ascribed to <a href="/wiki/Hippocrates" title="Hippocrates">Hippocrates</a> himself. The texts show an understanding of <a href="/wiki/Musculoskeletal" class="mw-redirect" title="Musculoskeletal">musculoskeletal</a> structure, and the beginnings of understanding of the function of certain organs, such as the kidneys. The <a href="/wiki/Tricuspid_valve" title="Tricuspid valve">tricuspid valve</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a> and its function is documented in the treatise <i>On the Heart</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In the 4th century BCE, <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> and several contemporaries produced a more empirically founded system, based on animal <a href="/wiki/Dissection" title="Dissection">dissection</a>. Through his work with animal dissections and evolutionary biology, Aristotle founded <a href="/wiki/Comparative_anatomy" title="Comparative anatomy">comparative anatomy</a>. Around this time, <a href="/wiki/Praxagoras" title="Praxagoras">Praxagoras</a> is credited as the first to identify the difference between <a href="/wiki/Artery" title="Artery">arteries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vein" title="Vein">veins</a>, and the relations between organs are described more accurately than in previous works.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The first recorded school of anatomy was in Alexandria from about 300 to the 2nd century BC.<sup id="cite_ref-Siddiquey_2009_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Siddiquey_2009-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy_I_Soter" title="Ptolemy I Soter">Ptolemy I Soter</a> was the first to allow for medical officials to cut open and examine dead bodies for the purposes of learning how human bodies operated. On some occasions King Ptolemy even took part in these dissections. Most of the early dissections were done on executed criminals. The first use of human <a href="/wiki/Cadaver" title="Cadaver">cadavers</a> for anatomical research occurred later in the 4th century BCE when <a href="/wiki/Herophilos" title="Herophilos">Herophilos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Erasistratus" title="Erasistratus">Erasistratus</a> gained permission to perform live dissections, or vivisection, on condemned criminals in <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a> under the auspices of the <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_dynasty" title="Ptolemaic dynasty">Ptolemaic dynasty</a>. Herophilos in particular developed a body of anatomical knowledge much more informed by the actual structure of the human body than previous works had been. Herophilos was the first physician to dissect human bodies and is considered to be the founder of Anatomy. He reversed the longstanding notion made by Aristotle that the heart was the "seat of intelligence". He argued instead that this seat was the brain.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> He established the brain as the center of intelligence, distinguished between veins and arteries, and made many other accurate observations about the structure of the human body, especially the nervous system.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> However, Herophilos was eventually accused by his contemporaries of dissecting live criminals. The number of victims is said to be around 600 prisoners.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="From_ancient_to_medieval">From ancient to medieval</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_anatomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: From ancient to medieval">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Galen">Galen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_anatomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Galen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The final major anatomist of ancient times was <a href="/wiki/Galen" title="Galen">Galen</a>, active in the 2nd century.<sup id="cite_ref-Siddiquey_2009_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Siddiquey_2009-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> He compiled much of the knowledge obtained by previous writers, and furthered the inquiry into the function of organs by performing <a href="/wiki/Vivisection" title="Vivisection">vivisection</a> on animals. Due to a lack of readily available human specimens, discoveries through animal dissection were broadly applied to human anatomy as well. Galen served as chief physician to the gladiators in <a href="/wiki/Pergamum" class="mw-redirect" title="Pergamum">Pergamum</a> (AD 158). Through his position with the gladiators, Galen was able to study all kinds of wounds without performing any actual human dissection. By default, Galen was able to view much of the abdominal cavity. His study on pigs and apes, however, gave him more detailed information about the organs and provided the basis for his medical tracts. Around 100 of these tracts survive and fill 22 volumes of modern text. His two great anatomical works are <i>On anatomical procedure</i> and <i>On the uses of the parts of the body of man</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The information in these tracts became the foundation of authority for all medical writers and physicians for the next 1300 years until they were challenged by <a href="/wiki/Vesalius" class="mw-redirect" title="Vesalius">Vesalius</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Harvey" title="William Harvey">Harvey</a> in the 16th century.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It was through his experiments that Galen was able to overturn many long-held beliefs, such as the theory that the arteries contained air which carried it to all parts of the body from the heart and the lungs. This belief was based originally on the arteries of dead animals, which appeared to be empty. Galen was able to demonstrate that living arteries contain blood, but his error, which became the established medical orthodoxy for centuries, was to assume that the blood goes back and forth from the heart in an ebb-and-flow motion.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_modern_anatomy">Early modern anatomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_anatomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Early modern anatomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Blue_Beryl-Anatomy_Vulnerable_Points.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/The_Blue_Beryl-Anatomy_Vulnerable_Points.jpg/220px-The_Blue_Beryl-Anatomy_Vulnerable_Points.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/The_Blue_Beryl-Anatomy_Vulnerable_Points.jpg/330px-The_Blue_Beryl-Anatomy_Vulnerable_Points.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/The_Blue_Beryl-Anatomy_Vulnerable_Points.jpg/440px-The_Blue_Beryl-Anatomy_Vulnerable_Points.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2138" data-file-height="2849" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Blue_Beryl-Anatomy_Vulnerable_Points.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Anatomy of vulnerable points, The Blue Beryl Anatomical Atlas by <a href="/wiki/Desi_Sangye_Gyatso" title="Desi Sangye Gyatso">Desi Sangye Gyatso</a> (1653–1705)</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Woodcut_of_anatomical_dissection._Wellcome_M0011499.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Woodcut_of_anatomical_dissection._Wellcome_M0011499.jpg/220px-Woodcut_of_anatomical_dissection._Wellcome_M0011499.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="328" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Woodcut_of_anatomical_dissection._Wellcome_M0011499.jpg/330px-Woodcut_of_anatomical_dissection._Wellcome_M0011499.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Woodcut_of_anatomical_dissection._Wellcome_M0011499.jpg/440px-Woodcut_of_anatomical_dissection._Wellcome_M0011499.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2744" data-file-height="4095" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Woodcut_of_anatomical_dissection._Wellcome_M0011499.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1493 woodcut of anatomy</div></div></div>
<p>From the 3rd century BCE until the 12th century, human anatomy was mainly learned through books and animal dissection.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> While it was claimed by 19th century polemicists that human dissection became restricted after <a href="/wiki/Pope_Boniface_VIII" title="Pope Boniface VIII">Boniface VIII</a> passed a <a href="/wiki/Papal_bull" title="Papal bull">papal bull</a> that forbade the dismemberment and boiling of corpses for funerary purposes and this is still repeated in some generalist works, this claim has been debunked as a myth by modern historians of science.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> For many decades human dissection was thought unnecessary when all the knowledge about a human body could be read about from early authors such as Galen.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In the 12th century, as universities were being established in Italy, Emperor Frederick II made it mandatory for students of medicine to take courses on human anatomy and surgery.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Students who had the opportunity to watch Vesalius in dissection at times had the opportunity to interact with the animal corpse. At the risk of letting their eagerness to participate become a distraction to their professors, medical students preferred this interactive teaching style at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-:02_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:02-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> In the universities the lectern would sit elevated before the audience and instruct someone else in the dissection of the body, but in his early years Mondino de Luzzi performed the dissection himself making him one of the first and few to use a hands on approach to teaching human anatomy.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Specifically in 1315, Mondino de' Liuzzi is credited with having "performed the first human dissection recorded for Western Europe."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mondino_-_Anathomia,_1541_-_3022668.tif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Mondino_-_Anathomia%2C_1541_-_3022668.tif/lossy-page1-220px-Mondino_-_Anathomia%2C_1541_-_3022668.tif.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="255" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Mondino_-_Anathomia%2C_1541_-_3022668.tif/lossy-page1-330px-Mondino_-_Anathomia%2C_1541_-_3022668.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Mondino_-_Anathomia%2C_1541_-_3022668.tif/lossy-page1-440px-Mondino_-_Anathomia%2C_1541_-_3022668.tif.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2145" data-file-height="2485" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mondino_-_Anathomia,_1541_-_3022668.tif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Mondino_de_Luzzi" title="Mondino de Luzzi">Mondino de Luzzi</a>'s <i>Anathomia</i>, 1541</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mondino_de_Luzzi" title="Mondino de Luzzi">Mondino de Luzzi</a> "Mundinus" was born around 1276 and died in 1326; from 1314 to 1324 he presented many lectures on human anatomy at Bologna university.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Mondino de'Luzzi put together a book called "Anathomia" in 1316 that consisted of detailed dissections that he had performed, this book was used as a text book in universities for 250 years.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> "Mundinus" carried out the first systematic human dissections since <a href="/wiki/Herophilus_of_Chalcedon" class="mw-redirect" title="Herophilus of Chalcedon">Herophilus of Chalcedon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Erasistratus_of_Ceos" class="mw-redirect" title="Erasistratus of Ceos">Erasistratus of Ceos</a> 1500 years earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-ZimmermanVeith1993_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ZimmermanVeith1993-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Crombie1959_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crombie1959-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The first major development in anatomy in Christian Europe since the fall of Rome occurred at <a href="/wiki/Bologna" title="Bologna">Bologna</a>, where anatomists dissected cadavers and contributed to the accurate description of organs and the identification of their functions. Following de Liuzzi's early studies, 15th century anatomists included <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Achillini" title="Alessandro Achillini">Alessandro Achillini</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Benivieni" title="Antonio Benivieni">Antonio Benivieni</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ZimmermanVeith1993_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ZimmermanVeith1993-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BenivieniPolybus1529_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BenivieniPolybus1529-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Pathological anatomy<sup id="cite_ref-Thorndike1958_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thorndike1958-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leonardo_da_Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_anatomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Leonardo da Vinci">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Studies_of_the_Arm_showing_the_Movements_made_by_the_Biceps.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Studies_of_the_Arm_showing_the_Movements_made_by_the_Biceps.jpg/220px-Studies_of_the_Arm_showing_the_Movements_made_by_the_Biceps.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="321" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Studies_of_the_Arm_showing_the_Movements_made_by_the_Biceps.jpg/330px-Studies_of_the_Arm_showing_the_Movements_made_by_the_Biceps.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Studies_of_the_Arm_showing_the_Movements_made_by_the_Biceps.jpg/440px-Studies_of_the_Arm_showing_the_Movements_made_by_the_Biceps.jpg 2x" data-file-width="568" data-file-height="828" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Studies_of_the_Arm_showing_the_Movements_made_by_the_Biceps.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Anatomical study of the arm, by <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>, c. 1510</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Muscleman,_Vesalius,_University_of_Oklahoma_Hsitory_of_Science_Collection.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Muscleman%2C_Vesalius%2C_University_of_Oklahoma_Hsitory_of_Science_Collection.jpg/170px-Muscleman%2C_Vesalius%2C_University_of_Oklahoma_Hsitory_of_Science_Collection.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="261" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Muscleman%2C_Vesalius%2C_University_of_Oklahoma_Hsitory_of_Science_Collection.jpg/255px-Muscleman%2C_Vesalius%2C_University_of_Oklahoma_Hsitory_of_Science_Collection.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f4/Muscleman%2C_Vesalius%2C_University_of_Oklahoma_Hsitory_of_Science_Collection.jpg 2x" data-file-width="326" data-file-height="500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Muscleman,_Vesalius,_University_of_Oklahoma_Hsitory_of_Science_Collection.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Image of muscular anatomy from <i><a href="/wiki/De_humani_corporis_fabrica" title="De humani corporis fabrica">De humani corporis fabrica</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Vesalius" class="mw-redirect" title="Vesalius">Andreas Vesalius</a>, 1543</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a> (1452–1519) was trained in anatomy by <a href="/wiki/Andrea_del_Verrocchio" title="Andrea del Verrocchio">Andrea del Verrocchio</a>. In 1489 Leonardo began a series of anatomical drawings depicting the ideal human form. This work was carried out intermittently for over 2 decades. During this time he made use of his anatomical knowledge in his artwork, making many sketches of skeletal structures, muscles and organs of humans and other vertebrates that he dissected.<sup id="cite_ref-Boas_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boas-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Initially adopting an <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotlean</a> understanding of anatomy, he later studied Galen and adopted a more empirical approach, eventually abandoning Galen altogether and relying entirely on his own direct observation.<sup id="cite_ref-Dover_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dover-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> His surviving 750 drawings represent groundbreaking studies in anatomy. Leonardo dissected around thirty human specimens until he was forced to stop under order of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_X" title="Pope Leo X">Pope Leo X</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="The previous reference had no mention on da Vinci or Pope Leo X (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>As an artist-anatomist, Leonardo made many important discoveries, and had intended to publish a comprehensive treatise on human anatomy.<sup id="cite_ref-Dover_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dover-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> For instance, he produced the first accurate depiction of the human spine, while his notes documenting his dissection of the Florentine <a href="/wiki/Centenarian" title="Centenarian">centenarian</a> contain the earliest known description of <a href="/wiki/Cirrhosis_of_the_liver" class="mw-redirect" title="Cirrhosis of the liver">cirrhosis of the liver</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arteriosclerosis" title="Arteriosclerosis">arteriosclerosis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dover_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dover-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> He was the first to develop drawing techniques in anatomy to convey information using cross-sections and multiple angles, although centuries would pass before anatomical drawings became accepted as crucial for learning anatomy.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> None of Leonardo's Notebooks were published during his lifetime, many being lost after his death, with the result that his anatomical discoveries remained unknown until they were later found and published centuries after his death.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vesalius">Vesalius</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_anatomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Vesalius">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Anatomia_del_corpo_humano.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Anatomia_del_corpo_humano.jpg/170px-Anatomia_del_corpo_humano.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="255" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Anatomia_del_corpo_humano.jpg/255px-Anatomia_del_corpo_humano.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Anatomia_del_corpo_humano.jpg/340px-Anatomia_del_corpo_humano.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="1802" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Anatomia_del_corpo_humano.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Muscular figure in allegorical pose by <a href="/wiki/Juan_Valverde_de_Amusco" title="Juan Valverde de Amusco">Juan Valverde de Amusco</a>, 1559</div></div></div>
<p>The Galenic doctrine in Europe was first seriously challenged in the 16th century. Thanks to the <a href="/wiki/Printing_press" title="Printing press">printing press</a>, all over Europe a collective effort proceeded to circulate the works of Galen and later publish criticisms on their works. Vesalius openly denied Galen's anatomical teachings that is based on observations of other mammals, not human bodies.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Andreas_Vesalius" title="Andreas Vesalius">Andreas Vesalius</a>, born and educated in Belgium, contributed the most to human anatomy. Vesalius's success was due in large part to him exercising the skills of mindful dissections for the sake of understanding anatomy, much to the tune of Galen's "anatomy project" instead of focusing on the work of other scholars of the time in recovering the ancient texts of Hippocrates, Galen and others (which much of the medical community was focused around at the time).<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Vesalius was the first to publish a treatise, <i>De humani corporis fabrica</i>, that challenged Galen "drawing for drawing". These drawings were a detailed series of explanations and vivid drawings of the anatomical parts of human bodies. Vesalius traveled all the way from <a href="/wiki/Leuven" title="Leuven">Leuven</a><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> to <a href="/wiki/Padua" title="Padua">Padua</a> for permission to dissect victims from the <a href="/wiki/Gallows" title="Gallows">gallows</a> without fear of persecution. His superbly executed drawings are triumphant descriptions of the differences between dogs and humans, but it took a century for Galen's influence to fade. His work led to anatomy marked a new era in the study of anatomy and its relation to medicine. Under Vesalius, anatomy became an actual discipline. "His skill in and attention to dissection featured prominently in his publications as well as his demonstrations, in his research as well as his teaching."<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
In 1540, Vesalius gave a public demonstration of the inaccuracies of Galen's anatomical theories, which are still the orthodoxy of the medical profession. Vesalius now has on display, for comparison purposes, the skeletons of a human being alongside that of an ape of which he was able to show, that in many cases, Galen's observations were indeed correct for the ape, but bear little relation to man. Clearly what was needed was a new account of human anatomy. While the lecturer explained human anatomy, as revealed by Galen more than 1000 years earlier, an assistant pointed to the equivalent details on a dissected corpse. At times, the assistant was unable to find the organ as described, but invariably the corpse rather than Galen was held to be in error. Vesalius then decided that he will dissect corpses himself and trust to the evidence of what he found. His approach was highly controversial, but his evident skill led to his appointment as professor of surgery and anatomy at the University of Padua.
</p><p>A succession of researchers proceeded to refine the body of anatomical knowledge, giving their names to a number of anatomical structures along the way. The 16th and 17th centuries also witnessed significant advances in the understanding of the <a href="/wiki/Circulatory_system" title="Circulatory system">circulatory system</a>, as the purpose of <a href="/wiki/Valve" title="Valve">valves</a> in veins was identified, the left-to-right ventricle flow of blood through the circulatory system was described, and the <a href="/wiki/Hepatic_vein" class="mw-redirect" title="Hepatic vein">hepatic veins</a> were identified as a separate portion of the circulatory system. The <a href="/wiki/Lymphatic_system" title="Lymphatic system">lymphatic system</a> was also identified as a separate system at this time.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="17th_and_18th_centuries">17th and 18th centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_anatomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 17th and 18th centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_medicine_of_Akbar_03.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/The_medicine_of_Akbar_03.jpg/220px-The_medicine_of_Akbar_03.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="318" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/The_medicine_of_Akbar_03.jpg/330px-The_medicine_of_Akbar_03.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/The_medicine_of_Akbar_03.jpg/440px-The_medicine_of_Akbar_03.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="1734" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_medicine_of_Akbar_03.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illustration of The medicine of Akbar, Muqim Arzani, 1722</div></div></div>
<p>The study of anatomy flourished in the 17th and 18th centuries. At the beginning of the 17th century, the use of dissecting human cadavers influenced anatomy, leading to a spike in the study of anatomy. The advent of the printing press facilitated the exchange of ideas. Because the study of anatomy concerned observation and drawings, the popularity of the anatomist was equal to the quality of his drawing talents, and one need not be an expert in Latin to take part.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Many famous artists studied anatomy, attended dissections, and published drawings for money, from <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a> to <a href="/wiki/Rembrandt" title="Rembrandt">Rembrandt</a>. For the first time, prominent universities could teach something about anatomy through drawings, rather than relying on knowledge of Latin. Contrary to popular belief, the Church neither objected to nor obstructed anatomical research.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Only certified anatomists were allowed to perform dissections, and sometimes then only yearly. These dissections were sponsored by the city councilors and often charged an admission fee, rather like a circus act for scholars. Many European cities, such as Amsterdam, London, Copenhagen, Padua, and Paris, all had Royal anatomists (or some such office) tied to local government. Indeed, <a href="/wiki/Nicolaes_Tulp" title="Nicolaes Tulp">Nicolaes Tulp</a> was Mayor of Amsterdam for three terms. Though it was a risky business to perform dissections, and unpredictable depending on the availability of fresh bodies, <i>attending</i> dissections was legal.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The supply of printed anatomy books from Italy and France led to an increased demand for human cadavers for dissections. Since few bodies were voluntarily donated for dissection, royal charters were established which allowed prominent universities to use the bodies of hung criminals for dissections. However, there was still a shortage of bodies that could not accommodate for the high demand of bodies. Until the middle of the 18th century, there was a quota of ten cadavers for each the Royal College of Physicians and the Company of Barber Surgeons, the only two groups permitted to perform dissections. During the first half of the 18th century, William Cheselden challenged the Company of Barber Surgeon's exclusive rights on dissections. He was the first to hold regular anatomy lectures and demonstrations. He also wrote <i>The Anatomy of the Humane Body,</i> a student handbook of anatomy.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> In 1752, the rapid growth of medical schools in England and the pressing demand for cadavers led to the passage of the Murder Act. This allowed medical schools in England to legally dissect bodies of executed murderers for anatomical education and research and also aimed to prevent murder. To further increase the supply of cadavers, the government increased the number of crimes in which hanging was a punishment. Although the number of cadavers increased, it was still not enough to meet the demand of anatomical and medical training.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Since few bodies were voluntarily donated for dissection, criminals that were hung for murder were dissected. However, there was a shortage of bodies that could not accommodate the high demand of bodies.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> To cope with shortages of cadavers and the rise in medical students during the 17th and 18th centuries, <a href="/wiki/Body-snatching" class="mw-redirect" title="Body-snatching">body-snatching</a> and even <a href="/wiki/Anatomy_murder" title="Anatomy murder">anatomy murder</a> were practiced to obtain cadavers.<sup id="cite_ref-Rosner,_Lisa._2010_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosner,_Lisa._2010-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> 'Body snatching' was the act of sneaking into a graveyard, digging up a corpse and using it for study. Men known as 'resurrectionists' emerged as outside parties, who would steal corpses for a living and sell the bodies to anatomy schools. The leading London anatomist <a href="/wiki/John_Hunter_(surgeon)" title="John Hunter (surgeon)">John Hunter</a> paid for a regular supply of corpses for his anatomy school.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> During the 17th and 18th centuries, the perception of dissections had evolved into a form of capital punishment. Dissections were considered a dishonor. The corpse was mutilated and not suitable for a funeral. By the end of the 18th century, many European countries had passed legislation similar to the Murder Act in England to meet the demand of fresh cadavers and to reduce crime. Countries allowed institutions to use unclaimed bodies of paupers, prison inmates, and people in psychiatric and charitable hospitals for dissection.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Unfortunately, the lack of bodies available for dissection and the controversial air that surrounded anatomy in the late 17th century and early 18th century caused a halt in progress that is evident by the lack of updates made to anatomical texts of the time between editions. Additionally, most of the investigations into anatomy were aimed at developing the knowledge of physiology and surgery. Naturally this meant that a close examination of the more detailed aspects of anatomy that could advance anatomical knowledge was not a priority.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Paris Medicine was notorious for its influence on medical thought and its contributions to medical knowledge. The new hospital medicine in France during the late 18th century was brought about in part by the Law of 1794 which made physicians and surgeons equals in the world of medical care. The law came as a response to the increase demand for medical professionals capable of caring for the increase in injuries and diseases brought about by French Revolution. The law also supplemented schools with bodies for anatomical lessons. Ultimately this created the opportunity for the field of medicine to grow in the direction of "localism of pathological anatomy, the development of appropriate diagnostic techniques, and the numerical approach to disease and therapeutics."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The British Parliament passed the <a href="/wiki/Anatomy_Act_1832" title="Anatomy Act 1832">Anatomy Act 1832</a>, which finally provided for an adequate and legitimate supply of corpses by allowing legal dissection of executed murderers. The view of anatomist at the time, however, became similar to that of an executioner. Having one's body dissected was seen as a punishment worse than death, "if you stole a pig, you were hung. If you killed a man, you were hung and then dissected." Demand grew so great that some anatomist resorted to dissecting their own family members as well as robbing bodies from their graves.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many Europeans interested in the study of anatomy traveled to Italy, then the centre of anatomy. Only in Italy could certain important research methods be used, such as dissections on women. <a href="/wiki/Realdo_Colombo" title="Realdo Colombo">Realdo Colombo</a> (also known as Realdus Columbus) and <a href="/wiki/Gabriele_Falloppio" title="Gabriele Falloppio">Gabriele Falloppio</a> were pupils of <a href="/wiki/Andreas_Vesalius" title="Andreas Vesalius">Vesalius</a>. Columbus, as Vesalius's immediate successor in Padua, and afterwards professor at Rome, distinguished himself by describing the shape and cavities of the heart, the structure of the pulmonary artery and aorta and their valves, and tracing the course of the blood from the right to the left side of the heart.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The rise in anatomy lead to various discoveries and findings. In 1628, English physician William Harvey observed circulating blood through dissections of his father's and sister's bodies. He published <i>De moto cordis et sanguinis</i>, a treatise in which he explained his theory.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_40-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> In addition, during the 17th century, Galileo Galilei introduced the experimental method to scientific research. This led to innovation and change in anatomy. In Tuscany and Florence, Marcello Malpighi founded microscopic anatomy, and Nils Steensen studied the anatomy of lymph nodes and salivary glands. By the end of the 17th century, Gaetano Zumbo developed anatomical wax modeling techniques.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Antonio Valsalva, a student of Malpighi and a professor of anatomy at University of Bologna, was one of the greatest anatomists of the time. He is known by many as the founder of anatomy and physiology of the ear.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> In the 18th century, Giovanni Batista Morgagni related pre-mortem symptoms with post-mortem pathological findings using pathological anatomy in his book <i>De Sedibus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> This led to the rise of morbid anatomy in France and Europe. The rise of morbid anatomy was one of the contributing factors to the shift in power between doctors and physicians, giving power to the physicians over patients.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> With the invention of the Stethoscope in 1816, R.T.H. Laennec was able to help bridge the gap between a symptomatic approach to medicine and disease, to one based on anatomy and physiology. His disease and treatments were based on "pathological anatomy" and because this approach to disease was rooted in anatomy instead of symptoms, the process of evaluation and treatment were also forced to evolve.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> From the late 18th century to the early 19th century, the work of professionals such as Giambattista Morgagni, Scott Matthew Baillie, and Xavier Bichat served to demonstrate exactly how the detailed anatomical inspection of organs could lead to a more empirical means of understanding disease and health that would combine medical theory with medical practice. This "pathological anatomy" paved the way for "clinical pathology that applied the knowledge of opening up corpses and quantifying illnesses to treatments."<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Along with the popularity of anatomy and dissection came an increasing interest in the preservation of dissected specimens. In the 17th century, many of the anatomical specimens were dried and stored in cabinets. In the Netherlands, there were attempts to replicate Egyptian mummies by preserving soft tissue. This became known as Balsaming. In the 1660s the Dutch were also attempting to preserve organs by injecting wax to keep the organ's shape. Dyes and mercury were added to the wax to better differentiate and see various anatomical structures for academic and research anatomy. By the late 18th century, Thomas Pole published <i>The Anatomic Instructor</i>, which detailed how to dry and preserve specimens and soft tissue.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In the late 16th century, anatomists began exploring and pushing for contention that the study of anatomy could contribute to advancing the boundaries of natural philosophy. However, the majority of students were more interested in the practicality of anatomy, and less so in the advancement of knowledge of the subject. Students were interested in the technique of dissection rather than the philosophy of anatomy, and this was reflected in their criticism of Professors such as Girolamo Fabrici.<sup id="cite_ref-:02_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:02-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><a href="/wiki/Anatomical_theatre" title="Anatomical theatre">Anatomical theatres</a> became a popular form for anatomical teaching in the early 16th century. The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Padua" title="University of Padua">University of Padua</a> was the first and most widely known theatre, founded in 1594. As a result, Italy became the center for human dissection. People came from all over to watch as professors taught lectures on the <a href="/wiki/Human_physiology" class="mw-redirect" title="Human physiology">human physiology</a> and anatomy, as anyone was welcome to witness the spectacle. Participants "were fascinated by corporeal display, by the body undergoing dissection".<sup id="cite_ref-Klestinec_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Klestinec-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> Most professors did not do the dissections themselves. Instead they sat in seats above the bodies while hired hands did the cutting. Students and observers would be placed around the table in a circular, stadium-like arena and listen as professors explained the various anatomical parts. As anatomy theaters gained popularity throughout the 16th century, protocols were adjusted to account for the disruptions of students. Students moved beyond simply being eager to participate, and began stealing and vandalizing cadavers. Students were thus instructed to sit quietly and were to be penalized for disrupting the dissection. Moreover, preparatory lectures were mandatory in order to introduce the "subsequent observation of anatomy". The demonstrations were structured into dissections and lectures. The dissections focused on the skill of autopsy/vivisection while the lectures would center on the philosophical questions of anatomy. This is exemplary of how anatomy was viewed not only as the study of structures but also the study of the "body as an extension of the soul".<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> The 19th century eventually saw a move from anatomical theatres to classrooms, reducing "the number of people who could benefit from each cadaver".<sup id="cite_ref-Siddiquey_2009_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Siddiquey_2009-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century_anatomy">19th century anatomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_anatomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 19th century anatomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_anatomy_in_the_19th_century" title="History of anatomy in the 19th century">History of anatomy in the 19th century</a></div>
<p>During the 19th century, anatomical research was extended with <a href="/wiki/Histology" title="Histology">histology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Developmental_biology" title="Developmental biology">developmental biology</a> of both humans and <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>. Women, who were not allowed to attend medical school, could attend the anatomy theatres. From 1822 the Royal College of Surgeons forced unregulated schools to close.<sup id="cite_ref-McLachlan,_J._2006._p.243-53_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLachlan,_J._2006._p.243-53-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Medical museums provided examples in comparative anatomy, and were often used in teaching.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_anatomy">Modern anatomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_anatomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Modern anatomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anatomical research in the past hundred years has taken advantage of technological developments and growing understanding of sciences such as <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_biology" title="Evolutionary biology">evolutionary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Molecular_biology" title="Molecular biology">molecular biology</a> to create a thorough understanding of the body's organs and structures. Disciplines such as <a href="/wiki/Endocrinology" title="Endocrinology">endocrinology</a> have explained the purpose of glands that anatomists previously could not explain; medical devices such as <a href="/wiki/MRI" class="mw-redirect" title="MRI">MRI</a> machines and <a href="/wiki/CAT_scan" class="mw-redirect" title="CAT scan">CAT scanners</a> have enabled researchers to study organs, living or dead, in unprecedented detail. Progress today in anatomy is centered in the development, evolution, and function of anatomical features, as the macroscopic aspects of human anatomy have largely been catalogued. Non-human anatomy is particularly active as researchers use techniques ranging from <a href="/wiki/Finite_element_analysis" class="mw-redirect" title="Finite element analysis">finite element analysis</a> to molecular biology.
</p><p>To save time, some medical schools such as Birmingham, England have adopted prosection, where a demonstrator dissects and explains to an audience, in place of dissection by students. This enables students to observe more than one body. Improvements in colour images and photography means that an anatomy text is no longer an aid to dissection but rather a central material to learn from. Plastic <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_model" title="Anatomical model">anatomical models</a> are regularly used in anatomy teaching, offering a good substitute to the real thing. Use of living models for anatomy demonstration is once again becoming popular within teaching of anatomy. Surface landmarks that can be <a href="/wiki/Palpated" class="mw-redirect" title="Palpated">palpated</a> on another individual provide practice for future clinical situations. It is possible to do this on oneself; in the Integrated Biology course at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Berkeley" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Berkeley">University of Berkeley</a>, students are encouraged to "introspect"<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> on themselves and link what they are being taught to their own body.<sup id="cite_ref-McLachlan,_J._2006._p.243-53_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLachlan,_J._2006._p.243-53-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Donations of bodies have declined with public confidence in the medical profession.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> In Britain, the <a href="/wiki/Human_Tissue_Act_2004" title="Human Tissue Act 2004">Human Tissue Act 2004</a> has tightened up the availability of resources to anatomy departments. The outbreaks of <a href="/wiki/Bovine_spongiform_encephalitis" class="mw-redirect" title="Bovine spongiform encephalitis">bovine spongiform encephalitis</a> (BSE) in the late 1980s and early 1990s further restricted the handling of brain tissue.<sup id="cite_ref-McLachlan,_J._2006._p.243-53_56-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLachlan,_J._2006._p.243-53-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The controversy of <a href="/wiki/Gunther_von_Hagens" title="Gunther von Hagens">Gunther von Hagens</a> and public displays of dissections, preserved by <a href="/wiki/Plastination" title="Plastination">plastination</a>, may divide opinions on what is ethical or legal.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
