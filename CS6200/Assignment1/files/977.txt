<p><a href="/wiki/The" title="The">The</a> <b>saxophone</b> (referred to colloquially as the <b>sax</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Woodwind_instrument" title="Woodwind instrument">woodwind instrument</a>. Saxophones are usually made of <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">brass</a> and played with a <a href="/wiki/Single-reed_instrument" title="Single-reed instrument">single-reed</a> <a href="/wiki/Mouthpiece_(woodwind)" title="Mouthpiece (woodwind)">mouthpiece</a> similar to that of the <a href="/wiki/Clarinet" title="Clarinet">clarinet</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-freedictionary_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-freedictionary-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Although most saxophones are made from <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">brass</a>, they are categorized as <a href="/wiki/Woodwind_instruments" class="mw-redirect" title="Woodwind instruments">woodwind instruments</a>, because sound is produced by an oscillating reed, traditionally made out of woody <a href="/wiki/Cane_(grass)" title="Cane (grass)">cane</a>, rather than lips vibrating in a mouthpiece cup as with the <a href="/wiki/Brass_instrument" title="Brass instrument">brass instrument</a> family. As with the other woodwind instruments, the <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(music)" title="Pitch (music)">pitch</a> of the note being played is controlled by covering holes in the body tube to control the <a href="/wiki/Resonant_frequency" class="mw-redirect" title="Resonant frequency">resonant frequency</a> of the air column by changing the effective length of the tube.<sup id="cite_ref-Cottrell_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cottrell-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The saxophone is used in <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">classical music</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Concert_band" title="Concert band">concert bands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chamber_music" title="Chamber music">chamber music</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_concert_works_for_saxophone" title="List of concert works for saxophone">solo repertoire</a>, and, occasionally, <a href="/wiki/Orchestra" title="Orchestra">orchestras</a>), <a href="/wiki/Military_band" title="Military band">military bands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marching_band" title="Marching band">marching bands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Big_band" title="Big band">big bands</a> and jazz combos), and contemporary music. The saxophone is also used as a solo and melody instrument or as a member of a <a href="/wiki/Horn_section" title="Horn section">horn section</a> in some styles of <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_roll" title="Rock and roll">rock and roll</a> and <a href="/wiki/Popular_music" title="Popular music">popular music</a>. Saxophone players are called <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/saxophonist" class="extiw" title="wikt:saxophonist">saxophonists</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-freedictionary_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-freedictionary-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Since the first saxophone was invented by the Belgian instrument maker <a href="/wiki/Adolphe_Sax" title="Adolphe Sax">Adolphe Sax</a> in the early 1840s,<sup id="cite_ref-Saxophone_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saxophone-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> saxophones have been produced in a variety of series distinguished by <a href="/wiki/Transposing_instrument" title="Transposing instrument">transpositions within instrument sets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tuning_standard" class="mw-redirect" title="Tuning standard">tuning standard</a>. Sax patented the saxophone on 28 June 1846, in two groups of seven instruments each. Each series consisted of instruments ranked by pitch, in alternating transposition. The series pitched in B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> and E<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> soon became dominant and most saxophones encountered today are from this series. Instruments from the series pitched in C and F never gained a foothold and constituted only a small percentage of instruments made by Sax. <i>High Pitch</i> (also marked "H" or "HP") saxophones tuned <a href="/wiki/Sharp_(music)" title="Sharp (music)">sharper</a> than the (concert) <a href="/wiki/A440_(pitch_standard)" title="A440 (pitch standard)">A = 440 Hz</a> standard were produced into the early twentieth century for sonic qualities suited for outdoor uses, but are not playable to modern tuning and are considered obsolete. <i>Low Pitch</i> (also marked "L" or "LP") saxophones are equivalent in tuning to modern instruments. <a href="/wiki/C_soprano_saxophone" title="C soprano saxophone">C soprano</a> and <a href="/wiki/C_melody_saxophone" title="C melody saxophone">C melody</a> saxophones were produced for the casual market as parlor instruments during the early twentieth century. Saxophones in F were introduced during the late 1920s but never gained acceptance. The modern saxophone family consists entirely of instruments in the B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> – E<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> series, historical and experimental instruments notwithstanding.  The saxophones with widest use and availability are the soprano, alto, tenor, and baritone saxophones.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pitch_and_range">Pitch and range</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saxophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pitch and range">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright" style=""><div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px"><div class="thumbimage noresize" style="width:320px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Soprano_Extension_in_C.svg" class="image"><img href="/wiki/File:Baritone_Extension_in_C.svg" class="image"><img alt="Baritone Extension in C.svg" class="thumbcaption">The traditional keyed range in C major of the <a href="/wiki/Soprano_sax" class="mw-redirect" title="Soprano sax">soprano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alto_sax" class="mw-redirect" title="Alto sax">alto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tenor_sax" class="mw-redirect" title="Tenor sax">tenor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baritone_sax" class="mw-redirect" title="Baritone sax">baritone</a> when playing a B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> major <a href="/wiki/Scale_(music)" title="Scale (music)">scale</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>In the <i>keyed</i> (below <a href="/wiki/Overtone_series" class="mw-redirect" title="Overtone series">overtone</a>-produced <i><a href="/wiki/Altissimo" title="Altissimo">altissimo</a></i>) ranges of the various saxophones, the <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(music)" title="Pitch (music)">pitch</a> is controlled by <i>keys</i> with shallow <i>cups</i> in which are fastened leather <i>pads</i> that seal <i>toneholes</i>, controlling the resonant length, and thereby frequency, of the air column within the <i>bore</i>.  Small holes called <i>vents</i>, located between the toneholes and the mouthpiece, are opened by an <i>octave key</i> to raise the pitch by eliminating the <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_frequency" title="Fundamental frequency">fundamental frequency</a>, leaving the <a href="/wiki/First_harmonic" class="mw-redirect" title="First harmonic">first harmonic</a> as the frequency defining the pitch.  Most modern saxophones are keyed to produce a low B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> (relative to the instrument's <a href="/wiki/Transposing_instrument" title="Transposing instrument">transposition</a>) with all keys closed; modern baritone saxophones commonly play a low A and altos keyed to low A have been produced in the past.  The highest keyed note has traditionally been F two and a half octaves above low B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span>, while the keyed range is extended to F<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-sharp">&#x266f;</span></span> on most recent performance-class instruments.  A high G key is most common on modern soprano saxophones.  Notes above F are considered part of the altissimo register of any saxophone, and can be produced using advanced embouchure techniques and fingering combinations. Keywork facilitating altissimo playing is a feature of modern saxophones. Modern saxophone players have extended the range to over four <a href="/wiki/Octave" title="Octave">octaves</a> on tenor and alto.  Music for most saxophones is usually notated using <a href="/wiki/Treble_clef" class="mw-redirect" title="Treble clef">treble clef</a>.
</p><p>Because all saxophones use the same key arrangement and fingering to produce a given notated pitch, it is not difficult for a competent player to switch among the various sizes when the music has been suitably transposed, and many do so. Since the baritone and alto are pitched in E<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span>, players can read concert pitch music notated in the <a href="/wiki/Bass_clef" class="mw-redirect" title="Bass clef">bass clef</a> by reading it as if it were treble clef and adding three <a href="/wiki/Sharp_(music)" title="Sharp (music)">sharps</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Key_signature" title="Key signature">key signature</a>. This process, referred to as <i>clef substitution</i>, makes it possible for the Eb instruments to play from parts written for <a href="/wiki/Baritone_horn" title="Baritone horn">baritone horn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bassoon" title="Bassoon">bassoon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Euphonium" title="Euphonium">euphonium</a>, <a href="/wiki/String_bass" class="mw-redirect" title="String bass">string bass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trombone" title="Trombone">trombone</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Tuba" title="Tuba">tuba</a>. This can be useful if a band or orchestra lacks one of those instruments.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_features">Design features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saxophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Design features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>straight</i> <a href="/wiki/Soprano_saxophone" title="Soprano saxophone">soprano</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sopranino_saxophone" title="Sopranino saxophone">sopranino</a> saxophones consist of a straight conical tube with a flared <i>bell</i> at the end opposite the mouthpiece.  Alto and larger saxophones include a detachable curved <i>neck</i> above the highest tone hole, directing the mouthpiece to the player's mouth and, with rare exceptions, a U-shaped <i>bow</i> that directs the bore upward and a curve in the throat of the bell directing it forward. The set of curves near the bell has become a distinctive feature of the saxophone family, to the extent that soprano and even sopranino saxes are sometimes made in the curved style.   The baritone, bass, and contrabass saxophones accommodate the length of the bore with extra bows and right-angle bends between the main body and the mouthpiece.
</p><p>The left hand operates keys from the upper part of the body tube while the right hand operates keys from the lower part.  The right thumb sits under a <i>thumb hook</i> and left thumb is placed on a <i>thumb rest</i> to stabilize and balance the saxophone, while the weight of most saxophones is shared by the right thumb and a neckstrap attached to a <i>strap ring</i> on the rear of the body of the instrument. With the smaller instruments, relatively more of the weight is supported by the thumb.  The left thumb operates the octave key.
</p><p>Keys consist of the cups, levers, and pivots that control the position of the pads over the toneholes.  At rest, some keys are open and some are closed, held in position by springs that are overridden by finger or hand (<i>palm</i> keys) pressure.  The keys are activated by pressure on <i>key touches</i>, either directly on the pad cup or connected to it with levers, either directly or with joints called <i>linkages.</i>  The levers between the key cups and the pivots are called <i>key arms</i>.
</p><p>The fingering for the saxophone is a combination of that of the <a href="/wiki/Oboe" title="Oboe">oboe</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Boehm_system_(clarinet)" title="Boehm system (clarinet)">Boehm system</a><sup id="cite_ref-New_Grove_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_Grove-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and is similar to the <a href="/wiki/Flute" title="Flute">flute</a> or upper register of the clarinet. The <i>stack</i> keys are operated by the first, second, and third fingers on each hand with slightly concave button-style key touches (<i>key buttons</i>) operating with the same motion as the pad cups that they control.  The stack keys are linked to higher stack keys with <i>regulation bar</i> and <i>bridge arm</i> linkages.  Key buttons are advantageous for operating keys with direct downward finger pressure but provide disadvantages operating keys with other finger and hand motions, hence, their use on keys operated with such motions has diminished with the evolution of saxophone designs.
</p><p>Palm keys and the <i>front F</i> key operated by the left hand, and the <i>high F</i>, <i>high F<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-sharp">&#x266f;</span></span></i> and <i>high G</i> keys operated by the right hand, control the upper end of the keyed range and are used to vent altissimo notes.  <i>Chromatic</i> keys operated by the right hand provide alternate fingerings for F<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-sharp">&#x266f;</span></span>, B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span>, and C within the stack range.  The  fourth fingers of the right and left hands open keys to raise pitch by a <a href="/wiki/Semitone" title="Semitone">semitone</a> as well as close keys towards the lower range of the instrument, with the lowest pitch <i>bell keys</i> operated by the left hand.  The keys operated by the fourth fingers are referred to as <i>table</i> keys.  Instruments that play to low A have a left thumb key for that note.
</p><p>On saxophones produced since the early 1920s the G<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-sharp">&#x266f;</span></span> key operated from the left hand table is closed by closing keys on the lower stack regardless of pressure on the G<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-sharp">&#x266f;</span></span> actuating mechanism (<i>F-linked</i>, or <i>stack-linked</i>, G<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-sharp">&#x266f;</span></span> mechanism).  That feature vastly increases the speed and playability of certain intervals to the point that saxophones with <i>direct G<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-sharp">&#x266f;</span></span></i> action, in which the key stays open when the lower stack keys are depressed, are considered obsolete.  Modern left hand tables also <i>articulate</i> the G<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-sharp">&#x266f;</span></span> key with the low C<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-sharp">&#x266f;</span></span>, B, and B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> keys to open it when any of those keys are depressed and the right hand stack keys are not.  That also provides significant advantages for playing certain intervals near the lower range of the instrument.  Some players willingly forego the benefits of the articulated G<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-sharp">&#x266f;</span></span> to play vintage instruments, but a front F key and a stack-linked G<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-sharp">&#x266f;</span></span> key are regarded as critical features by serious players.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Materials">Materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saxophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From the earliest days of the saxophone the body and key cups have been made from sheet brass stock, owing to its workability in forming complex shapes.  Mechanical keywork is assembled from components either hand-tooled or machined from other forms of brass stock.  <a href="/wiki/King_Musical_Instruments" title="King Musical Instruments">King</a> introduced saxophones with necks and bells of sterling silver during the 1930s and continued that "silversonic" scheme into the early 1960s. Yanagisawa revived the scheme during the 1980s and later introduced entire instruments of sterling silver.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  
<a href="/wiki/Julius_Keilwerth" title="Julius Keilwerth">Keilwerth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Mauriat" title="Paul Mauriat">P. Mauriat</a> have used <a href="/wiki/Nickel_silver" title="Nickel silver">nickel silver</a>, a copper-nickel-zinc alloy more commonly used for flutes, for the bodies of some saxophone models.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> 
For visual and tonal effect, higher copper <a href="/wiki/Aluminum_brass" class="mw-redirect" title="Aluminum brass">variants of brass</a>  are sometimes substituted for the more common "yellow brass" and "cartridge brass." <a href="/wiki/Yanagisawa_Wind_Instruments" title="Yanagisawa Wind Instruments">Yanagisawa</a> made its 902 and 992 series saxophones with the high copper alloy <a href="/wiki/Phosphor_bronze" title="Phosphor bronze">phosphor bronze</a> to achieve a darker, more "vintage" tone than the brass 901 and 991 models.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Other saxophones made of high copper alloys are sold under various brands.
</p><p>Other materials are used for some mechanical parts and keywork.  Since 1920, most saxophones have replaceable key buttons operating the stack keys, usually made from either plastic or <a href="/wiki/Mother_of_pearl" class="mw-redirect" title="Mother of pearl">mother of pearl</a>. Some saxophones are made with <a href="/wiki/Abalone" title="Abalone">abalone</a>, stone, or wood key buttons.  On some premium models, the key button material is used to form the convex key touches for other keys.  The rods and screw pins that the keywork's hinges pivot on, and the needle and <a href="/wiki/Leaf_spring" title="Leaf spring">leaf springs</a> that hold keys in their rest position, are usually made of blued or stainless steel. Mechanical buffers of felt, cork, leather, and various synthetic materials are used to reduce friction, to minimize mechanical noise from movement of keys, and to optimize the action of the keywork for positive pad sealing, intonation, speed, and "feel."  Nickel silver is sometimes used for hinges for its advantages of mechanical durability, although the most common material for such applications has remained brass.  Saxophones with high copper bodies still have brass keywork owing to its more suitable mechanical properties relative to those alloys.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Surface_finishes">Surface finishes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saxophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Surface finishes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Before final assembly, manufacturers usually apply a finish to the surface of the horn.  The most common finish is a thin coating of clear or colored <a href="/wiki/Acrylic_lacquer" class="mw-redirect" title="Acrylic lacquer">acrylic lacquer</a>. The lacquer serves to protect the brass from oxidation and maintains its shiny appearance. Silver or gold plating are offered as premium options on some models. Some silver plated saxophones are also lacquered.  Plating saxophones with gold is an expensive process because an underplating of silver is required for the gold to adhere to.<sup id="cite_ref-jazzbarisax_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jazzbarisax-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Nickel plating has been used on the bodies of early budget model saxophones and is commonly used on keywork when a more durable finish than lacquer is desired, mostly with student model saxophones. Chemical surface treatment of the base metal has come into use as an alternative to the lacquer and plating finishes in recent years.  Some saxophonists, retailers, and repair technicians argue that the type of lacquer or plating (or absence of lacquer)<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> may be a factor affecting the instrument's tone quality.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mouthpiece_and_reed">Mouthpiece and reed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saxophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mouthpiece and reed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Mouthpiece_(woodwind)" title="Mouthpiece (woodwind)">Mouthpiece (woodwind)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reed_(instrument)" class="mw-redirect" title="Reed (instrument)">Reed (instrument)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reed_clipper" title="Reed clipper">Reed clipper</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ligature_(musical_instrument)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ligature (musical instrument)">ligature (musical instrument)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mouthpiece_tenor_saxophone.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Mouthpiece_tenor_saxophone.jpg/200px-Mouthpiece_tenor_saxophone.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="133" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Mouthpiece_tenor_saxophone.jpg/300px-Mouthpiece_tenor_saxophone.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Mouthpiece_tenor_saxophone.jpg/400px-Mouthpiece_tenor_saxophone.jpg 2x" data-file-width="990" data-file-height="658" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mouthpiece_tenor_saxophone.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tenor saxophone mouthpieces, ligatures, reed, and cap</div></div></div>
<p>The saxophone uses a single-reed <a href="/wiki/Mouthpiece_(woodwind)" title="Mouthpiece (woodwind)">mouthpiece</a> similar to that of the clarinet. Each size of saxophone (alto, tenor, etc.) uses a different size of reed and mouthpiece.
</p><p>Most saxophonists use reeds made from <i><a href="/wiki/Arundo_donax" title="Arundo donax">Arundo donax</a></i> cane, but since middle of the twentieth century some have also been made of fiberglass and other composite materials.  Saxophone reeds are proportioned slightly differently from clarinet reeds, being wider for the same length.  Reeds are commercially available in a vast array of brands, styles, and strengths. Saxophonists experiment with reeds of different strength (hardnesses) and material to find which strength and cut suits their mouthpiece, embouchure, physiology, and playing style.
</p><p>Mouthpiece design has a profound impact on tone.<sup id="cite_ref-erousseau_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-erousseau-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Different mouthpiece design characteristics and features tend to be favored for different styles. Early mouthpieces were designed to produce a "warm" and "round" sound for classical playing. Among classical mouthpieces, those with a concave ("excavated") <i>chamber</i> are more true to Adolphe Sax's original design; these provide a softer or less piercing tone favored by the <a href="/wiki/Sigurd_Rasch%C3%A8r" title="Sigurd Raschèr">Raschèr</a> school of classical playing. Saxophonists who follow the French school of classical playing, influenced by <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Mule" title="Marcel Mule">Marcel Mule</a>, generally use mouthpieces with smaller chambers for a somewhat "brighter" sound with relatively more upper harmonics.  The use of the saxophone in dance orchestras and jazz ensembles from the 1920s onward placed emphasis on <i><a href="/wiki/Dynamic_range" title="Dynamic range">dynamic range</a></i> and projection, leading to innovation in mouthpiece chamber shapes and <i>tip</i> designs, as well as metal construction. At the opposite extreme from the classical mouthpieces are those with a small chamber and a low clearance above the reed between the tip and the chamber, called high <i>baffle</i>. These produce a bright sound with maximum projection, suitable for having a sound stand out among amplified instruments and are commonly used in modern pop and smooth jazz.
</p><p>Mouthpieces come in a wide variety of materials, including <a href="/wiki/Vulcanization" title="Vulcanization">vulcanized</a> rubber (sometimes called <a href="/wiki/Hard_rubber" class="mw-redirect" title="Hard rubber">hard rubber</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ebonite" title="Ebonite">ebonite</a>), plastic, and metals such as bronze or <a href="/wiki/Surgical_steel" class="mw-redirect" title="Surgical steel">surgical steel</a>. Less common materials that have been used include wood, glass, crystal, porcelain, and bone. Recently, <a href="/wiki/Delrin" class="mw-redirect" title="Delrin">Delrin</a> has been added to the stock of mouthpiece materials.
</p><p>The effect of mouthpiece materials on tone of the saxophone has been the subject of much debate. According to <a href="/wiki/Larry_Teal" title="Larry Teal">Larry Teal</a>, the mouthpiece material has little, if any, effect on the sound, and the physical dimensions give a mouthpiece its tone color.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> There are examples of "dark" sounding metal pieces and "bright" sounding hard rubber pieces. The lower rigidity of hard rubber relative to metal restricts some design characteristics affecting tone and response more than with metal. The extra bulk required near the tip with hard rubber affects mouth position and airflow characteristics.  Recently, increased <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> of the mouthpiece over the <i>shank</i>, which fits over the neck cork, has become a design feature to enhance the integrity of the <a href="/wiki/Harmonic_series_(music)" title="Harmonic series (music)">harmonic series</a> by stabilizing the mouthpiece/neck connection.  Shank weights (large rings of brass over the shank) are used with some Delrin mouthpieces to increase "resonance and projection."<sup id="cite_ref-goodson_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-goodson-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Other "hybrid" designs with a hard rubber body and a substantial metal shank have a similar mass distribution, although its contribution to sound characteristics is not highlighted in product descriptions.<sup id="cite_ref-bari_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bari-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saxophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_development_and_adoption">Early development and adoption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saxophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Early development and adoption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Adolphe_Sax.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Adolphe_Sax.jpg/220px-Adolphe_Sax.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="347" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Adolphe_Sax.jpg/330px-Adolphe_Sax.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Adolphe_Sax.jpg/440px-Adolphe_Sax.jpg 2x" data-file-width="748" data-file-height="1179" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Adolphe_Sax.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Adolphe_Sax" title="Adolphe Sax">Adolphe Sax</a>, the inventor of the saxophone</div></div></div>
<p>The saxophone was designed around 1840 by <a href="/wiki/Adolphe_Sax" title="Adolphe Sax">Adolphe Sax</a>, a Belgian instrument maker, <a href="/wiki/Flautist" class="mw-redirect" title="Flautist">flautist</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Clarinet" title="Clarinet">clarinetist</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Saxophone_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saxophone-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Born in <a href="/wiki/Dinant" title="Dinant">Dinant</a> and originally based in <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>, he moved to Paris in 1842 to establish his musical instrument business. Before working on the saxophone, he made several improvements to the <a href="/wiki/Bass_clarinet" title="Bass clarinet">bass clarinet</a> by improving its keywork and acoustics and extending its lower range. Sax was also a maker of the <a href="/wiki/Ophicleide" title="Ophicleide">ophicleide</a>, a large conical brass instrument in the bass register with keys similar to a woodwind instrument. His experience with these two instruments allowed him to develop the skills and technologies needed to make the first saxophones.
</p><p>As an outgrowth of his work improving the bass clarinet, Sax began developing an instrument with the projection of a brass instrument and the agility of a woodwind. He wanted it to <a href="/wiki/Overblowing" title="Overblowing">overblow</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Octave" title="Octave">octave</a>, unlike the clarinet, which rises in <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(music)" title="Pitch (music)">pitch</a> by a <a href="/wiki/Interval_(music)" title="Interval (music)">twelfth</a> when overblown. An instrument that overblows at the octave has identical <a href="/wiki/Fingering_(music)" title="Fingering (music)">fingering</a> for both <a href="/wiki/Register_(music)" title="Register (music)">registers</a>.
</p><p>Sax created an instrument with a single-reed mouthpiece and conical brass body. Having constructed saxophones in several sizes in the early 1840s, Sax applied for, and received, a 15-year patent for the instrument on 28 June 1846.<sup id="cite_ref-bassax_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bassax-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The patent encompassed 14 versions of the fundamental design, split into two categories of seven instruments each, and ranging from <a href="/wiki/Sopranino_saxophone" title="Sopranino saxophone">sopranino</a> to <a href="/wiki/Contrabass_saxophone" title="Contrabass saxophone">contrabass</a>.  A limited number of instruments in the series pitched in F and C were produced by Sax, but the series pitched in E<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> and B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> quickly became the standard.  All the instruments were given an initial written range from the B below the treble staff to the E<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> one half-step below the third <a href="/wiki/Ledger_line" title="Ledger line">ledger line</a> above staff, giving each saxophone a range of two and a half octaves.  Sax's patent expired in 1866.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Thereafter, numerous other instrument manufacturers implemented their own improvements to the design and keywork.
</p><p>Sax's original keywork, which was based on the <i>Triebert system 3</i> <a href="/wiki/Oboe" title="Oboe">oboe</a> for the left hand and the <i><a href="/wiki/Boehm_system_(clarinet)" title="Boehm system (clarinet)">Boehm</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Clarinet" title="Clarinet">clarinet</a> for the right, was simplistic and made certain legato passages and wide intervals extremely difficult to finger; that system would later evolve with extra keys, linkage mechanisms, and alternate fingerings to make some intervals less difficult.
</p><p>Early in the development of the saxophone the upper keyed range was extended to E, then F above the staff; 1880s era sheet music for saxophone was written for the range of low B to F.  In 1887 the <a href="/wiki/Buffet-Crampon" class="mw-redirect" title="Buffet-Crampon">Buffet-Crampon</a> company obtained a patent for extending the bell and adding an extra key to extend the range downwards by one <a href="/wiki/Semitone" title="Semitone">semitone</a> to B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>  This extension is currently standard in most modern designs, with the notable exception of baritone saxophones further extended and keyed to low A. The upper range to F would remain the standard for nearly a century until the altissimo F<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-sharp">&#x266f;</span></span> key became common on modern saxophones.
</p><p>In the 1840s and 1850s, Sax's invention gained use in small classical ensembles (both all-saxophone and mixed), as a solo instrument, and in French and British military bands.  Saxophone method books were published and saxophone instruction was offered at conservatories in France, Switzerland, Belgium, Spain, and Italy.  By 1856 the French <i><a href="/wiki/Garde_Republicaine" class="mw-redirect" title="Garde Republicaine">Garde Republicaine</a></i> band included eight saxophones, making it the large ensemble that featured the instrument most prominently.  The saxophone was used experimentally in orchestral scores, but never came into widespread use as an orchestral instrument.  In 1853-54 the orchestra of Louis Antoine Jullien featured a soprano saxophone on a concert tour of the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After an early period of interest and support from classical music communities in Europe, their interest in the instrument waned in the late nineteenth century.  Saxophone teaching at the Paris Conservatory was suspended from 1870 to 1900 and classical saxophone repertoire stagnated during that period.<sup id="cite_ref-bassax_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bassax-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  But it was during this same period that the saxophone began to be promoted in the United States, largely through the efforts of <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Gilmore" title="Patrick Gilmore">Patrick Gilmore</a>, leader of the <i>22nd Regiment band</i>, and Edward A. Lefebre, a Dutch emigre and saxophonist with family business associations with Sax.  Lefebre settled in New York in early 1872 after he arrived as a clarinetist with a British opera company.  Gilmore organized the <a href="/wiki/World_peace_jubilee_and_international_music_festival" class="mw-redirect" title="World peace jubilee and international music festival">World Peace Jubilee and International Music Festival</a> taking place in Boston that summer. The Garde Republicaine band performed and Lefebre was a clarinetist with the Great Festival Orchestra for that event.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In the fall of 1873 Gilmore was reorganizing the 22nd Regiment band under the influence of the Garde Republicaine band and recruited Lefebre, who had established a reputation in New York as a saxophonist over the previous year.  Gilmore's band soon featured a soprano-alto-tenor-baritone saxophone section, which also performed as a quartet.  The Gilmore-Lefebre association lasted until Gilmore's death in 1892, during which time Lefebre also performed in smaller ensembles of various sizes and instrumentation, and worked with composers to increase light classical and popular repertoire for saxophone.<sup id="cite_ref-Noyes,_Chapter_IV_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Noyes,_Chapter_IV-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Lefebre's later promotional efforts were extremely significant in broadening adoption of the saxophone.  Starting towards the end of the 1880s he consulted with the brass instrument manufacturer <a href="/wiki/C.G._Conn" title="C.G. Conn">C.G. Conn</a> to develop and start production of improved saxophones to replace the costly, scantly available, and mechanically unreliable European instruments in the American market.  The early 1890s saw regular production of saxophones commence at Conn and its offshoot <a href="/wiki/Buescher_Band_Instrument_Company" title="Buescher Band Instrument Company">Buescher Manufacturing Company</a>, which dramatically increased availability of saxophones in the US.  Lefebre worked with the music publisher <a href="/wiki/Carl_Fischer_Music" title="Carl Fischer Music">Carl Fischer</a> to distribute his transcriptions, arrangements, and original works for saxophone, and worked with the Conn Conservatory to further saxophone pedagogy in the US.  Lefebre's associations with Conn and Fischer lasted into the first decade of the twentieth century and Fischer continued to publish new arrangements of Lefebre's works posthumously.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_twentieth-century_growth_and_development">Early twentieth-century growth and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saxophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Early twentieth-century growth and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While the saxophone remained marginal and regarded mainly as a novelty instrument in the classical music world, many new musical niches were established for it during the early decades of the twentieth century.  Its early use in <a href="/wiki/Vaudeville" title="Vaudeville">Vaudeville</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ragtime" title="Ragtime">ragtime</a> bands around the turn of the century laid the groundwork for its use in dance orchestras and eventually jazz.  As the market for saxophones grew in the US, the manufacturing industry grew; the <a href="/wiki/Martin_Band_Instrument_Company" title="Martin Band Instrument Company">Martin Band Instrument Company</a> started producing saxophones between 1905 and 1912, and the Cleveland Band Instrument Company started producing saxophones under contract to the <a href="/wiki/H._N._White_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="H. N. White Company">H. N. White Company</a> in 1916.  The saxophone was promoted for the casual market with introduction of the <a href="/wiki/C_soprano_saxophone" title="C soprano saxophone">C-soprano</a> and <a href="/wiki/C_melody_saxophone" title="C melody saxophone">C-melody</a> (between alto and tenor) saxophones to play in key with pianos from the same sheet music.  Production of such instruments stopped during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>.  During the 1920s the saxophone came into use as a jazz instrument, fostered by the influences of the <a href="/wiki/Fletcher_Henderson" title="Fletcher Henderson">Fletcher Henderson</a> Orchestra and the <a href="/wiki/Duke_Ellington_Orchestra" class="mw-redirect" title="Duke Ellington Orchestra">Duke Ellington Orchestra</a>.  Starting in the late 1920s and early 1930s, the modern era of classical saxophone was launched largely through the efforts of <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Mule" title="Marcel Mule">Marcel Mule</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sigurd_Rasch%C3%A8r" title="Sigurd Raschèr">Sigurd Raschèr</a>, and classical repertoire for the instrument expanded rapidly.
</p><p>The use of the saxophone for more dynamic and more technically demanding styles of playing added incentive for improvements in keywork and acoustic design. Early saxophones had two separate <a href="/wiki/Octave_key" class="mw-redirect" title="Octave key">octave keys</a> operated by the left thumb to control the two octave vents required on alto and larger saxophones. A substantial advance in keywork around the turn of the century was the development of mechanisms by which the left thumb operates the two octave vents with a single octave key.  <a href="/wiki/Ergonomic" class="mw-redirect" title="Ergonomic">Ergonomic</a> design of keywork evolved rapidly during the 1920s and 1930s.  The front F mechanism supporting alternate fingerings for high E and F, and stack-linked G<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-sharp">&#x266f;</span></span> key action, became standard during the 1920s, followed by improvements to the left hand table key mechanisms controlling the G<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-sharp">&#x266f;</span></span> and bell keys.  New bore designs during the 1920s and 1930s resulted from the quest for improved <a href="/wiki/Intonation_(music)" title="Intonation (music)">intonation</a>, dynamic response, and tonal qualities.  The 1920s were also the era of design experiments such as the Buescher straight altos and tenors, the King <i>Saxello</i> soprano, the C.G. Conn <i>mezzo-soprano</i> saxophone keyed in F, and the <i>Conn-O-Sax</i> saxophone – English horn hybrid.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_modern_saxophone_emerges">The modern saxophone emerges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saxophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The modern saxophone emerges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The modern layout of the saxophone emerged during the 1930s and 1940s, first with right-side bell keys introduced by <a href="/wiki/C._G._Conn" class="mw-redirect" title="C. G. Conn">C. G. Conn</a> on baritones, then by <a href="/wiki/King_Musical_Instruments" title="King Musical Instruments">King</a> on altos and tenors. The mechanics of the left hand table were revolutionized by <a href="/wiki/Henri_Selmer_Paris" title="Henri Selmer Paris">Selmer</a> with their <a href="/wiki/Balanced_action" title="Balanced action">Balanced Action</a> instruments in 1936, capitalizing on the right-side bell key layout. In 1948 Selmer introduced their Super Action saxophones with offset left and right hand stack keys. Between 30 and 40 years after Selmer devised their final layout it had been adopted for virtually every saxophone being produced, from student to professional models.
</p><p>The high F<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-sharp">&#x266f;</span></span> key was also first introduced as an option on the Balanced Action model, although it took several decades for it to gain acceptance because of perceived deleterious effects on intonation in its early implementations.<sup id="cite_ref-Hales_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hales-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saxophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_military_bands_and_classical_music">In military bands and classical music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saxophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: In military bands and classical music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The saxophone first gained popularity in <a href="/wiki/Military_band" title="Military band">military bands</a>. Although the instrument was initially ignored in Germany, French and Belgian military bands were quick to include the instrument in their ensembles. Most French and Belgian military bands incorporate at least a quartet of saxophones, comprising an E<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> baritone, B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> tenor, E<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> alto and B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> soprano. These four instruments have proved the most popular of all of Sax's creations, with the E<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> contrabass and B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> bass usually considered impractically large and the E<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> sopranino insufficiently powerful. British military bands tend to include at minimum two saxophonists, on the alto and tenor.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The saxophone was introduced into the <a href="/wiki/Concert_band" title="Concert band">concert band</a>, which usually calls for an E<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> alto saxophone, a B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> tenor saxophone, and an E<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> baritone saxophone. A concert band may include two altos, one tenor, and one baritone. A B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> soprano saxophone is also used, in which case it is played by the first alto saxophonist. A bass saxophone in B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> is used in some concert band music (especially music by <a href="/wiki/Percy_Grainger" title="Percy Grainger">Percy Grainger</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:163px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sigurd_Rascher.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/2/28/Sigurd_Rascher.jpg" decoding="async" width="161" height="200" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="161" data-file-height="200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sigurd_Rascher.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Classical saxophonist <a href="/wiki/Sigurd_Rasch%C3%A8r" title="Sigurd Raschèr">Sigurd Raschèr</a></div></div></div>
<p>Saxophones are used in chamber music, such as <a href="/wiki/Saxophone_quartet" title="Saxophone quartet">saxophone quartets</a> and other chamber combinations of instruments. The classical saxophone quartet consists of a B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> soprano saxophone, E<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> alto saxophone, B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> tenor saxophone, and E<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> baritone saxophone (SATB). On occasion, the soprano is replaced with a second alto sax (AATB); a few professional saxophone quartets have featured non-standard instrumentation, such as <a href="/wiki/James_Fei" title="James Fei">James Fei</a>'s Alto Quartet<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> (four altos).
</p><p>There is a repertoire of classical compositions and arrangements for the <a href="/wiki/SATB" title="SATB">SATB</a> instrumentation dating back to the nineteenth century, particularly by French composers who knew Sax. However, the largest body of chamber works for saxophone are from the modern era of classical saxophone initiated by <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Mule" title="Marcel Mule">Marcel Mule</a> in 1928.  <a href="/wiki/Sigurd_Rasch%C3%A8r" title="Sigurd Raschèr">Sigurd Raschèr</a> followed as a soloist in orchestral works, starting in 1931, and also figured prominently in development of modern classical saxophone repertoire.  The Mule quartet is often considered the prototype for quartets due the level of virtuosity demonstrated by its members and its central role in the development of modern quartet repertoire. However, organized quartets existed before Mule's ensemble, the prime example being the quartet headed by Edward A. Lefebre (1834–1911), which was a subset of <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Gilmore" title="Patrick Gilmore">Patrick Gilmore</a>'s 22nd Regiment band between 1873 and 1893.<sup id="cite_ref-Noyes,_Chapter_IV_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Noyes,_Chapter_IV-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 20th and 21st centuries, the saxophone found increased popularity in symphony orchestras. The instrument has also been used in genres such as opera and choral music. Many musical theatre scores include parts for saxophone, sometimes doubling another woodwind or brass instrument.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_jazz_and_popular_music">In jazz and popular music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saxophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: In jazz and popular music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:(African_American)_Jazz_Band_and_Leader_Back_with_(African_American)_15th_New_York._Lieutenant_Jame_._._._-_NARA_-_533506.tif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/%28African_American%29_Jazz_Band_and_Leader_Back_with_%28African_American%29_15th_New_York._Lieutenant_Jame_._._._-_NARA_-_533506.tif/lossy-page1-300px-%28African_American%29_Jazz_Band_and_Leader_Back_with_%28African_American%29_15th_New_York._Lieutenant_Jame_._._._-_NARA_-_533506.tif.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="232" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/%28African_American%29_Jazz_Band_and_Leader_Back_with_%28African_American%29_15th_New_York._Lieutenant_Jame_._._._-_NARA_-_533506.tif/lossy-page1-450px-%28African_American%29_Jazz_Band_and_Leader_Back_with_%28African_American%29_15th_New_York._Lieutenant_Jame_._._._-_NARA_-_533506.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/%28African_American%29_Jazz_Band_and_Leader_Back_with_%28African_American%29_15th_New_York._Lieutenant_Jame_._._._-_NARA_-_533506.tif/lossy-page1-600px-%28African_American%29_Jazz_Band_and_Leader_Back_with_%28African_American%29_15th_New_York._Lieutenant_Jame_._._._-_NARA_-_533506.tif.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3000" data-file-height="2324" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:(African_American)_Jazz_Band_and_Leader_Back_with_(African_American)_15th_New_York._Lieutenant_Jame_._._._-_NARA_-_533506.tif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/SS_Potsdam_(1900)" title="SS Potsdam (1900)">SS Stockholm</a></i>. 369th Infantry Regiment Band and leader Lt. James Reese Europe, winter 1918-1919</div></div></div>
<p>Coincident with the more widespread availability of saxophones in the US around the turn of the century was the rise of <a href="/wiki/Ragtime" title="Ragtime">ragtime</a> music.  The bands featuring the <a href="/wiki/Syncopated" class="mw-redirect" title="Syncopated">syncopated</a> latin- and African-American rhythmic influences of ragtime were an exciting new feature of the American cultural landscape and provided the groundwork for new styles of dancing.  The rise of dance bands into the 1920s followed from the popularity of ragtime.  Two of the best known ragtime-playing brass bands with saxophones were those led by <a href="/wiki/W._C._Handy" title="W. C. Handy">W. C. Handy</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_R._Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="James R. Europe">James R. Europe</a>.  The saxophone was also used in <a href="/wiki/Vaudeville" title="Vaudeville">Vaudeville</a> entertainment during the same period.  Ragtime, Vaudeville, and dance bands introduced much of the American public to the saxophone.  Europe's 369th Infantry Regiment Band popularized ragtime in France during its 1918 tour.<sup id="cite_ref-Scott1919_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scott1919-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rudy_Wiedoeft" title="Rudy Wiedoeft">Rudy Wiedoeft</a> became the best known individual saxophone stylist and virtuoso during this period leading into the "saxophone craze" of the 1920s.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Following it, the saxophone became featured in music as diverse as the "sweet" music of <a href="/wiki/Paul_Whiteman" title="Paul Whiteman">Paul Whiteman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guy_Lombardo" title="Guy Lombardo">Guy Lombardo</a>, jazz, swing, and large stage show bands.
</p><p>The rise of the saxophone as a jazz instrument followed its widespread adoption in dance bands during the early 1920s.  The <a href="/wiki/Fletcher_Henderson_Orchestra" class="mw-redirect" title="Fletcher Henderson Orchestra">Fletcher Henderson Orchestra</a>, formed in 1923, featured arrangements to back up improvisation, bringing the first elements of jazz to the large dance band format.<sup id="cite_ref-aaj_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aaj-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Following the innovations of the Fletcher Henderson Orchestra, the <a href="/wiki/Duke_Ellington_Orchestra" class="mw-redirect" title="Duke Ellington Orchestra">Duke Ellington Orchestra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jean_Goldkette" title="Jean Goldkette">Jean Goldkette</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Victor_Recording_Orchestra" title="Victor Recording Orchestra">Victor Recording Orchestra</a> featured jazz solos with saxophones and other instruments.  The association of dance bands with jazz would reach its peak with the <a href="/wiki/Swing_music" title="Swing music">swing music</a> of the 1930s. The large show band format, influenced by the 1930s swing bands, would be used  as backing for popular vocalists and stage shows in the post World War II era, and provided a foundation for big band jazz.  Show bands with saxophone sections became a staple of television talk shows (such as the <a href="/wiki/Tonight_Show" class="mw-redirect" title="Tonight Show">Tonight Show</a> that featured bands led by <a href="/wiki/Doc_Severinsen" title="Doc Severinsen">Doc Severinsen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Branford_Marsalis" title="Branford Marsalis">Branford Marsalis</a>) and Las Vegas stage shows. The swing era fostered the later saxophone styles that permeated <a href="/wiki/Bebop" title="Bebop">bebop</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rhythm_and_blues" title="Rhythm and blues">rhythm and blues</a> in the early postwar era.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Coleman_Hawkins.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Coleman_Hawkins.jpg/200px-Coleman_Hawkins.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="229" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Coleman_Hawkins.jpg/300px-Coleman_Hawkins.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Coleman_Hawkins.jpg/400px-Coleman_Hawkins.jpg 2x" data-file-width="440" data-file-height="504" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Coleman_Hawkins.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Coleman Hawkins, the most influential saxophone stylist of Jazz's early period, c. 1945</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Coleman_Hawkins" title="Coleman Hawkins">Coleman Hawkins</a> established the tenor saxophone as a jazz solo instrument during his stint with Fletcher Henderson from 1923 to 1934.  Hawkins' <a href="/wiki/Arpeggiated" class="mw-redirect" title="Arpeggiated">arpeggiated</a>, rich-toned, vibrato-laden style was the main influence on swing era tenor players before <a href="/wiki/Lester_Young" title="Lester Young">Lester Young</a>, and his influence continued with other big-toned tenor players into the era of modern jazz.  Among the tenor players directly influenced by him were <a href="/wiki/Chu_Berry" title="Chu Berry">Chu Berry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Barnet" title="Charlie Barnet">Charlie Barnet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tex_Beneke" title="Tex Beneke">Tex Beneke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ben_Webster" title="Ben Webster">Ben Webster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vido_Musso" title="Vido Musso">Vido Musso</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herschel_Evans" title="Herschel Evans">Herschel Evans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buddy_Tate" title="Buddy Tate">Buddy Tate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Don_Byas" title="Don Byas">Don Byas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-New_Grove_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_Grove-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  Hawkins' band mate <a href="/wiki/Benny_Carter" title="Benny Carter">Benny Carter</a> and Duke Ellington's alto saxophonist <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Hodges" title="Johnny Hodges">Johnny Hodges</a> became influential on swing era alto styles, while <a href="/wiki/Harry_Carney" title="Harry Carney">Harry Carney</a> brought the baritone saxophone to prominence with the Duke Ellington Orchestra. The New Orleans player <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Bechet" title="Sidney Bechet">Sidney Bechet</a> gained recognition for playing the soprano saxophone during the 1920s, but the instrument did not come into wide use until the modern era of jazz.
</p><p>As Chicago style jazz evolved from <a href="/wiki/Dixieland" title="Dixieland">New Orleans jazz</a> in the 1920s, one of its defining features was the addition of saxophones to the ensemble.  The small Chicago ensembles offered more improvisational freedom than did the New Orleans or large band formats, fostering the innovations of saxophonists <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Dorsey" title="Jimmy Dorsey">Jimmy Dorsey</a> (alto), <a href="/wiki/Frankie_Trumbauer" title="Frankie Trumbauer">Frankie Trumbauer</a> (c-melody), <a href="/wiki/Bud_Freeman" title="Bud Freeman">Bud Freeman</a> (tenor) and <a href="/wiki/Stump_Evans" title="Stump Evans">Stump Evans</a> (baritone).  Dorsey and Trumbauer became important influences on tenor saxophonist Lester Young.<sup id="cite_ref-New_Grove_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_Grove-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Lester Young's approach on tenor saxophone differed from Hawkins', emphasizing more melodic "linear" playing that wove in and out of the chordal structure and longer phrases that differed from those suggested by the tune. He used vibrato less, fitting it to the passage he was playing. His tone was smoother and darker that that of his 1930s contemporaries. Young's playing was a major influence on the modern jazz saxophonists <a href="/wiki/Al_Cohn" title="Al Cohn">Al Cohn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stan_Getz" title="Stan Getz">Stan Getz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zoot_Sims" title="Zoot Sims">Zoot Sims</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dexter_Gordon" title="Dexter Gordon">Dexter Gordon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wardell_Gray" title="Wardell Gray">Wardell Gray</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lee_Konitz" title="Lee Konitz">Lee Konitz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Warne_Marsh" title="Warne Marsh">Warne Marsh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Parker" title="Charlie Parker">Charlie Parker</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Art_Pepper" title="Art Pepper">Art Pepper</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-New_Grove_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_Grove-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Portrait_of_Charlie_Parker_in_1947.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Portrait_of_Charlie_Parker_in_1947.jpg/200px-Portrait_of_Charlie_Parker_in_1947.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="197" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Portrait_of_Charlie_Parker_in_1947.jpg/300px-Portrait_of_Charlie_Parker_in_1947.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Portrait_of_Charlie_Parker_in_1947.jpg/400px-Portrait_of_Charlie_Parker_in_1947.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5415" data-file-height="5347" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Portrait_of_Charlie_Parker_in_1947.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Charlie Parker, leader of the bebop revolution, 1947</div></div></div>
<p>The influence of Lester Young with the <a href="/wiki/Count_Basie_Orchestra" title="Count Basie Orchestra">Count Basie Orchestra</a> in the late 1930s and the popularity of Hawkins' 1939 recording of "<a href="/wiki/Body_and_Soul_(1930_song)" title="Body and Soul (1930 song)">Body and Soul</a>" marked the saxophone as an influence on jazz equal to the trumpet, which had been the defining instrument of jazz since its beginnings in New Orleans. But the greatest influence of the saxophone on jazz was to occur a few years later when alto saxophonist <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Parker" title="Charlie Parker">Charlie Parker</a> became an icon of the <a href="/wiki/Bebop" title="Bebop">bebop</a> revolution that influenced generations of jazz musicians. The small-group format of bebop and post-bebop jazz ensembles gained ascendancy in the 1940s as musicians used the harmonic and melodic freedom pioneered by Parker, <a href="/wiki/Dizzy_Gillespie" title="Dizzy Gillespie">Dizzy Gillespie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thelonious_Monk" title="Thelonious Monk">Thelonious Monk</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bud_Powell" title="Bud Powell">Bud Powell</a> in extended jazz solos.
</p><p>During the 1950s, prominent alto players included <a href="/wiki/Sonny_Stitt" title="Sonny Stitt">Sonny Stitt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cannonball_Adderley" title="Cannonball Adderley">Cannonball Adderley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jackie_McLean" title="Jackie McLean">Jackie McLean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lou_Donaldson" title="Lou Donaldson">Lou Donaldson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sonny_Criss" title="Sonny Criss">Sonny Criss</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Desmond" title="Paul Desmond">Paul Desmond</a>, while prominent tenor players included Lester Young, Coleman Hawkins, <a href="/wiki/Dexter_Gordon" title="Dexter Gordon">Dexter Gordon</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Coltrane" title="John Coltrane">John Coltrane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sonny_Rollins" title="Sonny Rollins">Sonny Rollins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stan_Getz" title="Stan Getz">Stan Getz</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Zoot_Sims" title="Zoot Sims">Zoot Sims</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lucky_Thompson" title="Lucky Thompson">Lucky Thompson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eddie_%22Lockjaw%22_Davis" title="Eddie &quot;Lockjaw&quot; Davis">Eddie "Lockjaw" Davis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Gonsalves" title="Paul Gonsalves">Paul Gonsalves</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Serge_Chaloff" title="Serge Chaloff">Serge Chaloff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Mulligan" title="Gerry Mulligan">Gerry Mulligan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pepper_Adams" title="Pepper Adams">Pepper Adams</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leo_Parker" title="Leo Parker">Leo Parker</a> brought the baritone saxophone to prominence as a solo instrument.  <a href="/wiki/Steve_Lacy_(saxophonist)" title="Steve Lacy (saxophonist)">Steve Lacy</a> renewed attention to the soprano saxophone in the context of modern jazz and John Coltrane boosted the instrument's popularity during the 1960s. Smooth jazz musician <a href="/wiki/Kenny_G" title="Kenny G">Kenny G</a> also uses the soprano sax as his principal instrument.
</p><p>Saxophonists such as John Coltrane, <a href="/wiki/Ornette_Coleman" title="Ornette Coleman">Ornette Coleman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sam_Rivers" title="Sam Rivers">Sam Rivers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pharoah_Sanders" title="Pharoah Sanders">Pharoah Sanders</a> defined the forefront of creative exploration with the avant-garde movement of the 1960s. The new realms offered with <a href="/wiki/Modal_jazz" title="Modal jazz">Modal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harmolodics" title="Harmolodics">harmolodic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Free_jazz" title="Free jazz">free jazz</a> were explored with every device that saxophonists could conceive of. Sheets of sound, tonal exploration, upper harmonics, and multiphonics were hallmarks of the creative possibilities that saxophones offered. One lasting influence of the avant-garde movement is the exploration of non-Western ethnic sounds on the saxophone, for example, the African-influenced sounds used by Sanders and the <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indian</a>-influenced sounds used by Coltrane. The devices of the avant-garde movement have continued to be influential in music that challenges the boundaries between avant-garde and other categories of jazz, such as that of alto saxophonists <a href="/wiki/Steve_Coleman" title="Steve Coleman">Steve Coleman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greg_Osby" title="Greg Osby">Greg Osby</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Illinoisjacquet.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Illinoisjacquet.jpg/200px-Illinoisjacquet.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Illinoisjacquet.jpg/300px-Illinoisjacquet.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Illinoisjacquet.jpg/400px-Illinoisjacquet.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4928" data-file-height="4927" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Illinoisjacquet.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illinois Jacquet, early influence on R&amp;B saxophone, 1941</div></div></div>
<p>Some ensembles such as the <a href="/wiki/World_Saxophone_Quartet" title="World Saxophone Quartet">World Saxophone Quartet</a> use the soprano-alto-tenor-baritone (SATB) format of the classical saxophone quartet for jazz.  In the 1990s, World Saxophone Quartet founder <a href="/wiki/Hamiet_Bluiett" title="Hamiet Bluiett">Hamiet Bluiett</a> formed the quartet Baritone Nation (four baritones).
</p><p>The "jump swing" bands of the 1940s gave rise to <a href="/wiki/Rhythm_and_blues" title="Rhythm and blues">rhythm and blues</a>, featuring horn sections and exuberant, strong-toned, heavily rhythmic styles of saxophone playing with a melodic sense based on <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a> tonalities.  <a href="/wiki/Illinois_Jacquet" title="Illinois Jacquet">Illinois Jacquet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sam_Butera" title="Sam Butera">Sam Butera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arnett_Cobb" title="Arnett Cobb">Arnett Cobb</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Forrest" title="Jimmy Forrest">Jimmy Forrest</a> were major influences on R&amp;B tenor styles and <a href="/wiki/Louis_Jordan" title="Louis Jordan">Louis Jordan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eddie_%22Cleanhead%22_Vinson" class="mw-redirect" title="Eddie &quot;Cleanhead&quot; Vinson">Eddie "Cleanhead" Vinson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earl_Bostic" title="Earl Bostic">Earl Bostic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bull_Moose_Jackson" title="Bull Moose Jackson">Bull Moose Jackson</a> were major influences on alto.  The R&amp;B saxophone players influenced later genres including <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_roll" title="Rock and roll">rock and roll</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ska" title="Ska">ska</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Funk" title="Funk">funk</a>.  Horn section work continued with  <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Otis" title="Johnny Otis">Johnny Otis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ray_Charles" title="Ray Charles">Ray Charles</a> featuring horn sections and the <a href="/wiki/Memphis_Horns" class="mw-redirect" title="Memphis Horns">Memphis Horns</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Phenix_Horns" title="Phenix Horns">Phenix Horns</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tower_of_Power" title="Tower of Power">Tower of Power</a> achieving distinction for their section playing.  Horn sections were added to the Chicago and West Coast blues bands of <a href="/wiki/Lowell_Fulson" title="Lowell Fulson">Lowell Fulson</a>, <a href="/wiki/T-Bone_Walker" title="T-Bone Walker">T-Bone Walker</a>, <a href="/wiki/B.B._King" title="B.B. King">B.B. King</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Guitar_Slim" title="Guitar Slim">Guitar Slim</a>.  The rock/soul fusion bands <a href="/wiki/Chicago_(band)" title="Chicago (band)">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Electric_Flag" title="The Electric Flag">The Electric Flag</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Blood,_Sweat,_and_Tears" class="mw-redirect" title="Blood, Sweat, and Tears">Blood, Sweat, and Tears</a> featured horn sections.  <a href="/wiki/Junior_Walker" title="Junior Walker">Junior Walker</a>, <a href="/wiki/King_Curtis" title="King Curtis">King Curtis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maceo_Parker" title="Maceo Parker">Maceo Parker</a> became influential soul and funk saxophone stylists. <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Keys" title="Bobby Keys">Bobby Keys</a> and <a href="/wiki/Clarence_Clemons" title="Clarence Clemons">Clarence Clemons</a> were well known rock &amp; roll saxophone stylists.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unusual_variants">Unusual variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saxophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Unusual variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A number of experimental saxophones and saxophone-related instruments have appeared since Sax's original work, most with no lasting impact. During the early 1920s Reiffel &amp; Husted of Chicago produced a slide soprano saxophone.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
During the 1920s some straight alto and tenor saxophones were produced by <a href="/wiki/Buescher_Band_Instrument_Company" title="Buescher Band Instrument Company">Buescher</a>, which proved cumbersome to handle and difficult to transport.  Buescher custom produced one straight baritone saxophone as novelty instrument for a vaudeville performer.<sup id="cite_ref-Cohen_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/C.G._Conn" title="C.G. Conn">C.G. Conn</a> introduced two new variants in 1928–1929, the <i>Conn-O-Sax</i> and the <a href="/wiki/Mezzo-soprano_saxophone" title="Mezzo-soprano saxophone">mezzo-soprano saxophone</a> keyed in F.  The Conn-O-Sax is a straight-conical bore instrument in F (one step above the E<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> alto) with a slightly curved neck and spherical bell. This instrument, which combines a saxophone bore and keys with a bell shaped similar to that of a <a href="/wiki/Heckelphone" title="Heckelphone">heckelphone</a>, was intended to imitate the timbre of the <a href="/wiki/English_horn" class="mw-redirect" title="English horn">English horn</a> and was produced only in 1929 and 1930. The instrument has a key range from low A to high G.  Fewer than 100 Conn-O-Saxes are in existence and they are highly sought by collectors. The Conn mezzo-soprano experienced a similarly short production run as the economics of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> curtailed the market for what were regarded as novelty instruments.  Most were expended by Conn as objects of repair training exercises.
</p><p>The most successful of the unusual 1920s designs was the King <i>Saxello</i>, essentially a straight B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> soprano, but with a slightly curved neck and tipped bell, made by the <a href="/wiki/H._N._White_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="H. N. White Company">H. N. White Company</a>.  Such instruments now command prices up to $4,000 USD. Its lasting influence is shown in the number of companies, including Keilwerth, Rampone &amp; Cazzani (<i>altello</i> model), L.A. Sax and Sax Dakota USA, marketing straight-bore, tipped-bell soprano saxophones as saxellos (or "saxello sopranos").
</p><p>Interest in two 1920s variants was revived by jazz musician <a href="/wiki/Rahsaan_Roland_Kirk" title="Rahsaan Roland Kirk">Rahsaan Roland Kirk</a>, who called his straight Buescher alto a "stritch" and his Saxello a "manzello.". The Buescher straight alto was a production instrument while the manzello was in fact a Saxello with a custom-made large bell and modified keywork.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>  More recently, the mezzo-soprano, or a modern variant of it, came into use by jazz musicians <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Braxton" title="Anthony Braxton">Anthony Braxton</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Carter_(musician)" title="James Carter (musician)">James Carter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vinny_Golia" title="Vinny Golia">Vinny Golia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Joe_Lovano" title="Joe Lovano">Joe Lovano</a>.
</p><p>Some of the 1920s experimental designs, in addition to the Saxello, provide the basis for similar instruments produced during the modern era.  Straight altos and tenors have been revived by Keilwerth,<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> L.A. Sax<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> and Sax Dakota USA. A mezzo-soprano in the key of G has been produced by Danish woodwind technician Peter Jessen, most notably played by <a href="/wiki/Joe_Lovano" title="Joe Lovano">Joe Lovano</a>. This instrument is more in the timbral quality of Bb soprano saxophone.
</p><p>The <i>contralto</i> saxophone, similar in size to the orchestral c-soprano, was developed in the late 20th century by California instrument maker Jim Schmidt.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> This instrument has a larger bore and a new fingering system, and does not resemble the orchestral instrument except for its key and register.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Benedikt_Eppelsheim" title="Benedikt Eppelsheim">Benedikt Eppelsheim</a>, of Munich, Germany has introduced recent innovations at the upper and lower ends of the saxophone range.  The <a href="/wiki/Soprillo_sax" class="mw-redirect" title="Soprillo sax">soprillo sax</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Piccolo" title="Piccolo">piccolo</a>-sized straight instrument with the upper speaker hole built into the mouthpiece. The instrument, which extends Sax's original family, is pitched a full octave higher than the B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> soprano sax. 
The <a href="/wiki/Tubax" title="Tubax">tubax</a>, developed in 1999 by Eppelsheim,<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> plays the same range and with the same fingering as the E<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> contrabass saxophone; its bore, however, is narrower than that of a contrabass saxophone, resulting in a more compact instrument with a "reedier" tone (akin to the double-reed contrabass <a href="/wiki/Sarrusophone" title="Sarrusophone">sarrusophone</a>). It can be played with the smaller (and more commonly available) baritone saxophone mouthpiece and reeds. Eppelsheim has also produced subcontrabass tubaxes in C and B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span>, the latter being the lowest saxophone ever made.
</p><p>Among the most recent developments is the <a href="/wiki/Aulochrome" title="Aulochrome">aulochrome</a>, a double soprano saxophone invented by Belgian instrument maker <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fran%C3%A7ois_Louis_(inventor)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="François Louis (inventor) (page does not exist)">François Louis</a> in 2001.
</p><p>Since the 1950s, saxophones with non-metallic bodies have occasionally been in production.  Such instruments have failed to gain acceptance over a number of issues including durability, repairability, and deficiencies in key action and tone.<sup id="cite_ref-saxgourmet_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-saxgourmet-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Stohrer_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stohrer-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>  The best known of these efforts is the 1950s <a href="/wiki/Grafton_saxophone" title="Grafton saxophone">Grafton</a> <a href="/wiki/Acrylic_glass" class="mw-redirect" title="Acrylic glass">acrylic</a> alto saxophone used briefly by Charlie Parker and Ornette Coleman.  It had a production run of over 10 years as a budget model saxophone.  The <a href="/wiki/Polycarbonate" title="Polycarbonate">polycarbonate</a> <a href="/wiki/Vibratosax" title="Vibratosax">Vibratosax</a> is in production as a low cost alternative to metal saxophones. Wooden <a href="/wiki/Sawat_saxophone" title="Sawat saxophone">Sawat saxophones</a> are made in Thailand on a small scale.  Opinions vary on the significance of body materials to sound.
</p><p>The fingering scheme of the saxophone, which has had only minor changes since the instrument's original invention, has presented inherent acoustic problems related to closed keys below the first open tonehole that affect response of, and slightly muffle, some notes. There is also a lack of tactile consistency between key centers, requiring extra effort from the player to adjust modes of muscle memory when moving between key centers. Two efforts to remedy the acoustic problems and awkward aspects of the original fingering system are noteworthy.
</p><p>The Leblanc Rationale and System<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> saxophones have key mechanics designed to remedy the acoustic problems associated with closed keys below the first open tonehole. They also enable players to make half-step shifts of scales by depressing one key while keeping the rest of the fingering consistent with that of the fingering a half step away. Some Leblanc System features were built into the Vito Model 35 saxophones of the 1950s and 1960s.  Despite the advantages of that system, acceptance was impaired by the expense and mechanical reliability issues related to the complexity of certain key mechanisms.<sup id="cite_ref-Howard_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Howard-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The chromatic or linear fingering, saxophone is a project of instrument designer and builder Jim Schmidt, developing a horn maximizing tactile and logical consistency between every interval regardless of the key, and avoiding the acoustic problems associated closed keys below the first open tone hole.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Several working prototypes have been built and presented at trade shows.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Production of this original and expensive saxophone is on an individual order basis.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_instruments">Related instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saxophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Related instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Saxos_de_bamb%C3%BA_por_Angel_Sampedro_del_R%C3%ADo,_luthier_argentino.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Saxos_de_bamb%C3%BA_por_Angel_Sampedro_del_R%C3%ADo%2C_luthier_argentino.jpg/220px-Saxos_de_bamb%C3%BA_por_Angel_Sampedro_del_R%C3%ADo%2C_luthier_argentino.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Saxos_de_bamb%C3%BA_por_Angel_Sampedro_del_R%C3%ADo%2C_luthier_argentino.jpg/330px-Saxos_de_bamb%C3%BA_por_Angel_Sampedro_del_R%C3%ADo%2C_luthier_argentino.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Saxos_de_bamb%C3%BA_por_Angel_Sampedro_del_R%C3%ADo%2C_luthier_argentino.jpg/440px-Saxos_de_bamb%C3%BA_por_Angel_Sampedro_del_R%C3%ADo%2C_luthier_argentino.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2304" data-file-height="3072" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Saxos_de_bamb%C3%BA_por_Angel_Sampedro_del_R%C3%ADo,_luthier_argentino.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><b>Saxos de Bambú</b> by Ángel Sampedro del Río, Argentina</div></div></div>
<p>Inexpensive keyless folk versions of the saxophone made of <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">bamboo</a> (recalling a <a href="/wiki/Chalumeau" title="Chalumeau">chalumeau</a>) were developed in the 20th century by instrument makers in Hawaii, Jamaica, Thailand, Indonesia, Ethiopia, and Argentina. The Hawaiian instrument, called a <a href="/wiki/Xaphoon" title="Xaphoon">xaphoon</a>, was invented during the 1970s and is also marketed as a "bamboo sax", although its <a href="/wiki/Cylindrical_bore" class="mw-redirect" title="Cylindrical bore">cylindrical bore</a> more closely resembles that of a clarinet, and its lack of any keywork makes it more akin to a <a href="/wiki/Recorder_(musical_instrument)" title="Recorder (musical instrument)">recorder</a>. Jamaica's best known exponent of a similar type of homemade bamboo "saxophone" was the <a href="/wiki/Mento" title="Mento">mento</a> musician and instrument maker '<a href="/wiki/Sugar_Belly" title="Sugar Belly">Sugar Belly</a>' (William Walker).<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Minahasa" class="mw-redirect" title="Minahasa">Minahasa</a> region of the Indonesian island of <a href="/wiki/Sulawesi" title="Sulawesi">Sulawesi</a>, there exist entire bands made up of bamboo "saxophones"<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> and "brass" instruments of various sizes. These instruments are imitations of European instruments, made using local materials. Similar instruments are produced in Thailand.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Argentina, Ángel Sampedro del Río and Mariana García have produced bamboo saxophones of various sizes since 1985, the larger of which have bamboo keys to allow for the playing of lower notes.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many synthesizer <a href="/wiki/Wind_controller" title="Wind controller">wind controllers</a> are played and fingered like a saxophone.
</p>
