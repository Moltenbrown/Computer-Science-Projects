<p>The <b>Carnation Revolution</b> (<a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>: <i lang="pt">Revolução dos Cravos</i>), also known as the <b>25 April</b> (Portuguese: <i lang="pt">25 de Abril</i>), was initially a 25 April 1974 <a href="/wiki/Military_coup" class="mw-redirect" title="Military coup">military coup</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a> which overthrew the <a href="/wiki/Authoritarian" class="mw-redirect" title="Authoritarian">authoritarian</a>  <a href="/wiki/Estado_Novo_(Portugal)" title="Estado Novo (Portugal)">Estado Novo regime</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The revolution began as a coup organised by the <a href="/wiki/Armed_Forces_Movement" title="Armed Forces Movement">Armed Forces Movement</a> (Portuguese: <i lang="pt">Movimento das Forças Armadas</i>, MFA), composed of military officers who opposed the regime, but it was soon coupled with an unanticipated, popular <a href="/wiki/Civil_resistance" title="Civil resistance">civil resistance</a> campaign. The revolution led to the fall of the Estado Novo, terminated the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Colonial_War" title="Portuguese Colonial War">Portuguese Colonial War</a>, and started a <a href="/wiki/Processo_Revolucion%C3%A1rio_Em_Curso" title="Processo Revolucionário Em Curso">revolutionary process</a> that would result in a <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_transition_to_democracy" title="Portuguese transition to democracy">democratic Portugal</a>.
</p><p>Its name arose from the fact that <a href="/wiki/Bloodless_coup" class="mw-redirect" title="Bloodless coup">almost no shots were fired</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Celeste_Caeiro" title="Celeste Caeiro">Celeste Caeiro</a> offered <a href="/wiki/Dianthus_caryophyllus" title="Dianthus caryophyllus">carnations</a> to the soldiers when the population took to the streets to celebrate the end of the dictatorship; other demonstrators followed suit, and carnations were placed in the muzzles of guns and on the soldiers' uniforms.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In Portugal, 25 April is a <a href="/wiki/Public_holidays_in_Portugal" title="Public holidays in Portugal">national holiday</a> (<a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>: <i lang="pt">Dia da Liberdade</i>, Freedom Day) which commemorates the revolution.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnation_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since 1933, Portugal had been governed by an <a href="/wiki/Authoritarianism" title="Authoritarianism">authoritarian</a> <a href="/wiki/Dictatorship" title="Dictatorship">dictatorship</a>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Estado_Novo_(Portugal)" title="Estado Novo (Portugal)">Estado Novo</a></i> or New State. The Estado Novo, in turn, evolved from the <a href="/wiki/Ditadura_Nacional" title="Ditadura Nacional">Ditadura Nacional</a> (National Dictatorship) set up after the <a href="/wiki/28_May_1926_coup_d%27etat" class="mw-redirect" title="28 May 1926 coup d&#39;etat">28 May 1926 coup d'etat</a> (called the "National Revolution" under the Estado Novo).
</p><p>The revolution <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_transition_to_democracy" title="Portuguese transition to democracy">changed the government</a> to a <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democracy</a> and produced enormous social, economic, territorial, demographic and political changes. These changes evolved during (and after) a two-year transitional period known as <a href="/wiki/Processo_Revolucion%C3%A1rio_Em_Curso" title="Processo Revolucionário Em Curso">Processo Revolucionário Em Curso</a> (PREC, Ongoing Revolutionary Process), which was characterised by social turmoil and power disputes between left- and right-wing political forces.
</p><p>Despite repeated radio appeals by the revolutionaries asking the population to stay home, thousands of Portuguese citizens descended on the streets and mingled with the military <a href="/wiki/Insurgency" title="Insurgency">insurgents</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The military-led coup returned democracy to Portugal, ending the unpopular <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Colonial_War" title="Portuguese Colonial War">Colonial War</a> (in which thousands of Portuguese citizens had been <a href="/wiki/Conscription" title="Conscription">conscripted</a> into military service) and replacing the Estado Novo regime and its <a href="/wiki/Secret_police" title="Secret police">secret police</a> (which curbed <a href="/wiki/Civil_liberties" title="Civil liberties">civil liberties</a> and <a href="/wiki/Political_freedom" title="Political freedom">political freedom</a>). It began as a<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> protest by <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Armed_Forces" title="Portuguese Armed Forces">Portuguese Armed Forces</a> <a href="/wiki/Captain_(armed_forces)" title="Captain (armed forces)">captains</a> against a law: the Dec Lei nº 353/73 of 1973.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A group of low-ranking Portuguese officers organised as the <a href="/wiki/Armed_Forces_Movement" title="Armed Forces Movement">Armed Forces Movement</a> (MFA, Movimento das Forças Armadas), including some who had fought pro-independence <a href="/wiki/Guerrilla_warfare" title="Guerrilla warfare">guerrillas</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Empire" title="Portuguese Empire">Portuguese Empire</a>'s territories in Africa,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and overthrew the Estado Novo regime which had ruled Portugal since the 1930s. Portugal's new regime pledged to end the colonial wars, and began negotiations with the <a href="/wiki/African_independence_movements" title="African independence movements">African independence movements</a>. By the end of 1974, Portuguese troops were withdrawn from <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Guinea" title="Portuguese Guinea">Portuguese Guinea</a> and the latter was a <a href="/wiki/Member_states_of_the_United_Nations" title="Member states of the United Nations">UN member state</a>. This was followed by the independence of <a href="/wiki/Colonial_Cape_Verde" class="mw-redirect" title="Colonial Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Mozambique" title="Portuguese Mozambique">Mozambique</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colonial_S%C3%A3o_Tom%C3%A9_and_Pr%C3%ADncipe" class="mw-redirect" title="Colonial São Tomé and Príncipe">São Tomé and Príncipe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Angola" title="Portuguese Angola">Angola</a> in 1975. The Carnation Revolution also led to Portugal's withdrawal from <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Timor" title="Portuguese Timor">East Timor</a> in <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">south-east Asia</a>. These events prompted a <a href="/wiki/Mass_exodus" class="mw-redirect" title="Mass exodus">mass exodus</a> of Portuguese citizens from Portugal's African territories (mostly from <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Angola" title="Portuguese Angola">Angola</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Mozambique" title="Portuguese Mozambique">Mozambique</a>), creating over a million Portuguese <a href="/wiki/Refugee" title="Refugee">refugees</a> — the <i><a href="/wiki/Retornados" class="mw-redirect" title="Retornados">retornados</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although <a href="/wiki/PIDE" title="PIDE">PIDE</a> (the Estado Novo's political police) killed four people before surrendering, the revolution was unusual because the revolutionaries did not use violence to achieve their goals. Holding red <a href="/wiki/Dianthus_caryophyllus" title="Dianthus caryophyllus">carnations</a> (Portuguese: <i lang="pt">cravos</i>), many people joined revolutionary soldiers on the streets of Lisbon in apparent joy and audible euphoria.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Red is the colour of <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communism</a>, the ideological tendencies of many anti-Estado Novo insurgents.<sup id="cite_ref-infiltrated_communists_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-infiltrated_communists-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> It was the end of the Estado Novo (the longest-lived authoritarian regime in <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a>), and the dissolution of the Portuguese Empire. In the aftermath of the revolution, a <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="Portuguese Constitution">new constitution</a> was drafted, <a href="/wiki/Censorship" title="Censorship">censorship</a> was prohibited, <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_speech" title="Freedom of speech">free speech</a> was permitted, <a href="/wiki/Political_prisoner" title="Political prisoner">political prisoners</a> were released and the Portuguese overseas territories in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a> were granted independence. <a href="/wiki/East_Timor" title="East Timor">East Timor</a> was also offered independence, shortly before it was <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_invasion_of_East_Timor" title="Indonesian invasion of East Timor">invaded by Indonesia</a>.
</p>

<p>At the beginning of the 1970s, nearly a half-century of authoritarian rule weighed on Portugal. After the <a href="/wiki/28_May_1926_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="28 May 1926 coup d&#39;état">28 May 1926 coup d'état</a>, Portugal implemented an authoritarian regime incorporating <a href="/wiki/Social_Catholicism" class="mw-redirect" title="Social Catholicism">social Catholicism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Integralism" title="Integralism">integralism</a>. In 1933, the regime was recast and renamed Estado Novo (New State). <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_de_Oliveira_Salazar" title="António de Oliveira Salazar">António de Oliveira Salazar</a> was prime minister until 1968, when he had a <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a>. Salazar was replaced in September 1968 by <a href="/wiki/Marcello_Caetano" class="mw-redirect" title="Marcello Caetano">Marcello Caetano</a>, who was deposed during the revolution.
</p><p>Portugal's Estado Novo government was initially tolerated by its <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> partners due to its <a href="/wiki/Anti-communism" title="Anti-communism">anti-communist</a> stance. <a href="/wiki/Sham_election" class="mw-redirect" title="Sham election">Elections</a> were rarely contested; the opposition used the limited <a href="/wiki/Political_freedom" title="Political freedom">political freedoms</a> allowed during the brief election period to <a href="/wiki/Protest" title="Protest">protest</a> against the regime, withdrawing their candidates before the election to deny the regime <a href="/wiki/Legitimacy_(political)" title="Legitimacy (political)">political legitimacy</a>. In 1958, General <a href="/wiki/Humberto_Delgado" title="Humberto Delgado">Humberto Delgado</a> (a former member of the regime) stood against the regime's presidential candidate, <a href="/wiki/Am%C3%A9rico_Tom%C3%A1s" title="Américo Tomás">Américo Tomás</a>, and refused to allow his name to be withdrawn.
</p><p>Tomás won the election amidst claims of widespread electoral fraud. Immediately after the election, the Salazar government abandoned the practice of popularly electing the president and gave the task to the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_Portugal" class="mw-redirect" title="National Assembly of Portugal">National Assembly</a>, which was firmly under the regime's control. During Caetano's time in office, he made minor attempts at political reform that did not go nearly far enough for a generation that had no memory of the instability that preceded the 1926 coup. However, even these meager reforms were obstructed by Salazarist elements in the regime. The hardliners were supported by Tomás, who was unwilling to give Caetano as free a hand as Salazar had. The Estado Novo's political police, the <a href="/wiki/PIDE" title="PIDE">PIDE</a> (Polícia Internacional e de Defesa do Estado, later the DGS, Direcção-Geral de Segurança and originally the PVDE, Polícia de Vigilância e Defesa do Estado), <a href="/wiki/Persecution" title="Persecution">persecuted</a> opponents of the regime, who were often tortured, imprisoned or killed.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/International_community" title="International community">international community</a> disliked the Portuguese regime. The <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> was near its peak; <a href="/wiki/Western_Bloc" title="Western Bloc">Western-</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Bloc" title="Eastern Bloc">Eastern-bloc</a> states were supporting <a href="/wiki/Guerrilla_warfare" title="Guerrilla warfare">guerrillas</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_colonies" class="mw-redirect" title="Portuguese colonies">Portuguese colonies</a>, attempting to bring them under American or Soviet influence. The overseas policy of the Portuguese government, despite the desire of many colonial residents to remain under Portuguese rule, led to an abrupt <a href="/wiki/Decolonisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Decolonisation">decolonisation</a> after the Carnation Revolution and the fall of the regime in April 1974. For the Portuguese rulers, the overseas empire was a matter of <a href="/wiki/National_interest" title="National interest">national interest</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colonial_war">Colonial war</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnation_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Colonial war">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Colonial_War" title="Portuguese Colonial War">Portuguese Colonial War</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:AssaltonaMatadaSanga.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Armed soldier in a helicopter" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/AssaltonaMatadaSanga.jpg/220px-AssaltonaMatadaSanga.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="156" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/AssaltonaMatadaSanga.jpg/330px-AssaltonaMatadaSanga.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/AssaltonaMatadaSanga.jpg/440px-AssaltonaMatadaSanga.jpg 2x" data-file-width="545" data-file-height="386" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:AssaltonaMatadaSanga.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Air_Force" title="Portuguese Air Force">PoAF</a> helicopter in Africa</div></div></div>
<p>Independence movements began in the African colonies of <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Mozambique" title="Portuguese Mozambique">Portuguese Mozambique</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Congo" class="mw-redirect" title="Portuguese Congo">Portuguese Congo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Angola" title="Portuguese Angola">Portuguese Angola</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Guinea" title="Portuguese Guinea">Portuguese Guinea</a>. The unrest forced the Salazar and Caetano regimes to spend more of Portugal's <a href="/wiki/National_budget" class="mw-redirect" title="National budget">budget</a> on colonial administration and <a href="/wiki/Military_expenditure" class="mw-redirect" title="Military expenditure">military expenditure</a>, and the country became increasingly <a href="/wiki/International_isolation" title="International isolation">isolated</a> from the rest of the world. During the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Colonial_War" title="Portuguese Colonial War">war</a>, Portugal faced increasing dissent, <a href="/wiki/Arms_embargo" title="Arms embargo">arms embargoes</a> and other <a href="/wiki/International_sanctions" title="International sanctions">international sanctions</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Adrian_Hastings_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Adrian_Hastings-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The war became more unpopular in Portugal due to its length and cost, the worsening of <a href="/wiki/Diplomatic_relations" class="mw-redirect" title="Diplomatic relations">diplomatic relations</a> with other United Nations member states, and its role in perpetuating the Estado Novo regime. Its escalation led to the mutiny of <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Air_Force" title="Portuguese Air Force">FAP</a> in the Carnation Revolution. Atrocities such as the <a href="/wiki/Wiriyamu_Massacre" title="Wiriyamu Massacre">Wiriyamu Massacre</a> undermined the war's popularity and the government's diplomatic position, although details of the massacre are still disputed.<sup id="cite_ref-Adrian_Hastings_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Adrian_Hastings-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Westfall_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Westfall-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many left-wing students and anti-war activists were forced to leave Portugal to escape conscription, imprisonment and <a href="/wiki/Torture" title="Torture">torture</a> by government forces. Between 1945 and 1974, however, three generations of right-wing militants in Portuguese schools were guided by a <a href="/wiki/Revolutionary_nationalism" title="Revolutionary nationalism">revolutionary nationalism</a> partially influenced by European <a href="/wiki/Neo-fascism" title="Neo-fascism">neo-fascism</a>. The core of the radical students' struggle was an uncompromising defense of the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Empire" title="Portuguese Empire">Portuguese Empire</a> and an authoritarian regime.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_conditions">Economic conditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnation_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Economic conditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Portuguese_colonial_war_map1.PNG" class="image"><img alt="Colour-coded map of Portugal and Africa" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Portuguese_colonial_war_map1.PNG/220px-Portuguese_colonial_war_map1.PNG" decoding="async" width="220" height="240" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Portuguese_colonial_war_map1.PNG/330px-Portuguese_colonial_war_map1.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/77/Portuguese_colonial_war_map1.PNG 2x" data-file-width="360" data-file-height="392" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Portuguese_colonial_war_map1.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Portuguese colonies in Africa under the <i>Estado Novo</i> regime</div></div></div>
<p>The Estado Novo regime's economic policy encouraged the formation of large <a href="/wiki/Conglomerate_(company)" title="Conglomerate (company)">conglomerates</a>. The regime maintained a policy of <a href="/wiki/Corporatism" title="Corporatism">corporatism</a> which resulted in the placement of much of the economy in the hands of a number of strong conglomerates, including those founded by the families of <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_Champalimaud" title="António Champalimaud">António Champalimaud</a> (<a href="/wiki/Banco_Totta_%26_A%C3%A7ores" class="mw-redirect" title="Banco Totta &amp; Açores">Banco Totta &amp; Açores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Banco_Pinto_%26_Sotto_Mayor" class="mw-redirect" title="Banco Pinto &amp; Sotto Mayor">Banco Pinto &amp; Sotto Mayor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Secil" class="mw-redirect" title="Secil">Secil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cimpor" title="Cimpor">Cimpor</a>), <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Manuel_de_Mello" title="José Manuel de Mello">José Manuel de Mello</a> (<a href="/wiki/Companhia_Uni%C3%A3o_Fabril" title="Companhia União Fabril">Companhia União Fabril</a>), <a href="/wiki/Am%C3%A9rico_Amorim" title="Américo Amorim">Américo Amorim</a> (<a href="/wiki/Corticeira_Amorim" title="Corticeira Amorim">Corticeira Amorim</a>) and the dos Santos family (<a href="/wiki/Jer%C3%B3nimo_Martins" title="Jerónimo Martins">Jerónimo Martins</a>).
</p><p>The Companhia União Fabril (CUF) was one of the largest and most-diversified Portuguese conglomerates; its <a href="/wiki/Core_business" title="Core business">core businesses</a> included cement, <a href="/wiki/Petrochemical" title="Petrochemical">petrochemicals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agrochemical" title="Agrochemical">agrochemicals</a>, textiles, beer, beverages, <a href="/wiki/Metallurgy" title="Metallurgy">metallurgy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Naval_engineering" class="mw-redirect" title="Naval engineering">naval engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a>, banking, paper, tourism and mining. Although its corporate headquarters was in <a href="/wiki/Continental_Portugal" title="Continental Portugal">mainland Portugal</a>, it had branches, plants and projects throughout the Portuguese Empire (especially in the territories of <a href="/wiki/Cabinda_Province" title="Cabinda Province">Cabinda</a>, Angola and Mozambique).
</p><p>Other medium-sized family companies specialised in textiles (such as those in <a href="/wiki/Covilh%C3%A3" title="Covilhã">Covilhã</a> and the northwest), ceramics, porcelain, glass and crystal (such as those in <a href="/wiki/Alcoba%C3%A7a,_Portugal" title="Alcobaça, Portugal">Alcobaça</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caldas_da_Rainha" title="Caldas da Rainha">Caldas da Rainha</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marinha_Grande" title="Marinha Grande">Marinha Grande</a>), engineered wood (such as <a href="/wiki/SONAE" class="mw-redirect" title="SONAE">SONAE</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Porto" title="Porto">Porto</a>), canned fish (<a href="/wiki/Algarve" title="Algarve">Algarve</a> and the northwest), fishing, food and beverages (liqueurs, beer and <a href="/wiki/Port_wine" title="Port wine">port wine</a>), tourism (in <a href="/wiki/Estoril" title="Estoril">Estoril</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cascais" title="Cascais">Cascais</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sintra" title="Sintra">Sintra</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Algarve" title="Algarve">Algarve</a>) and agriculture (the <a href="/wiki/Alentejo" title="Alentejo">Alentejo</a>, known as the <a href="/wiki/Breadbasket" title="Breadbasket">breadbasket</a> of Portugal) by the early 1970s. Rural families engaged in agriculture and forestry.
</p><p>During the 1961–1974 Portuguese Colonial War (a <a href="/wiki/Counter-insurgency" title="Counter-insurgency">counterinsurgency</a> against <a href="/wiki/Guerrilla" class="mw-redirect" title="Guerrilla">guerrillas</a>), <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Congo" class="mw-redirect" title="Portuguese Congo">Portuguese Congo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Angola" title="Portuguese Angola">Portuguese Angola</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Mozambique" title="Portuguese Mozambique">Portuguese Mozambique</a> (colonies at the time) experienced economic growth in the production of oil, coffee, cotton, cashews, coconuts, timber, minerals (including diamonds), metals (such as iron and aluminium), bananas, citrus, tea, sisal, beer, cement, fish and other seafood, beef and textiles.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> <a href="/wiki/Labor_union" class="mw-redirect" title="Labor union">Labour unions</a> were prohibited, and <a href="/wiki/Minimum_wage" title="Minimum wage">minimum wage</a> laws were not enforced. The outbreak of colonial wars in Africa set off significant social changes, among them the rapid incorporation of women into the labour market. Marcelo Caetano fostered economic growth and social improvement, such as a monthly <a href="/wiki/Pension" title="Pension">pension</a> to rural workers who had never contributed to Portugal's <a href="/wiki/Social_security" title="Social security">social security</a>. The objectives of Caetano's pension reform were to increase equity and <a href="/wiki/Economic_efficiency" title="Economic efficiency">economic efficiency</a> and reduce <a href="/wiki/Fiscal_imbalance" title="Fiscal imbalance">fiscal imbalance</a>.
</p><p>After Salazar's stroke in 1968, Caetano took over as <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Portugal" title="Prime Minister of Portugal">prime minister</a>. He adopted a slogan of "continuous evolution", suggesting reforms of Salazar's system. Caetano's Primavera Marcelista (Marcelist Spring) included greater political tolerance and <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_the_press" title="Freedom of the press">freedom of the press</a>, and was seen as an opportunity for the opposition to gain <a href="/wiki/Concession_(politics)" title="Concession (politics)">concessions</a> from the regime. Portugal had a taste of democracy in 1969, and Caetano authorised the country's first democratic labour-union movement since the 1920s. However, after the elections of 1969 and 1973 it became clear that past <a href="/wiki/Political_repression" title="Political repression">political repression</a> would continue against <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anti-colonialism" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-colonialism">anti-colonialists</a> and other opponents of the regime.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Otelo_Saraiva_de_Carvalho_Campaign_Poster_1976.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Campaign poster of a smiling Otelo Saraiva de Carvalh" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Otelo_Saraiva_de_Carvalho_Campaign_Poster_1976.jpg/170px-Otelo_Saraiva_de_Carvalho_Campaign_Poster_1976.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="261" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Otelo_Saraiva_de_Carvalho_Campaign_Poster_1976.jpg/255px-Otelo_Saraiva_de_Carvalho_Campaign_Poster_1976.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0b/Otelo_Saraiva_de_Carvalho_Campaign_Poster_1976.jpg 2x" data-file-width="332" data-file-height="510" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Otelo_Saraiva_de_Carvalho_Campaign_Poster_1976.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1976 campaign poster for <a href="/wiki/Otelo_Saraiva_de_Carvalho" title="Otelo Saraiva de Carvalho">Otelo Saraiva de Carvalho</a>, a leader of the revolution</div></div></div>
<p>By the early 1970s, the Portuguese Colonial War had a steadily-increasing budget. The <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Armed_Forces" title="Portuguese Armed Forces">Portuguese military</a> was overstretched and there was no political solution in sight. Although the number of <a href="/wiki/Casualty_(person)" title="Casualty (person)">casualties</a> was relatively small, the war had entered its second decade; Portugal faced criticism from the international community, and was becoming increasingly isolated.
</p><p>The war had a profound impact on the country. Thousands of young men avoided <a href="/wiki/Conscription" title="Conscription">conscription</a> by <a href="/wiki/Illegal_emigration" title="Illegal emigration">emigrating illegally</a>, primarily to France and the United States. The revolutionary <a href="/wiki/Armed_Forces_Movement" title="Armed Forces Movement">Armed Forces Movement</a> (MFA) began as an attempt to liberate Portugal from the Estado Novo regime and challenge new military laws which were <a href="/wiki/Coming_into_force" title="Coming into force">coming into force</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-infopedia.pt_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-infopedia.pt-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The laws would reduce the military budget and reformulate the Portuguese military.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Younger military-academy graduates resented  Caetano's programme of commissioning militia officers who completed a brief training course and had served in the colonies' defensive campaigns at the same rank as academy graduates.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The war in the colonies was becoming increasingly unpopular in Portugal, and the military insurgency gained momentum.
</p><p>After the revolution, the MFA began to negotiate with African pro-independence guerrillas. The new government in <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a> was no longer inclined to support Portugal's chaotic, expensive empire, and the Portuguese territories in Africa were rapidly granted independence.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Revolution"><span id="Events"></span>Revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnation_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_the_Carnation_Revolution" title="Timeline of the Carnation Revolution">Timeline of the Carnation Revolution</a></div>
<p>In February 1974, Caetano decided to remove General <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_de_Sp%C3%ADnola" title="António de Spínola">António de Spínola</a> from the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Portugal" title="President of Portugal">presidency</a> in the face of Spínola's increasing disagreement with the promotion of military officers and the direction of Portuguese colonial policy. This occurred shortly after the publication of Spínola's book, <i>Portugal and the Future</i>, which expressed his political and military views of the Portuguese Colonial War. Several military officers who opposed the war formed the MFA to overthrow the government in a <a href="/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="Coup d&#39;état">military coup</a>. The MFA was headed by <a href="/wiki/V%C3%ADtor_Alves_(soldier)" title="Vítor Alves (soldier)">Vítor Alves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Otelo_Saraiva_de_Carvalho" title="Otelo Saraiva de Carvalho">Otelo Saraiva de Carvalho</a> and Vasco Lourenço, and was joined later by <a href="/wiki/Salgueiro_Maia" title="Salgueiro Maia">Salgueiro Maia</a>. The movement was aided by other Portuguese army officers who supported Spínola and democratic civil and military reform. It is speculated that <a href="/wiki/Francisco_da_Costa_Gomes" title="Francisco da Costa Gomes">Francisco da Costa Gomes</a> actually led the revolution.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The coup had two secret signals. The first was the airing at 10:55&#160;p.m. on 24 April of <a href="/wiki/Paulo_de_Carvalho" title="Paulo de Carvalho">Paulo de Carvalho</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/E_Depois_do_Adeus" title="E Depois do Adeus">E Depois do Adeus</a>" (Portugal's entry in the <a href="/wiki/1974_Eurovision_Song_Contest" class="mw-redirect" title="1974 Eurovision Song Contest">1974 Eurovision Song Contest</a>) on Emissores Associados de Lisboa, which alerted the rebel captains and soldiers to begin the coup. The second signal came at 12:20&#160;a.m. on 25 April, when <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A1dio_Renascen%C3%A7a" title="Rádio Renascença">Rádio Renascença</a> broadcast "<a href="/wiki/Gr%C3%A2ndola,_Vila_Morena" title="Grândola, Vila Morena">Grândola, Vila Morena</a>" (a song by <a href="/wiki/Zeca_Afonso" title="Zeca Afonso">Zeca Afonso</a>, an influential political <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk musician</a> and singer who was banned from Portuguese radio at the time). The MFA gave the signals to take over strategic points of power in the country.
</p><p>Six hours later, the Caetano government relented. Despite repeated radio appeals from the "captains of April" (the MFA) advising the population to stay home, thousands of Portuguese took to the streets&#160;– mingling with, and supporting, the military insurgents. A central gathering point was the Lisbon flower market, then richly stocked with carnations (which were in season). Some of the insurgents put carnations in their <a href="/wiki/Gun_barrel" title="Gun barrel">gun barrels</a>, an image broadcast on television worldwide<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> which gave the revolution its name. Although no <a href="/wiki/Mass_demonstration" class="mw-redirect" title="Mass demonstration">mass demonstrations</a> preceded the coup, spontaneous civilian involvement turned the military coup into a popular revolution "led by radical army officers, soldiers, workers and peasants that toppled the senile Salazar dictatorship, using the language of socialism and democracy. The attempt to radicalise the outcome," noted a contemporary observer of the time, "had little mass support and was easily suppressed by the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Socialist_Party" title="Portuguese Socialist Party">Portuguese Socialist Party</a> and its allies."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Caetano found refuge in the main Lisbon military police station at the Largo do Carmo. This building was surrounded by the MFA, which pressured him to cede power to General Spínola. Caetano and President <a href="/wiki/Am%C3%A9rico_Tom%C3%A1s" title="Américo Tomás">Américo Tomás</a> fled to Brazil; Caetano spent the rest of his life there, and Tomás returned to Portugal a few years later. The revolution was closely watched by neighbouring Spain, where the government (and the opposition) were planning the <a href="/wiki/Head_of_state_succession" title="Head of state succession">succession</a> of Spanish dictator <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco" title="Francisco Franco">Francisco Franco</a>. Franco died a year and a half later, in 1975.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnation_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_transition_to_democracy" title="Portuguese transition to democracy">Portuguese transition to democracy</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:25_Abril_1983_Porto_by_Henrique_Matos_01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Demonstration with red flags and a green mock tank" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/25_Abril_1983_Porto_by_Henrique_Matos_01.jpg/220px-25_Abril_1983_Porto_by_Henrique_Matos_01.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="158" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/25_Abril_1983_Porto_by_Henrique_Matos_01.jpg/330px-25_Abril_1983_Porto_by_Henrique_Matos_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/25_Abril_1983_Porto_by_Henrique_Matos_01.jpg/440px-25_Abril_1983_Porto_by_Henrique_Matos_01.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1201" data-file-height="862" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:25_Abril_1983_Porto_by_Henrique_Matos_01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Demonstration in <a href="/wiki/Porto" title="Porto">Porto</a>, 1983</div></div></div>
<p>After the coup, power was held by the <a href="/wiki/National_Salvation_Junta" title="National Salvation Junta">National Salvation Junta</a> (a military junta). Portugal experienced a turbulent period, known as the <a href="/wiki/Processo_Revolucion%C3%A1rio_Em_Curso" title="Processo Revolucionário Em Curso">Processo Revolucionário Em Curso</a> (Ongoing Revolutionary Process).
</p><p>The conservative forces surrounding Spinola and the MFA radicals initially confronted each other (covertly or overtly), and Spinola was forced to appoint key MFA figures to senior security positions. Right-wing military figures attempted an unsuccessful <a href="/wiki/Counter-coup" class="mw-redirect" title="Counter-coup">counter-coup</a>, resulting in Spinola's removal from office. Unrest within the MFA between leftist forces (often close to the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Portugal" class="mw-redirect" title="Communist Party of Portugal">Communist Party</a>) and more-moderate groups (often allied with the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_(Portugal)" title="Socialist Party (Portugal)">Socialists</a>) eventually led to the group's splintering and dissolution.
</p><p>This stage of the PREC lasted until the 25 November 1975 <a href="/wiki/Coup_of_25_November_1975" title="Coup of 25 November 1975">pro-communist coup, followed by a successful counter-coup by pro-democracy moderates</a>, and was marked by constant friction between liberal-democratic forces and leftist-communist political parties.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Portugal's <a href="/wiki/1975_Portuguese_Constituent_Assembly_election" title="1975 Portuguese Constituent Assembly election">first free election</a> was held on 25 April 1975 to write a new constitution replacing the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Portugal#Constitution_of_1933" title="Constitution of Portugal">Constitution of 1933</a>, which prevailed during the Estado Novo era. Another <a href="/wiki/1976_Portuguese_legislative_election" title="1976 Portuguese legislative election">election</a> was held in 1976 and the first constitutional government, led by centre-left socialist <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1rio_Soares" title="Mário Soares">Mário Soares</a>, took office.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decolonisation">Decolonisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnation_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Decolonisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Angolan_Civil_War" title="Angolan Civil War">Angolan Civil War</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mozambican_Civil_War" title="Mozambican Civil War">Mozambican Civil War</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_invasion_of_East_Timor" title="Indonesian invasion of East Timor">Indonesian invasion of East Timor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lusophobia" title="Lusophobia">Lusophobia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Guinea-Bissau_War_of_Independence" title="Guinea-Bissau War of Independence">Guinea-Bissau War of Independence</a></div>
<p>Before April 1974, the intractable Portuguese Colonial War in Africa consumed up to 40 percent of the Portuguese budget. Although part of <a href="/wiki/Guinea-Bissau" title="Guinea-Bissau">Guinea-Bissau</a> became independent <i>de facto</i> in 1973, <a href="/wiki/Bissau" title="Bissau">Bissau</a> (its capital) and the large towns were still under Portuguese control. In <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mozambique" title="Mozambique">Mozambique</a>, independence movements were active in more remote rural areas from which the Portuguese Army had retreated.
</p><p>A consequence of the Carnation Revolution was the sudden withdrawal of Portuguese administrative and military personnel from its overseas colonies. Hundreds of thousands of <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Africans" title="Portuguese Africans">Portuguese Africans</a> returned to Portugal. These people—workers, small businesspeople, and farmers—often had deep roots in the former colonies and became known as the <i>retornados</i>.
</p><p>Angola began a <a href="/wiki/Angolan_Civil_War" title="Angolan Civil War">decades-long civil war</a> which involved the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. Millions of Angolans died in the aftermath of independence due to armed conflict, <a href="/wiki/Malnutrition" title="Malnutrition">malnutrition</a> and disease. After a brief period of stability, Mozambique became embroiled in a <a href="/wiki/Mozambican_Civil_War" title="Mozambican Civil War">civil war</a> which left it one of the poorest nations in the world.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The country's situation has improved since the 1990s, and <a href="/wiki/Multi-party_elections" class="mw-redirect" title="Multi-party elections">multi-party elections</a> have been held.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/East_Timor" title="East Timor">East Timor</a> was <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_invasion_of_East_Timor" title="Indonesian invasion of East Timor">invaded by Indonesia</a>, and would be <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_occupation_of_East_Timor" title="Indonesian occupation of East Timor">occupied</a> until 1999. There were an estimated 102,800 conflict-related deaths from 1974 to 1999 (about 18,600 killings and 84,200 deaths from hunger and illness), most of which occurred during the Indonesian occupation.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After a long period of one-party rule, Guinea-Bissau experienced a brief <a href="/wiki/Guinea-Bissau_Civil_War" title="Guinea-Bissau Civil War">civil war</a> and a difficult transition to civilian rule in 1998. <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a> and <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Tom%C3%A9_and_Pr%C3%ADncipe" title="São Tomé and Príncipe">São Tomé and Príncipe</a> avoided civil war during the decolonisation period, and established <a href="/wiki/Multi-party_system" title="Multi-party system">multi-party political systems</a> by the early 1990s. <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a> remained a Portuguese colony until 1999, when <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> <a href="/wiki/Transfer_of_sovereignty_over_Macau" title="Transfer of sovereignty over Macau">took control</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Joint_Declaration_on_the_Question_of_Macau" title="Joint Declaration on the Question of Macau">joint declaration</a> and enacted a "<a href="/wiki/One_country,_two_systems" title="One country, two systems">one country, two systems</a>" policy similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>.
</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Economic_history_of_Portugal" title="Economic history of Portugal">Portuguese economy changed significantly</a> between 1961 and 1973. Total output (GDP at factor cost) had grown by 120 percent in real terms. The pre-revolutionary period was characterised by robust <a href="/wiki/Annual_growth_rate" title="Annual growth rate">annual growth</a> in <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">GDP</a> (6.9 percent), <a href="/wiki/Industrial_production" title="Industrial production">industrial production</a> (nine percent), <a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">consumption</a> (6.5 percent), and <a href="/wiki/Gross_fixed_capital_formation" title="Gross fixed capital formation">gross fixed capital formation</a> (7.8 percent). The revolutionary period experienced a slowly-growing economy, whose only impetus was its 1986 entrance into the <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Community" title="European Economic Community">European Economic Community</a>. Although Portugal never regained its pre-revolution growth, at the time of the revolution it was an <a href="/wiki/Underdeveloped_country" class="mw-redirect" title="Underdeveloped country">underdeveloped country</a> with poor <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a>, inefficient agriculture and the worst health and education indicators in Europe.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Pre-revolutionary Portugal had some social and economic achievements.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> After a long period of economic decline before 1914, the Portuguese economy recovered slightly until 1950. It began a period of economic growth in common with Western Europe, of which it was the poorest country until the 1980s. Portuguese economic growth between 1960 and 1973 (under the Estado Novo regime) created an opportunity for integration with the developed economies of Western Europe despite the colonial war. Through emigration, trade, tourism and foreign investment, individuals and companies changed their patterns of production and consumption. The increasing complexity of a growing economy sparked new technical and organisational challenges.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 13 November 1972, Fundo do Ultramar (The Overseas Fund, a <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_wealth_fund" title="Sovereign wealth fund">sovereign wealth fund</a>) was enacted with Decreto-Lei n.º 448/ /72 and the Ministry of Defense ordinance Portaria 696/72 to finance the war.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The increasing burden of the war effort meant that the government had to find continuous sources of financing. Decretos-Leis n.os 353, de 13 de Julho de 1973, e 409, de 20 de Agosto were enforced to reduce military expenses and increase the number of officers by incorporating <a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">militia</a> and military-academy officers as equals.<sup id="cite_ref-infopedia.pt_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-infopedia.pt-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The collective farms established in <a href="/wiki/Alentejo" title="Alentejo">Alentejo</a> after the revolution were inefficient. According to government estimates, about 900,000 hectares (2,200,000 acres) of agricultural land were seized between April 1974 and December 1975 as part of <a href="/wiki/Land_reform" title="Land reform">land reform</a>; about 32 percent of the appropriations were ruled illegal.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A complete citation is needed (January 2019)">full citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In January 1976, The government pledged to restore the illegally-occupied land to its owners in 1976, and enacted the Land Reform Review Law the following year. Restoration of illegally-occupied land began in 1978.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1960, Portugal's per-capita GDP was 38 percent of the European Economic Community average. By the end of the Salazar period in 1968 it had risen to 48 percent, and in 1973 it had reached 56.4 percent; the percentages were affected by the 40 percent of the budget which underwrote the African wars). In 1975 (the year of greatest revolutionary turmoil), Portugal's per-capita GDP declined to 52.3 percent of the EEC average. Due to revolutionary economic policies, oil shocks, recession in Europe and the return of hundreds of thousands of overseas Portuguese from its former colonies, Portugal began an economic crisis in 1974–1975.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Real_gross_domestic_product" title="Real gross domestic product">Real gross domestic product</a> growth resumed as a result of Portugal's economic resurgence since 1985. The country's 1991 per-capita GDP reached 54.9 percent of the EEC average, slightly exceeding the level at the height of the revolutionary period.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A January 2011 story in the <i>Diário de Notícias</i> (a right-wing Portuguese tabloid newspaper) reported that the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Portugal" title="Government of Portugal">government of Portugal</a> encouraged overspending and investment bubbles in public-private partnerships between 1974 and 2010, and the economy has been damaged by risky <a href="/wiki/Credit_(finance)" class="mw-redirect" title="Credit (finance)">credit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Public_debt" class="mw-redirect" title="Public debt">public debt</a> creation and mismanaged European <a href="/wiki/Structural_and_cohesion_funds" class="mw-redirect" title="Structural and cohesion funds">structural and cohesion funds</a> for almost four decades. Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_S%C3%B3crates" title="José Sócrates">José Sócrates</a>' cabinet was unable to foresee or forestall this when symptoms first appeared in 2005, and could not ameliorate the situation when the Portugal was on the verge of bankruptcy in 2011 and required financial assistance from the <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">International Monetary Fund</a> and the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Freedom_of_religion">Freedom of religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnation_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Freedom of religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Portugal" title="Constitution of Portugal">constitution of 1976</a> guarantees all religions the right to practice, and non-Catholic groups are recognised as legal entities with the <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_assembly" title="Freedom of assembly">right to assemble</a>. Non-Catholic <a href="/wiki/Conscientious_objector" title="Conscientious objector">conscientious objectors</a> have the right to apply for alternative military service. The <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>, however, still sought to impede other missionary activity.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The ban on <a href="/wiki/Jehovah%27s_Witnesses" title="Jehovah&#39;s Witnesses">Jehovah's Witnesses</a> activity was abolished. The Witnesses were registered as a religious organisation in December 1976, and organised their first Portuguese international convention in Lisbon in 1978.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Freedom_Day">Freedom Day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnation_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Freedom Day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Freedom Day (25 April) is a <a href="/wiki/Public_holidays_in_Portugal" title="Public holidays in Portugal">national holiday</a>, with state-sponsored and spontaneous commemorations of the <a href="/wiki/Civil_liberties" title="Civil liberties">civil liberties</a> and <a href="/wiki/Political_freedom" title="Political freedom">political freedoms</a> achieved after the revolution. It commemorates the 25 April 1974 coup and Portugal's first free elections on that date the following year.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commemorations"><span id="Monuments"></span>Commemorations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnation_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Commemorations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ponte25Abril1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Long, red suspension bridge against a cloudless sky" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Ponte25Abril1.jpg/260px-Ponte25Abril1.jpg" decoding="async" width="260" height="195" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Ponte25Abril1.jpg/390px-Ponte25Abril1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Ponte25Abril1.jpg/520px-Ponte25Abril1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="959" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ponte25Abril1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Originally named after former Prime Minister António de Oliveira Salazar, the 25 de Abril Bridge is a Lisbon icon</div></div></div>
<p>Construction of the 25 de Abril Bridge began on 5 November 1962. It opened on 6 August 1966 as the Salazar Bridge, named after Estado Novo leader <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_de_Oliveira_Salazar" title="António de Oliveira Salazar">António de Oliveira Salazar</a>. Soon after the Carnation Revolution, the bridge was renamed the <a href="/wiki/25_de_Abril_Bridge" title="25 de Abril Bridge">25 de Abril Bridge</a> to commemorate the revolution. Citizens who removed the large, brass "Salazar" sign from a main pillar of the bridge and painting a provisional "25 de Abril" in its place were recorded on film. Many Portuguese streets and squares are named <i>vinte e cinco de Abril</i>, for the day of the revolution. The Portuguese Mint chose the 40th anniversary of the Carnation Revolution for its 2014 <a href="/wiki/2_euro_commemorative_coin" class="mw-redirect" title="2 euro commemorative coin">2 euro commemorative coin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Analysis"><span id="Evaluations_of_the_revolution&#39;s_outcomes"></span>Analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnation_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After an early period of turmoil, Portugal emerged as a democratic country. The country divested itself of almost all of its former colonies and experienced severe economic turmoil. For the Portuguese and their former colonies this was a very difficult period, but  <a href="/wiki/Civil_rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil rights">civil rights</a> and <a href="/wiki/Political_freedom" title="Political freedom">political freedoms</a> were achieved.
</p><p>The international community censured the Estado Novo regime for refusing to grant <a href="/wiki/Independence" title="Independence">independence</a> to its colonies in Africa. Its leaders, <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_de_Oliveira_Salazar" title="António de Oliveira Salazar">Salazar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marcelo_Caetano" title="Marcelo Caetano">Caetano</a>, were accused of being oblivious to what <a href="/wiki/Harold_Macmillan" title="Harold Macmillan">Harold Macmillan</a> called the <a href="/wiki/Wind_of_Change_(speech)" title="Wind of Change (speech)">wind of change</a> in Africa.
</p>
