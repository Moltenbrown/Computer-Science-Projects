<p><b>Rajput</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> <i>raja-putra</i>, "son of a king") is a large multi-component cluster of castes, kin bodies, and local groups, sharing social status and ideology of genealogical descent originating from the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>. The term Rajput covers various <a href="/wiki/Patrilineal" class="mw-redirect" title="Patrilineal">patrilineal</a> clans historically associated with <a href="/wiki/Warrior" title="Warrior">warriorhood</a>: several clans claim Rajput status, although not all claims are universally accepted.
</p><p>The term "Rajput" acquired its present meaning only in the 16th century, although it is also <a href="/wiki/Anachronism" title="Anachronism">anachronistically</a> used to describe the earlier lineages that emerged in northern India from 6th century onwards. In the 11th century, the term "<i>rajaputra</i>" appeared as a non-hereditary designation for royal officials. Gradually, the Rajputs emerged as a social class comprising people from a variety of ethnic and geographical backgrounds. During the 16th and 17th centuries, the membership of this class became largely hereditary, although new claims to Rajput status continued to be made in the later centuries. Several <a href="/wiki/List_of_Rajput_dynasties_and_states" title="List of Rajput dynasties and states">Rajput-ruled kingdoms</a> played a significant role in many regions of central and <a href="/wiki/North_India" title="North India">northern India</a> until the 20th century.
</p><p>The Rajput population and the former Rajput states are found in north, west, central and east India. These areas include <a href="/wiki/Rajasthan" title="Rajasthan">Rajasthan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gujarat" title="Gujarat">Gujarat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uttar_Pradesh" title="Uttar Pradesh">Uttar Pradesh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Himachal_Pradesh" title="Himachal Pradesh">Himachal Pradesh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haryana" title="Haryana">Haryana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jammu_region" class="mw-redirect" title="Jammu region">Jammu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uttarakhand" title="Uttarakhand">Uttarakhand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bihar" title="Bihar">Bihar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Madhya_Pradesh" title="Madhya Pradesh">Madhya Pradesh</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Codice_Casanatense_Rajputs.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Codice_Casanatense_Rajputs.png/220px-Codice_Casanatense_Rajputs.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="158" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Codice_Casanatense_Rajputs.png/330px-Codice_Casanatense_Rajputs.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Codice_Casanatense_Rajputs.png/440px-Codice_Casanatense_Rajputs.png 2x" data-file-width="1159" data-file-height="835" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Codice_Casanatense_Rajputs.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"Resbutos", Rajputs as depicted by the Portuguese in the 16th century <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B3dice_Casanatense" title="Códice Casanatense">Códice Casanatense</a></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span></h3>
<p>The origin of the Rajputs has been a much-debated topic among the historians. <a href="/wiki/British_India" class="mw-redirect" title="British India">Colonial-era</a> writers characterised them as descendants of the foreign invaders such as the <a href="/wiki/Scythians" title="Scythians">Scythians</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Huna_people" title="Huna people">Hunas</a>, and believed that the <a href="/wiki/Agnikula" class="mw-redirect" title="Agnikula">Agnikula</a> myth was invented to conceal their foreign origin.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlf_Hiltebeitel1999439–440_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlf_Hiltebeitel1999439–440-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> According to this theory, the Rajputs originated when these invaders were assimilated into the <a href="/wiki/Kshatriya" title="Kshatriya">Kshatriya</a> category during the 6th or 7th century, following the collapse of the <a href="/wiki/Gupta_Empire" title="Gupta Empire">Gupta Empire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBhrigupati_Singh201538_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBhrigupati_Singh201538-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPradeep_Barua200524_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPradeep_Barua200524-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> While many of these colonial writers propagated this foreign-origin theory in order to legitimise the colonial rule, the theory was also supported by some Indian scholars, such as <a href="/wiki/D._R._Bhandarkar" title="D. R. Bhandarkar">D. R. Bhandarkar</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlf_Hiltebeitel1999439–440_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlf_Hiltebeitel1999439–440-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The Indian nationalist historians, such as <a href="/wiki/Chintaman_Vinayak_Vaidya" title="Chintaman Vinayak Vaidya">C. V. Vaidya</a>, believed the Rajputs to be descendants of the ancient <a href="/wiki/Vedic_Aryan" class="mw-redirect" title="Vedic Aryan">Vedic Aryan</a> Kshatriyas.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlf_Hiltebeitel1999440–441_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlf_Hiltebeitel1999440–441-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> A third group of historians, which includes Jai Narayan Asopa, theorized that the Rajputs were <a href="/wiki/Brahmin" title="Brahmin">Brahmins</a> who became rulers.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlf_Hiltebeitel1999441–442_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlf_Hiltebeitel1999441–442-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, recent research suggests that the Rajputs came from a variety of ethnic and geographical backgrounds.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECatherine_B._AsherCynthia_Talbot200699_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECatherine_B._AsherCynthia_Talbot200699-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The root word "<i>rajaputra</i>" (literally "son of a king") first appears as a designation for royal officials in the 11th century <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> inscriptions. According to some scholars, it was reserved for the immediate relatives of a king; others believe that it was used by a larger group of high-ranking men.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECynthia_Talbot2015119_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECynthia_Talbot2015119-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Over time, the derivative term "Rajput" came to denote a hereditary political status, which was not necessarily very high: the term could denote a wide range of rank-holders, from an actual son of a king to the lowest-ranked landholder.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrajadulal_Chattopadhyaya199479–80_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrajadulal_Chattopadhyaya199479–80-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Before the 15th century, the term "Rajput" was also associated with people of mixed-caste origin, who were considered inferior in rank to "Kshatriya".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESatish_Chandra198292_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESatish_Chandra198292-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Gradually, the term Rajput came to denote a <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">social class</a>, which was formed when the various tribal and nomadic groups became landed aristocrats, and transformed into the ruling class.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETanuja_Kothiyal20168_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETanuja_Kothiyal20168-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> These groups assumed the title "Rajput" as part of their claim to higher social positions and ranks.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERichard_Gabriel_Fox197116_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERichard_Gabriel_Fox197116-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The early medieval literature suggests that this newly formed Rajput class comprised people from multiple <a href="/wiki/Indian_caste_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian caste system">castes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrajadulal_Chattopadhyaya199460_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrajadulal_Chattopadhyaya199460-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Thus, the Rajput identity is not the result of a shared ancestry. Rather, it emerged when different social groups of medieval India sought to legitimize their newly acquired political power by claiming Kshatriya status. These groups started identifying as Rajput at different times, in different ways.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrajadulal_Chattopadhyaya199459_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrajadulal_Chattopadhyaya199459-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emergence_as_a_community">Emergence as a community</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pritam_niwas_with.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Pritam_niwas_with.jpg/220px-Pritam_niwas_with.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Pritam_niwas_with.jpg/330px-Pritam_niwas_with.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Pritam_niwas_with.jpg/440px-Pritam_niwas_with.jpg 2x" data-file-width="572" data-file-height="428" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pritam_niwas_with.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Chandramahal</i> in <a href="/wiki/City_Palace,_Jaipur" title="City Palace, Jaipur">City Palace, Jaipur</a>, built by <a href="/wiki/Kachwaha" title="Kachwaha">Kachwaha</a> Rajputs</div></div></div>
<p>Scholarly opinions differ on when the term Rajput acquired hereditary connotations and came to denote a clan-based community. Historian Brajadulal Chattopadhyaya, based on his analysis of inscriptions (primarily from Rajasthan), believed that by the 12th century, the term "<i>rajaputra</i>" was associated with fortified settlements, kin-based landholding, and other features that later became indicative of the Rajput status.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECynthia_Talbot2015119_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECynthia_Talbot2015119-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> According to Chattopadhyaya, the title acquired "an element of heredity" from c. 1300.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECynthia_Talbot2015120_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECynthia_Talbot2015120-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> A later study by of 11th–14th century inscriptions from western and central India, by Michael B. Bednar, concludes that the designations such as "<i>rajaputra</i>", "<i><a href="/wiki/Thakur_(title)" title="Thakur (title)">thakkura</a></i>" and "<i><a href="/wiki/Rawat_(title)" title="Rawat (title)">rauta</a></i>" were not necessarily hereditary during this period.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECynthia_Talbot2015120_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECynthia_Talbot2015120-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During its formative stages, the Rajput class was quite assimilative and absorbed people from a wide range of lineages.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETanuja_Kothiyal20168_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETanuja_Kothiyal20168-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> However, by the late 16th century, it had become genealogically rigid, based on the ideas of blood purity.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETanuja_Kothiyal20168–9_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETanuja_Kothiyal20168–9-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The membership of the Rajput class was now largely inherited rather than acquired through military achievements.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECynthia_Talbot2015120_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECynthia_Talbot2015120-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> A major factor behind this development was the consolidation of the <a href="/wiki/Mughal_Empire" title="Mughal Empire">Mughal Empire</a>, whose rulers had great interest in genealogy. As the various Rajput chiefs became Mughal feduatories, they no longer engaged in major conflicts with each other. This decreased the possibility of achieving prestige through military action, and made hereditary prestige more important.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECynthia_Talbot2015121_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECynthia_Talbot2015121-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The word "Rajput" thus acquired its present-day meaning in the 16th century.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIrfan_Habib200290_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIrfan_Habib200290-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavid_Ludden19994_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavid_Ludden19994-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> During 16th and 17th centuries, the Rajput rulers and their bards (<i><a href="/wiki/Charan" title="Charan">charans</a></i>) sought to legitimize the Rajput socio-political status on the basis of descent and kinship.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarbara_N._Ramusack200413_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarbara_N._Ramusack200413-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> They fabricated genealogies linking the Rajput families to the ancient dynasties, and associated them with myths of origins that established their Kshatriya status.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECynthia_Talbot2015120_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECynthia_Talbot2015120-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAndré_Wink1990282_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAndré_Wink1990282-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> This led to the emergence of what Indologist Dirk Kolff calls the "Rajput Great Tradition", which accepted only hereditary claims to the Rajput identity, and fostered a notion of eliteness and exclusivity.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECynthia_Talbot2015121–122_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECynthia_Talbot2015121–122-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The legendary epic poem <i><a href="/wiki/Prithviraj_Raso" title="Prithviraj Raso">Prithviraj Raso</a></i>, which depicts warriors from several different Rajput clans as associates of <a href="/wiki/Prithviraj_Chauhan" title="Prithviraj Chauhan">Prithviraj Chauhan</a>, fostered a sense of unity among these clans.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECynthia_Talbot2015121-125_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECynthia_Talbot2015121-125-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The text thus contributed to the consolidation of the Rajput identity by offering these clans a shared history.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECynthia_Talbot2015119_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECynthia_Talbot2015119-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite these developments, migrant soldiers made new claims to the Rajput status until as late as the 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETanuja_Kothiyal20168–9_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETanuja_Kothiyal20168–9-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In the 19th century, the colonial administrators of India re-imagined the Rajputs as similar to the Anglo-Saxon <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knights</a>. They compiled the Rajput genealogies in the process of settling land disputes, surveying castes and tribes, and writing history. These genealogies became the basis of distinguishing between the "genuine" and the "spurious" Rajput clans.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETanuja_Kothiyal201611_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETanuja_Kothiyal201611-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rajput_kingdoms">Rajput kingdoms</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Rajput_resistance_to_Muslim_conquests" title="Rajput resistance to Muslim conquests">Rajput resistance to Muslim conquests</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_Rajput_dynasties_and_states" title="List of Rajput dynasties and states">List of Rajput dynasties and states</a></div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Rajput_kingdoms" class="mw-redirect" title="Rajput kingdoms">Rajput kingdoms</a> were disparate: loyalty to a clan was more important than allegiance to the wider Rajput social grouping, meaning that one clan would fight another. This and the internecine jostling for position that took place when a clan leader (raja) died meant that Rajput politics were fluid and prevented the formation of a coherent Rajput empire.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPradeep_Barua200525_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPradeep_Barua200525-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>
The first major Rajput kingdom was the <a href="/wiki/Sisodia" title="Sisodia">Sisodia</a>-ruled kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Udaipur_State" title="Udaipur State">Mewar</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECatherine_B._AsherCynthia_Talbot200699_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECatherine_B._AsherCynthia_Talbot200699-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> However, the term "Rajput" has also been used as an <a href="/wiki/Anachronism" title="Anachronism">anachronistic</a> designation for leading martial lineages of 11th and 12th centuries that confronted the <a href="/wiki/Ghaznavid" class="mw-redirect" title="Ghaznavid">Ghaznavid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ghurid" class="mw-redirect" title="Ghurid">Ghurid</a> invaders such as the <a href="/wiki/Gurjara-Pratihara" class="mw-redirect" title="Gurjara-Pratihara">Pratiharas</a>, the Chahamanas (of <a href="/wiki/Chahamanas_of_Shakambhari" title="Chahamanas of Shakambhari">Shakambhari</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chahamanas_of_Naddula" title="Chahamanas of Naddula">Nadol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chahamanas_of_Jalor" title="Chahamanas of Jalor">Jalor</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Tomara_dynasty" title="Tomara dynasty">Tomaras</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Chaulukya" class="mw-redirect" title="Chaulukya">Chaulukyas</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Paramara" class="mw-redirect" title="Paramara">Paramaras</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Gahadavala" class="mw-redirect" title="Gahadavala">Gahadavalas</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Chandela" title="Chandela">Chandelas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeter_Jackson20039_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeter_Jackson20039-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECynthia_Talbot201533_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECynthia_Talbot201533-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>.Although the Rajput identity did not exist at this time, these lineages were classified as aristocratic Rajput clans in the later times.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECynthia_Talbot201533-35_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECynthia_Talbot201533-35-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup></p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Chittorgarh_fort.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Chittorgarh_fort.JPG/220px-Chittorgarh_fort.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Chittorgarh_fort.JPG/330px-Chittorgarh_fort.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Chittorgarh_fort.JPG/440px-Chittorgarh_fort.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3008" data-file-height="2000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Chittorgarh_fort.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Chittor_Fort" title="Chittor Fort">Chittor Fort</a>, built by a dynasty of <a href="/wiki/Sisodia" title="Sisodia">Sisodia</a> Rajputs, is one of the largest forts in India.</div></div></div><p>In the 15th century, the Muslim sultans of <a href="/wiki/Malwa_Sultanate" title="Malwa Sultanate">Malwa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gujarat_Sultanate" title="Gujarat Sultanate">Gujarat</a> put a joint effort to overcome the Mewar ruler <a href="/wiki/Rana_Kumbha" class="mw-redirect" title="Rana Kumbha">Rana Kumbha</a> but both the sultans were defeated.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Subsequently, in 1518 the Rajput <a href="/wiki/Mewar_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Mewar Kingdom">Mewar Kingdom</a> under <a href="/wiki/Rana_Sanga" title="Rana Sanga">Rana Sanga</a> achieved a major victory over Sultan <a href="/wiki/Ibrahim_Lodhi" class="mw-redirect" title="Ibrahim Lodhi">Ibrahim Lodhi</a> of Delhi Sultanate and afterwards Rana's influence extended up to the striking distance of Pilia Khar in <a href="/wiki/Agra" title="Agra">Agra</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Maharana_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maharana-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Accordingly, Rana Sanga came to be the most distinguished indigenous contender for supremacy but was defeated by the <a href="/wiki/Mughal_emperor" class="mw-redirect" title="Mughal emperor">Mughal</a> invader <a href="/wiki/Babur" title="Babur">Babur</a> at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Khanwa" title="Battle of Khanwa">Battle of Khanwa</a> in 1527.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPradeep_Barua200533–34_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPradeep_Barua200533–34-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From as early as the 16th century, <a href="/wiki/Purbiya" title="Purbiya">Purbiya</a> Rajput soldiers from the eastern regions of <a href="/wiki/Bihar" title="Bihar">Bihar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Awadh" title="Awadh">Awadh</a>, were recruited as mercenaries for Rajputs in the west, particularly in the <a href="/wiki/Malwa" title="Malwa">Malwa</a> region.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mughal_period">Mughal period</span></h3>
<h4><span id="Akbar.27s_policy_.28Akbar_-_Shah_Jahan.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Akbar's_policy_(Akbar_-_Shah_Jahan)">Akbar's policy (Akbar -  Shah Jahan)</span></h4>
<p>After the mid-16th century, many Rajput rulers formed close relationships with the <a href="/wiki/Mughal_Empire" title="Mughal Empire">Mughal emperors</a> and served them in different capacities.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> It was due to the support of the Rajputs that Akbar was able to lay the foundations of the Mughal empire in India.<sup id="cite_ref-Chaurasia_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chaurasia-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Some Rajput nobles gave away their daughters in marriage to Mughal emperors and princes for political motives.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDirk_H._A._Kolff2002132_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDirk_H._A._Kolff2002132-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/Akbar" title="Akbar">Akbar</a> accomplished 40 marriages for him, his sons and grandsons, out of which 17 were Rajput-Mughal alliances.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Akbar's successors as Mogul emperors, his son <a href="/wiki/Jahangir" title="Jahangir">Jahangir</a> and grandson <a href="/wiki/Shah_Jahan" title="Shah Jahan">Shah Jahan</a> had Rajput mothers.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> The ruling <a href="/wiki/Sisodia" title="Sisodia">Sisodia</a> Rajput family of <a href="/wiki/Mewar" title="Mewar">Mewar</a> made it a point of honour not to engage in matrimonial relationships with Mughals and thus claimed to stand apart from those Rajput clans who did so.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarbara_N._Ramusack200418–19_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarbara_N._Ramusack200418–19-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>Once Mewar had submitted and alliance of Rajputs reached a measure of stability matrimonial between leading Rajput states and Mughals became rare<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Aurangzeb.27s_policy"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Aurangzeb's_policy">Aurangzeb's policy</span></h4>
<p>Akbar's diplomatic policy regarding the Rajputs was later damaged by the intolerant rules introduced by his great-grandson <a href="/wiki/Aurangzeb" title="Aurangzeb">Aurangzeb</a>. A prominent example of these rules included the re-imposition of <a href="/wiki/Jaziya" class="mw-redirect" title="Jaziya">Jaziya</a>, which had been abolished by Akbar.<sup id="cite_ref-Chaurasia_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chaurasia-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> However, despite imposition of Jaziya Aurangzeb's army had a high proportion of Rajput officers in the upper ranks of the imperial army and they were all exempted from paying Jaziya<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> The Rajputs then revolted against the Mughal empire. Aurangzeb's conflicts with the Rajputs, which commenced in the early 1680s, henceforth became a contributing factor towards the downfall of the Mughal empire.<sup id="cite_ref-EB_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Chaurasia_41-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chaurasia-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 18th century, the Rajputs came under influence of the <a href="/wiki/Maratha_empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Maratha empire">Maratha empire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EB_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> By the late 18th century, the Rajput rulers begin negotiations with the <a href="/wiki/East_India_Company" title="East India Company">East India Company</a> and by 1818 all the Rajput states had formed an <a href="/wiki/Subsidiary_alliance" title="Subsidiary alliance">
alliance</a> with the company.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Settlement_of_the_Gangetic_plains">Settlement of the Gangetic plains</span></h3>
<p>From 1200 A.D., many Rajput groups moved eastwards towards the <a href="/wiki/Indo-Gangetic_plains" class="mw-redirect" title="Indo-Gangetic plains">Eastern Gangetic plains</a> forming their own chieftaincies. <sup id="cite_ref-Bayly1988_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bayly1988-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> These minor Rajput kingdoms were dotted all over the Gangetic plains in modern-day <a href="/wiki/Uttar_Pradesh" title="Uttar Pradesh">Uttar Pradesh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bihar" title="Bihar">Bihar</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ramusack2004_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ramusack2004-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> During this process, petty clashes occurred with the local population and in some cases, alliances were formed.<sup id="cite_ref-Bayly1988_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bayly1988-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Among these Rajput chieftaincies were the <a href="/wiki/Bhojpuri_region" title="Bhojpuri region">Bhojpur</a> zamindars<sup id="cite_ref-Chatterjee1996_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chatterjee1996-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Taluqdar" title="Taluqdar">taluks</a> of <a href="/wiki/Awadh" title="Awadh">Awadh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Fox1971_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fox1971-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The immigration of Rajput clan chiefs into these parts of the Gangetic plains also contributed the agricultural appropriation of previously forested areas, especially in South Bihar.<sup id="cite_ref-Prakash2003_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prakash2003-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> Some have linked this eastwards expansion with the onset of <a href="/wiki/Ghurid_dynasty" title="Ghurid dynasty">Ghurid invasion</a> in the West.<sup id="cite_ref-Prakash2003_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prakash2003-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_colonial_period">British colonial period</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Monitors_Mayo_College_Ajmer.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/20/Monitors_Mayo_College_Ajmer.jpg/220px-Monitors_Mayo_College_Ajmer.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/20/Monitors_Mayo_College_Ajmer.jpg/330px-Monitors_Mayo_College_Ajmer.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/20/Monitors_Mayo_College_Ajmer.jpg/440px-Monitors_Mayo_College_Ajmer.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1854" data-file-height="1372" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Monitors_Mayo_College_Ajmer.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Mayo_College" title="Mayo College">Mayo College</a> was established by the <a href="/wiki/British_Raj" title="British Raj">British</a> government in 1875 at <a href="/wiki/Ajmer" title="Ajmer">Ajmer</a>, Rajputana to educate Rajput princes and other nobles.</div></div></div>
<p>The medieval bardic chronicles (<i><a href="/wiki/Kavya" class="mw-redirect" title="Kavya">kavya</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Masnavi_(poetic_form)" class="mw-redirect" title="Masnavi (poetic form)">masnavi</a></i>) glorified the Rajput past, presenting warriorhood and honour as Rajput ideals. This later became the basis of the British reconstruction of the Rajput history and the nationalist interpretations of Rajputs' struggles with the Muslim invaders.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETanuja_Kothiyal20169–10_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETanuja_Kothiyal20169–10-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/James_Tod" title="James Tod">James Tod</a>, a British colonial official, was impressed by the military qualities of the Rajputs but is today considered to have been unusually enamoured of them. In his <i>Annals and Antiquities of Rajasthan</i>, James Tod writes:<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>What nation on earth could have maintained the semblance of civilization, the spirit or the customs of their forefathers, during so many centuries of overwhelming depression, but one of such singular character as the Rajpoot? ... Rajast'han exhibits the sole example in the history of mankind, of a people withstanding every outrage barbarity could inflict, or human nature sustain, from a foe whose religion commands annihilation; and bent to the earth, yet rising buoyant from the pressure, and making calamity a whetstone to courage .... Not an iota of their religion or customs have they lost ...
</p></blockquote><p>
Although the group venerate him to this day, he is viewed by many historians since the late nineteenth century as being a not particularly reliable commentator.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> Jason Freitag, his only significant biographer, has said that Tod is "manifestly biased".<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup></p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Derawar_fort_during_winters_by_Jazib_Saeed_Khan.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Derawar_fort_during_winters_by_Jazib_Saeed_Khan.jpg/220px-Derawar_fort_during_winters_by_Jazib_Saeed_Khan.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Derawar_fort_during_winters_by_Jazib_Saeed_Khan.jpg/330px-Derawar_fort_during_winters_by_Jazib_Saeed_Khan.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Derawar_fort_during_winters_by_Jazib_Saeed_Khan.jpg/440px-Derawar_fort_during_winters_by_Jazib_Saeed_Khan.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4320" data-file-height="3240" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Derawar_fort_during_winters_by_Jazib_Saeed_Khan.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Derawar_Fort" title="Derawar Fort">Derawar Fort</a> built by a <a href="/wiki/Hindu" class="mw-redirect" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> dynasty of <a href="/wiki/Bhatti" class="mw-redirect" title="Bhatti">Bhatti</a> Rajputs,<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> in modern-day <a href="/wiki/Bahawalpur" title="Bahawalpur">Bahawalpur</a>, Pakistan</div></div></div>
<p>The Rajput practices of <a href="/wiki/Female_infanticide_in_India" title="Female infanticide in India">female infanticide</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Sati_(practice)" title="Sati (practice)">sati</a></i> (widow immolation) were other matters of concern to the British. It was believed that the Rajputs were the primary adherents to these practices, which the British Raj considered savage and which provided the initial impetus for British <a href="/wiki/Ethnography" title="Ethnography">ethnographic</a> studies of the subcontinent that eventually manifested itself as a much wider exercise in <a href="/wiki/Social_engineering_(political_science)" title="Social engineering (political science)">social engineering</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In reference to the role of the Rajput soldiers serving under the British banner, Captain A. H. Bingley wrote:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Rajputs have served in our ranks from <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Plassey" title="Battle of Plassey">Plassey</a> to the present day (1899). They have taken part in almost every campaign undertaken by the Indian armies. Under Forde they defeated the French at Condore. Under Monro at Buxar they routed the forces of the Nawab of Oudh. Under Lake they took part in the brilliant series of victories which destroyed the power of the Marathas.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Independent_India">Independent India</span></h3>
<p>On <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_India" title="Partition of India">India's independence</a> in 1947, the princely states, including those of the Rajput, were given three options: join either India or Pakistan, or remain independent. Rajput rulers of the 22 princely states of <a href="/wiki/Rajputana" title="Rajputana">Rajputana</a> acceded to newly independent India, amalgamated into the new state of <a href="/wiki/Rajasthan" title="Rajasthan">Rajasthan</a> in 1949–1950.<sup id="cite_ref-Markovits2002_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Markovits2002-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> Initially the maharajas were granted funding from the <a href="/wiki/Privy_purse_in_India" class="mw-redirect" title="Privy purse in India">Privy purse</a> in exchange for their acquiescence, but a series of land reforms over the following decades weakened their power, and their privy purse was cut off during <a href="/wiki/Indira_Gandhi" title="Indira Gandhi">Indira Gandhi</a>'s administration under the 1971 <a href="/wiki/List_of_amendments_of_the_Constitution_of_India" title="List of amendments of the Constitution of India">Constitution 26th Amendment Act</a>. The estates, treasures, and practices of the old Rajput rulers now form a key part of Rajasthan's tourist trade and cultural memory.<sup id="cite_ref-Larson2001_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Larson2001-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1951, the Rajput <a href="/wiki/Rana_dynasty" title="Rana dynasty">Rana dynasty</a> of Nepal came to an end, having been the power behind the throne of the <a href="/wiki/Shah_dynasty" title="Shah dynasty">Shah dynasty</a> figureheads since 1846.<sup id="cite_ref-Upreti2002_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Upreti2002-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Rajput <a href="/wiki/Dogra_dynasty" title="Dogra dynasty">Dogra dynasty</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kashmir_and_Jammu_(princely_state)" class="mw-redirect" title="Kashmir and Jammu (princely state)">Kashmir and Jammu</a> also came to an end in 1947,<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> though title was retained until monarchy was abolished in 1971 by the 26th amendment to the Constitution of India.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Rajputs, in states such as <a href="/wiki/Madhya_Pradesh" title="Madhya Pradesh">Madhya Pradesh</a> are today considered to be a <a href="/wiki/Forward_Caste" class="mw-redirect" title="Forward Caste">Forward Caste</a> in India's system of <a href="/wiki/Reservation_in_India" title="Reservation in India">positive discrimination</a>. This means that they have no access to reservations here. But they are classified as  an <a href="/wiki/Other_Backward_Class" title="Other Backward Class">Other Backward Class</a> by the <a href="/wiki/National_Commission_for_Backward_Classes" title="National Commission for Backward Classes">National Commission for Backward Classes</a> in the state of <a href="/wiki/Karnataka" title="Karnataka">Karnataka</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>
<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> However, some Rajputs too like other agricultural castes demand reservations in Government jobs, which so far is not heeded to by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_India" title="Government of India">Government of India</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subdivisions">Subdivisions</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rajput_clans" title="Rajput clans">Rajput clans</a></div>
<p>The term "Rajput" denotes a cluster of castes,<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> clans, and lineages.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> It is a vaguely-defined term, and there is no universal consensus on which clans make up the Rajput community.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAyan_Shome2014196_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAyan_Shome2014196-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> In medieval Rajasthan (the historical <a href="/wiki/Rajputana" title="Rajputana">Rajputana</a>) and its neighbouring areas, the word Rajput came to be restricted to certain specific clans, based on patrilineal descent and intermarriages. On the other hand, the Rajput communities living in the region to the east of Rajasthan had a fluid and inclusive nature. The Rajputs of Rajasthan eventually refused to acknowledge the Rajput identity claimed by their eastern counterparts,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECatherine_B._AsherCynthia_Talbot200699_(Para_3)_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECatherine_B._AsherCynthia_Talbot200699_(Para_3)-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> such as the <a href="/wiki/Bundela" title="Bundela">Bundelas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECynthia_Talbot2015120_(Para_4)_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECynthia_Talbot2015120_(Para_4)-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> The Rajputs claim to be <a href="/wiki/Kshatriya" title="Kshatriya">Kshatriyas</a> or descendants of Kshatriyas, but their actual status varies greatly, ranging from princely lineages to common cultivators.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are several major subdivisions of Rajputs, known as <i>vansh</i> or <i>vamsha</i>, the step below the super-division <a href="/wiki/J%C4%81ti" title="Jāti">jāti</a><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShail_Mayaram2013269_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShail_Mayaram2013269-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> These <i>vansh</i> delineate claimed descent from various sources, and the Rajput are generally considered to be divided into three primary vansh:<sup id="cite_ref-Lunheim1993_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lunheim1993-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Suryavansha" class="mw-redirect" title="Suryavansha">Suryavanshi</a> denotes descent from the solar deity <a href="/wiki/Surya" title="Surya">Surya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chandravanshi" class="mw-redirect" title="Chandravanshi">Chandravanshi</a> (<a href="/wiki/Somavanshi" class="mw-redirect" title="Somavanshi">Somavanshi</a>) from the lunar deity <a href="/wiki/Chandra" title="Chandra">Chandra</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Agnivanshi" class="mw-redirect" title="Agnivanshi">Agnivanshi</a> from the fire deity <a href="/wiki/Agni" title="Agni">Agni</a>. The Agnivanshi clans include <a href="/wiki/Parmar" title="Parmar">Parmar</a>, Chaulukya (<a href="/wiki/Solanki_(clan)" title="Solanki (clan)">Solanki</a>), <a href="/wiki/Parihar" title="Parihar">Parihar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chauhan" title="Chauhan">Chauhan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Unnithan-Kumar1997_89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Unnithan-Kumar1997-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Lesser-noted <i>vansh</i> include Udayvanshi, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rajvanshi_(clan)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rajvanshi (clan) (page does not exist)">Rajvanshi</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Jha1997_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jha1997-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Rishivanshi" title="Rishivanshi">Rishivanshi</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. The histories of the various <i>vansh</i>s were later recorded in documents known as <i>vamshāavalīis</i>; André Wink counts these among the "status-legitimizing texts".<sup id="cite_ref-Wink2002_91-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wink2002-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Indian_Army-Rajput_regiment.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Indian_Army-Rajput_regiment.jpeg/220px-Indian_Army-Rajput_regiment.jpeg" decoding="async" width="220" height="125" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Indian_Army-Rajput_regiment.jpeg/330px-Indian_Army-Rajput_regiment.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Indian_Army-Rajput_regiment.jpeg/440px-Indian_Army-Rajput_regiment.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="3130" data-file-height="1772" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Indian_Army-Rajput_regiment.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A contingent of the <a href="/wiki/Rajput_Regiment" title="Rajput Regiment">Rajput Regiment</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Army" title="Indian Army">Indian Army</a>, during the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Republic_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian Republic Day">Republic day</a> parade</div></div></div>
<p>Beneath the <i>vansh</i> division are smaller and smaller subdivisions: <i>kul</i>, <i>shakh</i> ("branch"), <i>khamp</i> or <i>khanp</i> ("twig"), and <i>nak</i> ("twig tip").<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShail_Mayaram2013269_87-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShail_Mayaram2013269-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> Marriages within a <i>kul</i> are generally disallowed (with some flexibility for kul-mates of different <i>gotra</i> lineages). The <i>kul</i> serves as the primary identity for many of the Rajput clans, and each <i>kul</i> is protected by a family goddess, the <i><a href="/wiki/Kuldevi" class="mw-redirect" title="Kuldevi">kuldevi</a></i>. Lindsey Harlan notes that in some cases, <i>shakh</i>s have become powerful enough to be functionally <i>kul</i>s in their own right.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELindsey_Harlan199231_92-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELindsey_Harlan199231-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture_and_ethos">Culture and ethos</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Rajpootnee_Bride.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/The_Rajpootnee_Bride.jpg/220px-The_Rajpootnee_Bride.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="344" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/The_Rajpootnee_Bride.jpg/330px-The_Rajpootnee_Bride.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/The_Rajpootnee_Bride.jpg/440px-The_Rajpootnee_Bride.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2710" data-file-height="4240" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Rajpootnee_Bride.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Rajput bride, illustration in The Oriental Annual, or Scenes of India (1835)</div></div></div>
<p>The Bengal army of the <a href="/wiki/East_India_Company" title="East India Company">East India Company</a>  recruited heavily from upper castes such as Brahmins and Rajputs.However,after the revolt of 1857 by the Bengal sepoys, the British indian army  shifted recruitment to the Punjab.<sup id="cite_ref-Streets2004_93-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Streets2004-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup>
The Rajputs were designated as a <a href="/wiki/Martial_Race" class="mw-redirect" title="Martial Race">Martial Race</a> in the period of the British Raj. This was a designation created by administrators that classified each ethnic group as either "martial" or "non-martial": a "martial race" was typically considered brave and well built for fighting,<sup id="cite_ref-rand_94-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rand-94">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup> whilst the remainder were those whom the British believed to be unfit for battle because of their sedentary lifestyles.<sup id="cite_ref-Street_95-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Street-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rajput_lifestyle">Rajput lifestyle</span></h3>
<p>The double-edged scimitar known as the <a href="/wiki/Khanda_(sword)" title="Khanda (sword)">khanda</a> was a popular weapon among the Rajputs of that era.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (October 2018)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> On special occasions, a primary chief would break up a meeting of his vassal chiefs with <i>khanda nariyal</i>, the distribution of daggers and coconuts. Another affirmation of the Rajput's reverence for his sword was the <i>Karga Shapna</i> ("adoration of the sword") ritual, performed during the annual <a href="/wiki/Navaratri" title="Navaratri">Navaratri</a> festival, after which a Rajput is considered "free to indulge his passion for rapine and revenge".<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup>
The Rajput of Rajasthan also offer a sacrifice of water buffalo or goat to their family Goddess ( <a href="/wiki/Kuldevta" class="mw-redirect" title="Kuldevta">Kuldevta</a>) during <a href="/wiki/Navaratri" title="Navaratri">Navaratri</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Goat_sacrifice_to_Shilamata_97-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goat_sacrifice_to_Shilamata-97">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup> The ritual requires slaying of the animal with a single stroke. In the past this ritual was considered a rite of passage for young Rajput men.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELindsey_Harlan199288_98-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELindsey_Harlan199288-98">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Rajputs generally have adopted the custom of <a href="/wiki/Purdah" title="Purdah">purdah</a> (seclusion of women).<sup id="cite_ref-EB_51-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Alcoholism is considered a problem in the Rajput community of Rajasthan and hence Rajput women do not like their men drinking alcohol. It was reported in a 1983 study of alcoholism in India that it was customary for Rajput men (not all) in northern India to drink in groups. The women would at times be subjected to domestic violence such as beating after these men returned home from drinking.<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Until recent times polygamy was common among Rajputs also concubines were kept freely depending upon economic status of a person, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Rajasthan" title="Rajasthan">Rajasthan</a> where there were three classes of concubines first were called khawals, who were  generally jatins and gujarins<sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup>they lived in harem and children were treated as Rajputs except few restrictions. The other 
two classes were called Darogi and Goli.<sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the British rule their love for pork, i.e. wild boar, was also well known and the British identified them as a group based on this.
<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">&#91;104&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A complete citation is needed (May 2018)">full citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>By the late 19th century, there was a shift of focus among Rajputs from politics to a concern with kinship.<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup> Many Rajputs of Rajasthan are nostalgic about their past and keenly conscious of their genealogy, emphasising a Rajput ethos that is martial in spirit, with a fierce pride in lineage and tradition.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELindsey_Harlan199227_106-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELindsey_Harlan199227-106">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rajput_politics">Rajput politics</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:A_royal_Rajput_procession.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/A_royal_Rajput_procession.jpg/220px-A_royal_Rajput_procession.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/A_royal_Rajput_procession.jpg/330px-A_royal_Rajput_procession.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/A_royal_Rajput_procession.jpg/440px-A_royal_Rajput_procession.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="533" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:A_royal_Rajput_procession.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A royal Rajput procession, depicted on a mural at the <a href="/wiki/Mehrangarh_Fort" class="mw-redirect" title="Mehrangarh Fort">Mehrangarh Fort</a> in Jodhpur<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Rajput politics refers to the role played by the Rajput community in the electoral politics of India.<sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108">&#91;108&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109">&#91;109&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="Citation is the Ghostbusters&#39; company website; there must be more objective sources for this. (October 2018)">better&#160;source&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In states such as Rajasthan, Uttar Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, Bihar, Uttrakhand, Jammu, Himachal Pradesh, and Gujarat, the large populations of Rajputs gives them a decisive role.<sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">&#91;110&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">&#91;111&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112">&#91;112&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="None of the cited sources support the claims (October 2018)">better&#160;source&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arts">Arts</span></h3>
<p>The term <a href="/wiki/Rajput_painting" title="Rajput painting">Rajput painting</a> refers to works of art created at the Rajput-ruled courts of Rajasthan, <a href="/wiki/Central_India_Agency" title="Central India Agency">Central India</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Punjab_States_Agency#Historical_princely_states_of_the_Punjab_Hills" title="Punjab States Agency">Punjab Hills</a>. The term is also used to describe the style of these paintings, distinct from the <a href="/wiki/Mughal_painting" title="Mughal painting">Mughal painting</a> style.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKarine_Schomer1994338_113-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKarine_Schomer1994338-113">&#91;113&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/Ananda_Coomaraswamy" title="Ananda Coomaraswamy">Ananda Coomaraswamy</a>, Rajput painting symbolised the divide between Muslims and Hindus during Mughal rule. The styles of Mughal and Rajput painting are oppositional in character. He characterised Rajput painting as "popular, universal and mystic".<sup id="cite_ref-114" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-114">&#91;114&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Rajput painting varied geographically, corresponding to each of the various Rajput kingdoms and regions. The Delhi area, Punjab, Rajasthan, and Central India each had its own variant.<sup id="cite_ref-115" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-115">&#91;115&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="Nothing on page 3. Maybe a different page number? (September 2017)">failed verification</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
