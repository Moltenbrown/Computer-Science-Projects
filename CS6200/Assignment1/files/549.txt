<p><b>Heredity</b>, also called <b>inheritance</b> or <b>biological inheritance</b>, is the passing on of <a href="/wiki/Phenotypic_trait" title="Phenotypic trait">traits</a> from parents to their offspring; either through <a href="/wiki/Asexual_reproduction" title="Asexual reproduction">asexual reproduction</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sexual_reproduction" title="Sexual reproduction">sexual reproduction</a>, the offspring <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> or <a href="/wiki/Organisms" class="mw-redirect" title="Organisms">organisms</a> acquire the <a href="/wiki/Genetic_information" class="mw-redirect" title="Genetic information">genetic information</a> of their parents. Through heredity, variations between individuals can accumulate and cause <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> to <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolve</a> by <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a>. The study of heredity in <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a> is <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heredity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jug_Ear_Heredity.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Jug_Ear_Heredity.jpg/150px-Jug_Ear_Heredity.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="222" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Jug_Ear_Heredity.jpg/225px-Jug_Ear_Heredity.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Jug_Ear_Heredity.jpg/300px-Jug_Ear_Heredity.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1101" data-file-height="1633" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jug_Ear_Heredity.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Heredity of phenotypic traits: Father and son with <a href="/wiki/Prominent_ear" class="mw-redirect" title="Prominent ear">prominent ears</a> and crowns.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:183px;"><a href="/wiki/File:DNA_animation.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0c/DNA_animation.gif" decoding="async" width="181" height="313" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="181" data-file-height="313" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:DNA_animation.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> structure. <a href="/wiki/Nucleobase" title="Nucleobase">Bases</a> are in the centre, surrounded by phosphate–sugar chains in a <a href="/wiki/Double_helix" class="mw-redirect" title="Double helix">double helix</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>In humans, <a href="/wiki/Eye_color" title="Eye color">eye color</a> is an example of an inherited characteristic: an individual might inherit the "brown-eye trait" from one of the parents.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Inherited traits are controlled by <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a> and the complete set of genes within an organism's <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a> is called its <a href="/wiki/Genotype" title="Genotype">genotype</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pearson_2006_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pearson_2006-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The complete set of observable traits of the structure and behavior of an organism is called its <a href="/wiki/Phenotype" title="Phenotype">phenotype</a>. These traits arise from the interaction of its genotype with the <a href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Environment (biophysical)">environment</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> As a result, many aspects of an organism's phenotype are not inherited. For example, <a href="/wiki/Sun_tanning" title="Sun tanning">suntanned</a> skin comes from the interaction between a person's phenotype and sunlight;<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> thus, suntans are not passed on to people's children. However, some people tan more easily than others, due to differences in their genotype:<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> a striking example is people with the inherited trait of <a href="/wiki/Albinism" title="Albinism">albinism</a>, who do not tan at all and are very sensitive to <a href="/wiki/Sunburn" title="Sunburn">sunburn</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Heritable traits are known to be passed from one generation to the next via <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecule</a> that encodes genetic information.<sup id="cite_ref-Pearson_2006_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pearson_2006-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> DNA is a long <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a> that incorporates four types of <a href="/wiki/Base_pair" title="Base pair">bases</a>, which are interchangeable. The sequence of bases along a particular DNA molecule specifies the genetic information: this is comparable to a sequence of letters spelling out a passage of text.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Before a cell divides through <a href="/wiki/Mitosis" title="Mitosis">mitosis</a>, the DNA is copied, so that each of the resulting two cells will inherit the DNA sequence. A portion of a DNA molecule that specifies a single functional unit is called a <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">gene</a>; different genes have different sequences of bases. Within <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a>, the long strands of DNA form condensed structures called <a href="/wiki/Chromosome" title="Chromosome">chromosomes</a>. Organisms inherit genetic material from their parents in the form of <a href="/wiki/Homologous_chromosome" title="Homologous chromosome">homologous chromosomes</a>, containing a unique combination of DNA sequences that code for genes. The specific location of a DNA sequence within a chromosome is known as a <a href="/wiki/Locus_(genetics)" title="Locus (genetics)">locus</a>. If the DNA sequence at a particular locus varies between individuals, the different forms of this sequence are called <a href="/wiki/Allele" title="Allele">alleles</a>. DNA sequences can change through <a href="/wiki/Mutation" title="Mutation">mutations</a>, producing new alleles. If a mutation occurs within a gene, the new allele may affect the trait that the gene controls, altering the phenotype of the organism.<sup id="cite_ref-Futuyma_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Futuyma-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, while this simple correspondence between an allele and a trait works in some cases, most traits are more complex and are controlled by <a href="/wiki/Quantitative_trait_locus" title="Quantitative trait locus">multiple interacting genes</a> within and among organisms.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lin_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lin-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Developmental biologists suggest that complex interactions in genetic networks and communication among cells can lead to heritable variations that may underlie some of the mechanics in <a href="/wiki/Developmental_plasticity" title="Developmental plasticity">developmental plasticity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canalisation_(genetics)" title="Canalisation (genetics)">canalization</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Jablonka02_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jablonka02-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Recent findings have confirmed important examples of heritable changes that cannot be explained by direct agency of the DNA molecule. These phenomena are classed as <a href="/wiki/Epigenetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Epigenetic">epigenetic</a> inheritance systems that are causally or independently evolving over genes. Research into modes and mechanisms of epigenetic inheritance is still in its scientific infancy, however, this area of research has attracted much recent activity as it broadens the scope of <a href="/wiki/Heritability" title="Heritability">heritability</a> and evolutionary biology in general.<sup id="cite_ref-Jablonk09_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jablonk09-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/DNA_methylation" title="DNA methylation">DNA methylation</a> marking <a href="/wiki/Chromatin" title="Chromatin">chromatin</a>, self-sustaining <a href="/wiki/Metabolism#Evolution" title="Metabolism">metabolic loops</a>, gene silencing by <a href="/wiki/RNA_interference" title="RNA interference">RNA interference</a>, and the three dimensional <a href="/wiki/Protein_structure" title="Protein structure">conformation</a> of proteins (such as <a href="/wiki/Prions" class="mw-redirect" title="Prions">prions</a>) are areas where epigenetic inheritance systems have been discovered at the organismic level.<sup id="cite_ref-Bossdorf10_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bossdorf10-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Jablonka05_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jablonka05-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Heritability may also occur at even larger scales. For example, ecological inheritance through the process of <a href="/wiki/Niche_construction" title="Niche construction">niche construction</a> is defined by the regular and repeated activities of organisms in their environment. This generates a legacy of effect that modifies and feeds back into the selection regime of subsequent generations. Descendants inherit genes plus environmental characteristics generated by the ecological actions of ancestors.<sup id="cite_ref-Laland06_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Laland06-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Other examples of heritability in evolution that are not under the direct control of genes include the inheritance of <a href="/wiki/Dual_inheritance_theory" title="Dual inheritance theory">cultural traits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Group_selection" title="Group selection">group heritability</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Symbiogenesis" title="Symbiogenesis">symbiogenesis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapman98_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapman98-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wilson07_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson07-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bijma08_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bijma08-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> These examples of heritability that operate above the gene are covered broadly under the title of <a href="/wiki/Multilevel_selection" class="mw-redirect" title="Multilevel selection">multilevel or hierarchical selection</a>, which has been a subject of intense debate in the history of evolutionary science.<sup id="cite_ref-Wilson07_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson07-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Vrba86_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vrba86-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_theory_of_evolution">Relation to theory of evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heredity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Relation to theory of evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">Evolution</a></div>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a> proposed his theory of <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a> in 1859, one of its major problems was the lack of an underlying mechanism for heredity.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Darwin believed in a mix of blending inheritance and the inheritance of acquired <a href="/wiki/Phenotypic_trait" title="Phenotypic trait">traits</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pangenesis" title="Pangenesis">pangenesis</a>). Blending inheritance would lead to uniformity across populations in only a few generations and then would remove variation from a population on which natural selection could act.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> This led to Darwin adopting some Lamarckian ideas in later editions of <i><a href="/wiki/On_the_Origin_of_Species" title="On the Origin of Species">On the Origin of Species</a></i> and his later biological works.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Darwin's primary approach to heredity was to outline how it appeared to work (noticing that traits that were not expressed explicitly in the parent at the time of reproduction could be inherited, that certain traits could be <a href="/wiki/Sex-linked" class="mw-redirect" title="Sex-linked">sex-linked</a>, etc.) rather than suggesting mechanisms.
</p><p>Darwin's initial model of heredity was adopted by, and then heavily modified by, his cousin <a href="/wiki/Francis_Galton" title="Francis Galton">Francis Galton</a>, who laid the framework for the <a href="/wiki/Biometric" class="mw-redirect" title="Biometric">biometric</a> school of heredity.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Galton found no evidence to support the aspects of Darwin's pangenesis model, which relied on acquired traits.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The inheritance of acquired traits was shown to have little basis in the 1880s when <a href="/wiki/August_Weismann" title="August Weismann">August Weismann</a> cut the <a href="/wiki/Tail" title="Tail">tails</a> off many generations of <a href="/wiki/Mouse" title="Mouse">mice</a> and found that their offspring continued to develop tails.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heredity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aristotle%27s_model_of_Inheritance.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Aristotle%27s_model_of_Inheritance.png/310px-Aristotle%27s_model_of_Inheritance.png" decoding="async" width="310" height="233" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Aristotle%27s_model_of_Inheritance.png/465px-Aristotle%27s_model_of_Inheritance.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Aristotle%27s_model_of_Inheritance.png/620px-Aristotle%27s_model_of_Inheritance.png 2x" data-file-width="960" data-file-height="720" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aristotle%27s_model_of_Inheritance.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Aristotle%27s_biology#Inheritance" title="Aristotle&#39;s biology">Aristotle's model of inheritance</a>. The heat/cold part is largely symmetrical, though influenced on the father's side by other factors; but the form part is not.</div></div></div>
<p>Scientists in <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">Antiquity</a> had a variety of ideas about heredity: <a href="/wiki/Theophrastus" title="Theophrastus">Theophrastus</a> proposed that male flowers caused female flowers to ripen;<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hippocrates" title="Hippocrates">Hippocrates</a> speculated that "seeds" were produced by various body parts and transmitted to offspring at the time of conception;<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> thought that male and female fluids mixed at conception.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Aeschylus" title="Aeschylus">Aeschylus</a>, in 458 BC, proposed the male as the parent, with the female as a "nurse for the young life sown within her".<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ancient understandings of heredity transitioned to two debated doctrines in the 18th century. The Doctrine of Epigenesis and the Doctrine of Preformation were two distinct views of the understanding of heredity. The Doctrine of Epigenesis, originated by <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, claimed that an embryo continually develops. The modifications of the parent’s traits are passed off to an embryo during its lifetime. The foundation of this doctrine was based on the theory of <a href="/wiki/Inheritance_of_acquired_traits" class="mw-redirect" title="Inheritance of acquired traits">inheritance of acquired traits</a>. In direct opposition, the Doctrine of Preformation claimed that "like generates like" where the germ would evolve to yield offspring similar to the parents. The Preformationist view believed procreation was an act of revealing what had been created long before. However, this was disputed by the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Cell_theory" title="Cell theory">cell theory</a> in the 19th century, where the fundamental unit of life is the cell, and not some preformed parts of an organism. Various hereditary mechanisms, including <a href="/wiki/Blending_inheritance" title="Blending inheritance">blending inheritance</a> were also envisaged without being properly tested or quantified, and were later disputed. Nevertheless, people were able to develop domestic breeds of animals as well as crops through artificial selection. The inheritance of acquired traits also formed a part of early Lamarckian ideas on evolution.
</p><p>During the 18th century, Dutch microscopist <a href="/wiki/Antonie_van_Leeuwenhoek" title="Antonie van Leeuwenhoek">Antonie van Leeuwenhoek</a> (1632–1723) discovered "animalcules" in the sperm of humans and other animals.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Some scientists speculated they saw a "little man" (<a href="/wiki/Homunculus" title="Homunculus">homunculus</a>) inside each <a href="/wiki/Spermatozoon" title="Spermatozoon">sperm</a>. These scientists formed a school of thought known as the "spermists". They contended the only contributions of the female to the next generation were the womb in which the homunculus grew, and prenatal influences of the womb.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> An opposing school of thought, the ovists, believed that the future human was in the egg, and that sperm merely stimulated the growth of the egg. Ovists thought women carried eggs containing boy and girl children, and that the gender of the offspring was determined well before conception.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gregor_Mendel:_father_of_genetics">Gregor Mendel: father of genetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heredity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Gregor Mendel: father of genetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Independent_assortment_%26_segregation.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Independent_assortment_%26_segregation.svg/220px-Independent_assortment_%26_segregation.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="228" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Independent_assortment_%26_segregation.svg/330px-Independent_assortment_%26_segregation.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Independent_assortment_%26_segregation.svg/440px-Independent_assortment_%26_segregation.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="628" data-file-height="650" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Independent_assortment_%26_segregation.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Table showing how the genes exchange according to segregation or independent assortment during <a href="/wiki/Meiosis" title="Meiosis">meiosis</a> and how this translates into Mendel's laws</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gregor_Mendel" title="Gregor Mendel">Gregor Mendel</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Modern_synthesis_(20th_century)" title="Modern synthesis (20th century)">Modern synthesis (20th century)</a></div>
<p>The idea of particulate inheritance of genes can be attributed to the <a href="/wiki/Moravia" title="Moravia">Moravian</a><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> monk <a href="/wiki/Gregor_Mendel" title="Gregor Mendel">Gregor Mendel</a> who published his work on pea plants in 1865. However, his work was not widely known and was rediscovered in 1901. It was initially assumed that <a href="/wiki/Mendelian_inheritance" title="Mendelian inheritance">Mendelian inheritance</a> only accounted for large (qualitative) differences, such as those seen by Mendel in his pea plants&#160;– and the idea of additive effect of (quantitative) genes was not realised until <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Fisher" title="Ronald Fisher">R.A. Fisher</a>'s (1918) paper, "<a href="/wiki/The_Correlation_Between_Relatives_on_the_Supposition_of_Mendelian_Inheritance" class="mw-redirect" title="The Correlation Between Relatives on the Supposition of Mendelian Inheritance">The Correlation Between Relatives on the Supposition of Mendelian Inheritance</a>" Mendel's overall contribution gave scientists a useful overview that traits were inheritable. His pea plant demonstration became the foundation of the study of Mendelian Traits. These traits can be traced on a single locus.<sup id="cite_ref-Carlson_p._206_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson_p._206-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_development_of_genetics_and_heredity">Modern development of genetics and heredity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heredity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Modern development of genetics and heredity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/History_of_genetics" title="History of genetics">History of genetics</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_evolutionary_thought" title="History of evolutionary thought">History of evolutionary thought</a></div>
<p>In the 1930s, work by Fisher and others resulted in a combination of Mendelian and biometric schools into the <a href="/wiki/Modern_synthesis_(20th_century)" title="Modern synthesis (20th century)">modern evolutionary synthesis</a>. The modern synthesis bridged the gap between experimental geneticists and naturalists; and between both and palaeontologists, stating that:<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>All evolutionary phenomena can be explained in a way consistent with known genetic mechanisms and the observational evidence of naturalists.</li>
<li>Evolution is gradual: small genetic changes, recombination ordered by <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a>. Discontinuities amongst species (or other taxa) are explained as originating gradually through geographical separation and extinction (not saltation).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">Selection</a> is overwhelmingly the main mechanism of change; even slight advantages are important when continued. The object of selection is the <a href="/wiki/Phenotype" title="Phenotype">phenotype</a> in its surrounding environment. The role of <a href="/wiki/Genetic_drift" title="Genetic drift">genetic drift</a> is equivocal; though strongly supported initially by <a href="/wiki/Dobzhansky" class="mw-redirect" title="Dobzhansky">Dobzhansky</a>, it was downgraded later as results from ecological genetics were obtained.</li>
<li>The primacy of population thinking: the genetic diversity carried in natural populations is a key factor in evolution. The strength of natural selection in the wild was greater than expected; the effect of ecological factors such as niche occupation and the significance of barriers to gene flow are all important.</li></ol>
<p>The idea that <a href="/wiki/Speciation" title="Speciation">speciation</a> occurs after populations are reproductively isolated has been much debated.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> In plants, polyploidy must be included in any view of speciation. Formulations such as 'evolution consists primarily of changes in the <a href="/wiki/Allele_frequency" title="Allele frequency">frequencies of alleles</a> between one generation and another' were proposed rather later. The traditional view is that developmental biology ('<a href="/wiki/Evo-devo" class="mw-redirect" title="Evo-devo">evo-devo</a>') played little part in the synthesis, but an account of <a href="/wiki/Gavin_de_Beer" title="Gavin de Beer">Gavin de Beer</a>'s work by <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Jay_Gould" title="Stephen Jay Gould">Stephen Jay Gould</a> suggests he may be an exception.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Almost all aspects of the synthesis have been challenged at times, with varying degrees of success. There is no doubt, however, that the synthesis was a great landmark in evolutionary biology.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> It cleared up many confusions, and was directly responsible for stimulating a great deal of research in the post-<a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> era.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Trofim_Lysenko" title="Trofim Lysenko">Trofim Lysenko</a> however caused a backlash of what is now called <a href="/wiki/Lysenkoism" title="Lysenkoism">Lysenkoism</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> when he emphasised <a href="/wiki/Lamarckian" class="mw-redirect" title="Lamarckian">Lamarckian</a> ideas on the <a href="/wiki/Inheritance_of_acquired_traits" class="mw-redirect" title="Inheritance of acquired traits">inheritance of acquired traits</a>. This movement affected agricultural research and led to food shortages in the 1960s and seriously affected the USSR.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There is growing evidence that there is transgenerational inheritance of epigenetic changes in humans<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> and other animals.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dominant_and_recessive_alleles">Dominant and recessive alleles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heredity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Dominant and recessive alleles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Allele" title="Allele">allele</a> is said to be dominant if it is always expressed in the appearance of an organism (phenotype) provided that at least one copy of it is present. For example, in peas the allele for green pods, <i>G</i>, is dominant to that for yellow pods, <i>g</i>. Thus pea plants with the pair of alleles <i><b>either</b></i> <i>GG</i> (homozygote) <i><b>or</b></i> <i>Gg</i> (heterozygote) will have green pods. The allele for yellow pods is recessive. The effects of this allele are only seen when it is present in both chromosomes, <i>gg</i> (homozygote).
</p><p>The description of a mode of biological inheritance consists of three main categories:
</p>
<dl><dd><b>1. Number of involved <a href="/wiki/Locus_(genetics)" title="Locus (genetics)">loci</a></b>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Mendelian_inheritance" title="Mendelian inheritance">Monogenetic</a> (also called "simple") – one <a href="/wiki/Locus_(genetics)" title="Locus (genetics)">locus</a></li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oligogenetic&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Oligogenetic (page does not exist)">Oligogenetic</a> – few loci</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polygenesis_(genetics)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Polygenesis (genetics) (page does not exist)">Polygenetic</a> – many loci</li></ul></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><b>2. Involved <a href="/wiki/Chromosome" title="Chromosome">chromosomes</a></b></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><ul><li><a href="/wiki/Autosomal" class="mw-redirect" title="Autosomal">Autosomal</a> – loci are not situated on a <a href="/wiki/Sex_chromosome" title="Sex chromosome">sex chromosome</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gonosomal" class="mw-redirect" title="Gonosomal">Gonosomal</a> – loci are situated on a <a href="/wiki/Sex_chromosome" title="Sex chromosome">sex chromosome</a>
<ul><li>X-chromosomal – loci are situated on the <a href="/wiki/X_chromosome" title="X chromosome">X-chromosome</a> (the more common case)</li>
<li>Y-chromosomal – loci are situated on the <a href="/wiki/Y_chromosome" title="Y chromosome">Y-chromosome</a></li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial" class="mw-redirect" title="Mitochondrial">Mitochondrial</a> – loci are situated on the <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a></li></ul></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><b>3. Correlation <a href="/wiki/Genotype" title="Genotype">genotype</a>–<a href="/wiki/Phenotype" title="Phenotype">phenotype</a></b>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Dominance_relationship#Dominant_allele" class="mw-redirect" title="Dominance relationship">Dominant</a></li>
<li>Intermediate (also called "<a href="/wiki/Codominance" class="mw-redirect" title="Codominance">codominant</a>")</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Recessive" class="mw-redirect" title="Recessive">Recessive</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Overdominance" title="Overdominance">Overdominant</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Underdominance" title="Underdominance">Underdominant</a></li></ul></dd></dl>
<p>These three categories are part of every exact description of a mode of inheritance in the above order. In addition, more specifications may be added as follows:
</p>
<dl><dd><b>4. Coincidental and environmental interactions</b></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><ul><li><a href="/wiki/Penetrance" title="Penetrance">Penetrance</a>
<ul><li>Complete</li>
<li>Incomplete (percentual number)</li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Expressivity_(genetics)" title="Expressivity (genetics)">Expressivity</a>
<ul><li>Invariable</li>
<li>Variable</li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Heritability" title="Heritability">Heritability</a> (in polygenetic and sometimes also in oligogenetic modes of inheritance)</li>
<li>Maternal or paternal <a href="/wiki/Imprinting_(genetics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Imprinting (genetics)">imprinting</a> phenomena (also see <a href="/wiki/Epigenetics" title="Epigenetics">epigenetics</a>)</li></ul></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><b>5. Sex-linked interactions</b></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><ul><li>Sex-linked inheritance (<a href="/wiki/Gonosomal" class="mw-redirect" title="Gonosomal">gonosomal</a> loci)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sex-limited" class="mw-redirect" title="Sex-limited">Sex-limited</a> phenotype expression (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Cryptorchism" class="mw-redirect" title="Cryptorchism">cryptorchism</a>)</li>
<li>Inheritance through the maternal line (in case of <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a> loci)</li>
<li>Inheritance through the paternal line (in case of <a href="/wiki/Y_chromosome" title="Y chromosome">Y-chromosomal</a> loci)</li></ul></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><b>6. Locus–locus interactions</b>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Epistasis" title="Epistasis">Epistasis</a> with other loci (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Overdominance" title="Overdominance">overdominance</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gene_coupling&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gene coupling (page does not exist)">Gene coupling</a> with other loci (also see <a href="/wiki/Chromosomal_crossover" title="Chromosomal crossover">crossing over</a>)</li>
<li>Homozygotous <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lethal_factor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lethal factor (page does not exist)">lethal factors</a></li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semi-lethal_factor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Semi-lethal factor (page does not exist)">Semi-lethal factors</a></li></ul></dd></dl>
<p>Determination and description of a mode of inheritance is also achieved primarily through statistical analysis of pedigree data. In case the involved loci are known, methods of <a href="/wiki/Molecular_genetics" title="Molecular genetics">molecular genetics</a> can also be employed.
</p>
