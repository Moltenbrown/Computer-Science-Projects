<p>The <b>violin</b>, sometimes known as a <i><b><a href="/wiki/Fiddle" title="Fiddle">fiddle</a></b></i>, is a wooden <a href="/wiki/String_instrument" title="String instrument">string instrument</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Violin_family" title="Violin family">violin family</a>. Most violins have a hollow wooden body. It is the smallest and highest-pitched instrument in the family in regular use. Smaller violin-type instruments exist, including the <a href="/wiki/Violino_piccolo" title="Violino piccolo">violino piccolo</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kit_violin" title="Kit violin">kit violin</a>, but these are virtually unused. The violin typically has four <a href="/wiki/Strings_(music)" class="mw-redirect" title="Strings (music)">strings</a>, usually tuned in <a href="/wiki/Perfect_fifth" title="Perfect fifth">perfect fifths</a> with notes G3, D4, A4, E5, and is most commonly played by drawing a <a href="/wiki/Bow_(music)" title="Bow (music)">bow</a> across its strings, though it can also be played by plucking the strings with the fingers (<a href="/wiki/Pizzicato" title="Pizzicato">pizzicato</a>) and by striking the strings with the wooden side of the bow (<a href="/wiki/Col_legno" title="Col legno">col legno</a>). 
</p><p>Violins are important instruments in a wide variety of musical genres. They are most prominent in the <a href="/wiki/Western_classical_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Western classical music">Western classical tradition</a>, both in ensembles (from <a href="/wiki/Chamber_music" title="Chamber music">chamber music</a> to <a href="/wiki/Orchestra" title="Orchestra">orchestras</a>) and as solo instruments and in many varieties of <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk music</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bluegrass_music" title="Bluegrass music">bluegrass music</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Jazz_violin" title="Jazz violin">jazz</a>. <a href="/wiki/Electric_violin" title="Electric violin">Electric violins</a> with solid bodies and <a href="/wiki/Pickup_(music_technology)#Piezoelectric_pickups" title="Pickup (music technology)">piezoelectric pickups</a> are used in some forms of <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock music</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jazz_fusion" title="Jazz fusion">jazz fusion</a>, with the pickups plugged into <a href="/wiki/Instrument_amplifier" title="Instrument amplifier">instrument amplifiers</a> and speakers to produce sound. Further, the violin has come to be played in many non-Western music cultures, including <a href="/wiki/Indian_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian music">Indian music</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iranian_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Iranian music">Iranian music</a>. The name <i>fiddle</i> is often used regardless of the type of music played on it.
</p><p>The violin was first known in 16th-century <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, with some further modifications occurring in the 18th and 19th centuries to give the instrument a more powerful sound and projection. In Europe, it served as the basis for the development of other stringed instruments used in Western classical music, such as the viola.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-heron_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heron-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Violinists and collectors particularly prize the fine historical instruments made by the <a href="/wiki/Stradivari" class="mw-redirect" title="Stradivari">Stradivari</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guarneri" title="Guarneri">Guarneri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guadagnini" class="mw-redirect" title="Guadagnini">Guadagnini</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amati" title="Amati">Amati</a> families from the 16th to the 18th century in <a href="/wiki/Brescia" title="Brescia">Brescia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cremona" title="Cremona">Cremona</a> (Italy) and by <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Stainer" title="Jacob Stainer">Jacob Stainer</a> in <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>. According to their reputation, the quality of their sound has defied attempts to explain or equal it, though this belief is disputed.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT-201430407_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-201430407-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Great numbers of instruments have come from the hands of less famous makers, as well as still greater numbers of mass-produced commercial "trade violins" coming from cottage industries in places such as <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxony</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mirecourt" title="Mirecourt">Mirecourt</a>. Many of these trade instruments were formerly sold by <a href="/wiki/Sears" title="Sears">Sears, Roebuck and Co.</a> and other mass merchandisers.
</p><p>The parts of a violin are usually made from different types of <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>. Violins can be strung with <a href="/wiki/Catgut" title="Catgut">gut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nylon_6" title="Nylon 6">Perlon</a> or other synthetic, or steel strings. A person who makes or repairs violins is called a <a href="/wiki/Luthier" title="Luthier">luthier</a> or violinmaker. One who makes or repairs bows is called an <i>archetier</i> or <a href="/wiki/Bow_maker" title="Bow maker">bowmaker</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>The word "violin" was first used in English in the 1570s.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The word "violin" comes from "Italian <i>violino</i>, [a] diminutive of <a href="/wiki/Viola" title="Viola">viola</a>". The term "viola" comes from the expression for "tenor violin" in 1797, from Italian <i>viola</i>, from <a href="/wiki/Old_Proven%C3%A7al" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Provençal">Old Provençal</a> <i>viola</i>, [which came from] <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a> <i>vitula</i>" as a term which means "stringed instrument," perhaps [coming] from <a href="/wiki/Vitula_(goddess)" class="mw-redirect" title="Vitula (goddess)">Vitula</a>, Roman goddess of joy..., or from related Latin verb <i>vitulari</i>, "to exult, be joyful."<sup id="cite_ref-auto1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The related term "<i><a href="/wiki/Viola_da_gamba" class="mw-redirect" title="Viola da gamba">Viola da gamba</a></i>" means "bass viol" (1724) is from Italian, literally "a viola for the leg" (i.e. to hold between the legs)."<sup id="cite_ref-auto1_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  A violin is the "modern form of the smaller, medieval <a href="/wiki/Viola_da_braccio_(instrument)" title="Viola da braccio (instrument)">viola da braccio</a>." ("arm viola")<sup id="cite_ref-auto_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The violin is often called a fiddle, either when used in a folk music context, or even in Classical music scenes, as an informal nickname for the instrument.<sup id="cite_ref-ety_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ety-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The word "fiddle" was first used in English in the late 14th century.<sup id="cite_ref-ety_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ety-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The word "fiddle" comes from "fedele, fydyll, fidel, earlier fithele, from Old English <i>fiðele</i> "fiddle," which is related to <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> <i>fiðla</i>, Middle Dutch <i>vedele</i>, Dutch <i>vedel</i>, <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old High German</a> <i>fidula</i>, German <i>Fiedel</i>, "a fiddle;" all of uncertain origin." As to the origin of the word "fiddle", the "...usual suggestion, based on resemblance in sound and sense, is that it is from Medieval Latin <i>vitula</i>."<sup id="cite_ref-ety_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ety-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_violin" title="History of the violin">History of the violin</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gaudenzio_Ferrari_002.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Gaudenzio_Ferrari_002.jpg/220px-Gaudenzio_Ferrari_002.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="265" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Gaudenzio_Ferrari_002.jpg/330px-Gaudenzio_Ferrari_002.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Gaudenzio_Ferrari_002.jpg/440px-Gaudenzio_Ferrari_002.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1576" data-file-height="1896" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gaudenzio_Ferrari_002.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The cupola of Madonna dei Miracoli in <a href="/wiki/Saronno" title="Saronno">Saronno</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, with angels playing violin, viola and cello, dates from 1535 and is one of the earliest depictions of the violin family</div></div></div>
<p>The earliest stringed instruments were mostly plucked (for example, the Greek <a href="/wiki/Lyre" title="Lyre">lyre</a>). Two-stringed, bowed instruments, played upright and strung and <a href="/wiki/Bow_(music)" title="Bow (music)">bowed</a> with horsehair, may have originated in the nomadic <a href="/wiki/Equestrianism" title="Equestrianism">equestrian</a> cultures of Central Asia, in forms closely resembling the modern-day <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolian</a> <a href="/wiki/Morin_huur" class="mw-redirect" title="Morin huur">Morin huur</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakh</a> <a href="/wiki/Kobyz" title="Kobyz">Kobyz</a>. Similar and variant types were probably disseminated along East-West trading routes from Asia into the Middle East,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Grillet29_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grillet29-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The direct ancestor of all European bowed instruments is the <a href="/wiki/Arabic_music" title="Arabic music">Arabic</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Rebab" title="Rebab">rebab</a></i> (ربابة), which developed into the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_lyra" title="Byzantine lyra">Byzantine lyra</a> by the 9th century and later the European <a href="/wiki/Rebec" title="Rebec">rebec</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The first makers of violins probably borrowed from various developments of the Byzantine lyra. These included the <a href="/wiki/Vielle" title="Vielle">vielle</a> (also known as the <i>fidel</i> or <i>viuola</i>) and the <i><a href="/wiki/Lira_da_braccio" title="Lira da braccio">lira da braccio</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Grillet29_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grillet29-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
The violin in its present form emerged in early 16th-century <a href="/wiki/Northern_Italy" title="Northern Italy">northern Italy</a>. The earliest pictures of violins, albeit with three strings, are seen in northern Italy around 1530, at around the same time as the words "violino" and "vyollon" are seen in Italian and French documents. One of the earliest explicit descriptions of the instrument, including its tuning, is from the <i>Epitome musical</i> by <a href="/wiki/Jambe_de_Fer" class="mw-redirect" title="Jambe de Fer">Jambe de Fer</a>, published in <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">Lyon</a> in 1556.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> By this time, the violin had already begun to spread throughout <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.
</p><p>The violin proved very popular, both among street musicians and the nobility; the French king <a href="/wiki/Charles_IX_of_France" title="Charles IX of France">Charles IX</a> ordered Andrea Amati to construct 24 violins for him in 1560.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> One of these "noble" instruments, the <i>Charles IX</i>, is the oldest surviving violin. The finest Renaissance carved and decorated violin in the world is the <a href="/wiki/Gasparo_da_Sal%C3%B2" title="Gasparo da Salò">Gasparo da Salò</a> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr>1574) owned by <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II,_Archduke_of_Austria" title="Ferdinand II, Archduke of Austria">Ferdinand II, Archduke of Austria</a> and later, from 1841, by the Norwegian virtuoso <a href="/wiki/Ole_Bull" title="Ole Bull">Ole Bull</a>, who used it for forty years and thousands of concerts, for its very powerful and beautiful tone, similar to that of a Guarneri.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Messiah_Stradivarius" title="Messiah Stradivarius">"The Messiah"</a> or <i>"Le Messie"</i> (also known as the "Salabue") made by <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Stradivari" title="Antonio Stradivari">Antonio Stradivari</a> in 1716 remains pristine. It is now located in the <a href="/wiki/Ashmolean_Museum" title="Ashmolean Museum">Ashmolean Museum</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The most famous <a href="/wiki/Category:Violin_makers" title="Category:Violin makers">violin makers</a> (<a href="/wiki/Luthiers" class="mw-redirect" title="Luthiers">luthiers</a>) between the 16th century and the 18th century include:
</p>

<p>Significant changes occurred in the construction of the violin in the 18th century, particularly in the length and angle of the neck, as well as a heavier bass bar. The majority of old instruments have undergone these modifications, and hence are in a significantly different state than when they left the hands of their makers, doubtless with differences in sound and response.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> But these instruments in their present condition set the standard for perfection in violin craftsmanship and sound, and violin makers all over the world try to come as close to this ideal as possible.
</p><p>To this day, instruments from the so-called Golden Age of violin making, especially those made by Stradivari, Guarneri del Gesù and Montagnana are the most sought-after instruments by both collectors and performers.  The current record amount paid for a Stradivari violin is <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">£</a>9.8&#160;million (<a href="/wiki/US$" class="mw-redirect" title="US$">US$</a>15.9&#160;million), when the instrument known as the <a href="/wiki/Lady_Blunt_Stradivarius" title="Lady Blunt Stradivarius">Lady Blunt</a> was sold by <a href="/wiki/Tarisio_Auctions" title="Tarisio Auctions">Tarisio Auctions</a> in an online auction on June 20, 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction_and_mechanics">Construction and mechanics</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Violinconsruction3.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Violinconsruction3.JPG/400px-Violinconsruction3.JPG" decoding="async" width="400" height="353" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cc/Violinconsruction3.JPG 1.5x" data-file-width="591" data-file-height="521" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Violinconsruction3.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The construction of a violin</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Violin_construction_and_mechanics" title="Violin construction and mechanics">Violin construction and mechanics</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:106px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Violin.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/52/Violin.JPG" decoding="async" width="104" height="293" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="104" data-file-height="293" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Violin.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Violin and bow.</div></div></div>
<p>A violin generally consists of a <a href="/wiki/Spruce" title="Spruce">spruce</a> top (the <a href="/wiki/Sound_board_(music)" title="Sound board (music)">soundboard</a>, also known as the <i>top plate</i>, <i>table</i>, or <i>belly</i>), maple ribs and back, two endblocks, a <a href="/wiki/Neck_(music)" title="Neck (music)">neck</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Bridge_(instrument)" title="Bridge (instrument)">bridge</a>, a soundpost, four strings, and various fittings, optionally including a <a href="/wiki/Chinrest" title="Chinrest">chinrest</a>, which may attach directly over, or to the left of, the <a href="/wiki/Tailpiece" title="Tailpiece">tailpiece</a>. A distinctive feature of a violin body is its hourglass-like shape and the <a href="/wiki/Arch" title="Arch">arching</a> of its top and back. The hourglass shape comprises two upper bouts, two lower bouts, and two concave C-bouts at the <i>waist</i>, providing clearance for the <a href="/wiki/Bow_(music)" title="Bow (music)">bow</a>. The "voice" or sound of a violin depends on its shape, the wood it is made from, the graduation (the thickness profile) of both the top and back, the <a href="/wiki/Varnish#Violin" title="Varnish">varnish</a> that coats its outside surface and the skill of the luthier in doing all of these steps. The varnish and especially the wood continue to improve with age, making the fixed supply of old well-made violins built by famous luthiers much sought-after.
</p><p>The majority of glued joints in the instrument use animal <a href="/wiki/Hide_glue" class="mw-redirect" title="Hide glue">hide glue</a> rather than common white glue for a number of reasons. Hide glue is capable of making a thinner joint than most other glues, it is reversible (brittle enough to crack with carefully applied force, and removable with very warm water) when disassembly is needed, and since fresh hide glue sticks to old hide glue, more original wood can be preserved when repairing a joint. (More modern glues must be cleaned off entirely for the new joint to be sound, which generally involves scraping off some wood along with the old glue.) Weaker, diluted glue is usually used to fasten the top to the ribs, and the nut to the fingerboard, since common repairs involve removing these parts. The <a href="/wiki/Purfling" title="Purfling">purfling</a> running around the edge of the spruce top provides some protection against cracks originating at the edge. It also allows the top to flex more independently of the rib structure. Painted-on <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/faux" class="extiw" title="wikt:faux">faux</a> purfling on the top is usually a sign of an inferior instrument. The back and ribs are typically made of <a href="/wiki/Maple" title="Maple">maple</a>, most often with a matching striped <a href="/wiki/Figure_(wood)" title="Figure (wood)">figure</a>, referred to as <i>flame</i>, <i>fiddleback</i>, or <i>tiger stripe</i>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Neck_(music)" title="Neck (music)">neck</a> is usually maple with a flamed figure compatible with that of the ribs and back. It carries the <a href="/wiki/Fingerboard" title="Fingerboard">fingerboard</a>, typically made of ebony, but often some other wood stained or painted black on cheaper instruments. <a href="/wiki/Ebony" title="Ebony">Ebony</a> is the preferred material because of its hardness, beauty, and superior resistance to wear. Fingerboards are dressed to a particular transverse curve, and have a small lengthwise "scoop," or concavity, slightly more pronounced on the lower strings, especially when meant for gut or synthetic strings. Some old violins (and some made to appear old) have a grafted <a href="/wiki/Scroll_(music)" title="Scroll (music)">scroll</a>, evidenced by a glue joint between the pegbox and neck. Many authentic old instruments have had their necks reset to a slightly increased angle, and lengthened by about a centimeter. The neck graft allows the original scroll to be kept with a <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> violin when bringing its neck into conformance with modern standards.
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Soundpost_end.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Soundpost_end.jpg/120px-Soundpost_end.jpg" decoding="async" width="120" height="103" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Soundpost_end.jpg/180px-Soundpost_end.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Soundpost_end.jpg/240px-Soundpost_end.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1400" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Soundpost_end.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sound post seen through <a href="/wiki/F-hole" class="mw-redirect" title="F-hole">f-hole</a></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bridge_(instrument)" title="Bridge (instrument)">bridge</a> is a precisely cut piece of maple that forms the lower anchor point of the vibrating length of the strings and transmits the vibration of the strings to the body of the instrument. Its top curve holds the strings at the proper height from the fingerboard in an arc, allowing each to be sounded separately by the bow. The <a href="/wiki/Sound_post" title="Sound post">sound post</a>, or <i>soul post</i>, fits precisely inside the instrument between the back and top, at a carefully chosen spot near the treble foot of the bridge, which it helps support. It also influences the modes of vibration of the top and the back of the instrument.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Tailpiece" title="Tailpiece">tailpiece</a> anchors the strings to the lower bout of the violin by means of the tailgut, which loops around an ebony button called the tailpin (sometimes confusingly called the <i>endpin</i>, like the cello's spike), which fits into a tapered hole in the bottom block. Very often the E string will have a fine tuning lever worked by a small screw turned by the fingers. Fine tuners may also be applied to the other strings, especially on a student instrument, and are sometimes built into the tailpiece. The fine tuners enable the performer to make small changes in the pitch of a string. At the scroll end, the strings wind around the wooden <a href="/wiki/Tuning_peg" class="mw-redirect" title="Tuning peg">tuning pegs</a> in the pegbox. The tuning pegs are tapered and fit into holes in the peg box. The tuning pegs are held in place by the friction of wood on wood. Strings may be made of metal or less commonly gut or gut wrapped in metal. Strings usually have a colored <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a> wrapping at both ends, for identification of the string (e.g., G string, D string, A string or E string) and to provide friction against the pegs. The tapered pegs allow friction to be increased or decreased by the player applying appropriate pressure along the axis of the peg while turning it.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strings">Strings</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Violin_construction_and_mechanics#Strings" title="Violin construction and mechanics">strings section of Violin construction</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Strings_(music)" class="mw-redirect" title="Strings (music)">Strings</a> were first made of sheep gut (commonly known as <a href="/wiki/Catgut" title="Catgut">catgut</a>, which despite the name, did not come from cats), or simply gut, which was stretched, dried, and twisted. In the early years of the 20th century, strings were made of either gut or steel. Modern strings may be gut, solid <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>, stranded steel, or various synthetic materials such as perlon, wound with various metals, and sometimes plated with <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>. Most E strings are unwound, either plain or plated steel. Gut strings are not as common as they once were, but many performers use them to achieve a specific sound especially in <a href="/wiki/Historically_informed_performance" title="Historically informed performance">historically informed performance</a> of <a href="/wiki/Baroque_music" title="Baroque music">Baroque music</a>. Strings have a limited lifetime. Eventually, when oil, dirt, corrosion, and rosin accumulate, the mass of the string can become uneven along its length. Apart from obvious things, such as the winding of a string coming undone from wear, players generally change a string when it no longer plays "true" (with good intonation on the harmonics), losing the desired tone, brilliance and intonation. String longevity depends on string quality and playing intensity.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pitch_range">Pitch range</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Spectrumgstring.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Spectrumgstring.jpg/250px-Spectrumgstring.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Spectrumgstring.jpg/375px-Spectrumgstring.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Spectrumgstring.jpg/500px-Spectrumgstring.jpg 2x" data-file-width="699" data-file-height="477" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Spectrumgstring.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>3D <a href="/wiki/Spectrum" title="Spectrum">spectrum</a> diagram of the overtones of a violin G string (foreground). Note that the <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(music)" title="Pitch (music)">pitch</a> we hear is the peak around 200&#160;Hz.</div></div></div>
<p>A violin is tuned in fifths, in the notes G<sub>3</sub>, D<sub>4</sub>, A<sub>4</sub>, E<sub>5</sub>. The lowest note of a violin, tuned normally, is <a href="/wiki/Scientific_pitch_notation" title="Scientific pitch notation">G<sub>3</sub></a>, or G below <a href="/wiki/Middle_C" class="mw-redirect" title="Middle C">middle C</a>. (On rare occasions, the lowest string may be tuned down by as much as a fourth, to D<sub>3</sub>.) The highest note is less well defined: E<sub>7</sub>, the E two octaves above the open string (which is tuned to E<sub>5</sub>) may be considered a practical limit for orchestral violin parts,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> but it is often possible to play higher, depending on the length of the fingerboard and the skill of the violinist. Yet higher notes (up to C<sub>8</sub>) can be sounded by stopping the string, reaching the limit of the fingerboard, or by using <a href="/wiki/Artificial_harmonic" title="Artificial harmonic">artificial harmonics</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acoustics">Acoustics</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bowed_violin_string_in_slow_motion.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Bowed_violin_string_in_slow_motion.gif/220px-Bowed_violin_string_in_slow_motion.gif" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Bowed_violin_string_in_slow_motion.gif/330px-Bowed_violin_string_in_slow_motion.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Bowed_violin_string_in_slow_motion.gif/440px-Bowed_violin_string_in_slow_motion.gif 2x" data-file-width="480" data-file-height="360" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bowed_violin_string_in_slow_motion.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Helmholtz corner traveling back and forth along the string.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/String_instrument#Sound_production" title="String instrument">Sound production (string instruments)</a></div>
<p>The arched shape, the thickness of the wood, and its physical qualities govern the sound of a violin. Patterns of the <a href="/wiki/Node_(physics)" title="Node (physics)">node</a> made by sand or glitter sprinkled on the plates with the plate vibrated at certain frequencies, called <i><a href="/wiki/Ernst_Chladni" title="Ernst Chladni">Chladni</a> patterns</i>, are occasionally used by <a href="/wiki/Luthier" title="Luthier">luthiers</a> to verify their work before assembling the instrument.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sizes">Sizes</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:LembeyeLembeyon.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/LembeyeLembeyon.jpg/220px-LembeyeLembeyon.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/LembeyeLembeyon.jpg/330px-LembeyeLembeyon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/LembeyeLembeyon.jpg/440px-LembeyeLembeyon.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:LembeyeLembeyon.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fractional (&#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>16</sub></span>) and full size (&#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>4</sup>&#8260;<sub>4</sub></span>) violins</div></div></div>
<p>Apart from the standard, <i>full</i> (&#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>4</sup>&#8260;<sub>4</sub></span>) size, violins are also made in so-called <i>fractional</i> sizes of &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>7</sup>&#8260;<sub>8</sub></span>, &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>3</sup>&#8260;<sub>4</sub></span>, &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span>, &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>4</sub></span>, &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>8</sub></span>, &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>10</sub></span>, &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>16</sub></span>, &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>32</sub></span> and even &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>64</sub></span>. These smaller instruments are commonly used by young players, whose fingers are not long enough to reach the correct positions on full-sized instruments.
</p><p>While related in some sense to the dimensions of the instruments, the fractional sizes are not intended to be literal descriptions of relative proportions. For example, a &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>3</sup>&#8260;<sub>4</sub></span>-sized instrument is not three-quarters the length of a full size instrument. The body length (not including the neck) of a full-size, or &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>4</sup>&#8260;<sub>4</sub></span>, violin is 356&#160;mm (14.0&#160;in), smaller in some 17th-century models. A &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>3</sup>&#8260;<sub>4</sub></span> violin's body length is 335&#160;mm (13.2&#160;in), and a &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span> size is 310&#160;mm (12.2&#160;in). With the violin's closest family member, the viola, size is specified as body length in inches or centimeters rather than fractional sizes. A <a href="/wiki/Viola#Form" title="Viola">full-size viola</a> averages 40&#160;cm (16&#160;in). However, each individual adult will determine which size of viola to use.
</p><p>Occasionally, an adult with a small frame may use a so-called &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>7</sup>&#8260;<sub>8</sub></span> size violin instead of a full-size instrument.  Sometimes called a <i>lady's violin</i>, these instruments are slightly shorter than a full size violin, but tend to be high-quality instruments capable of producing a sound that is comparable to that of fine full size violins. 5 string violin sizes may differ from the normal 4 string.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mezzo_violin">Mezzo violin</span></h3>
<p>The instrument which corresponds to the violin in the <a href="/wiki/Violin_octet" title="Violin octet">violin octet</a> is the mezzo violin, tuned the same as a violin but with a slightly longer body. The strings of the mezzo violin are the same length as those of the standard violin.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tuning">Tuning</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Violin_peg_strings.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Violin_peg_strings.jpg/100px-Violin_peg_strings.jpg" decoding="async" width="100" height="78" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Violin_peg_strings.jpg/150px-Violin_peg_strings.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4a/Violin_peg_strings.jpg 2x" data-file-width="180" data-file-height="140" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Violin_peg_strings.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Scroll and pegbox, correctly strung</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Violin_-_open_strings_notes.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Violin_-_open_strings_notes.PNG/220px-Violin_-_open_strings_notes.PNG" decoding="async" width="220" height="79" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Violin_-_open_strings_notes.PNG/330px-Violin_-_open_strings_notes.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Violin_-_open_strings_notes.PNG/440px-Violin_-_open_strings_notes.PNG 2x" data-file-width="501" data-file-height="179" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Violin_-_open_strings_notes.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The pitches of open strings on a violin. The note names of the pitches are written in letter names below the stave and in their French <a href="/wiki/Solfege" class="mw-redirect" title="Solfege">solfege</a> equivalents above the stave. G=sol; D=re; A=la; E=mi <span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap;margin-right:.25em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Violin_open_strings.mid" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" decoding="async" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a></span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/05/Violin_open_strings.mid" class="internal" title="Violin open strings.mid">Play</a></span>&#160;<small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">(<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Media help"><span style="cursor:help;">help</span></a>·<a href="/wiki/File:Violin_open_strings.mid" title="File:Violin open strings.mid"><span style="cursor:help;">info</span></a>)</small></span></div></div></div>
<p>Violins are tuned by turning the <a href="/wiki/Tuning_peg" class="mw-redirect" title="Tuning peg">pegs</a> in the pegbox under the scroll, or by adjusting the <i>fine tuner</i> screws at the <a href="/wiki/Tailpiece" title="Tailpiece">tailpiece</a>. All violins have pegs; fine tuners (also called <i>fine adjusters</i>) are optional. Most fine tuners consist of a metal screw that moves a lever attached to the string end. They permit very small pitch adjustments much more easily than the pegs. By turning one clockwise, the pitch becomes sharper (as the string is under more tension) and turning one counterclockwise, the pitch becomes flatter (as the string is under less tension). Fine tuners on all four of the strings are very helpful when using those that have a steel core, and some players use them with synthetic strings as well. Since modern E strings are steel, a fine tuner is nearly always fitted for that string. Fine tuners are not used with gut strings, which are more <a href="/wiki/Young%27s_modulus" title="Young&#39;s modulus">elastic</a> than steel or synthetic-core strings and do not respond adequately to the very small movements of fine tuners.
</p><p>To tune a violin, the A string is first tuned to a <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(music)#Concert_pitch" title="Pitch (music)">standard</a> <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(music)" title="Pitch (music)">pitch</a> (usually 440&#160;<a href="/wiki/Hertz" title="Hertz">Hz</a>). (When accompanying or playing with a fixed-pitch instrument such as a piano or accordion, the violin tunes to it.) The other strings are then tuned against each other in intervals of <a href="/wiki/Perfect_fifth" title="Perfect fifth">perfect fifths</a> by bowing them in pairs. A minutely higher tuning is sometimes employed for solo playing to give the instrument a brighter sound; conversely, Baroque music is sometimes played using <a href="/wiki/Concert_pitch#History_of_pitch_standards_in_Western_music" title="Concert pitch">lower tunings</a> to make the violin's sound more gentle. After tuning, the instrument's bridge may be examined to ensure that it is standing straight and centered between the inner nicks of the <a href="/wiki/F-hole" class="mw-redirect" title="F-hole">f-holes</a>; a crooked bridge may significantly affect the sound of an otherwise well-made violin. After extensive playing, the holes into which the tuning pegs are inserted can become worn, which can lead the peg to slip under tension. This can lead to the pitch of the string dropping, or if the peg becomes completely loose, to the string completely losing tension. A violin in which the tuning pegs are slipping needs to be repaired by a luthier or violin repairperson. <a href="/wiki/Peg_dope" class="mw-redirect" title="Peg dope">Peg dope</a> or peg compound, used regularly, can delay the onset of such wear, while allowing the pegs to turn smoothly.
</p><p>The tuning G–D–A–E is used for most violin music, both in Classical music, jazz and <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk music</a>. Other tunings are occasionally employed; the G string, for example, can be tuned up to A. The use of nonstandard tunings in <a href="/wiki/European_classical_music" class="mw-redirect" title="European classical music">classical music</a> is known as <i><a href="/wiki/Scordatura" title="Scordatura">scordatura</a></i>; in some folk styles, it is called <i><a href="/wiki/Cross_tuning" title="Cross tuning">cross tuning</a></i>. One famous example of scordatura in classical music is <a href="/wiki/Camille_Saint-Sa%C3%ABns" title="Camille Saint-Saëns">Camille Saint-Saëns</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Danse_macabre_(Saint-Sa%C3%ABns)" title="Danse macabre (Saint-Saëns)">Danse Macabre</a></i>, where the solo violin's E string is tuned down to E<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> to impart an eerie dissonance to the composition. Other examples are the third movement of <i>Contrasts</i>, by <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9la_Bart%C3%B3k" title="Béla Bartók">Béla Bartók</a>, where the E string is tuned down to E<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> and the G tuned to a G<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-sharp">&#x266f;</span></span>, and the <a href="/wiki/Mystery_Sonatas" class="mw-redirect" title="Mystery Sonatas">Mystery Sonatas</a> by <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Ignaz_Franz_Biber" title="Heinrich Ignaz Franz Biber">Biber</a>, in which each movement has different scordatura tuning.
</p><p>In Indian classical music and Indian light music, the violin is likely to be tuned to D<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-sharp">&#x266f;</span></span>–A<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-sharp">&#x266f;</span></span>–D<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-sharp">&#x266f;</span></span>–A<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-sharp">&#x266f;</span></span> in the South Indian style. As there is no concept of absolute pitch in Indian classical music, any convenient tuning maintaining these relative pitch intervals between the strings can be used. Another prevalent tuning with these intervals is B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span>–F–B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span>–F, which corresponds to Sa–Pa–Sa–Pa in the Indian <a href="/wiki/Carnatic_music" title="Carnatic music">carnatic</a> classical music style. In the North Indian <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_classical_music" title="Hindustani classical music">Hindustani</a> style, the tuning is usually Pa-Sa-Pa-Sa instead of Sa–Pa–Sa–Pa. This could correspond to F–B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span>–F–B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span>, for instance. In Iranian classical music and Iranian light music, the violin ls different tunings in any <a href="/wiki/Persian_traditional_music" title="Persian traditional music">Dastgah</a>, the violin is likely to be tuned (E–A–E–A) in <a href="/wiki/Iranian_traditional_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Iranian traditional music">Dastgah-h Esfahan</a> or in  <a href="/wiki/Musical_composition" title="Musical composition">Dastgāh-e Šur</a> is (E–A–D–E) and (E–A–E–E), in <a href="/wiki/Persian_traditional_music" title="Persian traditional music">Dastgāh-e Māhur</a> is (E–A–D–A). In Arabic classical music, the A and E strings are lowered by a <a href="/wiki/Whole_step" class="mw-redirect" title="Whole step">whole step</a> i.e. G–D–G–D. This is to ease playing Arabic <a href="/wiki/Arabian_maqam" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabian maqam">maqams</a>, especially those containing <a href="/wiki/Quarter_tone" title="Quarter tone">quarter tones</a>.
</p><p>While most violins have four strings, there are violins with additional strings. Some have as many as seven strings. Seven strings is generally thought to be the maximum number of strings that can be put on a bowed string instrument, because with more than seven strings, it would be impossible to play a particular inner string individually with the bow. Instruments with seven strings are very rare. The extra strings on such violins typically are lower in pitch than the G-string; these strings are usually tuned to C, F, and B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span>. If the instrument's playing length, or string length from nut to bridge, is equal to that of an ordinary full-scale violin; i.e., a bit less than 13 inches (33&#160;cm), then it may be properly termed a violin. Some such instruments are somewhat longer and should be regarded as violas. Violins with five strings or more are typically used in jazz or folk music. Some custom-made instruments have extra strings which are not bowed, but which sound sympathetically, due to the vibrations of the bowed strings.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bows">Bows</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Bow_(music)" title="Bow (music)">Bow (music)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Violin_construction_and_mechanics#Bow" title="Violin construction and mechanics">Violin construction (bow)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Punte_archi_storici_da_violino.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Punte_archi_storici_da_violino.jpg/220px-Punte_archi_storici_da_violino.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Punte_archi_storici_da_violino.jpg/330px-Punte_archi_storici_da_violino.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Punte_archi_storici_da_violino.jpg/440px-Punte_archi_storici_da_violino.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3296" data-file-height="2472" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Punte_archi_storici_da_violino.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Heads of three violin bows: (upper) transitional (F. Tourte), swan-bill head of a long 18th-century model, pike-head of a 17th-century model</div></div></div>
<p>A violin is usually played using a <a href="/wiki/Bow_(music)" title="Bow (music)">bow</a> consisting of a stick with a ribbon of horsehair strung between the tip and frog (or nut, or heel) at opposite ends. A typical violin bow may be 75&#160;cm (30&#160;in) overall, and weigh about 60&#160;g (2.1&#160;oz). Viola bows may be about 5&#160;mm (0.20&#160;in) shorter and 10&#160;g (0.35&#160;oz) heavier. At the frog end, a screw adjuster tightens or loosens the hair. Just forward of the frog, a <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a> thumb cushion, called the grip, and winding protect the stick and provide a strong grip for the player's hand. Traditional windings are of wire (often silver or plated silver), silk, or <a href="/wiki/Baleen" title="Baleen">baleen</a> ("whalebone", now substituted by alternating strips of tan and black plastic.) Some fiberglass student bows employ a plastic sleeve as grip and winding.
</p><p>Bow hair traditionally comes from the tail of a <a href="/wiki/Grey_(horse)" class="mw-redirect" title="Grey (horse)">grey</a> male horse (which has predominantly white hair). Some cheaper bows use synthetic fiber. Solid rosin is rubbed onto the hair, to render it slightly sticky; when the bow is drawn across a string, the friction between them makes the string vibrate. Traditional materials for the more costly bow sticks include <a href="/wiki/Snakewood" title="Snakewood">snakewood</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Brazilwood" class="mw-redirect" title="Brazilwood">brazilwood</a> (which is also known as Pernambuco wood). Some recent bow design innovations use <a href="/wiki/Carbon_fiber" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon fiber">carbon fiber</a> (CodaBows) for the stick, at all levels of craftsmanship. Inexpensive bows for students are made of less costly timbers, or from fiberglass (Glasser).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Playing">Playing</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Playing_the_violin" class="mw-redirect" title="Playing the violin">Playing the violin</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Posture">Posture</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Man_Playing_Violin_on_Bench_at_Park.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Man_Playing_Violin_on_Bench_at_Park.jpg/170px-Man_Playing_Violin_on_Bench_at_Park.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="255" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Man_Playing_Violin_on_Bench_at_Park.jpg/255px-Man_Playing_Violin_on_Bench_at_Park.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Man_Playing_Violin_on_Bench_at_Park.jpg/340px-Man_Playing_Violin_on_Bench_at_Park.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1155" data-file-height="1732" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Man_Playing_Violin_on_Bench_at_Park.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Man playing violin on a park bench.</div></div></div>
<p>The violin is played either seated or standing up. Solo players (whether playing alone, with a piano or with an orchestra) play mostly standing up (unless prevented by a physical disability such as in the case of <a href="/wiki/Itzhak_Perlman" title="Itzhak Perlman">Itzhak Perlman</a>), while in the orchestra and in chamber music it is usually played seated. In the 2000s and 2010s, some orchestras performing <a href="/wiki/Baroque_music" title="Baroque music">Baroque music</a> (such as the <a href="/wiki/Freiburger_Barockorchester" title="Freiburger Barockorchester">Freiburg Baroque Orchestra</a>) have had all of their violins and violas, solo and ensemble, perform standing up.
</p><p>The standard way of holding the violin is with the left side of the jaw resting on the <a href="/wiki/Chinrest" title="Chinrest">chinrest</a> of the violin, and supported by the left shoulder, often assisted by a <a href="/wiki/Shoulder_rest" title="Shoulder rest">shoulder rest</a> (or a sponge and an elastic band for younger players who struggle with shoulder rests). The jaw and the shoulder must hold the violin firmly enough to allow it to remain stable when the left hand goes from a high position (a high pitched note far up on the fingerboard) to a low one (nearer to the pegbox). In the Indian posture, the stability of the violin is guaranteed by its scroll resting on the side of the foot.
</p><p>While teachers point out the vital importance of good posture both for the sake of the quality of the playing and to reduce the chance of <a href="/wiki/Repetitive_strain_injury" title="Repetitive strain injury">repetitive strain injury</a>, advice as to what good posture is and how to achieve it differs in details. However all insist on the importance of a natural relaxed position without tension or rigidity. Things which are almost universally recommended is keeping the left wrist straight (or very nearly so) to allow the fingers of the left hand to move freely and to reduce the chance of injury and keeping either shoulder in a natural relaxed position and avoiding raising either of them in an exaggerated manner. This, like any other unwarranted tension, would limit freedom of motion, and increase the risk of injury.
</p><p>Hunching can hamper good playing, because it throws the body off balance and makes the shoulders rise. Another sign that comes from unhealthy tension is pain in the left hand, which indicates too much pressure when holding the violin.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Left_hand_and_pitch_production">Left hand and pitch production</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Violin_first_position_fingering_chart.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Violin_first_position_fingering_chart.svg/300px-Violin_first_position_fingering_chart.svg.png" decoding="async" width="300" height="343" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Violin_first_position_fingering_chart.svg/450px-Violin_first_position_fingering_chart.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Violin_first_position_fingering_chart.svg/600px-Violin_first_position_fingering_chart.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1400" data-file-height="1600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Violin_first_position_fingering_chart.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>First position fingerings. Note that this diagram only shows the "first position" notes. There are notes of higher pitch beyond those indicated.</div></div></div>
<p>The left hand determines the sounding length of the string, and thus the pitch of the string, by "stopping" it (pressing it) against the fingerboard with the fingertips, producing different pitches. As the violin has no <a href="/wiki/Frets" class="mw-redirect" title="Frets">frets</a> to stop the strings, as is usual with the <a href="/wiki/Guitar" title="Guitar">guitar</a>, the player must know exactly where to place the fingers on the strings to play with good <a href="/wiki/Intonation_(music)" title="Intonation (music)">intonation</a> (tuning). Beginning violinists play open strings and the lowest position, nearest to the nut. Students often start with relatively easy keys, such as A Major and G major. Students are taught scales and simple melodies. Through practice of scales and arpeggios and <a href="/wiki/Ear_training" title="Ear training">ear training</a>, the violinist's left hand eventually "finds" the notes intuitively by <a href="/wiki/Proprioception" title="Proprioception">muscle memory</a>.
</p><p>Beginners sometimes rely on <a href="/wiki/Adhesive_tape" title="Adhesive tape">tapes</a> placed on the fingerboard for proper left hand finger placement, but usually abandon the tapes quickly as they advance. Another commonly used marking technique uses dots of <a href="/wiki/Correction_fluid" title="Correction fluid">white-out</a> on the fingerboard, which wear off in a few weeks of regular practice. This practice, unfortunately, is used sometimes in lieu of adequate ear-training, guiding the placement of fingers by eye and not by ear. Especially in the early stages of learning to play, the so-called "ringing tones" are useful. There are nine such notes in first position, where a <a href="/wiki/Stopped_note" title="Stopped note">stopped note</a> sounds a unison or octave with another (open) string, causing it to <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_resonance" title="Acoustic resonance">resonate sympathetically</a>. Students often use these ringing tones to check the intonation of the stopped note by seeing if it is harmonious with the open string. For example, when playing the stopped pitch "A" on the G string, the violinist could play the open D string at the same time, to check the intonation of the stopped "A". If the "A" is in tune, the "A" and the open D string should produce a harmonious perfect fourth.
</p><p>Violins are tuned in perfect fifths, like all the orchestral strings (violin, viola, cello) except the double bass, which is tuned in perfect fourths. Each subsequent note is stopped at a pitch the player perceives as the most harmonious, "when unaccompanied, [a violinist] does not play consistently in either the tempered or the natural [just] scale, but tends on the whole to conform with the <a href="/wiki/Pythagorean_tuning" title="Pythagorean tuning">Pythagorean scale</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> When violinists are playing in a <a href="/wiki/String_quartet" title="String quartet">string quartet</a> or a <a href="/wiki/String_orchestra" title="String orchestra">string orchestra</a>, the strings typically "sweeten" their tuning to suit the key they are playing in. When playing with an instrument tuned to <a href="/wiki/Equal_temperament" title="Equal temperament">equal temperament</a>, such as a <a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a>, skilled violinists adjust their tuning to match the equal temperament of the piano to avoid <a href="/wiki/Consonance_and_dissonance#Dissonance" title="Consonance and dissonance">discordant</a> notes.
</p><p>The fingers are conventionally numbered 1 (index) through 4 (little finger) in <a href="/wiki/Music_notation" class="mw-redirect" title="Music notation">music notation</a>, such as sheet music and etude books.  Especially in instructional editions of violin music, numbers over the notes may indicate which finger to use, with <i>0</i> or <i>O</i> indicating an open string. The chart to the right shows the arrangement of notes reachable in first position. Not shown on this chart is the way the spacing between note positions becomes closer as the fingers move up (in pitch) from the nut.  The bars at the sides of the chart represent the usual possibilities for beginners' tape placements, at 1st, high 2nd, 3rd, and 4th fingers. 
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Positions">Positions</span></h4>
<p>The placement of the left hand on the fingerboard is characterized by "positions".  First position, where most beginners start (although some methods start in third position), is the most commonly used position in string music. Music composed for beginning <a href="/wiki/Youth_orchestra" title="Youth orchestra">youth orchestras</a> is often mostly in first position. The lowest note available in this position in standard tuning is an open G; the highest note in first position is played with the fourth finger on the E-string, sounding a B. Moving the hand up the neck, so the first finger takes the place of the second finger, brings the player into <i>second position</i>. Letting the first finger take the first-position place of the third finger brings the player to <i>third position</i>, and so on. A change of positions, with its associated movement of the hand, is referred to as a <i>shift,</i> and effective shifting maintaining accurate intonation and a smooth legato (connected) sound is a key element of technique at all levels. Often a "guide finger" is used; the last finger to play a note in the old position continuously lightly touches the string during the course of the shift to end up on its correct place in the new position. In elementary shifting exercises the "guide finger" is often voiced while it glides up and down the string, so the player can establish by ear whether they are landing in the correct place, however outside of these exercises it should rarely be audible (unless the performer is consciously applying a <a href="/wiki/Portamento" title="Portamento">portamento</a> effect for expressive reasons).
</p><p>In the course of a shift in low positions, the thumb of the left hand moves up or down the neck of the instrument so as to remain in the same position relative to the fingers (though the movement of the thumb may occur slightly before, or slightly after, the movement of the fingers). In such positions, the thumb is often thought of as an 'anchor' whose location defines what position the player is in. In very high positions, the thumb is unable to move with the fingers as the body of the instrument gets in the way. Instead, the thumb works around the neck of the instrument to sit at the point at which the neck meets the right bout of the body, and remains there while the fingers move between the high positions.
</p><p>A note played outside of the normal compass of a position, without any shift, is referred to as an <i>extension</i>. For instance, in third position on the A string, the hand naturally sits with the first finger on D<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-natural">&#x266e;</span></span> and the fourth on either G<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-natural">&#x266e;</span></span> or G<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-sharp">&#x266f;</span></span>. Stretching the first finger back down to a C<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-sharp">&#x266f;</span></span>, or the fourth finger up to an A<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-natural">&#x266e;</span></span>, forms an extension. Extensions are commonly used where one or two notes are slightly out of an otherwise solid position, and give the benefit of being less intrusive than a shift or string crossing. The lowest position on the violin is referred to as "half position". In this position the first finger is on a "low first position" note, e.g. B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> on the A string, and the fourth finger is in a downward extension from its regular position, e.g. D<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-natural">&#x266e;</span></span> on the A string, with the other two fingers placed in between as required. As the position of the thumb is typically the same in "half position" as in first position, it is better thought of as a backwards extension of the whole hand than as a genuine position.
</p><p>The upper limit of the violin's range is largely determined by the skill of the player, who may easily play more than two octaves on a single string, and four octaves on the instrument as a whole. Position names are mostly used for the lower positions and in method books and etudes; for this reason, it is uncommon to hear references to anything higher than seventh position. The highest position, practically speaking, is 13th position. Very high positions are a particular technical challenge, for two reasons. Firstly, the difference in location of different notes becomes much narrower in high positions, making the notes more challenging to locate and in some cases to distinguish by ear. Secondly, the much shorter sounding length of the string in very high positions is a challenge for the right arm and bow in sounding the instrument effectively. The finer (and more expensive) an instrument, the better able it is to sustain good tone right to the top of the fingerboard, at the highest pitches on the E string.
</p><p>All notes (except those below the open D) can be played on more than one string. This is a standard design feature of stringed instruments; however, it differs from the piano, which has only one location for each of its 88 notes. For instance, the note of open A on the violin can be played as the open A, or on the D string (in first to fourth positions) or even on the G string (very high up in sixth to ninth positions). Each string has a different <a href="/wiki/Timbre" title="Timbre">tone quality</a>, because of the different weights (thicknesses) of the strings and because of the resonances of other open strings. For instance, the G string is often regarded as having a very full, sonorous sound which is particularly appropriate to late Romantic music. This is often indicated in the music by the marking, for example, <i>sul G</i> or <i>IV</i> (a Roman numeral indicating to play on the fourth string; by convention, the strings are numbered from thinnest, highest pitch (I) to the lowest pitch (IV). Even without an explicit instructions in the score, an advanced violinist will use her/his discretion and artistic sensibility to select which string to play specific notes or passages.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Open_strings">Open strings</span></h4>
<p>If a string is bowed or plucked without any finger stopping it, it is said to be an  <i>open string</i>. This gives a different sound from a stopped string, since the string vibrates more freely at the nut than under a finger. Further, it is impossible to use vibrato fully on an open string (though a partial effect can be achieved by stopping a note an octave up on an adjacent string and vibrating that, which introduces an element of vibrato into the overtones). In the classical tradition, violinists will often use a string crossing or shift of position to allow them to avoid the change of timbre introduced by an open string. This is particularly true for the open E which is often regarded as having a harsh sound. However, there are also situations where an open string may be specifically chosen for artistic effect (particularly in modern music), in classical music which is imitating the drone of an organ (J. S. Bach, in his Partita in E for solo violin, achieved this), fiddling (e.g., <i><a href="/wiki/Hoedown" title="Hoedown">Hoedown</a></i>) or where taking steps to avoid the open string is musically inappropriate (for instance in Baroque music where shifting position was less common). In quick passages of scales or arpeggios an open E string may simply be used for convenience if the note does not have time to ring and develop a harsh timbre. In folk music, fiddling and other <a href="/wiki/Traditional_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Traditional music">traditional music</a> genres, open strings are commonly used for their resonant timbre.
</p><p>Playing an open string simultaneously with a stopped note on an adjacent string produces a <a href="/wiki/Bagpipes" title="Bagpipes">bagpipe</a>-like drone, often used by composers in imitation of <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk music</a>. Sometimes the two notes are identical (for instance, playing a fingered A on the D string against the open A string), giving a ringing sort of "fiddling" sound. Playing an open string simultaneously with an identical stopped note can also be called for when more volume is required, especially in orchestral playing. Some classical violin parts have notes for which the composer requests the violinist to play an open string, because of the specific sonority created by an open string.
</p>
<h4><span id="Double_stops.2C_triple_stops.2C_chords_and_drones"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Double_stops,_triple_stops,_chords_and_drones">Double stops, triple stops, chords and drones</span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Double_stop" title="Double stop">Double stopping</a> is when two separate strings are stopped by the fingers, and bowed simultaneously, producing a sixth, third, fifth, etc. harmony. Double-stops can be indicated in any position, though the widest interval that can be double-stopped naturally in one position is an octave (with the first finger on the lower string and the fourth finger on the higher string). Nonetheless, intervals of tenths or even more are sometimes required to be double-stopped in advanced playing, resulting in a very stretched left-hand position with both fingers extended. The term "double stop" is often used to encompass sounding an open string alongside a fingered note.
</p><p>Where three or four more simultaneous notes are written, the violinist will typically "split" the chord, choosing the lower one or two notes to play first before promptly continuing onto the upper one or two notes. A "triple stop" with three simultaneous notes is possible in some circumstances. The bow will not naturally strike three strings at once, but if there is sufficient pressure in the bowstroke the middle string can be bent down temporarily so all three can sound. This is accomplished with a heavy stroke, typically quite near the heel, and quite loud. Double stops in <a href="/wiki/Orchestra" title="Orchestra">orchestra</a> are divided between the players if they are not at ease with them, with half of the musicians playing the lower note and the other half playing the higher note. Sometimes, the composer will write "divisi" when he intends the two lines to be played separately. Playing double stops is common when the violins do accompaniment and another instrument plays the melody. 
</p><p>In some Baroque music neither split-chord nor triple-stop is appropriate and violinists will arpeggiate all chords (and even what appear to be regular double stops), playing all notes individually as if they had been written as a slurred figure. In some musical styles, a sustained open string <a href="/wiki/Drone_(music)" title="Drone (music)">drone</a> can be played during a passage mainly written on an adjacent string, to provide a basic accompaniment. This is more often seen in folk traditions than in classical music. However, in Baroque violins, triple-stopping came more naturally due to the bridge being less curved, so it could be played that way.
</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Vibrato" title="Vibrato">Vibrato</a> is a technique of the left hand and arm in which the pitch of a note varies subtly in a pulsating rhythm. While various parts of the hand or arm may be involved in the motion, the end result is a movement of the fingertip bringing about a slight change in vibrating string length, which causes an undulation in pitch. Some violinists oscillate backwards, or lower in pitch from the actual note when using vibrato, since it is believed that perception favors the highest pitch in a varying sound.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Vibrato does little, if anything, to disguise an out-of-tune note; in other words, misapplied vibrato is a poor substitute for good intonation. Scales and other exercises meant to work on intonation are typically played without vibrato to make the work easier and more effective. Music students are often taught that unless otherwise marked in music, vibrato is assumed. However, it has to be noted that this is only a trend; there is nothing on the sheet music that compels violinists to add vibrato. This can be an obstacle to a classically trained violinist wishing to play in a style that uses little or no vibrato at all, such as baroque music played in period style and many traditional fiddling styles.
</p><p>Vibrato can be produced by a proper combination of finger, wrist and arm motions. One method, called <i>hand vibrato</i>, involves rocking the hand back at the wrist to achieve oscillation, while another method, <i>arm vibrato</i>, modulates the pitch by rocking at the elbow. A combination of these techniques allows a player to produce a large variety of tonal effects. The "when" and "what for" and "how much" of violin <a href="/wiki/Vibrato" title="Vibrato">vibrato</a> are artistic matters of style and taste. Different teachers, music schools and styles of music favour different vibrato styles. For example, overdone vibrato may become distracting. In acoustic terms, the interest that vibrato adds to the sound has to do with the way that the overtone mix<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> (or tone color, or timbre) and the directional pattern of sound projection change with changes in pitch. By "pointing" the sound at different parts of the room<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> in a rhythmic way, vibrato adds a "shimmer" or "liveliness" to the sound of a well-made violin. Vibrato is, in a large part, left to the discretion of the violinist.  Different types of vibrato will bring different moods to the piece, and the varying degrees and styles of vibrato are often characteristics that stand out in well-known violinists.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Vibrato_trill">Vibrato trill</span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Vibrato" title="Vibrato">Vibrato</a> can also be used for a fast trill. A trill initiated from just hammering the finger up and down on the fingerboard will create a harsher quality than with a vibrato trill. For example, if trilling on the first finger, the second finger is placed very slightly off the string and vibrato is implemented. The second finger will lightly touch the string above the first finger causing the pitch to change. This has a softer quality and many think it is nicer-sounding than a hammered trill. Note: this trill technique only works well for semi-tonal trills, it is far more difficult to vibrato trill for an interval of a tone or more.
</p>

<p>Lightly touching the string with a fingertip at a harmonic <a href="/wiki/Node_(physics)" title="Node (physics)">node</a>, but without fully pressing the string, and then plucking or bowing the string, creates <a href="/wiki/Harmonic" title="Harmonic">harmonics</a>. Instead of the normal tone, a higher pitched note sounds.  Each node is at an integer division of the string, for example half-way or one-third along the length of the string. A responsive instrument will sound numerous possible harmonic nodes along the length of the string. Harmonics are marked in music either with a little circle above the note that determines the pitch of the harmonic, or by diamond-shaped note heads. There are two types of harmonics: <b>natural harmonics</b> and <b><a href="/wiki/Artificial_harmonic" title="Artificial harmonic">artificial harmonics</a></b> (also known as <i>false harmonics</i>).
</p><p>Natural harmonics are played on an open string. The pitch of the open string when it is plucked or bowed is called the fundamental frequency.  Harmonics are also called <i>overtones</i> or <i>partials</i>. They occur at whole-number multiples of the fundamental, which is called the first harmonic.  The second harmonic is the first <a href="/wiki/Overtone" title="Overtone">overtone</a> (the octave above the open string), the third harmonic is the second overtone, and so on. The second harmonic is in the middle of the string and sounds an octave higher than the string's pitch.  The third harmonic breaks the string into thirds and sounds an octave and a fifth above the fundamental, and the fourth harmonic breaks the string into quarters sounding two octaves above the first. The sound of the second harmonic is the clearest of them all, because it is a common <a href="/wiki/Node_(physics)" title="Node (physics)">node</a> with all the succeeding even-numbered harmonics (4th, 6th, etc.). The third and succeeding odd-numbered harmonics are harder to play because they break the string into an odd number of vibrating parts and do not share as many nodes with other harmonics.
</p><p>Artificial harmonics are more difficult to produce than natural harmonics, as they involve both stopping the string and playing a harmonic on the stopped note. Using the <i>octave frame</i> (the normal distance between the first and fourth fingers in any given position) with the fourth finger just touching the string a <a href="/wiki/Interval_(music)" title="Interval (music)">fourth</a> higher than the stopped note produces the fourth harmonic, two octaves above the stopped note. Finger placement and pressure, as well as bow speed, pressure, and sounding point are all essential in getting the desired harmonic to sound. And to add to the challenge, in passages with different notes played as false harmonics, the distance between stopping finger and harmonic finger must constantly change, since the spacing between notes changes along the length of the string.
</p><p>The <i>harmonic finger</i> can also touch at a <a href="/wiki/Interval_(music)" title="Interval (music)">major third</a> above the pressed note (the fifth harmonic), or a <a href="/wiki/Interval_(music)" title="Interval (music)">fifth</a> higher (a third harmonic).  These harmonics are less commonly used; in the case of the major third, both the stopped note and touched note must be played slightly sharp otherwise the harmonic does not speak as readily.  In the case of the fifth, the stretch is greater than is comfortable for many violinists. In the general repertoire fractions smaller than a sixth are not used.  However, divisions up to an eighth are sometimes used and, given a good instrument and a skilled player, divisions as small as a twelfth are possible. There are a few books dedicated solely to the study of violin harmonics. Two comprehensive works are Henryk Heller's seven-volume <i>Theory of Harmonics</i>, published by <a href="/wiki/N._Simrock" title="N. Simrock">Simrock</a> in 1928, and Michelangelo Abbado's five-volume <i>Tecnica dei suoni armonici</i> published by Ricordi in 1934.
</p><p>Elaborate passages in artificial harmonics can be found in virtuoso violin literature, especially of the 19th and early 20th centuries. Two notable examples of this are an entire section of <a href="/wiki/Vittorio_Monti" title="Vittorio Monti">Vittorio Monti</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Cs%C3%A1rd%C3%A1s_(Monti)" title="Csárdás (Monti)">Csárdás</a></i> and a passage towards the middle of the third movement of <a href="/wiki/Pyotr_Ilyich_Tchaikovsky" title="Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky">Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Violin_Concerto" class="mw-redirect" title="Violin Concerto">Violin Concerto</a>. A section of the third movement of <a href="/wiki/Violin_Concerto_No._1_(Paganini)" title="Violin Concerto No. 1 (Paganini)">Violin Concerto No. 1 (Paganini)</a> consists of <a href="/wiki/Double-stop" class="mw-redirect" title="Double-stop">double-stopped</a> thirds in harmonics.
</p><p>When strings are worn, dirty and old, the harmonics may no longer be accurate in pitch. For this reason, violinists change their strings regularly.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Right_hand_and_tone_color">Right hand and tone color</span></h3>
<p>The strings may be sounded by drawing the hair of the bow held by the right hand across them (<i>arco</i>) or by plucking them (<i><a href="/wiki/Pizzicato" title="Pizzicato">pizzicato</a></i>) most often with the right hand. In some cases, the violinist will pluck strings with the left hand. This is done to facilitate transitions from pizz to arco playing. It is also used in some virtuoso showpieces. Left hand pizzes are usually open strings. Pizz is used on all of the violin family instruments; however, the systematic study of advanced pizzicato techniques is most developed in <a href="/wiki/Jazz_bass" title="Jazz bass">jazz bass</a>, a style in which the instrument is mostly played pizzicato.
</p><p>The right arm, hand, and bow and the bow speed are responsible for tone quality, <a href="/wiki/Rhythm" title="Rhythm">rhythm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dynamics_(music)" title="Dynamics (music)">dynamics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Articulation_(music)" title="Articulation (music)">articulation</a>, and most (but not all) changes in <a href="/wiki/Timbre" title="Timbre">timbre</a>. The player draws the bow over the string, causing the string to vibrate and produce a sustained tone. The bow is a wooden stick with tensioned horsetail hair, which has been rosined with a bar of <a href="/wiki/Rosin" title="Rosin">rosin</a>. The natural texture of the horsehair and the stickiness of the rosin help the bow to "grip" the string, and thus when the bow is drawn over the string, the bow causes the string to sound a pitch. 
</p><p>Bowing can be used to produce long sustained notes or melodies. With a <a href="/wiki/String_section" title="String section">string section</a>, if the players in a section change their bows at different times, a note can seem to be endlessly sustainable. As well, the bow can be used to play short, crisp little notes, such as repeated notes, scales and arpeggios, which provide a propulsive rhythm in many styles of music. 
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bowing_techniques">Bowing techniques</span></h4>
<p>The most essential part of bowing technique is the bow grip. It is usually with the thumb bent in the small area between the frog and the winding of the bow. The other fingers are spread somewhat evenly across the top part of the bow. The pinky finger is curled with the tip of the finger placed on the wood next to the screw. The violin produces louder notes with greater bow speed or more weight on the string.  The two methods are not equivalent, because they produce different timbres; pressing down on the string tends to produce a harsher, more intense sound. One can also achieve a louder sound by placing the bow closer to the bridge.
</p><p>The sounding point where the bow intersects the string also influences timbre (or "tone colour"). Playing close to the bridge (<i>sul ponticello</i>) gives a more intense sound than usual, emphasizing the higher harmonics; and playing with the bow over the end of the fingerboard (<i>sul tasto</i>) makes for a delicate, ethereal sound, emphasizing the <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_frequency" title="Fundamental frequency">fundamental frequency</a>. <a href="/wiki/Shinichi_Suzuki_(violinist)" title="Shinichi Suzuki (violinist)">Dr. Suzuki</a> referred to the sounding point as the <i><a href="/wiki/Fritz_Kreisler" title="Fritz Kreisler">Kreisler</a> highway</i>; one may think of different sounding points as <i>lanes</i> in the highway.
</p><p>Various methods of attack with the bow produce <a href="/wiki/Playing_the_violin#Bowing" class="mw-redirect" title="Playing the violin">different articulations</a>. There are many bowing techniques that allow for every range of playing style and many teachers, players, and orchestras spend a lot of time developing techniques and creating a unified technique within the group. These techniques include legato-style bowing (a smooth, connected, sustained sound suitable for melodies), <a href="/wiki/Coll%C3%A9_(violin)" class="mw-redirect" title="Collé (violin)">collé</a>, and a variety of bowings which produce shorter notes, including ricochet, <a href="/wiki/Sautill%C3%A9_(bow_stroke)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sautillé (bow stroke)">sautillé</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martel%C3%A9_(bowstroke)" title="Martelé (bowstroke)">martelé</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spiccato" title="Spiccato">spiccato</a>, and staccato.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Pizzicato">Pizzicato</span></h4>
<p>A note marked <i>pizz.</i> (abbreviation for <i><a href="/wiki/Pizzicato" title="Pizzicato">pizzicato</a></i>) in the written music is to be played by plucking the string with a finger of the right hand rather than by bowing. (The index finger is most commonly used here.) Sometimes in orchestra parts or virtuoso solo music where the bow hand is occupied (or for show-off effect), <i>left-hand pizzicato</i> will be indicated by a + (plus sign) below or above the note.  In left-hand pizzicato, two fingers are put on the string; one (usually the index or middle finger) is put on the correct note, and the other (usually the ring finger or little finger) is put above the note. The higher finger then plucks the string while the lower one stays on, thus producing the correct pitch. By increasing the force of the pluck, one can increase the volume of the note that the string is producing. Pizzicato is used in orchestral works and in solo showpieces. In orchestral parts, violinists often have to make very quick shifts from arco to pizzicato, and vice versa.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Col_legno">Col legno</span></h4>
<p>A marking of <i><a href="/wiki/Col_legno" title="Col legno">col legno</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> for "with the wood") in the written music calls for striking the string(s) with the stick of the bow, rather than by drawing the hair of the bow across the strings.  This bowing technique is somewhat rarely used, and results in a muted percussive sound. The eerie quality of a violin section playing <i>col legno</i> is exploited in some symphonic pieces, notably the "Witches' Dance" of the last movement of <a href="/wiki/Hector_Berlioz" title="Hector Berlioz">Berlioz's</a> <a href="/wiki/Symphonie_Fantastique" class="mw-redirect" title="Symphonie Fantastique">Symphonie Fantastique</a>. <a href="/wiki/Saint-Sa%C3%ABns" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint-Saëns">Saint-Saëns</a>'s symphonic poem <a href="/wiki/Danse_macabre_(Saint-Sa%C3%ABns)" title="Danse macabre (Saint-Saëns)"><i>Danse Macabre</i></a> includes the string section using the <i>col legno</i> technique to imitate the sound of dancing skeletons. "Mars" from Gustav Holst's "<a href="/wiki/The_Planets" title="The Planets">The Planets</a>" uses <i>col legno</i> to play a repeated rhythm in <span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span style="font-family: &#39;Georgia Pro&#39;, Georgia, &#39;DejaVu Serif&#39;, Times, &#39;Times New Roman&#39;, serif;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:0.85em;font-size:80%;text-align:center"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"><b>5</b></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"><b>4</b></sub></span></span></span> time signature. <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Britten" title="Benjamin Britten">Benjamin Britten</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Young_Person%27s_Guide_to_the_Orchestra" title="The Young Person&#39;s Guide to the Orchestra">The Young Person's Guide to the Orchestra</a></i> demands its use in the "<a href="/wiki/Percussion" class="mw-redirect" title="Percussion">Percussion</a>" Variation. <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Shostakovich" title="Dmitri Shostakovich">Dmitri Shostakovich</a> uses it in his Fourteenth Symphony in the movement 'At the Sante Jail'. Some violinists, however, object to this style of playing as it can damage the finish and impair the value of a fine bow, but most of such will compromise by using a cheap bow for at least the duration of the passage in question.
</p><p><b>Detaché</b>
</p><p>A smooth and even stroke during which bow speed and weight are the same from beginning of the stroke to the end.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Martel.C3.A9"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Martelé">Martelé</span></h4>
<p>Literally <i>hammered</i>, a strongly accented effect produced by releasing each bowstroke forcefully and suddenly. Martelé can be played in any part of the bow. It is sometimes indicated in written music by an arrowhead.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tremolo">Tremolo</span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tremolo" title="Tremolo">Tremolo</a> is the very rapid repetition (typically of a single note, but occasionally of multiple notes), usually played at the tip of the bow. Tremolo is marked with three short, slanted lines across the stem of the note. Tremolo is often used as a <a href="/wiki/Sound_effect" title="Sound effect">sound effect</a> in orchestral music, particularly in the <a href="/wiki/Romantic_music_era" class="mw-redirect" title="Romantic music era">Romantic music era</a> (1800-1910) and in opera music.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mute_or_sordino">Mute or <i>sordino</i></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Two_mutes.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Two_mutes.jpg/220px-Two_mutes.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="135" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Two_mutes.jpg/330px-Two_mutes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Two_mutes.jpg/440px-Two_mutes.jpg 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="307" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Two_mutes.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Ad hoc</i> clothespin mute and a rubber practice mute</div></div></div>
<p>Attaching a small metal, rubber, leather, or wooden device called a <i><a href="/wiki/Mute_(music)" title="Mute (music)">mute</a></i>, or <i><a href="/wiki/Sordino" class="mw-redirect" title="Sordino">sordino</a></i>, to the bridge of the violin gives a softer, more mellow tone, with fewer audible <a href="/wiki/Overtone" title="Overtone">overtones</a>; the sound of an entire orchestral string section playing with mutes has a hushed quality. The mute changes both the loudness and the <a href="/wiki/Timbre" title="Timbre">timbre</a> ("tone colour") of a violin. The conventional Italian markings for mute usage are <i>con sord.</i>, or <i>con sordino</i>, meaning 'with mute'; and <i>senza sord.</i>, meaning 'without mute'; or <i>via sord.</i>, meaning 'mute off'. 
</p><p>Larger metal, rubber, or wooden mutes are widely available, known as <i>practice mutes</i> or <i>hotel mutes</i>.  Such mutes are generally not used in performance, but are used to deaden the sound of the violin in practice areas such as hotel rooms. (For practicing purposes there is also the <a href="/wiki/Mute_violin" title="Mute violin">mute violin</a>, a violin without a sound box.) Some composers have used practice mutes for special effect, for example, at the end of <a href="/wiki/Luciano_Berio" title="Luciano Berio">Luciano Berio</a>'s <i>Sequenza VIII</i> for solo violin.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_styles">Musical styles</span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_music">Classical music</span></h3>

The very <a href="/wiki/Legato" title="Legato">legato</a> style of playing, with lavish use of <a href="/wiki/Portamento" title="Portamento">portamento</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rubato" class="mw-redirect" title="Rubato">rubato</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vibrato" title="Vibrato">vibrato</a> and the higher registers of the instrument is typical of violin playing in the late <a href="/wiki/Romantic_music" title="Romantic music">Romantic</a> period.</div></div></div>
<p>Since the <a href="/wiki/Baroque_music" title="Baroque music">Baroque</a> era, the violin has been one of the most important of all instruments in <a href="/wiki/European_classical_music" class="mw-redirect" title="European classical music">classical music</a>, for several reasons.  The tone of the violin stands out above other instruments, making it appropriate for playing a melody line.  In the hands of a good player, the violin is extremely agile, and can execute rapid and difficult sequences of notes.
</p><p>Violins make up a large part of an <a href="/wiki/Orchestra" title="Orchestra">orchestra</a>, and are usually divided into two sections, known as the first and second violins. Composers often assign the melody to the first violins, typically a more difficult part using higher positions, while second violins play harmony, accompaniment patterns or the melody an octave lower than the first violins. A <a href="/wiki/String_quartet" title="String quartet">string quartet</a> similarly has parts for first and second violins, as well as a <a href="/wiki/Viola" title="Viola">viola</a> part, and a bass instrument, such as the <a href="/wiki/Cello" title="Cello">cello</a> or, rarely, the <a href="/wiki/Double_bass" title="Double bass">double bass</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jazz">Jazz</span></h3>
<p>The earliest references to <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> <a href="/wiki/Jazz_violin" title="Jazz violin">performance using the violin</a> as a solo instrument are documented during the first decades of the 20th century. <a href="/wiki/Joe_Venuti" title="Joe Venuti">Joe Venuti</a>, one of the first jazz violinists, is known for his work with guitarist <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Lang" title="Eddie Lang">Eddie Lang</a> during the 1920s. Since that time there have been many <a href="/wiki/List_of_jazz_violinists" title="List of jazz violinists">improvising violinists</a> including <a href="/wiki/St%C3%A9phane_Grappelli" title="Stéphane Grappelli">Stéphane Grappelli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stuff_Smith" title="Stuff Smith">Stuff Smith</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eddie_South" title="Eddie South">Eddie South</a>, <a href="/wiki/Regina_Carter" title="Regina Carter">Regina Carter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Frigo" title="Johnny Frigo">Johnny Frigo</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Blake,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="John Blake, Jr.">John Blake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adam_Taubitz" title="Adam Taubitz">Adam Taubitz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leroy_Jenkins_(jazz_musician)" title="Leroy Jenkins (jazz musician)">Leroy Jenkins</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jean-Luc_Ponty" title="Jean-Luc Ponty">Jean-Luc Ponty</a>. While not primarily jazz violinists, <a href="/wiki/Darol_Anger" title="Darol Anger">Darol Anger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mark_O%27Connor" title="Mark O&#39;Connor">Mark O'Connor</a> have spent significant parts of their careers playing jazz.
The Swiss-Cuban violinist <a href="/wiki/Yilian_Ca%C3%B1izares" title="Yilian Cañizares">Yilian Cañizares</a> mixes jazz with Cuban music.<sup id="cite_ref-NZZ_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NZZ-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Violins also appear in ensembles supplying orchestral backgrounds to many jazz recordings.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indian_classical_music">Indian classical music</span></h3>
<p>The Indian violin, while essentially the same instrument as that used in Western music, is different in some senses.<sup id="cite_ref-FirstPost_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FirstPost-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The instrument is tuned so that the IV and III strings (G and D on a western-tuned violin) and the II and I (A and E) strings are sa–pa (do–sol) pairs and sound the same but are offset by an octave, resembling common <a href="/wiki/Scordatura" title="Scordatura">scordatura</a> or fiddle cross-tunings such as G–D–G–D or A–E–A–E. The tonic sa (do) is not fixed, but variably tuned to accommodate the vocalist or lead player. The way the musician holds the instrument varies from Western to Indian music. In Indian music the musician sits on the floor cross-legged with the right foot out in front of them. The scroll of the instrument rests on the foot. This position is essential to playing well due to the nature of Indian music. The hand can move all over the fingerboard and there is no set position for the left hand, so it is important for the violin to be in a steady, unmoving position.
</p>

<p>Up through at least the 1970s, most types of popular music used bowed <a href="/wiki/String_section" title="String section">string sections</a>. They were extensively used in popular music throughout the 1920s and early 1930s. With the rise of <a href="/wiki/Swing_music" title="Swing music">swing music</a>, however, from 1935 to 1945, the string sound was often used to add to the fullness of <a href="/wiki/Big_band" title="Big band">big band</a> music. Following the <a href="/wiki/Swing_era" title="Swing era">swing era</a>, from the late 1940s to the mid-1950s, strings began to be revived in <a href="/wiki/Traditional_pop" title="Traditional pop">traditional pop</a> music. This trend accelerated in the late 1960s, with a significant revival of the use of strings, especially in <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul music</a>. Popular <a href="/wiki/Motown" title="Motown">Motown</a> recordings of the late 1960s and 1970s relied heavily on strings as part of their trademark texture. The rise of <a href="/wiki/Disco" title="Disco">disco</a> music in the 1970s continued this trend with the heavy use of string instruments in popular disco orchestras (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Love_Unlimited_Orchestra" class="mw-redirect" title="Love Unlimited Orchestra">Love Unlimited Orchestra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biddu" title="Biddu">Biddu</a> Orchestra, <a href="/wiki/Monster_Orchestra" class="mw-redirect" title="Monster Orchestra">Monster Orchestra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salsoul_Orchestra" title="Salsoul Orchestra">Salsoul Orchestra</a>, <a href="/wiki/MFSB" title="MFSB">MFSB</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>With the rise of <a href="/wiki/Electronic_music" title="Electronic music">electronically created music</a> in the 1980s, violins declined in use, as synthesized string sounds played by a keyboardist with a <a href="/wiki/Synthesizer" title="Synthesizer">synthesizer</a> took their place. However, while the violin has had very little usage in mainstream <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock music</a>, it has some history in <a href="/wiki/Progressive_rock" title="Progressive rock">progressive rock</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Electric_Light_Orchestra" title="Electric Light Orchestra">Electric Light Orchestra</a>, <a href="/wiki/King_Crimson" title="King Crimson">King Crimson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kansas_(band)" title="Kansas (band)">Kansas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gentle_Giant" title="Gentle Giant">Gentle Giant</a>). The 1973 album <i><a href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contaminazione_(album)" class="extiw" title="it:Contaminazione (album)">Contaminazione</a></i> by Italy's <a href="/wiki/Il_Rovescio_della_Medaglia" title="Il Rovescio della Medaglia">RDM</a> plays violins off against synthesizers at its finale ("La grande fuga").<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The instrument has a stronger place in modern <a href="/wiki/Jazz_fusion" title="Jazz fusion">jazz fusion</a> bands, notably <a href="/wiki/The_Corrs" title="The Corrs">The Corrs</a>.  The fiddle is sometimes a part of <a href="/wiki/British_folk_rock" title="British folk rock">British folk rock</a> music, as exemplified by the likes of <a href="/wiki/Fairport_Convention" title="Fairport Convention">Fairport Convention</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steeleye_Span" title="Steeleye Span">Steeleye Span</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The popularity of <a href="/wiki/Crossover_music" title="Crossover music">crossover music</a> beginning in the last years of the 20th century has brought the violin back into the popular music arena, with both electric and acoustic violins being used by popular bands. <a href="/wiki/Dave_Matthews_Band" title="Dave Matthews Band">Dave Matthews Band</a> features violinist <a href="/wiki/Boyd_Tinsley" title="Boyd Tinsley">Boyd Tinsley</a>.  <a href="/wiki/The_Flock_(band)" title="The Flock (band)">The Flock</a> featured violinist <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Goodman" title="Jerry Goodman">Jerry Goodman</a> who later joined the <a href="/wiki/Jazz-rock_fusion" class="mw-redirect" title="Jazz-rock fusion">jazz-rock fusion</a> band, <a href="/wiki/The_Mahavishnu_Orchestra" class="mw-redirect" title="The Mahavishnu Orchestra">The Mahavishnu Orchestra</a>.  <a href="/wiki/James_(band)" title="James (band)">James</a>' <a href="/wiki/Saul_Davies" title="Saul Davies">Saul Davies</a>, who is also a <a href="/wiki/Guitar" title="Guitar">guitarist</a>, was enlisted by the band as a violinist. </p><p>Independent artists, such as <a href="/wiki/Owen_Pallett" title="Owen Pallett">Owen Pallett</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Shondes" title="The Shondes">The Shondes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Bird" title="Andrew Bird">Andrew Bird</a>, have also spurred increased interest in the instrument.<sup id="cite_ref-ChicagoMag_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ChicagoMag-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Independent_music" title="Independent music">Indie</a> bands have often embraced new and unusual arrangements, allowing them more freedom to feature the violin than many <a href="/wiki/Mainstream" title="Mainstream">mainstream</a> musical artists. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Folk_music_and_fiddling">Folk music and fiddling</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fiddle" title="Fiddle">Fiddle</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Anders_Zorn_-_Hins_Anders_(1904).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Anders_Zorn_-_Hins_Anders_%281904%29.jpg/220px-Anders_Zorn_-_Hins_Anders_%281904%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="282" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Anders_Zorn_-_Hins_Anders_%281904%29.jpg/330px-Anders_Zorn_-_Hins_Anders_%281904%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Anders_Zorn_-_Hins_Anders_%281904%29.jpg/440px-Anders_Zorn_-_Hins_Anders_%281904%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="825" data-file-height="1056" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Anders_Zorn_-_Hins_Anders_(1904).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Fiddler" class="mw-redirect" title="Fiddler">fiddler</a> Hins Anders Ersson painted by <a href="/wiki/Anders_Zorn" title="Anders Zorn">Anders Zorn</a>, 1904</div></div></div>
<p>Like many other instruments used in <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">classical music</a>, the violin descends from remote ancestors that were used for <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk music</a>.  Following a stage of intensive development in the late <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>, largely in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, the violin had improved (in volume, tone, and agility), to the point that it not only became a very important instrument in art music, but proved highly appealing to folk musicians as well, ultimately spreading very widely, sometimes displacing earlier bowed instruments. <a href="/wiki/Ethnomusicology" title="Ethnomusicology">Ethnomusicologists</a> have observed its widespread use in Europe, Asia, and the Americas.
</p><p>When played as a folk instrument, the violin is usually referred to in English as a <i>fiddle</i> (although the term <i>fiddle</i> can be used informally no matter what the genre of music). Worldwide, there are various stringed instruments such as the <a href="/wiki/Hurdy-gurdy" title="Hurdy-gurdy">wheel fiddle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Apache_fiddle" title="Apache fiddle">Apache fiddle</a> that are also called "fiddles". Fiddle music differs from classical in that the tunes are generally considered dance music,<sup id="cite_ref-OKHistory_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OKHistory-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> and various techniques, such as droning, shuffling, and ornamentation specific to particular styles are used. In many traditions of folk music, the tunes are not written but are memorized by successive generations of musicians and passed on<sup id="cite_ref-OKHistory_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OKHistory-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> in what is known as the <a href="/wiki/Oral_tradition" title="Oral tradition">oral tradition</a>. Many <a href="/wiki/Old-time_music" title="Old-time music">old-time</a> pieces call for <a href="/wiki/Cross_tuning" title="Cross tuning">cross-tuning</a>, or using tunings other than standard GDAE. Some players of American styles of folk fiddling (such as <a href="/wiki/Bluegrass_music" title="Bluegrass music">bluegrass</a> or old-time) have their bridge's top edge cut to a slightly flatter curve, making techniques such as a "double shuffle" less taxing on the bow arm, as it reduces the range of motion needed for alternating between <a href="/wiki/Double_stop" title="Double stop">double stops</a> on different string pairs. Fiddlers who use solid steel core strings may prefer to use a <a href="/wiki/Tailpiece" title="Tailpiece">tailpiece</a> with fine tuners on all four strings, instead of the single fine tuner on the E string used by many classical players.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electric_violins">Electric violins</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Electric_violin" title="Electric violin">Electric violin</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Transformation.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Transformation.JPG/260px-Transformation.JPG" decoding="async" width="260" height="195" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Transformation.JPG/390px-Transformation.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/44/Transformation.JPG 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="375" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Transformation.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Acoustic and electric violins</div></div></div>
<p><b>Electric violins</b> have a <a href="/wiki/Magnetism" title="Magnetism">magnetic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Piezoelectricity" title="Piezoelectricity">piezoelectric</a> <a href="/wiki/Pickup_(music_technology)" title="Pickup (music technology)">pickup</a> that converts string vibration to an electric signal. A <a href="/wiki/Patch_cable" title="Patch cable">patch cable</a> or wireless transmitter sends the signal to an amplifier of a <a href="/wiki/PA_system" class="mw-redirect" title="PA system">PA system</a>. Electric violins are usually constructed as such, but a pickup can be added to a conventional acoustic violin. An electric violin with a resonating body that produces listening-level sound independently of the electric elements can be called an <i>electro-acoustic violin</i>. To be effective as an acoustic violin, electro-acoustic violins retain much of the resonating body of the violin, and often resemble an acoustic violin or fiddle.  The body may be finished in bright colors and made from alternative materials to wood. These violins may need to be hooked up to an <a href="/wiki/Instrument_amplifier" title="Instrument amplifier">instrument amplifier</a> or <a href="/wiki/PA_system" class="mw-redirect" title="PA system">PA system</a>.  Some types come with a silent option that allows the player to use headphones that are hooked up to the violin. The first specially built <a href="/wiki/Electric_violin" title="Electric violin">electric violins</a> date back to 1928 and were made by Victor Pfeil, Oskar Vierling, George Eisenberg, <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Miessner" title="Benjamin Miessner">Benjamin Miessner</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Beauchamp" title="George Beauchamp">George Beauchamp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Benioff" title="Hugo Benioff">Hugo Benioff</a> and Fredray Kislingbury. These violins can be plugged into <a href="/wiki/Effect_unit" class="mw-redirect" title="Effect unit">effect units</a>, just like an <a href="/wiki/Electric_guitar" title="Electric guitar">electric guitar</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Distortion_(music)" title="Distortion (music)">distortion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wah-wah_pedal" title="Wah-wah pedal">wah-wah pedal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reverb" class="mw-redirect" title="Reverb">reverb</a>. Since electric violins do not rely on string tension and resonance to amplify their sound they can have more strings. For example, five-stringed electric violins are available from several manufacturers, and a seven string electric violin (with three lower strings encompassing the <a href="/wiki/Cello" title="Cello">cello</a>'s range) is also available.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> The majority of the first <a href="/wiki/Electric_violinist" class="mw-redirect" title="Electric violinist">electric violinists</a> were musicians playing <a href="/wiki/Jazz_fusion" title="Jazz fusion">jazz fusion</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Jean-Luc_Ponty" title="Jean-Luc Ponty">Jean-Luc Ponty</a>) and popular music.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Violin_authentication">Violin authentication</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Violin_authentication" title="Violin authentication">Violin authentication</a></div>
<p>Violin authentication is the process of determining the maker and manufacture date of a violin. This process is similar to that used to determine the <a href="/wiki/Provenance" title="Provenance">provenance</a> of art works.  As significant value may be attached to violins made either by specific makers or at specific times and locations, <a href="/wiki/Art_forgery" title="Art forgery">forgery</a> and other methods of <a href="/wiki/Fraud" title="Fraud">fraudulent</a> misrepresentation can be used to inflate the value of an instrument.
</p>
