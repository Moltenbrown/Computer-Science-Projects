<p><b>Miss</b> (pronounced <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is an English language <a href="/wiki/English_honorific" class="mw-redirect" title="English honorific">honorific</a> traditionally used only for an unmarried woman (not using another title such as "<a href="/wiki/Doctor_(title)" title="Doctor (title)">Doctor</a>" or "<a href="/wiki/Dame_(title)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dame (title)">Dame</a>"). Originating in the 17th century, it is a contraction of <i><a href="/wiki/Mistress_(form_of_address)" title="Mistress (form of address)">mistress</a></i>, which was used for all women. A period is not used to signify the contraction. Its counterparts are <a href="/wiki/Mrs." title="Mrs.">Mrs.</a>, usually used only for married women, and <a href="/wiki/Ms." title="Ms.">Ms.</a>, which can be used for married or unmarried women.
</p><p>The plural <i><b>Misses</b></i> may be used, such as in <i>The Misses Doe</i>. The traditional French "<b>Mademoiselle</b>" (abbreviation "<b>Mlle</b>") may also be used as the plural in English language conversation or correspondence. In Australian, British, and Irish schools the term miss is used interchangeably with female teacher.
</p>

<h2><span id="Use_as_a_title_.28honorific.29_in_the_United_States"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_as_a_title_(honorific)_in_the_United_States">Use as a title (honorific) in the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Miss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Use as a title (honorific) in the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The usage of "Miss" as a title in the United States is most frequently seen when referring to young women, or women whose marital status is unknown. Though <i>Miss</i> is less commonly used as a title by unmarried adult <a href="/wiki/Women" class="mw-redirect" title="Women">women</a> in the United States than in the past, some still prefer to be referred to as such. Twenty-first century <a href="/wiki/Etiquette" title="Etiquette">etiquette</a> honors an adult woman's personal preference of title. However, if the preference is not known, "Ms." is used. "Ms." is the preferred choice as the female title in business. It is the equivalent to the male title "Mr." as neither is marital status specific.
</p><p><i>Miss</i> was formerly the default title for businesswomen, but has largely been replaced by <a href="/wiki/Ms." title="Ms.">Ms.</a> in this context. It was a default title for actresses <i>(Miss <a href="/wiki/Helen_Hayes" title="Helen Hayes">Helen Hayes</a>, Miss <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Stanwyck" title="Barbara Stanwyck">Barbara Stanwyck</a>)</i> or other celebrities <i>(Miss <a href="/wiki/Amelia_Earhart" title="Amelia Earhart">Amelia Earhart</a>)</i>. Such default usage has also proved problematic; the poet <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Parker" title="Dorothy Parker">Dorothy Parker</a> was often referred to as <i>Miss Parker</i>, even though <i>Parker</i> was the name of her first husband and she herself preferred <i>Mrs. Parker</i>. Later in the century, the use of "Miss" or "Mrs." became a problem for <i>The New York Times</i> in referring to political candidate <a href="/wiki/Geraldine_Ferraro" title="Geraldine Ferraro">Geraldine Ferraro</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, a married woman who did not use her husband's surname, since <i>Mrs.</i> has been used with a woman's maiden name only in limited circumstances in public life before the 1980s. (See more at <a href="/wiki/Ms." title="Ms.">Ms.</a>)
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_alone_as_a_form_of_address">Use alone as a form of address</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Miss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Use alone as a form of address">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><i>Miss</i> is an honorific for addressing a woman who is not married, and is known by her maiden name. It is a shortened form of <i>mistress</i>, and departed from <i>misses/missus</i> which became used to signify marital attachment in the 18th and 19th centuries. It does not imply age, though youth corresponds (as marriage implies adulthood).
</p><p>Those seeking to diminish the importance of marriage status to a woman's social identity began to appropriate the office expedience of <i>Ms.</i> (unpunctuated in the UK) in the early 1970s, when it rose 700%. In formal correspondence as women entered the workforce, <i>Ms.</i> pronounced "mizz," served/serves some conservative social functions in avoiding unpleasantness where a woman might keep a marriage name after a divorce, or where the interpersonal gaffe of failing to acknowledge the important marriage event might cause offense (or indicate junior authorship of correspondence). The use of <i>Ms.</i>, nevertheless, is in decline. <sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Miss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In some American subcultures, such as the <a href="/wiki/American_South" class="mw-redirect" title="American South">American South</a> and some urban cultures, <i>Miss</i> is sometimes used irrespective of marital status with a woman's first name in direct or indirect informal address, as <i>Miss Ellen</i> from <i><a href="/wiki/Gone_with_the_Wind_(novel)" title="Gone with the Wind (novel)">Gone with the Wind</a></i> or <a href="/wiki/Miss_Ellie_Ewing" title="Miss Ellie Ewing"><i>Miss Ellie</i></a> from <i><a href="/wiki/Dallas_(1978_TV_series)" title="Dallas (1978 TV series)">Dallas</a></i>. This form was also used in <a href="/wiki/Upper_class" title="Upper class">upper class</a> households in some English-speaking countries by <a href="/wiki/Servant" class="mw-redirect" title="Servant">servants</a> to address or refer to the unmarried ladies of the household, and occasionally in family-run businesses in the same manner, though more commonly it was used to address servants if they were addressed by title at all. This is also common with female child-care givers; small children will refer to their preschool teacher or nanny as "Miss" and their first name, regardless of marital status.  In some school districts in the United States, this custom extends through the early primary school grades.
</p><p>In some styles of <a href="/wiki/Etiquette" title="Etiquette">etiquette</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (October 2011)">which?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, the eldest daughter of a family was addressed on paper simply as <i>Miss <a href="/wiki/John_Doe" title="John Doe">Doe</a></i>, with the younger daughters being addressed as <i>Miss Jane Doe</i> and <i>Miss Rebecca Doe</i>. In person, as in when making introductions, the styling would have been extended to unmarried cousins with the same surname.
</p><p>Another notable use of <i>Miss</i> is as the title of a <a href="/wiki/Beauty_queen" class="mw-redirect" title="Beauty queen">beauty queen</a> (given that in most pageants it is a requirement that contestants be unmarried), such as <i><a href="/wiki/Miss_America" title="Miss America">Miss America</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Miss_World" title="Miss World">Miss World</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Miss_Universe" title="Miss Universe">Miss Universe</a></i>. Other languages, such as <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>, <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>, have borrowed the English <i>Miss</i> to refer to the winner of a beauty pageant.
</p><p>In some Mexican schools (particularly, but not exclusively, in bilingual schools), the term <i>Miss</i> and <i>female teacher</i> are used interchangeably; students often address their female teachers simply as <i>Miss</i>.
</p>
