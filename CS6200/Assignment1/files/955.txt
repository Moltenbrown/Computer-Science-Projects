Steam engines of this kind propelled the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> in Great Britain and the world.</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Watt steam engine</b>, alternatively known as the <b>Boulton and Watt steam engine</b>, was an early <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engine</a> and was one of the driving forces of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a>. <a href="/wiki/James_Watt" title="James Watt">James Watt</a> developed the design sporadically from 1763 to 1775 with support from <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Boulton" title="Matthew Boulton">Matthew Boulton</a>. Watt's design saved so much more fuel compared with earlier designs that they were licensed based on the amount of fuel they would save. Watt never ceased developing the steam engine, introducing double-acting designs (with two cylinders) and various systems for taking off rotary power. Watt's design became synonymous with steam engines, and it was many years before significantly new designs began to replace the basic Watt design. 
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Newcomen_atmospheric_engine" title="Newcomen atmospheric engine">first steam engines</a>, introduced by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Newcomen" title="Thomas Newcomen">Thomas Newcomen</a> in 1712, were of the "atmospheric" design. At the end of the power stroke, the weight of the object being moved by the engine, pulled the piston to the top of the cylinder, as steam was introduced. Then the cylinder was cooled by a spray of water, which caused the steam to condense, forming a <a href="/wiki/Partial_vacuum" class="mw-redirect" title="Partial vacuum">partial vacuum</a> in the cylinder. Atmospheric pressure on the top of the piston pushed it down, lifting the work object. Watt noticed that it required significant amounts of heat to warm the cylinder back up to the point where steam could enter the cylinder without immediately condensing. When the cylinder was warm enough that it became filled with steam the next power stroke could commence. 
</p><p>Watt realised that the heat needed to warm the cylinder could be saved by adding a separate condensing cylinder. After the power cylinder was filled with steam, a valve was opened to the secondary cylinder, allowing the steam to flow into it and be condensed, which drew the steam from the main cylinder causing the power stroke. The condensing cylinder was water cooled to keep the steam condensing. At the end of the power stroke, the valve was closed so the power cylinder could be filled with steam as the piston moved to the top. The end result was the same cycle as Newcomen's design, but without any cooling of the power cylinder which was immediately ready for another stroke. 
</p><p>Watt worked on the design over a period of several years, introducing the condenser, and introducing improvements to practically every part of the design. Notably, Watt preformed a lengthy series of trials on ways to seal the piston in the cylinder, which considerably reduced leakage during the power stroke, preventing power loss. All of these changes produced a more reliable design which used half as much coal to produce the same amount of power.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The new design was introduced commercially in 1776, with the first example sold to the <a href="/wiki/Carron_Company" title="Carron Company">Carron Company</a> ironworks. Watt continued working to improve the engine, and in 1781 introduced a system using a <a href="/wiki/Sun_and_planet_gear" title="Sun and planet gear">sun and planet gear</a> to turn the linear motion of the engines into rotary motion. This made it useful not only in the original pumping role, but also as a direct replacement in roles where a <a href="/wiki/Water_wheel" title="Water wheel">water wheel</a> would have been used previously. This was a key moment in the industrial revolution, since power sources could now be located anywhere instead of, as previously, needing a suitable water source and <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a>. Boulton began developing a multitude of machines that made use of this rotary power, developing the first modern industrialized factory, the <a href="/wiki/Soho_Foundry" title="Soho Foundry">Soho Foundry</a>, which in turn produced new steam engine designs. Watt's early engines were like the original Newcomen designs in that they used low-pressure steam, and all of the power was produced by atmospheric pressure. When, in the early 1800's, other companies introduced high-pressure steam engines, Watt was reluctant to follow suit due to safety concerns.<sup id="cite_ref-Dickinson_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dickinson-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Wanting to improve on the performance of his engines, Watt began considering the use of higher-pressure steam, as well as designs using multiple cylinders in both the double-acting concept and the multiple-expansion concept. These double-acting engines required the invention of the <a href="/wiki/Parallel_motion" title="Parallel motion">parallel motion</a>, which allowed the <a href="/wiki/Piston_rod" title="Piston rod">piston rods</a> of the individual cylinders to move in straight lines, keeping the piston true in the cylinder, while the walking beam end moved through an arc, somewhat analogous to a <a href="/wiki/Crosshead" title="Crosshead">crosshead</a> in later steam engines.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction">Introduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watt_steam_engine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Introduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1698, the English mechanical designer <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Savery" title="Thomas Savery">Thomas Savery</a> invented a pumping appliance that used steam to draw water directly from a well by means of a vacuum created by condensing steam. The appliance was also proposed for draining <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mines</a>, but it could only draw fluid up approximately 25 feet, meaning it had to be located within this distance of the mine floor being drained. As mines became deeper, this was often impractical. It also consumed a large amount of fuel compared with later engines.<sup id="cite_ref-Harvnb&#124;Rosen&#124;year-2012&#124;pp_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harvnb|Rosen|year-2012|pp-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Newcomen_steam_engine.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Newcomen_steam_engine.jpg/220px-Newcomen_steam_engine.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="336" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Newcomen_steam_engine.jpg/330px-Newcomen_steam_engine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Newcomen_steam_engine.jpg/440px-Newcomen_steam_engine.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1734" data-file-height="2649" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Newcomen_steam_engine.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The model <a href="/wiki/Newcomen_atmospheric_engine" title="Newcomen atmospheric engine">Newcomen engine</a> upon which Watt experimented</div></div></div>
<p>The solution to draining deep mines was found by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Newcomen" title="Thomas Newcomen">Thomas Newcomen</a> who developed an <a href="/wiki/Newcomen_atmospheric_engine" title="Newcomen atmospheric engine">"atmospheric" engine</a> that also worked on the vacuum principle. It employed a cylinder containing a movable piston connected by a chain to one end of a rocking beam that worked a mechanical lift pump from its opposite end. At the bottom of each stroke, steam was allowed to enter the cylinder below the piston. As the piston rose within the cylinder, drawn upward by a counterbalance, it drew in steam at atmospheric pressure. At the top of the stroke the steam valve was closed, and cold water was briefly injected into the cylinder as a means of cooling the steam. This water condensed the steam and created a partial vacuum below the piston. The atmospheric pressure outside the engine was then greater than the pressure within the cylinder, thereby pushing the piston into the cylinder. The piston, attached to a chain and in turn attached to one end of the "rocking beam", pulled down the end of the beam, lifting the opposite end of the beam. Hence, the pump deep in the mine attached to opposite end of the beam via ropes and chains was driven. The pump pushed, rather than pulled the column of water upward, hence it could lift water any distance. Once the piston was at the bottom, the cycle repeated.<sup id="cite_ref-Harvnb&#124;Rosen&#124;year-2012&#124;pp_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harvnb|Rosen|year-2012|pp-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Newcomen engine was more powerful than the Savery engine. For the first time water could be raised from a depth of over 100 yards (91&#160;m).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  The first example from 1712 was able to replace a team of 500 horses that had been used to pump out the mine.  Seventy-five Newcomen pumping engines were installed at mines in Britain, France, Holland, Sweden and Russia. In the next fifty years only a few small changes were made to the engine design. It was a great advancement.
</p><p>While Newcomen engines brought practical benefits, they were inefficient in terms of the use of energy to power them. The system of alternately sending jets of steam, then cold water into the cylinder meant that the walls of the cylinder were alternately heated, then cooled with each stroke. Each charge of steam introduced would continue condensing until the cylinder approached working temperature once again. So at each stroke part of the potential of the steam was lost.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Separate_condenser">Separate condenser</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watt_steam_engine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Separate condenser">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Watt_steam_pumping_engine.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Watt_steam_pumping_engine.JPG/300px-Watt_steam_pumping_engine.JPG" decoding="async" width="300" height="285" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Watt_steam_pumping_engine.JPG/450px-Watt_steam_pumping_engine.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Watt_steam_pumping_engine.JPG/600px-Watt_steam_pumping_engine.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2367" data-file-height="2250" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Watt_steam_pumping_engine.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The major components of a Watt pumping engine</div></div></div>
<p>In 1763, <a href="/wiki/James_Watt" title="James Watt">James Watt</a> was working as instrument maker at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Glasgow" title="University of Glasgow">University of Glasgow</a> when he was assigned the job of repairing a model Newcomen engine and noted how inefficient it was.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1765, Watt conceived the idea of equipping the engine with a separate <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensation</a> chamber, which he called a <a href="/wiki/Condenser_(heat_transfer)" title="Condenser (heat transfer)">"condenser"</a>. Because the condenser and the working <a href="/wiki/Cylinder_(engine)" title="Cylinder (engine)">cylinder</a> were separate, condensation occurred without significant loss of heat from the cylinder. The condenser remained cold and below <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> at all times, while the cylinder remained hot at all times.
</p><p>Steam was drawn from the boiler to the cylinder under the <a href="/wiki/Piston" title="Piston">piston</a>. When the piston reached the top of the cylinder, the steam inlet valve closed and the valve controlling the passage to the condenser opened. The condenser being at a lower pressure, drew the steam from the cylinder into the condenser where it cooled and condensed from water vapor to liquid water, maintaining a partial vacuum in the condenser that was communicated to the space of the cylinder by the connecting passage. External atmospheric pressure then pushed the piston down the cylinder.
</p><p>The separation of the cylinder and condenser eliminated the loss of heat that occurred when steam was condensed in the working cylinder of a Newcomen engine. This gave the Watt engine greater efficiency than the Newcomen engine, reducing the amount of coal consumed while doing the same amount of work as a Newcomen engine.
</p><p>In Watt's design, the cold water was injected only into the condensation chamber. This type of condenser is known as a <i>jet condenser</i>. The condenser is located in a cold water bath below the cylinder. The volume of water entering the condenser as spray absorbed the latent heat of the steam, and was determined as seven times the volume of  the condensed steam. The condensate and the injected water was then removed by the air pump, and the surrounding cold water served to absorb the remaining thermal energy to retain a condenser temperature of 30&#160;°C to 45&#160;°C and the equivalent pressure of 0.04 to 0.1 bar <sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At each stroke the warm condensate was drawn off from the condenser and sent to a hot well by a vacuum pump, which also helped to evacuate the steam from under the power cylinder. The still-warm condensate was recycled as feedwater for the boiler.
</p><p>Watt's next improvement to the Newcomen design was to seal the top of the cylinder and surround the cylinder with a jacket. Steam was passed through the jacket before being admitted below the piston, keeping the piston and cylinder warm to prevent condensation within it. The second improvement was the utilisation of steam expansion against the vacuum on the other side of the piston. The steam supply was cut during the stroke, and the steam expanded against the vacuum on the other side. This increased the efficiency of the engine, but also created a variable torque on the shaft which was undesirable for many applications, in particular pumping. Watt therefore limited the expansion to a ratio of 1:2 (i.e. the steam supply was cut at half stroke). This increased the theoretical efficiency from 6.4% to 10.6%, with only a small variation in piston pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Watt did not use high pressure steam because of safety concerns.<sup id="cite_ref-Dickinson_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dickinson-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>85</span></sup>
</p><p>These improvements led to the fully developed version of 1776 that actually went into production.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Partnership_of_Matthew_Boulton_and_James_Watt">The Partnership of Matthew Boulton and James Watt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watt_steam_engine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Partnership of Matthew Boulton and James Watt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Boulton_and_Watt" title="Boulton and Watt">Boulton and Watt</a></div>
<p>The separate condenser showed dramatic potential for improvements on the Newcomen engine but Watt was still discouraged by seemingly insurmountable problems before a marketable engine could be perfected. It was only after entering into partnership with <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Boulton" title="Matthew Boulton">Matthew Boulton</a> that such became reality. Watt told Boulton about his ideas on improving the engine, and Boulton, an avid entrepreneur, agreed to fund development of a test engine at <a href="/wiki/Soho,_Birmingham" class="mw-redirect" title="Soho, Birmingham">Soho</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a>. At last Watt had access to facilities and the practical experience of craftsmen who were soon able to get the first engine working. As fully developed, it used about 75% less fuel than a similar Newcomen one.
</p><p>In 1775, Watt designed two large engines: one for the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bloomfield_Colliery&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bloomfield Colliery (page does not exist)">Bloomfield Colliery</a> at <a href="/wiki/Tipton" title="Tipton">Tipton</a>, completed in March 1776, and one for <a href="/wiki/John_Wilkinson_(industrialist)" title="John Wilkinson (industrialist)">John Wilkinson</a>'s ironworks at <a href="/wiki/Willey,_Shropshire" title="Willey, Shropshire">Willey, Shropshire</a>, which was at work the following month. A third engine, at <a href="/wiki/Stratford,_London" title="Stratford, London">Stratford-le-Bow</a> in east London, was also working that summer.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Watt had tried unsuccessfully for several years to obtain an accurately bored cylinder for his steam engines, and was forced to use hammered iron, which was out of round and caused leakage past the piston. Joseph Wickham Roe stated in 1916: "When [John] <a href="/wiki/John_Smeaton" title="John Smeaton">Smeaton</a> saw the first engine he reported to the Society of Engineers that 'Neither the tools nor the workmen existed who could manufacture such a complex machine with sufficient precision<span style="padding-right:.15em;">'</span>".<sup id="cite_ref-Roe1916_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roe1916-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1774, <a href="/wiki/John_Wilkinson_(industrialist)" title="John Wilkinson (industrialist)">John Wilkinson</a> invented a boring machine in which the shaft that held the cutting tool was supported on both ends and extended through the cylinder, unlike the cantilevered borers then in use. Boulton wrote in 1776 that "Mr. Wilkinson has bored us several cylinders almost without error; that of 50 inches diameter, which we have put up at Tipton, does not err on the thickness of an old shilling in any part".<sup id="cite_ref-Roe1916_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roe1916-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Boulton_and_Watt" title="Boulton and Watt">Boulton and Watt</a>'s practice was to help mine-owners and other customers to build engines, supplying men to erect them and some specialised parts. However, their main profit from their patent was derived from charging a licence fee to the engine owners, based on the cost of the fuel they saved. The greater fuel efficiency of their engines meant that they were most attractive in areas where fuel was expensive, particularly <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, for which three engines were ordered in 1777, for the <a href="/wiki/Wheal_Busy" title="Wheal Busy">Wheal Busy</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ting_Tang&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ting Tang (page does not exist)">Ting Tang</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chacewater" title="Chacewater">Chacewater</a> mines.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_improvements">Later improvements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watt_steam_engine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later improvements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:WattParallelMotion.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/WattParallelMotion.jpg/220px-WattParallelMotion.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="180" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/WattParallelMotion.jpg/330px-WattParallelMotion.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/WattParallelMotion.jpg/440px-WattParallelMotion.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2430" data-file-height="1992" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:WattParallelMotion.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Watt's <a href="/wiki/Parallel_motion" title="Parallel motion">parallel motion</a> on a pumping engine</div></div></div>
<p>The first Watt engines were atmospheric pressure engines, like the Newcomen engine but with the condensation taking place separate from the cylinder. Driving the engines using both low pressure steam and a partial vacuum raised the possibility of <a href="/wiki/Reciprocating_engine" title="Reciprocating engine">reciprocating engine</a> development.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> An arrangement of valves could alternately admit low pressure steam to the cylinder and then connect with the condenser. Consequently, the direction of the power stroke might be reversed, making it easier to obtain rotary motion. Additional benefits of the <a href="/wiki/Single-_and_Double-acting_cylinder" class="mw-redirect" title="Single- and Double-acting cylinder">double acting</a> engine were increased efficiency, higher speed (greater power) and more regular motion.
</p><p>Before the development of the double acting piston, the linkage to the beam and the piston rod had been by means of a chain, which meant that power could only be applied in one direction, by pulling. This was effective in engines that were used for pumping water, but the double action of the piston meant that it could push as well as pull. This was not possible as long as the beam and the rod were connected by a chain. Furthermore, it was not possible to connect the piston rod of the sealed cylinder <i>directly</i> to the beam, because while the rod moved vertically in a straight line, the beam was pivoted at its centre, with each side inscribing an arc. To bridge the conflicting actions of the beam and the piston, Watt developed his <a href="/wiki/Parallel_motion" title="Parallel motion">parallel motion</a>. This masterpiece of engineering uses a four bar <a href="/wiki/Linkage_(mechanical)" title="Linkage (mechanical)">linkage</a> coupled with a <a href="/wiki/Pantograph_(transport)" title="Pantograph (transport)">pantograph</a> to produce the required straight line motion much more cheaply than if he had used a slider type of linkage.  He was very proud of his solution.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:WattsSteamEngine.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/WattsSteamEngine.jpeg/220px-WattsSteamEngine.jpeg" decoding="async" width="220" height="265" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/WattsSteamEngine.jpeg/330px-WattsSteamEngine.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/WattsSteamEngine.jpeg/440px-WattsSteamEngine.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="1507" data-file-height="1818" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:WattsSteamEngine.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Watt steam engine<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Having the beam connected to the piston shaft by a means that applied force alternately in both directions also meant that it was possible to use the motion of the beam to turn a wheel. The simplest solution to transforming the action of the beam into a rotating motion was to connect the beam to a wheel by a <a href="/wiki/Crankshaft" title="Crankshaft">crank</a>, but because another party had patent rights on the use of the crank, Watt was obliged to come up with another solution.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> He adopted the <a href="/wiki/Epicyclic_gear" class="mw-redirect" title="Epicyclic gear">epicyclic</a> <a href="/wiki/Sun_and_planet_gear" title="Sun and planet gear">sun and planet gear</a> system suggested by an employee <a href="/wiki/William_Murdoch" title="William Murdoch">William Murdoch</a>, only later reverting, once the patent rights had expired, to the more familiar crank seen on most engines today.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The main wheel attached to the crank was large and heavy, serving as a <a href="/wiki/Flywheel" title="Flywheel">flywheel</a> which, once set in motion, by its momentum maintained a constant power and smoothed the action of the alternating strokes. To its rotating central shaft, belts and gears could be attached to drive a great variety of machinery.
</p><p>Because factory machinery needed to operate at a constant speed, Watt linked a steam regulator valve to a <a href="/wiki/Centrifugal_governor" title="Centrifugal governor">centrifugal governor</a> which he adapted from those used to automatically control the speed of windmills.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  The centrifugal was not a true speed <a href="/wiki/PID_controller" title="PID controller">controller</a> because it could not hold a set speed in response to a change in load.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>These improvements allowed the steam engine to replace the <a href="/wiki/Water_wheel" title="Water wheel">water wheel</a> and horses as the main sources of power for British industry, thereby freeing it from geographical constraints and becoming one of the main drivers in the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>.
</p><p>Watt was also concerned with fundamental research on the functioning of the steam engine. His most notable measuring device, still in use today, is the Watt <a href="/wiki/Indicator_diagram" title="Indicator diagram">indicator</a> incorporating a <a href="/wiki/Manometer" class="mw-redirect" title="Manometer">manometer</a> to measure steam pressure within the cylinder according to the position of the piston, enabling a diagram to be produced representing the pressure of the steam as a function of its volume throughout the cycle.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preserved_Watt_engines">Preserved Watt engines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watt_steam_engine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Preserved Watt engines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The oldest surviving Watt engine is <a href="/wiki/Old_Bess_(beam_engine)" title="Old Bess (beam engine)"><i>Old Bess</i></a> of 1777, now in the <a href="/wiki/Science_Museum,_London" title="Science Museum, London">Science Museum, London</a>. 
The oldest working engine in the world is the <a href="/wiki/Smethwick_Engine" title="Smethwick Engine">Smethwick Engine</a>, brought into service in May 1779 and now at <a href="/wiki/Thinktank,_Birmingham" class="mw-redirect" title="Thinktank, Birmingham">Thinktank</a> in Birmingham (formerly at the now defunct <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Science_and_Industry,_Birmingham" class="mw-redirect" title="Museum of Science and Industry, Birmingham">Museum of Science and Industry, Birmingham</a>).
The oldest still in its original engine house and still capable of doing the job for which it was installed is the 1812 Boulton and Watt engine at the <a href="/wiki/Crofton_Pumping_Station" title="Crofton Pumping Station">Crofton Pumping Station</a>. This was used to pump water for the <a href="/wiki/Kennet_and_Avon_Canal" title="Kennet and Avon Canal">Kennet and Avon Canal</a>; on certain weekends throughout the year the modern pumps are switched off and the two steam engines at Crofton still perform this function.
The oldest extant rotative steam engine, the <a href="/wiki/Whitbread_Engine" title="Whitbread Engine">Whitbread Engine</a> (from 1785, the third rotative engine ever built), is located in the <a href="/wiki/Powerhouse_Museum" title="Powerhouse Museum">Powerhouse Museum</a> in Sydney, Australia.
A Boulton-Watt engine of 1788 may be found in the <a href="/wiki/Science_Museum_(London)" class="mw-redirect" title="Science Museum (London)">Science Museum, London</a>.,<sup id="cite_ref-Science_Museum,_lap_engine,_1788_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Science_Museum,_lap_engine,_1788-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> while an 1817 <a href="/wiki/Blowing_engine" title="Blowing engine">blowing engine</a>, formerly used at the <a href="/wiki/Netherton,_West_Midlands" title="Netherton, West Midlands">Netherton</a> ironworks of M W Grazebrook now decorates Dartmouth Circus, a traffic island at the start of the <a href="/wiki/A38(M)_motorway" title="A38(M) motorway">A38(M) motorway</a> in Birmingham.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/The_Henry_Ford_Museum" class="mw-redirect" title="The Henry Ford Museum">The Henry Ford Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dearborn,_Michigan" title="Dearborn, Michigan">Dearborn, Michigan</a> houses a replica of a 1788 Watt rotative engine. It is a full-scale working model of a Boulton-Watt engine.  The American industrialist <a href="/wiki/Henry_Ford" title="Henry Ford">Henry Ford</a> commissioned the replica engine from the English manufacturer Charles Summerfield in 1932.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The museum also holds an original Boulton and Watt atmospheric pump engine, originally used for canal pumping in Birmingham,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> illustrated below, and in use in situ at the Bowyer Street pumping station<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> from 1796 until 1854, and afterwards removed to Dearborn in 1929.
</p>

<h2><span id="Watt_engine_produced_by_Hathorn.2C_Davey_and_Co"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Watt_engine_produced_by_Hathorn,_Davey_and_Co">Watt engine produced by Hathorn, Davey and Co</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watt_steam_engine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Watt engine produced by Hathorn, Davey and Co">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 1880s, Hathorn Davey and Co / Leeds produced a 1&#160;hp / 125 rpm atmospheric engine with external condenser but without steam expansion. It has been argued that this was probably the last commercial atmospheric engine to be manufactured. As an atmospheric engine, it did not have a pressurized boiler. It was intended for small businesses.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Daveys_engine_1885.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Daveys_engine_1885.jpg/220px-Daveys_engine_1885.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="342" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Daveys_engine_1885.jpg/330px-Daveys_engine_1885.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Daveys_engine_1885.jpg/440px-Daveys_engine_1885.jpg 2x" data-file-width="544" data-file-height="845" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Daveys_engine_1885.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Daveys Engine 1885</div></div></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_developments">Recent developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watt_steam_engine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Recent developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Watt's Expansion Engine is generally considered as of historic interest only. There are however some recent developments which may lead to a renaissance of the technology. Today, there is an enormous amount of waste steam and <a href="/wiki/Waste_heat" title="Waste heat">waste heat</a> with temperatures between 100 and 150&#160;°C generated by industry. In addition, solarthermal collectors, geothermal energy sources and biomass reactors produce heat in this temperature range. There are technologies to utilise this energy, in particular the <a href="/wiki/Organic_Rankine_cycle" title="Organic Rankine cycle">Organic Rankine Cycle</a>. In principle, these are steam turbines which do not use water but a fluid (a refrigerant) which evaporates at temperatures below 100&#160;°C. Such systems are however fairly complex. They work with pressures of 6 to 20 bars, so that the whole system has to be completely sealed.
</p><p>The Expansion Engine can offer significant advantages here, in particular for lower power ratings of 2 to 100&#160;kW: with expansion ratios of 1:5, the theoretical efficiency reaches 15%, which is in the range of ORC systems. The Expansion Engine uses water as working fluid which is simple, cheap, not toxic, nonflammable and not corrosive. It works at pressure near and below atmospheric, so that sealing is not a problem. And it is a simple machine, implying cost effectiveness. Researchers from the University of Southampton / UK are currently developing a modern version of Watt's engine in order to generate energy from waste steam and waste heat. They improved the theory, demonstrating that theoretical efficiencies of up to 17.4% (and actual efficiencies of 11%) are possible.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:25_Watt_Condensing_Engine_Model_2016.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/25_Watt_Condensing_Engine_Model_2016.jpg/220px-25_Watt_Condensing_Engine_Model_2016.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="160" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/25_Watt_Condensing_Engine_Model_2016.jpg/330px-25_Watt_Condensing_Engine_Model_2016.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/25_Watt_Condensing_Engine_Model_2016.jpg/440px-25_Watt_Condensing_Engine_Model_2016.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4400" data-file-height="3194" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:25_Watt_Condensing_Engine_Model_2016.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The 25 Watt Experimental Condensing Engine built and tested at Southampton University</div></div></div>
<p>In order to demonstrate the principle, a 25 watt experimental model engine was built and tested. The engine incorporates steam expansion as well as new features such as electronic control. The picture shows the model built and tested in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Currently, a project to build and test a scaled-up 2&#160;kW engine is under preparation.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
