<p>The <b>Duchy of Normandy</b> grew out of the 911 <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Saint-Clair-sur-Epte" title="Treaty of Saint-Clair-sur-Epte">Treaty of Saint-Clair-sur-Epte</a> between <a href="/wiki/Charles_the_Simple" title="Charles the Simple">King Charles III</a> of <a href="/wiki/West_Francia" title="West Francia">West Francia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rollo" title="Rollo">Rollo</a>, leader of the <a href="/wiki/Vikings" title="Vikings">Vikings</a>. The duchy was named for its inhabitants, the <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Normans</a>.
</p><p>From 1066 until 1204 it was held by the <a href="/wiki/List_of_English_monarchs" title="List of English monarchs">kings of England</a>, except for the brief rule of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Curthose" title="Robert Curthose">Robert Curthose</a> (1087–1106), eldest son of <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a> but unsuccessful claimant to the English throne; and <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Plantagenet,_Count_of_Anjou" title="Geoffrey Plantagenet, Count of Anjou">Geoffrey Plantagenet</a> (1144–1150), husband of <a href="/wiki/Empress_Matilda" title="Empress Matilda">Empress Matilda</a> and father of <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England" title="Henry II of England">Henry II</a>.
</p><p>In 1202, <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_France" title="Philip II of France">Philip II of France</a> declared <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a> forfeit to him and seized it by force of arms in 1204. It remained disputed territory until the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_(1259)" title="Treaty of Paris (1259)">Treaty of Paris</a> of 1259, when the English sovereign ceded his claim except for the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a>; i.e., the Bailiwicks of <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a>, and their dependencies (including <a href="/wiki/Sark" title="Sark">Sark</a>).
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_France" title="Kingdom of France">Kingdom of France</a>, the duchy was occasionally set apart as an <a href="/wiki/Appanage" title="Appanage">appanage</a> to be ruled by a member of the royal family. After 1469, however, it was permanently united to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_domain" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal domain">royal domain</a>, although the title was occasionally conferred as an honorific upon junior members of the royal family. The last French duke of Normandy in this sense was <a href="/wiki/Louis_XVII_of_France" title="Louis XVII of France">Louis-Charles</a>, duke from 1785 to 1789.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duchy_of_Normandy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duchy_of_Normandy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Viking" class="mw-redirect" title="Viking">Viking</a> attack up the <a href="/wiki/River_Seine" class="mw-redirect" title="River Seine">river Seine</a> took place in 820. By 911, the area had been raided many times and there were even small Viking settlements on the lower Seine. The text of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Saint-Clair-sur-Epte" title="Treaty of Saint-Clair-sur-Epte">Treaty of Saint-Clair-sur-Epte</a> has not survived. It is only known through the historian <a href="/wiki/Dudo_of_Saint-Quentin" title="Dudo of Saint-Quentin">Dudo of Saint-Quentin</a>, who was writing a century after the event. The exact date of the treaty is unknown, but it was likely in the autumn of 911. By the agreement, <a href="/wiki/Charles_the_Simple" title="Charles the Simple">Charles III</a>, king of the <a href="/wiki/West_Franks" class="mw-redirect" title="West Franks">West Franks</a>, granted to the Viking leader <a href="/wiki/Rollo" title="Rollo">Rollo</a> some lands along the lower Seine that were apparently already under <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Danish</a> control. Whether Rollo himself was a Dane or a <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norwegian</a> is not known. For his part, Rollo agreed to defend the territory from other Vikings and that he and his men would convert to <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Renaud_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Renaud-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The territory ceded to Rollo comprised the <i><a href="/wiki/Pagus" title="Pagus">pagi</a></i> of the <a href="/wiki/Pays_de_Caux" title="Pays de Caux">Caux</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%89vrecin" class="mw-redirect" title="Évrecin">Évrecin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roumois" title="Roumois">Roumois</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Talou&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Talou (page does not exist)">Talou</a>. This was territory formerly known as the county of Rouen, and which would become <a href="/wiki/Upper_Normandy" title="Upper Normandy">Upper Normandy</a>. A royal diploma of 918 confirms the donation of 911, using the verb <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/adnuo" class="extiw" title="wikt:adnuo">adnuo</a></i> ("I grant"). There is no evidence that Rollo owed any service or oath to the king for his lands, nor that there were any legal means for the king to take them back: they were granted outright.<sup id="cite_ref-Renaud_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Renaud-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Likewise, Rollo does not seem to have been created a count or given comital authority, but later <a href="/wiki/Saga" title="Saga">sagas</a> refer to him as <i>Rúðujarl</i> (earl of Rouen).<sup id="cite_ref-Helmerichs_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Helmerichs-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 924, King <a href="/wiki/Rudolph_of_France" title="Rudolph of France">Radulf</a> extended Rollo's county westward up to the <a href="/wiki/River_Vire" class="mw-redirect" title="River Vire">river Vire</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Bessin" title="Bessin">Bessin</a>, where some Danes from England had settled not long before. In 933, King Radulf granted the <a href="/wiki/Avranchin" title="Avranchin">Avranchin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cotentin" class="mw-redirect" title="Cotentin">Cotentin</a> to Rollo's son and successor, <a href="/wiki/William_I_Longsword" class="mw-redirect" title="William I Longsword">William Longsword</a>. These areas had been previously under <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Brittany" title="Duchy of Brittany">Breton</a> rule. The northern Cotentin had been settled by Norwegians coming from the region of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Sea" title="Irish Sea">Irish Sea</a>. There was initially much hostility between these Norwegian settlers and their new Danish overlords. These expansions brought the boundaries of Normandy roughly in line with those of the <a href="/wiki/Archdiocese_of_Rouen" class="mw-redirect" title="Archdiocese of Rouen">ecclesiastical province of Rouen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Renaud_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Renaud-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Norman polity had to contend with the Frankish and Breton systems of power that already existed in Normandy.  In the early 10th century, Normandy was not a political or monetary unit.  According to many academics, "the formation of a new aristocracy, monastic reform, episcopal revival, written bureaucracy, saints’ cults – with necessarily different timelines" were as important if not more than the ducal narrative espoused by Dudo.  The formation of the Norman state also coincided with the creation of an origin myth for the Norman ducal family through Dudo, such as Rollo being compared to a "good pagan" like the Trojan hero Aeneas.  Through this narrative, the Normans were assimilated closer to the Frankish core as they moved away from their pagan Scandinavian origins.<sup id="cite_ref-Abrams_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Abrams-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Norse_settlement">Norse settlement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duchy_of_Normandy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Norse settlement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There were two distinct patterns of Norse settlement in the duchy. In the Danish area in the <a href="/wiki/Roumois" title="Roumois">Roumois</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pays_de_Caux" title="Pays de Caux">Caux</a>, settlers intermingled with the indigenous <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Romance" class="mw-redirect" title="Gallo-Romance">Gallo-Romance</a>-speaking population. Rollo shared out the large estates with his companions and gave agricultural land to his other followers. Danish settlers cleared their own land to farm it, and there was no segregation of populations.<sup id="cite_ref-Renaud_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Renaud-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the northern <a href="/wiki/Cotentin_Peninsula" title="Cotentin Peninsula">Cotentin</a> on the other hand, the population was purely Norwegian. Coastal features bore Norse names as did the three <i>pagi</i> of Haga, Sarnes and Helganes (as late as 1027). The Norwegians may even have set up a <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%9Eing" class="mw-redirect" title="Þing">þing</a></i>, an assembly of all free men, whose meeting place may be preserved in the name of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Le_Tingland&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Le Tingland (page does not exist)">Le Tingland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Renaud_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Renaud-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Within a few generations of the founding of Normandy in 911, however, the Scandinavian settlers had intermarried with the natives and adopted much of their culture. But in 911, Normandy was not a political nor monetary unit. Frankish culture remained dominant and according to some scholars, 10th century Normandy was characterized by a diverse Scandinavian population interacting with the "local Frankish matrix" that existed in the region.  In the end, the Normans stressed assimilation with the local population.<sup id="cite_ref-Abrams_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Abrams-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In the 11th century, the anonymous author of the <i><a href="/wiki/Wulfram_of_Sens" title="Wulfram of Sens">Miracles of Saint Wulfram</a></i> referred to the formation of a Norman identity as "shaping [of] all races into one single people".<sup id="cite_ref-Renaud_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Renaud-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to some historians, the idea of "Norman" as a political identity was a deliberate creation of the court of Richard I in the 960s as a way to "to create a powerful if rather incoherent sense of group solidarity to galvanize the duchy's disparate elites around the duke".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Norman_Duchy">Norman Duchy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duchy_of_Normandy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Norman Duchy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Starting with Rollo, Normandy was ruled by an enduring and long-lived Viking dynasty. Illegitimacy was not a bar to succession and three of the first six rulers of Normandy were illegitimate sons of concubines. Rollo's successor, William Longsword managed in expanding his domain and came into conflict with <a href="/wiki/Arnulf_I,_Count_of_Flanders" title="Arnulf I, Count of Flanders">Arnulf of Flanders</a>, who had him assassinated in 942.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> This lead to a crisis in Normandy, with a minor succeeding as <a href="/wiki/Richard_I_of_Normandy" title="Richard I of Normandy">Richard I</a> and also lead to a temporary revival of <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse_religion" title="Old Norse religion">Norse paganism</a> in Normandy.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Richard I's son, <a href="/wiki/Richard_II,_Duke_of_Normandy" title="Richard II, Duke of Normandy">Richard II</a> was the first to be styled <i>duke</i> of Normandy, the ducal title becoming established between 987 and 1006.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Norman dukes created the most powerful, consolidated duchy in Western Europe between the years 980, when the dukes helped place <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Capet" title="Hugh Capet">Hugh Capet</a> on the French throne, and 1050.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Scholarly churchmen were brought into Normandy from the Rhineland, and they built and endowed monasteries and supported monastic schools. thus helping to integrate distant territories into a wider framework.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The dukes imposed heavy feudal burdens on the ecclesiastical <a href="/wiki/Fief" title="Fief">fiefs</a>, which supplied the armed knights that enabled the dukes to control the restive lay lords but whose bastards could not inherit. By the mid-11th century the Duke of Normandy could count on more than 300 armed and mounted knights from his ecclesiastical vassals alone.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> By the 1020s the dukes were able to impose <a href="/wiki/Vassal" title="Vassal">vassalage</a> on the lay nobility as well. Until Richard II, the Norman rulers did not hesitate to call Viking mercenaries for help to get rid of their enemies around Normandy, such as the king of the Francs himself. <a href="/wiki/Olaf_II_of_Norway" title="Olaf II of Norway">Olaf Haraldsson</a> crossed the Channel in such circumstances to support Richard II in the conflict against the count of <a href="/wiki/Chartres" title="Chartres">Chartres</a> and was baptized in Rouen in 1014.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rollo_statue_in_falaise.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Rollo_statue_in_falaise.JPG/250px-Rollo_statue_in_falaise.JPG" decoding="async" width="250" height="333" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Rollo_statue_in_falaise.JPG/375px-Rollo_statue_in_falaise.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Rollo_statue_in_falaise.JPG/500px-Rollo_statue_in_falaise.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2112" data-file-height="2816" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rollo_statue_in_falaise.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Statue of <a href="/wiki/Rollo_of_Normandy" class="mw-redirect" title="Rollo of Normandy">Rollo</a>, founder of the fiefdom of Normandy, standing in <a href="/wiki/Falaise,_Calvados" title="Falaise, Calvados">Falaise, Calvados</a>, birthplace of his descendant <a href="/wiki/William_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="William I of England">William the Conqueror</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Normandy" title="Duke of Normandy">Duke of Normandy</a> who became <a href="/wiki/King_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="King of England">King of England</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>In 1066, <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">Duke William</a> defeated <a href="/wiki/Harold_II_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Harold II of England">Harold II of England</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hastings" title="Battle of Hastings">Battle of Hastings</a> and was subsequently crowned King of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, through the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">Norman conquest of England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Normans" title="Anglo-Normans">Anglo-Norman</a> and French relations became complicated after the Norman Conquest. The Norman dukes retained control of their holdings in Normandy as <a href="/wiki/Vassal" title="Vassal">vassals</a> owing <a href="/wiki/Fealty" title="Fealty">fealty</a> to the King of France, but they were his equals as kings of England. From 1154 until 1214, with the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Angevin_Empire" title="Angevin Empire">Angevin Empire</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Angevin_kings_of_England" title="Angevin kings of England">Angevin kings of England</a> controlled half of France and all of England, dwarfing the power of the French king, yet the Angevins were still technically French vassals.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Duchy remained part of the Anglo-Norman realm until 1204,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> when <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_France" title="Philip II of France">Philip II of France</a> conquered the continental lands of the Duchy, which became part of the royal domain. The English sovereigns continued to claim them until the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_(1259)" title="Treaty of Paris (1259)">Treaty of Paris (1259)</a> but in fact kept only the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Having little confidence in the loyalty of the Normans, Philip installed French administrators and built a powerful fortress, the <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_de_Rouen" class="mw-redirect" title="Château de Rouen">Château de Rouen</a>, as a symbol of royal power.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="French_appanage">French appanage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duchy_of_Normandy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: French appanage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Normandy" title="History of Normandy">History of Normandy</a></div>
<p>Although within the royal demesne, Normandy retained some specificity. <a href="/wiki/Norman_law" title="Norman law">Norman law</a> continued to serve as the basis for court decisions. In 1315, faced with the constant encroachments of royal power on the liberties of Normandy, the barons and towns pressed the Norman Charter on the king. This document did not provide autonomy to the province but protected it against arbitrary royal acts. The judgments of the <a href="/wiki/Exchequer_of_Normandy" title="Exchequer of Normandy">Exchequer, the main court of Normandy</a>, were declared final. This meant that Paris could not reverse a judgment of Rouen.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Another important concession was that the King of France could not raise a new tax without the consent of the Normans. However the charter, granted at a time when royal authority was faltering, was violated several times thereafter when the monarchy had regained its power.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Duchy of Normandy survived mainly by the intermittent installation of a duke. In practice, the King of France sometimes gave that portion of his kingdom to a close member of his family, who then did homage to the king. <a href="/wiki/Philip_VI_of_France" title="Philip VI of France">Philippe VI</a> made <a href="/wiki/John_II_of_France" title="John II of France">Jean</a>, his eldest son and heir to his throne, the Duke of Normandy. In turn, Jean II appointed his heir, <a href="/wiki/Charles_V_of_France" title="Charles V of France">Charles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1465, <a href="/wiki/Louis_XI_of_France" title="Louis XI of France">Louis XI</a> was forced by the <a href="/wiki/League_of_the_Public_Weal" title="League of the Public Weal">League of the Public Weal</a> to cede the duchy to his eighteen-year-old brother, <a href="/wiki/Charles_de_Valois,_Duc_de_Berry" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles de Valois, Duc de Berry">Charles de Valois</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> This concession was a problem for the king since Charles was the puppet of the king's enemies. Normandy could thus serve as a basis for rebellion against the royal power. In 1469, therefore, Louis XI convinced his brother under duress to exchange Normandy for the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Aquitaine" title="Duke of Aquitaine">Duchy of Guyenne</a> (Aquitaine).<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Finally, at the request of the cowed <a href="/w/index.php?title=Estates_of_Normandy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Estates of Normandy (page does not exist)">Estates of Normandy</a> and to signify that the duchy would not be ceded again, at a session of the <a href="/wiki/Norman_Exchequer" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman Exchequer">Norman Exchequer</a> on 9 November 1469 the ducal ring was placed on an anvil and smashed.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Philippe_de_Commynes" class="mw-redirect" title="Philippe de Commynes">Philippe de Commynes</a> expressed what was probably a common Norman thought of the time: "It has always seemed good to the Normans and still does that their great duchy really should require a duke" (<i>A tousjours bien semblé aux Normands et faict encores que si grand duchié comme la leur requiert bien un duc</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Louis_XVII_of_France" title="Louis XVII of France">Dauphin Louis Charles</a>, the second son of <a href="/wiki/Louis_XVI_of_France" title="Louis XVI of France">Louis XVI</a>, was again given the nominal title of 'Duke of Normandy' before the death of his elder brother in 1789.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Law">Law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duchy_of_Normandy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Norman_law" title="Norman law">Norman law</a></div>
<p>There are traces of Scandinavian law in the customary laws of Normandy, which were first written down in the 13th century.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> A charter of 1050, listing several pleas before Duke <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William II</a>, refers to the penalty of banishment as <i>ullac</i> (from <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> <i>útlagr</i>). The word was still current in the 12th century, when it was used in the <i><a href="/wiki/Roman_de_Rou" title="Roman de Rou">Roman de Rou</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Marriage <i><a href="/wiki/More_danico" title="More danico">more danico</a></i> ("in the Danish manner"), that is, without any ecclesiastical ceremony in accordance with old Norse custom, was recognised as legal in Normandy and in the Norman church. The first three dukes of Normandy all practised it.<sup id="cite_ref-Renaud_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Renaud-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Scandinavian influence is especially apparent in laws relating to waters. The duke possessed the <i><a href="/wiki/Jus_naufragii" title="Jus naufragii">droit de varech</a></i> (from <a href="/wiki/Old_Danish" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Danish">Old Danish</a> <i>vrek</i>), the right to all shipwrecks.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> He also had a monopoly on whale and sturgeon. A similar monopoly belonged to the Danish king in the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_Jutland" class="mw-redirect" title="Law of Jutland">Jutlandic law</a> of 1241. Remarkably, whale and sturgeon still belong to the monarch in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> in the twenty-first century, as <a href="/wiki/Royal_fish" title="Royal fish">royal fish</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The Norman Latin terms for whalers (<i>valmanni</i>, from <i>hvalmenn</i>) and whaling station (<i>valseta</i>, from <i>hvalmannasetr</i>) both derive from Old Norse. Likewise, fishing in Normandy seems to have come under Scandinavian rules. A charter of 1030 uses the term <i>fisigardum</i> (from Old Norse <i>fiskigarðr</i>) for "fisheries", a term also found in the <a href="/wiki/Scanian_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Scanian law">Scanian law</a> of <i>c</i>. 1210.<sup id="cite_ref-Renaud_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Renaud-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There is no surviving reference to the <i><a href="/wiki/Hir%C3%B0" class="mw-redirect" title="Hirð">hirð</a></i> or the <i><a href="/wiki/Lei%C3%B0angr" class="mw-redirect" title="Leiðangr">leiðangr</a></i> in Normandy, but the latter probably existed. The surname Huscaille, first attested in 1263, probably derives from <i><a href="/wiki/H%C3%BAskarl" class="mw-redirect" title="Húskarl">húskarl</a></i>, but is late evidence for the existence of a <i>hirð</i> in the 10th century.<sup id="cite_ref-Renaud_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Renaud-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
