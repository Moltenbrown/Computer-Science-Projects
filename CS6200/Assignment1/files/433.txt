<a href="/wiki/File:046CupolaSPietro.jpg" class="image"><img alt="046CupolaSPietro.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/046CupolaSPietro.jpg/16px-046CupolaSPietro.jpg" decoding="async" width="16" height="12" class="noviewer" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/046CupolaSPietro.jpg/24px-046CupolaSPietro.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/046CupolaSPietro.jpg/32px-046CupolaSPietro.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="600" /></a> <a href="/wiki/Portal:Catholicism" class="mw-redirect" title="Portal:Catholicism">Catholicism&#32;portal</a><br /><a href="/wiki/File:P_christianity.svg" class="image"><img alt="P christianity.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/P_christianity.svg/16px-P_christianity.svg.png" decoding="async" width="16" height="14" class="noviewer" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/P_christianity.svg/24px-P_christianity.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/P_christianity.svg/32px-P_christianity.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="360" /></a> <a href="/wiki/Portal:Christianity" title="Portal:Christianity">Christianity&#32;portal</a></td></tr><tr><td style="text-align:right;font-size:115%"><div class="plainlinks hlist navbar mini"><ul><li class="nv-view"><a href="/wiki/Template:Particular_churches_sui_iuris" title="Template:Particular churches sui iuris"><abbr title="View this template">v</abbr></a></li><li class="nv-talk"><a href="/wiki/Template_talk:Particular_churches_sui_iuris" title="Template talk:Particular churches sui iuris"><abbr title="Discuss this template">t</abbr></a></li><li class="nv-edit"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Template:Particular_churches_sui_iuris&amp;action=edit"><abbr title="Edit this template">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The <b>East Syriac Rite</b> or <b>East Syrian Rite</b>, also called <i>Assyrian Rite</i>, <i>Persian Rite</i>, <i>Chaldean Rite</i>, or <i>Syro-Oriental Rite</i>, is an <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Christian" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Christian">Eastern Christian</a> <a href="/wiki/Liturgical_rite" class="mw-redirect" title="Liturgical rite">liturgical rite</a> that uses the <a href="/wiki/East_Syriac_dialect" class="mw-redirect" title="East Syriac dialect">East Syriac</a> dialect as its <a href="/wiki/Liturgical_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Liturgical language">liturgical language</a>. It is one of two main liturgical rites of <a href="/wiki/Syriac_Christianity" title="Syriac Christianity">Syriac Christianity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It originated in <a href="/wiki/Osroene" title="Osroene">Edessa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>, and was historically used in the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_East" title="Church of the East">Church of the East</a>, the largest branch of Christianity which operated primarily east of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, with pockets of adherents as far as <a href="/wiki/South_India" title="South India">South India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inner_Asia" title="Inner Asia">Inner Asia</a> and strongest in the <a href="/wiki/Sasanian_Empire" title="Sasanian Empire">Sasanian (Persian) Empire</a>. The Church of the East traces its origins to the 1st century when <a href="/wiki/Thomas_the_Apostle" title="Thomas the Apostle">Thomas the Apostle</a> and his disciples, <a href="/wiki/Thaddeus_of_Edessa" title="Thaddeus of Edessa">Mar Addai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Mari" title="Saint Mari">Mar Mari</a>, brought the faith to ancient Mesopotamia, now modern <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>, the eastern parts of <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>, southeastern <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, and regions along the <a href="/wiki/Syria%E2%80%93Turkey_border" title="Syria–Turkey border">Turkish–Syrian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iran%E2%80%93Iraq_border" title="Iran–Iraq border">Iran–Iraq</a> borders.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The East Syriac rite remains in use in churches descended from the Church of the East, namely the <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_Church_of_the_East" title="Assyrian Church of the East">Assyrian Church of the East</a> of <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> (including its archdiocese the <a href="/wiki/Chaldean_Syrian_Church" title="Chaldean Syrian Church">Chaldean Syrian Church</a> of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Church_of_the_East" title="Ancient Church of the East">Ancient Church of the East</a>, as well as in the two <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Catholic">Eastern Catholic</a> churches, the <a href="/wiki/Chaldean_Catholic_Church" title="Chaldean Catholic Church">Chaldean Catholic Church</a> of Iraq and the <a href="/wiki/Syro-Malabar_Catholic_Church" title="Syro-Malabar Catholic Church">Syro-Malabar Church</a> of India, which are both now in <a href="/wiki/Full_communion" title="Full communion">full communion</a> with the <a href="/wiki/See_of_Rome" class="mw-redirect" title="See of Rome">See of Rome</a>.
</p><p>Although the ancient Church of the East and the Catholic Church split in 431 AD through the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Ephesus" title="Council of Ephesus">Council of Ephesus</a>, in 1994 the Assyrian Church Patriarch <a href="/wiki/Dinkha_IV" title="Dinkha IV">Mar Dinkha IV</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">Pope John Paul II</a> signed a common declaration in the Vatican. The <a href="/wiki/Common_Christological_Declaration_Between_the_Catholic_Church_and_the_Assyrian_Church_of_the_East" title="Common Christological Declaration Between the Catholic Church and the Assyrian Church of the East">Common Christological Declaration</a> (1994) document asserted that the split that occurred due to the Council of Ephesus in 431 was "due in large part to misunderstandings," affirmed for both that "Christ is true God and true man," recognized "each other as sister Churches" and vowed to resolve remaining differences. In 2001, the committee established from the 1994 dialogue drew up guidelines for mutual admission to the Eucharist between the Chaldean Catholic Church and the Assyrian Church of the East, overcoming all other issues.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Syriac_Rite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Versions of the East Syriac Rite are currently used by:
</p>
<ul><li>Some bodies descended from the <b>Church of the East</b> including:
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Assyrian_Church_of_the_East" title="Assyrian Church of the East">Holy Apostolic Catholic Assyrian Church of the East</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Chaldean_Syrian_Church" title="Chaldean Syrian Church">Chaldean Syrian Church of India</a> based in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, an archbishopric of the Assyrian Church of the East.</li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ancient_Church_of_the_East" title="Ancient Church of the East">Ancient Church of the East</a> based in Iraq, an autocephalous Eastern church, that separated from the Assyrian Church of the East in 1964.</li></ul></li>
<li>Some <b>Eastern Catholic</b> bodies including:
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Chaldean_Catholic_Church" title="Chaldean Catholic Church">Chaldean Catholic Church</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>, in full communion with the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a> of Rome.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Syro-Malabar_Catholic_Church" title="Syro-Malabar Catholic Church">Syro-Malabar Catholic Church</a>  based in India, in full communion with the Holy See of Rome.</li></ul></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scope_of_usage">Scope of usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Syriac_Rite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Scope of usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bishop_Elias_of_Geogtapa_(1831).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Bishop_Elias_of_Geogtapa_%281831%29.jpg/220px-Bishop_Elias_of_Geogtapa_%281831%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="426" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Bishop_Elias_of_Geogtapa_%281831%29.jpg/330px-Bishop_Elias_of_Geogtapa_%281831%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c4/Bishop_Elias_of_Geogtapa_%281831%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="774" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bishop_Elias_of_Geogtapa_(1831).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illustration of Mar Elias, a bishop of the Church of the East, from the 18th–19th century</div></div></div>
<p>Variety of terms used as designations for this rite reflects its complex history and consequent denominational diversity. Common term <i>East Syriac Rite</i> is based on the liturgical use of <a href="/wiki/East_Syriac_dialect" class="mw-redirect" title="East Syriac dialect">East Syriac</a> dialect, while other terms reflect particular historical and denominational characteristics.
</p><p>The Syrian and Mesopotamian (Iraqi) Eastern Catholics are now commonly called <a href="/wiki/Chaldean_Catholic_Church" title="Chaldean Catholic Church">Chaldeans</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Chaldean_Assyrians" class="mw-redirect" title="Chaldean Assyrians">Assyro-Chaldeans</a>). The term <i>Chaldean</i>, which in <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">Syriac</a> generally meant magician or astrologer, denoted in Latin and other European languages (Greater) Syrian nationality, and the <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">Syriac</a> or Aramaic language. For Aramaic, it especially refers to that form which is found in certain chapters of Daniel. This usage continued until the Latin missionaries at Mosul in the seventeenth century adopted it to distinguish the Catholics of the East Syriac Rite from those of the <a href="/wiki/West_Syriac_Rite" title="West Syriac Rite">West Syriac Rite</a>, which they call "Syrians". It is also used to distinguish from the <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_Church_of_the_East" title="Assyrian Church of the East">Assyrian Church of the East</a>, some of whom call themselves <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_people" title="Assyrian people">Assyrians</a> or <a href="/w/index.php?title=Surayi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Surayi (page does not exist)">Surayi</a>, and even "Christians" only, though they do not repudiate the theological name "<a href="/wiki/Nestorian" class="mw-redirect" title="Nestorian">Nestorian</a>". Modern members of the Assyrian Church of the East and the Ancient Church of the East distinguish themselves from the rest of <a href="/wiki/Christendom" title="Christendom">Christendom</a> as the "<a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_East" title="Church of the East">Church of the East</a>" or "Easterns" as opposed to "Westerns", by which they denote Orthodox, <a href="/wiki/Syriac_Orthodox" class="mw-redirect" title="Syriac Orthodox">Syriac Orthodox</a> or Syrians.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In recent times they have been called, chiefly by the Anglicans, the "Assyrian Church", a name which can be defended on archaeological grounds. Brightman, in his "Liturgies Eastern and Western", includes Chaldean and Malabar Catholics and Assyrians under "Persian Rite".<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The catalogue of liturgies in the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a> has adopted the usual Roman Catholic nomenclature:
</p>
<ul><li>Chaldean Rite&#160;: that of the <a href="/wiki/Chaldean_Catholic_Church" title="Chaldean Catholic Church">Chaldean Catholic Church</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_Church_of_the_East" title="Assyrian Church of the East">Assyrian Church of the East</a></li>
<li>Malabar Rite (southern India)&#160;: <a href="/wiki/Syro-Malabar_Catholic_Church" title="Syro-Malabar Catholic Church">Syro-Malabar Catholic Church</a></li>
<li>Syriac Rite&#160;: Syriac Orthodox and <a href="/wiki/Syriac_Catholic_Church" title="Syriac Catholic Church">Syriac Catholic Church</a></li></ul>
<p>Most printed liturgies of these rites are Eastern Rite Catholic.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The language of all three forms of the East Syriac Rite is the Eastern dialect of <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">Syriac</a>, a modern form of which is still spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_Church_of_the_East" title="Assyrian Church of the East">Assyrian Church of the East</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Church_of_the_East" title="Ancient Church of the East">Ancient Church of the East</a> (which broke away from the Assyrian Church of the East in the 1960s due to a dispute involving changes to the liturgical calendar, but is now in the process of reunification),<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and the Chaldean Catholic Church.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Syriac_Rite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Chaldean rite originally grew out of the Jerusalem–Antioch liturgy. The tradition, resting on the legend of Abgar and of his correspondence with Christ, which has been shown to be apocryphal — is to the effect that <a href="/wiki/Saint_Thomas_the_Apostle" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Thomas the Apostle">St. Thomas the Apostle</a>, on his way to India, established Christianity in Mesopotamia, Assyria, and Persia, and left <a href="/wiki/Thaddeus_of_Edessa" title="Thaddeus of Edessa">Thaddeus of Edessa</a> (or Addai), "one of the Seventy", and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Mari" title="Saint Mari">Saint Mari</a> in charge there. The liturgy of the Church of the East is attributed to these two, but it is said to have been revised by the Patriarch <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yeshuyab_III&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Yeshuyab III (page does not exist)">Yeshuyab III</a> in about 650. Some, however, consider this liturgy to be a development of the Antiochian.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/First_Council_of_Ephesus" class="mw-redirect" title="First Council of Ephesus">First Council of Ephesus</a> (431) -- the third <a href="/wiki/First_seven_Ecumenical_Councils" class="mw-redirect" title="First seven Ecumenical Councils">Ecumenical Council</a>—the Church of Seleucia-Ctesiphon, which had hitherto been governed by a <a href="/wiki/Catholicos" title="Catholicos">catholicos</a>, refused to condemn <a href="/wiki/Nestorius" title="Nestorius">Nestorius</a>. Therefore, As part of the <a href="/wiki/Nestorian_Schism" title="Nestorian Schism">Nestorian Schism</a>, the Church of Seleucia-Ctesiphon cut itself off from Orthodox Christianity. In 498 the Catholicos assumed the title of "<a href="/wiki/Patriarch_of_the_East" class="mw-redirect" title="Patriarch of the East">Patriarch of the East</a>", and up until the 1400s the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_East" title="Church of the East">Church of the East</a> spread throughout Persia, Tartary, Mongolia, China, and India due to the efforts of Missionaries.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, At the end of the fourteenth century due to the conquests of <a href="/wiki/Tamerlane" class="mw-redirect" title="Tamerlane">Tamerlane</a> and his destruction of Christian settlements across Asia. In addition to other factors such as anti-Christian and Buddhist oppression during the <a href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Ming Dynasty">Ming Dynasty</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> the large Assyrian Church structure was all but destroyed- reducing it to a few small communities in Persia, their homeland in Mesopotamia, Cyprus, the <a href="/wiki/Malabar_Coast" title="Malabar Coast">Malabar Coast</a> of India, and the Island of <a href="/wiki/Socotra" title="Socotra">Socotra</a>. These remaining communities were later whittled away at in other events. The Church of the East in Cyprus united themselves to Rome in 1445, there was a <a href="/wiki/Schism_of_1552" title="Schism of 1552">Schism in 1552</a> between <a href="/wiki/Shimun_VIII_Yohannan_Sulaqa" title="Shimun VIII Yohannan Sulaqa">Mar Shimun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Patriarch_Elias_of_Antioch" class="mw-redirect" title="Patriarch Elias of Antioch">Mar Elia</a> which weakened the Church, the Christians of Socotra were Islamized in the 16th century, The Assyrian Church in India was divided and cut off from their hierarchy due to the Portuguese supported <a href="/wiki/Synod_of_Diamper" title="Synod of Diamper">Synod of Diamper</a> in 1599, and the Eliya line of the Assyrian Church of the East joined the Chaldean Catholic Church in 1830. Due to these events, the Church of the East was turned into a small community of around 50,000 people in the <a href="/wiki/Hakkari" title="Hakkari">Hakkari Mountains</a> under the headship of the Shiumn line. A small group of Indians eventually rejoined the Assyrian Church of the East, forming the <a href="/wiki/Chaldean_Syrian_Church" title="Chaldean Syrian Church">Chaldean Syrian Church</a> in the 1900s, although the main body of the <a href="/wiki/St_Thomas_Christians" class="mw-redirect" title="St Thomas Christians">Malabar Christians</a> joined Catholic or <a href="/wiki/West_Syriac_rite" class="mw-redirect" title="West Syriac rite">West Syriac rite</a> churches in their own set of schisms. Additionally, the secession of a large number to the Russian Church due to the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Ecclesiastical_Mission_in_Urmia" title="Russian Ecclesiastical Mission in Urmia">Russian Ecclesiastical Mission in Urmia</a>, a Kurdish massacre in 1843, and an attempt to form an Independent Catholic Chaldean Church on the model of the <a href="/wiki/Old_Catholics" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Catholics">Old Catholics</a> all resulted in more Eastern rite Assyrians separating.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Eucharistic_service">The Eucharistic service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Syriac_Rite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Eucharistic service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kanjirappally_Bishop_Mar_Mathew_Arackal_at_Tomb_of_Mar_Varghese_Payyappilly_Palakkappilly.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Kanjirappally_Bishop_Mar_Mathew_Arackal_at_Tomb_of_Mar_Varghese_Payyappilly_Palakkappilly.jpg/220px-Kanjirappally_Bishop_Mar_Mathew_Arackal_at_Tomb_of_Mar_Varghese_Payyappilly_Palakkappilly.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Kanjirappally_Bishop_Mar_Mathew_Arackal_at_Tomb_of_Mar_Varghese_Payyappilly_Palakkappilly.jpg/330px-Kanjirappally_Bishop_Mar_Mathew_Arackal_at_Tomb_of_Mar_Varghese_Payyappilly_Palakkappilly.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Kanjirappally_Bishop_Mar_Mathew_Arackal_at_Tomb_of_Mar_Varghese_Payyappilly_Palakkappilly.jpg/440px-Kanjirappally_Bishop_Mar_Mathew_Arackal_at_Tomb_of_Mar_Varghese_Payyappilly_Palakkappilly.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1944" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kanjirappally_Bishop_Mar_Mathew_Arackal_at_Tomb_of_Mar_Varghese_Payyappilly_Palakkappilly.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Syro-Malabar" class="mw-redirect" title="Syro-Malabar">Syro-Malabar Catholic</a> bishop holding the Mar Thoma Christian Cross which symbolizes the heritage and identity of the <a href="/wiki/Syrian_Malabar_Nasrani" class="mw-redirect" title="Syrian Malabar Nasrani">Syrian Church</a> of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Thomas_Christians" title="Saint Thomas Christians">Saint Thomas Christians</a> of <a href="/wiki/Christianity_in_India" title="Christianity in India">India</a></div></div></div>
<p>There are three <a href="/wiki/Anaphora_(liturgy)" title="Anaphora (liturgy)">Anaphorae</a>; those of <a href="/wiki/Holy_Qurbana_of_Addai_and_Mari" class="mw-redirect" title="Holy Qurbana of Addai and Mari">the Holy Apostles</a> (Saints <a href="/wiki/Addai" class="mw-redirect" title="Addai">Addai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Mari" title="Saint Mari">Mari</a>), <a href="/wiki/Nestorius" title="Nestorius">Mar Nestorius</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Theodore_the_Interpreter" class="mw-redirect" title="Theodore the Interpreter">Mar Theodore the Interpreter</a>. The first is the most popularly and extensively used. The second was traditionally used on the <a href="/wiki/Epiphany_(Christian)" class="mw-redirect" title="Epiphany (Christian)">Epiphany</a> and the feasts of <a href="/wiki/St._John_the_Baptist" class="mw-redirect" title="St. John the Baptist">St. John the Baptist</a> and of the <a href="/wiki/Greek_Doctor" class="mw-redirect" title="Greek Doctor">Greek Doctors</a>, both of which occur in Epiphany-tide on the Wednesday of the Rogation of the Ninevites, and on Maundy Thursday. The third is used (except when the second is ordered) from <a href="/wiki/Advent" title="Advent">Advent</a> to <a href="/wiki/Palm_Sunday" title="Palm Sunday">Palm Sunday</a>. The same pro-anaphoral part serves for all three.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Eucharistic Liturgy is preceded by a preparation, or "Office of the Prothesis", which includes the solemn kneading and baking of the loaves. These were traditionally leavened, the flour being mixed with a little oil and the <a href="/wiki/Holy_leaven" class="mw-redirect" title="Holy leaven">holy leaven</a> (<i>malka</i>), which, according to tradition, "was given and handed down to us by our holy fathers Mar Addai and Mar Mari and Mar Toma", and of which and of the holy oil a very strange story is told. The real leavening, however, is done by means of fermented dough (khmira) from the preparation of the last Eucharistic Liturgy. The Chaldean and Syro-Malabar Catholics now use unleavened bread.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Liturgy itself is introduced by the first verse of the Gloria in Excelsis and the Lord's prayer, with "farcings" (giyura), consisting of a form of the Sanctus. Then follow:<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Introit_Psalm" class="mw-redirect" title="Introit Psalm">Introit Psalm</a> (variable), called <i>Marmitha</i>, with a preliminary prayer, varying for Sundays and greater feasts and for "Memorials" and ferias. In the Malabar Rite, Pss. xiv, cl, and cxvi are said in alternate verses by priests and deacons.</li>
<li>The "Antiphon of the Sanctuary" (Unitha d' qanki), variable, with a similarly varying prayer.</li>
<li>The Lakhumara, an antiphon beginning "To Thee, Lord", which occurs in other services also preceded by a similarly varying prayer.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Trisagion" title="Trisagion">Trisagion</a>. Incense is used before this. In the Eastern Rite at low Mass the elements are put on the altar before the incensing.</li></ul>
<p>There are four or five Lections: (a) the Law and (b) the Prophecy, from the Old Testament, (c) the Lection from the Acts, (d) the Epistle, always from St. Paul, (e) the Gospel.
Some days have all five lections, some four, some only three. All have an Epistle and a Gospel, but, generally, when there is a Lection from Law there is none from the Acts, and vice versa. Sometimes there is none from either Law or Acts. The first three are called <i>Qiryani</i> (Lections), the third <i>Shlikha</i> (Apostle). Before the Epistle and Gospel, hymns called <i>Turgama</i> (interpretation) are, or should be, said; that before the Epistle is invariable, that of the Gospel varies with the day. They answer to the Greek prokeimena. The Turgama of the Epistle is preceded by proper psalm verses called <i>Shuraya</i> (beginning), and that of the Gospel by other proper psalm verses called <i>Zumara</i> (song). The latter includes Alleluia between the verses.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Deacon's Litany, or Eklene, called <i>Karazutha</i> (proclamation), resembles the <a href="/wiki/Ektenia" class="mw-redirect" title="Ektenia">"Great Synapte"</a> of the Greeks. During it the proper "Antiphon [Unitha] of the Gospel" is sung by the people.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Offertory">The <a href="/wiki/Offertory" title="Offertory">Offertory</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Syriac_Rite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Offertory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The deacons proclaim the expulsion of the unbaptized, and set the "hearers" to watch the doors. The priest places the bread and wine on the altar, with words (in the Church of the East, but not in the Chaldean Catholic Rite) which seem as if they were already consecrated. He sets aside a "memorial of the Virgin Mary, Mother of Christ" (Chaldean; usual Malabar Rite, "Mother of God"; but according to Raulin's Latin of the Malabar Rite, "Mother of God Himself and of the Lord Jesus Christ"), and of the patron of the Church (in the Malabar Rite, "of St.Thomas"). Then follows the proper "Antiphon of the Mysteries" (Unitha d' razi), answering to the offertory.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Creed">The Creed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Syriac_Rite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Creed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This is a variant of the <a href="/wiki/Nicene_Creed" title="Nicene Creed">Nicene Creed</a>. It is possible that the order or words "and was incarnate by the Holy Ghost and was made man, and was conceived and born of the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Mary" class="mw-redirect" title="Virgin Mary">Virgin Mary</a>", may enshrine a Nestorian idea, but the Chaldean Catholics do not seem to have noticed it, their only alteration being the addition of the <a href="/wiki/Filioque" title="Filioque">Filioque</a>. The Malabar Book has an exact translation of Latin. In Neale's translation of the Malabar Rite the Karazutha, the Offertory, and the Expulsion of the Unbaptized come before the Lections and the Creed follows immediately on the Gospel, but in the Propaganda edition of 1774 the Offertory follows the Creed, which follows the Gospel.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The first Lavabo, followed by a Kushapa ("beseeching", i.e., prayer said in kneeling) and a form of the "Orate fratres", with its response. Then the variations of the three Anaphora begin.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Kiss of Peace, preceded by a G'hantha, i.e., a prayer said with bowed head.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The prayer of Memorial (Dukhrana) of the Living and the Dead, and the Diptychs; the latter is now obsolete in the Church of the East.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Anaphora">The Anaphora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Syriac_Rite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Anaphora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As in all liturgies this begins with a form of a <a href="/wiki/Sursum_corda" title="Sursum corda">Sursum corda</a>, but the East Syriac form is more elaborate than any other, especially in the Anaphora of Theodore. Then follows the <a href="/wiki/Preface" title="Preface">Preface</a> of the usual type ending with the <a href="/wiki/Sanctus" title="Sanctus">Sanctus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Post-Sanctus (to use the Hispanico-Gallican term) is an amplification—similar in idea and often in phraseology to those in all liturgies except <a href="/wiki/Roman_Rite" title="Roman Rite">the Roman</a>—of the idea of the Sanctus into a recital of the work of Redemption, extending to some length and ending, in the Anaphorae of Nestorius and Theodore, with the recital of the Institution. In the Anaphora of the Apostles the recital of the Institution is wanting, though it has been supplied in the Anglican edition of the Church of the East book. Hammond (Liturgies Eastern and Western, p. lix) and most other writers hold that the Words of Institution belong to this Liturgy and should be supplied somewhere; Hammond (loc.cit) suggests many arguments for their former presence. The reason of their absence is uncertain. While some hold that this essential passage dropped out in times of ignorance, others say it never was there at all, being unnecessary, since the consecration was held to be effected by the subsequent Epiklesis alone. Another theory, evidently of Western origin and not quite consistent with the general Eastern theory of consecration by an Epiklesis following Christ's words, is that, being the formula of <a href="/wiki/Consecration" title="Consecration">consecration</a>, it was held too sacred to be written down. It does not seem to be quite certain whether Church of the East priests did or did not insert the Words of Institution in old times, but it seems that many of them do not do so now.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Prayer of the Great Oblation with a second memorial of the Living and the Dead, a Kushapa.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The G'hantha of the <a href="/wiki/Epiklesis" class="mw-redirect" title="Epiklesis">Epiklesis</a>, or Invocation of Holy Spirit. The Epiklesis itself is called Nithi Mar (May He come, O Lord) from its opening words. The Liturgy of the Apostles is so vague as to the purpose of the Invocation that, when the words of Institution are not said, it would be difficult to imagine this formula to be sufficient on any hypothesis, Eastern or Western. The Anaphorae of Nestorius and Theodore, besides having the Words of Institution, have definite Invocations, evidently copied from Antiochean or Byzantine forms. The older Chaldean and the Malabar Catholic books have inserted the Words of Institution with an Elevation, after the Epiklesis. But the 1901 Mosul edition puts the Words of Institution first.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Here follow a Prayer for Peace, a second Lavabo and a censing.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="The_Fraction.2C_Consignation.2C_Conjunction.2C_and_Commixture"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Fraction,_Consignation,_Conjunction,_and_Commixture">The Fraction, Consignation, Conjunction, and Commixture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Syriac_Rite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The Fraction, Consignation, Conjunction, and Commixture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Host is broken in two, and the sign of the <a href="/wiki/Cross" title="Cross">Cross</a> is made in the Chalice with one half, after which the other with the half that has been dipped in the chalice. The two halves are then reunited on the Paten. Then a cleft is made in the Host "qua parte intincta est in Sanguine" (Renaudot's tr.), and a particle is put in the chalice, after some intricate arranging on the paten.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Communion">Communion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Syriac_Rite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Communion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The veil is thrown open, the deacon exhorts the communicants to draw near, the priests breaks up the Host for distribution. Then follows the <a href="/wiki/Lord%27s_Prayer" title="Lord&#39;s Prayer">Lord's Prayer</a>, with Introduction and Embolism, and the Sancta Sanctis, and then the 'Antiphon of the Bema" (Communion) is sung. The <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">Communion</a> is in both species separately, the priest giving the Host and the deacon the Chalice. Then follows a variable antiphon of thanksgiving, a post-communion,  and a dismissal. Afterwards the Mkaprana, an unconsecrated portion of the holy loaf, is distributed to the communicants, but not, as in the case of the Greek antidoron, and as the name of the latter implies, to non-communicants. The Chaldean Catholics are communicated with the Host dipped in the Chalice. They reserve what is left of the Holy Gifts, while the Church of the East priests consume all before leaving the church.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Properly, and according to their own canons, the Church of the East ought to say Mass on every Sunday and Friday, on every festival, and daily during the first, middle, and last week of Lent and the octave of Easter. In practice it is only said on Sundays and greater festivals, at the best, and in many churches not so often, a sort of "dry Mass" being used instead. The Chaldean Catholic priests say Mass daily, and where there are many priests there will be many Masses in the same Church in one day, which is contrary to the Church of the East canons. The Anglican editions of the liturgies omit the names of heretics and call the Anaphorae of Nestorius and Theodore the "Second Hallowing" and "Third Hallowing". Otherwise there are no alterations except the addition of Words of Institution to the first Anaphorae. The recent Catholic edition has made the same alterations and substituted "Mother of God" for "Mother of Christ". In each edition the added Words of Institution follow the form of the rite of the edition. The prayers of the Mass, like those of the Orthodox Eastern Church, are generally long and diffuse. Frequently they end with a sort of doxology called Qanuna which is said aloud, the rest being recited in a low tone. The Qanuna in form and usage resembles the Greek ekphonesis.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The vestments used by the priest at Mass are the <i>Sudhra</i>, a girded alb with three crosses in red or black on the shoulder, the <i>Urara</i> (orarion) or stole worn crossed by priests, but not by bishops (as in the West), and the <i>Ma'apra</i>, a sort of linen cope. The deacon wears the sudhra, with a urara over the left shoulder.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Divine_Office">Divine Office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Syriac_Rite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Divine Office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The nucleus of this is, as it is usual, the recitation of the <a href="/wiki/Psalter" title="Psalter">Psalter</a>. There are only three regular hours of service (Evening, Midnight, and Morning) with a rarely used compline. In practice only Morning and Evening are commonly used, but these are extremely well attended daily by laity as well as clergy. When the Church of the East had monasteries (which is no longer the case) seven hours of prayer were the custom in them, and three hulali of the Psalter were recited at each. This would mean a daily recitation of the whole Psalter. The present arrangement provides for seven hulali at each ferial night service, ten on Sundays, three on "Memorials", and the whole Psalter on feasts of Our Lord.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At the evening service there is a selection of from four to seven psalms, varying with the day of the week, and also a Shuraya, or short psalm, with generally a portion of Ps. cxviii, varying with the day of the fortnight.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At the morning service the invariable psalms are cix, xc, ciii (1–6), cxii, xcii, cxlviii, cl, cxvi. On ferias and "Memorials" Ps. cxlvi is said after Ps. cxlviii, and on ferias Ps. 1, 1–18, comes at the end of the psalms. The rest of the services consist of prayers, antiphons, litanies, and verses (giyura) inserted, like the Greek stichera, but more extensively, between verses of psalms. On Sundays the Gloria in Excelsis and Benedicte are said instead of Ps. cxlvi.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Both morning and evening services end with several prayers, a blessing, (Khuthama, "Sealing" ), the kiss of peace, and the Creed. The variables, besides the psalms, are those of the feast or day, which are very few, and those of the day of the fortnight. These fortnights consist of weeks called "Before" (Qdham) and "After" (Wathar), according to which of the two choirs begins the service. Hence the book of the Divine Office is called Qdham u wathar, or at full length Kthawa daqdham wadhwathar, the "Book of Before and After".<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Liturgical_calendars">Liturgical calendars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Syriac_Rite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Liturgical calendars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:98px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Amen_in_East_Syriac_Aramaic.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ed/Amen_in_East_Syriac_Aramaic.jpg" decoding="async" width="96" height="80" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="96" data-file-height="80" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Amen_in_East_Syriac_Aramaic.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Amen" title="Amen">Amen</a> in East Syriac Aramaic</div></div></div>
<p>The year is divided into periods of about seven weeks each, called Shawu'i; these are Advent (called Subara, "Annunciation"), Ephiphany, Lent, Easter, the Apostles, Summer, "Elias and the Cross", "Moses", and the "Dedication" (Qudash idta). "Moses" and the "Dedication" have only four weeks each. The Sundays are generally named after the Shawu'a in which they occur, "Fourth Sunday of Epiphany", "Second Sunday of the Annunciation ", etc., though sometimes the name changes in the middle of a Shawu'a. Most of the "Memorials" (dukhrani), or saints' days, which have special lections, occur on the Fridays between Christmas and Lent, and are therefore movable feasts, such as Christmas, Ephiphany, the Assumption, and about thirty smaller days without proper lections are on fixed days. There are four shorter fasting periods besides the Great Fast (Lent); these are:<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>the Fast of Mar Zaya, the three days after the second Sunday of the Nativity;</li>
<li>the Fast of the Virgins, after the first Sunday of the Epiphany;</li>
<li>the Rogation of the Ninevites, seventy days before Easter;</li>
<li>the Fast of Mart Mariam (Our Lady), from the first to the fourteenth of August.</li></ul>
<p>The Fast of the Ninevites commemorates the repentance of Nineveh at the preaching of Jonas, and is carefully kept. Those of Mar Zaya and the Virgins are nearly obsolete. As compared with the Latin and Greek Calendars, that of the Chaldeans, whether Catholic or Assyrian, is very meagre. The Malabar Rite has largely adopted the Roman Calendar, and several Roman days have been added to that of the Chaldean Catholics. The Chaldean Easter coincides with that of the Roman Catholic Church.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_sacraments_and_occasional_services">Other sacraments and occasional services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Syriac_Rite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Other sacraments and occasional services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Crowning_ceremony_in_Chaldean_Syrian_Church.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Crowning_ceremony_in_Chaldean_Syrian_Church.jpg/220px-Crowning_ceremony_in_Chaldean_Syrian_Church.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Crowning_ceremony_in_Chaldean_Syrian_Church.jpg/330px-Crowning_ceremony_in_Chaldean_Syrian_Church.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Crowning_ceremony_in_Chaldean_Syrian_Church.jpg/440px-Crowning_ceremony_in_Chaldean_Syrian_Church.jpg 2x" data-file-width="960" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Crowning_ceremony_in_Chaldean_Syrian_Church.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Crowning ceremony in Chaldean Syrian Church</div></div></div>
<p>The other Sacraments in use in the Church of the East are <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">Baptism</a>, with which is always associated an <a href="/wiki/Oil_of_catechumens" title="Oil of catechumens">anointing</a>, which as in other eastern rites answers to Confirmation, <a href="/wiki/Holy_Order" class="mw-redirect" title="Holy Order">Holy Order</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matrimony" class="mw-redirect" title="Matrimony">Matrimony</a>, but not Penance or Unction of the sick.  The Chaldean Catholics now have a form not unlike the Byzantine and West Syriac. The nearest approach to Penance among the Nestorians is a form, counted as a sacrament, for the reconciliation of apostates and excommunicated persons, prayers from which are occasionally used in cases of other penitents. Assemani's arguments (ibid., cclxxxvi–viii) for a belief in Penance as a Sacrament among the ancient Nestorians or for the practice of auricular confession among the Malabar Nestorians are not conclusive. The Chaldeans have a similar form to that of the Roman Rite. The Assyrian Church of the East omits Matrimony from the list, and make up the number of the mysteries to seven by including the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Leaven" title="Holy Leaven">Holy Leaven</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sign_of_the_Cross" class="mw-redirect" title="Sign of the Cross">Sign of the Cross</a>, but they are now rather vague about the definition or numeration.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The only other rite of any interest is the consecration of churches. Oil, but not chrism, plays a considerable part in these rites, being used in Baptism, possibly in Confirmation, in the reconciliation of apostates, etc., in the consecration of churches, and the making of bread for the Eucharist. It is not used in ordination or for the sick. There are two sorts of oil; the one is ordinary olive oil, blessed or not blessed for the occasion, the other is the oil of the Holy Horn. The last, which, though really only plain oil, represents the chrism (or myron) of other rites, is believed to have been handed down from the Apostles with the Holy Leaven. The legend is that the Baptist caught the water which fell from the body of Christ at His baptism and preserved it. He gave it to St. John the Evangelist, who added to it some of the water which fell from the pierced side. At the Last Supper Jesus gave two loaves to St. John, bidding him keep one for the Holy Leaven. With this St. John mingled some of the Blood from the side of Christ. After Pentecost the Apostles mixed oil with the sacred water, and each took a horn of it, and the loaf they ground to pieces and mixed it with flour and salt to be the Holy Leaven. The Holy Horn is constantly renewed by the addition of oil blessed by a bishop on Maundy Thursday.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The baptismal service is modeled on the Eucharistic. The Mass of the Catechumens is almost identical, with of course appropriate Collects, psalms, Litanies, and Lections. After the introductory Gloria, Lord's Prayer, Marmitha (in this case Psalm 88) and its Collect, follow the imposition of hands and the signing with oil, after which follow an Antiphon of the Sanctuary and Ps. xliv, cix, cxxxi, with giyuri, Litanies, and Collects, then the lakhumara, Trisagion, and Lections (Epistle and Gospel ), and the Karazutha, after which the priest says the prayer of the imposition of hands, and the unbaptized are dismissed. An antiphon answering to that "of the mysteries" follows, and then the Creed is said. The bringing forward of the Holy Horn and the blessing of the oil take the place of the Offertory. The Anaphora is paralleled by Sursum corda, Preface, and Sanctus, a Nithi Mar, or Epiklesis, upon the oil, a commixture of the new oil with that of the Holy Horn, and the Lord's Prayer. Then the font is blessed and signed with the holy oil, and in the place of the Communion comes the Baptism itself. The children are signed with the oil on the breast and then anointed all over, and are dipped thrice in the font. The formula is: "N., be thou baptized in the name of the Father, in the name of the Son, in the name of the Holy Ghost. Amen." Then follows the post-baptismal thanksgiving. Confirmation follows immediately. There are two prayers of Confirmation and a signing between the eyes with the formula: "N., is baptized and perfected in the name, etc." It is not quite clear whether oil should be used with this signing or not. Then any oil that remains over is poured into the Holy Horn, held over the font, and the water in the font is loosed from its former consecration with rather curious ceremonies. The Chaldean Catholics have added the renunciations, profession of faith, and answers of the sponsors from the Roman Ritual, and anoint with chrism.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The marriage service (<i>Burakha</i>, 'Blessing") has nothing very distinctive about it, and resembles closely the Byzantine, and to some extent the Jewish rite.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The orders of the Church of the East are those of reader (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Qaruya&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Qaruya (page does not exist)">Qaruya</a>), subdeacon (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hiupathiaqna&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hiupathiaqna (page does not exist)">Hiupathiaqna</a>), deacon (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Shamasha&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Shamasha (page does not exist)">Shamasha</a>), priest (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Qashisha&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Qashisha (page does not exist)">Qashisha</a>), archdeacon (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Arkidhyaquna&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Arkidhyaquna (page does not exist)">Arkidhyaquna</a>) and bishop (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Apisqupa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Apisqupa (page does not exist)">Apisqupa</a>). The degree of archdeacon, though has an ordination service of its own, is only counted as a degree of the presbyterate, and is by some held to be the same as that of chorepiscopus (Kurapisqupa), which never involved episcopal ordination in the Church of the East. When a priest is engaged in sacerdotal functions, he is called Kahna (i.e., lereus; sacerdos) and a bishop is similarly Rab kahni (Chief of the Priests, <i><a href="/wiki/Archiereus" title="Archiereus">archiereus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pontifex" class="mw-redirect" title="Pontifex">pontifex</a></i>). Quashisha and Apisqupa only denote the degree. Kahnutha, priesthood, is used of the three degrees of deacon, priest, and bishop. The ordination formula is: "N. has been set apart, consecrated, and perfected to the work of the diaconate [or of the presbyterate] to the Levitical and stephanite Office [or for the office of the Aaronic priesthood], in the Name, etc., In the case of a bishop it is&#160;: "to the great work of the episcopate of the city of ..." A similar formula is used for archdeacons and metropolitans.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Consecration of churches (Siamidha or Qudash Madhbkha) consists largely of unctions. The altar is anointed all over, and there are four <a href="/wiki/Consecration_crosses" title="Consecration crosses">consecration crosses</a> on the four interior walls of the sanctuary, and these and the lintel of the door and various other places are anointed. The oil is not that of the Holy Horn, but fresh olive oil consecrated by the bishop.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manuscripts_and_editions">Manuscripts and editions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Syriac_Rite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Manuscripts and editions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Few of the manuscripts, except some lectionaries in the British Museum, were written before the 15th century, and most, whether Chaldean or Nestorian, are of the 17th and 18th. The books in use are:<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/w/index.php?title=%E1%B9%ACakhs%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ṭakhsā (page does not exist)">Ṭakhsā</a>, a priest's book, containing the Eucharistic service (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Q%C5%ABrb%C4%81n%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Qūrbānā (page does not exist)">Qūrbānā</a> or <a href="/w/index.php?title=Q%C5%ABd%C4%81%C5%A1%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Qūdāšā (page does not exist)">Qūdāšā</a>) in its three forms, with the administration of other sacraments, and various occasional prayers and blessings. It is nearly the <a href="/wiki/Euchologion" title="Euchologion">Euchologion</a> of the Greeks (see <a href="/wiki/Rite_of_Constantinople" class="mw-redirect" title="Rite of Constantinople">Rite of Constantinople</a>).</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=K%E1%B9%AF%C4%81%E1%B8%87d%C4%81_da-q%E1%B8%8Fam_wa%E1%B8%8F-w%C4%81%E1%B9%AFar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kṯāḇdā da-qḏam waḏ-wāṯar (page does not exist)">Kṯāḇdā da-qḏam waḏ-wāṯar</a>, "Book of the Before and After", contains the Ordinary of the Divine Office except the <a href="/wiki/Psalter" title="Psalter">Psalter</a>, arranged for two weeks.</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mazmor%C4%93_d-D%C4%81w%C4%AB%E1%B8%8F&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mazmorē d-Dāwīḏ (page does not exist)">Mazmorē d-Dāwīḏ</a> (David), the Psalter, divided into <a href="/w/index.php?title=H%C5%ABl%C4%81l%C4%93&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hūlālē (page does not exist)">Hūlālē</a>, which answer more or less to the <a href="/wiki/Kathismata" class="mw-redirect" title="Kathismata">kathismata</a> of the Greeks. <li><a href="/w/index.php?title=T%C5%ABrg%C4%81m%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tūrgāmā (page does not exist)">Tūrgāmā</a>, explanatory hymns sung before the Epistle and Gospel.</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=%E1%B8%A4%C5%AB%E1%B8%8Fr%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ḥūḏrā (page does not exist)">Ḥūḏrā</a>, containing the variables for Sundays, Lent and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fast_of_the_Ninevites&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Fast of the Ninevites (page does not exist)">Fast of the Ninevites</a>, and other holy days.</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ka%C5%A1k%C5%ABl&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kaškūl (page does not exist)">Kaškūl</a>, a selection from the Ḥūḏrā for weekdays.</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gazz%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gazzā (page does not exist)">Gazzā</a>, containing variables for festivals except Sundays.</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abukhalima&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Abukhalima (page does not exist)">Abukhalima</a>, a collectary, so called from its compiler, <a href="/wiki/Ignatius_Elias_III" title="Ignatius Elias III">Elias III</a>, Abu Khalim ibn alKhaditha, Metropolitan of <a href="/wiki/Nisibis" class="mw-redirect" title="Nisibis">Nisibis</a>, and patriarch (1175–99).</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C4%81%27%C5%ABtha_d-N%C4%ABnw%C4%81y%C4%93&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bā&#39;ūtha d-Nīnwāyē (page does not exist)">Bā'ūtha d-Nīnwāyē</a>, rhythmical prayers attributed to <a href="/wiki/Saint_Ephraem" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Ephraem">Saint Ephraem</a>, used during the Fast of the Ninevites.</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Takhsa_d%27amadha&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Takhsa d&#39;amadha (page does not exist)">Takhsa d'amadha</a>, the office baptism.</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burakha&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Burakha (page does not exist)">Burakha</a>, the marriage service.</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kathnita&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kathnita (page does not exist)">Kathnita</a>, the burial service for priests.</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anidha&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Anidha (page does not exist)">Anidha</a>, the burial service for lay people.</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Takhsa_d%27siamidha&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Takhsa d&#39;siamidha (page does not exist)">Takhsa d'siamidha</a>, the ordination services.</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Takhsa_d%27khusaya&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Takhsa d&#39;khusaya (page does not exist)">Takhsa d'khusaya</a>, the "Office of Pardon", or reconciliation of penitents.</li></ul>
<p>These last six are excerpts from the Takhsa.

<p>The Malabar Rite was revised in a Roman direction by <a href="/wiki/Aleixo_de_Menezes" title="Aleixo de Menezes">Aleixo de Menezes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Goa" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishop of Goa">Archbishop of Goa</a>, and the revision was authorized by the controversial <a href="/wiki/Synod_of_Diamper" title="Synod of Diamper">Synod of Diamper</a> in 1599. So effectively was the original Malabar Rite abolished by the Synod in favour of this revision, and by the schismatics (when in 1649, being cut off from their own patriarch by the Spaniards and Portuguese, they put themselves under the <a href="/wiki/Syriac_Orthodox_patriarch" class="mw-redirect" title="Syriac Orthodox patriarch">Syriac Orthodox patriarch</a>) in favour of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=West_Syriac_Liturgy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="West Syriac Liturgy (page does not exist)">West Syriac Liturgy</a>, that no copy is known to exist, but it is evident from the revised form that it could not have differed materially from the existing East Syriac Rite.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenner1913_5-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenner1913-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
