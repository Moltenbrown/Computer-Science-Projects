<p><b>Samuel Phillips Huntington</b> (April 18, 1927 – December 24, 2008) was an American political scientist, adviser and academic. He spent more than half a century at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, where he was director of Harvard's Center for International Affairs and the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University_Professor" title="Harvard University Professor">Albert J. Weatherhead III University Professor</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_Jimmy_Carter" title="Presidency of Jimmy Carter">presidency of Jimmy Carter</a>, Huntington was the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> Coordinator of Security Planning for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Security_Council" title="United States National Security Council">National Security Council</a>. During the 1980s Apartheid era in South Africa, he served as an adviser to P.W. Botha's <a href="/wiki/Civil_Cooperation_Bureau" title="Civil Cooperation Bureau">Security Services</a>. He is best known for his 1993 theory, the "<a href="/wiki/Clash_of_Civilizations" title="Clash of Civilizations">Clash of Civilizations</a>", of a post–<a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> <a href="/wiki/New_world_order_(politics)" title="New world order (politics)">new world order</a>. He argued that future wars would be fought not between countries, but between cultures, and that <a href="/wiki/Islamic_extremism" title="Islamic extremism">Islamic extremism</a> would become the biggest threat to world peace. Huntington is credited with helping to shape U.S. views on civilian–military relations, political development, and comparative government even into the current Trump administration.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samuel_P._Huntington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Huntington was born on April 18, 1927, in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, the son of Dorothy Sanborn (née Phillips), a short-story writer, and Richard Thomas Huntington, a publisher of hotel trade journals.<sup id="cite_ref-Bloomberg_Hart_20071227_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomberg_Hart_20071227-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> His grandfather was publisher <a href="/wiki/John_Sanborn_Phillips" title="John Sanborn Phillips">John Sanborn Phillips</a>. He graduated with distinction from <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a> at age 18, served in the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Army" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Army">U.S. Army</a>, earned his <a href="/wiki/Master%27s_degree" title="Master&#39;s degree">Master's degree</a> from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a>, and completed his <a href="/wiki/Ph.D." class="mw-redirect" title="Ph.D.">Ph.D.</a> at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> where he began teaching at age 23.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_career">Academic career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samuel_P._Huntington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Academic career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Huntington was a member of Harvard's department of government from 1950 until he was denied tenure in 1959.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  Along with <a href="/wiki/Zbigniew_Brzezinski" title="Zbigniew Brzezinski">Zbigniew Brzezinski</a>, who had also been denied tenure, he moved to <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a> in New York. From 1959 to 1962 he was an associate professor of government at Columbia, where he was also deputy director of their <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_War_and_Peace_Studies" class="mw-redirect" title="Institute of War and Peace Studies">Institute of War and Peace Studies</a>. Huntington was invited to return to Harvard with tenure in 1963 and remained there until his death. He was elected a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a> in 1965.<sup id="cite_ref-AAAS_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAAS-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Huntington and <a href="/wiki/Warren_Demian_Manshel" title="Warren Demian Manshel">Warren Demian Manshel</a> co-founded and co-edited <i><a href="/wiki/Foreign_Policy" title="Foreign Policy">Foreign Policy</a></i>. Huntington stayed as co-editor until 1977.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Huntington's first major book was <i><a href="/wiki/The_Soldier_and_the_State:_The_Theory_and_Politics_of_Civil-Military_Relations" class="mw-redirect" title="The Soldier and the State: The Theory and Politics of Civil-Military Relations">The Soldier and the State: The Theory and Politics of Civil-Military Relations</a></i> (1957), which was highly controversial when it was published, but presently is regarded as the most influential book on American <a href="/wiki/Civil-military_relations" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil-military relations">civil-military relations</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> He became prominent with his <i>Political Order in Changing Societies</i> (1968), a work that challenged the conventional opinion of <a href="/wiki/Modernization" class="mw-redirect" title="Modernization">modernization</a> theorists, that economic and social progress would produce stable democracies in recently <a href="/wiki/Decolonization" title="Decolonization">decolonized</a> countries. He also was co-author of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Crisis_of_Democracy:_On_the_Governability_of_Democracies" class="mw-redirect" title="The Crisis of Democracy: On the Governability of Democracies">The Crisis of Democracy: On the Governability of Democracies</a></i>, a report issued by the <a href="/wiki/Trilateral_Commission" title="Trilateral Commission">Trilateral Commission</a> in 1976. In 1977, his friend Brzezinski – who had been appointed <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Adviser" class="mw-redirect" title="National Security Adviser">National Security Adviser</a> in the administration of <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">Jimmy Carter</a> – invited Huntington to become <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> Coordinator of Security Planning for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Security_Council" title="United States National Security Council">National Security Council</a>. He served in this position until the end of 1978.
</p><p>Huntington continued to teach undergraduates until his retirement in 2007.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samuel_P._Huntington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Huntington met his wife, Nancy Arkelyan, when they were working together on a speech for 1956 presidential candidate <a href="/wiki/Adlai_Stevenson_II" title="Adlai Stevenson II">Adlai Stevenson</a>. They had two sons, Nicholas and Timothy.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After several years of declining health, Huntington died on December 24, 2008, at age 81 on <a href="/wiki/Martha%27s_Vineyard" title="Martha&#39;s Vineyard">Martha's Vineyard</a>, Massachusetts.<sup id="cite_ref-Bloomberg_Hart_20071227_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomberg_Hart_20071227-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_arguments">Notable arguments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samuel_P._Huntington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Notable arguments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_Order_in_Changing_Societies"><i>Political Order in Changing Societies</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samuel_P._Huntington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Political Order in Changing Societies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Political_Order_in_Changing_Societies" title="Political Order in Changing Societies">Political Order in Changing Societies</a></div>
<p>During 1968, just as the United States' war in Vietnam was becoming most intense, Huntington published <i>Political Order in Changing Societies</i>, which was a critique of the modernization theory which had affected much U.S. policy regarding the developing world during the prior decade.
</p><p>Huntington argues that, as societies modernize, they become more complex and disordered. If the process of social modernization that produces this disorder is not matched by a process of political and institutional modernization—a process which produces political institutions capable of managing the stress of modernization—the result may be violence.
</p><p>During the 1970s, Huntington was an advisor to governments, both democratic and dictatorial. During 1972, he met with <a href="/wiki/Em%C3%ADlio_Garrastazu_M%C3%A9dici" title="Emílio Garrastazu Médici">Medici</a> government representatives in Brazil; a year later he published the report "Approaches to Political Decompression", warning against the risks of a too-rapid political liberalization, proposing  gradual liberalization, and a strong party state modeled upon the image of the Mexican <a href="/wiki/Institutional_Revolutionary_Party" title="Institutional Revolutionary Party">Institutional Revolutionary Party</a> (PRI). After a prolonged transition, Brazil became democratic during 1985.
</p><p>During the 1980s, he became a valued adviser to the South African regime, which used his ideas on political order to craft its "total strategy" to reform apartheid and suppress growing resistance.  He assured South Africa's rulers that increasing the repressive power of the state (which at that time included police violence, detention without trial, and torture) can be necessary to effect reform. The reform process, he told his South African audience, often requires "duplicity, deceit, faulty assumptions and purposeful blindness."  He thus gave his imprimatur  to his hosts' project of "reforming" apartheid rather than eliminating it.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Huntington frequently cited Brazil as a success, alluding to his role in his 1988 presidential address to the <a href="/wiki/American_Political_Science_Association" title="American Political Science Association">American Political Science Association</a>, commenting that political science <i>played a modest role in this process</i>. Critics, such as British political scientist Alan Hooper, note that contemporary Brazil has an especially unstable party system, wherein the best institutionalized party, <a href="/wiki/Luiz_In%C3%A1cio_Lula_da_Silva" title="Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva">Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Workers%27_Party_(Brazil)" title="Workers&#39; Party (Brazil)">Workers' Party</a> emerged in opposition to controlled transition. Moreover, Hooper claims that the lack of civil participation in contemporary Brazil results from that top-down process of political participation transitions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Third_Wave"><i>The Third Wave</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samuel_P._Huntington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Third Wave">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In his 1991 book, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Third_Wave:_Democratization_in_the_Late_Twentieth_Century" title="The Third Wave: Democratization in the Late Twentieth Century">The Third Wave: Democratization in the Late Twentieth Century</a></i>, Huntington made the argument that beginning with Portugal's revolution during 1974, there has been a <a href="/wiki/Third_Wave_Democracy" class="mw-redirect" title="Third Wave Democracy">third wave of democratization</a> which describes a global trend which includes more than 60 countries throughout Europe, Latin America, Asia, and Africa which have undergone some form of democratic transition. Huntington won the 1992 <a href="/wiki/University_of_Louisville" title="University of Louisville">University of Louisville</a> <a href="/wiki/Grawemeyer_Award" title="Grawemeyer Award">Grawemeyer Award</a> for this book.<sup id="cite_ref-grawemeyer.org_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grawemeyer.org-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id=".22The_Clash_of_Civilizations.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;The_Clash_of_Civilizations&quot;">"The Clash of Civilizations"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samuel_P._Huntington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: &quot;The Clash of Civilizations&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Clash_of_Civilizations" title="Clash of Civilizations">Clash of Civilizations</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Clash_of_Civilizations_mapn2.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Clash_of_Civilizations_mapn2.png/220px-Clash_of_Civilizations_mapn2.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="107" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Clash_of_Civilizations_mapn2.png/330px-Clash_of_Civilizations_mapn2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Clash_of_Civilizations_mapn2.png/440px-Clash_of_Civilizations_mapn2.png 2x" data-file-width="1260" data-file-height="612" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Clash_of_Civilizations_mapn2.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of the nine "civilizations" from Huntington's "Clash of Civilizations."</div></div></div>
<p>During 1993, Huntington provoked great debate among <a href="/wiki/International_relations" title="International relations">international relations</a> theorists with the interrogatively titled "The Clash of Civilizations?", an influential, oft-cited article published in <i><a href="/wiki/Foreign_Affairs" title="Foreign Affairs">Foreign Affairs</a></i> magazine. In the article, he argued that, after the fall of the Soviet Union, Islam would become the biggest obstacle to Western domination of the world. The West's next big war therefore, he said, would inevitably be with Islam.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Its description of post-Cold War <a href="/wiki/Geopolitics" title="Geopolitics">geopolitics</a> and the "inevitability of instability" contrasted with the influential <a href="/wiki/The_End_of_History_and_the_Last_Man" title="The End of History and the Last Man">End of History</a> thesis advocated by <a href="/wiki/Francis_Fukuyama" title="Francis Fukuyama">Francis Fukuyama</a>.
</p><p>Huntington expanded "The Clash of Civilizations?" to book length and published it as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Clash_of_Civilizations_and_the_Remaking_of_World_Order" class="mw-redirect" title="The Clash of Civilizations and the Remaking of World Order">The Clash of Civilizations and the Remaking of World Order</a></i> in 1996. The article and the book posit that post-Cold War conflict would most frequently and violently occur because of cultural rather than ideological differences. That, whilst in the Cold War, conflict occurred between the Capitalist West and the Communist Bloc East, it now was most likely to occur between the world's major civilizations—identifying seven, and a possible eighth: (i) Western, (ii) Latin American, (iii) Islamic, (iv) Sinic (Chinese), (v) Hindu, (vi) Orthodox, (vii) Japanese, and (viii) African. This cultural organization contrasts the contemporary world with the classical notion of sovereign states. To understand current and future conflict, cultural rifts must be understood, and culture—rather than the State—must be accepted as the reason for war. Thus, Western nations will lose predominance if they fail to recognize the irreconcilable nature of cultural tensions. Huntington argued that this post-Cold War shift in geopolitical organization and structure requires the West to strengthen itself culturally, by abandoning the imposition of its ideal of democratic universalism and its incessant military interventionism. Underscoring this point, Huntington wrote in the 1996 expansion, "In the emerging world of ethnic conflict and civilizational clash, Western belief in the universality of Western culture suffers three problems: it is false; it is immoral; and it is dangerous."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The identification of Western Civilization with <a href="/wiki/Western_Christianity" title="Western Christianity">Western Christianity</a> (Catholic-Protestant) was not Huntington's original idea, it was rather the traditional Western opinion and subdivision before the Cold War era.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
Critics (for example articles in <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Monde_Diplomatique" class="mw-redirect" title="Le Monde Diplomatique">Le Monde Diplomatique</a></i>) call <i>The Clash of Civilizations and the Remaking of World Order</i> the theoretical legitimization of American-caused Western aggression against China and the world's Islamic and Orthodox cultures. Other critics argue that Huntington's taxonomy is simplistic and arbitrary, and does not take account of the internal dynamics and partisan tensions within civilizations. Furthermore, critics argue that Huntington neglects ideological mobilization by elites and unfulfilled socioeconomic needs of the population as the real causal factors driving conflict, that he ignores conflicts that do not fit well with the civilizational borders identified by him, and they charge that his new paradigm is nothing but <a href="/wiki/Realism_(international_relations)" title="Realism (international relations)">realist</a> thinking in which "states" became replaced by "civilizations".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Huntington's influence upon U.S. policy has been likened to that of British historian <a href="/wiki/Arnold_J._Toynbee" title="Arnold J. Toynbee">Arnold Toynbee</a>'s controversial religious theories about Asian leaders during the early twentieth century.
</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> obituary on Samuel Huntington notes, however, that his "emphasis on ancient religious empires, as opposed to states or ethnicities, [as sources of global conflict] gained ... more cachet after the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">Sept. 11 attacks</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Huntington wrote that <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> might divide along the cultural line between the more Catholic <a href="/wiki/Western_Ukraine" title="Western Ukraine">western Ukraine</a> and Orthodox <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Ukraine" title="Eastern Ukraine">eastern Ukraine</a>:
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>While a statist approach highlights the possibility of a Russian-Ukrainian war, a civilizational approach minimizes that and instead highlights the possibility of Ukraine splitting in half, a separation which cultural factors would lead one to predict might be more violent than <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_Czechoslovakia" title="Dissolution of Czechoslovakia">that of Czechoslovakia</a> but far less bloody than <a href="/wiki/Breakup_of_Yugoslavia" title="Breakup of Yugoslavia">that of Yugoslavia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Who_Are_We_and_immigration"><i>Who Are We</i> and immigration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samuel_P._Huntington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Who Are We and immigration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Huntington's last book, <i><a href="/wiki/Who_Are_We%3F_The_Challenges_to_America%27s_National_Identity" title="Who Are We? The Challenges to America&#39;s National Identity">Who Are We? The Challenges to America's National Identity</a></i>, was published during May 2004. Its subject is the meaning of American <a href="/wiki/National_identity" title="National identity">national identity</a> and the possible cultural threat posed to it by large-scale <a href="/wiki/Latino" title="Latino">Latino</a> <a href="/wiki/Immigration" title="Immigration">immigration</a>, which Huntington warns could "divide the United States into two peoples, two <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">cultures</a>, and two <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">languages</a>". In this controversial book, he called for America to force immigrants to "adopt English" and the U.S. to turn to "Protestant religions" to "save itself against the threats" of Latino and Islamic immigrants.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samuel_P._Huntington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Huntington is credited with inventing the phrase <i><a href="/wiki/Davos_Man" class="mw-redirect" title="Davos Man">Davos Man</a></i>, referring to <a href="/wiki/Global_elite" class="mw-redirect" title="Global elite">global elites</a> who "have little need for national loyalty, view national boundaries as obstacles that thankfully are vanishing, and see national governments as residues from the past whose only useful function is to facilitate the elite's global operations". The phrase refers to the <a href="/wiki/World_Economic_Forum" title="World Economic Forum">World Economic Forum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Davos" title="Davos">Davos</a>, Switzerland, where leaders of the <a href="/wiki/Economic_globalization" title="Economic globalization">global economy</a> meet.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the 1980s, the <a href="/wiki/South_African_apartheid" class="mw-redirect" title="South African apartheid">South African apartheid</a> government of <a href="/wiki/P.W._Botha" class="mw-redirect" title="P.W. Botha">P.W. Botha</a> became increasingly preoccupied with security. On Huntington's advice, Botha's government established a powerful state security apparatus to "protect" the state against an anticipated upsurge in political violence that the reforms were expected to cause. The 1980s became a period of considerable political unrest, with the government becoming increasingly dominated by Botha's circle of generals and police chiefs (known as securocrats), who managed the various States of Emergencies.<sup id="cite_ref-test_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-test-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Academy_of_Sciences_controversy">National Academy of Sciences controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samuel_P._Huntington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: National Academy of Sciences controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1986, Huntington was nominated for membership to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a>. The entire academy voted on the nomination, although many scientists were unfamiliar with the nominee. Professor <a href="/wiki/Serge_Lang" title="Serge Lang">Serge Lang</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a> <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a>, disturbed this electoral status quo by challenging Huntington's nomination. Lang campaigned for others to deny Huntington membership, and eventually succeeded; Huntington was twice nominated and twice rejected. A detailed description of these events was published by Lang in "Academia, Journalism, and Politics: A Case Study: The Huntington Case" which occupies the first 222 pages of his 1998 book <i>Challenges</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Lang was inspired by the writings of mathematician <a href="/wiki/Neal_Koblitz" title="Neal Koblitz">Neal Koblitz</a>, who accused Huntington of misusing mathematics and engaging in <a href="/wiki/Pseudo-science" class="mw-redirect" title="Pseudo-science">pseudo-science</a>. Lang claimed that Huntington distorted the historical record and used pseudo-mathematics to make his conclusions seem convincing. As an example he used Huntington's 1968 book <i>Political Order in Changing Societies</i>, in which Huntington argued that South Africa was a "satisfied society" in the 1960s. Lang did not believe the conclusion, so he looked at how Huntington justified this claim and concluded that he used a method which was simply not valid. Lang suspected that he was using false pseudo-mathematical argument to give arguments that he wanted to justify greater authority. It was, said Lang, "a type of language which gives the illusion of science without any of its substance." Lang documents his accusations in his book <i>Challenges</i>.
</p><p>Huntington's prominence as a Harvard professor and (as then) director of Harvard's Center for International Affairs led to significant coverage of his defeated nomination to the NAS by the media, including <i>The New York Times</i> and <i>The New Republic</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> His supporters included <a href="/wiki/Herbert_A._Simon" title="Herbert A. Simon">Herbert A. Simon</a>, a 1978 laureate of the <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_Sweden_Prize_in_Economic_Sciences_in_Memory_of_Alfred_Nobel" class="mw-redirect" title="Bank of Sweden Prize in Economic Sciences in Memory of Alfred Nobel">Bank of Sweden Prize in Economic Sciences in Memory of Alfred Nobel</a>. The <i><a href="/wiki/Mathematical_Intelligencer" class="mw-redirect" title="Mathematical Intelligencer">Mathematical Intelligencer</a></i> offered Simon and Koblitz an opportunity to engage in a written debate, which they accepted.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="Did the debate happen? What was it? (September 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
