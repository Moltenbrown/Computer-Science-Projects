	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Canon law</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Christian law" redirects here. For other types of religious law in Christianity, see <a href="/wiki/Religious_law#Christianity" title="Religious law">Religious law §&#160;Christianity</a>.</div>
<p><b>Canon law</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>kanon</i>, a 'straight measuring rod, <a href="/wiki/Ruler" title="Ruler">ruler</a>') is a set of ordinances and regulations made by <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_jurisdiction" title="Ecclesiastical jurisdiction">ecclesiastical authority</a> (Church leadership), for the government of a Christian organization or church and its members. It is the internal <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecclesiastical">ecclesiastical</a> law, or operational policy, governing the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a> (both the <a href="/wiki/Latin_Church" title="Latin Church">Latin Church</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholic_Churches" title="Eastern Catholic Churches">Eastern Catholic Churches</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodoxy" class="mw-redirect" title="Oriental Orthodoxy">Oriental Orthodox</a> churches, and the individual national churches within the <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Communion" title="Anglican Communion">Anglican Communion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The way that such church law is <a href="/wiki/Legislative_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Legislative power">legislated</a>, interpreted and at times <a href="/wiki/Court" title="Court">adjudicated</a> varies widely among these three bodies of churches. In all three traditions, a <a href="/wiki/Canon_(canon_law)" title="Canon (canon law)">canon</a> was originally<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> a rule adopted by a <a href="/wiki/Church_council" class="mw-redirect" title="Church council">church council</a>; these canons formed the foundation of canon law.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canon_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Canon_(canon_law)" title="Canon (canon law)">Canon (canon law)</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>kanon</i> / <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Greek language">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc">κανών</span>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> Qaanoon / قانون, <a href="/wiki/Hebrew" class="mw-redirect" title="Hebrew">Hebrew</a> kaneh / קנה, "straight"; a rule, code, standard, or measure; the root meaning in all these languages is "reed" (<i>cf.</i> the Romance-language ancestors of the English word <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cane" class="extiw" title="wikt:cane">cane</a></i>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Canons_of_the_Apostles">Canons of the Apostles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canon_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Canons of the Apostles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Canons_of_the_Apostles" title="Canons of the Apostles">Canons of the Apostles</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Church_Orders" title="Ancient Church Orders">Ancient Church Orders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_Constitutions" title="Apostolic Constitutions">Apostolic Constitutions</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Didache" title="Didache">Didache</a></div>
<p>The <i>Apostolic Canons</i><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> or <i>Ecclesiastical Canons of the Same Holy Apostles</i><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> is a collection of ancient ecclesiastical decrees (eighty-five in the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern</a>, fifty in the <a href="/wiki/Latin_Church" title="Latin Church">Western</a> Church) concerning the government and discipline of the <a href="/wiki/Early_Christian" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Christian">Early Christian</a> Church, incorporated with the <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_Constitutions" title="Apostolic Constitutions">Apostolic Constitutions</a> which are part of the <a href="/wiki/Ante-Nicene_Fathers" class="mw-redirect" title="Ante-Nicene Fathers">Ante-Nicene Fathers</a>.
In the Fourth century the <a href="/wiki/First_Council_of_Nicaea" title="First Council of Nicaea">First Council of Nicaea</a> (325) calls canons the disciplinary measures of the Church: the term canon, κανὠν, means in Greek, a rule. There is a very early distinction between the rules enacted by the Church and the legislative measures taken by the State called <i>leges</i>, Latin for laws.<sup id="cite_ref-Metz_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Metz-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canon_law_(Catholic_Church)" class="mw-redirect" title="Canon law (Catholic Church)">canon law</a> is the system of laws and legal principles made and enforced by the <a href="/wiki/Hierarchy_of_the_Catholic_Church" title="Hierarchy of the Catholic Church">Church's hierarchical authorities</a> to regulate its external organization and government and to order and direct the activities of Catholics toward the mission of the Church.<sup id="cite_ref-Ramstein_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ramstein-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Latin_Church" title="Latin Church">Latin Church</a>, positive ecclesiastical laws, based directly or indirectly upon immutable divine law or <a href="/wiki/Natural_law" title="Natural law">natural law</a>, derive formal authority in the case of universal laws from the supreme legislator (i.e., the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Pontiff" class="mw-redirect" title="Supreme Pontiff">Supreme Pontiff</a>), who possesses the totality of legislative, executive, and judicial power in his person,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> while particular laws derive formal authority from a legislator inferior to the supreme legislator. The actual subject material of the canons is not just doctrinal or moral in nature, but all-encompassing of the human condition.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a> also includes the main five rites (groups) of churches which are in full union with the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a> and the Latin Church:
</p>
<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Alexandrian_Rite" title="Alexandrian Rite">Alexandrian Rite</a> Churches which include the <a href="/wiki/Coptic_Catholic_Church" title="Coptic Catholic Church">Coptic Catholic Church</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Catholic_Church" title="Ethiopian Catholic Church">Ethiopian Catholic Church</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/West_Syriac_Rite" title="West Syriac Rite">West Syriac Rite</a> which includes the <a href="/wiki/Maronite_Church" title="Maronite Church">Maronite Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syriac_Catholic_Church" title="Syriac Catholic Church">Syriac Catholic Church</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Syro-Malankara_Catholic_Church" title="Syro-Malankara Catholic Church">Syro-Malankara Catholic Church</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Armenian_Rite" title="Armenian Rite">Armenian Rite</a> Church which includes the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Catholic_Church" title="Armenian Catholic Church">Armenian Catholic Church</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Rite" title="Byzantine Rite">Byzantine Rite</a> Churches which include the <a href="/wiki/Albanian_Greek_Catholic_Church" title="Albanian Greek Catholic Church">Albanian Greek Catholic Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belarusian_Greek_Catholic_Church" title="Belarusian Greek Catholic Church">Belarusian Greek Catholic Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Bulgarian Church">Bulgarian Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Catholic_Church_of_Croatia_and_Serbia" class="mw-redirect" title="Byzantine Catholic Church of Croatia and Serbia">Byzantine Catholic Church of Croatia and Serbia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greek_Church" class="mw-disambig" title="Greek Church">Greek Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_Greek_Catholic_Church" title="Hungarian Greek Catholic Church">Hungarian Greek Catholic Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italo-Albanian_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Italo-Albanian Church">Italo-Albanian Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Macedonian_Greek_Catholic_Church" title="Macedonian Greek Catholic Church">Macedonian Greek Catholic Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Melkite_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Melkite Church">Melkite Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romanian_Church_United_with_Rome,_Greek-Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Romanian Church United with Rome, Greek-Catholic">Romanian Church United with Rome, Greek-Catholic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russian_Orthodox_Church" title="Russian Orthodox Church">Russian Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ruthenian_Greek_Catholic_Church" title="Ruthenian Greek Catholic Church">Ruthenian Greek Catholic Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovak_Greek_Catholic_Church" title="Slovak Greek Catholic Church">Slovak Greek Catholic Church</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_Greek_Catholic_Church" title="Ukrainian Greek Catholic Church">Ukrainian Catholic Church</a>.</li>

<p>All of these church groups are in full communion with the <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Supreme Pontiff</a> and are subject to the <i><a href="/wiki/Code_of_Canons_of_the_Eastern_Churches" title="Code of Canons of the Eastern Churches">Code of Canons of the Eastern Churches</a></i>.
</p>
<h3><span id="History.2C_sources_of_law.2C_and_codifications"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="History,_sources_of_law,_and_codifications">History, sources of law, and codifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canon_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History, sources of law, and codifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Legal_history_of_the_Catholic_Church" title="Legal history of the Catholic Church">Legal history of the Catholic Church</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a> has what is claimed to be the oldest continuously functioning internal legal system in <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western Europe</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-White_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> much later than <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">Roman law</a> but predating the evolution of modern European <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(legal_system)" title="Civil law (legal system)">civil law</a> traditions. What began with<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> rules ("canons") adopted by the <a href="/wiki/Twelve_Apostles" class="mw-redirect" title="Twelve Apostles">Apostles</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Jerusalem" title="Council of Jerusalem">Council of Jerusalem</a> in the first century has developed into a highly complex legal system encapsulating not just norms of the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>, but some elements of the <a href="/wiki/Halakha" title="Halakha">Hebrew</a> (<a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a>), <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">Roman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Visigoths#Law" title="Visigoths">Visigothic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_law" title="Anglo-Saxon law">Saxon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Celtic_law" title="Celtic law">Celtic legal traditions</a>.
</p><p>The history of Latin canon law can be divided into four periods: the <i>jus antiquum</i>, the <i>jus novum</i>, the <i>jus novissimum</i> and the <i>Code of Canon Law</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Manual113_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Manual113-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In relation to the Code, history can be divided into the <i>jus vetus</i> (all law before the Code) and the <i>jus novum</i> (the law of the Code, or <i>jus codicis</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Manual113_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Manual113-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The canon law of the Eastern Catholic Churches, which had developed some different disciplines and practices, underwent its own process of codification, resulting in the <a href="/wiki/Code_of_Canons_of_the_Eastern_Churches" title="Code of Canons of the Eastern Churches">Code of Canons of the Eastern Churches</a> promulgated in 1990 by <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">Pope John Paul II</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Catholic_canon_law_as_legal_system">Catholic canon law as legal system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canon_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Catholic canon law as legal system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Jurisprudence_of_Catholic_canon_law" title="Jurisprudence of Catholic canon law">Jurisprudence of Catholic canon law</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philosophy,_theology,_and_fundamental_theory_of_canon_law" title="Philosophy, theology, and fundamental theory of canon law">Philosophy, theology, and fundamental theory of canon law</a></div>
<p>Roman canon law is a fully developed legal system, with all the necessary elements: courts, lawyers, judges, a fully articulated legal code<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> principles of legal interpretation, and coercive penalties, though it lacks civilly-binding force in most secular jurisdictions. One example where it did not previously apply was in the English legal system, as well as systems, such as the U.S., that <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">derived from it</a>. Here criminals could apply for the <a href="/wiki/Benefit_of_clergy" title="Benefit of clergy">Benefit of clergy</a>. Being in holy orders, or fraudulently claiming to be, meant that criminals could opt to be tried by <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_court" title="Ecclesiastical court">ecclesiastical</a> rather than secular courts. The ecclesiastical courts were generally more lenient. Under the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Tudor" title="House of Tudor">Tudors</a>, the scope of clerical benefit was steadily reduced by <a href="/wiki/Henry_VII_of_England" title="Henry VII of England">Henry VII</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth I</a>. The Vatican disputed secular authority over priests' criminal offences, and this in turn contributed to the <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">English Reformation</a>. The benefit of clergy was systematically removed from English legal systems over the next 200 years, although it still occurred in South Carolina in <a href="/wiki/Henry_Shultz" title="Henry Shultz">1827</a>.
</p><p>The structure that the fully developed Roman Law provides is a contribution to the Canon Law.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The academic degrees in canon law are the J.C.B. (<i>Juris Canonici Baccalaureatus</i>, Bachelor of Canon Law, normally taken as a graduate degree), J.C.L. (<i>Juris Canonici Licentiatus</i>, <a href="/wiki/Licentiate_of_Canon_Law" title="Licentiate of Canon Law">Licentiate of Canon Law</a>) and the J.C.D. (<i>Juris Canonici Doctor</i>, <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Canon_Law" title="Doctor of Canon Law">Doctor of Canon Law</a>). Because of its specialized nature, advanced degrees in civil law or theology are normal prerequisites for the study of canon law.
</p><p>Much of the legislative style was adapted from the Roman Law <a href="/wiki/Codex_iuris_civilis" class="mw-redirect" title="Codex iuris civilis">Code of Justinian</a>. As a result, Roman ecclesiastical courts tend to follow the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Law">Roman Law</a> style of continental Europe with some variation, featuring collegiate panels of judges and an investigative form of proceeding, called "<a href="/wiki/Inquisitorial_system" title="Inquisitorial system">inquisitorial</a>", from the Latin "inquirere", to enquire. This is in contrast to the <a href="/wiki/Adversarial_system" title="Adversarial system">adversarial</a> form of proceeding found in the common law system of English and U.S. law, which features such things as juries and single judges.
</p><p>The institutions and practices of canon law paralleled the legal development of much of Europe, and consequently both modern <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(legal_system)" title="Civil law (legal system)">civil law</a> and <a href="/wiki/Common_law_(legal_system)" class="mw-redirect" title="Common law (legal system)">common law</a> bear the influences of canon law. Edson Luiz Sampel, a Brazilian expert in canon law, says that canon law is contained in the genesis of various institutes of civil law, such as the law in continental Europe and Latin American countries. Sampel explains that canon law has significant influence in contemporary society.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Canonical jurisprudential theory generally follows the principles of <a href="/wiki/Aristotelianism" title="Aristotelianism">Aristotelian</a>-<a href="/wiki/Thomism" title="Thomism">Thomistic</a> <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_law" title="Philosophy of law">legal philosophy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-White_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> While the term "law" is never explicitly defined in the Code,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Catechism_of_the_Catholic_Church" title="Catechism of the Catholic Church">Catechism of the Catholic Church</a> cites <a href="/wiki/Treatise_on_Law" title="Treatise on Law">Aquinas in defining law</a> as "...an ordinance of reason for the common good, promulgated by the one who is in charge of the community"<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and reformulates it as "...a rule of conduct enacted by competent authority for the sake of the common good."<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Code_for_the_Eastern_Churches">The Code for the Eastern Churches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canon_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Code for the Eastern Churches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Code_of_Canons_of_the_Eastern_Churches" title="Code of Canons of the Eastern Churches">Code of Canons of the Eastern Churches</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Oriental_canon_law" title="Oriental canon law">Oriental canon law</a></div>
<p>The law of the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholic_Churches" title="Eastern Catholic Churches">Eastern-rite Churches</a> in full communion with the Roman papacy was in much the same state as that of the Latin or Western Church before 1917; much more diversity in legislation existed in the various Eastern Catholic Churches. Each had its own special law, in which custom still played an important part. One major difference in Eastern Europe however, specifically in the Orthodox Christian churches was in regards to divorce. Divorce started to slowly be allowed in specific instances such as adultery being committed, abuse, abandonment, impotence and barrenness being the primary justifications for divorce. Eventually, the church began to allow remarriage to occur (for both spouses) post-divorce.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In 1929 Pius XI informed the Eastern Churches of his intention to work out a Code for the whole of the Eastern Church. The <a href="/wiki/Eastern_canonical_reforms_of_Pius_XII" title="Eastern canonical reforms of Pius XII">publication of these Codes</a> for the Eastern Churches regarding the law of persons was made between 1949 through 1958<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> but finalized nearly 30 years later.<sup id="cite_ref-Metz_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Metz-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The first <a href="/wiki/1917_Code_of_Canon_Law" title="1917 Code of Canon Law">Code of Canon Law (1917)</a> was almost exclusively for the <a href="/wiki/Latin_Church" title="Latin Church">Latin Church</a>, with extremely limited application to the Eastern Churches.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> After the <a href="/wiki/Second_Vatican_Council" title="Second Vatican Council">Second Vatican Council</a>, (1962 - 1965), another edition was published specifically for the Roman Rite in 1983. Most recently, 1990, the Vatican produced the <i>Code of Canons</i> of the Eastern Churches which became the 1st code of <i>Eastern Catholic Canon Law</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orthodox_Church">Orthodox Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canon_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Orthodox Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Church</a>, principally through the work of 18th-century <a href="/wiki/Mount_Athos" title="Mount Athos">Athonite</a> monastic scholar <a href="/wiki/Nicodemus_the_Hagiorite" title="Nicodemus the Hagiorite">Nicodemus the Hagiorite</a>, has compiled canons and commentaries upon them in a work known as the <i>Pēdálion</i> (Greek: Πηδάλιον, "Rudder"), so named because it is meant to "steer" the Church in her discipline. The dogmatic determinations of the Councils are to be applied rigorously, since they are considered to be essential for the Church's unity and the faithful preservation of the Gospel.<sup id="cite_ref-Pastavos_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pastavos-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anglican_Communion">Anglican Communion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canon_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Anglican Communion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Canon_law_(Anglican_Communion)" class="mw-redirect" title="Canon law (Anglican Communion)">Canon law (Anglican Communion)</a></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_court" title="Ecclesiastical court">ecclesiastical courts</a> that formerly decided many matters such as disputes relating to marriage, divorce, wills, and defamation, still have jurisdiction of certain church-related matters (e.g. discipline of clergy, alteration of church property, and issues related to churchyards). Their separate status dates back to the 12th century when the <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Normans</a> split them off from the mixed secular/religious county and local courts used by the Saxons. In contrast to the other courts of England the law used in ecclesiastical matters is at least partially a <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(legal_system)" title="Civil law (legal system)">civil law</a> system, not <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a>, although heavily governed by parliamentary statutes. Since the <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">Reformation</a>, ecclesiastical courts in England have been royal courts. The teaching of canon law at the Universities of Oxford and Cambridge was abrogated by <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a>; thereafter practitioners in the <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_courts" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecclesiastical courts">ecclesiastical courts</a> were trained in <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(legal_system)" title="Civil law (legal system)">civil law</a>, receiving a <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Civil_Law" title="Doctor of Civil Law">Doctor of Civil Law</a> (D.C.L.) degree from <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">Oxford</a>, or a Doctor of Laws (<a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Laws" class="mw-redirect" title="Doctor of Laws">LL.D.</a>) degree from <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">Cambridge</a>. Such lawyers (called "doctors" and "civilians") were centered at "<a href="/wiki/Doctors%27_Commons" title="Doctors&#39; Commons">Doctors Commons</a>", a few streets south of <a href="/wiki/St_Paul%27s_Cathedral" title="St Paul&#39;s Cathedral">St Paul's Cathedral</a> in London, where they monopolized <a href="/wiki/Probate" title="Probate">probate</a>, matrimonial, and <a href="/wiki/Admiralty_law" title="Admiralty law">admiralty</a> cases until their jurisdiction was removed to the <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> courts in the mid-19th century.
</p><p>Other churches in the <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Communion" title="Anglican Communion">Anglican Communion</a> around the world (e.g., the <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_Church_in_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Episcopal Church in the United States">Episcopal Church in the United States</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Church_of_Canada" title="Anglican Church of Canada">Anglican Church of Canada</a>) still function under their own private systems of canon law.
</p><p>Currently, (2004), there are principles of canon law common to the churches within the Anglican Communion; their existence can be factually established; each province or church contributes through its own legal system to the principles of canon law common within the Communion; these principles have a strong persuasive authority and are fundamental to the self-understanding of each of the churches of the Communion; these principles have a living force, and contain in themselves the possibility of further development; and the existence of these principles both demonstrates unity and promotes unity within the Anglican Communion.
<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Presbyterian_and_Reformed_churches">Presbyterian and Reformed churches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canon_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Presbyterian and Reformed churches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Presbyterian_polity" title="Presbyterian polity">Presbyterian polity</a></div>
<p>In Presbyterian and Reformed churches, canon law is known as "practice and procedure" or  "church order", and includes the church's laws respecting its government, discipline, legal practice and worship.
</p><p>Roman canon law had been criticized by the Presbyterians as early as 1572 in the <a href="/wiki/Admonition_to_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Admonition to Parliament">Admonition to Parliament</a>. The protest centered on the standard defense that canon law could be retained so long as it did not contradict the civil law. According to Polly Ha, the Reformed Church Government refuted this claiming that the bishops had been enforcing canon law for 1500 years.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lutheranism">Lutheranism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canon_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Lutheranism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Concord" title="Book of Concord">Book of Concord</a> is the historic <a href="/wiki/Doctrinal_statement" class="mw-redirect" title="Doctrinal statement">doctrinal statement</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutheran Church</a>, consisting of ten <a href="/wiki/Creed" title="Creed">credal</a> documents recognized as authoritative in <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutheranism</a> since the 16th century.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>  However, the Book of Concord is a confessional document (stating orthodox belief) rather than a book of ecclesiastical rules or discipline, like canon law. Each Lutheran national church establishes its own system of church order and discipline, though these are referred to as "canons."
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Methodist_Church">United Methodist Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canon_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: United Methodist Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Discipline_(United_Methodist)" title="Book of Discipline (United Methodist)">Book of Discipline (United Methodist)</a></div>
<p>The Book of Discipline contains the laws, rules, policies and guidelines for The United Methodist Church. Its last edition was published in 2016.
</p>
