<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scottish">Scottish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morrow_(surname)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Scottish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Morrow name is ultimately a Scottish one, being first found in Lowland Scotland where the name can come from the Gaelic <i>Moireach</i> or <i>Moireabh</i> meaning "a sea farer" or "a settlement by the sea" respectively, or from <i>Murchadh</i> meaning "sea warrior". It has been variously spelt Morow, Murrow and Morrewe, the latter being first found on the Ragman Rolls of 1296.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Many Morrows emigrated to Ireland in the early 17th century, first during the Hamilton-Montgomery settlement when they came from Ayrshire and Lanarkshire and settled Down and Antrim and then during the Ulster Plantation when they mostly settled in counties Donegal, Londonderry, and Armagh. A number of Morrows who served in the Covenanter army were transported to Virginia after being captured by Cromwell at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Worcester" title="Battle of Worcester">Battle of Worcester</a> in 1651. Many Scottish Morrows can be found throughout American and Canadian history since the colonial era; Henry Morrow, from Scotland, was living in Massachusetts in 1664<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>. Notables descended from Scottish Morrows include the Americans <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Z._Morrow" title="Thomas Z. Morrow">Thomas Z. Morrow</a> and his son <a href="/wiki/Edwin_P._Morrow" title="Edwin P. Morrow">Edwin P. Morrow</a>, and the Canadian politician <a href="/wiki/Donald_Morrow" title="Donald Morrow">Donald Morrow</a>, and his father Robert Morrow.
</p><p>Notable Scottish Morrows:
</p>
<ul><li>Hugh Morrow, in 1296, on the Ragman Rolls</li>
<li>John Morrow, a follower of <a href="/wiki/Robert_the_Bruce" title="Robert the Bruce">Robert the Bruce</a> who was imprisoned in the Tower of London in 1333</li>
<li>Thomas Morrow, Abbot of Paisley 1418-1444</li>
<li>Angus Morrow in 1431 fought against Angus Mackay in Strathnaver</li>
<li>David Morrow, a Scotsman from Ayrshire who settled in <a href="/wiki/Killyleagh" title="Killyleagh">Killyleagh</a>, Co. Down in 1606</li>
<li>Duncan Morrow, witness to the ordination of Patrick Macdowell in 1503 in Stirling</li>
<li>Andrew MacMorrow of Wigtown (1478) <sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Irish">Irish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morrow_(surname)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Irish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although a Scottish name, it has been used to anglicise a number of Irish Gaelic names, mainly "<a href="/wiki/Murchadh" title="Murchadh">Murchadh</a>" (<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/muir" class="extiw" title="wikt:muir">muir</a> and <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cath#Irish" class="extiw" title="wikt:cath">cath</a>) meaning "Sea Battle" or "Sea Warrior", which was also made as Morrogh, Murrow, Moroghoe, and Murphy. In Petty’s Census of 1659 O'Morrow and McMarrowe are recorded. However, it is more common for these Irish names to have been anglicised as Murphy, and it was more likely to find 'McMorrow' as an anglicisation of these names rather than the Scottish 'Morrow' on its own.
</p><p>Most Irish Morrows, especially in Ulster, will be descended from Scottish settlers due to the <a href="/wiki/Plantation_of_Ulster" title="Plantation of Ulster">Plantation of Ulster</a> during the 17th century. These "Scotch-Irish" Morrows would join the emigration from Ireland to the North American colonies in the early 18th century, and their descendants in America include <a href="/w/index.php?title=Major_Samuel_Morrow&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Major Samuel Morrow (page does not exist)">Major Samuel Morrow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jeremiah_Morrow" title="Jeremiah Morrow">Jeremiah Morrow</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dwight_Morrow" title="Dwight Morrow">Dwight Morrow</a>.
</p><p>Notable Irish Morrows,
</p>
<ul><li>Jeremiah Morrow, grandfather of <a href="/wiki/Jeremiah_Morrow" title="Jeremiah Morrow">Jeremiah Morrow</a>, governor of Ohio</li>
<li>Adam Morrow, one of the "defenders of Derry", who signed a petition to William of Orange after the siege of Derry had been lifted in 1689</li>
<li>Joseph Morrow, a soldier in the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a> from South Carolina who was born in Ireland in 1760</li>
<li>John Morrow, a Presbyterian United Irishman who was exiled from Ireland due to his participation in the <a href="/wiki/1798_rebellion" class="mw-redirect" title="1798 rebellion">rebellion of 1798</a></li>
<li>Captain Francis Murrough, an Irish officer in the Jacobite army of King James II<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Andrew Morrough of Cork (also spelt Morrogh)<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
