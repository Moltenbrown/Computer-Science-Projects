	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Mr.</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This article is about the title. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Mr._(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Mr. (disambiguation)">Mr. (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Mister" redirects here. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Mister_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Mister (disambiguation)">Mister (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p><i><b>Mister</b></i>, usually written in its abbreviated form <i><b>Mr.</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">US</a>) or <i><b>Mr</b></i> <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_English" class="mw-redirect" title="Commonwealth English">(UK</a>), is a commonly used <a href="/wiki/English_honorific" class="mw-redirect" title="English honorific">English honorific</a> for men under the rank of <a href="/wiki/Knighthood" class="mw-redirect" title="Knighthood">knighthood</a>. The title 'Mr' derived from earlier forms of <i><a href="/wiki/Master_(form_of_address)" title="Master (form of address)">master</a></i>, as the equivalent female titles <i>Mrs</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Miss" title="Miss">Miss</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Ms." title="Ms.">Ms</a></i> all derived from earlier forms of <i><a href="/wiki/Mistress_(form_of_address)" title="Mistress (form of address)">mistress</a></i>. <i>Master</i> is sometimes still used as an honorific for <a href="/wiki/Boy" title="Boy">boys</a> and young men, but its use is increasingly uncommon.
</p><p><span id="Messrs"></span>
The modern plural form is <i><b>Misters</b></i>, although its usual formal abbreviation <i><b>Messrs</b></i>(.)<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;note 1&#93;</a></sup> derives from use of the French title <i><i lang="fr" title="French language text">messieurs</i></i> in the 18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-MessrsTheOED_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MessrsTheOED-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mluds_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mluds-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <i><i lang="fr" title="French language text">Messieurs</i></i> is the plural of <i><i lang="fr" title="French language text"><a href="/wiki/Monsieur" title="Monsieur">monsieur</a></i></i> (originally <i><i lang="fr" title="French language text">mon sieur</i></i>, "my <a href="/wiki/Lord" title="Lord">lord</a>"), formed by <a href="/wiki/Declension_(nouns)" class="mw-redirect" title="Declension (nouns)">declining</a> both of its constituent parts separately.<sup id="cite_ref-mluds_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mluds-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historic_etiquette">Historic etiquette</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historic etiquette">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Historically, <i>mister</i> was applied only to those above one's own status, providing they had no higher title such as <i><a href="/wiki/Sir" title="Sir">Sir</a></i> or <i>my <a href="/wiki/Lord" title="Lord">lord</a></i>, in the English class system. This understanding is now obsolete, as it was gradually expanded as a mark of respect to those of equal status and then to all men without a higher style.
</p><p>In the 19th century and earlier, in Britain, two gradations of 'gentleman' were recognised; the higher was entitled to use '<a href="/wiki/Esquire" title="Esquire">esquire</a>' (usually abbreviated to Esq, which followed the name), while the lower employed 'Mr' before the name. Today, on post from <a href="/wiki/Buckingham_Palace" title="Buckingham Palace">Buckingham Palace</a> a man who is a UK citizen is addressed as 'Esq', while a man of foreign nationality is addressed as 'Mr'.
</p><p>In past centuries, <i>Mr</i> was used with a first name to distinguish among family members who might otherwise be confused in conversation: <i>Mr Doe</i> would be the eldest present; younger brothers or cousins were then referred to as <i>Mr Richard Doe</i> and <i>Mr William Doe</i> and so on. Such usage survived longer in family-owned business or when <a href="/wiki/Domestic_worker" title="Domestic worker">domestic workers</a> were referring to adult male family members with the same surname: "Mr Robert and Mr Richard will be out this evening, but Mr Edward is dining in." In other circumstances, similar usage to indicate respect combined with familiarity, is common in most anglophone cultures including that of the southern United States.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_titles">Professional titles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Professional titles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Mr</i> is sometimes combined with certain titles (<i>Mr President, Mr Speaker, Mr Justice, Mr <a href="/wiki/Dean_(religion)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dean (religion)">Dean</a></i>). The feminine equivalent is <i><a href="/wiki/Madam" title="Madam">Madam</a></i>. All of these except <i>Mr Justice</i> are used in direct address and without the name. In certain professional contexts in different regions, <i>Mr</i> has specific meanings; the following are some examples.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medicine">Medicine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Medicine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> and in some <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a> countries (such as <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and some states of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>), many <a href="/wiki/Surgeon" title="Surgeon">surgeons</a> use the title <i>Mr</i> (or <i>Miss</i>, <i>Ms</i>, <i>Mrs</i>, as appropriate), rather than <i>Dr</i> (<a href="/wiki/Doctor_(title)" title="Doctor (title)">Doctor</a>). Until the 19th century, earning a medical degree was not required to become a qualified surgeon. Hence, the modern practice of reverting from <i>Dr</i> to <i>Mr</i> after successfully completing qualifying exams in surgery (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Membership_of_the_Royal_College_of_Surgeons" class="mw-redirect" title="Membership of the Royal College of Surgeons">Membership of the Royal College of Surgeons</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Australasian_College_of_Surgeons" title="Royal Australasian College of Surgeons">Royal Australasian College of Surgeons</a>) is a historical reference to the origins of surgery in the United Kingdom as non-medically qualified <a href="/wiki/Barber_surgeons" class="mw-redirect" title="Barber surgeons">barber surgeons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_usage">Military usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Military usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Military" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Military">United States Military</a>, <a href="/wiki/Warrant_officer_(United_States)" title="Warrant officer (United States)">warrant officers</a> and chief warrant officers are addressed as <i>Mister</i> by senior commissioned officers. In the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">United States Coast Guard</a> it is proper to use <i>Mister</i> to refer to <a href="/wiki/Commissioned_officers" class="mw-redirect" title="Commissioned officers">commissioned officers</a> below the rank of <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_commander" title="Lieutenant commander">lieutenant commander</a>, or to subordinate commissioned officers, though the use of <i>Mister</i> implies familiarity compared to the use of rank title for an unknown officer. Women officers below the rank of <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_commander" title="Lieutenant commander">lieutenant commander</a> may be addressed as Miss, Ms. or Mrs. as appropriate.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/British_Armed_Forces" title="British Armed Forces">British Armed Forces</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Warrant_officer" title="Warrant officer">warrant officer</a> is addressed as <i>Sir</i> by other ranks and non-commissioned officers; <a href="/wiki/Commissioned_officer" class="mw-redirect" title="Commissioned officer">commissioned officers</a>, particularly of junior rank, should address a <a href="/wiki/Warrant_officer" title="Warrant officer">warrant officer</a> using his surname and the prefix <i>Mister</i>; for example, "Mr Smith", although often their rank or appointment is used, for example "Sergeant Major", "Regimental Sergeant Major", or "RSM".
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/British_Armed_Forces" title="British Armed Forces">British Armed Forces</a> a <a href="/wiki/Subaltern" title="Subaltern">subaltern</a> is often referred to by his surname and the prefix <i>Mister</i> by both other ranks and more senior commissioned officers, e.g., "Report to Mister Smithe-Jones at once" rather than "Report to 2nd Lieutenant Smithe-Jones at once".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Judges">Judges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Judges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Courts_of_England_and_Wales" title="Courts of England and Wales">Courts of England and Wales</a>, Judges of the High Court are called, for example, <i>Mr Justice Crane</i> (unless they are entitled to be addressed as <i>Lord Justice</i>). Where a forename is necessary to avoid ambiguity it is always used, for example <i>Mr Justice Robert Goff</i> to distinguish from a predecessor <i>Mr Justice Goff</i>. The female equivalent is <i>Mrs Justice Hallett</i>, not <i>Madam Justice Hallett</i>. When more than one judge is sitting and one needs to be specific, one would refer to <i>My Lord, Mr Justice Crane</i>. High Court Judges are entitled to be styled with the prefix <i>The Honourable</i> while holding office: e.g., the Honourable Mr Justice Robert Goff. In writing, such as in the law reports, the titles "Mr Justice" or "Mrs Justice" are both abbreviated to a "J" placed after the name. For example, <i>Crane J</i> would be substituted for <i>Mr Justice Crane</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>   Female judges are still properly addressed "My Lord", however "My Lady" is more modernly acceptable.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Chief_Justice_of_the_United_States" title="Chief Justice of the United States">Chief Justice of the United States</a> may be referred to as either "Mr Chief Justice," or "Chief Justice." For example, "Mr Chief Justice Roberts," or "Chief Justice Roberts."
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Catholic_clerics">Catholic clerics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Catholic clerics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Among  <a href="/wiki/Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> clergy, "Mr" is the correct title and form of address for seminarians and other students for the priesthood and was once the proper title for all secular and parish priests, the use of the title "Father" being reserved to <a href="/wiki/Religious_(Catholicism)" class="mw-redirect" title="Religious (Catholicism)">religious</a> clergy only. The use of the title "Father" for parish clergy became customary around the 1820s.
</p><p>A diocesan seminarian is correctly addressed as "Mr", and once <a href="/wiki/Ordination" title="Ordination">ordained</a> a transitional deacon, is addressed in formal correspondence (though rarely in conversation) as the Reverend Mister (or "Rev. Mr"). In clerical <a href="/wiki/Religious_institute" title="Religious institute">religious institutes</a> (those primarily made up of priests), Mr is the title given to scholastics. For instance, in the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">Jesuits</a>, a man preparing for priesthood who has completed the novitiate but who is not yet ordained is properly, "Mr John Smith, SJ" and is addressed verbally as "Mister Smith"—this is to distinguish him from Jesuit brothers, and priests. (Although, before the 1820s, many Jesuit priests were also called "Mr".) Orders founded before the 16th century do not, as a rule, follow this practice: a <a href="/wiki/Franciscan" class="mw-redirect" title="Franciscan">Franciscan</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominican</a>, for instance, becomes a friar after novitiate and so is properly titled "Brother" or, if a priest, "Father".
</p><p>Permanent <a href="/wiki/Deacons" class="mw-redirect" title="Deacons">deacons</a> in the United States are styled as "Deacon" or "the Reverend Deacon" followed by their first and last names (e.g., "Deacon John Jones", rather than "the Reverend Mr").<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> It is also customary in some places, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholic_Churches" title="Eastern Catholic Churches">Eastern Catholic Churches</a> to address deacons while speaking, like presbyters, as "Father" or "Father Deacon".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_usages">Other usages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other usages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>"Mister" can also be used in combination with another word to refer to someone who is regarded as the personification of, or master of, a particular field or subject, especially in the fields of popular entertainment and sports, as <a href="/wiki/Gordie_Howe" title="Gordie Howe">Gordie Howe</a> is referred to as "Mr Hockey" or <a href="/wiki/Reggie_Jackson" title="Reggie Jackson">Reggie Jackson</a> is known as "Mr October."</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Italian_football" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian football">Italian football</a>, deference to a coach is shown by players, staff and fans referring to him as "Il Mister," or directly, "Mister". This is traditionally attributed to the conversion of the local game of <a href="/wiki/Calcio_Fiorentino" title="Calcio Fiorentino">Calcio</a> to English-rules <a href="/wiki/Association_Football" class="mw-redirect" title="Association Football">Association Football</a> by British sailors, who would have been the first coaches.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
