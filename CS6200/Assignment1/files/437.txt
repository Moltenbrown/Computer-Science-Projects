<p>A <b>conjugal family</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_family" title="Nuclear family">nuclear family</a> that may consist of a married couple and their children (by <a href="/wiki/Childbirth" title="Childbirth">birth</a> or <a href="/wiki/Adoption" title="Adoption">adoption</a>) or a couple who are unmarried or underage. Conjugal means there is a marriage relationship. The family relationship is principally focused inward and ties to extended <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">kin</a> are voluntary and based on emotional bonds, rather than strict duties and obligations. The spouses and their children are considered to be of prime importance, and other more distant relatives less important. The marriage bond is important and stressed.
</p><p>Since the notion of "family" has changed over time (for example, increasing acceptance of <a href="/wiki/LGBT_parenting" title="LGBT parenting">same-sex parenting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stepfamily" title="Stepfamily">stepfamily</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Adoption" title="Adoption">adoption</a>), the meaning of the term "<a href="/wiki/Nuclear_family" title="Nuclear family">nuclear family</a>" became muddled (some use it to refer exclusively to opposite-sex parents, while others do not). To combat the ambiguity, the term "conjugal family" was created.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Western_societies">In Western societies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conjugal_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In Western societies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are basic characteristics to a conjugal family. In most western societies, people are free to choose their spouse themselves, rather than their spouse being selected for them by <a href="/wiki/Arranged_marriage" title="Arranged marriage">arranged marriage</a>. In western societies, the majority- but not all- pairings are opposite-sex.
</p><p>Western societies often treat marriage as a legally-binding relationship, rather than an informal agreement. In these societies, both partners usually share control of their children's upbringing. They both have roles as a parent to protect their children, oversee the development of their children in society, and see to the survival of their children.
</p><p>In some cases, both partners have separate lines of income to support the family; in others, only one receives substantial income (colloquially called the "breadwinner") while the other spends more time caring for the family directly (looking after the children, taking care of the home, etc.).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Throughout_the_years">Throughout the years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conjugal_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Throughout the years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Conjugal family roles have changed over the years. Historically, marriages were exclusively opposite-sex and it was assumed that the male would be the head of the household and provide for the family while the woman would stay in the home and care for the children. However, <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/conjugal#Adjective" class="extiw" title="wikt:conjugal">conjugal</a> roles have evolved over the years; in modern times, same-sex couples are more accepted and in opposite-sex couples, the husband and wife are treated equally.
</p>
