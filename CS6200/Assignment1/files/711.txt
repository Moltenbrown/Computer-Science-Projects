	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Body proportions</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Parmigianino_004.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Parmigianino_004.jpg/220px-Parmigianino_004.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="301" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Parmigianino_004.jpg/330px-Parmigianino_004.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Parmigianino_004.jpg/440px-Parmigianino_004.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2024" data-file-height="2772" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Parmigianino_004.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Madonna_with_the_Long_Neck" title="Madonna with the Long Neck">Madonna with the Long Neck</a></i>, c. 1534-1540, by <a href="/wiki/Parmigianino" title="Parmigianino">Parmigianino</a>. As in other <a href="/wiki/Mannerist" class="mw-redirect" title="Mannerist">Mannerist</a> works, the proportions of the body - here the <a href="/wiki/Neck" title="Neck">neck</a> - are exaggerated for artistic effect.</div></div></div>
<p>While there is significant variation in anatomical proportions between people, there are many references to <b>body proportions</b> that are intended to be canonical, either in <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">art</a>, <a href="/wiki/Measurement" title="Measurement">measurement</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Measurement" title="Measurement">measurement</a>, body proportions are often used to relate two or more measurements based on the body.  A <a href="/wiki/Cubit" title="Cubit">cubit</a>, for instance, is supposed to be six <a href="/wiki/Palm_(unit)" title="Palm (unit)">palms</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Span_(unit)" title="Span (unit)">span</a> is taken to be 9 inches and was previously considered as half a cubit. While convenient, these ratios may not reflect the physiognomic variation of the individuals using them.
</p><p>Similarly, in <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">art</a>, body proportions are the study of relation of <a href="/wiki/Human_body" title="Human body">human</a> or animal body parts to each other and to the whole.  These ratios are used in depictions of the figure (to varying degrees naturalistic, idealized or stylized), and may become part of an <a href="/wiki/Aesthetic_canon" title="Aesthetic canon">aesthetic canon</a> within a culture.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basics_of_human_proportions">Basics of human proportions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Body_proportions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Basics of human proportions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:132px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Braus_1921_1.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Braus_1921_1.png/130px-Braus_1921_1.png" decoding="async" width="130" height="229" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Braus_1921_1.png/195px-Braus_1921_1.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Braus_1921_1.png/260px-Braus_1921_1.png 2x" data-file-width="1380" data-file-height="2428" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Braus_1921_1.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Human proportions marked out in an illustration from a 20th-century anatomy text-book. Hermann Braus, 1921</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Braus_1921_3.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Braus_1921_3.png/300px-Braus_1921_3.png" decoding="async" width="300" height="211" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Braus_1921_3.png/450px-Braus_1921_3.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Braus_1921_3.png/600px-Braus_1921_3.png 2x" data-file-width="1594" data-file-height="1120" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Braus_1921_3.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A) <a href="/wiki/Black_lemur" title="Black lemur">black lemur</a><br />B) chimpanzee<br />C) gorilla<br />D) human</div></div></div>
<p>It is important in <a href="/wiki/Figure_drawing" title="Figure drawing">figure drawing</a> to draw the human figure in proportion. Though there are subtle differences between individuals, human proportions fit within a fairly standard range, though artists have historically tried to create idealised standards, which have varied considerably over different periods and regions. In modern figure drawing, the basic unit of measurement is the 'head', which is the distance from the top of the head to the chin. This unit of measurement is reasonably standard, and has long been used by artists to establish the proportions of the human figure.  <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_art" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Egyptian art">Ancient Egyptian art</a> used a canon of proportion based on the "fist", measured across the knuckles, with 18 fists from the ground to the hairline on the forehead.  This was already established by the <a href="/wiki/Narmer_Palette" title="Narmer Palette">Narmer Palette</a> from about the 31st century BC, and remained in use until at least the conquest by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a> some 3,000 years later.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The proportions used in figure drawing are:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<ul><li>An average person is generally 7-and-a-half heads tall (including the head).</li>
<li>An ideal figure, used when aiming for an impression of nobility or grace, is drawn at 8 heads tall.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Heroic" class="mw-redirect" title="Heroic">heroic</a> figure, used in the heroic for the depiction of <a href="/wiki/Gods" class="mw-redirect" title="Gods">gods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Superheroes" class="mw-redirect" title="Superheroes">superheroes</a>, is eight-and-a-half heads tall.  Most of the additional length comes from a bigger chest and longer legs.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_ideal">Western ideal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Body_proportions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Western ideal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jessica_Alba_Face_Proportions.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Jessica_Alba_Face_Proportions.png/220px-Jessica_Alba_Face_Proportions.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="209" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Jessica_Alba_Face_Proportions.png/330px-Jessica_Alba_Face_Proportions.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/04/Jessica_Alba_Face_Proportions.png 2x" data-file-width="391" data-file-height="372" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jessica_Alba_Face_Proportions.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/University_of_Toronto" title="University of Toronto">University of Toronto</a> study found that ideal facial proportions of <a href="/wiki/Jessica_Alba" title="Jessica Alba">Jessica Alba</a> <sup id="cite_ref-pmid19896961_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid19896961-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pmid23441307_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid23441307-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> were close to the average of all female profiles.<sup id="cite_ref-twsZE23_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-twsZE23-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leg-to-body_ratio">Leg-to-body ratio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Body_proportions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Leg-to-body ratio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A study using Polish participants by Sorokowski found 5% longer legs than an individual used as a reference was considered most attractive.<sup id="cite_ref-tws19jan_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tws19jan-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The study concluded this preference might stem from the influence of leggy runway models.<sup id="cite_ref-Sorokowski_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sorokowski-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The Sorokowski study was criticized for using a picture of the same person with digitally altered leg lengths which Marco Bertamini<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (March 2016)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> felt were unrealistic.<sup id="cite_ref-Bertamini_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bertamini-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another study using British and American participants, found <i>mid-ranging</i> leg-to-body ratios to be most ideal.<sup id="cite_ref-Frederick_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Frederick-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A study by Swami et al. of American men and women showed a preference for men with legs as long as the rest of their body and women with 40%<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> longer legs than the rest of their body. The researcher concluded that this preference might be influenced by American culture where long-legged women are portrayed as more attractive.<sup id="cite_ref-Swami_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Swami-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The Swami et al. study was criticized for using a picture of the same person with digitally altered leg lengths which Marco Bertamini felt were unrealistic. Bertamini also criticized the Swami study for only changing the leg length while keeping the arm length constant. Bertamini's own study which used stick figures mirrored Swami's study, however, by finding a preference for leggier women.<sup id="cite_ref-Bertamini_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bertamini-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another common measurement of related to leg-to-body ratio is sitting-height ratio (SHR). Sitting height ratio is the ratio of the head plus spine length to total height which is highly correlated to leg-to-body ratio. SHR has been found to be very different between individuals of different ancestry. It has been reported that individuals with African ancestry have on average longer leg length, i.e. lower SHR than individuals of European ancestry.<sup id="cite_ref-LegLengthBeauty_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LegLengthBeauty-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> A study in 2015 revealed that this difference is mainly due to genetic differences and the SHR difference between African and European individuals is as large as 1 standard deviation.<sup id="cite_ref-SHR_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SHR-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Muscular_men_and_thin_women">Muscular men and thin women</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Body_proportions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Muscular men and thin women">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A 1999 study found that "the (action) figures have grown much more muscular over time, with many contemporary figures far exceeding the muscularity of even the largest human bodybuilders," reflecting an American cultural ideal of a super muscular man. Also, female dolls reflect the cultural ideal of thinness in women.<sup id="cite_ref-Pope_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pope-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Da_Vinci_Vitruve_Luc_Viatour.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Da_Vinci_Vitruve_Luc_Viatour.jpg/170px-Da_Vinci_Vitruve_Luc_Viatour.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="231" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Da_Vinci_Vitruve_Luc_Viatour.jpg/255px-Da_Vinci_Vitruve_Luc_Viatour.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Da_Vinci_Vitruve_Luc_Viatour.jpg/340px-Da_Vinci_Vitruve_Luc_Viatour.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2258" data-file-height="3070" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Da_Vinci_Vitruve_Luc_Viatour.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Vitruvian_Man" title="Vitruvian Man">Vitruvian Man</a></i>, c. 1492, by <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a></div></div></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_art">In art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Body_proportions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Mathematics_and_art" title="Mathematics and art">Mathematics and art</a></div>
<p>The ancient Greek sculptor <a href="/wiki/Polykleitos" title="Polykleitos">Polykleitos</a> (c.450–420 BC), known for his ideally proportioned bronze <i><a href="/wiki/Doryphoros" title="Doryphoros">Doryphoros</a></i>, wrote an influential <i>Canon</i> describing the proportions to be followed in sculpture.<sup id="cite_ref-Stewart_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stewart-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> 
The <i>Canon</i> applies the basic mathematical concepts of Greek geometry, such as the ratio, proportion, and <i>symmetria</i> (Greek for "harmonious proportions") creating a system capable of describing the human form through a series of continuous <a href="/wiki/Geometric_series" title="Geometric series">geometric progressions</a>. Polykleitos uses the <a href="/wiki/Distal_phalanges" class="mw-redirect" title="Distal phalanges">distal phalanx</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Little_finger" title="Little finger">little finger</a> as the basic module for determining the proportions of the human body, scaling this length up repeatedly by <span class="nowrap">&#8730;<span style="border-top:1px solid; padding:0 0.1em;">2</span></span> to obtain the ideal size of the other phalanges, the hand, forearm, and upper arm in turn.<sup id="cite_ref-Tobin_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tobin-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a> believed that the ideal human proportions were determined by the harmonious proportions that he believed governed the universe, such that the ideal man would fit cleanly into a circle as depicted in his famed drawing of <i><a href="/wiki/Vitruvian_man" class="mw-redirect" title="Vitruvian man">Vitruvian man</a></i> (c. 1492).<sup id="cite_ref-DaVinci_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DaVinci-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Leonardo’s recorded information was about relative body proportions – with comparisons of hand, foot, and other feature’s lengths to other body parts – more than to actual measurements.&#160;He was a prodigious note writer and small sketch draftsman. After 1490, he left voluminous manuscripts including studies on proportions. These were later collected and translated into English by Jean Paul Richter and published in 1883.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Avard_T._Fairbanks" class="mw-redirect" title="Avard T. Fairbanks">Avard T. Fairbanks</a> taught in five universities. His proportions study was used for college instruction. The main male and female illustrations (1936) were drawn to accompany Leonardo’s system of relative proportions. He erected more than 100 public monuments over the course of his 75 year career. His son, Eugene F. Fairbanks published books based on this study and his own study of children’s proportions.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
