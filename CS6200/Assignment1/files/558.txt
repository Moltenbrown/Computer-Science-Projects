<p>In <a href="/wiki/Confucian_ethics" class="mw-redirect" title="Confucian ethics">Confucian</a>, Chinese <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_ethics" title="Buddhist ethics">Buddhist</a> and Taoist<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKohn2004passim_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKohn2004passim-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> ethics, <b>filial piety</b> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%AD%9D" class="extiw" title="wikt:孝">孝</a></span>, <i>xiào</i>) is a virtue of respect for one's parents, elders, and ancestors. The Confucian <i><a href="/wiki/Classic_of_Filial_Piety" title="Classic of Filial Piety">Classic of Filial Piety</a></i>, thought to be written around the <a href="/wiki/Qin_dynasty" title="Qin dynasty">Qin</a>-<a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han</a> period, has historically been the authoritative source on the Confucian tenet of filial piety. The book, a purported dialogue between <a href="/wiki/Confucius" title="Confucius">Confucius</a> and his student <a href="/wiki/Tseng_Tzu" class="mw-redirect" title="Tseng Tzu">Tseng Tzu</a>, is about how to set up a good society using the principle of filial piety. Filial piety is central to Confucian <a href="/wiki/Role_ethics" title="Role ethics">role ethics</a>.
</p><p>In more general terms, filial piety means to be good to one's parents; to take care of one's parents; to engage in good conduct not just towards parents but also outside the home so as to bring a good name to one's parents and ancestors; to show love, respect and support; display courtesy; to ensure male heirs, uphold fraternity among brothers; wisely advise one's parents, including dissuading them from moral unrighteousness; display sorrow for their sickness and death; to bury them and carry out sacrifices after their death.
</p><p>Filial piety is considered a key virtue in <a href="/wiki/Chinese_culture" title="Chinese culture">Chinese</a> and other East Asian cultures, and it is the main subject of many stories. One of the most famous collections of such stories is <i><a href="/wiki/The_Twenty-four_Filial_Exemplars" title="The Twenty-four Filial Exemplars">The Twenty-four Cases of Filial Piety</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh">二十四孝</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>&#58; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Ershi-si xiao</span></i>). These stories depict how children exercised their filial piety in the past. While China has always had a diversity of religious beliefs, filial piety has been common to almost all of them; historian Hugh D.R. Baker calls respect for the family the only element common to almost all Chinese people.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filial_piety&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The western term <i>filial piety</i> was originally derived from studies of Western societies, based on Mediterranean cultures. However, <a href="/wiki/Pietas" title="Pietas">filial piety among the ancient Romans</a>, for example, was largely different from the Chinese in its logic and enactment.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHamilton199078,_84_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHamilton199078,_84-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Filial piety is illustrated by the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_character" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese character">Chinese character</a> <i>xiao</i> (孝). The character is a combination of the character <i>lao</i> (old) above the character <i>zi</i> (son), that is, an elder being carried by a son.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIkels20042–3_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIkels20042–3-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> This indicates that the older generation should be supported by the younger generation.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYee2006_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYee2006-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Korean_Confucianism" title="Korean Confucianism">Korean Confucianism</a>, the character <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%AD%9D" class="extiw" title="wikt:孝">孝</a> is pronounced <i>hyo</i> (효). In Vietnamese, the character <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%AD%9D" class="extiw" title="wikt:孝">孝</a> is written in the <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_alphabet" title="Vietnamese alphabet">Vietnamese alphabet</a> as <i>hiếu</i>. In Japanese, the term is generally rendered in spoken and written language as 親孝行, <i>oyakōkō</i>, adding the characters for <i>parent</i> and <i>conduct</i> to the Chinese character to make the word more specific.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filial_piety&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Confucian teachings about filial piety can be found in numerous texts, including the <a href="/wiki/Four_Books_and_Five_Classics" title="Four Books and Five Classics">Four Books</a>, that is the <i><a href="/wiki/Great_Learning" title="Great Learning">Great Learning</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh">大学</span>), the <i><a href="/wiki/Doctrine_of_the_Mean" title="Doctrine of the Mean">Doctrine of the Mean</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh">中庸</span>), <i><a href="/wiki/Analects" title="Analects">Analects</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh">论语</span>) and the book <i><a href="/wiki/Mencius_(book)" title="Mencius (book)">Mencius</a></i>, as well as the works <i><a href="/wiki/Classic_of_Filial_Piety" title="Classic of Filial Piety">Classic of Filial Piety</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh">孝经</span>) and the <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Rites" title="Book of Rites">Book of Rites</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh">礼记</span>) .<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESung2009a179,_186–7_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESung2009a179,_186–7-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In the <i>Classic of Filial Piety</i>, <a href="/wiki/Confucius" title="Confucius">Confucius</a> (551–479 BCE) says that "[f]ilial piety is the root of virtue and the basis of philosophy"<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJordan1998267_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJordan1998267-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and modern philosopher <a href="/wiki/Fung_Yu-lan" class="mw-redirect" title="Fung Yu-lan">Fung Yu-lan</a> describes filial piety as "the ideological basis for traditional [Chinese] society".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKingBond198533_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKingBond198533-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For Confucius, filial piety is not merely ritual outside respect to one's parents, but an inward attitude as well.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESung200115_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESung200115-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Filial piety consists of several aspects. Filial piety is an awareness of repaying the burden born by one's parents.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECong2004158_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECong2004158-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> As such, filial piety is done to reciprocate the care one's parents have given.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKwan200025_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKwan200025-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> However, it is also practiced because of an obligation towards one's ancestors.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHsu199863_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHsu199863-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHo1994350_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHo1994350-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to some modern scholars, <i>xiào</i> is the root of <i>rén</i> (仁; <a href="/wiki/Ren_(Confucianism)" title="Ren (Confucianism)">benevolence, humaneness</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHsuO&#39;ConnorLee2009159_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHsuO&#39;ConnorLee2009159-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> but other scholars state that <i>rén</i>, as well as <i>yì</i> (義; <a href="/wiki/Yi_(Confucianism)" title="Yi (Confucianism)">righteousness</a>) and <i>li</i> (禮; <a href="/wiki/Li_(Confucianism)" title="Li (Confucianism)">propriety</a>) should be interpreted as the roots of <i>xiào</i>. <i>Rén</i> means favorable behavior to those who we are close to.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKwan200031_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKwan200031-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> <i>Yì</i> refers to respect to those considered worthy of respect, such as parents and superiors. <i>Li</i> is defined as behaving according to social norms and cultural values.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKwan200031_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKwan200031-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Moreover, it is defined in the texts as <i>deference</i>, which is respectful submission, and <i>reverence</i>, meaning deep respect and awe.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESung200115_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESung200115-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Filial piety was taught by Confucius as part of a broad ideal of <a href="/wiki/Junzi" title="Junzi">self-cultivation</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh">君子</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>&#58; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">jūnzǐ</span></i>) toward being a perfect human being.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHsuO&#39;ConnorLee2009158–9_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHsuO&#39;ConnorLee2009158–9-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Modern philosopher <a href="/wiki/Hu_Shih" title="Hu Shih">Hu Shih</a> argued that filial piety gained its central role in Confucian ideology only among later Confucianists. He proposed that Confucius originally taught the quality of <i>rén</i> in general, and did not yet emphasize <i>xiào</i> that much. Only later Confucianists such as <a href="/wiki/Tseng_Tzu" class="mw-redirect" title="Tseng Tzu">Tseng Tzu</a> focused on <i>xiào</i> as the single, most important Confucianist quality.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKingBond198533_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKingBond198533-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Detailed_descriptions">Detailed descriptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filial_piety&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Detailed descriptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:24_parangons_of_filial_piety_-_Huang_Tingjian.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Man emptying pot, assisted by second person" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/24_parangons_of_filial_piety_-_Huang_Tingjian.jpg/170px-24_parangons_of_filial_piety_-_Huang_Tingjian.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="237" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/24_parangons_of_filial_piety_-_Huang_Tingjian.jpg/255px-24_parangons_of_filial_piety_-_Huang_Tingjian.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/24_parangons_of_filial_piety_-_Huang_Tingjian.jpg/340px-24_parangons_of_filial_piety_-_Huang_Tingjian.jpg 2x" data-file-width="503" data-file-height="700" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:24_parangons_of_filial_piety_-_Huang_Tingjian.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Koteiken, one of <a href="/wiki/The_Twenty-four_Filial_Exemplars" title="The Twenty-four Filial Exemplars">The Twenty-four Cases of Filial Piety</a>, depicted emptying a chamber pot for his mother. <a href="/wiki/Utagawa_Kuniyoshi" title="Utagawa Kuniyoshi">Utagawa Kuniyoshi</a>, 1848.</div></div></div>
<p>Confucian ethics does not regard filial piety as a choice, but rather as an unconditional obligation of the child.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFungCheng2010486_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFungCheng2010486-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The relationship between parents and children is the most fundamental of the <a href="/wiki/Five_cardinal_relationships" class="mw-redirect" title="Five cardinal relationships">five cardinal relationships</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh">五倫</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>&#58; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">wǔlún</span></i>) described by Confucius in his <a href="/wiki/Role_ethics" title="Role ethics">role ethics</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESung2009a180_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESung2009a180-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and filial piety, together with fraternal love, underlies this system.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOh199148_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOh199148-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> It is the fundamental principle of Confucian morality:<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESung2009b355_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESung2009b355-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> filial piety was seen as the basis for an orderly society, together with loyalty of the ministers toward the ruler, and servitude of the wife toward the husband.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKutcher200613_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKutcher200613-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> In short, filial piety is central to Confucian role ethics<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChangKalmanson201068_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChangKalmanson201068-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> and is the cardinal virtue, that defines, limits or even eliminates all other virtues.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHsu199861_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHsu199861-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to the traditional texts, filial piety consists of physical care, love, service, respect and obedience.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Children should attempt not to bring their parents in disgrace.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECong2004159_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECong2004159-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Confucian texts such as <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Rites" title="Book of Rites">Book of Rites</a></i> give details on how filial piety should be practiced.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYee2006_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYee2006-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Respect is envisioned by detailed manners such as the way children salute their parents, speak to them (words and tone used) or enter and leave the room in which their parents are, as well as seating arrangements and gifts.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Care means making sure parents are comfortable in every single way: this involves food, accommodation, clothes, hygiene, and basically to have them "see and hear pleasurable things", in Confucius' words,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESung200115–6_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESung200115–6-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> and to have them live without worry.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKwan200025_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKwan200025-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> But the most important expression of and exercise in filial piety were the burial and mourning rituals to be held in honor of one's parents.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKutcher200614_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKutcher200614-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHsuO&#39;ConnorLee2009159_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHsuO&#39;ConnorLee2009159-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Filial piety means to be good to one's parents; to take care of one's parents; to engage in good conduct not just towards parents but also outside the home so as to bring a good name to one's parents and ancestors;<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESung200117_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESung200117-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> to perform the duties of one's job well (preferably the same job as one's parents to fulfill their aspirations)<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKwan200025_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKwan200025-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> as well as to carry out sacrifices to the ancestors;<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESung200118_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESung200118-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> not be rebellious,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHo1994350_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHo1994350-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> to be polite, and well-mannered; to show love, respect and support, to be near home to serve one's parents;<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKwan200024_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKwan200024-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> display courtesy;<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESung200115–6_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESung200115–6-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> ensure male heirs,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKwan200025_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKwan200025-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> uphold fraternity among brothers;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> wisely advise one's parents, including dissuading them from moral unrighteousness;<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKwan200024_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKwan200024-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> display sorrow for their sickness and death;<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESung200116–7_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESung200116–7-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> bury them and carry out sacrifices after their death.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKutcher20061_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKutcher20061-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, a filial child should promote the public name of its family, and it should cherish the affection of its parents.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKwan200025_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKwan200025-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Traditional texts essentially describe filial piety in terms of a son–father relationship, but in practice, it involves all parent–child relationships, as well as relationships with stepparents, grandparents and ancestors.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJordan1998269_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJordan1998269-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>But filial piety also involves the role of the parent to the child. The father has a duty to provide for the son, to teach him in traditions of <a href="/wiki/Ancestor_worship" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancestor worship">ancestor worship</a>, to find a spouse for him, and leave a good heritage.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHsu199862_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHsu199862-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJordan1998269_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJordan1998269-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> A father is supposed to be 'stern and dignified' to his children, whereas a mother is supposed to be 'gentle and compassionate'. The parent's virtues are to be practiced, regardless of the child's piety, and vice versa.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJordan1998269_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJordan1998269-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Nevertheless, filial piety mostly identified the <em>child</em>'s duty, and in this, it differed from the Roman concept of <i><a href="/wiki/Patria_potestas" class="mw-redirect" title="Patria potestas">patria potestas</a></i>, which defined mostly the father's authoritative power. Whereas in Roman culture and later in the Judeo-Christian West people in authority legitimated their influence by referring to a higher transcending power, in Chinese culture authority was defined by the roles of the subordinates (son, subject, wife) to their superior (fathers, emperor, husband) and vice versa. As roles and duties were depersonalized, supremacy became a matter of role and position, rather than person, as it was in the West.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHamilton199092–4_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHamilton199092–4-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Anthropologist <a href="/w/index.php?title=Francis_Hsu_(anthropologist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Francis Hsu (anthropologist) (page does not exist)">Francis Hsu</a> argued that a child's obedience from a Confucian perspective was regarded as unconditional, but anthropologist <a href="/wiki/David_K._Jordan" title="David K. Jordan">David K. Jordan</a> and psychologist David Yau-fai Ho disagree.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJordan1998269_35-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJordan1998269-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHo1994350_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHo1994350-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Jordan states that in classical Chinese thought, 'remonstrance' was part of filial piety, meaning that a pious child needs to dissuade a parent from performing immoral actions.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJordan1998269_35-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJordan1998269-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Ho points out in this regard that the Confucian classics do not advocate 'foolish filial piety' (愚孝 <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>&#58; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">yúxiào</span></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHo1994350_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHo1994350-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> However, Jordan does add that if the parent does not listen to the child's dissuasion, the child must still obey the parent,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJordan1998270_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJordan1998270-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> and Ho states that "rebellion or outright defiance" is never approved in Confucian ethics.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHo1994350_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHo1994350-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Filial piety not only extends to behavior of children toward their parents, but also involves gratitude toward the human body they received from their parents,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESung2009b355_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESung2009b355-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> as the body is seen as an extension of one's parents.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKwan200024_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKwan200024-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> This involves prohibitions on damaging or hurting the body, and this doctrine has affected how the Confucianists regarded the shaving of the head by Buddhist monks,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESung2009b355_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESung2009b355-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> but also has created a taboo on suicide, regarded as 'unfilial behavior' (不孝 <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>&#58; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">bùxiào</span></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_with_society_at_large">Relation with society at large</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filial_piety&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Relation with society at large">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:132px;"><a href="/wiki/File:2017-02-25_A_Stone_That_Was_Written_%27Filial_piety%27_of_the_Kwanyang_Elementary_School.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Mushroom-shaped tone with inscription in Korean letters" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/2017-02-25_A_Stone_That_Was_Written_%27Filial_piety%27_of_the_Kwanyang_Elementary_School.jpg/130px-2017-02-25_A_Stone_That_Was_Written_%27Filial_piety%27_of_the_Kwanyang_Elementary_School.jpg" decoding="async" width="130" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/2017-02-25_A_Stone_That_Was_Written_%27Filial_piety%27_of_the_Kwanyang_Elementary_School.jpg/195px-2017-02-25_A_Stone_That_Was_Written_%27Filial_piety%27_of_the_Kwanyang_Elementary_School.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/2017-02-25_A_Stone_That_Was_Written_%27Filial_piety%27_of_the_Kwanyang_Elementary_School.jpg/260px-2017-02-25_A_Stone_That_Was_Written_%27Filial_piety%27_of_the_Kwanyang_Elementary_School.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3096" data-file-height="4128" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:2017-02-25_A_Stone_That_Was_Written_%27Filial_piety%27_of_the_Kwanyang_Elementary_School.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A memorial stone at a Korean elementary school, with the inscription "filial piety".</div></div></div>
<p>Filial piety is regarded as a principle that ordered society, without which chaos would prevail.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKutcher200613_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKutcher200613-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> It is described as "an inevitable fact of nature", as opposed to mere convention,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJordan1998278_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJordan1998278-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> and it is seen to follow naturally out of the father–son relationship.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHamilton199084_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHamilton199084-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> In the Chinese tradition of patriarchy, roles are upheld to maintain the harmony of the whole.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHamilton199093_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHamilton199093-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/Neo-Confucian" class="mw-redirect" title="Neo-Confucian">Neo-Confucian</a> philosopher <a href="/wiki/Cheng_Hao" title="Cheng Hao">Cheng Hao</a> (1032–1085 CE), relationships and their corresponding roles "belong to the eternal principle of the cosmos from which there is no escape between heaven and earth".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHamilton199095_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHamilton199095-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The idea of filial piety became popular in China because of the many functions it had and many roles it undertook, as the traditional Confucian scholars such as <a href="/wiki/Mencius" title="Mencius">Mencius</a> (4th century BCE) regarded the family as a fundamental unit that formed the root of the nation. Though the virtue of <i>xiào</i> was about respect by children toward their parents, it was meant to regulate how the young generation behaved toward elders in the extended family and in society in general.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChow2009320_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChow2009320-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWangYuenSlaney2008252_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWangYuenSlaney2008252-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, devotion to one's parents was often associated with one's devotion to the state,<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;note 1&#93;</a></sup> described as the "parallel conception of society"<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKutcher20062_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKutcher20062-48">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> or the "Model of Two".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOh199148_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOh199148-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The <i>Classic of Filial Piety</i> states that an obedient and filial son will grow up to become a loyal official (<a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>&#58; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">chung</span></i>)—filial piety was therefore seen as a truth that shaped the citizens of the state,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKutcher200613_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKutcher200613-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> and the loyalty of the minister to his emperor was regarded as the extension of filial piety.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHamilton1990100,_n.2_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHamilton1990100,_n.2-49">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Filial piety was regarded as being a dutiful person in general.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHamilton199095_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHamilton199095-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Nevertheless, the two were not equated. Mencius teaches that ministers should overthrow an immoral tyrant, should he harm the state—the loyalty to the king was considered conditional, not as unconditional as in filial piety towards one parents.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFungCheng2010486_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFungCheng2010486-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_East_Asian_languages_and_cultures">In East Asian languages and cultures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filial_piety&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In East Asian languages and cultures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Confucian teachings about filial piety have left their mark on East Asian languages and culture. In Chinese, there is a saying that "among hundreds of behaviors, filial piety is the most important one" (<a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>&#58; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">bai xing xiao wei xian</span></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWangYuenSlaney2008252_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWangYuenSlaney2008252-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJordan1998267_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJordan1998267-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In modern Chinese, filial piety is rendered with the words <i>Xiao xun</i>, meaning 'respect and obedience'.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKwan200023,_quote:_&quot;Xiao_xun,_the_Chinese_term_for_filial_piety,_commands_the_younger_in_the_family_to_be_respectful_(i.e.,_xiao)_and_obedient_(i.e.,_xun)_to_one&#39;s_parents_and_other_elderly_members_related_to_the_family&quot;_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKwan200023,_quote:_&quot;Xiao_xun,_the_Chinese_term_for_filial_piety,_commands_the_younger_in_the_family_to_be_respectful_(i.e.,_xiao)_and_obedient_(i.e.,_xun)_to_one&#39;s_parents_and_other_elderly_members_related_to_the_family&quot;-50">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> While China has always had a diversity of religious beliefs, filial piety has been common to almost all of them; historian Hugh D.R. Baker calls respect for the family the only element common to almost all Chinese people.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBaker197998_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaker197998-51">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> Historian <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ch%27%C3%BC_T%27ung-tsu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ch&#39;ü T&#39;ung-tsu (page does not exist)">Ch'ü T'ung-tsu</a> stated about the codification of patriarchy in Chinese law that "[i]t was all a question of filial piety".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHamilton199092_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHamilton199092-52">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Filial piety also forms the basis for the veneration of the aged, for which the Chinese are known.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHo1994350_14-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHo1994350-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKingBond198533_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKingBond198533-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> However, filial piety among the Chinese has led them to be mostly focused on taking care of close kin, and be less interested in wider issues of more distant people:<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHsu199863_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHsu199863-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHamilton199091_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHamilton199091-53">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> nevertheless, this should not be mistaken for individualism. In Japan, however, devotion to kinship relations was and still is much more broadly construed, involving more than just kin.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHsu199863_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHsu199863-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Korean culture, filial piety is also of crucial importance.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYim1998165_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYim1998165-54">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> In Taiwan, filial piety is considered one of eight important virtues, among which filial piety is considered supreme. It is "central in all thinking about human behavior".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJordan1998267_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJordan1998267-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Taiwan generally has more traditional values with regard to the parent–child relationship than the People's Republic of China (PRC). This is reflected in attitudes about how desirable it is for the elderly to live independently.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWhyte2004123_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhyte2004123-55">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_behavioral_sciences">In behavioral sciences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filial_piety&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In behavioral sciences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Filial_piety,_a_son_and_his_mom_in_Zhengzhou.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Man guiding an elderly woman when crossing the road" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Filial_piety%2C_a_son_and_his_mom_in_Zhengzhou.jpg/220px-Filial_piety%2C_a_son_and_his_mom_in_Zhengzhou.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Filial_piety%2C_a_son_and_his_mom_in_Zhengzhou.jpg/330px-Filial_piety%2C_a_son_and_his_mom_in_Zhengzhou.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Filial_piety%2C_a_son_and_his_mom_in_Zhengzhou.jpg/440px-Filial_piety%2C_a_son_and_his_mom_in_Zhengzhou.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1365" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Filial_piety,_a_son_and_his_mom_in_Zhengzhou.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>According to R.M. Lee, in the final stage of the development of filial piety, it is a means to realize one's ethical ideals.</div></div></div>
<p>Social scientists have done much research about filial piety and related concepts.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFungCheng2010315_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFungCheng2010315-56">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> It is a highly influential factor in studies about Asian families and intergenerational studies, as well as studies on <a href="/wiki/Socialization" title="Socialization">socialization</a> patterns.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYee2006_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYee2006-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Filial piety has been defined by several scholars as the recognition by children of the aid and care their parents have given them, and the respect returned by those children.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESung200114_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESung200114-57">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Psychologist K.S. Yang has defined it as a "specific, complex <a href="/wiki/Syndrome#Other_fields" title="Syndrome">syndrome</a> or set of cognition, affects, intentions, and behaviors concerning being good or nice to one's parents".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKwan200023_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKwan200023-58">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> As of 2006, psychologists measured filial piety in inconsistent ways, which has prevented much progress from being made.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYee2006_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYee2006-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Filial piety is defined by behaviors such as daily maintenance, respect and sickness care offered to the elderly.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFungCheng2010315_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFungCheng2010315-56">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Although in scholarly literature five forms of <a href="/wiki/Reverence_(attitude)" class="mw-redirect" title="Reverence (attitude)">reverence</a> have been described, multi-cultural researcher <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kyu-taik_Sung&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kyu-taik Sung (page does not exist)">Kyu-taik Sung</a> has added eight more to that, to fully cover the traditional definitions of elder respect in Confucian texts:<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESung200117–8_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESung200117–8-59">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Care respect: making sure parents are comfortable in every single way;</li>
<li>Victual respect: taking the parents' preferences into account, e.g. favorite food;</li>
<li>Gift respect: giving gifts or favors, e.g. presiding meetings;</li>
<li>Presentational respect: polite and appropriate decorum;</li>
<li>Linguistic respect: use of honorific language;</li>
<li>Spatial respect: having elders sit at a place of honor, building graves at respectful places;</li>
<li>Celebrative respect: celebrating birthdays or other events in honor of elders;</li>
<li>Public respect: voluntary and public services for elders;</li>
<li>Acquiescent respect: listening to elders without talking back;</li>
<li>Consultative respect: consulting elders in personal and family matters;</li>
<li>Salutatory respect: bowing or saluting elders;</li>
<li>Precedential respect: allowing elders to have priority in distributing goods and services;</li>
<li>Funeral respect: mourning and burying elders in a respectful way;</li>
<li>Ancestor respect: commemorating ancestors and making sacrifices for them.</li></ul>
<p>These forms of respect are based on <a href="/wiki/Qualitative_research" title="Qualitative research">qualitative research</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESung200119_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESung200119-60">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> Some of these forms involve some action or work, whereas other forms are more symbolic. Female elders tend to receive more care respect, whereas male elders tend to receive more symbolic respect.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESung200122–4_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESung200122–4-61">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Apart from attempting to define filial piety, psychologists have also attempted to explain its cognitive development. Psychologist R.M. Lee distinguishes a five-fold development, which he bases on <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Kohlberg" title="Lawrence Kohlberg">Lawrence Kohlberg</a>'s theory of <a href="/wiki/Moral_development" title="Moral development">moral development</a>. In the first stage, filial piety is comprehended as just the giving of material things, whereas in the second stage this develops into an understanding that emotional and spiritual support is more important. In the third stage, the child realizes that filial piety is crucial in establishing and keeping parent–child relationships; in the fourth stage, this is expanded to include relationships outside of one's family. In the final stage, filial piety is regarded as a means to realize one's ethical ideals.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKwan200029_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKwan200029-62">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kano_Motonobu_-_Scenes_from_24_Paragons_of_Filial_Piety_-_2004.46A_-_Indianapolis_Museum_of_Art.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Painting with several scenes in a natural setting" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Kano_Motonobu_-_Scenes_from_24_Paragons_of_Filial_Piety_-_2004.46A_-_Indianapolis_Museum_of_Art.jpg/310px-Kano_Motonobu_-_Scenes_from_24_Paragons_of_Filial_Piety_-_2004.46A_-_Indianapolis_Museum_of_Art.jpg" decoding="async" width="310" height="142" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Kano_Motonobu_-_Scenes_from_24_Paragons_of_Filial_Piety_-_2004.46A_-_Indianapolis_Museum_of_Art.jpg/465px-Kano_Motonobu_-_Scenes_from_24_Paragons_of_Filial_Piety_-_2004.46A_-_Indianapolis_Museum_of_Art.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Kano_Motonobu_-_Scenes_from_24_Paragons_of_Filial_Piety_-_2004.46A_-_Indianapolis_Museum_of_Art.jpg/620px-Kano_Motonobu_-_Scenes_from_24_Paragons_of_Filial_Piety_-_2004.46A_-_Indianapolis_Museum_of_Art.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2759" data-file-height="1263" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kano_Motonobu_-_Scenes_from_24_Paragons_of_Filial_Piety_-_2004.46A_-_Indianapolis_Museum_of_Art.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Painting with scenes from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Twenty-four_Filial_Exemplars" title="The Twenty-four Filial Exemplars">The Twenty-four Cases of Filial Piety</a></i>. Kano Motonobu, 1550</div></div></div>
<p>Psychologists have found correlations between filial piety and lower socio-economic status, female gender, elders, minorities, and non-westernized cultures. Traditional filial piety beliefs have been connected with positive outcomes for the community and society, care for elder family members, positive family relationships and solidarity. On the other side, it has also been related to an orientation to the past, resistance to cognitive change, superstition and fatalism; dogmatism, authoritarianism and conformism, as well as a belief in the superiority of one's culture; and lack of active, critical and creative learning attitudes.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Ho connects the value of filial piety with authoritarian moralism and cognitive conservatism in Chinese patterns of socialization, basing himself on findings among subjects in Hong Kong and Taiwan. He defines <i>authoritarian moralism</i> as hierarchical authority ranking in family and institutions, as well as the pervasiveness of using moral precepts as criteria of measuring people. <i>Cognitive moralism</i> he derives from social psychologist <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Greenwald" title="Anthony Greenwald">Anthony Greenwald</a>, and means a "disposition to preserve existing knowledge structures" and resistance to change. He concludes that filial piety appears to have a negative effect on psychological development, but at the same time, partly explains the high motivation of Chinese people to achieve academic results.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHo1994351–2,_362_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHo1994351–2,_362-64">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In family counselling research, filial piety has been seen to help establish bonding with parents.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKwan200027_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKwan200027-65">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> Ho argues that the value filial piety brings along an obligation to raise one's children in a moral way to prevent disgrace to the family.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHo1994361_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHo1994361-66">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> However, filial piety has also been found to perpetuate dysfunctional family patterns such as child abuse: there may be both positive and negative psychological effects.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKwan200027,_34–5_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKwan200027,_34–5-67">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> Francis Hsu made the argument that when taken to the level of the family at large, pro-family attitudes informed by filial piety can lead to nepotism, corruption and eventually are at tension with the good of the state as whole.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJordan1998276_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJordan1998276-68">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Chinese parent–child relations, the aspect of authority goes hand-in-hand with the aspect of benevolence. E.g. many Chinese parents support their children's education fully and do not allow their children to work during their studies, allowing them to focus on their studies. Because of the combination of benevolence and authoritarianism in such relations, children feel obliged to respond to parents' expectations, and internalize them.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKwan200032_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKwan200032-69">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> Ho found, however, that in Chinese parent–child relations, fear was also a contributing factor in meeting parents' filial expectations: children may not internalize their parents' expectations, but rather perform <em>roles</em> as good children in a detached way, through <a href="/wiki/Affect_(psychology)" title="Affect (psychology)">affect</a>–role dissociation.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKwan200033_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKwan200033-70">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> Studying Korean family relations, scholar Dawnhee Yim argues that internalization of parents' obligations by children may lead to guilt, as well as suppression of hostile thoughts toward parents, leading to psychological problems.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYim1998165–6_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYim1998165–6-71">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> Jordan found that despite filial piety being asymmetrical in nature, Chinese interviewees felt that filial piety contained an element of reciprocity: "... it is easy to see the parent whom one serves today as the self who is served tomorrow." Furthermore, the practice of filial piety provides the pious child with a sense of adulthood and moral heroism.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJordan1998274–5_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJordan1998274–5-72">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filial_piety&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-Confucian_history">Pre-Confucian history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filial_piety&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Pre-Confucian history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ancestor_veneration_in_China" title="Ancestor veneration in China">Ancestor veneration in China</a></div>
<p>The origins of filial piety in East Asia lie in ancestor worship,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHsuO&#39;ConnorLee2009159_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHsuO&#39;ConnorLee2009159-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and can already be found in the pre-Confucian period. <a href="/wiki/Epigraphical" class="mw-redirect" title="Epigraphical">Epigraphical</a> findings such as oracle bones contain references to filial piety; texts such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Classic_of_Changes" class="mw-redirect" title="Classic of Changes">Classic of Changes</a></i> (10th–4th century BCE) may contain early references to the idea of parallel conception of the filial son and the loyal minister.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKutcher200612_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKutcher200612-73">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Confucianism">Early Confucianism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filial_piety&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Early Confucianism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Filial_mourning" title="Filial mourning">Filial mourning</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Annoted_The_Classic_of_Filial_Piety.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Pages with Chinese characters and illustrations" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Annoted_The_Classic_of_Filial_Piety.jpg/260px-Annoted_The_Classic_of_Filial_Piety.jpg" decoding="async" width="260" height="185" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Annoted_The_Classic_of_Filial_Piety.jpg/390px-Annoted_The_Classic_of_Filial_Piety.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Annoted_The_Classic_of_Filial_Piety.jpg/520px-Annoted_The_Classic_of_Filial_Piety.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1735" data-file-height="1234" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Annoted_The_Classic_of_Filial_Piety.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Page of the illustrated version of the <a href="/wiki/Classic_of_Filial_Piety" title="Classic of Filial Piety">Classic of Filial Piety</a>, annotated by Kuan (1286–1324)</div></div></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/T%27ang_dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="T&#39;ang dynasty">T'ang dynasty</a> (6th–10th century), not performing filial piety was declared illegal, and even earlier, during the <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasty</a> (2nd century BCE–3rd century CE), this was already punished by beheading.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECong2004159_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECong2004159-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Behavior regarded as unfilial such as mistreating or abandoning one's parents or grandparents, or refusing to complete the mourning period for them was punished by exile and beating, at best.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHamilton1990102–3,_n.56_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHamilton1990102–3,_n.56-74">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From the Han Dynasty onward, the practice of mourning rites came to be seen as the cornerstone of filial piety and was strictly practiced and enforced. This was a period of unrest, and the state promoted the practice of long-term mourning to reestablish their authority. Filial piety toward one's parents was expected to lead to loyalty to the ruler, expressed in the Han proverb "The Emperor rules all-under-heaven with filial piety".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKutcher20062_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKutcher20062-48">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Government officials were expected to take leave for a mourning period of two years after their parents died.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKutcher20061–2_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKutcher20061–2-75">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> Local officials were expected to encourage filial piety to one's parents—and by extension, to the state—by behaving as an example of such piety.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKutcher20062,_12_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKutcher20062,_12-76">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> Indeed, the king himself would perform an exemplary role in expressing filial piety, through the ritual of 'serving the elderly' (<a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>&#58; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">yang lao zhi li</span></i>). Nearly all Han emperors had the word <i>xiào</i> in their <a href="/wiki/Temple_name" title="Temple name">temple name</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKutcher200614_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKutcher200614-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChanTan20042_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChanTan20042-77">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> The promotion of filial piety in this manner, as part of the idea of <i>li</i>, was more an acceptable way to create order in society than resorting to law.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKutcher2006194_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKutcher2006194-78">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Filial piety became a keystone of Han morality.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChanTan20042_77-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChanTan20042-77">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the early Confucian period, the principles of filial piety were brought back by Japanese and Korean students to their respective homelands, where they became central to the education system. In Japan, rulers gave awards to people deemed to practice exemplary filial conduct.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHsu199862_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHsu199862-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the Mongolian rule in the <a href="/wiki/Yuan_dynasty" title="Yuan dynasty">Yuan dynasty</a> (13th–14th century), the practice of filial piety was perceived to deteriorate. In the <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming dynasty</a> (14th–17th century), emperors and <a href="/wiki/Scholar-official" title="Scholar-official">literati</a> attempted to revive the customs of filial piety, though in that process, filial piety was reinterpreted, as rules and rituals were modified.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKutcher200635_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKutcher200635-79">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> Even on the grassroots level a revival was seen, as societies that provided vigilance against criminals started to promote Confucian values. A book that was composed by members of this movement was <i><a href="/wiki/The_Twenty-four_Filial_Exemplars" title="The Twenty-four Filial Exemplars">The Twenty-four Cases of Filial Piety</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKutcher200645_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKutcher200645-80">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction_of_Buddhism">Introduction of Buddhism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filial_piety&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Introduction of Buddhism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Filial_piety_in_Buddhism" title="Filial piety in Buddhism">Filial piety in Buddhism</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dazu_2007_797.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Buddha image gesturing, and surrounded by reliefs depicting stories" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Dazu_2007_797.jpg/170px-Dazu_2007_797.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Dazu_2007_797.jpg/255px-Dazu_2007_797.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Dazu_2007_797.jpg/340px-Dazu_2007_797.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1944" data-file-height="2592" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dazu_2007_797.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Buddha image with scenes of stories in which he repaid his parents. <a href="/wiki/Baodingshan" title="Baodingshan">Baodingshan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dazu" class="mw-redirect" title="Dazu">Dazu</a>, China</div></div></div>
<p>Filial piety is an important aspect of <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_ethics" title="Buddhist ethics">Buddhist ethics</a> since <a href="/wiki/Early_Buddhism" title="Early Buddhism">early Buddhism</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStrong1983_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStrong1983-81">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> and was essential in the apologetics and texts of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Buddhism" title="Chinese Buddhism">Chinese Buddhism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECh&#39;en1973_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECh&#39;en1973-82">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Early_Buddhist_Texts" title="Early Buddhist Texts">Early Buddhist Texts</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Nikaya" class="mw-redirect" title="Nikaya">Nikāyas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agama_(Buddhism)" class="mw-redirect" title="Agama (Buddhism)">Āgamas</a>, filial piety is prescribed and practiced in three ways: to repay the gratitude toward one's parents; as a good karma or <a href="/wiki/Merit_(Buddhism)" title="Merit (Buddhism)">merit</a>; and as a way to contribute to and sustain the social order.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEXing2016214_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEXing2016214-83">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_scriptures" class="mw-redirect" title="Buddhist scriptures">Buddhist scriptures</a>, narratives are given of the <a href="/wiki/Gautama_Buddha" title="Gautama Buddha">Buddha</a> and his <a href="/wiki/%C5%9Ar%C4%81vaka" title="Śrāvaka">disciples</a> practicing filial piety toward their parents, based on the qualities of gratitude and reciprocity.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEXing2016220_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEXing2016220-84">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEXing201283_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEXing201283-85">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> Initially, scholars of Buddhism like Kenneth Ch'en saw Buddhist teachings on filial piety as a distinct feature of Chinese Buddhism. Later scholarship, led by people such as <a href="/wiki/John_S._Strong" title="John S. Strong">John Strong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gregory_Schopen" title="Gregory Schopen">Gregory Schopen</a>, has come to believe that filial piety was part of Buddhist doctrine since <a href="/wiki/Early_Buddhism" title="Early Buddhism">early times</a>. Strong and Schopen have provided <a href="/wiki/Epigraphy" title="Epigraphy">epigraphical</a> and textual evidence to show that early Buddhist laypeople, monks and nuns often displayed strong devotion to their parents, concluding filial piety was already an important part of the <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_devotion" title="Buddhist devotion">devotional life</a> of early Buddhists.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchopen199757,_62,_65–7_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchopen199757,_62,_65–7-86">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStrong1983172–3_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStrong1983172–3-87">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When Buddhism was introduced in China, it had no organized celibacy.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZurcher2007281_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZurcher2007281-88">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucianism</a> emphasized filial piety to parents and loyalty to the emperor, and Buddhist monastic life was seen to go against its tenets<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECh&#39;en196882_89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECh&#39;en196882-89">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup>. In the 3rd–5th century, as criticism of Buddhism increased, Buddhist monastics and lay authors responded by writing about and translating Buddhist doctrines and narratives that supported filiality, comparing them to Confucianism and thereby defending Buddhism and its value in society.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECh&#39;en196882–3_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECh&#39;en196882–3-90">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Mouzi_Lihuolun" title="Mouzi Lihuolun">Mouzi Lihuolun</a></i> referred to <a href="/wiki/Thirteen_Classics" title="Thirteen Classics">Confucian and Daoist classics</a>, as well as historical precedents to respond to critics of Buddhism.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEXing201810_91-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEXing201810-91">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> The <i>Mouzi</i> stated that while on the surface the Buddhist monk seems to reject and abandon his parents, he is actually aiding his parents as well as himself on the path towards enlightenment.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKunio2004115–6_92-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKunio2004115–6-92">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sun_Chuo" title="Sun Chuo">Sun Chuo</a> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr>300–380) further argued that monks were working to ensure the salvation of all people and making their family proud by doing so,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKunio2004115–6_92-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKunio2004115–6-92">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Liu_Xie" title="Liu Xie">Liu Xie</a> stated that Buddhists practiced filial piety by <a href="/wiki/Transfer_of_merit" title="Transfer of merit">sharing merit</a> with their departed relatives.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEXing201812_93-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEXing201812-93">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup> Buddhist monks were also criticized for not expressing their respect to the Chinese emperor by <a href="/wiki/Kowtow" title="Kowtow">prostrating</a> and other devotion, which in Confucianism was associated with the virtue of filial piety. <a href="/wiki/Huiyuan_(Buddhist)" title="Huiyuan (Buddhist)">Huiyuan</a> (334–416) responded that although monks did not express such piety, they did pay homage in heart and mind; moreover, their teaching of morality and virtue to the public helped support imperial rule.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECh&#39;en196894_94-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECh&#39;en196894-94">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEXing2016224_95-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEXing2016224-95">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From the 6th century onward, Chinese Buddhists began to realize that they had to stress Buddhism's own particular ideas about filial piety in order to for Buddhism to survive.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStrong1983178_96-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStrong1983178-96">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Syama_Jataka" title="Syama Jataka">Śyāma</a>, Sujāti and other Buddhist stories of self-sacrifice spread a belief that a filial child should even be willing to sacrifice its own body.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKnapp2014135–6,_141,_145_97-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKnapp2014135–6,_141,_145-97">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStrong1983178_96-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStrong1983178-96">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Ullambana_Sutra" class="mw-redirect" title="Ullambana Sutra">Ullambana Sūtra</a></i> introduced the idea of <a href="/wiki/Transfer_of_merit" title="Transfer of merit">transfer of merit</a> through the story of <i><a href="/wiki/Mulian_Saves_His_Mother" class="mw-redirect" title="Mulian Saves His Mother">Mulian Saves His Mother</a></i> and led to the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Ghost_Festival" title="Ghost Festival">Ghost Festival</a>. By this Buddhists attempted to show that filial piety also meant taking care of one's parents in the next life, not just this life.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilson2014194_98-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilson2014194-98">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, authors in China—and to some extent Japan—wrote that in Buddhism, all <a href="/wiki/Sentient_being_(Buddhism)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sentient being (Buddhism)">living beings</a> have once been one's parents, and that practicing <a href="/wiki/Karu%E1%B9%87%C4%81" title="Karuṇā">compassion</a> to all living beings as though they were one's parents is the more superior form of filial piety.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELi-tian201041,_46_99-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELi-tian201041,_46-99">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup> Another aspect emphasized was the great suffering a mother goes through when giving birth and raising a child. Chinese Buddhists described how difficult it is to repay the goodness of one's mother, and how many <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_views_on_sin" title="Buddhist views on sin">sins</a> mothers often committed in raising her children.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIdema2009xvii_100-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIdema2009xvii-100">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup> The mother became the primary source of well-being and indebtedness for the son, which was in contrast with pre-Buddhist perspectives emphasizing the father.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECole19942_101-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECole19942-101">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup> Nevertheless, although some critics of Buddhism did not have much impact during this time, this changed in the period leading up to the <a href="/wiki/Neo-Confucianism" title="Neo-Confucianism">Neo-Confucianist</a> revival, when Emperor <a href="/wiki/Wu_Zong" class="mw-redirect" title="Wu Zong">Wu Zong</a> (841–845) started the <a href="/wiki/Great_Anti-Buddhist_Persecution" title="Great Anti-Buddhist Persecution">Great Anti-Buddhist Persecution</a>, citing lack of filial piety as one of his reasons for attacking Buddhist institutions.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith19937,_10–1_102-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith19937,_10–1-102">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Filial piety is still an important value in a number of Asian Buddhist cultures. In China, Buddhism continued to uphold a role in state rituals and mourning rites for ancestors, up until late imperial times (13th–20th century).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith199312–3_103-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith199312–3-103">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup> Also, <i><a href="/wiki/Sutra" title="Sutra">sūtras</a></i> and narratives about filial piety are still widely used.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEXing2016224_95-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEXing2016224-95">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup> The Ghost Festival is still popular in many Asian countries, especially those countries which are influenced by both Buddhism and Confucianism.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETruitt2015292_104-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETruitt2015292-104">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_imperial_period">Late imperial period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filial_piety&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Late imperial period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Guo_Ju_from_24_Paragons_of_Filial_Piety_by_Okumura_Masanobu.JPG" class="image"><img alt="Woodblock print with color, showing an old man and a young women gazing at the sky" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Guo_Ju_from_24_Paragons_of_Filial_Piety_by_Okumura_Masanobu.JPG/170px-Guo_Ju_from_24_Paragons_of_Filial_Piety_by_Okumura_Masanobu.JPG" decoding="async" width="170" height="345" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Guo_Ju_from_24_Paragons_of_Filial_Piety_by_Okumura_Masanobu.JPG/255px-Guo_Ju_from_24_Paragons_of_Filial_Piety_by_Okumura_Masanobu.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Guo_Ju_from_24_Paragons_of_Filial_Piety_by_Okumura_Masanobu.JPG/340px-Guo_Ju_from_24_Paragons_of_Filial_Piety_by_Okumura_Masanobu.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1674" data-file-height="3402" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Guo_Ju_from_24_Paragons_of_Filial_Piety_by_Okumura_Masanobu.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Woodblock print with illustration of <a href="/wiki/The_Twenty-four_Filial_Exemplars" title="The Twenty-four Filial Exemplars">The Twenty-four Cases of Filial Piety</a>. <a href="/wiki/Okumura_Masanobu" title="Okumura Masanobu">Okumura Masanobu</a>, early 1730s.</div></div></div>
<p>During the 17th century, some missionaries tried to prevent Chinese people from worshiping their ancestors. This was regarded as an assault on Chinese culture.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHsuO&#39;ConnorLee2009159_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHsuO&#39;ConnorLee2009159-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Qing dynasty</a>, however, filial piety was redefined by the emperor <a href="/wiki/Kangxi" class="mw-redirect" title="Kangxi">Kangxi</a> (1654–1722), who felt it more important that his officials were loyal to him than that they were filial sons: civil servants were often not allowed to go on extended leave to perform mourning rituals for their parents. The parallel conception of society therefore disappeared from Chinese society.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKutcher2006120_105-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKutcher2006120-105">&#91;104&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Unlike western societies, <a href="/wiki/Patriarchalism" title="Patriarchalism">patriarchalism</a> and its enactment in law grew more strict in late imperial China. The duties of the obedient child were much precisely and rigidly prescribed, to the extent that legal scholar <a href="/wiki/Hsu_Dau-lin" title="Hsu Dau-lin">Hsu Dau-lin</a> argued about this period that it "engendered a highly authoritarian spirit which was entirely alien to Confucius himself". Indeed, the late imperial Chinese held patriarchalism high as an organizing principle of society, as laws and punishments gradually became more strict and severe.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHamilton199087–8,_97_106-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHamilton199087–8,_97-106">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>But during the same time, in Japan, a classic work about filial practices was compiled, called <i>Biographies of Japanese Filial Children</i> (<a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Japanese" title="Romanization of Japanese">Japanese pronunciation</a>: <i>Fu San Ko Shi Dan</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHsu199862_36-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHsu199862-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="19th.E2.80.9320th_century"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="19th–20th_century">19th–20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filial_piety&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 19th–20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the rise of communism in China in the early 20th century, Confucian values and family-centered living were discouraged by the state.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESung2009a180_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESung2009a180-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/New_Culture_Movement" title="New Culture Movement">New Culture Movement</a> of 1911, Chinese intellectuals and foreign missionaries attacked the principle of filial piety, the latter considering it an obstruction of progress.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHsu199861_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHsu199861-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In Japan, filial piety was not regarded as an obstacle to modernization, though scholars are in disagreement as to why this was the case.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHsu199862_36-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHsu199862-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Francis Hsu believed that "the human networks through which it found concrete expressions" were different in Japan, and there never was a movement against filial piety as there was in China.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHsu199862_36-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHsu199862-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The late imperial trend of increased patriarchalism made it difficult for the Chinese to build strong patrimonial groups that went beyond kin.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHamilton199097_107-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHamilton199097-107">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup> Though filial piety was practiced much in both countries, the Chinese way was more limited to close kin than in Japan. When industrialization increased, filial piety was therefore criticized more in China than in Japan, because China felt it limited the way the country could meet the challenges from the West.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHsu199867_108-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHsu199867-108">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup> For this reason, China developed a more critical stance to filial piety and other aspects of Confucianism than other East Asian countries, including not only Japan, but also Taiwan. In the 1950s, <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">Mao Zedong</a>'s socialist measures led to the dissolution of family businesses and more dependence on the state instead; Taiwan's socialism did not go that far in state control. <sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWhyte2004107–8_109-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhyte2004107–8-109">&#91;108&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ethnographic evidence from the 19th and early 20th century shows that Chinese people still very much cared for their elders, and very often lived with one or more married sons.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWhyte2004106_110-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhyte2004106-110">&#91;109&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Developments_in_modern_society">Developments in modern society</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filial_piety&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Developments in modern society">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 21st century-Chinese societies, filial piety expectations and practice have decreased. One cause for this is the rise of the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_family" title="Nuclear family">nuclear family</a> without much co-residence with parents. Families are becoming smaller because of <a href="/wiki/Family_planning" title="Family planning">family planning</a> and housing shortage. Other causes of decrease in practice are individualism, the loss of status of elderly, emigration of young people to cities and the independence of young people and women.<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">&#91;110&#93;</a></sup> To amplify this trend, the number of elderly people has increased fast.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESung2009a180_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESung2009a180-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The relationship between husband and wife came to be more emphasized, and the extended family less and less. Kinship ties between the husband and wife's families have become more bi-lateral and equal.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWhyte2004108_112-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhyte2004108-112">&#91;111&#93;</a></sup> The way respect to elders is expressed is also changing. Communication with elders tends to be become more reciprocal and less one-way, and kindness and courtesy is replacing obedience and subservience.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESung200121_113-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESung200121-113">&#91;112&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Care-giving">Care-giving</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filial_piety&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Care-giving">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Headrest_with_Scenes_of_Filial_Piety_LACMA_M.73.48.83.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Stone headrest with illustration of young person saluting a woman" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Headrest_with_Scenes_of_Filial_Piety_LACMA_M.73.48.83.jpg/170px-Headrest_with_Scenes_of_Filial_Piety_LACMA_M.73.48.83.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="126" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Headrest_with_Scenes_of_Filial_Piety_LACMA_M.73.48.83.jpg/255px-Headrest_with_Scenes_of_Filial_Piety_LACMA_M.73.48.83.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Headrest_with_Scenes_of_Filial_Piety_LACMA_M.73.48.83.jpg/340px-Headrest_with_Scenes_of_Filial_Piety_LACMA_M.73.48.83.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2100" data-file-height="1552" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Headrest_with_Scenes_of_Filial_Piety_LACMA_M.73.48.83.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Stone headrest with scenes of filial piety, <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming dynasty</a> (1368–1644)</div></div></div>
<p>In modern Chinese societies, elderly care has much changed. Studies have shown that there is a discrepancy between the parents' filial expectations and the actual behaviors of their children.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFungCheng2010315_56-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFungCheng2010315-56">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Especially the discrepancy with regard to respect shown by the children makes elderly people unhappy.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFungCheng2010315_56-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFungCheng2010315-56">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYee2006_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYee2006-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Industrialization and urbanization have affected the practice of filial piety, with care being given more in financial ways rather than personal.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYee2006_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYee2006-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> But as of 2009, care-giving of the young to elderly people had not undergone any revolutionary changes in Mainland China, and family obligations still remained strong, still "almost automatic".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESung2009a181,_185_114-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESung2009a181,_185-114">&#91;113&#93;</a></sup> Respect to elders remains a central value for East Asian people.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESung200122_115-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESung200122-115">&#91;114&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Comparing data from the 1990s from Taiwan and PRC, sociologist <a href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_Whyte&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Martin Whyte (page does not exist)">Martin Whyte</a> concludes that Taiwan, despite this being an economically more modern nation than China, the elderly often received less government support, but received more assistance from their children than in China.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWhyte2004117–8_116-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhyte2004117–8-116">&#91;115&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Work_ethos_and_business_practices">Work ethos and business practices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filial_piety&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Work ethos and business practices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In mainland China business culture, the culture of filial piety is decreasing in influence. As of 2003, western-style business practices and <a href="/wiki/Managerial_style" class="mw-redirect" title="Managerial style">managerial style</a> were promoted by the Chinese government to modernize the country.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFuTsui2003426_117-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFuTsui2003426-117">&#91;116&#93;</a></sup> However, in Japan, employees usually regard their employer as a sort of father, to which they feel obliged to express filial devotion.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOh199150_118-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOh199150-118">&#91;117&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_with_law">Relation with law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filial_piety&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Relation with law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Filial_responsibility_laws" title="Filial responsibility laws">Filial responsibility laws</a></div>
<p>In some societies with large Chinese communities, legislation has been introduced, to establish or uphold filial piety. In the 2000s, Singapore introduced a law that makes it an offense to refuse to support one's elderly parents; Taiwan has taken similar punitive measures. Hong Kong, on the other hand, has attempted to influence its population by providing incentives for fulfilling their obligations. For example, certain tax allowances are given to citizens that are willing to live with their elderly parents.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChow2009319–20_119-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChow2009319–20-119">&#91;118&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some scholars have argued that medieval China's reliance on governance by filial piety formed a society that was better able to prevent crime and other misconduct than societies that did so through only legal means.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKutcher2006194_78-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKutcher2006194-78">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="East_Asian_immigrants">East Asian immigrants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filial_piety&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: East Asian immigrants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Chinese who immigrate to the United States generally continue to send money to their parents out of filial piety.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHsu198599_120-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHsu198599-120">&#91;119&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
