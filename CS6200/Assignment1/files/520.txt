<p>A <b>wife</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Female" title="Female">female</a> <a href="/wiki/Significant_other" title="Significant other">partner</a> in a continuing <a href="/wiki/Marital_relationship" class="mw-redirect" title="Marital relationship">marital relationship</a>.
</p><p>The term continues to be applied to a woman who has separated from her partner, and ceases to be applied to such a woman only when her marriage has come to an end, following a legally recognized <a href="/wiki/Divorce" title="Divorce">divorce</a> or the death of her spouse. On the death of her partner, a wife is referred to as a <a href="/wiki/Widow" title="Widow">widow</a>, but not after she is divorced from her partner.
</p><p>The rights and obligations of a wife in relation to her partner and her status in the community and in law vary between cultures and have varied over time.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Summary">Summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wedding_and_Engagement_Rings_2151px.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Wedding_and_Engagement_Rings_2151px.jpg/220px-Wedding_and_Engagement_Rings_2151px.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="186" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Wedding_and_Engagement_Rings_2151px.jpg/330px-Wedding_and_Engagement_Rings_2151px.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Wedding_and_Engagement_Rings_2151px.jpg/440px-Wedding_and_Engagement_Rings_2151px.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2151" data-file-height="1815" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wedding_and_Engagement_Rings_2151px.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/White_gold" class="mw-redirect" title="White gold">white gold</a> <a href="/wiki/Wedding_ring" title="Wedding ring">wedding ring</a> and a single-<a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">diamond</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>-banded engagement ring. In many cultures, wives show their marital status through various symbols.</div></div></div>
<p>The word is of Germanic origin, from <a href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" class="mw-redirect" title="Proto-Germanic">Proto-Germanic</a> *<i>wībam</i>, "woman". In <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> it had the form <i>wif</i>, and in Old English <i>wīf</i>, "<a href="/wiki/Woman" title="Woman">woman</a> or wife". It is related to Modern <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> <i>Weib</i> (woman, female),<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish</a> <i>viv</i> (wife, usually poetic) and may derive ultimately from the Indo-European root <i>ghwībh-</i> "shame; pudenda" (cf. <a href="/wiki/Tocharian_languages" title="Tocharian languages">Tocharian</a> B <i>kwīpe</i> and Tocharian A <i>kip</i>, each meaning "female pudenda", with clear sexual overtones).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The original meaning of the phrase "wife" as simply "woman", unconnected with marriage or a husband/wife, is preserved in words such as "<a href="/wiki/Midwife" title="Midwife">midwife</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Fishmonger" title="Fishmonger">fishwife</a>".
</p><p>In many cultures, marriage is generally expected that a woman will take her husband's <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a>, though that is not universal. A married woman may indicate her <a href="/wiki/Marital_status" title="Marital status">marital status</a> in a number of ways: in <a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">Western culture</a> a married woman would commonly wear a <a href="/wiki/Wedding_ring" title="Wedding ring">wedding ring</a> but in other cultures other <a href="/wiki/Visual_markers_of_marital_status" title="Visual markers of marital status">markers of marital status</a> may be used. A married woman is commonly given the <a href="/wiki/Honorific" title="Honorific">honorific</a> title "<a href="/wiki/Mrs" class="mw-redirect" title="Mrs">Mrs</a>", but some married women prefer to be referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Ms." title="Ms.">Ms</a>", a title which is also used when the marital status of a woman is unknown.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_terminology">Related terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Related terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bride_at_Nikah.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Bride_at_Nikah.jpg/220px-Bride_at_Nikah.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Bride_at_Nikah.jpg/330px-Bride_at_Nikah.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Bride_at_Nikah.jpg/440px-Bride_at_Nikah.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="960" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bride_at_Nikah.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A young bride at her <a href="/wiki/Nikah" class="mw-redirect" title="Nikah">Nikah</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>A woman on her wedding day is usually described as a <i><a href="/wiki/Bride" title="Bride">bride</a></i>, even after the <i><a href="/wiki/Wedding" title="Wedding">wedding</a> ceremony</i>, while being described as a wife is also appropriate after the wedding or after the honeymoon. If she is marrying a man, her partner is known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Groom" class="mw-redirect" title="Groom">bridegroom</a></i> during the wedding, and within the marriage is called her <i><a href="/wiki/Husband" title="Husband">husband</a></i>.
</p><p>In the older custom, still followed, e. g., by Roman Catholic ritual, the word <i>bride</i> actually means <a href="/wiki/Fianc%C3%A9e" class="mw-redirect" title="Fiancée">fiancée</a> and applies up to the exchange of matrimonial consent (the actual marriage act); from then on, even while the rest of the very ceremony is ongoing, the woman is a wife, and no longer a bride, and the bridal couple is no longer referred to as such but as the newlywed couple.
</p><p>"Wife" refers to the institutionalized relation <i>to the other spouse</i>, unlike <a href="/wiki/Mother" title="Mother">mother</a>, a term that puts a woman into the context of her children. In some societies, especially historically, a <i><a href="/wiki/Concubine" class="mw-redirect" title="Concubine">concubine</a></i> was a woman who was in an ongoing, usually matrimonially oriented relationship with a man who could not be married to her, often because of a difference in social status.
</p><p>The term <i>wife</i> is most commonly applied to a woman in a union sanctioned by law (including <a href="/wiki/Religious_law" title="Religious law">religious law</a>), not to a woman in an informal <a href="/wiki/Cohabitation" title="Cohabitation">cohabitation</a> relationship, which may be known as a girlfriend, partner, cohabitant, significant other, <a href="/wiki/Concubine" class="mw-redirect" title="Concubine">concubine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mistress_(lover)" title="Mistress (lover)">mistress</a> etc. However, a woman in a so-called <a href="/wiki/Common_law_marriage" class="mw-redirect" title="Common law marriage">common law marriage</a> may describe herself as a common law wife, de facto wife, or simply a wife. Those seeking to advance <a href="/wiki/Gender_neutrality" title="Gender neutrality">gender neutrality</a> may refer to both marriage partners as "spouses", and many countries and societies are rewording their statute law by replacing "wife" and "husband" with "spouse". A former wife whose spouse is deceased is a <i><a href="/wiki/Widow" title="Widow">widow</a></i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Termination_of_the_status_of_a_wife">Termination of the status of a wife</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Termination of the status of a wife">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The status of a wife may be terminated by <a href="/wiki/Divorce" title="Divorce">divorce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Annulment" title="Annulment">annulment</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">death</a> of a spouse. In the case of divorce, terminology such as <i>former-wife</i> or <i>ex-wife</i> is often used. With regard to annulment, such terms are not, strictly speaking, correct, because annulment, unlike divorce, is usually <a href="/wiki/Ex_post_facto_law" title="Ex post facto law">retroactive</a>, meaning that an annulled marriage is considered to be invalid from the beginning almost as if it had never taken place. In the case of the death of the other spouse, the term used is <a href="/wiki/Widow" title="Widow">widow</a>. The social status of such women varies by culture, but in some places, they may be subject to potentially harmful practices, such as <a href="/wiki/Widow_inheritance" title="Widow inheritance">widow inheritance</a> or <a href="/wiki/Levirate_marriage" title="Levirate marriage">levirate marriage</a>; or divorced women may be socially stigmatized.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In some cultures, the termination of the status of wife made life itself meaningless, as in the case of those cultures that practiced <a href="/wiki/Sati_(practice)" title="Sati (practice)">sati</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Funeral" title="Funeral">funeral</a> ritual within some <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asian</a> communities, in which a recently widowed woman <a href="/wiki/Self-immolation" title="Self-immolation">committed suicide by fire</a>, typically on the husband's <a href="/wiki/Funeral_pyre" class="mw-redirect" title="Funeral pyre">funeral pyre</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_rights_of_the_wife">Legal rights of the wife</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legal rights of the wife">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Coverture" title="Coverture">Coverture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marital_power" title="Marital power">Marital power</a></div>
<p>The legal rights of a wife have been since the 19th century, and still are, in many jurisdictions subject to debate. This subject was in particular addressed by <a href="/wiki/John_Stuart_Mill" title="John Stuart Mill">John Stuart Mill</a>, in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Subjection_of_Women" title="The Subjection of Women">The Subjection of Women</a></i> (1869). Historically, many societies have given sets of rights and obligations to husbands that have been very different from the sets of rights and obligations given to wives. In particular, the control of marital property, inheritance rights, and the right to dictate the activities of children of the marriage, have typically been given to male marital partners. However, this practice was curtailed to a great deal in many countries in the twentieth century, and more modern statutes tend to define the rights and duties of a spouse without reference to gender.  Among the last European countries to establish full <a href="/wiki/Gender_equality" title="Gender equality">gender equality</a> in marriage were Switzerland,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Greece,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Spain,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and France<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> in the 1980s. In various <a href="/wiki/Marriage_laws" class="mw-redirect" title="Marriage laws">marriage laws</a> around the world, however, the husband continues to have authority; for instance the Civil Code of <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> states at Article 1105: <i>"In relations between husband and wife; the position of the head of the family is the exclusive right of the husband"</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exchanges_of_goods_or_money">Exchanges of goods or money</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Exchanges of goods or money">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Thai_Bride_Price_2008.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Thai_Bride_Price_2008.jpg/220px-Thai_Bride_Price_2008.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Thai_Bride_Price_2008.jpg/330px-Thai_Bride_Price_2008.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Thai_Bride_Price_2008.jpg/440px-Thai_Bride_Price_2008.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4368" data-file-height="2912" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Thai_Bride_Price_2008.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A traditional, formal presentation of the <a href="/wiki/Bride_price" title="Bride price">bride price</a> at a <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thai</a> engagement ceremony.</div></div></div> 
<p>Traditionally, and still in some parts of the world, the bride or her family bring her husband a <i><a href="/wiki/Dowry" title="Dowry">dowry</a></i>, or the husband or his family pay a <i><a href="/wiki/Bride_price" title="Bride price">bride price</a></i> to the bride's family, or both are exchanged between the families; or the husband pays the wife a <i><a href="/wiki/Dower" title="Dower">dower</a></i>. The purpose of the dowry varies by culture and has varied historically. In some cultures, it was paid not only to support the establishment of a new family, but also served as a condition that if the husband committed grave offenses upon his wife, the dowry had to be returned to the wife or her family; but during the marriage, the dowry was often made <a href="/wiki/Inalienable_right" class="mw-redirect" title="Inalienable right">inalienable</a> by the husband.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Today, dowries continue to be expected in parts of <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> such as India, Pakistan, Nepal, Bangladesh, and Sri Lanka, and conflicts related to their payment sometimes result in violence such as <a href="/wiki/Dowry_deaths" class="mw-redirect" title="Dowry deaths">dowry deaths</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bride_burning" title="Bride burning">bride burning</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Changing_of_name_upon_marriage">Changing of name upon marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Changing of name upon marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Maiden_and_married_names" title="Maiden and married names">Maiden and married names</a></div>
<p>In some cultures, particularly in the <a href="/wiki/English-speaking_world" title="English-speaking world">Anglophone</a> <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">West</a>, wives often change their surnames to that of the husband upon getting married. For some, this is a controversial practice, due to its tie to the historical doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Coverture" title="Coverture">coverture</a> and to the historically subordinated roles of wives. Others argue that today this is merely a harmless tradition that should be accepted as a free choice.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Some jurisdictions consider this practice as discriminatory and contrary to women's rights, and have restricted or banned it; for example, since 1983, when <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> adopted a new <a href="/wiki/Marriage_law" title="Marriage law">marriage law</a> which guaranteed <a href="/wiki/Gender_equality" title="Gender equality">gender equality</a> between the spouses,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> women in Greece are required to <i>keep</i> their birth names for their whole life.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Childbearing">Childbearing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Childbearing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Non_marital_by_countries.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Non_marital_by_countries.gif/350px-Non_marital_by_countries.gif" decoding="async" width="350" height="208" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/12/Non_marital_by_countries.gif 1.5x" data-file-width="440" data-file-height="262" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Non_marital_by_countries.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Percentage of births to unmarried women, selected countries, 1980 and 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-non_mar1_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-non_mar1-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Traditionally, and still in many cultures, the role of a wife was closely tied to that of a <a href="/wiki/Mother" title="Mother">mother</a>, by a strong expectation that a wife ought to bear children, while, conversely, an unmarried woman should not have a <a href="/wiki/Legitimacy_(family_law)" title="Legitimacy (family law)">child out of wedlock</a>. These views have changed in many parts of the world. Children born outside marriage have become more common in many countries.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Although some wives in particular in Western countries <a href="/wiki/Childfree" class="mw-redirect" title="Childfree">choose not to have children</a>, such a choice is not accepted in some parts of the world. In <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ghana" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Ghana">northern Ghana</a>, for example, the payment of bride price signifies a woman's requirement to bear children, and women using birth control are at risk of threats and coercion.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In addition, some religions are interpreted as requiring children in marriage; for instance <a href="/wiki/Pope_Francis" title="Pope Francis">Pope Francis</a> said in 2015 that choosing not to have children was "selfish".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Differences_in_cultures">Differences in cultures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Differences in cultures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">The various divisions of the following chapters share the previous terminology in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a>, notwithstanding religious and cultural, but also customary differences.</div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antiquity">Antiquity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Antiquity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Seuso_and_his_wife_at_Lake_Balaton.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Seuso_and_his_wife_at_Lake_Balaton.jpg/220px-Seuso_and_his_wife_at_Lake_Balaton.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="291" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Seuso_and_his_wife_at_Lake_Balaton.jpg/330px-Seuso_and_his_wife_at_Lake_Balaton.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/27/Seuso_and_his_wife_at_Lake_Balaton.jpg 2x" data-file-width="382" data-file-height="506" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Seuso_and_his_wife_at_Lake_Balaton.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Seuso and his wife</div></div></div>
<p>Many traditions like a dower, dowry and bride price have long traditions in antiquity. The exchange of any item or value goes back to the oldest sources, and the wedding ring likewise was always used as a symbol for keeping faith to a person.
</p><p><br />
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_cultures">Western cultures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Western cultures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_status">Historical status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Historical status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In ancient Rome, The Emperor <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a> introduced marriage legislation, the <a href="/wiki/Lex_Papia_Poppaea" title="Lex Papia Poppaea">Lex Papia Poppaea</a>, which rewarded marriage and childbearing. The legislation also imposed penalties on young persons who failed to marry and on those who committed adultery. Therefore, marriage and childbearing was made law between the ages of twenty-five and sixty for men, and twenty and fifty for women.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Women who were Vestals Virgins, were selected between the ages of 6 and 10 to serve as priestesses in the temple of goddess Vesta in the Roman Forum for 30 years after which time they could marry. <sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Noble women were known to marry as young as 12 years of age,<sup id="cite_ref-Beryl_Rawson_1999_p._21_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beryl_Rawson_1999_p._21-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> whereas women in the lower classes were more likely to marry slightly further into their teenage years. <sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Ancient Roman law required brides to be at least 12 years old, a standard adopted by Roman Catholic <a href="/wiki/Canon_law" title="Canon law">canon law</a>. In ancient Roman law, first marriages to brides aged 12–25 required the consent of the bride and her father, but by the late antique period Roman law permitted women over 25 to marry without parental consent. <sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The father had the right and duty to seek a good and useful match for his children, and might arrange a child's <a href="/wiki/Betrothal" class="mw-redirect" title="Betrothal">betrothal</a> long before he or she came of age. <sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> To further the interests of their birth families, daughters of the elite should marry into respectable families. <sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> If a daughter could prove the proposed husband to be of bad character, she could legitimately refuse the match. <sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The age of lawful consent to a marriage was 12 for maidens and 14 for youths.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In late antiquity, Most Roman women seem to have married in their late teens to early twenties, but <a href="/wiki/Nobiles" title="Nobiles">noble women</a> married younger than those of the lower classes, and an aristocratic maiden was expected to be virgin until her first marriage.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> In late antiquity, under Roman law, daughters inherited equally from their parents if no will was produced. <sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> In addition, Roman law recognized wives' property as legally separate from husbands's property,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> as did some legal systems in parts of Europe and colonial Latin America.
</p><p>Christian cultures claim to be guided by the Holy <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> in regard to their view on the position of a wife in society as well as her marriage. The Holy New Testament condemns divorce for both men and women (1 Cor 7:10–11), and assumes <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamy</a> on the part of the husband: the wife is to have her "own" husband, and the husband is to have his "own" wife (1 Cor 7:2). In the medieval period, this was understood to mean that a wife should not share a husband with other wives. As a result, divorce was relatively uncommon in the pre-modern West, particularly in the medieval and <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_period" title="Early modern period">early modern period</a>, and husbands in the Roman, later medieval and early modern period did not publicly take more than one wife.  
</p><p>In pre-modern times, it was unusual to marry for love alone,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> although it became an ideal in literature by the early modern period.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> In the 12th century, the Roman Catholic Church drastically changed legal standards for marital consent by allowing daughters over 12 and sons over 14 to marry without their parents' approval, even if their marriage was made clandestinely.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Parish studies have confirmed that late medieval women did sometimes marry against their parents' approval.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The Roman Catholic Church's policy of considering clandestine marriages and marriages made without parental consent to be valid was controversial, and in the 16th century both the French monarchy and the Lutheran church sought to end these practices, with limited success.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The Holy New Testament made no pronouncements about wives' property rights, which in practice were influenced more by secular laws than religion. Most influential in the pre-modern West was the <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(legal_system)" title="Civil law (legal system)">civil law</a>, except in English-speaking countries where English <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> emerged in the High Middle Ages. In addition, local customary law influenced wives' property rights; as a result wives' property rights in the pre-modern West varied widely from region to region. Because wives' property rights and daughters' inheritance rights varied widely from region to region due to differing legal systems, the amount of property a wife might own varied greatly. Under the English common law system, which dates to the later medieval period, daughters and younger sons were usually excluded from landed property if no will was produced. Under English common law moved to a system where a wife with a living husband ("feme couvert") could own little property in her own name.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Unable to easily support herself, marriage was very important to most women's economic status. This problem has been dealt with extensively in literature, where the most important reason for women's limited power was the denial of equal education and equal property rights for females.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The situation was assessed by the English conservative moralist Sir <a href="/wiki/William_Blackstone" title="William Blackstone">William Blackstone</a>: "The husband and wife are one, and the husband is the one."<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Married women's property rights in the English-speaking world improved with the <a href="/wiki/Married_Women%27s_Property_Act_1882" title="Married Women&#39;s Property Act 1882">Married Women's Property Act 1882</a> and similar legal changes, which allowed wives with living husbands to own property in their own names. Until late in the 20th century, women could in some regions or times sue a man for <a href="/wiki/Wreath_money" class="mw-redirect" title="Wreath money">wreath money</a> when he took her <a href="/wiki/Virginity" title="Virginity">virginity</a> without taking her as his wife.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>If a woman did not want to marry, another option was entering a <a href="/wiki/Convent" title="Convent">convent</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Nun" title="Nun">nun</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> to become a "<a href="/wiki/Bride_of_Christ" title="Bride of Christ">bride of Christ</a>",<sup id="cite_ref-Taking_The_White_Veil_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taking_The_White_Veil-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> a state in which her chastity and economic survival would be protected.<sup id="cite_ref-Taking_The_White_Veil_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taking_The_White_Veil-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Both a wife and a nun wore <a href="/wiki/Christian_headcovering" title="Christian headcovering">Christian headcovering</a>, which proclaimed their state of protection by the rights of marriage.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Much more significant than the option of becoming a nun, was the option of non-religious spinsterhood in the West. An unmarried woman, a <a href="/wiki/Feme_sole" class="mw-redirect" title="Feme sole">feme sole</a>, had the right to own property and make contracts in her own name.  As first demonstrated quantitatively by John Hajnal, in the 19th and early 20th centuries the percentage of non-clerical Western women who never married was typically as high as 10–15%, a prevalence of female celibacy never yet documented for any other major traditional civilization.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> In addition, early modern Western women married at quite high ages (typically mid to late 20s) relative to other major traditional cultures. The high age at first marriage for Western women has been shown by many parish reconstruction studies to be a traditional Western marriage pattern that dates back at least as early as the mid-16th century.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_status">Contemporary status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Contemporary status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the 20th century, the role of the wife in Western marriage changed in two major ways; the first was the breakthrough from an "institution to companionate marriage";<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> for the first time since the Middle Ages, wives became distinct <a href="/wiki/Legal_entities" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal entities">legal entities</a>, and were allowed their own property and allowed to sue. Until then, partners were a single legal entity, but only a husband was allowed to exercise this right, called <a href="/wiki/Coverture" title="Coverture">coverture</a>. The second change was the drastic alteration of middle and upper-class family life, when in the 1960s these wives began to work outside their home, and with the social acceptance of <a href="/wiki/Divorce" title="Divorce">divorces</a> the single-parent family, and <a href="/wiki/Stepfamily" title="Stepfamily">stepfamily</a> or "blended family" as a more "individualized marriage".<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Today, some women may wear a <a href="/wiki/Wedding_ring" title="Wedding ring">wedding ring</a> in order to show her status as a wife.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Western countries today, married women usually have an <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Profession" title="Profession">profession</a> and they (or their husbands) can take time off from their work in a legally procured system of <a href="/wiki/Ante-natal_care" class="mw-redirect" title="Ante-natal care">ante-natal care</a>, statutory <a href="/wiki/Maternity_leave" class="mw-redirect" title="Maternity leave">maternity leave</a>, and they may get <a href="/wiki/Maternity_pay" class="mw-redirect" title="Maternity pay">maternity pay</a> or a maternity <a href="/wiki/Allowance_(money)" title="Allowance (money)">allowance</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> The status of marriage, as opposed to unmarried pregnant women, allows the <a href="/wiki/Spouse" title="Spouse">spouse</a> to be responsible for the child, and to speak on behalf of their wife; a partner is also responsible for the wife's child in states where they are automatically assumed to be the biological legal parent.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Vice versa, a wife has more legal authority in some cases when she speaks on behalf of a spouse than she would have if they were not married, e.g. when her spouse is in a coma after an accident, a wife may have the right of advocacy.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> If they <a href="/wiki/Divorce" title="Divorce">divorce</a>, she also might receive—or pay—<a href="/wiki/Alimony" title="Alimony">alimony</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Law_and_divorce_around_the_world" class="mw-redirect" title="Law and divorce around the world">Law and divorce around the world</a>).
</p><p><br />
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia_cultures">Asia cultures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Asia cultures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hinduism">Hinduism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Hinduism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Indo-Aryan_languages" title="Indo-Aryan languages">Indo-Aryan languages</a>, a wife is known as <i>Patni</i>, which means a woman who shares everything in this world with her husband and he does the same, including their identity. Decisions are ideally made in mutual consent. A wife usually takes care of anything inside her household, including the family's health, the children's education, a parent's needs.
</p><p>The majority of Hindu marriages in rural and traditional India are arranged marriages. Once they find a suitable family (family of same caste, culture and financial status), the boy and the girl see and talk to each other to decide the final outcome. In recent times however the western culture has had significant influence and the new generations are more open to the idea of marrying for love.
</p><p>Indian law has recognized  rape, sexual, emotional or verbal abuse of a woman by her husband as crimes. 
In <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>, a wife is known as a <i>Patni</i> or <i>Ardhangini</i> (similar to "the better half") meaning a part of the husband or his family. In Hinduism, a woman or man can get married, but only have one husband or wife respectively.
</p><p>In India, women may wear vermillion powder on their foreheads, an ornament called Mangalsutra (Hindi मंगलसूत्र ) which is a form of necklace, or rings on their toes (which are not worn by single women) to show their status as married women.
</p>

</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wife_in_Abrahamic_religions">Wife in Abrahamic religions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Wife in Abrahamic religions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wife_in_Christianity">Wife in Christianity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Wife in Christianity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Christian marriage is to be between one woman (adult female) and one man (adult male) and that God Himself joined them and that no human is to separate them, according to Christ (Matthew 19:4-6). The Holy New Testament states that an unmarried Christian woman is to celibate or is to become the Christian wife of one husband to avoid sexual immorality and for sexual passion (1 Cor 7:1-2 &amp; 8-9). Holy New Testament permits divorce of a Christian wife by a Christian husband only if she has committed adultery (Matthew 5:32). The Holy New Testament allows a Christian widow to (re)marry a man she chooses (1 Cor 7:39) but forbids a divorced Christian woman to (re)marry a man because she would be committing adultery if she did (Matthew 5:32), she is to remain unmarried and celibate or be reconciled with her husband (1 Cor 7:1-2 &amp; 8-9 and 1 Cor 7:10-11). A Christian wife can divorce a non-Christian husband if he wants a divorce (1 Cor 7:12-16). Christian husbands are to love their Christian wives as Christ loved the Church (Ephesians 5:25) and as he loves himself (Ephesian 5:33). The Christian wife is to respect her husband (Ephesians 5:33). Christian husbands are to not be harsh with their Christian wives (Colossians 3:19) and to treat them as a delicate vessel and with honor (1 Peter 3:7).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wife_in_Islam">Wife in Islam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Wife in Islam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Women_in_Islam" title="Women in Islam">Women in Islam</a> have a range of rights and obligations (see main article <a href="/wiki/Rights_and_obligations_of_spouses_in_Islam" class="mw-redirect" title="Rights and obligations of spouses in Islam">Rights and obligations of spouses in Islam</a>). Marriage takes place on the basis of a <a href="/wiki/Islamic_marriage_contract" title="Islamic marriage contract">marriage contract</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Arranged_marriage" title="Arranged marriage">arranged marriage</a> is relatively common in traditionalist families, whether in Muslim countries or as first or second generation immigrants elsewhere.
</p><p>Women in general are supposed to wear specific clothes, as stated by the <a href="/wiki/Hadith" title="Hadith">hadith</a>, like the <a href="/wiki/Hijab" title="Hijab">hijab</a>, which may take different styles depending on the culture of the country, where traditions may seep in.<sup>[<a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a>&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus%3Atext%3A2002.02.0006%3Asura%3D24%3Averse%3D31">24:31</a>]</sup><sup>[<a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a>&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus%3Atext%3A2002.02.0006%3Asura%3D33%3Averse%3D59">33:59</a>]</sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> The husband must pay a <i><a href="/wiki/Mahr" title="Mahr">mahr</a></i> to the bride.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Traditionally, the wife in Islam is seen as a protected, chaste person that manages the household and the family. She has the ever important role of raising the children and bringing up the next generation of Muslims. In Islam, it is highly recommended that the wife remains at home although they are fully able to own property or work. The husband is obligated to spend on the wife for all of her needs while she is not obligated to spend even if she is wealthy. <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a> is said to have commanded all Muslim men to treat their wives well. There is a Hadith by <a href="/wiki/Al-Tirmidhi" title="Al-Tirmidhi">Al-Tirmidhi</a>, in which Muhammad is said to have stated "The believers who show the most perfect faith are those who have the best character and the best of you are those who are best to their wives."<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Traditionally, Muslim married women are not distinguished from unmarried women by an outward symbol (such as a wedding ring). However, women's wedding rings have recently been adopted in the past thirty years from the Western culture.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wife_in_Judaism">Wife in Judaism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Wife in Judaism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rabbinic_Judaism"><a href="/wiki/Rabbinic_Judaism" title="Rabbinic Judaism">Rabbinic Judaism</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Rabbinic Judaism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Women_in_Judaism" title="Women in Judaism">Women in Judaism</a> have a range of rights and obligations ( see main article <a href="/wiki/Jewish_views_on_marriage" title="Jewish views on marriage">Jewish views on marriage</a>). Marriage takes place on the basis of a Jewish marriage contract, called a <a href="/wiki/Ketubah" title="Ketubah">Ketubah</a>. There is a blur of arranged marriages and love marriages in traditional families. 
</p><p>Married women, in traditional families, wear specific clothes, like the <a href="/wiki/Tichel" title="Tichel">tichel</a>. 
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hebrew_Bible"><a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Hebrew Bible">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In ancient times the husband paid a mohar. <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://bible.oremus.org/?passage=Genesis+34:12%E2%80%9334:12&amp;version=nrsv">Genesis 34:12</a>
</p><p>In ancient times there were Israelite women who were Judge, <a href="/wiki/Queen_regnant" title="Queen regnant">Queen regnant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Queen_regent" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen regent">Queen regent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Queen_mother" title="Queen mother">Queen mother</a>, <a href="/wiki/Queen_consort" title="Queen consort">Queen consort</a>, and Prophetess:
Deborah was the wife of an Israelite man whose name was Lapidoth, which means "torches." Deborah was a Judge and a Prophetess. <sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> 
Esther was the Jewish wife of a Persian King named Ahasuerus. Esther was Queen consort to the King of Persia and at the same time she was Queen regnant of the Jewish people in Persia and their Prophetess. <sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
Bathsheba was the Queen consort of King-Prophet David and then the Queen mother of King-Prophet Solomon. He rose from his throne when she entered and bowed to her and ordered that a throne be brought and he had her sit at his right hand, which is in stark contrast to when she was Queen consort and bowed to King-Prophet David when she entered. <sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> Prophet Jeremiah portrays a Queen mother as sharing in her son’s rule over the kingdom in <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://biblia.com/bible/nasb95/Jer%2013.18-20">Jeremiah 13:18-20</a>. 
The wife of Prophet Isaiah was a Prophetess. <a href="/wiki/Isaiah_8:3" class="mw-redirect" title="Isaiah 8:3">Isaiah 8:3</a> 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Expectation_of_fidelity_and_violence_related_to_adultery">Expectation of fidelity and violence related to adultery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Expectation of fidelity and violence related to adultery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Adulteri.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Adulteri.jpg/170px-Adulteri.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="239" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Adulteri.jpg/255px-Adulteri.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Adulteri.jpg/340px-Adulteri.jpg 2x" data-file-width="391" data-file-height="549" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Adulteri.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Inca" class="mw-redirect" title="Inca">Inca</a> woman and man to be stoned for adultery, by <a href="/wiki/Huam%C3%A1n_Poma" class="mw-redirect" title="Huamán Poma">Huamán Poma</a></div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Adultery" title="Adultery">Adultery</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Honor_killing" title="Honor killing">Honor killing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stoning" title="Stoning">Stoning</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Crime_of_passion" title="Crime of passion">Crime of passion</a></div>
<p>There is a widely held expectation, which has existed for most of recorded history and in most cultures, that a wife is not to have <a href="/wiki/Sexual_relation" class="mw-redirect" title="Sexual relation">sexual relations</a> with anyone other than her legal husband. A breach of this expectation of <a href="/wiki/Fidelity" title="Fidelity">fidelity</a> is commonly referred to as <a href="/wiki/Adultery" title="Adultery">adultery</a> or <a href="/wiki/Extramarital_sex" title="Extramarital sex">extramarital sex</a>. Historically, adultery has been considered to be a serious <a href="/wiki/Offense_(law)" class="mw-redirect" title="Offense (law)">offense</a>, sometimes a <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">crime</a>, and even a <a href="/wiki/Sin" title="Sin">sin</a>. Even if that is not so, it may still have legal consequences, particularly as a ground for a <a href="/wiki/Divorce" title="Divorce">divorce</a>. Adultery may be a factor to consider in a <a href="/wiki/Property_settlement" class="mw-redirect" title="Property settlement">property settlement</a>, it may affect the status of children, the <a href="/wiki/Custody_of_children" class="mw-redirect" title="Custody of children">custody of children</a>; moreover, adultery can result in social <a href="/wiki/Ostracism" title="Ostracism">ostracism</a> in some parts of the world. 
In addition, <a href="/wiki/Affinity_(canon_law)" title="Affinity (canon law)">affinity</a> rules of Catholicism, of Judaism and of Islam prohibit an ex-wife or widow from engaging in sexual relations with and from marrying a number of relatives of the former husband.
</p><p>In parts of the world, adultery may result in violent acts, such as <a href="/wiki/Honor_killings" class="mw-redirect" title="Honor killings">honor killings</a> or <a href="/wiki/Stoning" title="Stoning">stoning</a>. Some jurisdictions, especially those that apply <a href="/wiki/Sharia_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Sharia law">Sharia law</a>, allow for such acts to take place legally.
</p><p>As of September 2010, stoning is a legal punishment in countries such as <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a>, and some states in <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> as punishment for <a href="/wiki/Zina" title="Zina">zina al-mohsena</a> ("adultery of married persons").<sup id="cite_ref-FAQ_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
