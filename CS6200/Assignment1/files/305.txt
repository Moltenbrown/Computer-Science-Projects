	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Brooks (surname)</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><table class="infobox" style="width:22em"><caption>Brooks</caption><tbody><tr><th colspan="2" style="text-align:center;background:#ADD8E6">Origin</th></tr><tr><th scope="row">Meaning</th><td>"residing near a stream or brook"</td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Region of origin</th><td><a href="/wiki/Anjou,_Is%C3%A8re" title="Anjou, Isère">Anjou, France</a> / <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> / <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a></td></tr><tr><td colspan="2" style="text-align:center;border-top: #a2a9b1 1px solid;"><sup id="cite_ref-BDR_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BDR-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><b>Brooks</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Toponymic" class="mw-redirect" title="Toponymic">toponymic</a> <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a> that is thought to have been derived residing near a <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">stream</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">brook</a>) from both the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a> surname Bäckland, meaning bäck "brook, stream" and lund "grove" and <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> , <a href="/wiki/Old_Irish" title="Old Irish">Gaelic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scottish</a> from the possessive case of Brook (i.e. ‘of the brook’) from pre 7th century English origins; Old English broc and appearing in the Medieval predecessors of "Brooks" such as "Ate-Broc" and "Atte-Broc". The surname Brooks has many <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic</a> (Anglo Saxon) links and is sometimes associated with the countries <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> , <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> within <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> and has wide links to the <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_Peninsula" title="Scandinavian Peninsula">Scandinavian Peninsula</a>. Commonly recorded in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> , where it is sometimes found with the aristocratic "von", England, Scotland and Ireland it has many possible origins.<sup id="cite_ref-CrescentRecord_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CrescentRecord-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The word "brook" derives from the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>broc</i> and appears in the Medieval predecessors of "Brooks" such as "Ate-Broc" and "Atte-Broc".<sup id="cite_ref-CrescentRecord_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CrescentRecord-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OnlineEtym_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OnlineEtym-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Webster&#39;sDict_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Webster&#39;sDict-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The surname arrived in North America from England in the mid-seventeenth century.<sup id="cite_ref-CrescentRecord_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CrescentRecord-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The surname is recorded in Ireland from the 1600s. O'Laughlin reports that "some of the name could stem from Irish origins, the name being changed into the English word 'Brook' or Brooks."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
The surname is also found among English-speaking <a href="/wiki/Ashkenazi_Jews" title="Ashkenazi Jews">Ashkenazi Jews</a>, deriving from the male Hebrew given name <i><a href="/wiki/Boruch" title="Boruch">Boruch</a></i>, meaning "blessed".
</p>
