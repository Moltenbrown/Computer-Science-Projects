<p><b>Perspective</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <i lang="la">perspicere</i> 'to see through') in the <a href="/wiki/Graphic_arts" title="Graphic arts">graphic arts</a> is an approximate representation, generally on a flat surface (such as paper), of an image as it is seen by the eye. The two most characteristic features of perspective are that objects appear smaller as their distance from the observer increases; and that they are subject to <i>foreshortening</i>, meaning that an object's dimensions along the line of sight appear shorter than its dimensions across the line of sight.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance" title="Italian Renaissance">Italian Renaissance</a> painters and architects including <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Brunelleschi" title="Filippo Brunelleschi">Filippo Brunelleschi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Masaccio" title="Masaccio">Masaccio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Uccello" title="Paolo Uccello">Paolo Uccello</a>, <a href="/wiki/Piero_della_Francesca" title="Piero della Francesca">Piero della Francesca</a> and <a href="/wiki/Luca_Pacioli" title="Luca Pacioli">Luca Pacioli</a> studied linear perspective, wrote treatises on it, and incorporated it into their artworks, thus contributing to the <a href="/wiki/Mathematics_and_art" title="Mathematics and art">mathematics of art</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Perspective_(graphical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Perspectiva-1.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Perspectiva-1.svg/220px-Perspectiva-1.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="170" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Perspectiva-1.svg/330px-Perspectiva-1.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Perspectiva-1.svg/440px-Perspectiva-1.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="578" data-file-height="446" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Perspectiva-1.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A cube in two-point perspective</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Perspectiva-2.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d2/Perspectiva-2.svg/220px-Perspectiva-2.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="170" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d2/Perspectiva-2.svg/330px-Perspectiva-2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d2/Perspectiva-2.svg/440px-Perspectiva-2.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="578" data-file-height="446" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Perspectiva-2.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rays of light travel from the object, through the picture plane, and to the viewer's eye. This is the basis for graphical perspective.</div></div></div>
<p>Perspective works by representing the light that passes from a scene through an imaginary rectangle (realized as the plane of the painting), to the viewer's eye, as if a viewer were looking through a window and painting what is seen directly onto the windowpane. If viewed from the same spot as the windowpane was painted, the painted image would be identical to what was seen through the unpainted window. Each painted object in the scene is thus a flat, scaled down version of the object on the other side of the window.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Because each portion of the painted object lies on the straight line from the viewer's eye to the equivalent portion of the real object it represents, the viewer sees no difference (sans <a href="/wiki/Depth_perception" title="Depth perception">depth perception</a>) between the painted scene on the windowpane and the view of the real scene.
All perspective drawings assume the viewer is a certain distance away from the drawing. Objects are scaled relative to that viewer. An object is often not scaled evenly: a circle often appears as an ellipse and a square can appear as a trapezoid.  This distortion is referred to as <b>foreshortening</b>.
</p><p>Perspective drawings have a horizon line, which is often implied. This line, directly opposite the viewer's eye, represents objects infinitely far away. They have shrunk, in the distance, to the infinitesimal thickness of a line. It is analogous to (and named after) the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">horizon</a>.
</p><p>Any perspective representation of a scene that includes parallel lines has one or more <a href="/wiki/Vanishing_point" title="Vanishing point">vanishing points</a> in a perspective drawing. A one-point perspective drawing means that the drawing has a single vanishing point, usually (though not necessarily) directly opposite the viewer's eye and usually (though not necessarily) on the horizon line. All lines parallel with the viewer's line of sight recede to the horizon towards this vanishing point. This is the standard "receding railroad tracks" phenomenon. A two-point drawing would have lines parallel to two different <a href="/wiki/Angle" title="Angle">angles</a>. Any number of vanishing points are possible in a drawing, one for each set of parallel lines that are at an angle relative to the plane of the drawing.
</p><p>Perspectives consisting of many parallel lines are observed most often when drawing architecture (architecture frequently uses lines parallel to the x, y, and z axes). Because it is rare to have a scene consisting solely of lines parallel to the three <a href="/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system" title="Cartesian coordinate system">Cartesian</a> axes (x, y, and z), it is rare to see perspectives in practice with only one, two, or three vanishing points; even a simple house frequently has a peaked roof which results in a minimum of six sets of parallel lines, in turn corresponding to up to six vanishing points.
</p><p>Of the many types of perspective drawings, the most common categorizations of artificial perspective are one-, two- and three-point. The names of these categories refer to the number of <a href="/wiki/Vanishing_point" title="Vanishing point">vanishing points</a> in the perspective drawing.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aerial_perspective">Aerial perspective</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Perspective_(graphical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Aerial perspective">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aerial_perspective" title="Aerial perspective">Aerial perspective</a></div>
<p>Aerial (or atmospheric) perspective depends on distant objects being more obscured by atmospheric elements, so more distant objects are less visible to the viewer. In general, distant objects become lighter in daytime and darker at night as they recede.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Aerial perspective can be combined with, but does not depend on, one or more vanishing points.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="One-point_perspective">One-point perspective</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Perspective_(graphical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: One-point perspective">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A drawing has one-point perspective when it contains only one vanishing point on the horizon line. This type of perspective is typically used for images of roads, railway tracks, hallways, or buildings viewed so that the front is directly facing the viewer. Any objects that are made up of lines either directly parallel with the viewer's line of sight or directly perpendicular (the railroad slats) can be represented with one-point perspective. These parallel lines converge at the vanishing point.
</p><p>One-point perspective exists when the <a href="/wiki/Picture_plane" title="Picture plane">picture plane</a> is parallel to two axes of a rectilinear (or Cartesian) scene—a scene which is composed entirely of linear elements that intersect only at right angles. If one axis is parallel with the picture plane, then all elements are either parallel to the picture plane (either horizontally or vertically) or perpendicular to it. All elements that are parallel to the picture plane are drawn as parallel lines. All elements that are perpendicular to the picture plane converge at a single point (a vanishing point) on the horizon.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Two-point_perspective">Two-point perspective</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Perspective_(graphical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Two-point perspective">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A drawing has two-point perspective when it contains two <a href="/wiki/Vanishing_point" title="Vanishing point">vanishing points</a> on the horizon line. In an illustration, these vanishing points can be placed arbitrarily along the horizon. Two-point perspective can be used to draw the same objects as one-point perspective, rotated: looking at the corner of a house, or at two forked roads shrinking into the distance, for example. One point represents one set of <a href="/wiki/Parallel_lines" class="mw-redirect" title="Parallel lines">parallel lines</a>, the other point represents the other. Seen from the corner, one wall of a house would recede towards one vanishing point while the other wall recedes towards the opposite vanishing point.
</p><p>Two-point perspective exists when the painting plate is parallel to a <a href="/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system" title="Cartesian coordinate system">Cartesian scene</a> in one axis (usually the <a href="/wiki/Z-axis" class="mw-redirect" title="Z-axis">z-axis</a>) but not to the other two axes. If the scene being viewed consists solely of a cylinder sitting on a horizontal plane, no difference exists in the image of the cylinder between a one-point and two-point perspective.
</p><p>Two-point perspective has one set of lines parallel to the picture plane and two sets oblique to it. Parallel lines oblique to the picture plane converge to a vanishing point, which means that this set-up will require two vanishing points.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Perspective-3point.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Perspective-3point.svg/170px-Perspective-3point.svg.png" decoding="async" width="170" height="180" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Perspective-3point.svg/255px-Perspective-3point.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Perspective-3point.svg/340px-Perspective-3point.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="402" data-file-height="425" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Perspective-3point.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Three-point perspective</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Three-point_perspective">Three-point perspective</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Perspective_(graphical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Three-point perspective">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Three-point perspective is often used for buildings seen from above (or below). In addition to the two vanishing points from before, one for each wall, there is now one for how the vertical lines of the walls recede. For an object seen from above, this third vanishing point is below the ground. For an object seen from below, as when the viewer looks up at a tall building, the third vanishing point is high in space.
</p><p>Three-point perspective exists when the perspective is a view of a Cartesian scene where the picture plane is not parallel to any of the scene's three axes. Each of the three vanishing points corresponds with one of the three axes of the scene.
One, two and three-point perspectives appear to embody different forms of calculated perspective, and are generated by different methods. Mathematically, however, all three are identical; the difference is merely in the relative orientation of the rectilinear scene to the viewer.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Curvilinear_perspective">Curvilinear perspective</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Perspective_(graphical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Curvilinear perspective">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Curvilinear_perspective" title="Curvilinear perspective">Curvilinear perspective</a></div>
<p>By superimposing two perpendicular, curved sets of two-point perspective lines, a four-or-above-point <a href="/wiki/Curvilinear_perspective" title="Curvilinear perspective">curvilinear perspective</a> can be achieved. This perspective can be used with a central horizon line of any orientation, and can depict both a <a href="/wiki/Worm%27s-eye_view" title="Worm&#39;s-eye view">worm's-eye</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bird%27s-eye_view" title="Bird&#39;s-eye view">bird's-eye view</a> at the same time.
</p><p>Additionally, a central vanishing point can be used (just as with one-point perspective) to indicate frontal (foreshortened) depth.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreshortening">Foreshortening</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Perspective_(graphical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Foreshortening">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Perspective-foreshortening.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Perspective-foreshortening.svg/220px-Perspective-foreshortening.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Perspective-foreshortening.svg/330px-Perspective-foreshortening.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Perspective-foreshortening.svg/440px-Perspective-foreshortening.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Perspective-foreshortening.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Two different projections of a stack of two cubes, illustrating oblique parallel projection foreshortening ("A") and perspective foreshortening ("B")</div></div></div>
<p>Foreshortening is the <a href="/wiki/Visual_effect" class="mw-redirect" title="Visual effect">visual effect</a> or <a href="/wiki/Optical_illusion" title="Optical illusion">optical illusion</a> that causes an object or <a href="/wiki/Distance" title="Distance">distance</a> to appear shorter than it actually is because it is angled toward the viewer. Additionally, an object is often not scaled evenly: a circle often appears as an ellipse and a square can appear as a trapezoid.
</p><p>Although foreshortening is an important element in art where <a href="/wiki/Visual" class="mw-redirect" title="Visual">visual</a> perspective is being depicted, foreshortening occurs in other types of two-dimensional representations of three-dimensional scenes. Some other types where foreshortening can occur include <a href="/wiki/Oblique_projection" title="Oblique projection">oblique parallel projection</a> drawings. Foreshortening also occurs when imaging rugged terrain using a <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_aperture_radar" class="mw-redirect" title="Synthetic aperture radar">synthetic aperture radar</a> system.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In painting, foreshortening in the depiction of the human figure was improved during the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance" title="Italian Renaissance">Italian Renaissance</a>, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Lamentation_of_Christ_(Mantegna)" title="Lamentation of Christ (Mantegna)">Lamentation over the Dead Christ</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Mantegna" title="Andrea Mantegna">Andrea Mantegna</a> (1480s) is one of the most famous of a number of works that show off the new technique, which thereafter became a standard part of the training of artists.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Perspective_(graphical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Copy_of_Sosus_of_Pergamon_doves_mosaic.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Copy_of_Sosus_of_Pergamon_doves_mosaic.jpg/170px-Copy_of_Sosus_of_Pergamon_doves_mosaic.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="140" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Copy_of_Sosus_of_Pergamon_doves_mosaic.jpg/255px-Copy_of_Sosus_of_Pergamon_doves_mosaic.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Copy_of_Sosus_of_Pergamon_doves_mosaic.jpg/340px-Copy_of_Sosus_of_Pergamon_doves_mosaic.jpg 2x" data-file-width="606" data-file-height="500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Copy_of_Sosus_of_Pergamon_doves_mosaic.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Second-century mosaic from <a href="/wiki/Hadrian%27s_Villa" title="Hadrian&#39;s Villa">Hadrian's Villa</a>, after <a href="/wiki/Sosus_of_Pergamon" title="Sosus of Pergamon">Sosus of Pergamon</a></div></div></div>
<p>Rudimentary attempts to create the illusion of depth were made in ancient times, with artists achieving <a href="/wiki/Isometric_projection" title="Isometric projection">isometric projection</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. Various early <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> works depict perspective lines with an implied convergence, albeit without a unifying vanishing point. The first to master perspective was Italian Renaissance <a href="/wiki/Architect" title="Architect">architect</a> <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Brunelleschi" title="Filippo Brunelleschi">Filippo Brunelleschi</a>, who developed the adherence of perspective to a vanishing point in the early fifteenth century. His discovery was immediately influential on subsequent <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_art" title="Renaissance art">Renaissance art</a> and was explored contemporaneously in manuscripts by <a href="/wiki/Leon_Battista_Alberti" title="Leon Battista Alberti">Leon Battista Alberti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Piero_della_Francesca" title="Piero della Francesca">Piero della Francesca</a> and others.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Perspective_(graphical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The earliest art paintings and drawings typically sized many objects and characters hierarchically according to their spiritual or thematic importance, not their distance from the viewer, and did not use foreshortening. The most important figures are often shown as the highest in a composition, also from <a href="/wiki/Hieratic" title="Hieratic">hieratic</a> motives, leading to the so-called "vertical perspective", common in the <a href="/wiki/Art_of_Ancient_Egypt" class="mw-redirect" title="Art of Ancient Egypt">art of Ancient Egypt</a>, where a group of "nearer" figures are shown below the larger figure or figures. The only method to indicate the relative position of elements in the composition was by overlapping, of which much use is made in works like the <a href="/wiki/Parthenon_Marbles" class="mw-redirect" title="Parthenon Marbles">Parthenon Marbles</a> (c.&#160;447–438&#160;BC). Additionally, oblique foreshortening of round elements like shields and wheels is evident in <a href="/wiki/Red-figure_pottery" title="Red-figure pottery">red-figure pottery</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> By the first century&#160;BC, some <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> paintings included isometric projection or generally converging lines without a consistent vanishing point.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> First century Roman <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">frescoes</a> have been found which use multiple vanishing points in a systematic but not fully consistent manner.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Systematic attempts to evolve a system of perspective are usually considered to have begun around the fifth century BC in the <a href="/wiki/Art_of_ancient_Greece" class="mw-redirect" title="Art of ancient Greece">art of ancient Greece</a>, as part of a developing interest in <a href="/wiki/Illusionism" class="mw-redirect" title="Illusionism">illusionism</a> allied to theatrical scenery. This was detailed within <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Poetics_(Aristotle)" title="Poetics (Aristotle)">Poetics</a></i> as <i>skenographia</i>: using flat panels on a stage to give the illusion of depth.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The philosophers <a href="/wiki/Anaxagoras" title="Anaxagoras">Anaxagoras</a> and <a href="/wiki/Democritus" title="Democritus">Democritus</a> worked out geometric theories of perspective for use with <i>skenographia</i>. <a href="/wiki/Alcibiades" title="Alcibiades">Alcibiades</a> had paintings in his house designed using <i>skenographia</i>, so this art was not confined merely to the stage. <a href="/wiki/Euclid" title="Euclid">Euclid</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Euclid%27s_Optics" title="Euclid&#39;s Optics">Optics</a></i> <span class="nowrap">(c. 300 BC)</span> introduced a mathematical theory of perspective, but there is some debate over the extent to which Euclid's perspective coincides with the modern mathematical definition.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Song_Dynasty_Hydraulic_Mill_for_Grain.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Song_Dynasty_Hydraulic_Mill_for_Grain.JPG/220px-Song_Dynasty_Hydraulic_Mill_for_Grain.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="153" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Song_Dynasty_Hydraulic_Mill_for_Grain.JPG/330px-Song_Dynasty_Hydraulic_Mill_for_Grain.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Song_Dynasty_Hydraulic_Mill_for_Grain.JPG/440px-Song_Dynasty_Hydraulic_Mill_for_Grain.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1580" data-file-height="1100" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Song_Dynasty_Hydraulic_Mill_for_Grain.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">Song dynasty</a> watercolor painting of a mill in an <a href="/wiki/Oblique_projection" title="Oblique projection">oblique projection</a>, 12th&#160;century</div></div></div>
<p>Chinese artists made use of <a href="/wiki/Oblique_projection" title="Oblique projection">oblique projection</a> from the first or second century until the 18th century. It is not certain how they came to use the technique; some authorities suggest that the Chinese acquired the technique from India, which acquired it from Ancient Rome.<sup id="cite_ref-Cucker269_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cucker269-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Oblique projection is also seen in Japanese art, such as in the <a href="/wiki/Ukiyo-e" title="Ukiyo-e">Ukiyo-e</a> paintings of <a href="/wiki/Torii_Kiyonaga" title="Torii Kiyonaga">Torii Kiyonaga</a> (1752–1815).<sup id="cite_ref-Cucker269_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cucker269-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;b&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Various paintings and drawings from the Middle Ages show amateur attempts at projections of objects, where parallel lines are successfully represented in isometric projection, or by nonparallel ones without a vanishing point.
</p><p>By the later periods of antiquity, artists, especially those in less popular traditions, were well aware that distant objects could be shown smaller than those close at hand for increased realism, but whether this convention was actually used in a work depended on many factors. Some of the paintings found in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_the_Vettii" title="House of the Vettii">ruins of Pompeii</a> show a remarkable realism and perspective for their time.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> It has been claimed that comprehensive systems of perspective were evolved in antiquity, but most scholars do not accept this. Hardly any of the many works where such a system would have been used have survived. A passage in <a href="/wiki/Philostratus_III" class="mw-redirect" title="Philostratus III">Philostratus</a> suggests that classical artists and theorists thought in terms of "circles" at equal distance from the viewer, like a classical semi-circular theatre seen from the stage.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The roof beams in rooms in the <a href="/wiki/Vergilius_Vaticanus" title="Vergilius Vaticanus">Vatican Virgil</a>, from about 400 AD, are shown converging, more or less, on a common vanishing point, but this is not systematically related to the rest of the composition.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In the Late Antique period use of perspective techniques declined. The art of the new cultures of the <a href="/wiki/Migration_Period" title="Migration Period">Migration Period</a> had no tradition of attempting compositions of large numbers of figures and Early Medieval art was slow and inconsistent in relearning the convention from classical models, though the process can be seen underway in <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_art" title="Carolingian art">Carolingian art</a>.
</p><p>Medieval artists in Europe, like those in the Islamic world and China, were aware of the general principle of varying the relative size of elements according to distance, but even more than classical art was perfectly ready to override it for other reasons. Buildings were often shown obliquely according to a particular convention. The use and sophistication of attempts to convey distance increased steadily during the period, but without a basis in a systematic theory. <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_art" title="Byzantine art">Byzantine art</a> was also aware of these principles, but also used the <a href="/wiki/Reverse_perspective" title="Reverse perspective">reverse perspective</a> convention for the setting of principal figures.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lorenzetti_Ambrogio_annunciation-_1344..jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Lorenzetti_Ambrogio_annunciation-_1344..jpg/220px-Lorenzetti_Ambrogio_annunciation-_1344..jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="250" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Lorenzetti_Ambrogio_annunciation-_1344..jpg/330px-Lorenzetti_Ambrogio_annunciation-_1344..jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Lorenzetti_Ambrogio_annunciation-_1344..jpg/440px-Lorenzetti_Ambrogio_annunciation-_1344..jpg 2x" data-file-width="1318" data-file-height="1500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lorenzetti_Ambrogio_annunciation-_1344..jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The floor tiles in <a href="/wiki/Ambrogio_Lorenzetti" title="Ambrogio Lorenzetti">Ambrogio Lorenzetti</a>'s <i>Annunciation</i> (1344) strongly anticipate modern perspective.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ambrogio_Lorenzetti" title="Ambrogio Lorenzetti">Ambrogio Lorenzetti</a> painted a floor using a rudimentary vanishing point in his <i><a href="/wiki/Presentation_at_the_Temple_(Ambrogio_Lorenzetti)" title="Presentation at the Temple (Ambrogio Lorenzetti)">Presentation at the Temple</a></i> (1342). However the rest of the painting is not consistent with the perspective of the floor.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renaissance:_mathematical_basis">Renaissance: mathematical basis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Perspective_(graphical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Renaissance: mathematical basis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Mathematics_and_art" title="Mathematics and art">Mathematics and art</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Filippo_Brunelleschi" title="Filippo Brunelleschi">Filippo Brunelleschi</a> conducted a series of experiments between 1415 and 1420, which included making drawings of various <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florentine</a> buildings in correct perspective.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Vasari" class="mw-redirect" title="Vasari">Vasari</a> and others, in about 1420, Brunelleschi demonstrated his discovery by having people look through a hole in the back of a painting he had made. Through it, they would see a building such as the <a href="/wiki/Florence_Baptistery" title="Florence Baptistery">Florence Baptistery</a>. When Brunelleschi lifted a mirror in front of the viewer, it reflected his painting of the buildings which had been seen previously, so that the vanishing point was perfectly centered from the perspective of the participant. To the viewer, the painting of the Baptistery and the building itself were nearly indistinguishable.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Soon after, nearly every artist in Florence and in Italy used geometrical perspective in their paintings,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> notably <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Uccello" title="Paolo Uccello">Paolo Uccello</a>, <a href="/wiki/Masolino_da_Panicale" title="Masolino da Panicale">Masolino da Panicale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Donatello" title="Donatello">Donatello</a>. Donatello started sculpting elaborate checkerboard floors into the simple <a href="/wiki/Manger" title="Manger">manger</a> portrayed in the birth of Christ. Although hardly historically accurate, these checkerboard floors obeyed the primary laws of geometrical perspective: the lines converged approximately to a vanishing point, and the rate at which the horizontal lines receded into the distance was graphically determined. This became an integral part of <a href="/wiki/Quattrocento" title="Quattrocento">Quattrocento</a> art. <a href="/wiki/Melozzo_da_Forl%C3%AC" title="Melozzo da Forlì">Melozzo da Forlì</a> first used the technique of upward foreshortening (in Rome, <a href="/wiki/Loreto_(AN)" class="mw-redirect" title="Loreto (AN)">Loreto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forl%C3%AC" title="Forlì">Forlì</a> and others), and was celebrated for that. Not only was perspective a way of showing depth, it was also a new method of <a href="/wiki/Composition_(visual_arts)" title="Composition (visual arts)">composing</a> a painting. Paintings began to show a single, unified scene, rather than a combination of several.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Loreto_Fresko.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Loreto_Fresko.jpg/220px-Loreto_Fresko.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Loreto_Fresko.jpg/330px-Loreto_Fresko.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Loreto_Fresko.jpg/440px-Loreto_Fresko.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Loreto_Fresko.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Melozzo" class="mw-redirect" title="Melozzo">Melozzo</a>'s use of upward foreshortening in his frescoes</div></div></div>
<p>As shown by the quick proliferation of accurate perspective paintings in Florence, Brunelleschi likely understood (with help from his friend the mathematician <a href="/wiki/Paolo_dal_Pozzo_Toscanelli" title="Paolo dal Pozzo Toscanelli">Toscanelli</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> but did not publish, the mathematics behind perspective. Decades later, his friend Leon Battista Alberti wrote <i><span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/De_pictura" title="De pictura">De pictura</a></span></i> (1435/1436), a treatise on proper methods of showing distance in painting. Alberti's primary breakthrough was not to show the mathematics in terms of conical projections, as it actually appears to the eye. Instead, he formulated the theory based on planar projections, or how the rays of light, passing from the viewer's eye to the landscape, would strike the picture plane (the painting). He was then able to calculate the apparent height of a distant object using two similar triangles. The mathematics behind similar triangles is relatively simple, having been long ago formulated by Euclid. In viewing a wall, for instance, the first triangle has a <a href="/wiki/Vertex_(geometry)" title="Vertex (geometry)">vertex</a> at the user's eye, and vertices at the top and bottom of the wall. The bottom of this triangle is the distance from the viewer to the wall. The second, similar triangle, has a point at the viewer's eye, and has a length equal to the viewer's eye from the painting. The height of the second triangle can then be determined through a simple ratio, as proven by Euclid. Alberti was also trained in the science of optics through the school of Padua and under the influence of <a href="/wiki/Biagio_Pelacani_da_Parma" class="mw-redirect" title="Biagio Pelacani da Parma">Biagio Pelacani da Parma</a> who studied <a href="/wiki/Alhazen" class="mw-redirect" title="Alhazen">Alhazen</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Optics" title="Book of Optics">Book of Optics</a></i> <sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Alhazen's <i>Book of Optics</i>, translated around 1200 into Latin, laid the mathematical foundation for perspective in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Entrega_de_las_llaves_a_San_Pedro_(Perugino).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Entrega_de_las_llaves_a_San_Pedro_%28Perugino%29.jpg/220px-Entrega_de_las_llaves_a_San_Pedro_%28Perugino%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="133" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Entrega_de_las_llaves_a_San_Pedro_%28Perugino%29.jpg/330px-Entrega_de_las_llaves_a_San_Pedro_%28Perugino%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Entrega_de_las_llaves_a_San_Pedro_%28Perugino%29.jpg/440px-Entrega_de_las_llaves_a_San_Pedro_%28Perugino%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3187" data-file-height="1923" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Entrega_de_las_llaves_a_San_Pedro_(Perugino).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Pietro_Perugino" title="Pietro Perugino">Pietro Perugino</a>'s use of perspective in <i><a href="/wiki/Delivery_of_the_Keys_(Perugino)" title="Delivery of the Keys (Perugino)">Delivery of the Keys</a></i> (1482), a fresco at the <a href="/wiki/Sistine_Chapel" title="Sistine Chapel">Sistine Chapel</a></div></div></div>
<p>Piero della Francesca elaborated on <i>De pictura</i> in his <i><a href="/wiki/De_Prospectiva_pingendi" title="De Prospectiva pingendi">De Prospectiva pingendi</a></i> in the 1470s, making many references to Euclid.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Alberti had limited himself to figures on the ground plane and giving an overall basis for perspective. Della Francesca fleshed it out, explicitly covering solids in any area of the picture plane. Della Francesca also started the now common practice of using illustrated figures to explain the mathematical concepts, making his treatise easier to understand than Alberti's. Della Francesca was also the first to accurately draw the <a href="/wiki/Platonic_solids" class="mw-redirect" title="Platonic solids">Platonic solids</a> as they would appear in perspective. <a href="/wiki/Luca_Pacioli" title="Luca Pacioli">Luca Pacioli</a>'s 1509 <i><a href="/wiki/Divina_proportione" title="Divina proportione">Divina proportione</a></i> (<i>Divine Proportion</i>), illustrated by <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>, summarizes the use of perspective in painting, including much of Della Francesca's treatise.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Leonardo applied one-point perspective as well as <a href="/wiki/Shallow_focus" title="Shallow focus">shallow focus</a> to some of his works.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Perspective remained, for a while, the domain of Florence. <a href="/wiki/Jan_van_Eyck" title="Jan van Eyck">Jan van Eyck</a>, among others, was unable to create a consistent structure for the converging lines in paintings, as in London's <i><a href="/wiki/Arnolfini_Portrait" title="Arnolfini Portrait">Arnolfini Portrait</a></i>, because he was unaware of the theoretical breakthrough just then occurring in Italy. However he achieved very subtle effects by manipulations of scale in his interiors. Gradually, and partly through the movement of academies of the arts, the Italian techniques became part of the training of artists across Europe, and later other parts of the world.
</p><p>Two-point perspective was demonstrated as early as 1525 by <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht Dürer">Albrecht Dürer</a>, who studied perspective by reading Piero and Pacioli's works, in his <i>Unterweisung der messung</i> ("Instruction of the measurement").<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The culmination of these Renaissance traditions finds its ultimate synthesis in the research of the 17th-century architect, geometer, and optician <a href="/wiki/Girard_Desargues" title="Girard Desargues">Girard Desargues</a> on perspective, optics and projective geometry. Further <a href="/wiki/Projective_geometry#History" title="Projective geometry">advances in projective geometry</a> in the 19th and 20th centuries contributed to the development of <a href="/wiki/Analytic_geometry" title="Analytic geometry">analytic geometry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_geometry" title="Algebraic geometry">algebraic geometry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">relativity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Present:_computer_graphics">Present: computer graphics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Perspective_(graphical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Present: computer graphics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>3-D <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer_game" class="mw-redirect" title="Personal computer game">computer games</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ray_tracing_(graphics)" title="Ray tracing (graphics)">ray-tracers</a> often use a modified version of perspective. Like the painter, the computer program is generally not concerned with every ray of light that is in a scene. Instead, the program simulates rays of light traveling backwards from the monitor (one for every pixel), and checks to see what it hits. In this way, the program does not have to compute the trajectories of millions of rays of light that pass from a light source, hit an object, and miss the viewer.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (June 2013)">dubious</span></a>&#32;<span class="metadata"> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Talk:Perspective_(graphical)#Dubious" title="Talk:Perspective (graphical)">discuss</a></span></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/CAD_software" class="mw-redirect" title="CAD software">CAD software</a>, and some computer games (especially games using 3-D polygons) use linear algebra, and in particular matrix multiplication, to create a sense of perspective. The scene is a set of points, and these points are projected to a plane (computer screen)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (June 2013)">dubious</span></a>&#32;<span class="metadata"> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Talk:Perspective_(graphical)#Dubious" title="Talk:Perspective (graphical)">discuss</a></span></i>&#93;</sup> in front of the view point (the viewer's eye). The problem of perspective is simply finding the corresponding coordinates on the plane corresponding to the points in the scene. By the theories of linear algebra, a matrix multiplication directly computes the desired coordinates, thus bypassing any <a href="/wiki/Descriptive_geometry" title="Descriptive geometry">descriptive geometry</a> theorems used in perspective drawing.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (June 2013)">dubious</span></a>&#32;<span class="metadata"> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Talk:Perspective_(graphical)#Dubious" title="Talk:Perspective (graphical)">discuss</a></span></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations">Limitations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Perspective_(graphical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Limitations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:William_Hogarth_-_Absurd_perspectives.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/William_Hogarth_-_Absurd_perspectives.png/220px-William_Hogarth_-_Absurd_perspectives.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="280" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/William_Hogarth_-_Absurd_perspectives.png/330px-William_Hogarth_-_Absurd_perspectives.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/William_Hogarth_-_Absurd_perspectives.png/440px-William_Hogarth_-_Absurd_perspectives.png 2x" data-file-width="2003" data-file-height="2549" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:William_Hogarth_-_Absurd_perspectives.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Satire_on_False_Perspective" title="Satire on False Perspective">Satire on False Perspective</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/William_Hogarth" title="William Hogarth">William Hogarth</a>, 1753</div></div></div>
<p>Perspective images are calculated assuming a particular vanishing point. In order for the resulting image to appear identical to the original scene, a viewer of the perspective must view the image from the exact vantage point used in the calculations relative to the image. This cancels out what would appear to be distortions in the image when viewed from a different point. These apparent distortions are more pronounced away from the center of the image as the angle between a projected ray (from the scene to the eye) becomes more acute relative to the picture plane. In practice, unless the viewer chooses an extreme angle, like looking at it from the bottom corner of the window, the perspective normally looks more or less correct. This is referred to as "Zeeman's Paradox".<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> It has been suggested that a drawing in perspective still seems to be in perspective at other spots because we still perceive it as a drawing, because it lacks depth of field cues.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For a typical perspective, however, the field of view is narrow enough (often only 60 degrees) that the distortions are similarly minimal enough that the image can be viewed from a point other than the actual calculated vantage point without appearing significantly distorted. When a larger angle of view is required, the standard method of projecting rays onto a flat picture plane becomes impractical. As a theoretical maximum, the field of view of a flat picture plane must be less than 180 degrees (as the field of view increases towards 180 degrees, the required breadth of the picture plane approaches infinity).
</p><p>To create a projected ray image with a large field of view, one can project the image onto a curved surface.  To have a large field of view horizontally in the image, a surface that is a vertical cylinder (i.e., the axis of the cylinder is parallel to the z-axis) will suffice (similarly, if the desired large field of view is only in the vertical direction of the image, a horizontal cylinder will suffice). A cylindrical picture surface will allow for a projected ray image up to a full 360 degrees in either the horizontal or vertical dimension of the perspective image (depending on the orientation of the cylinder). In the same way, by using a spherical picture surface, the field of view can be a full 360 degrees in any direction (note that for a spherical surface, all projected rays from the scene to the eye intersect the surface at a right angle).
</p><p>Just as a standard perspective image must be viewed from the calculated vantage point for the image to appear identical to the true scene, a projected image onto a cylinder or sphere must likewise be viewed from the calculated vantage point for it to be precisely identical to the original scene. If an image projected onto a cylindrical surface is "unrolled" into a flat image, different types of distortions occur. For example, many of the scene's straight lines will be drawn as curves. An image projected onto a spherical surface can be flattened in various ways:
</p>
<ul><li>An image equivalent to an unrolled cylinder</li>
<li>A portion of the sphere can be flattened into an image equivalent to a standard perspective</li>
<li>An image similar to a <a href="/wiki/Fisheye_lens" title="Fisheye lens">fisheye</a> photograph</li></ul>
