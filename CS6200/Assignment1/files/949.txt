	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Abraham ben Abraham</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Abraham ben Abraham</b> (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span lang="he" dir="rtl">אברהם בן אברהם</span>&#8206;, lit. "Avraham the son of Avraham") (c. 1700 &#8211; May 23, 1749), also known as <a href="/wiki/Count" title="Count">Count</a> <b>Valentine (Valentin, Walentyn) Potocki</b> (<b>Pototzki</b> or <b>Pototski</b>), was a purported <a href="/wiki/Szlachta" title="Szlachta">Polish nobleman</a> (<i>szlachta</i>) of the <a href="/wiki/Potocki" title="Potocki">Potocki</a> family who <a href="/wiki/Conversion_to_Judaism" title="Conversion to Judaism">converted</a> to <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a> and was <a href="/wiki/Execution_by_burning" class="mw-redirect" title="Execution by burning">burned at the stake</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a> because he had renounced <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Catholicism</a> and had become an <a href="/wiki/613_mitzvot" class="mw-redirect" title="613 mitzvot">observant</a> <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jew</a>. According to <a href="/wiki/Jew" class="mw-redirect" title="Jew">Jewish</a> oral traditions, he was known to the revered <a href="/wiki/Talmud" title="Talmud">Talmudic</a> sage, the <a href="/wiki/Vilna_Gaon" title="Vilna Gaon">Vilna Gaon</a> (<a href="/wiki/Rabbi" title="Rabbi">Rabbi</a> Elijah Ben Shlomo Zalman [1720–1797]), and his ashes were interred in the relocated grave of the Vilna Gaon in Vilna's new Jewish cemetery.<sup id="cite_ref-krohn_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-krohn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hamodia_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hamodia-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the Orthodox Jewish community accepts the teachings about Abraham ben Abraham, including the involvement of the <a href="/wiki/Vilna_Gaon" title="Vilna Gaon">Vilna Gaon</a>,
<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  "secular scholars have largely concluded that it is a legend."<sup id="cite_ref-Haaretz_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haaretz-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Jewish_traditions">Jewish traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abraham_ben_Abraham&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Jewish traditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:193px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vilna_Gaon_portrait.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/23/Vilna_Gaon_portrait.gif" decoding="async" width="191" height="268" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="191" data-file-height="268" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption">The <a href="/wiki/Vilna_Gaon" title="Vilna Gaon">Vilna Gaon</a> (1720-1797) was, according to the Jewish tradition, a mentor to Abraham ben Abraham.</div></div></div>
<p>There are several versions of this story, especially among the Jews of <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Lithuania" title="History of the Jews in Lithuania">Lithuania</a>, <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Poland" title="History of the Jews in Poland">Poland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Russia" title="History of the Jews in Russia">Russia</a>, who know and still refer to Potocki as the <i><a href="/wiki/Ger_tzedek" class="mw-redirect" title="Ger tzedek">Ger Tzedek</a></i> ("<a href="/wiki/Religious_conversion#Conversion_to_Judaism" title="Religious conversion">righteous proselyte</a>") of <a href="/wiki/Vilna" class="mw-redirect" title="Vilna">Vilna</a> (Vilnius). Virtually all Jewish sources agree that he was a Polish nobleman who converted to Judaism and was burned at the stake by the Roman Catholic Church at Vilnius on May 23, 1749 (7 <a href="/wiki/Sivan" title="Sivan">Sivan</a> 5509, corresponding to the second day of the <a href="/wiki/Jewish_holiday" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish holiday">Jewish holiday</a> of <a href="/wiki/Shavuot" title="Shavuot">Shavuot</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Diaspora" title="Diaspora">Diaspora</a>), because he had renounced Catholicism and had become an observant Jew.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEProuser2005_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEProuser2005-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Multiple oral histories, backed up by several 19th-century and later printed versions of the story, from many Jewish communities over the past 250 years, serve as evidence of Potocki's story. Jewish oral traditions teach many more details of Avraham ben Avraham's life and death.
</p><p>There is also one contemporary written account from 1755, by <a href="/wiki/Yaakov_Emden" class="mw-redirect" title="Yaakov Emden">Rabbi Yaakov Emden</a>. ויקם עדות ביעקב דף כה, ב (Vayakam Edus b'Yaakov, 1755, p.&#160;25b). A rough translation:
</p>
<blockquote><p>A few years ago, it happened in Vilna the capital of Lithuania that a great prince from the family of Pototska converted. They captured him and imprisoned him for many days thinking they could return him to their religion. He knew that he would not escape harsh tortures and a cruel death if he would not return. They wanted to save him from the death and punishment that would await him if he held out. He paid no attention to them or to the begging of his mother the countess. He was not afraid or worried about dying in all the bitter anguish they had done to him. After waiting for him for a long time, they tried to take it easy on him for the honor of his family. He ridiculed all the temptations of the priests who would speak to him every day because he was an important minister. He scorned them and laughed at them, and chose death of long and cruel agony, to the temporary life of this world. He accepted and suffered all from love, and died sanctifying God's name. May he rest in peace.<sup id="cite_ref-emdenquote_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-emdenquote-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>As to why there are few full sources, the Jewish view is reflected in this excerpt from the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shema_Yisrael_Torah_Network&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Shema Yisrael Torah Network (page does not exist)">Shema Yisrael Torah Network</a> website:
</p>
<blockquote><p>There are a few reasons why there are so few contemporary sources about the ger tzedek story. It can be assumed that the noble Pototzki family, which was a religious Polish-Catholic family, was not happy that one of their sons defected to Judaism. The Pototzki family was said to have generally dealt kindly with the Jews living on its lands. Mentioning the conversion would have been interpreted as an open provocation of the area's ruler, which would have not resulted in any good. In addition, undoubtedly the conversion of one of the upper-class gentiles aroused great interest among the populace, and his refusal to return to their faith caused them great embarrassment ... Nevertheless, we believe the words of our rabbis, which clearly indicate that there was a connection between the Gra (i.e. the <a href="/wiki/Vilna_Gaon" title="Vilna Gaon">Vilna Gaon</a>) and the Ger Tzedek.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>In his lecture on the topic,<sup id="cite_ref-LeimanLecture_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LeimanLecture-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Prof. Sid Leiman quotes an author from a century ago who related the same reason - heard from a member of the Pototzki family.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_traditional_story">The traditional story</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abraham_ben_Abraham&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The traditional story">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Polish author <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B3zef_Ignacy_Kraszewski" title="Józef Ignacy Kraszewski">Józef Ignacy Kraszewski</a>, based on the story written in Hebrew from 1766: Hurwitz, Ammude bet Yehudah, Amsterdam relates that young Potocki and his friend Zaremba, who traveled from <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> to study in a <a href="/wiki/Seminary" title="Seminary">seminary</a> in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, became interested in an old Jew whom they found poring over a large volume when they entered his wine shop. This Jew might have been their own countryman, Menahem Man ben Aryeh Löb of Visun, who was tortured and executed in Vilna at the age of seventy (July 3, 1749). Tradition has brought this Jewish martyr into close connection with the <i>Ger Tzedek</i>, but fear of the censor has prevented writers in Russia from saying anything explicit on the subject. His teachings and explanations of the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a>, to which they, as Roman Catholics, were total strangers, so impressed them that they prevailed upon him to instruct them in the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew language</a>. In six months they acquired proficiency in the Biblical language and a strong inclination toward <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a>. They resolved to go to <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>, which was one of the few places in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> at that time where a Christian could openly embrace Judaism. But Potocki first went to <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, whence, after convincing himself that he could no longer remain a Catholic, he went to Amsterdam and took upon himself the covenant of Abraham, assuming the name of Avraham ben Avraham ("Abraham the son of Abraham"; "the son of Abraham" is the traditional styling of a convert to Judaism, as <a href="/wiki/Abraham" title="Abraham">Abraham</a> was the first who converted to Judaism from polytheism).
</p><p>Potocki's parents got word of his leave from the seminary in Paris and the rumors that he had converted to Judaism, and began searching for him. Potocki then fled from France and hid in a <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">synagogue</a> in Vilna, wearing a long beard and <a href="/wiki/Peyot" class="mw-redirect" title="Peyot">peyot</a> like the <a href="/wiki/Perushim" title="Perushim">Perushim</a> (devout Jews who separated themselves from the community to learn and pray). When the Vilna Gaon heard of his whereabouts, he advised him to hide instead in the small town of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ilye&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ilye (page does not exist)">Ilye</a> (<a href="/wiki/Vilna_Governorate" title="Vilna Governorate">Vilna Governorate</a>). There a Jewish tailor who sewed uniforms for Polish bureaucrats overheard some clients talking about the fugitive divinity student and suspected that the stranger in the synagogue might be he. Later this tailor's son, who liked to disturb the men studying in the synagogue, was sharply rebuked by Potocki; some say Potocki grabbed the boy by the ear and pulled him out the door. The tailor reported him to the Bishop of Vilna, and Potocki was arrested.<sup id="cite_ref-krohn_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-krohn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Potocki's parents visited him in prison and begged him to renounce his Judaism publicly, promising to build him a castle where he could practice the religion privately. According to Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Ben-Zion_Alfes" title="Ben-Zion Alfes">Ben-Zion Alfes</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Maggid" title="Maggid">Maggid</a> of Vilna, Potocki refused his mother, saying, "I love you dearly, but I love the truth even more".<sup id="cite_ref-krohn_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-krohn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After a long imprisonment and a trial for <a href="/wiki/Heresy" title="Heresy">heresy</a>, Potocki was condemned to death by being burned alive at the stake. After the decree was handed down, the Vilna Gaon sent Potocki a message offering to rescue him using <a href="/wiki/Kabbalah" title="Kabbalah">Kabbalah</a>. Potocki refused, preferring instead to die <i>al kiddush Hashem</i> and inquired of the Vilna Gaon which blessing he should make immediately before his death. The Vilna Gaon answered, "...m'kadesh es Shimcha be'rabbim" (Who sanctifies His Name in public) and sent an emissary to hear and answer "<a href="/wiki/Amen" title="Amen">Amen</a>".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> His mother used all her influence to procure a <a href="/wiki/Pardon" title="Pardon">pardon</a> for him, but the execution was moved up one day so that she would not be able to deliver it in time.<sup id="cite_ref-krohn_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-krohn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Potocki was executed in Vilna on the second day of the Jewish holiday of Shavuot. It was unsafe for any Jew to witness the burning; nevertheless one Jew, Leiser Zhiskes, who had no beard, went among the crowd and succeeded by bribery in securing some of the ashes of the martyr, which were later buried in the <a href="/wiki/Jewish_cemeteries_of_Vilnius" title="Jewish cemeteries of Vilnius">Jewish cemetery</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-krohn_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-krohn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sSide_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sSide-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Potocki walked proudly to the execution site, singing a song that was later sung in the <a href="/wiki/Volozhin_yeshiva" title="Volozhin yeshiva">Volozhin yeshiva</a> and that was also sung by Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Isser_Zalman_Meltzer" title="Isser Zalman Meltzer">Isser Zalman Meltzer</a> after <a href="/wiki/Yom_Kippur" title="Yom Kippur">Yom Kippur</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-krohn_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-krohn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Some sources say that Rabbi Alexander Ziskind, author of <i>Yesod VeShoresh HaAvodah</i>, stood near Potocki and said "Amen" to the blessing he said before he died.<sup id="cite_ref-krohn_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-krohn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Following Potocki's execution, the town that had furnished the firewood for the execution burned down. There were also an unusual number of fires in Vilna, and a building that stood opposite the execution site bore a black stain from the "smoke and fumes of the burning". No amount of paint or whitewashing would remove the stain, and finally the building was taken down.<sup id="cite_ref-krohn_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-krohn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The authorities would not allow a monument to be erected over Potocki's ashes, but a "strange tree" grew at the site. Those who tried to cut down the tree were mysteriously injured in the process.<sup id="cite_ref-krohn_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-krohn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Around 1919 a <a href="/wiki/Tomb" title="Tomb">tomb</a> was erected over the ashes and Jews came to pray there. Following the destruction of the old cemetery of Vilna by the <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">Nazis</a> during World War II, a new cemetery was built and the Vilna Gaon was interred in a new <i><a href="/wiki/Ohel_(grave)" title="Ohel (grave)">ohel</a></i>. Potocki's ashes were reinterred alongside the Vilna Gaon's grave,<sup id="cite_ref-krohn_1-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-krohn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and an inscribed stone memorial to him was mounted on the wall of the <i>ohel</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-hamodia_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hamodia-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Potocki's comrade Zaremba returned to Poland several years before him, married the daughter of a great nobleman, and had a son. He remained true to the promise to embrace Judaism and took his wife and child to Amsterdam, where, after he and his son had been circumcised, his wife also converted to Judaism; they then went to the <a href="/wiki/Land_of_Israel" title="Land of Israel">Land of Israel</a>.
</p><p>According to Jewish tradition, following Avraham ben Avraham's death, the Vilna Gaon believed that the spiritual constitution of the world had become altered in such a way that a Jew was no longer bound to <a href="/wiki/Handwashing_in_Judaism" title="Handwashing in Judaism">wash his hands in the morning</a> (<i>netilat yadayim</i>) within four <i>amot</i> (cubits) of his bed, as explicitly taught in the codes of Jewish law such as the <a href="/wiki/Shulchan_Aruch" title="Shulchan Aruch">Shulchan Aruch</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Halakha" title="Halakha">halachic</a> works. Rather, a Jew's entire house would be considered as four <i>amot</i> for this <a href="/wiki/Mitzvah" title="Mitzvah">mitzvah</a>. This custom, begun at Avraham ben Avraham's death, commenced with the Vilna Gaon and later became the practice of the <a href="/wiki/Hebron_Yeshiva" title="Hebron Yeshiva">Slabodka yeshiva</a> in Europe, becoming today the routine of many leading Israeli rabbis who follow the Slabodka tradition.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_evidence">Historical evidence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abraham_ben_Abraham&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Historical evidence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Secondary_source" title="Secondary source">secondary sources</a> &#8211; encyclopaedias of Jewish culture, history and religion &#8211; include an entry on Potocki, a Polish <a href="/wiki/Magnate" title="Magnate">magnate</a> and member of the powerful Potocki family, who converted to <a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Judaism" title="Orthodox Judaism">Orthodox Judaism</a> in 18th-century Netherlands and who, after his return to Vilna, was tried by an <a href="/wiki/Inquisition" title="Inquisition">Inquisition</a> court which sentenced him to burning at the stake.<sup id="cite_ref-Tazbir_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tazbir-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Historians (ex. <a href="/wiki/Janusz_Tazbir" title="Janusz Tazbir">Janusz Tazbir</a><sup id="cite_ref-Tazbir_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tazbir-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>, <a href="/wiki/Jacek_Moskwa" title="Jacek Moskwa">Jacek Moskwa</a><sup id="cite_ref-Moskwa_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moskwa-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rimantas_Miknys&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rimantas Miknys (page does not exist)">Rimantas Miknys</a><sup id="cite_ref-Moskwa_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moskwa-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Magda_Teter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Magda Teter (page does not exist)">Magda Teter</a><sup id="cite_ref-Teter_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Teter-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>) who have studied the story of Potocki, however, believe it to be invented,<sup id="cite_ref-Tazbir_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tazbir-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Moskwa_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moskwa-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Teter_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Teter-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> although it is unknown when or by whom (Moskwa points to a possibility that the author was Kraszewski himself, who is known to have invented some tales he claimed were true<sup id="cite_ref-Moskwa_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moskwa-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>). Teter mentioned that the story ("a carefully crafted tale of conversion") was likely created and developed as a "response to a number of challenges that the Polish Jewish community faced from the mid-eighteenth century".<sup id="cite_ref-Teter_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Teter-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Tazbir_.282003.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Tazbir_(2003)">Tazbir (2003)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abraham_ben_Abraham&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tazbir (2003)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Polish historian <a href="/wiki/Janusz_Tazbir" title="Janusz Tazbir">Janusz Tazbir</a> asserted that the story &#8211; he uses the term "legend" &#8211; originated at the turn of the 19th century and was published in a Jewish periodical issued in London as "The Jewish Expositor and Friend of Israel" (vol. 8, 1822).<sup id="cite_ref-Tazbir_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tazbir-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> He notes that the literary version of the legend was created by <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B3zef_Ignacy_Kraszewski" title="Józef Ignacy Kraszewski">Józef Ignacy Kraszewski</a>, a well-known Polish writer of the 19th century, author of numerous historical novels, who included the story about Potocki in the third volume of the history of Vilna (1841), <i>Wilno od początków jego do roku 1750</i> (1840–1842),<sup id="cite_ref-Tazbir_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tazbir-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> in which he claims to have followed a <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a> original, thought by some to be from <i>Ammudei Beit Yehudah</i> (<a href="/wiki/Judah_Hurwitz" title="Judah Hurwitz">Judah Hurwitz</a>, Amsterdam 1766). (Extant copies of <i>Ammudei Beit Yehudah</i> contain no reference to the story other than a brief mention of the execution of the elderly Rabbi Mann in Wilno). The story was then popularised through Russian translations, and there is evidence that a cult of Potocki's grave in Vilna has existed until the <a href="/wiki/Jewish_cemeteries_of_Vilnius" title="Jewish cemeteries of Vilnius">Jewish cemetery</a> (at <a href="/wiki/%C5%BDirm%C5%ABnai" title="Žirmūnai">Pióromont</a> also known as <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0nipi%C5%A1k%C4%97s" title="Šnipiškės">Snipiszki</a> quarter) was destroyed by <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">Nazis</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and later by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviets</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Moskwa_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moskwa-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Some sources claim his remains were rescued along with those of <a href="/wiki/Vilna_Gaon" title="Vilna Gaon">Vilna Gaon</a>, though it is said that there is no modern monument or grave clearly identified as Potocki's in Vilna or elsewhere.<sup id="cite_ref-Moskwa_12-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moskwa-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> However, historian Sid Leiman has identified what he thinks is a likely Potocki's grave by examination of the gravestones near the Vilna Gaon's.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Tazbir claimed that the tragic fate of Potocki, passed through Jewish oral tradition, remains unconfirmed by 18th-century Polish or Jewish <a href="/wiki/Primary_source" title="Primary source">primary sources</a> and that there is no evidence in any archives or genealogy tree that Potocki existed.<sup id="cite_ref-Tazbir_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tazbir-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> However, it has since been noted that the basic facts were related in a work published by Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Emden" title="Jacob Emden">Jacob Emden</a> in 1756, as noted above. In this work Rabbi Emden wrote (page 25b) "... what occurred in our times, a few years ago, in Wilno the head of Lithuania, that a great nobleman of the family Potocki converted and was seized and incarcerated ...".
</p><p>Tazbir also notes that the Polish nobility was guaranteed the freedom of faith (by acts like <a href="/wiki/Neminem_captivabimus" title="Neminem captivabimus">Neminem captivabimus</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Confederation" title="Warsaw Confederation">Warsaw Confederation</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment" title="Capital punishment">capital punishment</a> was extremely rare.<sup id="cite_ref-Tazbir_11-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tazbir-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> (though see also <a href="/wiki/Iwan_Tyszkiewicz" title="Iwan Tyszkiewicz">Iwan Tyszkiewicz</a>).  He argues that the described fate should have caused an uproar among the Polish nobility (consider, for example, the case of <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Zborowski" title="Samuel Zborowski">Samuel Zborowski</a>), and would be the only historical example of execution by burning of a nobleman &#8211; yet no contemporary source from the <a href="/wiki/Polish-Lithuanian_Commonwealth" class="mw-redirect" title="Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth">Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth</a> mentions this event even in a passing reference.<sup id="cite_ref-Tazbir_11-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tazbir-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In addition, it is difficult to believe that the death at the stake of a Polish aristocrat, from one of the most powerful Polish magnate families, charged with a religious crime, was not echoed in any of the diaries or polemical writings concerning religion and tolerance, topics in which the Polish nobility, and the entire European <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a>, were particularly interested.<sup id="cite_ref-Tazbir_11-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tazbir-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Tazbir has concluded that "the court trial and death of Walentyn Potocki should be recognised as an historical legend deprived of all source–material foundations".<sup id="cite_ref-Tazbir_11-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tazbir-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><br />
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Similar_story_of_Abraham_Isacowicz">Similar story of Abraham Isacowicz</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abraham_ben_Abraham&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Similar story of Abraham Isacowicz">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="box-Original_research plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Original_research" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/40px-Ambox_important.svg.png" decoding="async" width="40" height="40" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/60px-Ambox_important.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/80px-Ambox_important.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="40" data-file-height="40" /></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>possibly contains <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research">original research</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Abraham_ben_Abraham&amp;action=edit">improve it</a> by <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verifying</a> the claims made and adding <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#Inline_citations" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">inline citations</a>. Statements consisting only of original research should be removed.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">August 2015</span>)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Some have suggested that the Potocki legend is an embellishment of a different story. A report published in the July 1753 edition of <a href="/wiki/The_London_Magazine" title="The London Magazine">The London Magazine</a><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> describes the story of a very similar execution. The correspondent dated his report June 11, two days after the end of the <a href="/wiki/Shavuot" title="Shavuot">Shavuot</a> holiday. It describes "an apostate named Raphael Sentimany, a native of <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a>", who converted to Judaism at the age of 12 and adopted the name <b>Abraham Isacowicz.</b> The report describes his imprisonment and execution in Wilno as the Potocki legend describes. The report also states that he was executed on June 9, which was the second day of Shavuot, just as in the Potocki story. The only important differences between the Sentimany execution and the Potocki legend are that the <a href="/wiki/Martyr" title="Martyr">martyr</a>'s Jewish surname was Isacowicz, called Rafael Sentimany rather than Valenty Potocki, was killed in 1753 rather than 1749, and that he was a Croatian immigrant rather than a Polish noble.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Raphael Sentimany is also mentioned in the anonymous British work "Admonitions from the Dead, in Epistles to the Living", published in 1754, in a manner suggestive of the wide exposure of the original report of Abraham ben Abraham's execution.
</p>
