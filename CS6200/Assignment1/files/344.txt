<p>The <b>Sorbian languages</b> (<a href="/wiki/Upper_Sorbian_language" title="Upper Sorbian language">Upper Sorbian</a>: <i lang="hsb">serbska rěč</i>, <a href="/wiki/Lower_Sorbian_language" title="Lower Sorbian language">Lower Sorbian</a>: <i lang="dsb">serbska rěc</i>) are two closely related, but only partially mutually intelligible, <a href="/wiki/West_Slavic_languages" title="West Slavic languages">West Slavic languages</a> spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Sorbs" title="Sorbs">Sorbs</a>, a <a href="/wiki/West_Slavs" title="West Slavs">West Slavic</a> minority in the <a href="/wiki/Lusatia" title="Lusatia">Lusatia</a> region of eastern <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. They are classified under the <a href="/wiki/West_Slavic_languages" title="West Slavic languages">West Slavic</a> branch of the <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European languages</a> and are therefore closely related to the other two West Slavic subgroups: <a href="/wiki/Lechitic_languages" title="Lechitic languages">Lechitic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Czech%E2%80%93Slovak_languages" title="Czech–Slovak languages">Czech–Slovak</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Faska_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Faska-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Historically, the languages have also been known as <b>Wendish</b> (named after the <a href="/wiki/Wends" title="Wends">Wends</a>, earliest Slavic people in modern Poland and Germany) or <b>Lusatian</b>. Their collective <a href="/wiki/ISO_639" title="ISO 639">ISO 639</a>-2 code is <tt>wen</tt>.
</p><p>The two Sorbian languages and <a href="/wiki/Literary_language" title="Literary language">literary standards</a> are <a href="/wiki/Upper_Sorbian_language" title="Upper Sorbian language">Upper Sorbian</a> (<i lang="hsb" title="Upper Sorbian language text">hornjoserbsce</i>), spoken by about 40,000 people in <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxony</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lower_Sorbian_language" title="Lower Sorbian language">Lower Sorbian</a> (<i lang="dsb" title="Lower Sorbian language text">dolnoserbski</i>) spoken by about 10,000 people in <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg" title="Brandenburg">Brandenburg</a>. The area where the two languages are spoken is known as <a href="/wiki/Lusatia" title="Lusatia">Lusatia</a> (<i lang="hsb" title="Upper Sorbian language text">Łužica</i> in Upper Sorbian, <i lang="dsb" title="Lower Sorbian language text">Łužyca</i> in Lower Sorbian, or <i lang="de" title="German language text">Lausitz</i> in <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sorbian_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the settlement of the formerly Germanic territories (the part largely corresponding to the former <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a>) by the Sorbs' Slavic ancestors in the fifth and sixth centuries, the Sorbian language (or its predecessors) had been in use in much of what was the southern half of East Germany for several centuries, and still had its stronghold in (Upper and Lower) Lusatia, where it enjoys national protection and fostering to the present day. Outside Lusatia, it has been superseded by German. From the 13th century on, the language suffered official discrimination.<sup id="cite_ref-Faska_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Faska-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bible_translations_into_Sorbian" title="Bible translations into Sorbian">Bible translations into Sorbian</a> provided the foundations for its writing system.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic_distribution">Geographic distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sorbian_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geographic distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Germany, Upper and Lower Sorbian are officially recognized and protected as minority languages.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="Need the year this event took place (September 2017)">year&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In the home areas of the Sorbs, both languages are recognized as second official languages next to German.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="Need the year this event took place (September 2017)">year&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bautzen_Ortsschild.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Bautzen_Ortsschild.jpg/220px-Bautzen_Ortsschild.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Bautzen_Ortsschild.jpg/330px-Bautzen_Ortsschild.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Bautzen_Ortsschild.jpg/440px-Bautzen_Ortsschild.jpg 2x" data-file-width="992" data-file-height="744" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bautzen_Ortsschild.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A bilingual sign in <a href="/wiki/Bautzen" title="Bautzen">Bautzen</a></div></div></div>
<p>The city of <a href="/wiki/Bautzen" title="Bautzen">Bautzen</a> in <a href="/wiki/Upper_Lusatia" title="Upper Lusatia">Upper Lusatia</a> is the centre of Upper Sorbian culture. Bilingual signs can be seen around the city, including the name of the city, "Bautzen/<i lang="hsb" title="Upper Sorbian language text">Budyšin</i>".
The city of <a href="/wiki/Cottbus" title="Cottbus">Cottbus</a> (<i lang="dsb" title="Lower Sorbian language text">Chóśebuz</i>) is considered the cultural centre of Lower Sorbian; here, too, bilingual signs are found.
Sorbian has also been spoken in the small Sorbian ("Wendish") settlement of <a href="/wiki/Serbin,_Texas" title="Serbin, Texas">Serbin</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lee_County,_Texas" title="Lee County, Texas">Lee County, Texas</a>, and  a few speakers possibly still remain there. Until 1949, newspapers were published in Sorbian there. The local dialect has been heavily influenced by surrounding speakers of German and <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>.
</p><p>The German terms "Wends" (<i>Wenden</i>) and "Wendish" (<i>wendisch/Wendisch</i>) once denoted "Slav(ic)" generally;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> they are today mostly replaced by "Sorbs" (<i>Sorben</i>) and "Sorbian" (<i>sorbisch/Sorbisch</i>) with reference to Sorbian communities in Germany.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistic_features">Linguistic features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sorbian_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Linguistic features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Both Upper and Lower Sorbian have the <a href="/wiki/Dual_(grammatical_number)" title="Dual (grammatical number)">dual</a> for <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">nouns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">pronouns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adjectives</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">verbs</a>; very few living Indo-European languages retain this as a productive feature of the grammar. For example, the word <i lang="dsb" title="Lower Sorbian language text">ruka</i> is used for one hand, <i lang="dsb" title="Lower Sorbian language text">ruce</i> for two hands, and <i lang="dsb" title="Lower Sorbian language text">ruki</i> for more than two hands. As with most of the <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">Slavic languages</a>, Sorbian uses no articles.
</p>
