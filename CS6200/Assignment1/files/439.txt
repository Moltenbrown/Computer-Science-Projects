<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matrifocal_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The concept of the matrifocal family was introduced to the study of Caribbean societies by Raymond Smith in 1956. He linked the emergence of matrifocal families with how households are formed in the region: "The household group tends to be matri-focal in the sense that a woman in the status of 'mother' is usually the de facto leader of the group, and conversely the husband-father, although de jure head of the household group (if present), is usually marginal to the complex of internal relationships of the group. By 'marginal' we mean that he associates relatively infrequently with the other members of the group, and is on the fringe of the effective ties which bind the group together".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Smith emphasises that a matrifocal family is not simply woman-centred, but rather mother-centred; women in their role as mothers become key to organising the family group; men tend to be marginal to this organisation and to the household (though they may have a more central role in other networks). Where matrifocal families are common, marriage is less common.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In later work, Smith tends to emphasise the household less, and to see matrifocality more in terms of how the family network forms with mothers as key nodes in the network. Throughout, Smith argues that matrifocal kinship should be seen as a subsystem in a larger stratified society and its cultural values.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He increasingly emphasises how the Afro-Caribbean matrifocal family is best understood within of a class-race hierarchy where marriage is connected to perceived status and prestige.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>"A family or domestic group is matrifocal when it is centred on a woman and her children. In this case the father(s) of these children are intermittently present in the life of the group and occupy a secondary place. The children's mother is not necessarily the wife of one of the children's fathers."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In general, according to Laura Hobson Herlihy citing P. Mohammed, women have "high status" if they are "the main wage earners", they "control&#160;... the household economy", and males tend to be absent.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Men's absences are often of long durations.<sup id="cite_ref-MiskitoCoast-p137_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MiskitoCoast-p137-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> One of R.&#160;T. Smith's contemporary critics, M.&#160;G. Smith, notes that while households may appear matrifocal taken by themselves, the <i>linkages between</i> households may be patrifocal. That is, a man in his role as father may be providing (particularly economic) support to a mother in one or more households whether he lives in that household or not. Both for men and for women having children with more than one partner is a common feature of this kind of system.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Alternative terms for 'matrifocal' or 'matrifocality' include <i>matricentric</i>, <i>matripotestal</i>, and <i>women-centered kinship networks</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-MiskitoCoast-p134_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MiskitoCoast-p134-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The matrifocal is distinguished from the <a href="/wiki/Matrilocal_residence" title="Matrilocal residence">matrilocal</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Matrilineality" title="Matrilineality">matrilineal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matrilateral" title="Matrilateral">matrilateral</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matriarchy" title="Matriarchy">matriarchy</a> (the last because matrifocality does not imply that women have power in the larger community).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics_and_distribution">Characteristics and distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matrifocal_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics and distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Godelier" title="Maurice Godelier">Maurice Godelier</a>, matrifocality is "typical of Afro-Caribbean groups" and some African-American communities.<sup id="cite_ref-MetamorphosesKin-p457n31_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MetamorphosesKin-p457n31-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> These include families in which a father has a wife and one or more mistresses; in a few cases, a mother may have more than one lover.<sup id="cite_ref-MetamorphosesKin-p457n31_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MetamorphosesKin-p457n31-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  Matrifocality was also found, according to Rasmussen per Herlihy, among the <a href="/wiki/Tuareg_people" title="Tuareg people">Tuareg people</a> in northern Africa;<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> according to Herlihy citing other authors, in some <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea#Geography" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean</a> communities;<sup id="cite_ref-MiskitoCoast-p137_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MiskitoCoast-p137-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and, according to Herlihy quoting Scott, in urban <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In their study of family life in Bethnal Green, London, during the 1950s, Young and Wilmott found both <a href="/wiki/Matrifocal" class="mw-redirect" title="Matrifocal">matrifocal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matrilineal" class="mw-redirect" title="Matrilineal">matrilineal</a> elements at work: mothers were a focus for distributing economic resources through the family network; they were also active in passing down the rights to tenancies in matrilineal succession to their daughters.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Herlihy found matrifocality among the <a href="/wiki/Miskito_people" title="Miskito people">Miskitu people</a>, in the village of Kuri, on the Caribbean coast of northeastern <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a> in the late 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> According to Herlihy, the "main power"<sup id="cite_ref-MiskitoCoast-p134_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MiskitoCoast-p134-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> of Kuri women lies "in their ability to craft everyday social identities and kinship relations&#160;.... Their power lies beyond the scope of the Honduran state, which recognizes male surnames and males as legitimate heads of households."<sup id="cite_ref-MiskitoCoast-p134_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MiskitoCoast-p134-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Herlihy found in Kuri a trend toward matriliny<sup id="cite_ref-MiskitoCoast-p141_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MiskitoCoast-p141-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and a correlation with matrilineality,<sup id="cite_ref-MiskitoCoast-p145_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MiskitoCoast-p145-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> while some patriarchal norms also existed.<sup id="cite_ref-MiskitoCoast-p145_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MiskitoCoast-p145-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Herlihy found that the "women knew more than most men about village histories, genealogies, and local folklore"<sup id="cite_ref-MiskitoCoast-p141_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MiskitoCoast-p141-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and that "men typically did not know local kinship relations, the proper terms of reference, or reciprocity obligations in their wife's family"<sup id="cite_ref-MiskitoCoast-p141_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MiskitoCoast-p141-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and concluded that Miskitu women "increasingly assume responsibility for the social reproduction of identities and ultimately for preserving worldwide cultural and linguistic diversity".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Nair" title="Nair">Nair</a> community in Kerala and the <a href="/wiki/Bunt_(community)" title="Bunt (community)">Bunt</a> community in Tulunadu in South India are prime examples of matrifocality.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> This can be attributed to the fact that if males were largely warriors by profession, a community was bound to lose male members at youth, leading to a situation where the females assumed the role of running the family.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matrifocal_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 14th century, in <a href="/wiki/Jiangnan" title="Jiangnan">Jiangnan</a>, South China, under Mongol rule by the <a href="/wiki/Yuan_dynasty" title="Yuan dynasty">Yuan dynasty</a>, Kong Qi kept a diary of his view of some families as practicing gynarchy, not defined as it is in major dictionaries<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> but defined by Paul J. Smith as "the creation of short-term family structures dominated by women"<sup id="cite_ref-FearGynarchyAgeChaos-JESHO-p45-n134_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FearGynarchyAgeChaos-JESHO-p45-n134-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> and not as matrilineal or matriarchal.<sup id="cite_ref-FearGynarchyAgeChaos-JESHO-p45-n134_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FearGynarchyAgeChaos-JESHO-p45-n134-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The gynarchy possibly could be passed down through generations.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> According to Paul J. Smith, it was to this kind of gynarchy that "Kong ascribed...the general <a href="/wiki/Societal_collapse" title="Societal collapse">collapse of society</a>"<sup id="cite_ref-FearGynarchyAgeChaos-JESHO-p45-n134_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FearGynarchyAgeChaos-JESHO-p45-n134-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> and Kong believed that men in Jiangnan tended to "forfeit...authority to women".<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Matrifocality arose, Godelier said, in some <a href="/wiki/Afro-Caribbean" title="Afro-Caribbean">Afro-Caribbean</a> and <a href="/wiki/African_American" class="mw-redirect" title="African American">African American</a> cultures as a consequence of <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">enslavement</a> of thousands.<sup id="cite_ref-MetamorphosesKin-p457n31_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MetamorphosesKin-p457n31-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Slaves were forbidden to marry and their children belonged to the slavemasters.<sup id="cite_ref-MetamorphosesKin-p457n31_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MetamorphosesKin-p457n31-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Women in slave families "often" sought impregnation by White masters so the children would have lighter <a href="/wiki/Discrimination_based_on_skin_color" title="Discrimination based on skin color">skin color</a> and be more successful in life,<sup id="cite_ref-MetamorphosesKin-p457n31_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MetamorphosesKin-p457n31-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> lessening the role of Black husbands. Some societies, particularly Western European, allow women to enter the paid labor force or receive government aid and thus be able to afford to raise children alone<sup id="cite_ref-MetamorphosesKin-p457n31_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MetamorphosesKin-p457n31-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> while some other societies "oppose&#160;... [women] living on their own."<sup id="cite_ref-MetamorphosesKin-p457n31_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MetamorphosesKin-p457n31-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In feminist belief (more common in the 1970s than in the 1990s–2000s and criticized within feminism and within archaeology, anthropology, and theology as lacking a scholarly basis), there was a "matrifocal (if not matriarchal) Golden Age" before <a href="/wiki/Patriarchy" title="Patriarchy">patriarchy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
