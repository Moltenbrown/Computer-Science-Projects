	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Nuclear family</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>A <b>nuclear family</b>, <b>elementary family</b> or <b>conjugal family</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">family</a> group consisting of two parents and their <a href="/wiki/Child" title="Child">children</a> (one or more).<sup id="cite_ref-BritannicaOnline_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BritannicaOnline-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It is in contrast to a <a href="/wiki/Single-parent" class="mw-redirect" title="Single-parent">single-parent</a> family, the larger <a href="/wiki/Extended_family" title="Extended family">extended family</a>, and a family with more than two parents. Nuclear families typically center on a <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married</a> couple;<sup id="cite_ref-BritannicaOnline_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BritannicaOnline-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> the nuclear family may have any number of children. There are differences in definition among observers; some definitions allow only biological children that are full-blood siblings,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> but others allow for a stepparent and any mix of dependent children including stepchildren and adopted children.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Family structures of a married couple and their children were present in Western Europe and New England in the 17th century, influenced by church and theocratic governments.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> With the emergence of <a href="/wiki/Proto-industrialization" title="Proto-industrialization">proto-industrialization</a> and early capitalism, the nuclear family became a financially viable social unit.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The term <i>nuclear family</i> first appeared in the early twentieth century. Alternative definitions have evolved to include family units headed by <a href="/wiki/LGBT_parenting" title="LGBT parenting">same-sex parents</a><sup id="cite_ref-BritannicaOnline_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BritannicaOnline-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and perhaps additional adult relatives who take on a cohabiting parental role;<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> in the latter case, it also receives the name of <a href="/wiki/Conjugal_family" title="Conjugal family">conjugal family</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BritannicaOnline_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BritannicaOnline-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The concept that narrowly defines a nuclear family as central to stability in modern society that has been promoted by <a href="/wiki/Familialist" class="mw-redirect" title="Familialist">familialists</a> who are <a href="/wiki/Social_conservative" class="mw-redirect" title="Social conservative">social conservatives</a> in the United States, and has been challenged as historically and sociologically inadequate to describe the complexity of actual family relations.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In "Freudian Theories of Identification and Their Derivatives" <a href="/wiki/Urie_Bronfenbrenner" title="Urie Bronfenbrenner">Urie Bronfenbrenner</a> states, "Very little is known about the extent variation in the behavior of fathers and mothers towards sons and daughters, and even less about the possible effects on such differential treatment." Little is known about how parental behavior and identification processes work, and how children interpret sex role learning. In his theory, he uses "identification" with the father in the sense that the son will follow the sex role provided by his father and then for the father to be able to identify the difference of the "cross sex" parent for his daughter.
</p><p>Historians <a href="/wiki/Alan_Macfarlane" title="Alan Macfarlane">Alan Macfarlane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Laslett" title="Peter Laslett">Peter Laslett</a> postulated that nuclear families have been a primary arrangement in England since the 13th century. This primary arrangement was different from the normal arrangements in Southern Europe, in parts of Asia, and the Middle East where it was common for young adults to remain in or marry into the family home. In England, multi-generational households were uncommon because young adults would save enough money to move out, into their own household once they married. Sociologist Brigitte Berger argued, "the young nuclear family had to be flexible and mobile as it searched for opportunity and property. Forced to rely on their own ingenuity, its members also needed to plan for the future and develop bourgeois habits of work and saving."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Berge also mentions that this could be one of the reasons why the Industrial Revolution began in England and other Northwest European countries. However, the historicity of the nuclear family in England has been challenged by Cord Oestmann.<sup id="cite_ref-Oestmann1994_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oestmann1994-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As a <a href="/wiki/Fertility_factor_(demography)" title="Fertility factor (demography)">fertility factor</a>, single nuclear family households generally have a higher number of children than co-operative living arrangements according to studies from both the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western world</a><sup id="cite_ref-balbo2013_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-balbo2013-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There have been studies done that shows a difference in the number of children wanted per household according to where they live. Families that live in rural areas wanted to have more kids than families in urban areas. A study done in Japan between October 2011 and February 2012 further researched the effect of area of residence on mean desired number of children.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Researchers of the study came to the conclusion that the women living in rural areas with larger families were more likely to want more children, compared to women that lived in urban areas in Japan.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_of_the_term">Usage of the term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Usage of the term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:W.H._Shumard_family,_circa_1955.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A man, woman, and two children smiling outside of a house" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/W.H._Shumard_family%2C_circa_1955.jpg/220px-W.H._Shumard_family%2C_circa_1955.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/W.H._Shumard_family%2C_circa_1955.jpg/330px-W.H._Shumard_family%2C_circa_1955.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/W.H._Shumard_family%2C_circa_1955.jpg/440px-W.H._Shumard_family%2C_circa_1955.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:W.H._Shumard_family,_circa_1955.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An American nuclear family composed of the mother, father, and their children circa 1955</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Merriam-Webster" title="Merriam-Webster">Merriam-Webster</a> dates the term back to 1947,<sup id="cite_ref-Merriam-Webster_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Merriam-Webster-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> while the <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> has a reference to the term from 1925; thus it is relatively new.
</p><p>
In its most common usage, the term <i>nuclear family</i> refers to a household consisting of a <a href="/wiki/Father" title="Father">father</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Mother" title="Mother">mother</a> and their <a href="/wiki/Child" title="Child">children</a><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> all in one household dwelling.<sup id="cite_ref-Merriam-Webster_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Merriam-Webster-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/George_Murdock" title="George Murdock">George Murdock</a>, an observer of families, offered an early description: <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style></p><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The family is a social group characterized by common residence, economic cooperation and reproduction. It contains adults of both sexes, at least two of whom maintain a socially approved sexual relationship, and one or more children, own or adopted, of the sexually cohabiting adults.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>Many individuals are part of two nuclear families in their lives: the family of origin in which they are offspring, and the family of procreation in which they are a parent.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While the phrase dates approximately from the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_Age" title="Atomic Age">Atomic Age</a>, the term "nuclear" is not used here in the context of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_warfare" title="Nuclear warfare">nuclear warfare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">nuclear fission</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a>; rather, it arises from a more general use of the noun <i>nucleus</i>, itself originating in the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>nux</i>, meaning "nut", i.e. the core of something – thus, the nuclear family refers to all members of the family being part of the same core rather than directly to <a href="/wiki/Atomic_weapons" class="mw-redirect" title="Atomic weapons">atomic weapons</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compared_with_extended_family">Compared with extended family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Compared with extended family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Extended_family" title="Extended family">Extended family</a></div>
<p>An extended family group consists of non-nuclear (or "non-immediate") family members considered together with nuclear (or "immediate") family members. When extended family is involved they also influence children's development just as much as the parents would on their own.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> In an extended family resources are usually shared among those involved adding more of a community aspect to the family unit. This is not just talking about sharing money and physical thing but includes sharing time for example extended family such as <a href="/wiki/Grandparent" title="Grandparent">grandparents</a> can watch over their grandchildren allowing parents to continue and pursue careers and creating a healthy and supportive environment the children to grow up in and allows the parents to have much less stress.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Extended families help keep the kids in the family healthier because of all the resources the kids get now that they have other individuals able to help them and support them as they grow up.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Changes_to_family_formation">Changes to family formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Changes to family formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Families_US.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Families_US.svg/300px-Families_US.svg.png" decoding="async" width="300" height="252" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Families_US.svg/450px-Families_US.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Families_US.svg/600px-Families_US.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="630" data-file-height="530" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Families_US.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>From 1970 to 2000, family arrangements in the US became more diverse with no particular household arrangement prevalent enough to be identified as the "average"</div></div></div>
<p>In 2005, information from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a> showed that 70% of children in the US live in two-parent families,<sup id="cite_ref-Marriages,_Families_&amp;_Intimate_Relationships_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marriages,_Families_&amp;_Intimate_Relationships-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> with 66% of those living with parents who were married, and 60% living with their biological parents. The information also explained that "the figures suggest that the tumultuous shifts in family structure since the late 1960s have leveled off since 1990".<sup id="cite_ref-uscensus_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uscensus-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When considered separately from couples without children, <a href="/wiki/Single-parent" class="mw-redirect" title="Single-parent">single-parent</a> families, and unmarried couples with children, the United States nuclear families appear to constitute a minority of households – with a rising prevalence of other family arrangements. In 2000, nuclear families with the original biological parents constituted roughly 24.10% of American households, compared with 40.30% in 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-Marriages,_Families_&amp;_Intimate_Relationships_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marriages,_Families_&amp;_Intimate_Relationships-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Roughly two-thirds of all children in the United States will spend at least some time in a single-parent household.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> According to some sociologists, "[The nuclear family] no longer seems adequate to cover the wide diversity of household arrangements we see today." (Edwards 1991; Stacey 1996). A new term has been introduced<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (March 2014)">by whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, <i>postmodern family</i>, intended to describe the great variability in family forms, including single-parent families and couples without children."<sup id="cite_ref-Marriages,_Families_&amp;_Intimate_Relationships_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marriages,_Families_&amp;_Intimate_Relationships-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Nuclear family households are now less common compared to household with couples without children, single-parent families, and unmarried couples with children.
</p><p>In the UK, the number of nuclear families fell from 39.0% of all households in 1968 to 28.0% in 1992. The decrease accompanied an equivalent increase in the number of single-parent households and in the number of adults living alone.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to some sociologists, "[The nuclear family] no longer seems adequate to cover the wide diversity of household arrangements we see today." (Edwards 1991; Stacey 1996). A new term has been introduced<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (March 2014)">by whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, <i>postmodern family</i>, intended to describe the great variability in family forms, including single-parent families and couples without children."<sup id="cite_ref-Marriages,_Families_&amp;_Intimate_Relationships_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marriages,_Families_&amp;_Intimate_Relationships-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Professor Wolfgang Haak of <a href="/wiki/University_of_Adelaide" title="University of Adelaide">Adelaide University</a>, detects traces of the nuclear family in prehistoric Central Europe. A 2005 archeological dig in Elau in Germany, analyzed by Haak, revealed genetic evidence suggesting that the 13 individuals found in a grave were closely related. Haak said, "By establishing the genetic links between the two adults and two children buried together in one grave, we have established the presence of the classic nuclear family in a prehistoric context in Central Europe.... Their unity in death suggest[s] a unity in life."<sup id="cite_ref-:17_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:17-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> This paper does not regard the nuclear family as "natural" or as the only model for human family life. "This does not establish the elemental family to be a universal model or the most ancient institution of human communities. For example, polygamous unions are prevalent in ethnographic data and models of household communities have apparently been involving a high degree of complexity from their origins."<sup id="cite_ref-:17_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:17-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In this study evidence suggests that the nuclear family was embedded with an extended family. The remains of three children (probably siblings based on DNA evidence) were found buried with a woman who was not their mother but may have been an "aunt or a step-mother".<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="North_American_conservatism">North American conservatism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: North American conservatism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Familialism" title="Familialism">Familialism</a></div>
<table class="box-Expand_section plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><a href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg" class="image"><img alt="[icon]" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" decoding="async" width="20" height="14" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="44" data-file-height="31" /></a></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs expansion</b>. <small>You can help by <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=">adding to it</a>.</small>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">June 2013</span>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>For <a href="/wiki/Social_conservatism" title="Social conservatism">social conservatism</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Social_conservatism_in_the_United_States" title="Social conservatism in the United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_conservatism_in_Canada" title="Social conservatism in Canada">Canada</a>, the idea that the nuclear family is <a href="/wiki/Tradition" title="Tradition">traditional</a> is an important aspect, where <a href="/wiki/Family_values" title="Family values">family is seen as the primary unit of society</a>. These movements oppose <a href="/wiki/Alternative_family" class="mw-redirect" title="Alternative family">alternative family</a> forms and social institutions that are seen by them to undermine <a href="/wiki/Parental_authority" class="mw-redirect" title="Parental authority">parental authority</a>. The numbers of nuclear families is slowly dwindling in the US as more women pursue higher education, develop professional lives, and delay having children until later in their life.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Children and <a href="/wiki/Marriage_in_the_United_States" title="Marriage in the United States">marriage</a> have become less appealing as many women continue to face societal, familial, and/or peer pressure to give up their education and successful career to focus on stabilizing the home.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> As diversity in the United States continues to increase, it is becoming difficult for the classically traditional family unit—a heterosexual couple with two biological children, usually a first born male and a younger female, to stay the norm.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Data from 2014 also suggests that <a href="/wiki/Single_parent" title="Single parent">single parents</a> and the likelihood of children living with one is also determined by race. Pew Research Center has found that 54% of African-American individuals will be single parents compared to 19% of Caucasian individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_25-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Several factors account for the differences in family structure including economic and social class. Differences in education level also change the amount of single parents. In 2014, those with less than a high school education are 46% more likely to be a single parent compared to 12% who have graduated from college.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_25-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Critics of the term "traditional family" point out that in most cultures and at most times, the <a href="/wiki/Extended_family" title="Extended family">extended family</a> model has been most common, not the nuclear family,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> though it has had a longer tradition in England<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> than in other parts of Europe and Asia which contributed large numbers of immigrants to the Americas.  The nuclear family became the most common form in the U.S. in the 1960s and 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
