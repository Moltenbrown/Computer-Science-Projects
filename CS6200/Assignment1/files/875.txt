<p><b>Ravenna</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;r&#39; in &#39;rye&#39;">r</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;v&#39; in &#39;vie&#39;">v</span><span title="/ɛ/: &#39;e&#39; in &#39;dress&#39;">ɛ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling">rə-<span style="font-size:90%">VEN</span>-ə</i></a>, <small>Italian:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Italian" title="Help:IPA/Italian">[raˈvenna]</a></span>, <small>also</small> <small>locally&#32;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Italian_dialects" title="Help:IPA/Italian dialects">[raˈvɛnna]</a></span>&#32;<span class="nowrap" style="font-size:85%">(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap;margin-right:.25em;"><a href="/wiki/File:It-Ravenna.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" decoding="async" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a></span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8b/It-Ravenna.ogg" class="internal" title="It-Ravenna.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</span>; <a href="/wiki/Romagnol_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Romagnol language">Romagnol</a>: <i lang="rgn">Ravèna</i>) is the capital city of the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Ravenna" title="Province of Ravenna">Province of Ravenna</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Emilia-Romagna" title="Emilia-Romagna">Emilia-Romagna</a> region of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Italy" title="Northern Italy">Northern Italy</a>. It was the <a href="/wiki/Capital_city" title="Capital city">capital city</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a> from 402 until that empire collapsed in 476. It then served as the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Ostrogothic_Kingdom" title="Ostrogothic Kingdom">Ostrogothic Kingdom</a> until it was re-conquered in 540 by the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a>. Afterwards, the city formed the centre of the Byzantine <a href="/wiki/Exarchate_of_Ravenna" title="Exarchate of Ravenna">Exarchate of Ravenna</a> until the invasion of the <a href="/wiki/Lombards" title="Lombards">Lombards</a> in 751, after which it became the seat of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Lombards" title="Kingdom of the Lombards">Kingdom of the Lombards</a>.
</p><p>Although it is an inland city, Ravenna is connected to the <a href="/wiki/Adriatic_Sea" title="Adriatic Sea">Adriatic Sea</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Candiano_Canal" title="Candiano Canal">Candiano Canal</a>. It is known for its well-preserved late Roman and Byzantine architecture, with eight buildings comprising the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> "Early Christian Monuments of Ravenna".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ravenna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_Ravenna" title="Timeline of Ravenna">Timeline of Ravenna</a></div>
<p>The origin of the name <i>Ravenna</i> is unclear, although it is believed the name is <a href="/wiki/Etruscan_language" title="Etruscan language">Etruscan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Some have speculated that "ravenna" is related to "Rasenna" (later "Rasna"), the term that the Etruscans used for themselves, but there is no agreement on this point.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_era">Ancient era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ravenna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancient era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ostrogothic_Ravenna" title="Ostrogothic Ravenna">Ostrogothic Ravenna</a></div>
<p>The origins of Ravenna are uncertain.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The first settlement is variously attributed to (and then has seen the copresence of) the <a href="/wiki/Thessalians" class="mw-redirect" title="Thessalians">Thessalians</a>, the Etruscans and the <a href="/wiki/Umbri" title="Umbri">Umbrians</a>. Afterwards its territory was settled also by the <a href="/wiki/Senones" title="Senones">Senones</a>, especially the southern countryside of the city (that wasn't part of the lagoon), the <i>Ager Decimanus</i>. Ravenna consisted of houses built on piles on a series of small islands in a marshy lagoon – a situation similar to <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> several centuries later. The Romans ignored it during their conquest of the <a href="/wiki/Po_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Po River">Po River</a> Delta, but later accepted it into the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a> as a federated town in 89 <a href="/wiki/Common_Era" title="Common Era">BC</a>. In 49 BC, it was the location where <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a> gathered his forces before crossing the <a href="/wiki/Rubicon" title="Rubicon">Rubicon</a>. Later, after his battle against <a href="/wiki/Mark_Antony" title="Mark Antony">Mark Antony</a> in 31 BC, Emperor <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a> founded the military harbor of <a href="/wiki/Classe,_ancient_port_of_Ravenna" title="Classe, ancient port of Ravenna">Classe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This harbor, protected at first by its own walls, was an important station of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Navy" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Navy">Roman Imperial Fleet</a>. Nowadays the city is landlocked, but Ravenna remained an important <a href="/wiki/Seaport" class="mw-redirect" title="Seaport">seaport</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Adriatic" class="mw-redirect" title="Adriatic">Adriatic</a> until the early <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. During the Germanic campaigns, <a href="/wiki/Thusnelda" title="Thusnelda">Thusnelda</a>, widow of <a href="/wiki/Arminius" title="Arminius">Arminius</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Marbod" class="mw-redirect" title="Marbod">Marbod</a>, King of the <a href="/wiki/Marcomanni" title="Marcomanni">Marcomanni</a>, were confined at Ravenna.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ravenna(Peutinger_Map).png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Ravenna%28Peutinger_Map%29.png/220px-Ravenna%28Peutinger_Map%29.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="87" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Ravenna%28Peutinger_Map%29.png/330px-Ravenna%28Peutinger_Map%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Ravenna%28Peutinger_Map%29.png/440px-Ravenna%28Peutinger_Map%29.png 2x" data-file-width="498" data-file-height="197" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ravenna(Peutinger_Map).png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The city of Ravenna in the 4th century as shown on the Peutinger Map</div></div></div>
<p>Ravenna greatly prospered under Roman rule. Emperor <a href="/wiki/Trajan" title="Trajan">Trajan</a> built a 70&#160;km (43.50&#160;mi) long <a href="/wiki/Aqueduct_(Roman)" class="mw-redirect" title="Aqueduct (Roman)">aqueduct</a> at the beginning of the 2nd century. During the <a href="/wiki/Marcomannic_Wars" title="Marcomannic Wars">Marcomannic Wars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic</a> settlers in Ravenna revolted and managed to seize possession of the city. For this reason, Marcus Aurelius decided not only against bringing more barbarians into Italy, but even banished those who had previously been brought there.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In AD 402, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Honorius" class="mw-redirect" title="Emperor Honorius">Emperor Honorius</a> transferred the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a> from <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a> to Ravenna. At that time it was home to 50,000 people.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The transfer was made partly for defensive purposes: Ravenna was surrounded by swamps and marshes, and was perceived to be easily defensible (although in fact the city fell to opposing forces numerous times in its history); it is also likely that the move to Ravenna was due to the city's port and good sea-borne connections to the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Roman_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Roman Empire">Eastern Roman Empire</a>. However, in 409, King <a href="/wiki/Alaric_I" title="Alaric I">Alaric I</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Visigoths" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of the Visigoths">Visigoths</a> simply bypassed Ravenna, and went on to <a href="/wiki/Sack_of_Rome_(410)" title="Sack of Rome (410)">sack</a> <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> in 410 and to take <a href="/wiki/Galla_Placidia" title="Galla Placidia">Galla Placidia</a>, daughter of Emperor <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_I" title="Theodosius I">Theodosius I</a>, hostage. After many vicissitudes, Galla Placidia returned to Ravenna with her son, Emperor <a href="/wiki/Valentinian_III" title="Valentinian III">Valentinian III</a>, due to the support of her nephew <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_II" title="Theodosius II">Theodosius II</a>. Ravenna enjoyed a period of peace, during which time the Christian religion was favoured by the imperial court, and the city gained some of its most famous monuments, including the Orthodox Baptistery, the misnamed <a href="/wiki/Mausoleum_of_Galla_Placidia" title="Mausoleum of Galla Placidia">Mausoleum of Galla Placidia</a> (she was not actually buried there), and <a href="/wiki/San_Giovanni_Evangelista,_Ravenna" title="San Giovanni Evangelista, Ravenna">San Giovanni Evangelista</a>.
</p><p>The late 5th century saw the dissolution of Roman authority in the west, and <a href="/wiki/Romulus_Augustulus" title="Romulus Augustulus">the last person to hold the title of emperor in the West</a> was deposed in 476 by the general <a href="/wiki/Odoacer" title="Odoacer">Odoacer</a>. Odoacer ruled as King of Italy for 13 years, but in 489 the Eastern Emperor <a href="/wiki/Zeno_(emperor)" title="Zeno (emperor)">Zeno</a> sent the <a href="/wiki/Ostrogoth" class="mw-redirect" title="Ostrogoth">Ostrogoth</a> King <a href="/wiki/Theodoric_the_Great" title="Theodoric the Great">Theodoric the Great</a> to re-take the Italian peninsula. After losing the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Verona_(489)" title="Battle of Verona (489)">Battle of Verona</a>, <a href="/wiki/Odoacer" title="Odoacer">Odoacer</a> retreated to Ravenna, where he withstood a siege of three years by Theodoric, until the taking of <a href="/wiki/Rimini" title="Rimini">Rimini</a> deprived Ravenna of supplies. Theodoric took Ravenna in 493, supposedly slew Odoacer with his own hands, and Ravenna became the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Ostrogothic_Kingdom" title="Ostrogothic Kingdom">Ostrogothic Kingdom</a> of Italy. Theodoric, following his imperial predecessors, also built many splendid buildings in and around Ravenna, including his palace church <a href="/wiki/Sant%27Apollinare_Nuovo" class="mw-redirect" title="Sant&#39;Apollinare Nuovo">Sant'Apollinare Nuovo</a>, an Arian cathedral (now Santo Spirito) and Baptistery, and his own <a href="/wiki/Mausoleum_of_Theodoric" title="Mausoleum of Theodoric">Mausoleum</a> just outside the walls.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mausoleum_of_Theoderic.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Mausoleum_of_Theoderic.JPG/220px-Mausoleum_of_Theoderic.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Mausoleum_of_Theoderic.JPG/330px-Mausoleum_of_Theoderic.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Mausoleum_of_Theoderic.JPG/440px-Mausoleum_of_Theoderic.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2304" data-file-height="1728" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mausoleum_of_Theoderic.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Mausoleum of Theodoric.</div></div></div>
<p>Both Odoacer and Theodoric and their followers were <a href="/wiki/Arianism" title="Arianism">Arian</a> Christians, but co-existed peacefully with the Latins, who were largely Catholic Orthodox. Ravenna's Orthodox bishops carried out notable building projects, of which the sole surviving one is the Capella Arcivescovile. Theodoric allowed Roman citizens within his kingdom to be subject to Roman law and the Roman judicial system. The Goths, meanwhile, lived under their own laws and customs. In 519, when a mob had burned down the synagogues of Ravenna, Theodoric ordered the town to rebuild them at its own expense.
</p><p>Theodoric died in 526 and was succeeded by his young grandson <a href="/wiki/Athalaric" title="Athalaric">Athalaric</a> under the authority of his daughter <a href="/wiki/Amalasunta" class="mw-redirect" title="Amalasunta">Amalasunta</a>, but by 535 both were dead and Theodoric's line was represented only by Amalasuntha's daughter <a href="/wiki/Matasuntha" title="Matasuntha">Matasuntha</a>. Various Ostrogothic military leaders took the Kingdom of Italy, but none were as successful as Theodoric had been. Meanwhile, the <a href="/wiki/Orthodoxy" title="Orthodoxy">orthodox Christian</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_Byzantine_emperors" title="List of Byzantine emperors">Byzantine</a> Emperor <a href="/wiki/Justinian_I" title="Justinian I">Justinian I</a>, opposed both Ostrogoth rule and the <a href="/wiki/Arianism" title="Arianism">Arian</a> variety of Christianity. In 535 his general <a href="/wiki/Belisarius" title="Belisarius">Belisarius</a> <a href="/wiki/Gothic_War_(535%E2%80%93554)" title="Gothic War (535–554)">invaded Italy</a> and in 540 conquered Ravenna. After the conquest of Italy was completed in 554, Ravenna became the seat of Byzantine government in Italy.
</p><p>From 540 to 600, Ravenna's bishops embarked upon a notable building program of churches in Ravenna and in and around the port city of Classe. Surviving monuments include the <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_San_Vitale" title="Basilica of San Vitale">Basilica of San Vitale</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_Sant%27Apollinare_in_Classe" title="Basilica of Sant&#39;Apollinare in Classe">Basilica of Sant'Apollinare in Classe</a>, as well as the partially surviving San Michele in Africisco.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exarchate_of_Ravenna">Exarchate of Ravenna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ravenna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Exarchate of Ravenna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ravenna_SantApollinare_Classe3.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Ravenna_SantApollinare_Classe3.jpg/250px-Ravenna_SantApollinare_Classe3.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="169" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Ravenna_SantApollinare_Classe3.jpg/375px-Ravenna_SantApollinare_Classe3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Ravenna_SantApollinare_Classe3.jpg/500px-Ravenna_SantApollinare_Classe3.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3765" data-file-height="2538" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ravenna_SantApollinare_Classe3.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Transfiguration_of_Jesus" title="Transfiguration of Jesus">Transfiguration of Jesus</a>. Allegorical image with <a href="/wiki/Crux_gemmata" title="Crux gemmata">Crux gemmata</a> and lambs represent apostles, 533–549, <a href="/wiki/Apse" title="Apse">apse</a> of <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_Sant%27Apollinare_in_Classe" title="Basilica of Sant&#39;Apollinare in Classe">Basilica of Sant'Apollinare in Classe</a></div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Exarchate_of_Ravenna" title="Exarchate of Ravenna">Exarchate of Ravenna</a></div>
<p>Following the conquests of <a href="/wiki/Belisarius" title="Belisarius">Belisarius</a> for the Emperor <a href="/wiki/Justinian_I" title="Justinian I">Justinian I</a> in the 6th century, Ravenna became the seat of the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine</a> governor of Italy, the <a href="/wiki/Exarch" title="Exarch">Exarch</a>, and was known as the <a href="/wiki/Exarchate_of_Ravenna" title="Exarchate of Ravenna">Exarchate of Ravenna</a>. It was at this time that the <a href="/wiki/Ravenna_Cosmography" title="Ravenna Cosmography">Ravenna Cosmography</a> was written.
</p><p>Under Byzantine rule, the archbishop of the <a href="/wiki/Archbishopric_of_Ravenna" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishopric of Ravenna">Archdiocese of Ravenna</a> was temporarily granted <a href="/wiki/Autocephaly" title="Autocephaly">autocephaly</a> from the Roman Church by the emperor, in 666, but this was soon revoked. Nevertheless, the archbishop of Ravenna held the second place in Italy after the pope, and played an important role in many theological controversies during this period.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages_and_Renaissance">Middle Ages and Renaissance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ravenna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Middle Ages and Renaissance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Lombards" title="Lombards">Lombards</a>, under <a href="/wiki/King_Liutprand" class="mw-redirect" title="King Liutprand">King Liutprand</a>, occupied Ravenna in 712, but were forced to return it to the Byzantines.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> However, in 751 the Lombard king, <a href="/wiki/Aistulf" title="Aistulf">Aistulf</a>, succeeded in conquering Ravenna, thus ending Byzantine rule in northern Italy.
</p><p>King <a href="/wiki/Pepin_the_Short" title="Pepin the Short">Pepin</a> of the <a href="/wiki/King_of_the_Franks" class="mw-redirect" title="King of the Franks">Franks</a> attacked the Lombards under orders of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Stephen_II" title="Pope Stephen II">Pope Stephen II</a>. Ravenna then gradually came under the direct authority of the <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Popes</a>, although this was contested by the archbishops at various times. <a href="/wiki/Pope_Adrian_I" title="Pope Adrian I">Pope Adrian I</a> authorized <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a> to take away anything from Ravenna that he liked, and an unknown quantity of Roman columns, <a href="/wiki/Mosaic" title="Mosaic">mosaics</a>, statues, and other portable items were taken north to enrich his capital of <a href="/wiki/Aachen" title="Aachen">Aachen</a>.
</p><p>In 1198 Ravenna led a league of <a href="/wiki/Romagna" title="Romagna">Romagna</a> cities against the Emperor, and the Pope was able to subdue it. After the war of 1218 the <a href="/wiki/Traversari" title="Traversari">Traversari</a> family was able to impose its rule in the city, which lasted until 1240. After a short period under an Imperial vicar, Ravenna was returned to the <a href="/wiki/Papal_States" title="Papal States">Papal States</a> in 1248 and again to the Traversari until, in 1275, the <a href="/wiki/Da_Polenta" class="mw-redirect" title="Da Polenta">Da Polenta</a> established their long-lasting seigniory. One of the most illustrious residents of Ravenna at this time was the exiled poet <a href="/wiki/Dante" class="mw-redirect" title="Dante">Dante</a>. The last of the Da Polenta, <a href="/wiki/Ostasio_III_da_Polenta" title="Ostasio III da Polenta">Ostasio III</a>, was ousted by the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Republic of Venice</a> in 1440, and the city was annexed to the Venetian territories in the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Cremona_(1441)" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaty of Cremona (1441)">Treaty of Cremona</a>.
</p><p>Ravenna was ruled by Venice until 1509, when the area was invaded in the course of the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Wars" title="Italian Wars">Italian Wars</a>. In 1512, during the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_League_of_Cambrai" title="War of the League of Cambrai">Holy League</a> wars, Ravenna was sacked by the French following the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Ravenna_(1512)" title="Battle of Ravenna (1512)">Battle of Ravenna</a>. Ravenna was also known during the Renaissance as the birthplace of the <a href="/wiki/Monster_of_Ravenna" title="Monster of Ravenna">Monster of Ravenna</a>.
</p><p>After the Venetian withdrawal, Ravenna was again ruled by legates of the Pope as part of the Papal States. The city was damaged in a tremendous flood in May 1636. Over the next 300 years, a network of <a href="/wiki/Canals" class="mw-redirect" title="Canals">canals</a> diverted nearby rivers and drained nearby swamps, thus reducing the possibility of flooding and creating a large belt of agricultural land around the city.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_age">Modern age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ravenna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Modern age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Apart from another short occupation by Venice (1527–1529), Ravenna was part of the Papal States until 1796, when it was annexed to the French puppet state of the <a href="/wiki/Cisalpine_Republic" title="Cisalpine Republic">Cisalpine Republic</a>, (<a href="/wiki/Italian_Republic_(Napoleonic)" title="Italian Republic (Napoleonic)">Italian Republic</a> from 1802, and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy_(Napoleonic)" title="Kingdom of Italy (Napoleonic)">Kingdom of Italy</a> from 1805). It was returned to the Papal States in 1814. Occupied by Piedmontese troops in 1859, Ravenna and the surrounding <a href="/wiki/Romagna" title="Romagna">Romagna</a> area became part of the new unified <a href="/wiki/Unification_of_Italy" class="mw-redirect" title="Unification of Italy">Kingdom of Italy</a> in 1861. During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, troops of the British <a href="/wiki/27th_Lancers" title="27th Lancers">27th Lancers</a> entered and occupied Ravenna on 5 December 1944. The town suffered very little damage.
</p>

<p>Eight early Christian monuments of Ravenna are inscribed on the <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_List" class="mw-redirect" title="World Heritage List">World Heritage List</a>. These are
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Baptistry_of_Neon" class="mw-redirect" title="Baptistry of Neon">Orthodox Baptistry</a> also called <i>Baptistry of Neon</i> (c. 430)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mausoleum_of_Galla_Placidia" title="Mausoleum of Galla Placidia">Mausoleum of Galla Placidia</a> (c. 430)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Arian_Baptistry" class="mw-redirect" title="Arian Baptistry">Arian Baptistry</a> (c. 500)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Archbishop%27s_Chapel,_Ravenna" title="Archbishop&#39;s Chapel, Ravenna">Archiepiscopal Chapel</a> (c. 500)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_Sant%27Apollinare_Nuovo" title="Basilica of Sant&#39;Apollinare Nuovo">Basilica of Sant'Apollinare Nuovo</a> (c. 500)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mausoleum_of_Theodoric" title="Mausoleum of Theodoric">Mausoleum of Theodoric</a> (520)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_San_Vitale" title="Basilica of San Vitale">Basilica of San Vitale</a> (548)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_Sant%27Apollinare_in_Classe" title="Basilica of Sant&#39;Apollinare in Classe">Basilica of Sant'Apollinare in Classe</a> (549)</li></ul>
<p>Other attractions include:
</p>
<ul><li>The church of <a href="/wiki/San_Giovanni_Evangelista,_Ravenna" title="San Giovanni Evangelista, Ravenna">St. John the Evangelist</a> is from the 5th century, erected by <a href="/wiki/Galla_Placidia" title="Galla Placidia">Galla Placidia</a> after she survived a storm at sea. It was restored after the World War II bombings. The belltower contains four bells, the two majors dating back to 1208.</li>
<li>The 6th-century church of the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Church_of_the_Spirito_Santo,_Ravenna&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Church of the Spirito Santo, Ravenna (page does not exist)">Spirito Santo</a></i>, which has been quite drastically altered since the 6th century. It was originally the Arian cathedral. The façade has a 16th-century portico with five arcades.</li>
<li>The <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Francesco,_Ravenna&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="San Francesco, Ravenna (page does not exist)">St. Francis basilica</a>, rebuilt in the 10th–11th centuries over a precedent edifice dedicated to the Apostles and later to St. Peter. Behind the humble brick façade, it has a nave and two aisles. Fragments of mosaics from the first church are visible on the floor, which is usually covered by water after heavy rains (together with the crypt). Here the funeral ceremony of <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante Alighieri</a> was held in 1321. The poet is buried in a tomb annexed to the church, the local authorities having resisted for centuries all demands by Florence for return of the remains of its most famous exile.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> church of <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_Maggiore,_Ravenna&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Santa Maria Maggiore, Ravenna (page does not exist)">Santa Maria Maggiore</a></i> (525–532, rebuilt in 1671). It houses a picture by <a href="/wiki/Luca_Longhi" title="Luca Longhi">Luca Longhi</a>.</li>
<li>The church of <i><a href="/wiki/San_Giovanni_Battista,_Ravenna" title="San Giovanni Battista, Ravenna">San Giovanni Battista</a></i> (1683), also in Baroque style, with a Middle Ages campanile.</li>
<li>The basilica of <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_in_Porto,_Ravenna&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Santa Maria in Porto, Ravenna (page does not exist)">Santa Maria in Porto</a></i> (16th century), with a rich façade from the 18th century. It has a nave and two aisles, with a high <a href="/wiki/Cupola" title="Cupola">cupola</a>. It houses the image of famous <i>Greek Madonna</i>, which was allegedly brought to Ravenna from Constantinople.</li>
<li>The nearby <a href="/w/index.php?title=Communal_Gallery,_Ravenna&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Communal Gallery, Ravenna (page does not exist)">Communal Gallery</a> has various works from Romagnoli painters.</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rocca_Brancaleone&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rocca Brancaleone (page does not exist)">Rocca Brancaleone</a></i> ("Brancaleone Castle"), built by the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Venetians</a> in 1457. Once part of the city walls, it is now a public park. It is divided into two parts: the true Castle and the Citadel, the latter having an extent of 14,000&#160;m<sup>2</sup> (150,694.75&#160;sq&#160;ft).</li>
<li>The "so-called Palace of Theodoric", in fact the entrance to the former church of <a href="/wiki/Holy_Saviour" class="mw-redirect" title="Holy Saviour">San Salvatore</a>. It includes mosaics from the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Theodoric" title="Palace of Theodoric">true palace</a> of the Ostrogoth king.</li>
<li>The church of <i><a href="/wiki/Sant%27Eufemia,_Ravenna" title="Sant&#39;Eufemia, Ravenna">Sant'Eufemia</a></i> (18th century), gives access to the so-called <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stone_Carpets_Domus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Stone Carpets Domus (page does not exist)">Stone Carpets Domus</a> (6th–7th century): this houses splendid mosaics from a Byzantine palace.</li>
<li>The <a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Museum,_Ravenna&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="National Museum, Ravenna (page does not exist)">National Museum</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Archiepiscopal_Museum,_Ravenna" title="Archiepiscopal Museum, Ravenna">Archiepiscopal Museum</a></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ravenna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The city annually hosts the <a href="/wiki/Ravenna_Festival" title="Ravenna Festival">Ravenna Festival</a>, one of Italy's prominent classical music gatherings. Opera performances are held at the <a href="/wiki/Teatro_Comunale_Alighieri" title="Teatro Comunale Alighieri">Teatro Alighieri</a> while concerts take place at the Palazzo Mauro de André as well as in the ancient <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_San_Vitale" title="Basilica of San Vitale">Basilica of San Vitale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_Sant%27Apollinare_in_Classe" title="Basilica of Sant&#39;Apollinare in Classe">Basilica of Sant'Apollinare in Classe</a>. Chicago Symphony Orchestra music director <a href="/wiki/Riccardo_Muti" title="Riccardo Muti">Riccardo Muti</a>, a longtime resident of the city, regularly participates in the festival, which invites orchestras and other performers from around the world.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ravenna_in_literature">Ravenna in literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ravenna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ravenna in literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl><dt>Pre-1800</dt></dl>
<ul><li>The city is mentioned in Canto V in <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Divina_Commedia" class="mw-redirect" title="Divina Commedia">Inferno</a></i>.</li>
<li>Also in the 16th century, <a href="/wiki/Nostradamus" title="Nostradamus">Nostradamus</a> provides four prophecies:
<ul><li>"The Magnavacca (canal) at Ravenna in great trouble, Canals by fifteen shut up at Fornase", in reference to fifteen French saboteurs.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>As the place of a battle extending to <a href="/wiki/Perugia" title="Perugia">Perugia</a> and a sacred escape in its aftermath, leaving rotting horses left to eat</li>
<li>In relation to the snatching of a lady "near Ravenna" and then the <a href="/wiki/Piedmont" title="Piedmont">legate of Lisbon</a> seizing 70 souls at sea</li>
<li>Ravenna is one of three-similarly named contenders for the birth of the third and final <a href="/wiki/Antichrist" title="Antichrist">Antichrist</a> who enslaves <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Ravne_na_Koro%C5%A1kem" title="Ravne na Koroškem">Ravne na Koroškem</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></li></ul></li>
<li>Ravenna is the setting for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Witch_(play)" title="The Witch (play)">The Witch</a></i>, a play by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Middleton" title="Thomas Middleton">Thomas Middleton</a> (1580–1627)</li></ul>
<dl><dt>Post-1800</dt></dl>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Lord_Byron" title="Lord Byron">Lord Byron</a> lived in Ravenna between 1819 and 1821, led by the love for a local aristocratic and married young woman, <a href="/wiki/Teresa,_Contessa_Guiccioli" title="Teresa, Contessa Guiccioli">Teresa Guiccioli</a>. Here he continued <i><a href="/wiki/Don_Juan_(Byron)" class="mw-redirect" title="Don Juan (Byron)">Don Juan</a></i> and wrote:
<ul><li><i>Ravenna Diary</i>, <i>My Dictionary</i> and <i>Recollections</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Oscar_Wilde" title="Oscar Wilde">Oscar Wilde</a> (1854–1900) wrote a poem <i>Ravenna</i> in 1878.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Symbolist, lyrical poet <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Blok" title="Alexander Blok">Alexander Blok</a> (1880–1921) wrote a poem entitled <i>Ravenna</i> (May–June 1909) inspired by his Italian journey (spring 1909).</li>
<li>During his travels, German poet and philosopher <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Hesse" title="Hermann Hesse">Hermann Hesse</a> (1877–1962) came across Ravenna and was inspired to write two poems of the city. They are entitled <i>Ravenna (1)</i> and <i>Ravenna (2)</i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/T._S._Eliot" title="T. S. Eliot">T. S. Eliot</a>'s (1888–1965) poem "Lune de Miel" (written in French) describes a honeymooning couple from Indiana sleeping not far from the ancient <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_Sant%27_Apollinare_in_Classe" class="mw-redirect" title="Basilica of Sant&#39; Apollinare in Classe">Basilica of Sant' Apollinare in Classe</a> (just outside Ravenna), famous for the carved capitals of its columns, which depict <a href="/wiki/Acanthus_(ornament)" title="Acanthus (ornament)">acanthus</a> leaves buffeted by the wind, unlike the leaves in repose on similar columns elsewhere.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/J.R.R._Tolkien" class="mw-redirect" title="J.R.R. Tolkien">J.R.R. Tolkien</a> (1892-1973) may have based his city of <a href="/wiki/Minas_Tirith" title="Minas Tirith">Minas Tirith</a> at least in part on Ravenna.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ravenna_in_film">Ravenna in film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ravenna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ravenna in film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Michelangelo_Antonioni" title="Michelangelo Antonioni">Michelangelo Antonioni</a> filmed his 1964 movie <a href="/wiki/Red_Desert_(film)" title="Red Desert (film)"><i>Red Desert</i></a> (<i>Deserto Rosso</i>) within the industrialised areas of the Pialassa valley within the city limits.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ravenna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ravenna has an important commercial and tourist <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Ravenna" title="Port of Ravenna">port</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ravenna_railway_station" title="Ravenna railway station">Ravenna railway station</a> has direct <a href="/wiki/Trenitalia" title="Trenitalia">Trenitalia</a> service to <a href="/wiki/Bologna" title="Bologna">Bologna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ferrara" title="Ferrara">Ferrara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lecce" title="Lecce">Lecce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parma" title="Parma">Parma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rimini" title="Rimini">Rimini</a>,  and <a href="/wiki/Verona" title="Verona">Verona</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ravenna_Airport" title="Ravenna Airport">Ravenna Airport</a> is located in Ravenna. The nearest commercial airports are those of <a href="/wiki/Forl%C3%AC_Airport" title="Forlì Airport">Forlì</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rimini_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Rimini Airport">Rimini</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bologna_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Bologna Airport">Bologna</a>.
</p><p>Freeways crossing Ravenna include: <a href="/wiki/Autostrada_A14_(Italy)" title="Autostrada A14 (Italy)">A14-bis</a> from the hub of Bologna; on the north-south axis of <a href="/wiki/European_route_E45" title="European route E45">EU routes E45</a> (from Rome) and <a href="/wiki/European_route_E55" title="European route E55">E55</a> (SS-309 "Romea" from Venice); and on the regional Ferrara-Rimini axis of SS-16 (partially called "Adriatica").
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ravenna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The historical <a href="/wiki/Football_in_Italy" title="Football in Italy">Italian football</a> of the city is <a href="/wiki/Ravenna_F.C." title="Ravenna F.C.">Ravenna F.C.</a> Currently it plays in the third league of Italian football, commonly known as "Serie C".
</p><p><a href="/wiki/A.P.D._Ribelle_1927" title="A.P.D. Ribelle 1927">A.P.D. Ribelle 1927</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Football_in_Italy" title="Football in Italy">Italian football</a> of <a href="/wiki/Castiglione_di_Ravenna" class="mw-redirect" title="Castiglione di Ravenna">Castiglione di Ravenna</a>, a fraction of Ravenna and was founded in 1927. Currently it plays in Italy's <a href="/wiki/Serie_D" title="Serie D">Serie D</a> after promotion from Eccellenza Emilia-Romagna Girone B in the 2013-14 season.
</p><p>The president is Marcello Missiroli and the manager is Enrico Zaccaroni.
</p><p>Its home ground is <i>Stadio Massimo Sbrighi</i> of the fraction with 1,000 seats. The team's colors are white and blue.
</p><p>The beaches of Ravenna hosted the <a href="/wiki/2011_FIFA_Beach_Soccer_World_Cup" title="2011 FIFA Beach Soccer World Cup">2011 FIFA Beach Soccer World Cup</a>, in September 2011.
</p>
