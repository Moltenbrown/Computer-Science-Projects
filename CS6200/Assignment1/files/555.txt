	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Progenitor</h1>

<div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Founder of a family lineage; often legendary</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Genealogy" title="Genealogy">genealogy</a>, the <b>progenitor</b> (rarer: <b>primogenitor</b>; <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">Stammvater</i> or <i>Ahnherr</i>) is the – sometimes <a href="/wiki/Legendary_progenitor" title="Legendary progenitor">legendary</a> – founder of a <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">family</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">line of descent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clan" title="Clan">clan</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tribe" title="Tribe">tribe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">noble house</a> or <a href="/wiki/People_group" class="mw-redirect" title="People group">people group</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Duden-Stammvater_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duden-Stammvater-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Genealogy" title="Genealogy">Genealogy</a> (commonly known as family history) understands a progenitor to be the earliest <a href="/wiki/Chronicle" title="Chronicle">recorded</a> <a href="/wiki/Ancestor" title="Ancestor">ancestor</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Consanguineous" class="mw-redirect" title="Consanguineous">consanguineous</a> family group of <a href="/wiki/Lineal_descendant" title="Lineal descendant">descendants</a>. 
</p><p>Progenitors are sometimes used to describe the status of a genealogical research project, or in order to compare the availability of genealogical data in different times and places. Often, progenitors are implied to be <a href="/wiki/Patrilineality" title="Patrilineality">patrilineal</a>. If a patrilineal <a href="/wiki/Dynasty" title="Dynasty">dynasty</a> is considered, each such dynasty has exactly one progenitor.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">Aristocratic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dynasty" title="Dynasty">dynastic families</a> often look back to an ancestor who is seen as the founder and progenitor of their house (i.e. family line). Even the old <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">Roman legal concept</a> of <a href="/wiki/Agnate" class="mw-redirect" title="Agnate">agnates</a> (<a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "descendants") was based on the idea of the unbroken family line of a progenitor, but only includes male members of the family, whilst the women were referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Cognatic" class="mw-redirect" title="Cognatic">cognatic</a>".
</p><p>It is rarely possible to confirm <a href="/wiki/Biological_parenthood" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological parenthood">biological parenthood</a>, however, (see <a href="/wiki/Bastardy" class="mw-redirect" title="Bastardy">bastardy</a>) in the case of ancient family lines. In addition, the progenitor is often a distant ancestor, only known as a result of <a href="/wiki/Oral_tradition" title="Oral tradition">oral tradition</a>. Where people groups and communities rely solely on a <a href="/wiki/Patrilinear" class="mw-redirect" title="Patrilinear">patrilinear</a> family line, their common ancestor often became the subject of a legend surrounding the origin of the family. By contrast, families and peoples with a <a href="/wiki/Matrilinear" class="mw-redirect" title="Matrilinear">matrilinear</a> history trace themselves back to an original female <b>progenitrix</b>. Matrilinear <a href="/wiki/Rules_of_descent" class="mw-redirect" title="Rules of descent">rules of descent</a> are found in about 200 of the 1300 known <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Indigenous people">indigenous peoples</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_group" title="Ethnic group">ethnic groups</a> worldwide, whilst around 600 have patrilineal rules of descent (from father to son).<sup id="cite_ref-Gray_1998_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gray_1998-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Mythology" class="mw-redirect" title="Mythology">mythological beliefs</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Romans</a> the god of war, <a href="/wiki/Mars_(mythology)" title="Mars (mythology)">Mars</a>, was viewed as the progenitor of the Romans;<sup id="cite_ref-wissen-Mars_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wissen-Mars-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> which is why the <a href="/wiki/Mars_symbol" class="mw-redirect" title="Mars symbol">Mars symbol</a> (<a href="/wiki/%E2%99%82" class="mw-redirect" title="♂">♂</a>, a shield and spear), is used to refer to the <a href="/wiki/Masculinity" title="Masculinity">male sex</a>. Besides cities and countries, <a href="/wiki/People_groups" class="mw-redirect" title="People groups">people groups</a> may also have a progenitor (often a god) in their mythologies, for example, the <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic" class="mw-redirect" title="Hellenistic">Hellenistic</a> <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greeks</a> look back to <a href="/wiki/Hellen" title="Hellen">Hellen</a> as their progenitor. In Indian  <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a> <i>Manu</i> is the progenitor of all mankind. In the <a href="/wiki/Abrahamic_religions" title="Abrahamic religions">Abrahamic religions</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Adam" title="Adam">Adam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Noah" title="Noah">Noah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abraham" title="Abraham">Abraham</a> and others are described as progenitors (see also <a href="/wiki/Biblical_patriarchy" title="Biblical patriarchy">Biblical patriarchy</a>).
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Archaeogenetics" title="Archaeogenetics">archaeogenetics</a> (archaeological genetics), a human <a href="/wiki/Y-chromosomal_Adam" title="Y-chromosomal Adam">Y-chromosomal Adam</a> has been named as the <a href="/wiki/Most_recent_common_ancestor" title="Most recent common ancestor">most recent common ancestor</a> from whom all currently living people are descended patrilinearly. This Adam lived in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> at a time variously estimated from 60,000 to 338,000 years ago.
</p>
