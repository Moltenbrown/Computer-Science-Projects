<p><b>Hawaiian kinship</b>, also referred to as the <b>generational system</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Kinship_and_descent" class="mw-redirect" title="Kinship and descent">kinship</a> system used to define <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">family</a>. Identified by <a href="/wiki/Lewis_H._Morgan" title="Lewis H. Morgan">Lewis H. Morgan</a> in his 1871 work <i>Systems of Consanguinity and Affinity of the Human Family</i>, the Hawaiian system is one of the six major kinship systems (<a href="/wiki/Eskimo_kinship" title="Eskimo kinship">Eskimo</a>, Hawaiian, <a href="/wiki/Iroquois_kinship" title="Iroquois kinship">Iroquois</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crow_kinship" title="Crow kinship">Crow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Omaha_kinship" title="Omaha kinship">Omaha</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sudanese_kinship" title="Sudanese kinship">Sudanese</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kinship_system">Kinship system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawaiian_kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Kinship system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a href="/wiki/File:Hawaiian-kinship-chart.svg" class="image" title="Graphic of the Hawaiian kinship system"><img alt="Graphic of the Hawaiian kinship system" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Hawaiian-kinship-chart.svg/700px-Hawaiian-kinship-chart.svg.png" decoding="async" width="700" height="217" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Hawaiian-kinship-chart.svg/1050px-Hawaiian-kinship-chart.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Hawaiian-kinship-chart.svg/1400px-Hawaiian-kinship-chart.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="920" data-file-height="285" /></a></div></div>
<p>Within common typologies, the Hawaiian system is the simplest classificatory system of <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">kinship</a>. In it, differences are distinguished by generation and by gender. There is a parental generation and a generation of children.  In this system, a person (called <i>Ego</i> in anthropology) refers to all females of his parents' generation as "Mother" and all of the males as "Father".  In the generation of children, all brothers and male cousins are referred to as "Brother", all sisters and female cousins as "Sister".
</p><p>The Hawaiian system is usually associated with <a href="/wiki/Ambilineality" title="Ambilineality">ambilineal descent</a> groups. It is found in approximately one third of the world's societies, although these are usually small societies.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawaiian_kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Hawaiian system is named for the pre-contact kinship system of <a href="/wiki/Native_Hawaiians" title="Native Hawaiians">Native Hawaiian</a> people in the <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaiian Islands</a>. Today the Hawaiian system is most common among <a href="/wiki/Malayo-Polynesian_languages" title="Malayo-Polynesian languages">Malayo-Polynesian-speaking</a> cultures; the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_language" title="Hawaiian language">Hawaiian language</a> itself is Malayo-Polynesian.
</p><p>This form of kinship is frequent in societies with ambilineal descent groups, where economic production and child-rearing are shared.
</p>
