<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ethnonym" title="Ethnonym">ethnonym</a> "<b>Adyghe</b>" (<a href="/wiki/Adyghe_language" title="Adyghe language">Adyghe</a>: <span lang="ady">Адыгэ/Adygè</span>, <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">Ады́ги</span>) is used as an <a href="/wiki/Endonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Endonym">endonym</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Caucasian_languages" title="Northwest Caucasian languages">Caucasian</a>-speaking <a href="/wiki/Circassians" title="Circassians">Circassians</a> of the <a href="/wiki/North_Caucasus" title="North Caucasus">North Caucasus</a> and as a <a href="/wiki/Demonym" title="Demonym">demonym</a> for the inhabitants of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Adygea" class="mw-redirect" title="Republic of Adygea">Republic of Adygea</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Federal_subjects_of_Russia" title="Federal subjects of Russia">federal subject</a> of <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> located in the southwestern part of <a href="/wiki/European_Russia" title="European Russia">European Russia</a>, enclaved within <a href="/wiki/Krasnodar_Krai" title="Krasnodar Krai">Krasnodar Krai</a>, where it is also rendered as <b>Adygeans</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">Адыгейцы</span>).<sup id="ref_a_1" class="reference"><a href="#cnote_a">[a]</a></sup> The Adygeans (of Adygea) speak the <a href="/wiki/Adyghe_language" title="Adyghe language">Adyghe language</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethnology">Ethnology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adyghe_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ethnology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
 <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Circassians" title="Circassians">Circassians</a></div>
<p>The Adyghe people are one of the Circassian peoples, along with the <a href="/wiki/Cherkess" title="Cherkess">Cherkess</a> (of <a href="/wiki/Karachay-Cherkessia" title="Karachay-Cherkessia">Karachay-Cherkessia</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Kabards" class="mw-redirect" title="Kabards">Kabards</a> (of <a href="/wiki/Kabardino-Balkaria" title="Kabardino-Balkaria">Kabardino-Balkaria</a>), from whom they are geographically separated by the Slav-inhabited <a href="/wiki/Laba_River" title="Laba River">Laba</a> region.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMinahan200236_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMinahan200236-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The languages of the Adygheans, Cherkess and Kabards are <a href="/wiki/Mutually_intelligible" class="mw-redirect" title="Mutually intelligible">mutually intelligible</a>, however, there is a consensus that these are typologically distinct languages.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The standard (literary) Adyghe language is based on the dialect of the Temirgoy tribe.<sup id="cite_ref-FortescueMithun2017_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FortescueMithun2017-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Adygheans, known as "western Circassians" per Soviet terminology from the 1930s, use a written language separated by Soviet policy from those of the <a href="/wiki/Cherkess" title="Cherkess">Cherkess</a> ("central Circassians") and <a href="/wiki/Kabards" class="mw-redirect" title="Kabards">Kabards</a> ("eastern Circassians"), despite the possibility to have a unified one. The <a href="/wiki/Adyghe-Cherkess_Autonomous_Oblast" class="mw-redirect" title="Adyghe-Cherkess Autonomous Oblast">Adyghe-Cherkess Autonomous Oblast</a> was established in July 1922, "Cherkess" being dropped from the name in August 1936.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOlsonPappasPappas1994152_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOlsonPappasPappas1994152-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Circassian peoples are <a href="/wiki/Circassian_tribes" class="mw-redirect" title="Circassian tribes">divided into tribes</a> or clans (<i>tlapq</i>). There are ten Adyghe tribes (or subgroups),<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOlsonPappasPappas199415_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOlsonPappasPappas199415-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMinahan200237_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMinahan200237-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> out of which four are identified<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (April 2018)">by whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> as speaking mutually intelligible Adyghe dialects: Abzakh, Bzhedug, Temirgoy and Shapsug.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOlsonPappasPappas199415_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOlsonPappasPappas199415-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FortescueMithun2017_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FortescueMithun2017-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
The Shapsug were counted as a separate ethnic group in the censuses.<sup id="cite_ref-2010census_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2010census-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population">Population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adyghe_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
 <div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Adyghe_in_Adygea_(2002).png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Adyghe_in_Adygea_%282002%29.png/300px-Adyghe_in_Adygea_%282002%29.png" decoding="async" width="300" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Adyghe_in_Adygea_%282002%29.png/450px-Adyghe_in_Adygea_%282002%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Adyghe_in_Adygea_%282002%29.png/600px-Adyghe_in_Adygea_%282002%29.png 2x" data-file-width="1940" data-file-height="1896" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Adyghe_in_Adygea_(2002).png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Distribution of Adygeans in Adygea, Russia (2002 census)</div></div></div>
<p>According to the 2010 census of Russia, the number of self-declared ethnic "Adyghe" is 124,835 (0.09%). The community included 107,048 in Adygea itself (ca. 25% of the republic's population), 13,834 in Krasnodar Krai, 569 in <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2010census_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2010census-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The majority of the population in Adygea declare as Russians (63.6%). In 2002 it was estimated that the community numbered 131,000 in all of Russia.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMinahan200236_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMinahan200236-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The other closely related groups, Kabards and Cherkess, numbered 516,826 (0.38%) and 73,184 (0.05%), respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-2010census_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2010census-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Those that declared as Shapsug, who speak an Adyghe dialect and are regarded an Adyghe subgroup, numbered 3,882.<sup id="cite_ref-2010census_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2010census-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There is a significant Adyghe diaspora. It was estimated in 1997 that there were 71,000 Adyghe-speakers in Turkey, 44,000 in Jordan, and 25,000 in Syria.<sup id="cite_ref-Lyovin1997_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lyovin1997-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adyghe_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Circassians" class="mw-redirect" title="History of the Circassians">History of the Circassians</a></div>
<p>The political history of the Adyghe in Adygea since the Russian Revolution is complex. On 27 July 1922, a Circassian (Adygea) Autonomous Oblast was established in the <a href="/wiki/Kuban-Black_Sea_Oblast" title="Kuban-Black Sea Oblast">Kuban-Black Sea Oblast</a>, which would later become <a href="/wiki/Krasnodar_Krai" title="Krasnodar Krai">Krasnodar Krai</a>. After several name changes, the <a href="/wiki/Adyghe_Autonomous_Oblast" title="Adyghe Autonomous Oblast">Adyghe Autonomous Oblast</a> was established on 3 August 1928. On 5 October 1990, the Adygea ASSR was proclaimed and separated from Krasnodar Krai. On 24 March 1992, it became the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Adygea" class="mw-redirect" title="Republic of Adygea">Republic of Adygea</a>. A significant population of the Adyghe community now lives in the Black Sea region of Northern Turkey where their culture is preserved in villages in the area.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Annotations">Annotations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adyghe_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Annotations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div style=""><ol class="references" style="list-style-type:upper-alpha;">
<li id="cnote_a"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#ref_a_1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In Russian, Adygea's Circassian residents are called <i>Adygejtsy</i> (<span lang="ru" title="Russian language text">Адыгейцы</span>, meaning 'those [of the Republic] of Adygea'), whereas the Circassians in general are called <i>Adygi</i> (<span lang="ru" title="Russian language text">Адыги</span>, meaning 'the Adyghe'). In English terminology, on the other hand, many foreigners fail to distinguish between these Russian-specific administrative (former) and ethnolinguistic (latter) terms, and misuse the word Adyghe to refer only to the Adyghe in Republic of Adygea (only a small part of the historical Circassian lands). The majority of the Circassians in Russia today reside in neighbouring regions of Adygea.</span></li>
</ol></div>
