<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Anglicisation" title="Anglicisation">anglicisation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Personal_name" title="Personal name">personal names</a></b> is the change of non-English-language personal names to spellings nearer English sounds, or substitution of equivalent or similar English personal names in the place of non-English personal names.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anglicisation_of_personal_names">Anglicisation of personal names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglicisation_of_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Anglicisation of personal names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Classical.2C_medieval_and_Renaissance_figures"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical,_medieval_and_Renaissance_figures">Classical, medieval and Renaissance figures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglicisation_of_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classical, medieval and Renaissance figures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_English_translated_personal_names" title="List of English translated personal names">List of English translated personal names</a></div>
<p>A small number of figures, mainly very well-known classical and religious writers, appear under English names—or more typically under Latin names, in English texts. This practice became prevalent as early as in English-language translations of the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>, where translators typically renamed figures such as Yeshu and Simon bar-Jonah as <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">Peter</a>, and treated most of the other figures in the New Testament similarly. (In contrast, translations of the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a> traditionally use the original names, more or less faithfully transliterated from the original Hebrew.) Transatlantic explorers such as Zuan Caboto and Cristoforo Colombo became popularly known as <a href="/wiki/John_Cabot" title="John Cabot">John Cabot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a>; English-speakers anglicized and Latinized the name of the Polish astronomer Mikołaj Kopernik to (Nicholas) <a href="/wiki/Copernicus" class="mw-redirect" title="Copernicus">Copernicus</a>, and the English-speaking world typically knows the French-born theologian Jean Calvin as <a href="/wiki/John_Calvin" title="John Calvin">John Calvin</a>. Such Anglicizations became less usual after the sixteenth century.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-English-language_areas_of_the_British_Isles">Non-English-language areas of the British Isles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglicisation_of_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Non-English-language areas of the British Isles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Irish_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish names">Irish names</a></div>
<p>Most <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Gaelic language">Gaelic language</a> surnames of Ireland, Scotland, and the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> have been anglicised at some time. The Gaels were among the first Europeans to adopt surnames during the Dark Ages. Originally, most Gaelic surnames were composed of the given name of a child's father, preceded by Mac (<i>son</i>) or Nic (or <i>Ní</i>, both being variants of <i>nighean</i>, meaning <i>daughter</i>) depending on the sex. These surnames would not be passed down another generation, and a woman would keep her birth surname after marriage. The same was originally true of Germanic surnames which followed the pattern [father's given name]+son/daughter (this is still the case in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, as exemplified by the singer <a href="/wiki/Bj%C3%B6rk" title="Björk">Björk Guðmundsdóttir</a> and former <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Iceland" title="Prime Minister of Iceland">Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Sigmundur_Dav%C3%AD%C3%B0_Gunnlaugsson" title="Sigmundur Davíð Gunnlaugsson">Sigmundur Davíð Gunnlaugsson</a>).
</p><p>Over the centuries, under the influence of post-Medieval English practice, this type of surname has become static over generations, handed down the male lineage to all successive generations so that it is no longer indicates the given name of a holder's father any more than the suffix -son on a Germanic language surname does today. Among English-speaking peoples of Gaelic heritage, the use of <i>Nic</i> as a prefix for daughters has been replaced by <i>Mac</i>, regardless of sex (as per <a href="/wiki/Geraldine_McGowan" title="Geraldine McGowan">Geraldine McGowan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alyth_McCormack" class="mw-redirect" title="Alyth McCormack">Alyth McCormack</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sarah_McLachlan" title="Sarah McLachlan">Sarah McLachlan</a>). Wives also began to take on the surnames of their husbands.
</p><p>Another common pattern of surname was similar to that preceded by Mac/Nic, but instead was preceded by Ó or Ui, signifying a grandchild or descendant. Not all Gaelic surnames signified relationship to a forebear, however. Some signified an ancestral people or homeland, such as <i>MacDhubhghaill</i> (son of a dark-haired foreigner; referring to one type of Scandinavian), <i>MacFhionnghaill</i> (son of a fair-haired foreigner; also referring to a Scandinavian people), MacLachlainn or MacLachlainneach (son of the Scandinavian). Others indicated the town or village of a family's origin, sometimes disguised as an ancestor's name as in <a href="/wiki/%C3%93_Creachmhaoil" title="Ó Creachmhaoil">Ó Creachmhaoil</a>, which prefixes a <a href="/wiki/Toponym" class="mw-redirect" title="Toponym">toponym</a> as though it was the name of a person. As with other culturo-linguistic groups, other types of surnames were often used as well, including trade-names such as <i>MacGhobhainn</i>, <i>Mac a'Ghobhainn</i> or <i>Mac Gabhainn</i> (<i>son of the smith</i>), and physical characteristics such as hair colour.
</p><p>In Anglicising Gaelic names, the prefixes <i>Mac</i>, <i>Nic</i>, and <i>Ó</i> were frequently removed (the name <i>MacPhearais</i>, by example, was sometimes anglicised as <i>Pierce</i> or <i>Piers</i>, identically to the given name; <i>Ó Leannáin</i> and <i>Ó Lionáin</i> have both been anglicised as <i>Lennon</i>; <i>Ó Ceallaigh</i> and <i>Ó Cadhla</i> have been anglicised as <i>Kelly</i>). Where they were retained, <i>Mac</i> was often rendered <i>Mc</i>, <i>M', </i>or <i>Mag-</i> (the last is seen in renderings such as <i>Maguire</i> for <i>Mac Uidhir</i>) and <i>Ó/Ui</i> became <i>O'</i>. <i>MacGhobhainn</i>, <i>Mac a'Ghobhainn</i> and <i>Mac Gabhainn</i> (<i>son of the smith</i>) were anglicised as <i>McGowan</i>, <i>Gowan</i>, <i>McGavin</i>, and <i>Gavin</i>. In surnames which had been prefixed <i>Mac</i>, the final hard <i>c</i> sound remained when the <i>Mac</i> was removed. As Gaelic spelling rules required the first letter of a name preceded by Mac or Nic to be lenited (unless it was an l, n, or r) with the addition of an h after (silencing it, or changing its sound), and for the last vowel to be slender (i or e) if male, the Anglicised form of a Gaelic name could look quite different. By example, <i>MacPhearais</i> (<i>Mac+Pearas</i>=<i>son of Pierce</i>) has been anglicised as <i>Corish</i>, and <i>MacInnis</i> has been anglicised as <i>Guinness</i>.
</p><p>Gaelic names were also sometimes anglicised by translating the prefix <i>Mac</i> into the suffix <i>son</i>, as per the Germanic practice. <i>MacPhearais</i>, consequently, has been anglicised as <i>Pearson</i>, and <i>MacAoidh</i> (Mac+Aodh) has been anglicised as <i>Hewson</i> (it is also anglicised as <i>McHugh</i> and <i>Hughes</i>). The Gaelic <i>MacSheain</i> or <i>MacSheathain</i> (son of Seán) has similarly been anglicised <i>Johnson</i> (it has also been less thoroughly anglicised as <i>MacIain</i> and <i>MacIan</i>).
</p><p>The other main changes made in Anglicisation from Gaelic are the removal of silent letters, and respelling according to English phonetics (as <i>Ó Creachmhaoil</i> or <i>Creachmhaoil</i> became <i>Crockwell</i>, and <i>MacDhubhghaill</i> became <i>Dougal</i>). ( <a href="/wiki/%C3%93_Briain" class="mw-redirect" title="Ó Briain">Ó Briain</a> has often become O'Brien, <a href="/wiki/%C3%93_Rothl%C3%A1in" title="Ó Rothláin">Ó Rothláin</a> became Rowland, <a href="/wiki/O%27Neill_dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="O&#39;Neill dynasty">Ó Néill</a> became O'Neill and some surnames like Ó Súilleabháin may be shortened to just O'Sullivan or Sullivan. Similarly, native <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scottish</a> names were altered such as Somhairle to Sorley, Mac Gill-Eain to MacLean, and Mac Aoidh to Mackay. Many <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Welsh</a> names have also been altered, such as 'ap Hywell' to <a href="/wiki/Powell_(surname)" title="Powell (surname)">Powell</a>, or 'ap Siôn' to <a href="/wiki/Jones_(surname)" title="Jones (surname)">Jones</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Immigration_to_English-speaking_countries">Immigration to English-speaking countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglicisation_of_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Immigration to English-speaking countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Anglicisation of non-English-language names was common for immigrants, or even visitors, to English-speaking countries. An example is the German composer <a href="/wiki/Johann_Christian_Bach" title="Johann Christian Bach">Johann Christian Bach</a>, the "London Bach," who was known as "John Bach" after emigrating to England.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the time in which there were large influxes of immigrants from <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> to the United States and United Kingdom during the 19th and 20th centuries, the given names and surnames of many immigrants were changed. This became known colloquially as the "<a href="/wiki/Ellis_Island_Special" title="Ellis Island Special">Ellis Island Special</a>," after the U.S. immigrant processing center on <a href="/wiki/Ellis_Island" title="Ellis Island">Ellis Island</a>; contrary to popular myth, no names were ever legally changed at Ellis Island, and immigrants almost always changed them at their own discretion.
</p><p>Traditionally common Christian given names could be substituted: such as James for the etymologically connected Jacques. Alternatively phonetical similarities, such as Joe for Giò (Giovanni or Giorgio); or abbreviation, Harry for Harilaos.
</p><p>The anglicisation of a personal name now usually depends on the preferences of the bearer. Name changes are less common today for Europeans emigrating to the United States than they are for people originating in, or descending from those who emigrated from, <a href="/wiki/East_Asian" class="mw-redirect" title="East Asian">East Asian</a> countries.
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglicisation_of_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Coastal_Salish">Coastal Salish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglicisation_of_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Coastal Salish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p><a href="/wiki/Coastal_Salish" class="mw-redirect" title="Coastal Salish">Coastal Salish</a> people were often given "Boston Names" by early European settlers.  These English names often had similar sounds to original <a href="/wiki/Lushootseed" title="Lushootseed">Lushootseed</a> names.<sup id="cite_ref-HistoryLink-Comeford_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HistoryLink-Comeford-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When <a href="/wiki/Lushootseed" title="Lushootseed">Lushootseed</a> names were integrated into English, they were often recorded and pronounced very differently.  An example of this is <a href="/wiki/Chief_Seattle" title="Chief Seattle">Chief Seattle</a>. The name Seattle is an Anglicisation of the modern Duwamish conventional spelling <b>Si'ahl</b>, equivalent to the modern <a href="/wiki/Lushootseed_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Lushootseed language">Lushootseed</a> spelling <i>siʔaɫ</i> <small>IPA:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[ˈsiʔaːɬ]</a></span>. He is also known as <b>Sealth</b>, <b>Seathle</b>, <b>Seathl</b>, or <b>See-ahth</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
