<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Etymological_Relationships_Tree.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/9c/Etymological_Relationships_Tree.png/220px-Etymological_Relationships_Tree.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="156" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/9c/Etymological_Relationships_Tree.png/330px-Etymological_Relationships_Tree.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/9c/Etymological_Relationships_Tree.png/440px-Etymological_Relationships_Tree.png 2x" data-file-width="611" data-file-height="433" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Etymological_Relationships_Tree.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diagram showing relationships between etymologically-related words</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, <b>cognates</b> are <a href="/wiki/Word" title="Word">words</a> that have a common <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymological</a> origin.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Cognates are often inherited from a <a href="/wiki/Proto-language" title="Proto-language">shared parent language</a>, but they may also involve <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">borrowings</a> from some other language. For example, the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> words <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dish#English" class="extiw" title="wikt:dish">dish</a></i> and <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/desk#English" class="extiw" title="wikt:desk">desk</a></i> and the <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> word <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Tisch#German" class="extiw" title="wikt:Tisch">Tisch</a></i> ("table") are cognates because they all come from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/discus#Latin" class="extiw" title="wikt:discus">discus</a></i>, which relates to their flat surfaces. Cognates may have <a href="/wiki/Language_change" title="Language change">evolved</a> similar, different or even opposite meanings, but in most cases there are some similar sounds or letters in the words, in some cases appearing to be dissimilar. Some words sound similar, but do not come from the same root; these are called <a href="/wiki/False_cognate" title="False cognate">false cognates</a>, while some are truly cognate but differ in meaning; these are called <a href="/wiki/False_friend" title="False friend">false friends</a>.
</p><p>The word <i>cognate</i> derives from the Latin noun <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cognatus" class="extiw" title="wikt:cognatus">cognatus</a></i>, which means "blood relative".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cognate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cognates do not need to have the same meaning, which <a href="/wiki/Semantic_drift" class="mw-redirect" title="Semantic drift">may have changed</a> as the languages developed separately. For example <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/starve#English" class="extiw" title="wikt:starve">starve</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sterven#Dutch" class="extiw" title="wikt:sterven">sterven</a></i> or <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sterben" class="extiw" title="wikt:sterben">sterben</a></i> ("to die") all derive from the same <a href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" class="mw-redirect" title="Proto-Germanic">Proto-Germanic</a> root, <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Germanic/sterban%C4%85" class="extiw" title="wikt:Appendix:Proto-Germanic/sterbaną">*sterbaną</a></i> ("die"). <i>Discus</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>δίσκος</i> (from the verb <i>δικεῖν</i> "to throw"). A later and separate English reflex of <i>discus</i>, probably through <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">medieval Latin</a> <i>desca</i>, is <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/desk#English" class="extiw" title="wikt:desk">desk</a></i> (see <a href="/wiki/OED" class="mw-redirect" title="OED">OED</a> s.v. <i>desk</i>).
</p><p>Cognates also do not need to have similar forms: English <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/father" class="extiw" title="wikt:father">father</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/p%C3%A8re#French" class="extiw" title="wikt:père">père</a></i>, and Armenian <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D5%B0%D5%A1%D5%B5%D6%80" class="extiw" title="wikt:հայր">հայր</a> (<i>hayr</i>) all descend directly from <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a> <i>*ph₂tḗr</i>. An extreme case is Armenian <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D5%A5%D6%80%D5%AF%D5%B8%D6%82" class="extiw" title="wikt:երկու">երկու</a> (<i>erku</i>) and English <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/two" class="extiw" title="wikt:two">two</a></i>, which descend from <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a> <i>*dwóh₁</i> (note that the sound change <i>*dw</i> &gt; <i>erk</i> in Armenian is regular).
</p>

Proto-Indo-European <span title="Proto-Indo-European language" class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/h%E2%82%82st%E1%B8%97r" class="extiw" title="wikt:Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/h₂stḗr">*h₂stḗr</a></span> "star".
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a> <span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">سلام</span> <i>salām</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew" class="mw-redirect" title="Hebrew">Hebrew</a> <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886049626">.mw-parser-output .script-hebrew,.mw-parser-output .script-Hebr{font-size:1.15em;font-family:"Ezra SIL","Ezra SIL SR","Keter Aram Tsova","Taamey Ashkenaz","Taamey David CLM","Taamey Frank CLM","Frank Ruehl CLM","Keter YG","Shofar","David CLM","Hadasim CLM","Simple CLM","Nachlieli","SBL BibLit","SBL Hebrew",Cardo,Alef,"Noto Serif Hebrew","Noto Sans Hebrew","David Libre",David,"Times New Roman",Gisha,Arial,FreeSerif,FreeSans}</style><span class="script-hebrew" style="font-size: 115%;" dir="rtl">שלום</span> <i>shalom</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_Neo-Aramaic" title="Assyrian Neo-Aramaic">Assyrian Neo-Aramaic</a> <i>shlama</i> and the <a href="/wiki/Amharic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Amharic language">Amharic</a> <i>selam</i> ("peace") are also cognates, derived from the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Semitic" class="mw-redirect" title="Proto-Semitic">Proto-Semitic</a> <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Semitic/%C5%A1al%C4%81m-" class="extiw" title="wikt:Appendix:Proto-Semitic/šalām-">*šalām-</a> "peace".
</p><p>Cognates may often be less easily recognised than the above examples, and authorities sometimes differ in their interpretations of the evidence. The English word <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/milk#English" class="extiw" title="wikt:milk">milk</a></i> is clearly a cognate of German <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Milch#German" class="extiw" title="wikt:Milch">Milch</a></i>, Dutch <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/melk#Dutch" class="extiw" title="wikt:melk">melk</a></i>, Russian <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%BC%D0%BE%D0%BB%D0%BE%D0%BA%D0%BE#Russian" class="extiw" title="wikt:молоко">молоко (moloko)</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Bosnian_language" title="Bosnian language">Bosnian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serbian_language" title="Serbian language">Serbian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Croatian_language" title="Croatian language">Croatian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovene_language" title="Slovene language">Slovenian</a> <i>mleko</i>, also Montenegrin <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mlijeko#Serbo-Croatian" class="extiw" title="wikt:mlijeko">mlijeko</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> On the other hand, French <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lait#French" class="extiw" title="wikt:lait">lait</a></i>, Catalan <i>llet</i>, Italian <i>latte</i>, Romanian <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lapte#Romanian" class="extiw" title="wikt:lapte">lapte</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/leche#Spanish" class="extiw" title="wikt:leche">leche</a></i> and <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/leite#Portuguese" class="extiw" title="wikt:leite">leite</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galician_language" title="Galician language">Galician</a>) (all meaning "milk") are less-obvious cognates of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Greek language">Ancient Greek</a> <i><span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B3%CE%AC%CE%BB%CE%B1%CE%BA%CF%84%CE%BF%CF%82#Ancient_Greek" class="extiw" title="wikt:γάλακτος">γάλακτος</a></span></i> <i>gálaktos</i> (genitive singular of <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B3%CE%AC%CE%BB%CE%B1#Ancient_Greek" class="extiw" title="wikt:γάλα">γάλα</a></span> <i>gála</i>, "milk"), a relationship that is more evidently seen through the intermediate Latin <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lac#Latin" class="extiw" title="wikt:lac">lac</a></i> "milk" as well as the English word <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lactic#English" class="extiw" title="wikt:lactic">lactic</a></i> and other terms borrowed from Latin. All of them come from Proto-Indo-European <span title="Proto-Indo-European language" class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/h%E2%82%82mel%C7%B5-" class="extiw" title="wikt:Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/h₂melǵ-">h₂melǵ-</a></span> "milk".
</p><p>Some cognates are semantic opposites. For instance, while the Hebrew word <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049626"/><span class="script-hebrew" style="font-size: 115%;" dir="rtl">חוצפה</span> <i><a href="/wiki/Chutzpah" title="Chutzpah">chutzpah</a></i> means "impudence", its <a href="/wiki/Classical_Arabic" title="Classical Arabic">Classical Arabic</a> cognate <span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">حصافة</span> <i>ḥaṣāfah</i> means "sound judgment."<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Another example is English <i>empathy</i> "understanding of thoughts" and Greek <i>εμπάθεια</i> "malice".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Within_the_same_language">Within the same language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cognate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Within the same language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cognates within a single language, or <i><a href="/wiki/Doublet_(linguistics)" title="Doublet (linguistics)">doublets</a></i>, may have meanings that are slightly or even totally different.  For example, English <i>ward</i> and <i>guard</i> (&lt;PIE <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/wer-" class="extiw" title="wikt:Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/wer-">*wer-</a></i>, "to perceive, watch out for") are cognates, as are <i>shirt</i> (garment on top) and <i>skirt</i> (garment on bottom) (&lt;PIE <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/sker-" class="extiw" title="wikt:Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/sker-">*sker-</a></i>, "to cut"). In some cases, including this one, one cognate ("skirt") has an ultimate source in another language related to English,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  but the other one ("shirt") is native.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  That happened with many loanwords, such as <i>skirt</i> in this example, which was borrowed from <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Norse language">Old Norse</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Danelaw" title="Danelaw">Danelaw</a>.
</p><p>Sometimes both doublets come from other languages, often the same one but at different times. For example, the word <i>chief</i> (meaning the leader of any group) comes from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_French" title="Middle French">Middle French</a> <i>chef</i> ("head"), and its modern pronunciation preserves the Middle French consonant sound; the word <i>chef</i> (the leader of the cooks) was borrowed from the same source centuries later, but by then, the consonant had changed to a "sh" sound in French. Such word sets can also be called <a href="/wiki/Etymological_twins" class="mw-redirect" title="Etymological twins">etymological twins</a>, and they may come in groups of higher numbers, as with, for example, the words <i>wain</i> (native), <i>waggon/wagon</i> (Dutch), and <i>vehicle</i> (Latin) in English.
</p><p>A word may also <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">enter another language</a>, develop a new form or meaning there, and be re-borrowed into the original language; that is called <a href="/wiki/Reborrowing" title="Reborrowing">reborrowing</a>. For example, the Greek word <i>κίνημα</i> (<i>kinima</i>, "movement") became French <i>cinéma</i> (compare American English <i>movie</i>) and then later returned to Greece as <i>σινεμά</i> (<i>sinema</i>, "the art of film", "movie theater"). In Greek, <i>κίνημα</i> (<i>kinima</i>, "movement") and <i>σινεμά</i> (<i>sinema</i>, "filmmaking, cinema") are now doublets.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A less obvious English-language doublet pair is <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/grammar" class="extiw" title="wikt:grammar">grammar</a></i> and <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/glamour" class="extiw" title="wikt:glamour">glamour</a></i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="False_cognates">False cognates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cognate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: False cognates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/False_cognates" class="mw-redirect" title="False cognates">False cognates</a></div>
<p>False cognates are words that people commonly believe are related (have a common origin), but that linguistic examination reveals are unrelated. For example, on the basis of superficial similarities, the Latin verb <i>habēre</i> and German <i>haben</i>, both meaning 'to have', appear to be cognates. However, because the words evolved from different roots, in this case, different <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European" class="mw-redirect" title="Proto-Indo-European">Proto-Indo-European</a> (PIE) roots, they cannot be cognate (see for example <a href="/wiki/Grimm%27s_law" title="Grimm&#39;s law">Grimm's law</a>). German <i>haben</i>, like English <i>have</i>, comes from PIE <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Indo-European/kh%E2%82%82py%C3%A9-" class="extiw" title="wikt:Reconstruction:Proto-Indo-European/kh₂pyé-">*kh₂pyé-</a></i> 'to grasp', and its real cognate in Latin is <i>capere</i>, 'to seize, grasp, capture'. Latin <i>habēre</i>, on the other hand, is from PIE <i>*gʰabʰ</i>, 'to give, to receive', and hence cognate with English <i>give</i> and German <i>geben</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Likewise, English <i>much</i> and Spanish <i>mucho</i> look similar and have a similar meaning but are not cognates, as they evolved from different roots: <i>much</i> from Proto-Germanic <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Germanic/mikilaz" class="extiw" title="wikt:Appendix:Proto-Germanic/mikilaz">*mikilaz</a></i> &lt; PIE <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/meg-" class="extiw" title="wikt:Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/meg-">*meǵ-</a></i> and <i>mucho</i> from Latin <i>multum</i> &lt; PIE <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Indo-European/mel-" class="extiw" title="wikt:Reconstruction:Proto-Indo-European/mel-">*mel-</a></i>. Instead, its real cognate is Spanish <i>maño</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
