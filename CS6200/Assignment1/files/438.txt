	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Immediate family</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For the 1988 film, see <a href="/wiki/Immediate_Family_(film)" title="Immediate Family (film)">Immediate Family (film)</a>. For the 1992 book, see <a href="/wiki/Immediate_Family_(book)" title="Immediate Family (book)">Immediate Family (book)</a>.</div>
<p>The <b>immediate family</b> is a defined group of relations, used in rules or laws to determine which members of a person's <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">family</a> are affected by those rules.  It normally includes a person's <a href="/wiki/Parents" class="mw-redirect" title="Parents">parents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siblings" class="mw-redirect" title="Siblings">siblings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spouses" class="mw-redirect" title="Spouses">spouses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Children" class="mw-redirect" title="Children">children</a>, or an individual related by blood whose close association is an equivalent of a family relationship. It can contain others connected by <a href="/wiki/Birth" title="Birth">birth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adoption" title="Adoption">adoption</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">marriage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Civil_partnership" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil partnership">civil partnership</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Cohabitation" title="Cohabitation">cohabitation</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Grandparents" class="mw-redirect" title="Grandparents">grandparents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great-grandparents" class="mw-redirect" title="Great-grandparents">great-grandparents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grandchildren" class="mw-redirect" title="Grandchildren">grandchildren</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great-grandchildren" class="mw-redirect" title="Great-grandchildren">great-grandchildren</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aunts" class="mw-redirect" title="Aunts">aunts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uncles" class="mw-redirect" title="Uncles">uncles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siblings-in-law" class="mw-redirect" title="Siblings-in-law">siblings-in-law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Half-siblings" class="mw-redirect" title="Half-siblings">half-siblings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cousins" class="mw-redirect" title="Cousins">cousins</a>, adopted children and step-parents/step-children, and cohabiting partners.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The term <b>close relatives</b> is used similarly.
</p><p>The concept of "immediate family" acknowledges that a person has or may feel particular responsibilities towards family members, which may make it difficult to act fairly towards non-family (hence the refusal of many companies to employ immediate family members of current employees),<sup id="cite_ref-notemploy_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-notemploy-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> or which call for special allowance to recognise this responsibility (such as compensation on death,<sup id="cite_ref-Scot_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scot-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> or permission to leave work to attend a funeral).<sup id="cite_ref-death_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-death-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It is used by <a href="/wiki/Travel_insurance" title="Travel insurance">travel insurance</a> policies to determine a set of people on the basis of whose health someone might need to cancel a journey or return early.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>   The concept is used by some countries' inheritance laws.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Immediate_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The exact meaning of "immediate family" varies, and will sometimes be defined in <a href="/wiki/Legislation" title="Legislation">legislation</a> or rules for a particular purpose. This can change over time: in 2005 the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a> proposed to change the definition for purposes of compensation payments after deaths. The definition was to be expanded from "a remaining spouse, sexual cohabitant, parent, step-parent or step-child, parent-in-law or child-in-law, or an individual related by blood whose close association is an equivalent of a family relationship who was accepted by the deceased as a child of his/her family" to include "any person who had accepted the deceased as a child of the family, the brother or sister of the deceased, any person brought up in the same household as a child and who was him/herself accepted as a child of the family, the <a href="/wiki/Same_sex_partner" class="mw-redirect" title="Same sex partner">same sex partner</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Deceased" class="mw-redirect" title="Deceased">deceased</a>, or any person who was the grandparent or grandchild of the deceased".
In <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, for purposes of subdivision of Labor Code Section 2066, "immediate family member" means spouse, domestic partner, cohabitant, child, stepchild, grandchild, parent, stepparent, mother-in-law, father-in-law, son-in-law, daughter-in-law, grandparent, great grandparent, brother, sister, half-brother, half-sister, stepsibling, brother-in-law, sister-in-law, aunt, uncle, niece, nephew, or first cousin (that is, a child of an aunt or uncle).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a> Code of State Regulations,19 CSR 15-7.021 (18) (H) states that "an immediate family member is defined as a parent; sibling; child by blood, adoption, or marriage; spouse; grandparent or grandchild."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Australian" class="mw-redirect" title="Australian">Australian</a> Fair Work Act 2009, Section 12, defines immediate family as "a spouse, de facto partner, child, parent, grandparent, grandchild or sibling of the employee; or a child, parent, grandparent or sibling of a spouse or de facto partner of the employee.", and "the definition of the term ‘de facto partner’ includes a former de facto partner."<sup id="cite_ref-Aust_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aust-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A travel <a href="/wiki/Insurance_policy" title="Insurance policy">insurance policy</a> which covers curtailment due to the death or illness of a member of the policy-holder's "immediate family" uses a wide definition but adds residential requirements: "Immediate Family is your Partner, and: parents, children, stepchildren, fostered or adopted children, brothers, sisters, aunts, uncles, cousins, nephews, nieces, grandchildren, or grandparents, of either you or your Partner, who live in your Home Country." and "Partner is your spouse or someone of either sex with whom you have a permanent relationship, and who also lives with you at your Home."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wills_and_inheritance">Wills and inheritance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Immediate_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Wills and inheritance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, there are many rules and rights surrounding immediate family members in the wake of a family death. If the will names a non-immediate family member to be the executor of the will, he or she cannot have an "official role in these matters".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Even if a person is not a joint owner, if they are an immediate family member a bank is likely to give them access to the deceased's safety deposit box.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Some states that enforce no-contest provisions do not enforce them against immediate family members.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In some states, the immediate family may receive a family allowance – a court-ordered amount paid to them from estate funds to provide help for living expenses.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> They may also entitled to homestead allowance – an amount of equity in the family home.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In many states, household goods (sentimental but not expensive goods) can be passed onto immediate family members without probate.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beneficiaries" class="mw-redirect" title="Beneficiaries">beneficiaries</a> are almost always immediate family members.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Generally,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Naming_conventions_(geographic_names)" title="Wikipedia:Naming conventions (geographic names)"><span title="The geographic scope near this tag is ambiguous. (April 2012)">where?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> the distribution to the immediate family leaves the spouse with the third or a half of the property and the issue with the balance.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Usually, the pattern was to leave the entire estate to one's immediate family or other relatives, thereby "preserving the family as a significant social unit".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The willing to other people or institutions is associated with either a wealth or an absence of an immediate family. If there is an absence of an immediate family, the testator is given the freedom to diverge from the usual distribution of property and wealth.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Workforce">Workforce</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Immediate_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Workforce">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In some cases, a <a href="/wiki/Company" title="Company">company</a> will not employ an immediate family member of a current employee.<sup id="cite_ref-notemploy_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-notemploy-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Sometimes, "authorized" absence due to the death of one's immediate family member allows an employee to still be eligible for an attendance bonus.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In these cases, the company the employee belongs to will acknowledge the significance of the absence and will, for example, be granted 3 working days off between the day of death and the day after the burial inclusive.<sup id="cite_ref-death_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-death-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Full-time workers can apply for bereavement leave, and part-time or temporary employees can apply for leave without pay.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Independence">Independence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Immediate_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Independence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rules may specify that a person may not hold a particular office if they, "or any member of their immediate family" do not meet particular criteria. For example, a director of a <a href="/wiki/NYSE" class="mw-redirect" title="NYSE">NYSE</a> company must be independent: neither they nor their family members may have been
an executive officer of the company within the past 3 years, received more than $100,000 in direct compensation from the company in the past 3 years, or been employed as an <a href="/wiki/Executive_officer" title="Executive officer">executive officer</a> of another firm within the past 3 years.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
