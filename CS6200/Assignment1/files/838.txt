<p>The <b>Baltic Finnic peoples</b>, <b>Baltic Sea Finns</b> or <b>Baltic Finns</b>, often simply referred to as the <b>Finnic peoples</b>, are <a href="/wiki/Finno-Ugric_peoples" title="Finno-Ugric peoples">Finno-Ugric peoples</a> inhabiting the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> region<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">Northern Europe</a> who speak <a href="/wiki/Finnic_languages" title="Finnic languages">Finnic languages</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Finns" title="Finns">Finns</a> proper, <a href="/wiki/Estonians" title="Estonians">Estonians</a> (including <a href="/wiki/V%C3%B5ros" title="Võros">Võros</a> and <a href="/wiki/Setos" title="Setos">Setos</a>), <a href="/wiki/Karelians" title="Karelians">Karelians</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Ludic_language" title="Ludic language">Ludes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Olonets_Karelian_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Olonets Karelian language">Olonets</a>), <a href="/wiki/Vepsians" title="Vepsians">Veps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Izhorians" title="Izhorians">Izhorians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Votes" title="Votes">Votes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Livonians" title="Livonians">Livonians</a>, as well as their descendants worldwide. In some cases the <a href="/wiki/Kvens" class="mw-redirect" title="Kvens">Kvens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ingrians" class="mw-redirect" title="Ingrians">Ingrians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tornedalians" title="Tornedalians">Tornedalians</a> and speakers of <a href="/wiki/Me%C3%A4nkieli" class="mw-redirect" title="Meänkieli">Meänkieli</a> are also included separately rather than as a part of Finns proper.
</p><p>The bulk of the Finnic peoples (more than 98%) are ethnic <a href="/wiki/Finns" title="Finns">Finns</a> and <a href="/wiki/Estonians" title="Estonians">Estonians</a>, who reside in the only two independent Finnic <a href="/wiki/Nation_states" class="mw-redirect" title="Nation states">nation states</a>—<a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Finnic peoples are also significant minority groups in neighbouring countries of <a href="/wiki/Finns_in_Sweden" class="mw-redirect" title="Finns in Sweden">Sweden</a>, Norway and Russia.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theories_of_origin">Theories of origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Finnic_peoples&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Theories of origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the Migration Theory that was based primarily on <a href="/wiki/Comparative_linguistics" title="Comparative linguistics">comparative linguistics</a>, the proto-<a href="/wiki/Finns" title="Finns">Finns</a> migrated from an ancient homeland somewhere in north-western <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> or western Russia to the shores of the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> around 1000 BC, at which time Finns and Estonians separated. The Migration Theory has been called into question since 1980 based on <a href="/wiki/Genealogy" title="Genealogy">genealogy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Craniometry" title="Craniometry">craniometry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeology</a>. Recently, a modified form of the Migration Theory has gained new support among the younger generation of linguists, who consider that archaeology, genes and craniometric data cannot supply evidence of prehistoric languages.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the last 30 years, scientific research in physical anthropology, craniometric analyses, and <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Human_Y-chromosome_DNA_haplogroup" title="Human Y-chromosome DNA haplogroup">Y-chromosomal DNA</a> frequencies have reduced the likelihood of the Migration Theory—a major westward migration as recently as 3,000 years ago. The Settlement Continuity Theory asserts that at least the genetic ancestors of the <a href="/wiki/Finno-Ugric_peoples" title="Finno-Ugric peoples">Finno-Ugric peoples</a> were among the earliest <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">indigenous peoples</a> of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FCLWI_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FCLWI-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-YC_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-YC-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The origin of the people who lived around the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> area during the <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a> Era continues to be debated by scientists. From the middle of the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> onward, there is a certain extent of agreement among scholars: it has been suggested that Finno-Ugric tribes arrived in the Baltic region from the east or south-east approximately 4000–3000 BC by merging with the original inhabitants, who then adopted the proto-Finno-Ugric language and the <a href="/wiki/Pit%E2%80%93Comb_Ware_culture" title="Pit–Comb Ware culture">Pit–Comb Ware culture</a> of the newcomers. The members of this new Finno-Ugric-speaking ethnic group are regarded as the ancestors of modern Estonians.<sup id="cite_ref-YC_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-YC-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The Y-chromosomal data has also revealed a common Finno-Ugric ancestry for the males of the neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Balts" title="Balts">Balts</a>, speakers of the Indo-European <a href="/wiki/Baltic_languages" title="Baltic languages">Baltic languages</a>. According to the studies, Baltic males are most closely related to the Finno-Ugric-speaking <a href="/wiki/Volga_Finns" title="Volga Finns">Volga Finns</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Mari_people" title="Mari people">Mari</a>, rather than to Baltic Finns.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The results suggest that the territories of Estonia, Latvia and Lithuania have been settled by <a href="/wiki/Finno-Ugric_languages" title="Finno-Ugric languages">Finno-Ugric</a>-speaking tribes since the early Mesolithic period.<sup id="cite_ref-YC_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-YC-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On the other hand, some linguists do not consider it likely that a Baltic Finnic language form could have existed at such an early date. According to these views, the Finno-Ugric languages appeared in Finland and the Baltic region only during the Early Bronze Age (ca. 1800 BC), if not later.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Finnic_oral_poetry">Finnic oral poetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Finnic_peoples&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Finnic oral poetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Finnic peoples share a common cultural heritage: the art of ancient "rune" (poem) singing in the <a href="/wiki/Kalevala_meter" class="mw-redirect" title="Kalevala meter">Kalevala meter</a>, estimated to be 2,500–3,000 years old. The Finnish and Estonian national epics, <a href="/wiki/Kalevala" title="Kalevala">Kalevala</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kalevipoeg" title="Kalevipoeg">Kalevipoeg</a>, are both written in this meter.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The Veps are the only Baltic Finnish people with no significant corpus of Kalevala meter oral poetry. The poetic tradition has included epic poems (known mostly in <a href="/wiki/Karelia" title="Karelia">Karelia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ingria" title="Ingria">Ingria</a>, perhaps as survivals from an earlier, wider distribution), lyric poems and magic chants.
</p><p>The ancient rune singing has inspired the creation of the <a href="/wiki/National_epic" title="National epic">national epic</a> of <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kalevala" title="Kalevala">Kalevala</a> compiled by <a href="/wiki/Elias_L%C3%B6nnrot" title="Elias Lönnrot">Elias Lönnrot</a>, and the music of <a href="/wiki/Arvo_P%C3%A4rt" title="Arvo Pärt">Arvo Pärt</a>, the best-known Estonian composer in the classical field.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">J. R. R. Tolkien</a> has highlighted the importance of <a href="/wiki/Kalevala" title="Kalevala">Kalevala</a> as a source for his <a href="/wiki/Tolkien%27s_legendarium" title="Tolkien&#39;s legendarium">legendarium</a>, including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Silmarillion" title="The Silmarillion">The Silmarillion</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings" title="The Lord of the Rings">The Lord of the Rings</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_Finnic_peoples">History of the Finnic peoples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Finnic_peoples&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History of the Finnic peoples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Baltic_Finns.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Baltic_Finns.jpg/200px-Baltic_Finns.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="105" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Baltic_Finns.jpg/300px-Baltic_Finns.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Baltic_Finns.jpg/400px-Baltic_Finns.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1148" data-file-height="604" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Baltic_Finns.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Finnic peoples in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Races_of_Europe_(Ripley)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Races of Europe (Ripley)">The Races of Europe</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/William_Z._Ripley" title="William Z. Ripley">William Z. Ripley</a> in 1899.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Mesolithic_Period">The Mesolithic Period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Finnic_peoples&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Mesolithic Period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The region has been populated since the end of the last glacial era, about 10,000 BC. The earliest traces of human settlement are connected with <a href="/w/index.php?title=Suomusj%C3%A4rvi_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Suomusjärvi culture (page does not exist)">Suomusjärvi culture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kunda_culture" title="Kunda culture">Kunda culture</a>. The Early <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a> <a href="/wiki/Pulli_settlement" title="Pulli settlement">Pulli settlement</a> is located by the <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A4rnu" title="Pärnu">Pärnu</a> River. It has been dated to the beginning of the 9th millennium BC. The Kunda Culture received its name from the <i>Lammasmäe</i> settlement site in northern Estonia, which dates from earlier than 8500.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Bone and stone artefacts similar to those found at Kunda have been discovered elsewhere in Estonia, as well as in <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Neolithic_Period">The Neolithic Period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Finnic_peoples&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Neolithic Period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Around 5300 <a href="/wiki/Before_Common_Era" class="mw-redirect" title="Before Common Era">BCE</a> pottery and agriculture entered Finland.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The earliest representatives belong to the <a href="/wiki/Pit%E2%80%93Comb_Ware_culture" title="Pit–Comb Ware culture">Pit–Comb Ware culture</a>, known for their distinctive decorating patterns. This marks the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a>,
</p>
<h4><span id="Pit.E2.80.93Comb_Ware_culture"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Pit–Comb_Ware_culture">Pit–Comb Ware culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Finnic_peoples&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pit–Comb Ware culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:European-middle-neolithic-en.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/European-middle-neolithic-en.svg/300px-European-middle-neolithic-en.svg.png" decoding="async" width="300" height="257" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/European-middle-neolithic-en.svg/450px-European-middle-neolithic-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/European-middle-neolithic-en.svg/600px-European-middle-neolithic-en.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1042" data-file-height="893" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:European-middle-neolithic-en.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Neolithic period</div></div></div>
<p>Until the early 1980s, the arrival of Finnic peoples, the ancestors of the Estonians, Finns, and Livonians on the shores of the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> around 3000 BC, was associated with the Pit–Comb Ware culture<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> However, such a linking of archaeologically defined cultural entities with linguistic ones cannot be proven and it has been suggested that the increase of settlement finds in the period is more likely to have been associated with an economic boom related to the warming of climate. Some researchers have even argued that a form of <a href="/wiki/Uralic_languages" title="Uralic languages">Uralic languages</a> may have been spoken in Estonia and Finland since the end of the last glaciation.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bronze_Age">Bronze Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Finnic_peoples&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Bronze Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> in Estonia is dated to approximately 1800 BC, in present-day Finland some time after 1500 BCE. The coastal regions of Finland were a part of the Nordic Bronze Culture, whereas in the inland regions the influences came from the bronze-using cultures of Northern Russia. The development of the borders between the Finnic peoples and the <a href="/wiki/Balts" title="Balts">Balts</a> was under way. The first fortified settlements, Asva and Ridala on the island of <a href="/wiki/Saaremaa" title="Saaremaa">Saaremaa</a> and Iru in the Northern Estonia, began to be built. The development of shipbuilding facilitated the spread of bronze. Changes took place in burial customs, a new type of burial ground spread from Germanic to Estonian areas, stone cist graves and cremation burials became increasingly common beside small numbers of boat-shaped stone graves.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Iron_Age">The Iron Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Finnic_peoples&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The Iron Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Archaeology_of_Northern_Europe" title="Archaeology of Northern Europe">Archaeology of Northern Europe</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pre-Roman_Iron_Age" class="mw-redirect" title="Pre-Roman Iron Age">Pre-Roman Iron Age</a> began in about 500 BC and lasted until the middle of the 1st century. The oldest iron items were imported, although since the 1st century iron was smelted from local marsh and lake ore. Settlement sites were located mostly in places that offered natural protection. Fortresses were built, although used temporarily. The appearance of square <a href="/wiki/Celtic_field" title="Celtic field">Celtic fields</a> surrounded by enclosures in Estonia date from the Pre-Roman Iron Age. The majority of stones with man-made indents, which presumably were connected with magic designed to increase crop fertility, date from this period. A new type of grave, quadrangular burial mounds, began to develop. Burial traditions show the clear beginning of social stratification.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Roman_Iron_Age" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Iron Age">Roman Iron Age</a> is roughly dated to between <a href="/wiki/AD_50" title="AD 50">AD 50</a> and 450, the era that was affected by the influence of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>. In material culture this is reflected by a few Roman coins, some <a href="/wiki/Jewellery" title="Jewellery">jewellery</a> and artefacts. The abundance of iron artefacts in Southern Estonia speaks of closer mainland ties with southern areas, while coastal Finland and the islands of western and northern Estonia communicated with their neighbours mainly by sea.
</p><p>Between 200 and 400 AD, a group of Baltic Sea Finns in southwestern Finland, who had traveled to Finland proper from the areas of nowadays Estonia, began moving inland towards Häme. This second wave is now known as the Finns proper, or Varsinais-Suomalaiset. The first wave were the Tavastians, who arrived ca 5000AD.
</p><p>Between 200 and 400 AD, the Finns proper themselves became divided geographically in three 
parts:
</p>
<ol><li>Tavastians: The tribes who went inland to Tavastia&#160;;</li>
<li>Southwestern Finns: The tribes who stayed in the Southern side of the Kokemäki river delta&#160;;</li>
<li>Southern Ostrobothnians: The tribes who stayed on the Northern side of the river delta.</li></ol>
<p>During the Migration Period in 400–600 AD, the Estonian influence gradually weakened. At the same time, the Scandinavian influence grew stronger, with Southern Ostrobothnia likely developing into a Finnish-Scandinavian dual language entity.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Northern_Europe_in_814.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Northern_Europe_in_814.jpg/300px-Northern_Europe_in_814.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="192" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Northern_Europe_in_814.jpg/450px-Northern_Europe_in_814.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Northern_Europe_in_814.jpg/600px-Northern_Europe_in_814.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1476" data-file-height="946" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Northern_Europe_in_814.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Northern Europe in 814.</div></div></div>
<p>By the end of the period, clearly defined tribal dialectical areas—Finns, <a href="/wiki/Tavastians" title="Tavastians">Tavastians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karelians" title="Karelians">Karelians</a>, Northern Estonians, Southern Estonians, and Western Estonians including the islanders—had emerged, the population of each having formed its own understanding of identity.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Middle_Ages">Early Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Finnic_peoples&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Early Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Finnic_peoples_in_chronicles">Finnic peoples in chronicles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Finnic_peoples&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Finnic peoples in chronicles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The word <i>Finn</i> is first mentioned in the form <i>fenni</i> in the 1st century AD by Roman historian <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a>. However, it is possible that he was referring to the people of northern Europe in general, particularly the Lappic or <a href="/wiki/Sami_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Sami people">Sami people</a>. After that the name <i>finni</i> is used by <a href="/wiki/Claudius_Ptolemaeus" class="mw-redirect" title="Claudius Ptolemaeus">Claudius Ptolemaeus</a> (around 150) and the Eastern Roman writer Jordanes in his <i><a href="/wiki/Getica" title="Getica">Getica</a></i> (551). References to Finnic tribes become much more numerous from the <a href="/wiki/Viking_Age" title="Viking Age">Viking Age</a> (800–1050). It was not until about 1171 that the word <i>Finni</i> was employed to mean the <a href="/wiki/Finns" title="Finns">Finns</a>.
</p><p>The term <i>Eesti</i>, the name of the Estonians, occurs first again in Tacitus; however, it might have indicated Balts. In Northern sagas (13th century), the term started to be used to indicate the Estonians.
</p><p>In a Norwegian text (11th–12th century), the name <i>Kiriali</i>, referring to Karelians, and the term <i>cornuti Finni</i>, interpreted as referring to the Lapps or Sami people, first appear.
</p><p>The opening chapter of the <a href="/wiki/Old_East_Slavic" title="Old East Slavic">Old East Slavic</a> <a href="/wiki/Primary_Chronicle" title="Primary Chronicle">Primary Chronicle</a> (early 12th century) lists the following peoples living "in the share of <a href="/wiki/Japheth" title="Japheth">Japheth</a>" among others: <a href="/wiki/Chud" title="Chud">Chud</a>, <a href="/wiki/Merya" class="mw-redirect" title="Merya">Merya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muromians" class="mw-redirect" title="Muromians">Muroma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vepsians" title="Vepsians">Ves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mordvins" title="Mordvins">Mordvin</a> (<a href="/wiki/Moksha_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Moksha people">Moksha</a> and <a href="/wiki/Erzya_language" title="Erzya language">Erzya</a>), Chud beyond the portages, <a href="/wiki/Komi_peoples" title="Komi peoples">Perm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Setos" title="Setos">Pechera</a>, Yam, Ugra and <a href="/wiki/Livonian_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Livonian people">Liv</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RPGP24_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RPGP24-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The name <i>Sum</i>, possibly meaning <i>Suomi</i> (Finland in Finnish), is found in the Primary Chronicle. The names of other Finnic tribes are also listed, including <a href="/wiki/Vepsians" title="Vepsians">Veps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mari_people" title="Mari people">Cheremis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mordvins" title="Mordvins">Mordvins</a> (<a href="/wiki/Moksha_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Moksha people">Moksha</a> and <a href="/wiki/Erzya_language" title="Erzya language">Erzya</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Komi_peoples" title="Komi peoples">Permians</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Chudes" class="mw-redirect" title="Chudes">Chudes</a>, as mentioned in the earliest East Slavic chronicles, are in a 12th-century context usually considered to be <a href="/wiki/Estonians" title="Estonians">Estonians</a>, although the name sometimes referred to all Finnic peoples in north-western <a href="/wiki/Kievan_Rus%27" title="Kievan Rus&#39;">Rus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Tvauri_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tvauri-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> According to the East Slavic <a href="/wiki/Primary_Chronicle" title="Primary Chronicle">Primary Chronicle</a>, the Chudes' lands were bounded by the Varangian Sea (Baltic Sea).<sup id="cite_ref-RPGP24_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RPGP24-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In 1030 <a href="/wiki/Yaroslav_I_the_Wise" class="mw-redirect" title="Yaroslav I the Wise">Yaroslav I the Wise</a> invaded the country of the Chudes and laid the foundations of Yuriev (the historical Russian name of <a href="/wiki/Tartu" title="Tartu">Tartu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Tvauri_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tvauri-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> They remained until 1061 when, according to chronicles, Yuryev was burned down by the Chudes. According to <a href="/wiki/Old_East_Slavic" title="Old East Slavic">Old East Slavic</a> chronicles, the Chudes were one of the founders of the <a href="/wiki/Rus%27_Khaganate" title="Rus&#39; Khaganate">Rus' state</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-PPFP141_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PPFP141-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Northern (or eastern) Chudes were also a mythical people in folklore among Northern Russians and their neighbours. In Komi mythology, the Northern Chudes represent the mythic ancestors of the <a href="/wiki/Komi_peoples" title="Komi peoples">Komi</a> people.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Finnic_peoples&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 13th century the east Baltic world was transformed by military conquest: first the Livs and Estonians, then the Finns underwent defeat, baptism, military occupation and sometimes extermination by groups of Germans, Danes and Swedes.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Finland was governed as a part of Sweden, while Estonia was under a Baltic German knightly brotherhood. Finland and Estonia became independent at the beginning of the modern age in 1917–1918 (see <a href="/wiki/History_of_Finland" title="History of Finland">history of Finland</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_Estonia" title="History of Estonia">history of Estonia</a>). The Karelians remained under Russian and then Soviet rule, and their absolute and relative numbers dwindled. When <a href="/wiki/Urbanization_by_country" title="Urbanization by country">urbanization</a> was peaking, less-numerous peoples rapidly lost capacity to maintain their village-based cultures and so were often assimilated to the mainstream society.
</p>
