<p>A <b>shepherd</b> or <b>sheepherder</b> is a person who tends, herds, feeds, or guards herds of <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">sheep</a>. <i>Shepherd</i> derives from <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>sceaphierde (</i>sceap<i> 'sheep' + </i>hierde<i> '<a href="/wiki/Herder" title="Herder">herder</a>').</i>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shepherd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shepherd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Shepherding is among the oldest occupations, beginning some 5,000 years ago in <a href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" class="mw-redirect" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a>. Sheep were kept for their <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sheep_meat" class="mw-redirect" title="Sheep meat">meat</a> and especially their <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a>. Over the next thousand years, sheep and shepherding spread throughout <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>. <a href="/wiki/Henri_Fleisch" title="Henri Fleisch">Henri Fleisch</a> tentatively suggested the <a href="/wiki/Shepherd_Neolithic" title="Shepherd Neolithic">Shepherd Neolithic</a> <a href="/wiki/Industry_(archaeology)" title="Industry (archaeology)">industry</a> of <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a> may date to the <a href="/wiki/Epipaleolithic" class="mw-redirect" title="Epipaleolithic">Epipaleolithic</a> and that it may have been used by one of the first cultures of <a href="/wiki/Nomadic_pastoralism" title="Nomadic pastoralism">nomadic shepherds</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Beqaa_Valley" title="Beqaa Valley">Beqaa Valley</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CopelandWescombe1966_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CopelandWescombe1966-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some sheep were integrated in the family farm along with other animals such as <a href="/wiki/Chicken" title="Chicken">chickens</a> and pigs. To maintain a large flock, the sheep must be able to move from pasture to pasture.  This required the development of an occupation separate from that of the farmer. The duty of shepherds was to keep their flock intact, protect it from predators and guide it to market areas in time for <a href="/wiki/Sheep_shearing" title="Sheep shearing">shearing</a>. In ancient times, shepherds also commonly milked their sheep, and made <a href="/wiki/Cheese" title="Cheese">cheese</a> from this milk; few shepherds still do this today.
</p><p>In many societies, shepherds were an important part of the economy. Unlike farmers, shepherds were often wage earners, being paid to watch the sheep of others. Shepherds also lived apart from society, being largely nomadic. It was mainly a job of solitary males without children, and new shepherds thus needed to be recruited externally.  Shepherds were most often the younger sons of farming peasants who did not inherit any land. In other societies, each family would have a family member to shepherd its flock, often a child, youth or an elder who couldn't help much with harder work; these shepherds were fully integrated in society.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Koniak%C3%B3w_miyszani_owiec_(redyk_wiosenny)_05.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Koniak%C3%B3w_miyszani_owiec_%28redyk_wiosenny%29_05.jpg/220px-Koniak%C3%B3w_miyszani_owiec_%28redyk_wiosenny%29_05.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Koniak%C3%B3w_miyszani_owiec_%28redyk_wiosenny%29_05.jpg/330px-Koniak%C3%B3w_miyszani_owiec_%28redyk_wiosenny%29_05.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Koniak%C3%B3w_miyszani_owiec_%28redyk_wiosenny%29_05.jpg/440px-Koniak%C3%B3w_miyszani_owiec_%28redyk_wiosenny%29_05.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3888" data-file-height="2592" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Koniak%C3%B3w_miyszani_owiec_(redyk_wiosenny)_05.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Shepherds at work, 2017, <a href="/wiki/Beskids" title="Beskids">Beskids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carpathian_Mountains" title="Carpathian Mountains">Carpathian Mountains</a></div></div></div>
<p>Shepherds would normally work in groups either looking after one large flock, or each bringing their own and merging their responsibilities. They would live in small cabins, often shared with their sheep, and would buy food from local communities. Less often shepherds lived in covered wagons that traveled with their flocks.
</p><p>Shepherding developed only in certain areas. In the lowlands and river valleys, it was far more efficient to grow grain and cereals than to allow sheep to graze, thus the raising of sheep was confined to rugged and mountainous areas. In pre-modern times shepherding was thus centered on regions such as the Middle East, Greece, the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Carpathian_Mountains" title="Carpathian Mountains">Carpathian Mountains</a>, Scotland and Northern England.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Shepherd%27s_crook" title="Shepherd&#39;s crook">shepherd's crook</a> is a strong multi-purpose stick or staff, often fashioned with a hooked end.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_times">Modern times</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shepherd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern times">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Russellshepherd.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Russellshepherd.jpg/170px-Russellshepherd.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="225" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Russellshepherd.jpg/255px-Russellshepherd.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Russellshepherd.jpg/340px-Russellshepherd.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4529" data-file-height="6007" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Russellshepherd.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Shepherd with his horse and dog on <a href="/wiki/Gravelly_Range" title="Gravelly Range">Gravelly Range</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madison_County,_Montana" title="Madison County, Montana">Madison County, Montana</a>, August 1942</div></div></div>
<p>In modern times, shepherding has changed dramatically. The abolition of <a href="/wiki/Common_land" title="Common land">common lands</a> in Europe in the eighteenth and nineteenth century moved shepherding from independent nomads to employees of massive estates. Some families in Africa and Asia have their wealth in sheep, so a young son is sent out to guard them while the rest of the family tend to other chores. In the US, many sheep herds are flocked over public <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Land_Management" title="Bureau of Land Management">BLM</a> lands.
</p><p>Wages are higher than was the case in the past. Keeping a shepherd in constant attendance can be costly. Also, the eradication of sheep predators in parts of the world have lessened the need for shepherds. In places like Britain, hardy breeds of sheep are frequently left alone without a shepherd for long periods of time. More productive breeds of sheep can be left in fields and moved periodically to fresh pasture when necessary. Hardier breeds of sheep can be left on hillsides. The sheep farmer will attend to the sheep when necessary at times like lambing or shearing.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cyprus">Cyprus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shepherd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cyprus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>First Shepherd's Fair was announced to take place in the Cyprus Village of Pachna, on August 31, 2014, in the printed editions of <i><a href="/wiki/Cyprus_Weekly" title="Cyprus Weekly">Cyprus Weekly</a></i> and in the Greek language daily, <i><a href="/wiki/Phileleftheros" title="Phileleftheros">Phileleftheros</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia_and_New_Zealand">Australia and New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shepherd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Australia and New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Shepherds_watch_box.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Shepherds_watch_box.JPG/220px-Shepherds_watch_box.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Shepherds_watch_box.JPG/330px-Shepherds_watch_box.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Shepherds_watch_box.JPG/440px-Shepherds_watch_box.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1944" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Shepherds_watch_box.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Shepherd's watch box, <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a></div></div></div>
<p>European exploration led to the spread of sheep around the world, and shepherding became especially important in Australia and New Zealand where there was great pastoral expansion. In Australia <a href="/wiki/Squatting_(pastoral)" class="mw-redirect" title="Squatting (pastoral)">squatters</a> spread beyond the <a href="/wiki/Nineteen_Counties" title="Nineteen Counties">Nineteen Counties</a> of New South Wales to elsewhere, taking over vast holdings called properties and now <a href="/wiki/Station_(Australian_agriculture)" title="Station (Australian agriculture)">stations</a>.
</p><p>Once driven overland to these properties, sheep were pastured in large unfenced runs. There, they required constant supervision.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Shepherds were employed to keep the sheep from straying too far, to keep the mobs as healthy as possible and to prevent attacks from <a href="/wiki/Dingo" title="Dingo">dingoes</a> and introduced predators such as feral dogs and foxes. Lambing time further increased the shepherd's responsibilities.
</p><p>Shepherding was an isolated, lonely job that was firstly given to <a href="/wiki/Convict_assignment" title="Convict assignment">assigned</a> <a href="/wiki/Convicts_in_Australia" title="Convicts in Australia">convict</a> servants. The accommodation was usually poor and the food was lacking in nutrition, leading to <a href="/wiki/Dysentery" title="Dysentery">dysentery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scurvy" title="Scurvy">scurvy</a>. When free labour was more readily available others took up this occupation. Some shepherds were additionally brought to Australia on the ships that carried sheep and were contracted to caring for them on their arrival in the colony. Sheep owners complained about the inefficiency of shepherds and the shepherds' fears of getting lost in <a href="/wiki/The_bush" title="The bush">the bush</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Typically sheep were watched by shepherds during the day, and by a hut-keeper during the night. Shepherds took the sheep out to graze before sunrise and returned them to brush-timber yards at sunset. The hut-keeper usually slept in a movable shepherd's watch box placed near the yard in order to deter attacks on the sheep. Dogs were also often chained close by to warn of any impending danger to the sheep or shepherd by dingoes or natives.
</p><p>In 1839 the usual wage for a shepherd was about AU£50 per year, plus weekly rations of 12 pounds (5.4&#160;kg) meat, 10 pounds (4.5&#160;kg) flour, 2 pounds (0.91&#160;kg) sugar and 4 ounces (110&#160;g) tea. The wage during the depression of the 1840s dropped to £20 a year.
</p><p>During the 1850s many shepherds left to try their luck on the goldfields causing acute labour shortages in the pastoral industry. This labour shortage leads to the widespread practice of fencing properties, which in turn reduced the demand for shepherds.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Over 95% of <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a> sheep were grazing in paddocks by the mid-1880s. An 1890s census of fencing in New South Wales recorded that 2.6 million kilometres of fencing had been erected there with a contemporary cost of A$3 billion. <a href="/wiki/Boundary_rider" title="Boundary rider">Boundary riders</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stockman_(Australia)" title="Stockman (Australia)">stockmen</a> replaced shepherds working on foot, who have not been employed in Australia and New Zealand since the start of the 20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shepherd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Pashupati" title="Pashupati">Pashupati</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dhangar" title="Dhangar">Dhangar</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kuruba" title="Kuruba">Kuruba</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dumuzid" title="Dumuzid">Dumuzid</a>, later known as Tammuz, was an important rural <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">deity</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Mesopotamian_religion" title="Ancient Mesopotamian religion">ancient Mesopotamian religion</a>, who was revered as the patron god of shepherds.<sup id="cite_ref-BlackGreen_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BlackGreen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Jacobsen2008_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jacobsen2008-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In his role as <i>Dumuzid sipad</i> ("Dumuzid the Shepherd"), he was believed to be the provider of milk,<sup id="cite_ref-Jacobsen2008_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jacobsen2008-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> which was a rare, seasonal commodity in ancient <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumer</a> due to the fact that it could not easily be stored without <a href="/wiki/Food_spoilage" title="Food spoilage">spoiling</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Jacobsen2008_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jacobsen2008-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Under this same title, Dumuzid was thought to have been the fifth <a href="/wiki/Antediluvian" title="Antediluvian">antediluvian</a> king of the Sumerian city-state of <a href="/wiki/Bad-tibira" title="Bad-tibira">Bad-tibira</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BlackGreen_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BlackGreen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In the Sumerian poem <i><a href="/wiki/Inanna" title="Inanna">Inanna</a> Prefers the Farmer</i>, Dumuzid competes with the farmer <a href="/wiki/Enkidu" title="Enkidu">Enkidu</a> for the affection of the goddess Inanna and ultimately wins her favor.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Ancient Near Eastern peoples associated Dumuzid with the springtime, when the land was fertile and abundant,<sup id="cite_ref-Jacobsen2008_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jacobsen2008-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ackerman_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ackerman-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> but, during the summer months, when the land was dry and barren, it was thought that Dumuzid had "died".<sup id="cite_ref-Jacobsen2008_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jacobsen2008-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ackerman_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ackerman-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Metaphorically, the term "shepherd" is used for God, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Judeo-Christian" title="Judeo-Christian">Judeo-Christian</a> tradition (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Psalm_23" title="Psalm 23">Psalm 23</a>, Ezekiel 34), and in Christianity especially for <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a>, who called himself the <a href="/wiki/Good_Shepherd" title="Good Shepherd">Good Shepherd</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The Ancient <a href="/wiki/Israelites" title="Israelites">Israelites</a> were a <a href="/wiki/Herder" title="Herder">pastoral</a> people and there were many shepherds among them. It may also be worth noting that many biblical figures were shepherds, among them the patriarchs <a href="/wiki/Abraham" title="Abraham">Abraham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jacob" title="Jacob">Jacob</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Israelites#Biblical_Israel" title="Israelites">twelve tribes</a>, the prophet <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a>, <a href="/wiki/King_David" class="mw-redirect" title="King David">King David</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a> <a href="/wiki/Prophet" title="Prophet">prophet</a> <a href="/wiki/Amos_(prophet)" title="Amos (prophet)">Amos</a>, who was a shepherd in the rugged area around <a href="/wiki/Tekoa_(Biblical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tekoa (Biblical)">Tekoa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>, angels <a href="/wiki/Annunciation_to_the_shepherds" title="Annunciation to the shepherds">announced the birth of Jesus to shepherds</a>.
</p><p>The same metaphor is also applied to <a href="/wiki/Priest" title="Priest">priests</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Sweden" title="Church of Sweden">Church of Sweden</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Lutheran" class="mw-redirect" title="Lutheran">Lutheran</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Communion" title="Anglican Communion">Anglican</a> <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishops</a> having the shepherd's <a href="/wiki/Crosier" title="Crosier">crook</a> among their insignia (see also <i><a href="/wiki/Lycidas" title="Lycidas">Lycidas</a></i>). In both cases, the implication is that the faithful are the "flock" who have to be tended. This is in part inspired by Jesus's injunctions to Peter, "Feed my sheep", which is the source of the pastoral image in <i>Lycidas</i>. The term "<a href="/wiki/Pastor" title="Pastor">Pastor</a>", originally the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word for "shepherd", is now used solely to denote the clergy of most Christian denominations.
</p><p><span class="nowrap">The Good Shepherd</span> is one of the thrusts of Biblical scripture. This illustration encompasses many ideas, including God's care for his people. The tendency of humans to put themselves into danger's way and their inability to guide and take care of themselves apart from the direct power and leading of God is also reinforced with the metaphor of sheep in need of a shepherd.
</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a>, the Prophet of <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>, every messenger of God had the occupation of being a shepherd at one point in their lives, as he himself was as a young man. Narrated by Jabir bin Abdullah: We were with <a href="/wiki/Allah" title="Allah">Allah</a>’s Apostle picking the fruits of the <a href="/wiki/Salvadora_persica" title="Salvadora persica">Arak trees</a>, and Allah’s Apostle (peace and blessings of Allah be to him) said, "Pick the black fruit, for it is the best." The companions asked, "Were you a shepherd?" He replied, "There was no prophet who was not a shepherd." (<a href="/wiki/Sahih_Bukhari" class="mw-redirect" title="Sahih Bukhari">Sahih Bukhari</a>, Chapter ‘Prophets’, Volume 4, Book 55, <a href="/wiki/Hadith" title="Hadith">Hadith</a> 618) This includes Jesus, Moses, Abraham, and all other prophets according to Islam.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sikhism" title="Sikhism">Sikhism</a> also has many mentions of shepherd tales. There are many relevant quotations, such as "We are the cattle, God almighty is our shepherd."
</p><p>This concept has also been used frequently by critics of organized religion to present an unflattering portrayal.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shepherd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Idyll" title="Idyll">idyll</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pastoralism" title="Pastoralism">pastoralism</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Eugene_Verboeckhoven,_A_Shepherdess_with_her_Flock.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Eugene_Verboeckhoven%2C_A_Shepherdess_with_her_Flock.jpg/220px-Eugene_Verboeckhoven%2C_A_Shepherdess_with_her_Flock.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="151" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Eugene_Verboeckhoven%2C_A_Shepherdess_with_her_Flock.jpg/330px-Eugene_Verboeckhoven%2C_A_Shepherdess_with_her_Flock.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Eugene_Verboeckhoven%2C_A_Shepherdess_with_her_Flock.jpg/440px-Eugene_Verboeckhoven%2C_A_Shepherdess_with_her_Flock.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="1372" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Eugene_Verboeckhoven,_A_Shepherdess_with_her_Flock.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>A Shepherdess with her Flock</i> by <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Joseph_Verboeckhoven" title="Eugène Joseph Verboeckhoven">Verboeckhoven</a></div></div></div>
<p>The shepherd, with other such figures as the <a href="/wiki/Goatherd" title="Goatherd">goatherd</a>, is the inhabitant of idealized <a href="/wiki/Arcadia_(utopia)" title="Arcadia (utopia)">Arcadia</a>, which is an idyllic and natural countryside. These works are, indeed, called <a href="/wiki/Pastoral" title="Pastoral">pastoral</a>, after the term for herding. The first surviving instances are the <i>Idylls</i> of <a href="/wiki/Theocritus" title="Theocritus">Theocritus</a>, and the <i>Eclogues</i> of <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a>, both of which inspired many imitators such as <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Spenser" title="Edmund Spenser">Edmund Spenser</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Shepheardes_Calender" title="The Shepheardes Calender">The Shepheardes Calender</a></i>. The shepherds of the pastoral are often heavily conventional and bear little relation to the actual work of shepherds.
</p><p>Shepherds and shepherdesses have been frequently immortalized in art and sculpture. Among the best known is the <a href="/wiki/Neoclassicism" title="Neoclassicism">neoclassical</a> Danish sculptor <a href="/wiki/Bertel_Thorvaldsen" title="Bertel Thorvaldsen">Bertel Thorvaldsen</a>'s <i>Shepherd Boy with Dog</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In the poem "The passionate shepherd to his love", by Christopher Marlowe, a shepherd is depicted as a partaker of rural paradise, and capable of giving things worth more than that a town resident could give.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the Latin American literary classic <i>Empire of Dreams</i> (Yale, 1994) by Giannina Braschi, shepherds invade the city of New York in a pastoral revolution.
</p><p>The shepherd, in such works, appears as a virtuous soul because of his living close to nature, uncorrupted by the temptations of the city. So <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Spenser" title="Edmund Spenser">Edmund Spenser</a> writes in his <i><a href="/wiki/Colin_Clouts_Come_Home_Againe" title="Colin Clouts Come Home Againe">Colin Clouts Come Home Againe</a></i> of a shepherd who went to the city, saw its wickedness, and returned home wiser, and in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Faerie_Queene" title="The Faerie Queene">The Faerie Queene</a></i> makes the shepherds the only people to whom the Blatant Beast is unknown.
</p><p>Many tales involving <a href="/wiki/Child_abandonment#Child_abandonment_in_literature" title="Child abandonment">foundlings</a> portray them being rescued by shepherds: <a href="/wiki/Oedipus" title="Oedipus">Oedipus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romulus_and_Remus" title="Romulus and Remus">Romulus and Remus</a>, the title characters of <a href="/wiki/Longus" title="Longus">Longus</a>'s <i>Daphnis and Chloe</i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Winter%27s_Tale" title="The Winter&#39;s Tale">The Winter's Tale</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>. These characters are often of much higher social status than the characters who save and raise them, the shepherds themselves being secondary characters. Similarly, the heroes and heroines of <a href="/wiki/Fairy_tale" title="Fairy tale">fairy tales</a> written by the <a href="/wiki/Pr%C3%A9cieuses" title="Précieuses">précieuses</a> often appeared as shepherds and shepherdesses in pastoral settings, but these figures were royal or noble, and their simple setting does not cloud their innate nobility.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Hans_Christian_Andersen" title="Hans Christian Andersen">Hans Christian Andersen</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/The_Shepherdess_and_the_Chimney_Sweep" title="The Shepherdess and the Chimney Sweep">The Shepherdess and the Chimney Sweep</a>" (1845), the porcelain shepherdess carries a gilt crook and wears shoes of gilt as well. Her lover is a porcelain chimney sweep with a princely face "as fair and rosy as a girl's", completely unsmudged with soot.
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Shepherd" title="The Shepherd">The Shepherd</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Forsyth" title="Frederick Forsyth">Frederick Forsyth</a> is the story of a flight from Germany to England undertaken by a young <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Vampire" title="De Havilland Vampire">Vampire</a> pilot one Christmas Eve.
</p><p>Biographies of <a href="/wiki/David_Ben-Gurion" title="David Ben-Gurion">David Ben-Gurion</a> published in the early years of <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> emphasized his having been a shepherd immediately after his arrival in the country in the 1900s. Later, however, historians concluded that he had been involved only very briefly in this profession and was not good at it.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
