<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:RomanItaly.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/RomanItaly.png/220px-RomanItaly.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="271" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/RomanItaly.png/330px-RomanItaly.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/RomanItaly.png/440px-RomanItaly.png 2x" data-file-width="720" data-file-height="887" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:RomanItaly.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Evolution of the term "<i>Italia</i>" in antiquity.</div></div></div>
<p><span lang="la" style="font-style:normal;" title="Latin language text"><b>Italia</b></span> (the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> name for the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Peninsula" title="Italian Peninsula">Italian Peninsula</a>) was the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">homeland of the Romans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metropole" title="Metropole">metropole</a> of Rome's <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">empire</a> in <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">classical antiquity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Roman_mythology" title="Roman mythology">Roman mythology</a>, Italy was the new home promised by <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_(god)" class="mw-redirect" title="Jupiter (god)">Jupiter</a> to <a href="/wiki/Aeneas" title="Aeneas">Aeneas of Troy</a> and his descendants, ancestors of the <a href="/wiki/Romulus_and_Remus" title="Romulus and Remus">founders of Rome</a>. Aside from the legendary accounts, Rome was an Italian city-state that changed its form of government from <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Rome" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Rome">kingdom</a> to <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">republic</a> and then grew within the context of a peninsula dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Italy" title="Northern Italy">North</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Etruscans" class="mw-redirect" title="Etruscans">Etruscans</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Central_Italy" title="Central Italy">Centre</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greeks</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Magna_Graecia" title="Magna Graecia">South</a>.
</p><p>The consolidation of Italy into a single entity occurred during the <a href="/wiki/Roman_expansion_in_Italy" title="Roman expansion in Italy">Roman expansion in the peninsula</a>, when Rome formed a <a href="/wiki/Socii" title="Socii">permanent association</a> with most of the local tribes and cities.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The strength of the Italian alliance was a crucial factor in the <a href="/wiki/Rise_of_Rome" title="Rise of Rome">rise of Rome</a>, starting with the <a href="/wiki/Punic_wars" class="mw-redirect" title="Punic wars">Punic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Macedonian_wars" class="mw-redirect" title="Macedonian wars">Macedonian</a> wars between the 3rd and 2nd century BC. As <a href="/wiki/Provinces" class="mw-redirect" title="Provinces">provinces</a> were being established throughout the Mediterranean, Italy maintained a special status which made it "not a province, but the <i lang="la" title="Latin language text"><a href="/wiki/Dominus_(title)" title="Dominus (title)">Domina</a></i> (ruler) of the provinces".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Such a status meant that Roman magistrates exercised the <i lang="la" title="Latin language text"><a href="/wiki/Imperium" title="Imperium">Imperium</a> domi</i> (police power) within Italy, rather than the <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">Imperium militiae</i> (military power) used abroad. Italy's inhabitants had <a href="/wiki/Latin_Rights" title="Latin Rights">Latin Rights</a> as well as religious and financial privileges.
</p><p>The period between the end of the 2nd century BC and the 1st century BC was <a href="/wiki/Roman_Revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Revolution">turbulent</a>, beginning with the <a href="/wiki/Servile_Wars" title="Servile Wars">Servile Wars</a>, continuing with the opposition of <a href="/wiki/Optimates" title="Optimates">aristocratic élite</a> to <a href="/wiki/Populares" title="Populares">populist reformers</a> and leading to a <a href="/wiki/Social_War_(91%E2%80%9388_BC)" title="Social War (91–88 BC)">Social War</a> in the middle of Italy. However, <a href="/wiki/Roman_citizenship" title="Roman citizenship">Roman citizenship</a> was recognized to the rest of the Italics by the end of the conflict and then extended to <a href="/wiki/Cisalpine_Gaul" title="Cisalpine Gaul">Cisalpine Gaul</a> when <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a> became <a href="/wiki/Roman_Dictator" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Dictator">Roman Dictator</a>. In the context  of the transition <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_reforms_of_Augustus" title="Constitutional reforms of Augustus">from Republic to Principate</a>, Italy swore allegiance to <a href="/wiki/Octavian_Augustus" class="mw-redirect" title="Octavian Augustus">Octavian Augustus</a> and was then organized in eleven regions from the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Ionian_Sea" title="Ionian Sea">Ionian Sea</a>.
</p><p>More than <a href="/wiki/Pax_Romana" title="Pax Romana">two centuries of stability</a> followed, during which Italy was referred to as the <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">rectrix mundi</i> (queen of the world) and <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">omnium terrarum parens</i> (motherland of all lands).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Several emperors made notable accomplishments in this period: <a href="/wiki/Claudius" title="Claudius">Claudius</a> incorporated Britain into the Roman Empire, <a href="/wiki/Vespasian" title="Vespasian">Vespasian</a> subjugated the <a href="/wiki/Great_Revolt_of_Judea" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Revolt of Judea">Great Revolt of Judea</a> and reformed the financial system, <a href="/wiki/Trajan" title="Trajan">Trajan</a> conquered <a href="/wiki/Dacia" title="Dacia">Dacia</a> and defeated <a href="/wiki/Parthia" title="Parthia">Parthia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Aurelius" title="Marcus Aurelius">Marcus Aurelius</a> epitomized the ideal of the <a href="/wiki/Philosopher_king" title="Philosopher king">philosopher king</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Crisis_of_the_third_century" class="mw-redirect" title="Crisis of the third century">crisis of the third century</a> hit Italy particularly hard and left the eastern half of the Empire more prosperous. In 286 AD the Roman Emperor <a href="/wiki/Diocletian" title="Diocletian">Diocletian</a> moved the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a> from Rome to <a href="/wiki/Mediolanum" title="Mediolanum">Mediolanum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Nevertheless, the islands of <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a> were added to Italy by <a href="/wiki/Diocletian" title="Diocletian">Diocletian</a> in 292 AD, and Italian cities such as Mediolanum and <a href="/wiki/Ravenna" title="Ravenna">Ravenna</a> continued to serve as capitals for the West. 
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Rome" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishop of Rome">Bishop of Rome</a> gained importance during <a href="/wiki/Constantine" class="mw-redirect" title="Constantine">Constantine</a>'s reign and was given religious primacy with the <a href="/wiki/Edict_of_Thessalonica" title="Edict of Thessalonica">Edict of Thessalonica</a> under <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_I" title="Theodosius I">Theodosius I</a>. Italy was invaded several times by the <a href="/wiki/Barbarian" title="Barbarian">barbarians</a> and fell under the control of <a href="/wiki/Odoacer" title="Odoacer">Odoacer</a>, when <a href="/wiki/Deposition_of_Romulus_Augustus" title="Deposition of Romulus Augustus">Romulus Augustus was deposed</a> in 476 AD. In the sixth century, Italy's territory was divided between the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic peoples</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="Vast oversimplification (June 2019)">further explanation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> After that, Italy remained divided until 1861, when <a href="/wiki/Italian_unification" title="Italian unification">it was reunited</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy" title="Kingdom of Italy">Kingdom of Italy</a>, which became the present-day <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian Republic</a> in 1946.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following the end of the <a href="/wiki/Social_War_(91%E2%80%9388_BC)" title="Social War (91–88 BC)">Social War</a> in 88 BC, Rome had allowed <a href="/wiki/Socii" title="Socii">its Italian allies</a> full rights in Roman society and granted <a href="/wiki/Roman_citizenship" title="Roman citizenship">Roman citizenship</a> to all the <a href="/wiki/Italic_peoples" title="Italic peoples">Italic peoples</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After having been for centuries the heart of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, from the 3rd century the government and the cultural center began to move eastward: first the <a href="/wiki/Constitutio_Antoniniana" title="Constitutio Antoniniana">Edict of Caracalla</a> in 212 AD extended Roman citizenship to all free men within the imperial boundaries. Then, <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> became the dominant religion during <a href="/wiki/Constantine_the_Great" title="Constantine the Great">Constantine</a>'s reign (306–337), raising the power of other <a href="/wiki/Pentarchy" title="Pentarchy">Eastern political centres</a>. Although not founded as a capital city in 330, <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> grew in importance. It finally gained the rank of eastern capital when given an <a href="/wiki/Urban_prefect" class="mw-redirect" title="Urban prefect">urban prefect</a> in 359 and the <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_the_Roman_Empire" title="Senate of the Roman Empire">senators</a> who were <i>clari</i> became senators of the lowest rank as <i><a href="/wiki/Clarissimus" class="mw-redirect" title="Clarissimus">clarissimi</a></i>.
</p><p>As a result, Italy began to decline in favour of the provinces, which resulted in the division of the Empire into two administrative units in 395: the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a>, with its capital at <a href="/wiki/Mediolanum" title="Mediolanum">Mediolanum</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Roman_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Roman Empire">Eastern Roman Empire</a>, with its capital at <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>). In 402, the capital was moved to <a href="/wiki/Ravenna" title="Ravenna">Ravenna</a> from Milan, confirming the decline of the city of Rome (which was <a href="/wiki/Sack_of_Rome_(410)" title="Sack of Rome (410)">sacked</a> in 410 for the first time in seven centuries).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name <i>Italia</i> covered an area whose borders evolved over time. According to <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Geographica" title="Geographica">Geographica</a></i>, before the expansion of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a>, the name was used by Greeks to indicate the land between the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Messina" title="Strait of Messina">strait of Messina</a> and the line connecting the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Salerno" title="Gulf of Salerno">gulf of Salerno</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Taranto" title="Gulf of Taranto">gulf of Taranto</a> (corresponding roughly to the current region of <a href="/wiki/Calabria" title="Calabria">Calabria</a>); later the term was extended by Romans to include the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Peninsula" title="Italian Peninsula">Italian Peninsula</a> up to the <a href="/wiki/Rubicon" title="Rubicon">Rubicon</a>, a river located between <a href="/wiki/Northern_Italy" title="Northern Italy">Northern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Italy" title="Central Italy">Central Italy</a>.
</p><p>In 49 BC, with the <i><a href="/wiki/Lex_Roscia" title="Lex Roscia">Lex Roscia</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a> gave Roman citizenship to the people of the <a href="/wiki/Cisalpine_Gaul" title="Cisalpine Gaul">Cisalpine Gaul</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> while in 42 BCE the hitherto existing province was abolished, thus extending Italy to the north up to the southern foot of the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-au_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-au-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Under Augustus, the peoples of today's <a href="/wiki/Aosta_Valley" title="Aosta Valley">Aosta Valley</a> and of the western and northern Alps were subjugated (so the western border of Roman Italy was moved to the <a href="/wiki/Var_(river)" title="Var (river)">Varus river</a>), and the Italian eastern border was brought to the <a href="/wiki/Ra%C5%A1a_(river)" title="Raša (river)">Arsia</a> in <a href="/wiki/Istria" title="Istria">Istria</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-au_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-au-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Lastly, in the late 3rd century, Italy came to also include the islands of <a href="/wiki/Sicilia_(Roman_province)" title="Sicilia (Roman province)">Sicily</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corsica_and_Sardinia" class="mw-redirect" title="Corsica and Sardinia">Corsica and Sardinia</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Raetia" title="Raetia">Raetia</a> and part of <a href="/wiki/Pannonia" title="Pannonia">Pannonia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The city of <a href="/wiki/Emona" title="Emona">Emona</a> (modern <a href="/wiki/Ljubljana" title="Ljubljana">Ljubljana</a>, Slovenia) was the easternmost town of Italy.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Augustan_organization">Augustan organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Augustan organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the beginning of the Roman imperial era, Italy was a collection of territories with different political statuses. Some cities, called <i><a href="/wiki/Municipium" title="Municipium">municipia</a></i>, had some independence from Rome, while others, the <i><a href="/wiki/Colonia_(Roman)" title="Colonia (Roman)">coloniae</a></i>, were founded by the Romans themselves. Around 7 BC, <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a> divided Italy into eleven <i>regiones</i>, as reported by <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Pliny%27s_Natural_History" class="mw-redirect" title="Pliny&#39;s Natural History">Naturalis Historia</a></i>:

<p>Italy was privileged by Augustus and his heirs, with the construction, among other public structures, of a dense network of <a href="/wiki/Roman_roads" title="Roman roads">Roman roads</a>. The Italian economy flourished: agriculture, handicraft and industry had a sensible growth, allowing the export of goods to the other provinces.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The Italian population may have grown as well: three census were ordered by Augustus, to record the number of Roman citizens throughout the empire. The surviving totals were 4,063,000 in 28 BC, 4,233,000 in 8 BC, and 4,937,000 in AD 14, but it is still debated whether these counted all citizens, all adult male citizens, or citizens <i><a href="/wiki/Sui_iuris" title="Sui iuris">sui iuris</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Estimates for the population of mainland Italy, including Cisalpine Gaul, at the beginning of the 1st century range from 6,000,000 according to <a href="/wiki/Karl_Julius_Beloch" title="Karl Julius Beloch">Karl Julius Beloch</a> in 1886, to 14,000,000 according to <a href="/wiki/Elio_Lo_Cascio" title="Elio Lo Cascio">Elio Lo Cascio</a> in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diocletian_and_Constantinian_re-organizations">Diocletian and Constantinian re-organizations<span id="Diocletian_re-organization"></span><span id="Constantinian_re-organization"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diocletian and Constantinian re-organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Crisis_of_the_Third_Century" title="Crisis of the Third Century">Crisis of the Third Century</a> the Roman Empire nearly collapsed under the combined pressures of invasions, military anarchy and civil wars, and hyperinflation. In 284, emperor <a href="/wiki/Diocletian" title="Diocletian">Diocletian</a> restored political stability. He carried out thorough administrative reforms to maintain order. He created the so-called <a href="/wiki/Tetrarchy" title="Tetrarchy">Tetrarchy</a> whereby the empire was ruled by two senior emperors called <a href="/wiki/Augustus_(honorific)" class="mw-redirect" title="Augustus (honorific)">Augusti</a> and two junior vice-emperors called Caesars. He decreased the size of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">Roman provinces</a> by doubling their number to reduce the power of the provincial governors. He grouped the provinces into several <a href="/wiki/Roman_diocese" title="Roman diocese">dioceses</a> (Latin: diocesis) and put them under the supervision of the imperial <a href="/wiki/Vicarius" title="Vicarius">vicarius</a> (vice, deputy), who was the head of the diocese. During the Crisis of the Third Century the importance of Rome declined because the city was far from the troubled frontiers. Diocletian and his colleagues usually resided in four imperial seats. The Augusti, Diocletian and <a href="/wiki/Maximian" title="Maximian">Maximian</a>, who were responsible for the East and West respectively, established themselves at <a href="/wiki/Nicomedia" title="Nicomedia">Nicomedia</a>, in north-western <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a> (closer to the  Persian frontier in the east) and <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>, in northern Italy (closer to the European frontiers) respectively. The seats of the Caesars were <a href="/wiki/Augusta_Treverorum" class="mw-redirect" title="Augusta Treverorum">Augusta Treverorum</a> (on the River <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> frontier) for <a href="/wiki/Constantius_Chlorus" title="Constantius Chlorus">Constantius Chlorus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sirmium" title="Sirmium">Sirmium</a> (on the River <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a> frontier) for <a href="/wiki/Galerius" title="Galerius">Galerius</a> who also resided at Thessaloniki.
</p><p>Under Diocletian Italy became the <b>Dioecesis Italiciana</b>. It included <a href="/wiki/Raetia" title="Raetia">Raetia</a>. It was subdivided the following provinces:
</p>
<ul><li><ul><li><i>Liguria</i> (today's <a href="/wiki/Liguria" title="Liguria">Liguria</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Piedmont" title="Piedmont">Piedmont</a>)</li>
<li><i>Transpadana</i> (western Piedmont and <a href="/wiki/Lombardy" title="Lombardy">Lombardy</a>)</li>
<li><i>Rhaetia</i> (eastern <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, western and central <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> and part of southern <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>)</li>
<li><i>Venetia et Histria</i> (today's <a href="/wiki/Veneto" title="Veneto">Veneto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Friuli-Venezia_Giulia" class="mw-redirect" title="Friuli-Venezia Giulia">Friuli-Venezia Giulia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trentino-Alto_Adige" class="mw-redirect" title="Trentino-Alto Adige">Trentino-Alto Adige</a>)</li>
<li><i>Aemilia</i> (<a href="/wiki/Emilia-Romagna" title="Emilia-Romagna">Emilia-Romagna</a>)</li>
<li><i>Tuscia</i> (<a href="/wiki/Etruria" title="Etruria">Etruria</a>) <i>et Umbria</i> (<a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">Tuscany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Umbria" title="Umbria">Umbria</a>)</li>
<li><i>Flaminia</i> (<a href="/wiki/Picenum" title="Picenum">Picenum</a> and the former <a href="/wiki/Ager_Gallicus" title="Ager Gallicus">Ager Gallicus</a>, in today's <a href="/wiki/Marche" title="Marche">Marche</a>)</li>
<li><i>Latium et Campania</i> (the coastal parts of <a href="/wiki/Lazio" title="Lazio">Lazio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Campania" title="Campania">Campania</a>)</li>
<li><i>Samnium</i> (<a href="/wiki/Abruzzo" title="Abruzzo">Abruzzo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molise" title="Molise">Molise</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irpinia" title="Irpinia">Irpinia</a>)</li>
<li><i>Apulia et Calabria</i> (today's <a href="/wiki/Apulia" title="Apulia">Apulia</a>)</li>

<p><a href="/wiki/Constantine_the_Great" title="Constantine the Great">Constantine</a> subdivided the empire into four <a href="/wiki/Praetorian_prefecture" title="Praetorian prefecture">Praetorian prefectures</a>. The <i>Diocesis Italiciana</i> became the <a href="/wiki/Praetorian_prefecture_of_Italy" title="Praetorian prefecture of Italy">Praetorian prefecture of Italy</a> (<i>praefectura praetoria Italiae</i>), and was subdivided into two dioceses. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_Roman_Empire">Western Roman Empire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Western Roman Empire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><p>
In 330, <a href="/wiki/Constantine_the_Great" title="Constantine the Great">Constantine</a> inaugurated <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>. He established the imperial court, a Senate, financial and judicial administrations, as well as the military structures. The new city, however, did not receive an urban prefect until 359 which raised it to the status of eastern capital. After the death of <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_I" title="Theodosius I">Theodosius</a> in 395 and the subsequent division of the empire Italy was part of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a>. As a result of Alaric's invasion in 402 the western seat was moved from <a href="/wiki/Mediolanum" title="Mediolanum">Mediolanum</a> to <a href="/wiki/Ravenna" title="Ravenna">Ravenna</a>. <a href="/wiki/Alaric_I" title="Alaric I">Alaric</a>, king of <a href="/wiki/Visigoth" class="mw-redirect" title="Visigoth">Visigoths</a>, sacked Rome itself in 410; something that hadn't happened for eight centuries. Northern Italy was attacked by <a href="/wiki/Attila" title="Attila">Attila</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Huns" title="Huns">Huns</a> in 452. Rome was sacked in 455  again by the <a href="/wiki/Vandals" title="Vandals">Vandals</a> under the command of <a href="/wiki/Genseric" class="mw-redirect" title="Genseric">Genseric</a>. </p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Praetorian_Prefectures_of_the_Roman_Empire_395_AD.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Praetorian_Prefectures_of_the_Roman_Empire_395_AD.png/300px-Praetorian_Prefectures_of_the_Roman_Empire_395_AD.png" decoding="async" width="300" height="198" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Praetorian_Prefectures_of_the_Roman_Empire_395_AD.png/450px-Praetorian_Prefectures_of_the_Roman_Empire_395_AD.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Praetorian_Prefectures_of_the_Roman_Empire_395_AD.png/600px-Praetorian_Prefectures_of_the_Roman_Empire_395_AD.png 2x" data-file-width="900" data-file-height="595" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Praetorian_Prefectures_of_the_Roman_Empire_395_AD.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The "Praetorian Prefecture of Italy" (in yellow) stretched from the Danube river to North Africa</div></div></div>
<p>According to <i><a href="/wiki/Notitia_Dignitatum" title="Notitia Dignitatum">Notitia Dignitatum</a></i>, one of the very few surviving documents of Roman government updated to the 420s, Roman Italy was governed by a <a href="/wiki/Praetorian_prefect" title="Praetorian prefect">praetorian prefect</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Praetorian_prefecture_of_Italy" title="Praetorian prefecture of Italy">Prefectus praetorio Italiae</a></i> (who also governed the <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Africa" title="Diocese of Africa">Diocese of Africa</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Pannonia" title="Diocese of Pannonia">Diocese of Pannonia</a>), one <i><a href="/wiki/Vicarius" title="Vicarius">vicarius</a></i>, and one <i><a href="/wiki/Comes_rei_militaris" class="mw-redirect" title="Comes rei militaris">comes rei militaris</a></i>. The regions of Italy were governed at the end of the fourth century by eight <i><a href="/wiki/Consularis" title="Consularis">consulares</a></i> (<i>Venetiae et Histriae</i>, <i>Aemiliae</i>, <i>Liguriae</i>, <i>Flaminiae et Piceni annonarii</i>, <i>Tusciae et Umbriae</i>, <i>Piceni suburbicarii</i>, <i>Campaniae</i>, and <i>Siciliae</i>), two <i><a href="/wiki/Corrector" title="Corrector">correctores</a></i> (<i>Apuliae et Calabriae</i> and <i>Lucaniae et Bruttiorum</i>) and seven <i><a href="/wiki/Praeses" title="Praeses">praesides</a></i> (<i>Alpium Cottiarum</i>, <i>Rhaetia Prima</i> and <i>Secunda</i>, <i>Samnii</i>, <i>Valeriae</i>, <i>Sardiniae</i>, and <i>Corsicae</i>). In the fifth century, with the Emperors controlled by their barbarian generals, the Western imperial government maintained weak control over Italy, whose coasts were periodically under attack. In 476, with the abdication of <a href="/wiki/Romulus_Augustulus" title="Romulus Augustulus">Romulus Augustulus</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a> had formally fallen unless one considers Julius Nepos, the legitimate emperor recognized by Constantinople as the last. He was assassinated in 480He may have been recognized by Odoacer. Italy remained under <a href="/wiki/Odoacer" title="Odoacer">Odoacer</a> and his <a href="/wiki/Odoacer#King_of_Italy" title="Odoacer">Kingdom of Italy</a>, and then under the <a href="/wiki/Ostrogothic_Kingdom" title="Ostrogothic Kingdom">Ostrogothic Kingdom</a>. The Germanic successor states under <a href="/wiki/Odoacer" title="Odoacer">Odoacer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Theodoric_the_Great" title="Theodoric the Great">Theodoric the Great</a> continued to use the Roman administrative machinery, as well as being nominal subjects of the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Emperor" class="mw-redirect" title="Byzantine Emperor">Eastern emperor</a> at <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>. In 535 <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_emperor" class="mw-redirect" title="Byzantine emperor">Roman Emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Justinian" class="mw-redirect" title="Justinian">Justinian</a> invaded Italy which suffered twenty years of disastrous war. In August 554, Justinian issued a <a href="/wiki/Pragmatic_sanction" title="Pragmatic sanction">Pragmatic sanction</a> which maintained most of the organization of <a href="/wiki/Diocletian" title="Diocletian">Diocletian</a>. The "Prefecture of Italy" thus survived, and came under Roman control in the course of <a href="/wiki/Justinian" class="mw-redirect" title="Justinian">Justinian</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Gothic_War_(535%E2%80%93552)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gothic War (535–552)">Gothic War</a>.
As a result of the Lombard invasion in 568, the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantines</a> lost most of Italy, except the territories of the <a href="/wiki/Exarchate_of_Ravenna" title="Exarchate of Ravenna">Exarchate of Ravenna</a> - a corridor from Venice to Lazio - and footholds in the south Naples and the toe and heel of the peninsula.
</p>
