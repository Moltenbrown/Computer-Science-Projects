	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Bayeux Tapestry</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Odo_bayeux_tapestry.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Odo_bayeux_tapestry.png/290px-Odo_bayeux_tapestry.png" decoding="async" width="290" height="210" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Odo_bayeux_tapestry.png/435px-Odo_bayeux_tapestry.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Odo_bayeux_tapestry.png/580px-Odo_bayeux_tapestry.png 2x" data-file-width="1500" data-file-height="1088" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Odo_bayeux_tapestry.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A scene from the Bayeux Tapestry depicting <a href="/wiki/Odo,_Bishop_of_Bayeux" class="mw-redirect" title="Odo, Bishop of Bayeux">Odo, Bishop of Bayeux</a>, rallying Duke <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William's</a> troops during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hastings" title="Battle of Hastings">Battle of Hastings</a> in 1066</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Bayeux Tapestry</b> (<span class="nowrap"><small><a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">UK</a>: </small><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;b&#39; in &#39;buy&#39;">b</span><span title="/aɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;tide&#39;">aɪ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/j/: &#39;y&#39; in &#39;yes&#39;">j</span><span title="/ɜː/: r-less &#39;ur&#39; in &#39;nurse&#39;">ɜː</span></span>,<span class="wrap"> </span><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;b&#39; in &#39;buy&#39;">b</span><span title="/eɪ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;face&#39;">eɪ</span></span>-/</a></span></span>, <span class="nowrap"><small><a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">US</a>: </small><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;b&#39; in &#39;buy&#39;">b</span><span title="/eɪ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;face&#39;">eɪ</span><span title="/j/: &#39;y&#39; in &#39;yes&#39;">j</span><span title="/uː/: &#39;oo&#39; in &#39;goose&#39;">uː</span></span>,<span class="wrap"> </span><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;b&#39; in &#39;buy&#39;">b</span><span title="/aɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;tide&#39;">aɪ</span></span>-/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Tapisserie de Bayeux</i> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">[tapisʁi də bajø]</a></span> or <i lang="fr" title="French language text">La telle du conquest</i>; <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <i lang="la">Tapete Baiocense</i>) is an <a href="/wiki/Embroidery" title="Embroidery">embroidered</a> cloth nearly 70 metres (230&#160;ft) long and 50 centimetres (20&#160;in) tall,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> which depicts the events leading up to the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">Norman conquest of England</a> concerning <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William, Duke of Normandy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Harold_Godwinson" title="Harold Godwinson">Harold, Earl of Wessex, later King of England</a>, and culminating in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hastings" title="Battle of Hastings">Battle of Hastings</a>. It is thought to date to the 11th century, within a few years after the battle. It tells the story from the point of view of the conquering Normans, but is now agreed to have been made in England.
</p><p>According to Sylvette Lemagnen, conservator of the tapestry, in her 2005 book <i>La Tapisserie de Bayeux</i>:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The Bayeux tapestry is one of the supreme achievements of the Norman <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_art" title="Romanesque art">Romanesque</a>&#160;.... Its survival almost intact over nine centuries is little short of miraculous&#160;... Its exceptional length, the harmony and freshness of its colours, its exquisite workmanship, and the genius of its guiding spirit combine to make it endlessly fascinating.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>The cloth consists of some seventy scenes, many with Latin <i><a href="/wiki/Tituli" class="mw-redirect" title="Tituli">tituli</a></i>, embroidered on linen with coloured woollen yarns. It is likely that it was commissioned by <a href="/wiki/Odo,_Earl_of_Kent" class="mw-redirect" title="Odo, Earl of Kent">Bishop Odo</a>, William's half-brother, and made in England—not Bayeux—in the 1070s. In 1729 the hanging was rediscovered by scholars at a time when it was being displayed annually in <a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Cathedral" title="Bayeux Cathedral">Bayeux Cathedral</a>. The tapestry is now exhibited at the Musée de la Tapisserie de Bayeux in <a href="/wiki/Bayeux" title="Bayeux">Bayeux</a>, <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a>, France <span class="nowrap">(<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Bayeux_Tapestry&amp;params=49.2744_N_0.7003_W_type:landmark_region:FR_source:wikidata"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">49°16′28″N</span> <span class="longitude">0°42′01″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">&#xfeff; / &#xfeff;</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">49.2744°N 0.7003°W</span><span style="display:none">&#xfeff; / <span class="geo">49.2744; -0.7003</span></span></span></a></span>).</span>
</p><p>The designs on the Bayeux Tapestry are <a href="/wiki/Embroidery" title="Embroidery">embroidered</a> rather than <a href="/wiki/Weaving" title="Weaving">woven</a>, so that it is not technically a <a href="/wiki/Tapestry" title="Tapestry">tapestry</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Nevertheless, it has always been referred to as a tapestry until recent years when the name "Bayeux Embroidery" has gained ground among certain art historians.  It can be seen as a rare example of secular <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_art" title="Romanesque art">Romanesque art</a>.  Tapestries adorned both churches and wealthy houses in Medieval Western Europe, though at 0.5 by 68.38 metres (1.6 by 224.3&#160;ft, and apparently incomplete) the Bayeux Tapestry is exceptionally large.  Only the figures and decoration are embroidered, on a background left plain, which shows the subject very clearly and was necessary to cover large areas.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayeux_Tapestry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Odo_of_Bayeux.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Odo_of_Bayeux.jpg/170px-Odo_of_Bayeux.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="305" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5a/Odo_of_Bayeux.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="200" data-file-height="359" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Odo_of_Bayeux.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Odo,_Earl_of_Kent" class="mw-redirect" title="Odo, Earl of Kent">Bishop Odo</a> of Bayeux</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BayeuxCathedrale_01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/BayeuxCathedrale_01.jpg/220px-BayeuxCathedrale_01.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="229" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/BayeuxCathedrale_01.jpg/330px-BayeuxCathedrale_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/BayeuxCathedrale_01.jpg/440px-BayeuxCathedrale_01.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="4169" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BayeuxCathedrale_01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Cathedral" title="Bayeux Cathedral">Bayeux Cathedral</a>, home of the tapestry in the Middle Ages and until the beginning of the 19th century</div></div></div>
<p>The earliest known written reference to the tapestry is a 1476 inventory of <a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Cathedral" title="Bayeux Cathedral">Bayeux Cathedral</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> but its origins have been the subject of much speculation and controversy.
</p><p>French legend maintained the tapestry was commissioned and created by <a href="/wiki/Matilda_of_Flanders" title="Matilda of Flanders">Queen Matilda</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="William I of England">William the Conqueror's</a> wife, and her <a href="/wiki/Lady-in-waiting" title="Lady-in-waiting">ladies-in-waiting</a>. Indeed, in France, it is occasionally known as "La Tapisserie de la Reine Mathilde" (Tapestry of Queen Matilda). However, scholarly analysis<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> in the 20th century concluded it was probably commissioned by William's half-brother, <a href="/wiki/Odo_of_Bayeux" title="Odo of Bayeux">Bishop Odo</a>, who, after the Conquest, became <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Kent" title="Earl of Kent">Earl of Kent</a> and, when William was absent in Normandy, <a href="/wiki/Regent" title="Regent">regent</a> of England.
</p><p>The reasons for the Odo commission theory include: 1) three of the bishop's followers mentioned in the <i><a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a></i> appear on the tapestry; 2) it was found in Bayeux Cathedral, built by Odo; and 3) it may have been commissioned at the same time as the cathedral's construction in the 1070s, possibly completed by 1077 in time for display on the cathedral's dedication.
</p><p>Assuming Odo commissioned the tapestry, it was probably designed and constructed in England by <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_art" title="Anglo-Saxon art">Anglo-Saxon artists</a> (Odo's main power base being by then in <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a>); the Latin text contains hints of Anglo-Saxon; other embroideries originate from England at this time; and the vegetable dyes can be found in cloth traditionally woven there.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wilson_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Coatsworth_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coatsworth-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Howard B. Clarke has proposed that the designer of the tapestry was <a href="/wiki/Scolland" title="Scolland">Scolland</a>, the abbot of <a href="/wiki/St_Augustine%27s_Abbey" title="St Augustine&#39;s Abbey">St Augustine's Abbey</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canterbury" title="Canterbury">Canterbury</a>, because of his previous position as head of the scriptorium at <a href="/wiki/Mont_Saint-Michel" class="mw-redirect" title="Mont Saint-Michel">Mont Saint-Michel</a> (famed for its illumination), his travels to <a href="/wiki/Trajan%27s_Column" title="Trajan&#39;s Column">Trajan's Column</a>, and his connections to <a href="/wiki/Wadard" title="Wadard">Wadard</a> and Vital, two individuals identified in the tapestry.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  The actual physical work of stitching was most likely undertaken by female needleworkers. <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_art" title="Anglo-Saxon art">Anglo-Saxon</a> needlework of the more detailed type known as <a href="/wiki/Opus_Anglicanum" title="Opus Anglicanum">Opus Anglicanum</a> was famous across Europe.  It was perhaps commissioned for display in the hall of his palace and then bequeathed to the cathedral he built, following the pattern of the documented but lost hanging of <a href="/wiki/Byrhtnoth" title="Byrhtnoth">Byrhtnoth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Alternative theories exist. <a href="/wiki/Carola_Hicks" title="Carola Hicks">Carola Hicks</a> has suggested it could possibly have been commissioned by <a href="/wiki/Edith_of_Wessex" title="Edith of Wessex">Edith of Wessex</a>, widow of <a href="/wiki/Edward_the_Confessor" title="Edward the Confessor">Edward the Confessor</a> and sister of Harold.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>  Wolfgang Grape has challenged the consensus that the embroidery is Anglo-Saxon, distinguishing between Anglo-Saxon and other Northern European techniques;<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Medieval material authority Elizabeth Coatsworth<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> contradicted this: "The attempt to distinguish Anglo-Saxon from other Northern European embroideries before 1100 on the grounds of technique cannot be upheld on the basis of present knowledge."<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> George Beech suggests the tapestry was executed at the <a href="/wiki/Abbey_of_Saint-Florent_de_Saumur" title="Abbey of Saint-Florent de Saumur">Abbey of Saint-Florent de Saumur</a> in the Loire Valley, and says the detailed depiction of the Breton campaign argues for additional sources in France.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Andrew Bridgeford has suggested that the tapestry was actually of English design and encoded with secret messages meant to undermine Norman rule.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Construction.2C_design_and_technique"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction,_design_and_technique">Construction, design and technique</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayeux_Tapestry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Construction, design and technique">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayeux_tapestry_laid_work_detail..jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Bayeux_tapestry_laid_work_detail..jpg/220px-Bayeux_tapestry_laid_work_detail..jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="151" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Bayeux_tapestry_laid_work_detail..jpg/330px-Bayeux_tapestry_laid_work_detail..jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Bayeux_tapestry_laid_work_detail..jpg/440px-Bayeux_tapestry_laid_work_detail..jpg 2x" data-file-width="462" data-file-height="318" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayeux_tapestry_laid_work_detail..jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Detail of <a href="/wiki/Backstitch" title="Backstitch">stem stitching</a> and laid work.</div></div></div>
<p>In common with other embroidered hangings of the <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">early medieval period</a>, this piece is conventionally referred to as a "tapestry", although it is not a true <a href="/wiki/Tapestry" title="Tapestry">tapestry</a> in which the design is <a href="/wiki/Weaving" title="Weaving">woven</a> into the cloth; it is in fact an <a href="/wiki/Embroidery" title="Embroidery">embroidery</a>.
</p><p>The Bayeux tapestry is embroidered in <a href="/wiki/Crewel" class="mw-redirect" title="Crewel">crewel</a> (<a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a> <a href="/wiki/Yarn" title="Yarn">yarn</a>) on a <a href="/wiki/Plain_weave" title="Plain weave">tabby-woven</a> <a href="/wiki/Linen" title="Linen">linen</a> ground 68.38 metres long and 0.5 metres wide (224.3&#160;ft ×&#160;1.6&#160;ft) and using two methods of stitching: <a href="/wiki/Backstitch" title="Backstitch">outline or stem stitch</a> for lettering and the outlines of figures, and <a href="/wiki/Couching_(embroidery)" class="mw-redirect" title="Couching (embroidery)">couching or laid work</a> for filling in figures.<sup id="cite_ref-Wilson_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Coatsworth_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coatsworth-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Nine linen panels, between fourteen and three metres in length, were sewn together after each was embroidered and the joins were disguised with subsequent embroidery.<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> At the first join (start of <a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry_tituli#14" title="Bayeux Tapestry tituli">scene 14</a>) the borders do not line up properly but the technique was improved so that the later joins are practically invisible.<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The design involved a broad central zone with narrow decorative borders top and bottom.<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> By inspecting the woollen threads behind the linen it is apparent all these aspects were embroidered together at a session and the awkward placing of the <i>tituli</i> is not due to them being added later.<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Later generations have patched the hanging in numerous places and some of the embroidery (especially in the <a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry_tituli#58" title="Bayeux Tapestry tituli">final scene</a>) has been reworked.<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The tapestry may well have maintained much of its original appearance—it now compares closely with a careful drawing made in 1730.<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry_tituli#58" title="Bayeux Tapestry tituli">end of the tapestry</a> has been missing from time immemorial and the final <i>titulus</i> "Et fuga verterunt Angli" ("and the English left fleeing") is said to be "entirely spurious", added shortly before 1814 at a time of anti-English sentiment.<sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lucien_Musset" title="Lucien Musset">Musset</a>  speculates the hanging was originally about 1.5 metres longer.<sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> At the last section still remaining the embroidery has been almost completely restored but this seems to have been done with at least some regard to the original stitching.<sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The stylised tree is quite unlike any other tree in the tapestry.<sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry_tituli#1" title="Bayeux Tapestry tituli">start of the tapestry</a> has also been restored but to a much lesser extent.<sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Norton<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;note 1&#93;</a></sup> has reviewed the various measurements of the length of the tapestry itself and of its nine individual linen panels. He has also attempted to estimate the size and architectural design of the 11th-century Bayeux Cathedral. He considers the tapestry would have fitted well if it had been hung along the south, west and north <a href="/wiki/Arcade_(architecture)" title="Arcade (architecture)">arcades</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Nave" title="Nave">nave</a> and that the scenes it depicts can be correlated with positions of the arcade <a href="/wiki/Bay_(architecture)" title="Bay (architecture)">bays</a> in a way that would have been dramatically satisfying. He agrees with earlier speculation that a final panel is missing—one that shows William's coronation and which he thinks was some three metres long. Norton concludes that the tapestry was definitely designed to be hung in Bayeux Cathedral specifically; that it was designed to appeal to a Norman audience; and that it was probably designed for Bishop Odo so as to be displayed at the dedication of the cathedral in 1077 in the presence of William, Matilda, their sons, and Odo.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The main yarn colours are terracotta or russet, blue-green, dull gold, olive green, and blue, with small amounts of dark blue or black and sage green. Later repairs are worked in light yellow, orange, and light greens.<sup id="cite_ref-Wilson_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Laid yarns are couched in place with yarn of the same or contrasting colour.
</p><p>The tapestry's central zone contains most of the action, which sometimes overflows into the borders either for dramatic effect or because depictions would otherwise be very cramped (for example at Edward's <a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry_tituli#27" title="Bayeux Tapestry tituli">death scene</a>). Events take place in a long series of scenes which are generally separated by highly stylised trees. However, the trees are not placed consistently and the greatest scene shift, between Harold's audience with Edward after his return to England and Edward's <a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry_tituli#26" title="Bayeux Tapestry tituli">burial scene</a>, is not marked in any way at all.<sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <i>tituli</i> are normally in the central zone but occasionally use the top border. The borders are otherwise mostly purely decorative and only sometimes does the decoration complement the action in the central zone. The decoration consists of birds, beasts, fish and scenes from fables, agriculture, and hunting. There are frequent oblique bands separating the vignettes. There are nude figures, some of corpses from battle, others of a ribald nature.<sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Harrow_(tool)" title="Harrow (tool)">harrow</a>, a newly invented implement, is depicted (<a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry_tituli#10" title="Bayeux Tapestry tituli">scene 10</a>) and this is the earliest known depiction. The picture of <a href="/wiki/Halley%27s_Comet" title="Halley&#39;s Comet">Halley's Comet</a>, which appears in the upper border (<a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry_tituli#32" title="Bayeux Tapestry tituli">scene 32</a>), is the first known picture of this comet.<sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1724 a linen backing cloth was sewn on comparatively crudely and, in around the year 1800, large ink numerals were written on the backing which broadly enumerate each scene and which are still commonly used for reference.<sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_to_the_events_depicted">Background to the events depicted</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayeux_Tapestry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Background to the events depicted">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a series of pictures supported by a written commentary the tapestry tells the story of the events of 1064–1066 culminating in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hastings" title="Battle of Hastings">Battle of Hastings</a>. The two main protagonists are <a href="/wiki/Harold_Godwinson" title="Harold Godwinson">Harold Godwinson</a>, recently crowned King of England, leading the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon</a> English, and <a href="/wiki/William_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="William I of England">William, Duke of Normandy</a>, leading a mainly <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Norman</a> army, sometimes called the <a href="/wiki/Companions_of_William_the_Conqueror" title="Companions of William the Conqueror">companions of William the Conqueror</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tapisserie_agriculture.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Tapisserie_agriculture.JPG/220px-Tapisserie_agriculture.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Tapisserie_agriculture.JPG/330px-Tapisserie_agriculture.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Tapisserie_agriculture.JPG/440px-Tapisserie_agriculture.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tapisserie_agriculture.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Courier" title="Courier">messengers</a> with <a href="/wiki/Guy_I,_Count_of_Ponthieu" title="Guy I, Count of Ponthieu">Guy</a>, with a portrayal of medieval agriculture in the border</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayeux_Tapestry_scene1_Edward.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Bayeux_Tapestry_scene1_Edward.jpg/220px-Bayeux_Tapestry_scene1_Edward.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Bayeux_Tapestry_scene1_Edward.jpg/330px-Bayeux_Tapestry_scene1_Edward.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Bayeux_Tapestry_scene1_Edward.jpg/440px-Bayeux_Tapestry_scene1_Edward.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3570" data-file-height="2405" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayeux_Tapestry_scene1_Edward.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Edward_the_Confessor" title="Edward the Confessor">Edward the Confessor</a> sends Harold to Normandy</div></div></div>
<p>William was the <a href="/wiki/Illegitimate" class="mw-redirect" title="Illegitimate">illegitimate</a> son of <a href="/wiki/Robert_the_Magnificent" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert the Magnificent">Robert the Magnificent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Normandy" title="Duke of Normandy">Duke of Normandy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Herleva" title="Herleva">Herleva</a> (or Arlette), a <a href="/wiki/Tanner_(occupation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tanner (occupation)">tanner</a>'s daughter. William became Duke of Normandy at the age of seven and was in control of Normandy by the age of nineteen. His half-brother was Bishop <a href="/wiki/Odo_of_Bayeux" title="Odo of Bayeux">Odo of Bayeux</a>.
</p><p>King <a href="/wiki/Edward_the_Confessor" title="Edward the Confessor">Edward the Confessor</a>, king of England and about sixty years old at the time the tapestry starts its narration, had no children or any clear successor. Edward's mother, <a href="/wiki/Emma_of_Normandy" title="Emma of Normandy">Emma of Normandy</a>, was William's great aunt. At that time <a href="/wiki/Succession_to_the_British_throne#England" title="Succession to the British throne">succession to the English throne</a> was not by <a href="/wiki/Primogeniture" title="Primogeniture">primogeniture</a> but was decided jointly by the king and by an assembly of nobility, the <a href="/wiki/Witenagemot" title="Witenagemot">Witenagemot</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Harold_Godwinson" title="Harold Godwinson">Harold Godwinson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Wessex" title="Earl of Wessex">Earl of Wessex</a> and the most powerful noble in England, was Edward's brother-in-law. The Norman chronicler <a href="/wiki/William_of_Poitiers" title="William of Poitiers">William of Poitiers</a><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> reported that Edward had previously determined that William would succeed him on the throne, and Harold had sworn to honour this, and yet later that Harold had claimed Edward, on his deathbed, had made him heir over William. However, other sources, such as <a href="/wiki/Eadmer" title="Eadmer">Eadmer</a> dispute this claim.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Events_depicted_in_the_tapestry">Events depicted in the tapestry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayeux_Tapestry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Events depicted in the tapestry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The tapestry begins with a panel of Edward the Confessor sending Harold to Normandy.<sup><a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry_tituli#1" title="Bayeux Tapestry tituli">(scene 1)</a></sup> Later Norman sources say that the mission was for Harold to pledge loyalty to William but the tapestry does not suggest any specific purpose.<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> By mischance, Harold arrives at the wrong location in France and is taken prisoner by <a href="/wiki/Guy_I,_Count_of_Ponthieu" title="Guy I, Count of Ponthieu">Guy, Count of Ponthieu</a>.<sup><a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry_tituli#7" title="Bayeux Tapestry tituli">(scene 7)</a></sup> After exchanges of messages borne by mounted messengers, Harold is released to William who then invites Harold to accompany him on a campaign against <a href="/wiki/Conan_II,_Duke_of_Brittany" title="Conan II, Duke of Brittany">Conan II, Duke of Brittany</a>. On the way, just outside the monastery of <a href="/wiki/Mont_Saint-Michel" class="mw-redirect" title="Mont Saint-Michel">Mont Saint-Michel</a>, the army become mired in quicksand and Harold saves two Norman soldiers.<sup><a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry_tituli#17" title="Bayeux Tapestry tituli">(scene 17)</a></sup> William's army chases Conan from <a href="/wiki/Dol_de_Bretagne" class="mw-redirect" title="Dol de Bretagne">Dol de Bretagne</a> to <a href="/wiki/Rennes" title="Rennes">Rennes</a>, and Conan finally surrenders at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Dinan" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Dinan">Dinan</a>.<sup><a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry_tituli#20" title="Bayeux Tapestry tituli">(scene 20)</a></sup> William gives Harold arms and armour (possibly <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knighting</a> him) and Harold takes an oath on <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saintly</a> <a href="/wiki/Relic" title="Relic">relics</a>.<sup><a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry_tituli#23" title="Bayeux Tapestry tituli">(scene 23)</a></sup> Although the writing on the tapestry explicitly states an oath is taken there is no clue as to what is being promised.<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Harold leaves for home and meets again with the old king Edward, who appears to be remonstrating with him.<sup><a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry_tituli#25" title="Bayeux Tapestry tituli">(scene 25)</a></sup> Harold is in a somewhat submissive posture and seems to be in disgrace.<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> However, possibly deliberately, the king's intentions are not made clear.<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The scene then shifts by about one year to when Edward has become mortally ill and the tapestry strongly suggests that, on his deathbed, he bequeaths the crown to Harold.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;note 2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> What is probably the <a href="/wiki/Coronation" title="Coronation">coronation</a> ceremony<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;note 3&#93;</a></sup> is attended by <a href="/wiki/Stigand" title="Stigand">Stigand</a>, whose position as Archbishop of Canterbury was controversial.<sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup><a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry_tituli#31" title="Bayeux Tapestry tituli">(scene 31)</a></sup> Stigand is performing a liturgical function, possibly not the crowning itself.<sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The tapestry labels the celebrant as "Stigant Archieps" (Stigand the archbishop) although by that time he had been <a href="/wiki/Excommunication" title="Excommunication">excommunicated</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Papacy" class="mw-redirect" title="Papacy">papacy</a> who considered his appointment unlawful.<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>A star with a streaming tail, probably <a href="/wiki/Halley%27s_Comet" title="Halley&#39;s Comet">Halley's Comet</a>, then appears.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;note 4&#93;</a></sup> At this point, the lower border of the tapestry shows a fleet of ghost-like ships thus hinting at a future invasion.<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup><a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry_tituli#33" title="Bayeux Tapestry tituli">(scene 33)</a></sup> The news of Harold's coronation is taken to Normandy, whereupon we are told that William is ordering a fleet of ships to be built although it is Bishop Odo shown issuing the instructions.<sup><a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry_tituli#35" title="Bayeux Tapestry tituli">(scene 35)</a></sup> The invaders reach England, and land unopposed. William orders his men to find food, and a meal is cooked.<sup><a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry_tituli#43" title="Bayeux Tapestry tituli">(scene 43)</a></sup> A house is burnt by two soldiers, which may indicate some ravaging of the local countryside on the part of the invaders, and underneath, on a smaller scale than the arsonists, a woman holds her boy's hand as she asks for humanity.<sup><a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry_tituli#47" title="Bayeux Tapestry tituli">(scene 47)</a></sup> News is brought to William.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;note 5&#93;</a></sup> The Normans build a <a href="/wiki/Motte_and_bailey" class="mw-redirect" title="Motte and bailey">motte and bailey</a> at Hastings to defend their position. Messengers are sent between the two armies, and William makes a speech to prepare his army for battle.<sup><a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry_tituli#51" title="Bayeux Tapestry tituli">(scene 51)</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Normans_Bayeux.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Normans_Bayeux.jpg/220px-Normans_Bayeux.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="111" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Normans_Bayeux.jpg/330px-Normans_Bayeux.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Normans_Bayeux.jpg/440px-Normans_Bayeux.jpg 2x" data-file-width="553" data-file-height="280" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Normans_Bayeux.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hastings" title="Battle of Hastings">Battle of Hastings</a></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hastings" title="Battle of Hastings">Battle of Hastings</a> was fought on 14 October 1066 less than three weeks after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Stamford_Bridge" title="Battle of Stamford Bridge">Battle of Stamford Bridge</a> but the tapestry does not provide this context. The English fight on foot behind a <a href="/wiki/Shield_wall" title="Shield wall">shield wall</a>, whilst the Normans are on horses.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;note 6&#93;</a></sup> Two fallen knights are named as <a href="/wiki/Leofwine_Godwinson" title="Leofwine Godwinson">Leofwine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gyrth_Godwinson" title="Gyrth Godwinson">Gyrth</a>, Harold's brothers, but both armies are shown fighting bravely.<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Bishop Odo brandishes his baton or mace and rallies the Norman troops in battle.<sup><a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry_tituli#54" title="Bayeux Tapestry tituli">(scene 54)</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;note 7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> To reassure his knights that he is still alive and well, William raises his helmet to show his face.<sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The battle becomes very bloody with troops being slaughtered and dismembered corpses littering the ground. King Harold is killed.<sup><a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry_tituli#57" title="Bayeux Tapestry tituli">(scene 57)</a></sup> This scene can be interpreted in different ways, as the name "Harold" appears above a number of knights, making it difficult to identify which character is Harold, since one character appears with an arrow shot in his head under the name "Harold" while another character is slain by a sword underneath the words "he is slain". The final remaining scene shows unarmoured English troops fleeing the battlefield. The last part of the tapestry is missing; however, it is thought that the story contained only one additional scene.<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Latin_text">Latin text</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayeux_Tapestry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Latin text">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry_tituli" title="Bayeux Tapestry tituli">Bayeux Tapestry tituli</a></div>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Tituli" class="mw-redirect" title="Tituli">Tituli</a></i> are included in many scenes to point out names of people and places or to explain briefly the event being depicted.<sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The text is in Latin but at times the style of words and spelling shows an English influence.<sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> A dark blue wool, almost black, is mostly used but towards the end of the tapestry other colours are used, sometimes for each word and other times for each letter.<sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The complete text and English translation are displayed beside images of each scene at <a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry_tituli" title="Bayeux Tapestry tituli">Bayeux Tapestry tituli</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recorded_history">Recorded history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayeux_Tapestry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Recorded history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first reference to the tapestry is from 1476 when it was listed in an inventory of the treasures of Bayeux Cathedral. It survived the sack of Bayeux by the <a href="/wiki/Huguenots" title="Huguenots">Huguenots</a> in 1562; and the next certain reference is from 1724.<sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Antoine Lancelot sent a report to the <i>Académie Royale des Inscriptions et Belles-Lettres</i> concerning a sketch he had received about a work concerning William the Conqueror. He had no idea where or what the original was, although he suggested it could have been a tapestry.<sup id="cite_ref-stenton_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stenton-32">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Despite further enquiries he discovered no more.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MontfauconHaroldEye.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/MontfauconHaroldEye.jpg/220px-MontfauconHaroldEye.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="135" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/MontfauconHaroldEye.jpg/330px-MontfauconHaroldEye.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/MontfauconHaroldEye.jpg/440px-MontfauconHaroldEye.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1164" data-file-height="715" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MontfauconHaroldEye.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Bernard_de_Montfaucon" title="Bernard de Montfaucon">Montfaucon</a> / Benoît drawing showing Harold's death</div></div></div>
<p>The Benedictine scholar <a href="/wiki/Bernard_de_Montfaucon" title="Bernard de Montfaucon">Bernard de Montfaucon</a> made more successful investigations and found that the sketch was of a small portion of a tapestry preserved at Bayeux Cathedral. In 1729 and 1730 he published drawings and a detailed description of the complete work in the first two volumes of his <i>Les Monuments de la Monarchie française</i>. The drawings were by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine_Beno%C3%AEt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Antoine Benoît (page does not exist)">Antoine Benoît</a>, one of the ablest draughtsmen of that time.<sup id="cite_ref-stenton_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stenton-32">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The tapestry was first briefly noted in English in 1743 by <a href="/wiki/William_Stukeley" title="William Stukeley">William Stukeley</a>, in his <i>Palaeographia Britannica</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The first detailed account in English was written by <a href="/wiki/Smart_Lethieullier" title="Smart Lethieullier">Smart Lethieullier</a>, who was living in Paris in 1732–3, and was acquainted with Lancelot and de Montfaucon: it was not published, however, until 1767, as an appendix to <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Ducarel" title="Andrew Ducarel">Andrew Ducarel</a>'s <i>Anglo-Norman Antiquities</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>, in 1792, the tapestry was confiscated as public property to be used for covering military wagons.<sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> It was rescued from a wagon by a local lawyer who stored it in his house until the troubles were over, whereupon he sent it to the city administrators for safekeeping.<sup id="cite_ref-stenton_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stenton-32">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> After <a href="/wiki/Reign_of_Terror" title="Reign of Terror">the Terror</a> the Fine Arts Commission, set up to safeguard national treasures in 1803, required it to be removed to Paris for display at the <a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_Napol%C3%A9on" class="mw-redirect" title="Musée Napoléon">Musée Napoléon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-stenton_32-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stenton-32">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> When <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a> abandoned his planned invasion of Britain the tapestry's propaganda value was lost and it was returned to Bayeux where the council displayed it on a winding apparatus of two cylinders.<sup id="cite_ref-stenton_32-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stenton-32">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Despite scholars' concern that the tapestry was becoming damaged the council refused to return it to the Cathedral.<sup id="cite_ref-stenton_32-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stenton-32">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stothard_Bayeux_41_42_Plate_10.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Stothard_Bayeux_41_42_Plate_10.jpg/220px-Stothard_Bayeux_41_42_Plate_10.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Stothard_Bayeux_41_42_Plate_10.jpg/330px-Stothard_Bayeux_41_42_Plate_10.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Stothard_Bayeux_41_42_Plate_10.jpg/440px-Stothard_Bayeux_41_42_Plate_10.jpg 2x" data-file-width="8600" data-file-height="6200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stothard_Bayeux_41_42_Plate_10.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Charles_Alfred_Stothard" title="Charles Alfred Stothard">Stothard</a> / <a href="/wiki/James_Basire" title="James Basire">Basire</a> engravings: scenes showing the Norman troops crossing the Channel and landing in Sussex</div></div></div>
<p>In 1816 the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Antiquaries_of_London" title="Society of Antiquaries of London">Society of Antiquaries of London</a> commissioned its historical draughtsman, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Alfred_Stothard" title="Charles Alfred Stothard">Charles Stothard</a>, to visit Bayeux to make an accurate hand-coloured <a href="/wiki/Facsimile" title="Facsimile">facsimile</a> of the tapestry. His drawings were subsequently engraved by <a href="/wiki/James_Basire" title="James Basire">James Basire</a> jr. and published by the Society in 1819–23.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Stothard's images are still of value as a record of the tapestry as it was before 19th-century restoration.
</p><p>By 1842 the tapestry was displayed in a special-purpose room in the Bibliothèque Publique. It required special storage in 1870 with the threatened invasion of Normandy in the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War" title="Franco-Prussian War">Franco-Prussian War</a> and again in 1939–1944 by the <a href="/wiki/Ahnenerbe" title="Ahnenerbe">Ahnenerbe</a> during the <a href="/wiki/German_occupation_of_France_during_World_War_II" class="mw-redirect" title="German occupation of France during World War II">German occupation of France</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Normandy_landings" title="Normandy landings">Normandy landings</a>. On 27 June 1944 the <a href="/wiki/Gestapo" title="Gestapo">Gestapo</a> took the tapestry to the <a href="/wiki/Louvre" title="Louvre">Louvre</a> and on 18 August, three days before the <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a> withdrew from Paris, <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Himmler" title="Heinrich Himmler">Himmler</a> sent a message (intercepted by <a href="/wiki/Government_Code_and_Cypher_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Government Code and Cypher School">Bletchley Park</a>) ordering it to be taken to "a place of safety", thought to be Berlin.<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> It was only on 22 August that the <a href="/wiki/SS" class="mw-redirect" title="SS">SS</a> attempted to take possession of the tapestry, by which time the Louvre was again in French hands.<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> After the <a href="/wiki/Liberation_of_Paris" title="Liberation of Paris">liberation of Paris</a>, on 25 August, the tapestry was again put on public display in the Louvre, and in 1945 it was returned to Bayeux,<sup id="cite_ref-stenton_32-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stenton-32">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> where it is exhibited at the Musée de la Tapisserie de Bayeux.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_reputation_and_history">Later reputation and history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayeux_Tapestry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Later reputation and history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The inventory listing of 1476 shows that the tapestry was being hung annually in Bayeux Cathedral for the week of the <a href="/wiki/Nativity_of_Saint_John_the_Baptist" title="Nativity of Saint John the Baptist">Feast of St John the Baptist</a>; and this was still the case in 1728, although by that time the purpose was merely to air the hanging, which was otherwise stored in a chest.<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Clearly, the work was being well cared for. In the eighteenth century, the artistry was regarded as crude or even barbarous—red and yellow multi-coloured horses upset some critics. It was thought to be unfinished because the linen was not covered with embroidery.<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> However, its exhibition in the Louvre in 1797 caused a sensation, with <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Moniteur_Universel" title="Le Moniteur Universel">Le Moniteur</a></i>, which normally dealt with foreign affairs, reporting on it on its first two pages.<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> It inspired a popular musical, <i>La Tapisserie de la Reine Mathilde</i>. It was because the tapestry was regarded as an antiquity rather than a work of art that in 1804 it was returned to Bayeux, wherein 1823 one commentator, A. L. Léchaudé d'Anisy, reported that "there is a sort of purity in its primitive forms, especially considering the state of the arts in the eleventh century".<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The tapestry was becoming a tourist attraction, with <a href="/wiki/Robert_Southey" title="Robert Southey">Robert Southey</a> complaining of the need to queue to see the work. In the 1843 <i>Hand-book for Travellers in France</i> by <a href="/wiki/John_Murray_III" title="John Murray III">John Murray III</a>, a visit was included on "Recommended Route 26 (Caen to Cherbourg via Bayeux)", and this guidebook led <a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">John Ruskin</a> to go there; he would describe the tapestry as "the most interesting thing in its way conceivable". <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a>, however, was not impressed: "It is certainly the work of amateurs; very feeble amateurs at the beginning and very heedless some of them too."<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the Second World War <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Himmler" title="Heinrich Himmler">Heinrich Himmler</a> coveted the work, regarding it as "important for our glorious and cultured Germanic history".<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2018, <a href="/wiki/President_of_France" title="President of France">French President</a> <a href="/wiki/Emmanuel_Macron" title="Emmanuel Macron">Emmanuel Macron</a> announced that the Bayeux Tapestry would be loaned to Britain for public display. It is expected to be exhibited at the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a> in London, but not before 2020. It will be the first time that it has left France in 950 years.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mysteries">Mysteries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayeux_Tapestry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mysteries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayeux_Tapestry_scene57_Harold_death.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Bayeux_Tapestry_scene57_Harold_death.jpg/220px-Bayeux_Tapestry_scene57_Harold_death.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="192" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Bayeux_Tapestry_scene57_Harold_death.jpg/330px-Bayeux_Tapestry_scene57_Harold_death.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Bayeux_Tapestry_scene57_Harold_death.jpg/440px-Bayeux_Tapestry_scene57_Harold_death.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2948" data-file-height="2568" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayeux_Tapestry_scene57_Harold_death.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Harold's death. Legend above: <i>Harold rex interfectus est</i>, "King Harold is killed"</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Harold_arrow,_Bayeux_tapestry_detail.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Harold_arrow%2C_Bayeux_tapestry_detail.jpeg/220px-Harold_arrow%2C_Bayeux_tapestry_detail.jpeg" decoding="async" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Harold_arrow%2C_Bayeux_tapestry_detail.jpeg/330px-Harold_arrow%2C_Bayeux_tapestry_detail.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Harold_arrow%2C_Bayeux_tapestry_detail.jpeg/440px-Harold_arrow%2C_Bayeux_tapestry_detail.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="464" data-file-height="261" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Harold_arrow,_Bayeux_tapestry_detail.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Detail of arrow</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aelfgyva.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Aelfgyva.jpg/220px-Aelfgyva.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="261" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Aelfgyva.jpg/330px-Aelfgyva.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Aelfgyva.jpg/440px-Aelfgyva.jpg 2x" data-file-width="608" data-file-height="720" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aelfgyva.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Ubi unus clericus et Ælfgyva</i></div></div></div>
<p>The depiction of events on the tapestry raises several mysteries:
</p>
<ul><li>The identification of <a href="/wiki/Harold_Godwinson" title="Harold Godwinson">Harold II of England</a> in the vignette depicting his death is disputed. Some recent historians disagree with the traditional view that Harold is the figure struck in the eye with an arrow. The view that it is Harold is supported by the fact that the words <i>Harold Rex</i> (King Harold) appear right above the figure's head. However, the arrow is a later addition following a period of repair,<sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> as can be seen by comparison with <a href="/wiki/Bernard_de_Montfaucon" title="Bernard de Montfaucon">Bernard de Montfaucon</a>'s engravings of the tapestry as it was in 1729, in which the arrow is absent (see illustration above). However, needle holes in the linen do suggest that something had originally been in the place of the arrow, though it may have been a lance rather than an arrow.<sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> A figure is slain with a sword in the subsequent plate, and the phrase above the figure refers to Harold's death (<i>interfectus est</i>, "he is slain"). This would appear to be more consistent with the labeling used elsewhere in the work. It was common medieval <a href="/wiki/Iconography" title="Iconography">iconography</a> that a <a href="/wiki/Perjurer" class="mw-redirect" title="Perjurer">perjurer</a> was to die with a weapon through the eye. Therefore, the tapestry might be said to emphasize William's rightful claim to the throne by depicting Harold as an oath breaker. Whether he actually died in this way remains a mystery and is much debated.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>There is a panel with what appears to be a <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">clergyman</a> touching or possibly striking a woman's face. No one knows the significance of this scene or the caption above it: <i>ubi unus clericus et Ælfgyva</i> ("where [or <i>in which</i>] a certain cleric and Ælfgyva"), where Ælfgyva is the Latinised spelling of <a href="/wiki/%C3%86lfgifu" title="Ælfgifu">Ælfgifu</a>, a popular Anglo-Saxon woman's name (literally "elf-gift").<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The use of the <a href="/wiki/Grapheme" title="Grapheme">grapheme</a> <a href="/wiki/%C3%86" title="Æ">Æ</a> shows familiarity with English spelling.<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> There are two naked male figures in the border below this figure; the one directly below the figure is squatting and displaying <a href="/wiki/Genitalia" class="mw-redirect" title="Genitalia">genitalia</a>, a scene that was frequently censored in former reproductions. However, similar naked figures appear elsewhere in the lower border where there seems to be no connection at all with the main action.<sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Harold had a younger sister named Ælfgifu (her name is spelt Alveva in the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_book" class="mw-redirect" title="Domesday book">Domesday book</a>) who was possibly promised to William by Harold or even betrothed to him, but she died c. 1066, prior to the invasion.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>At least two panels of the tapestry are missing, perhaps even another 6.4&#160;m (7.0&#160;yd) in total. This missing area may have included William's coronation.<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reliability">Reliability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayeux_Tapestry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Reliability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Bayeux Tapestry was probably commissioned by the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Normandy" title="House of Normandy">House of Normandy</a> and essentially depicts a Norman viewpoint. However, Harold is shown as brave, and his soldiers are not belittled. Throughout, William is described as <i>dux</i> ("duke"), whereas Harold, also called <i>dux</i> up to his coronation, is subsequently called <i>rex</i> ("king").<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The fact that the narrative extensively covers Harold's activities in Normandy (in 1064) indicates that the intention was to show a strong relationship between that expedition and the Norman Conquest starting two years later. It is for this reason that the tapestry is generally seen by modern scholars as an <a href="/wiki/Apologia" title="Apologia">apologia</a> for the Norman Conquest.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayeux_Tapestry_scene29-30-31_Harold_coronation.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Bayeux_Tapestry_scene29-30-31_Harold_coronation.jpg/220px-Bayeux_Tapestry_scene29-30-31_Harold_coronation.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="98" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Bayeux_Tapestry_scene29-30-31_Harold_coronation.jpg/330px-Bayeux_Tapestry_scene29-30-31_Harold_coronation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Bayeux_Tapestry_scene29-30-31_Harold_coronation.jpg/440px-Bayeux_Tapestry_scene29-30-31_Harold_coronation.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3564" data-file-height="1580" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayeux_Tapestry_scene29-30-31_Harold_coronation.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Coronation" title="Coronation">Coronation</a> of Harold</div></div></div>
<p>The tapestry's narration seems to place stress on Harold's oath to William, although its rationale is not made clear.<sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Norman sources claim that the English succession was being pledged to William, but English sources give varied accounts.<sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Today it is thought that the Norman sources are to be preferred.<sup id="cite_ref-bates_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bates-39">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Both the tapestry and Norman sources<sup id="cite_ref-poitiers_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-poitiers-40">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> name Stigand, the excommunicated archbishop of Canterbury, as the man who crowned Harold, possibly to discredit Harold's kingship; one English source<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> suggests that he was crowned by <a href="/wiki/Ealdred_(bishop)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ealdred (bishop)">Ealdred</a>, archbishop of York, and favoured by the papacy, making Harold's position as legitimate king more secure. Contemporary scholarship has not decided the matter, although it is generally thought that Ealdred performed the coronation.<sup id="cite_ref-gibbs_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gibbs-42">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cowdrey_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cowdrey-43">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although political propaganda or personal emphasis may have somewhat distorted the historical accuracy of the story, the Bayeux Tapestry constitutes a visual record of medieval arms, apparel, and other objects unlike any other artifact surviving from this period. There is no attempt at continuity between scenes, either in individuals' appearance or clothing. The knights carry shields, but show no system of hereditary <a href="/wiki/Coats_of_arms" class="mw-redirect" title="Coats of arms">coats of arms</a>—the beginnings of modern heraldic structure were in place, but would not become standard until the middle of the 12th century.<sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> It has been noted that the warriors are depicted fighting with bare hands, while other sources indicate the general use of gloves in battle and hunt.
</p><p>American historian <a href="/wiki/Stephen_D._White" title="Stephen D. White">Stephen D. White</a>, in a study of the tapestry,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> has "cautioned against reading it as an English or Norman story, showing how the animal fables visible in the borders may instead offer a commentary on the dangers of conflict and the futility of pursuing power".<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Artistic_context">Artistic context</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayeux_Tapestry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Artistic context">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tapestry fragments have been found in Scandinavia dating from the ninth century and it is thought that Norman and Anglo-Saxon embroidery developed from this sort of work. Examples are to be found in the <a href="/wiki/Oseberg_ship#Grave_goods" class="mw-redirect" title="Oseberg ship">grave goods of the Oseberg ship</a> and the <a href="/wiki/%C3%96verhogdal_tapestries" title="Överhogdal tapestries">Överhogdal tapestries</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A monastic text from <a href="/wiki/Ely,_Cambridgeshire" title="Ely, Cambridgeshire">Ely</a>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Liber_Eliensis" title="Liber Eliensis">Liber Eliensis</a></i>, mentions a woven narrative wall-hanging commemorating the deeds of <a href="/wiki/Byrhtnoth" title="Byrhtnoth">Byrhtnoth</a>, killed in 991. Wall-hangings were common by the tenth century with English and Norman texts particularly commending the skill of Anglo-Saxon seamstresses. Mural paintings imitating draperies still exist in France and Italy and there are twelfth-century mentions of other wall-hangings in Normandy and France. A poem by <a href="/wiki/Baldric_of_Dol" title="Baldric of Dol">Baldric of Dol</a> might even describe the Bayeux Tapestry itself.<sup id="cite_ref-musset_21-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musset-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The Bayeux Tapestry was therefore not unique at the time it was created: rather it is remarkable for being the sole surviving example of medieval narrative needlework.<sup id="cite_ref-digby_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-digby-46">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Replicas_and_continuations">Replicas and continuations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayeux_Tapestry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Replicas and continuations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayeux_Tapestry_replica_in_Reading_Museum.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Bayeux_Tapestry_replica_in_Reading_Museum.jpg/220px-Bayeux_Tapestry_replica_in_Reading_Museum.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Bayeux_Tapestry_replica_in_Reading_Museum.jpg/330px-Bayeux_Tapestry_replica_in_Reading_Museum.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Bayeux_Tapestry_replica_in_Reading_Museum.jpg/440px-Bayeux_Tapestry_replica_in_Reading_Museum.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3195" data-file-height="2396" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayeux_Tapestry_replica_in_Reading_Museum.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Start of the Bayeux Tapestry replica in <a href="/wiki/Reading_Museum" title="Reading Museum">Reading Museum</a></div></div></div>
<p>A number of replicas of the Bayeux Tapestry have been created.
</p>
<ul><li>Through the collaboration of <a href="/wiki/William_Morris" title="William Morris">William Morris</a> with textile manufacturer <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Wardle_(industrialist)" title="Thomas Wardle (industrialist)">Thomas Wardle</a>, Wardle's wife <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Wardle" title="Elizabeth Wardle">Elizabeth</a>, who was an accomplished seamstress, embarked on creating a reproduction in 1885.<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> She organised some 37 women in her <i>Leek School of Art Embroidery</i><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;note 8&#93;</a></sup> to collaborate working from a full-scale water-colour facsimile drawing provided by the <a href="/wiki/South_Kensington_Museum" class="mw-redirect" title="South Kensington Museum">South Kensington Museum</a>. The full-size replica was finished in 1886 and is now exhibited in <a href="/wiki/Reading_Museum" title="Reading Museum">Reading Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Reading,_Berkshire" title="Reading, Berkshire">Reading, Berkshire</a>, England.<sup id="cite_ref-reading_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reading-48">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> The naked figure in the original tapestry (in the border below the Ælfgyva figure) is depicted wearing a brief garment because the drawing which was worked from was similarly <a href="/wiki/Bowdlerised" class="mw-redirect" title="Bowdlerised">bowdlerised</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hicks_20-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hicks-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Ray Dugan of <a href="/wiki/University_of_Waterloo" title="University of Waterloo">University of Waterloo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, Canada, completed a stitched replica in 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Since its completion, it has been displayed in various museums and galleries in Canada and the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2000, the Danish-based Bayeux Group, part of the Viking Group <a href="/wiki/Lindholm_H%C3%B8je" title="Lindholm Høje">Lindholm Høje</a>, began making an accurate replica of the Bayeux Tapestry, using the original sewing techniques.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> The replica was completed in June 2014 and went on permanent exhibition at <a href="/wiki/B%C3%B8rglum_Abbey" title="Børglum Abbey">Børglum Abbey</a> in May 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Dr. E. D. Wheeler, former judge and former dean at <a href="/wiki/Oglethorpe_University" title="Oglethorpe University">Oglethorpe University</a>, commissioned a hand-painted, full-size replica of the Bayeux Tapestry and donated it to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_West_Georgia" title="University of West Georgia">University of West Georgia</a> in <a href="/wiki/Carrollton,_Georgia" title="Carrollton, Georgia">Carrollton</a> in 1994. In 2014, the replica was acquired by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Georgia" title="University of North Georgia">University of North Georgia</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dahlonega,_Georgia" title="Dahlonega, Georgia">Dahlonega</a>.</li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1066_Medieval_Mosaic_(Bayeux_Tapestry)_sections.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/1066_Medieval_Mosaic_%28Bayeux_Tapestry%29_sections.jpg/220px-1066_Medieval_Mosaic_%28Bayeux_Tapestry%29_sections.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/1066_Medieval_Mosaic_%28Bayeux_Tapestry%29_sections.jpg/330px-1066_Medieval_Mosaic_%28Bayeux_Tapestry%29_sections.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/1066_Medieval_Mosaic_%28Bayeux_Tapestry%29_sections.jpg/440px-1066_Medieval_Mosaic_%28Bayeux_Tapestry%29_sections.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4896" data-file-height="3264" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1066_Medieval_Mosaic_(Bayeux_Tapestry)_sections.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sections of the 1066 Medieval Mosaic re-creation in New Zealand</div></div></div>
<ul><li>An approximately half scale mosaic version of the Bayeux Tapestry was formerly on display at <a href="/wiki/Geraldine,_New_Zealand" title="Geraldine, New Zealand">Geraldine, New Zealand</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-linton_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-linton-53">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> It was made up of 1.5 million 7&#160;mm<sup>2</sup> pieces of spring steel—off-cuts from patterning disks of knitting machines—and was created by Michael Linton over a period of twenty years from 1979. The work was in 32 sections, and included a hypothetical reconstruction of the missing final section leading up to William the Conqueror's coronation at <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Abbey" title="Westminster Abbey">Westminster Abbey</a> on Christmas Day, 1066.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Jason Welch, a woodcarver from <a href="/wiki/North_Creake" title="North Creake">North Creake</a>, Norfolk, England, created a replica of the tapestry between 2011 and 2014 in carved and painted wooden relief on 25 five-foot planks. He undertook the project to help cope with the grief of losing his 16-year-old son.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Mia Hansson, from Skanör, Sweden, started a reproduction on 13 July 2016. As of February 2019<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bayeux_Tapestry&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup> she had completed approximately 16 metres, saying that she expected to finish in some 8 years.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Other modern artists have attempted to complete the work by creating panels depicting subsequent events up to William's coronation, though the actual content of the missing panels is unknown. In 1997, the embroidery artist Jan Messent completed a reconstruction showing William accepting the surrender of English nobles at <a href="/wiki/Berkhamsted" title="Berkhamsted">Berkhamsted</a> (<i>Beorcham</i>), Hertfordshire, and his coronation.<sup id="cite_ref-berko-history_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-berko-history-57">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-berko-castle_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-berko-castle-58">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> In early 2013, 416 residents of <a href="/wiki/Alderney" title="Alderney">Alderney</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a> finished a continuation including William's coronation and the building of the <a href="/wiki/Tower_of_London" title="Tower of London">Tower of London</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc-alderney_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-alderney-60">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayeux_Tapestry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Street_art_in_Bayeux_about_tapestry.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Street_art_in_Bayeux_about_tapestry.jpg/170px-Street_art_in_Bayeux_about_tapestry.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Street_art_in_Bayeux_about_tapestry.jpg/255px-Street_art_in_Bayeux_about_tapestry.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Street_art_in_Bayeux_about_tapestry.jpg/340px-Street_art_in_Bayeux_about_tapestry.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2448" data-file-height="3264" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Street_art_in_Bayeux_about_tapestry.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Street art in Bayeux referring to the Tapestry</div></div></div>
<p>Because it resembles a modern <a href="/wiki/Comic_strip" title="Comic strip">comic strip</a> or movie <a href="/wiki/Storyboard" title="Storyboard">storyboard</a>, is widely recognised, and is so distinctive in its artistic style, the Bayeux Tapestry has frequently been used or reimagined in a variety of different popular culture contexts. George Wingfield Digby wrote in 1957:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>It was designed to tell a story to a largely illiterate public; it is like a strip cartoon, racy, emphatic, colourful, with a good deal of blood and thunder and some ribaldry.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>It has been cited by <a href="/wiki/Scott_McCloud" title="Scott McCloud">Scott McCloud</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Understanding_Comics" title="Understanding Comics">Understanding Comics</a></i> as an example of early <a href="/wiki/Sequential_art" title="Sequential art">sequential-narrative art</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Talbot" title="Bryan Talbot">Bryan Talbot</a>, a British comic book artist, has called it "the first known <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_British_comic" class="mw-redirect" title="History of the British comic">British comic strip</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It has inspired many modern <a href="/wiki/Editorial_cartoon" class="mw-redirect" title="Editorial cartoon">political</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Cartoon" title="Cartoon">cartoons</a>, including:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/John_Hassall_(illustrator)" title="John Hassall (illustrator)">John Hassall</a>'s satirical pastiche <i>Ye Berlyn Tapestrie</i>, published in 1915, which tells the story of the German invasion of Belgium in August 1914<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rea_Irvin" title="Rea Irvin">Rea Irvin</a>'s cover for the <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">New Yorker</a></i> magazine of 15 July 1944 marking <a href="/wiki/Normandy_landings" title="Normandy landings">D-Day</a><sup id="cite_ref-newyorker_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newyorker-65">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/George_Gale_(cartoonist)" title="George Gale (cartoonist)">George Gale</a>'s pastiche chronicling the saga leading up to Britain's entry into the <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Community" title="European Economic Community">European Economic Community</a>, published across six pages in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a>'</i>s "Europa" supplement on 1 January 1973<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The tapestry has inspired modern embroideries, most notably and directly:
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Overlord_Embroidery" title="Overlord Embroidery">Overlord Embroidery</a> (1974), commemorating <a href="/wiki/Operation_Overlord" title="Operation Overlord">Operation Overlord</a> and the <a href="/wiki/D-Day" class="mw-redirect" title="D-Day">Normandy landings</a> of 1944, now at <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth" title="Portsmouth">Portsmouth</a></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Prestonpans_Tapestry" title="Prestonpans Tapestry">Prestonpans Tapestry</a> (2010), which chronicles the events surrounding the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Prestonpans" title="Battle of Prestonpans">Battle of Prestonpans</a> in 1745</li></ul>
<p>Other embroideries more loosely inspired by it include the <a href="/wiki/Hastings_Embroidery" title="Hastings Embroidery">Hastings Embroidery</a> (1966), the <a href="/wiki/New_World_Tapestry" title="New World Tapestry">New World Tapestry</a> (1980–2000), the <a href="/wiki/Quaker_Tapestry" title="Quaker Tapestry">Quaker Tapestry</a> (1981–89), the <a href="/wiki/Great_Tapestry_of_Scotland" title="Great Tapestry of Scotland">Great Tapestry of Scotland</a> (2013), the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Diaspora_Tapestry" title="Scottish Diaspora Tapestry">Scottish Diaspora Tapestry</a> (2014–15), <i><a href="/wiki/Magna_Carta_(An_Embroidery)" title="Magna Carta (An Embroidery)">Magna Carta (An Embroidery)</a></i> (2014–15), and (in this case a true woven tapestry) the <a href="/wiki/Game_of_Thrones_Tapestry" title="Game of Thrones Tapestry">Game of Thrones Tapestry</a> (2017).
</p><p>A number of films have used sections of the tapestry in their opening credits or closing titles, including <a href="/wiki/Disney" class="mw-redirect" title="Disney">Disney</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Bedknobs_and_Broomsticks" title="Bedknobs and Broomsticks">Bedknobs and Broomsticks</a></i>, Anthony Mann's <i><a href="/wiki/El_Cid_(film)" title="El Cid (film)">El Cid</a></i>, Franco Zeffirelli's <i><a href="/wiki/Hamlet" title="Hamlet">Hamlet</a></i>, Frank Cassenti's <i><a href="/wiki/La_Chanson_de_Roland" class="mw-redirect" title="La Chanson de Roland">La Chanson de Roland</a></i>, Kevin Reynolds' <i><a href="/wiki/Robin_Hood:_Prince_of_Thieves" title="Robin Hood: Prince of Thieves">Robin Hood: Prince of Thieves</a></i>, and Richard Fleischer's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Vikings_(film)" title="The Vikings (film)">The Vikings</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The design and embroidery of the tapestry form one of the narrative strands of <a href="/wiki/Marta_Morazzoni" title="Marta Morazzoni">Marta Morazzoni</a>'s 1988 novella <i>The Invention of Truth</i>.
</p><p>The tapestry is referred to in <a href="/wiki/Tony_Kushner" title="Tony Kushner">Tony Kushner</a>'s play <i><a href="/wiki/Angels_in_America:_A_Gay_Fantasia_on_National_Themes" class="mw-redirect" title="Angels in America: A Gay Fantasia on National Themes">Angels in America</a></i>. The apocryphal account of Queen Matilda's creation of the tapestry is used, perhaps in order to demonstrate that Louis, one of the main characters, holds himself to mythological standards.<sup id="cite_ref-sparknote_angels_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sparknote_angels-68">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

Retrieved <span class="nowrap">30 October</span> 2014</span>. <q>Louis's problem is exacerbated by his tendency towards abstraction and his unreasonably high standards for himself. In Scene Three, he tells Emily about La Reine Mathilde, who supposedly created the Bayeux Tapestry. Louis describes La Reine's unceasing devotion to William the Conqueror and laments his own comparative lack of devotion. But as critic Allen J. Frantzen has pointed out, this popular story about Mathilde and the tapestry is wrong—it was actually created in England decades after the conquest. Louis, then, is holding himself to a mythological standard of loyalty, and he curses himself based on a positively unreal example. This is part of a larger pattern of excessive guilt and harshness toward himself, which, paradoxically, prevents him from judging his own weaknesses accurately and trying to correct them. Because no one could possibly live up to Mathilde's example, Louis initially justifies his moral failure. Later, in Perestroika, he will arrive at a more genuine remorse and an honest understanding of what he has done.</q></cite><span title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Ajournal&amp;rft.genre=unknown&amp;rft.jtitle=SparkNotes.com&amp;rft.atitle=SparkNote+on+Angels+in+America&amp;rft.au=SparkNotes+Editors&amp;rft_id=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.sparknotes.com%2Fdrama%2Fangels%2Fsection3.rhtml&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3ABayeux+Tapestry" class="Z3988"></span><span class="cs1-maint citation-comment">CS1 maint: extra text: authors list (<a href="/wiki/Category:CS1_maint:_extra_text:_authors_list" title="Category:CS1 maint: extra text: authors list">link</a>)</span><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886058088"/></span>
