<p><b>European emigration</b> can be defined as subsequent <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Europe" title="Ethnic groups in Europe">emigration waves from the European continent</a> to other continents. The origins of the various European <a href="/wiki/Diaspora" title="Diaspora">diasporas</a><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> can be traced to the people who left the European nation states or stateless ethnic communities on the European continent.<sup id="cite_ref-books.google.co.uk_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.co.uk-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From 1815 to 1932, 60&#160;million people left Europe (with many returning home), primarily to "areas of European settlement" in the Americas (especially to the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a><sup id="cite_ref-books.google.co.uk_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.co.uk-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>), <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-MascitelliMycak2016_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MascitelliMycak2016-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-European_Migration_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-European_Migration-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
These populations also multiplied rapidly in their new habitat; much more so than the populations of Africa and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. As a result, on the eve of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, 38% of the world's total population was of European ancestry.<sup id="cite_ref-European_Migration_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-European_Migration-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>More contemporary, European emigration can also refer to emigration from one European country to another, especially in the context of the internal mobility in the European Union (<a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_movement_for_workers_in_the_European_Union" title="Freedom of movement for workers in the European Union">intra-EU mobility</a>) or mobility within the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="Eurasian Union">Eurasian Union</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_emigration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="8th_-_early_5th_century_BC:_Greeks">8th - early 5th century BC: Greeks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_emigration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 8th - early 5th century BC: Greeks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:272px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Greek_Colonization_Archaic_Period.png" class="image"><img alt="Map of Greek coastal settlements throughout the Mediterranean and Black Sea" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Greek_Colonization_Archaic_Period.png/270px-Greek_Colonization_Archaic_Period.png" decoding="async" width="270" height="153" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Greek_Colonization_Archaic_Period.png/405px-Greek_Colonization_Archaic_Period.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Greek_Colonization_Archaic_Period.png/540px-Greek_Colonization_Archaic_Period.png 2x" data-file-width="684" data-file-height="388" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Greek_Colonization_Archaic_Period.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of Greek territories and colonies during the Archaic period. (800–480 BC)</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Archaic_Greece" title="Archaic Greece">Archaic Greece</a>, trading and colonizing activities of the Greek tribes from the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Asia Minor</a> propagated <a href="/wiki/Greek_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Greek culture">Greek culture</a>, religion and <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">language</a> around the Mediterranean and <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> basins. Greek <a href="/wiki/City-state" title="City-state">city-states</a> were established in Southern Europe, northern <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a> and the Black Sea coast, and the Greeks founded over 400 colonies in these areas.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> 
<a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a>'s conquest of the <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Achaemenid Empire</a> marked the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">Hellenistic period</a>, which was characterized by a new wave of Greek colonization in Asia and Africa; the Greek ruling classes established their presence in <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_dynasty" title="Ptolemaic dynasty">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seleucid_Empire" title="Seleucid Empire">southwest Asia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Indo-Greek_Kingdom" title="Indo-Greek Kingdom">Northwest India</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
Many Greeks migrated to the new Hellenistic cities founded in Alexander's wake, as geographically-dispersed as <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan" title="Uzbekistan">Uzbekistan</a><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwait</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1450-1800">1450-1800</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_emigration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1450-1800">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colonial_settlers">Colonial settlers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_emigration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Colonial settlers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The European continent has been a central part of a complex <a href="/w/index.php?title=Migration_system&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Migration system (page does not exist)">migration system</a>, which included swaths of North Africa, the Middle East and <a href="/wiki/Asia-Minor" class="mw-redirect" title="Asia-Minor">Asia Minor</a> well before the <a href="/wiki/Modern_Era" class="mw-redirect" title="Modern Era">Modern Era</a>. Yet, only the population growth of the late <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> allowed for larger population movements, inside and outside of the continent.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus" title="Voyages of Christopher Columbus">discovery of the Americas</a> in 1492 stimulated a steady stream of voluntary migration from Europe. About 200,000 <a href="/wiki/Spanish_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Spanish people">Spaniards</a> settled in their American colonies prior to 1600, a small settlement compared to the 3 to 4&#160;million <a href="/wiki/Amerindians" class="mw-redirect" title="Amerindians">Amerindians</a> who lived in <a href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Spanish colonization of the Americas">Spanish territory in the Americas</a>.
</p><p>Roughly one and a half million Europeans settled in the New World between 1500 and 1800 (see table). The Table excludes European immigrants to the Spanish Empire from 1650-1800 and to Brazil from 1700 to 1800.  While the absolute number of European emigrants during the <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_period" title="Early modern period">early modern period</a> was very small compared to later waves of migration in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, the relative size of these early modern migrations was nevertheless substantial.
</p><p>During the 1500s, Spain and Portugal sent a steady flow of <a href="/wiki/Government_official" class="mw-redirect" title="Government official">government</a> and church officials, members of the lesser <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">nobility</a>, people from the working classes and their families averaging roughly three-thousand people per year from a population of around eight million. A total of around 437,000 left Spain in the 150-year period from 1500 to 1650 to <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Caribbean Islands">Caribbean Islands</a>. It has been estimated that over 1.86 million Spaniards emigrated to Latin America in the period between 1492 and 1824, one million  in the 18th century, with millions more continuing to immigrate following independence.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Between 1500 and 1700 only 100,000 <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_people" title="Portuguese people">Portuguese</a> crossed the Atlantic to settle in  <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>. However,  with the discovery of numerous highly productive gold mines in the Minas Gerais region, the Portuguese emigration to Brazil increased by five fold. From 1500, when the Portuguese reached Brazil, until its independence in 1822, from 500,000 to 700,000 Portuguese settled in Brazil, 600,000 of whom arrived in the 18th century alone.<sup id="cite_ref-Levy_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levy-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> From 1700 til 1760 over half a million Portuguese immigrants entered Brazil. In the 18th century, thanks to the gold rush, the capital of the province of <a href="/wiki/Minas_Gerais" title="Minas Gerais">Minas Gerais</a>, the town of Villa Rica (today, <a href="/wiki/Ouro_Preto" title="Ouro Preto">Ouro Preto</a>) became for a time one of the most populous cities in the New World. This massive influx of Portuguese immigration and influence created a city which remains to this day, one of the best examples of 18th century European architecture in the Americas.<sup id="cite_ref-books.google.co.uk_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.co.uk-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
However, the development of the mining economy in the 18th century raised wages and employment opportunities in the Portuguese colony and emigration increased: in the 18th century alone, about 600,000 Portuguese settled in Brazil, a mass emigration given that <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> had a population of only 2&#160;million people.<sup id="cite_ref-1944-_1988_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1944-_1988-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Between one-half and two-thirds of European immigrants to the <a href="/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies" title="Thirteen Colonies">Thirteen Colonies</a> between the 1630s and the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a> came under indentures.<sup id="cite_ref-Galenson_1984:_1_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Galenson_1984:_1-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The practice was sufficiently common that the <a href="/wiki/Habeas_Corpus_Act_1679" title="Habeas Corpus Act 1679">Habeas Corpus Act 1679</a>, in part, prevented imprisonments overseas; it also made provisions for those with existing transportation contracts and those "praying to be transported" in lieu of remaining in prison upon conviction.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> In any case, while half the European immigrants to the <a href="/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies" title="Thirteen Colonies">Thirteen Colonies</a> had been indentured servants, at any one time they were outnumbered by workers who had never been indentured, or whose indenture had expired. Free wage labour was more common for Europeans in the colonies.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Indentured persons were numerically important mostly in the region from <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> north to <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>. Other colonies saw far fewer of them. The total number of European immigrants to all 13 colonies before 1775 was about 500,000-550,000; of these 55,000 were <a href="/wiki/Penal_transportation" title="Penal transportation">involuntary prisoners</a>. Of the 450,000 or so European arrivals who came voluntarily, Tomlins estimates that 48% were indentured.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>  About 75% were under the age of 25. The age of legal adulthood for men was 24 years; those over 24 generally came on contracts lasting about 3 years.<sup id="cite_ref-Tomlins_2001_at_notes_31,_42,_66_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tomlins_2001_at_notes_31,_42,_66-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Regarding the children who came, Gary Nash reports that, "many of the servants were actually nephews, nieces, cousins and children of friends of emigrating <a href="/wiki/English_people" title="English people">Englishmen</a>, who paid their passage in return for their labour once in America."<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Figures for immigration the Spanish Empire 1650-1800 and Brazil 1700-1800 are not given in the Table. 
</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, immigration was dominated by <a href="/wiki/British_people" title="British people">British</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irish_people" title="Irish people">Irish</a>, French and other Northern Europeans.<sup id="cite_ref-Boris_Fauto._Fazer_a_América:_a_imigração_em_massa_para_a_América_Latina_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boris_Fauto._Fazer_a_América:_a_imigração_em_massa_para_a_América_Latina-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> From 1650 to 1800, almost 800,000 Spaniards emigrated to the New World, especially under the Bourbon Dynasty in the 18th century. Emigration to <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">New France</a> laid the origins of modern <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, with important early immigration of colonists from Northern France.<sup id="cite_ref-1944-_1988_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1944-_1988-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-independence_emigration">Post-independence emigration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_emigration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Post-independence emigration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mass European emigration to the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> took place in the 19th and 20th centuries. This was the effect of a dramatic <a href="/wiki/Demographic_transition" title="Demographic transition">demographic transition</a> in 19th-century-Europe, subsequent wars and political changes on the continent. From the end of the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a> until 1920, some 60&#160;million Europeans emigrated. Of these, 71% went to North America, 21% to <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a> (mainly <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>) and 7% to Australia. About 11&#160;million of these people went to Latin America, of whom 38% were <a href="/wiki/Italian_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian people">Italians</a>, 28% were Spaniards and 11% were Portuguese.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Between 1821 and 1890, 9.55&#160;million Europeans settled in the United States, mainly <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">Germans</a>, Irish, English, Scandinavians, Italians and Scots. 18 million more arrived from 1890 to 1914, including 2.5 million from Canada. Despite the large number of immigrants arriving, people born outside of the United States formed a relatively small number of the U.S. population: in 1910, foreigners accounted for 14.7 percent of the country's population or 13.5 million people. The huge number of immigrants to Argentina, which had a much smaller population, had a much greater impact on the country's ethnic composition. By 1914, 30% of Argentina's population was foreign-born, with 12% of its population born in Italy, the largest immigrant group. Next was Canada: by 1881, 14% of Canada's population was foreign-born, and the proportion increased to 22% in 1921. 
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Carmen_Miranda_1941.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Carmen_Miranda_1941.JPG/220px-Carmen_Miranda_1941.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="286" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Carmen_Miranda_1941.JPG/330px-Carmen_Miranda_1941.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Carmen_Miranda_1941.JPG/440px-Carmen_Miranda_1941.JPG 2x" data-file-width="957" data-file-height="1245" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Carmen_Miranda_1941.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Singer <a href="/wiki/Carmen_Miranda" title="Carmen Miranda">Carmen Miranda</a>, nicknamed "the Brazilian bombshell", was born in Portugal and emigrated to Brazil in 1910, when she was ten months old.</div></div></div>
<p>In Brazil, the proportion of immigrants in the national population was much smaller, and immigrants tended to be concentrated in the central and Southern parts of the country. The proportion of foreigners in Brazil peaked in 1920, at just 7 percent or 2 million people, mostly Italians, Portuguese, Germans and Spaniards; however, the influx of 4 million European immigrants between 1880 and 1920 significantly altered the racial composition of the country.<sup id="cite_ref-Boris_Fauto._Fazer_a_América:_a_imigração_em_massa_para_a_América_Latina_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boris_Fauto._Fazer_a_América:_a_imigração_em_massa_para_a_América_Latina-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> From 1901 to 1920, immigration was responsible for only 7 percent of Brazilian population growth, but in the years of high immigration, from 1891 to 1900, the share was as high as 30 percent (higher than Argentina's 26 percent in the 1880s).<sup id="cite_ref-Sánchez-Alonso_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sánchez-Alonso-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The countries in the Americas that received a major wave of European immigrants from 1820s to the early 1930s were: the United States (32.5&#160;million), Argentina (6.5&#160;million), Canada (5.1&#160;million), Brazil (4.4&#160;million), <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> (1.4&#160;million), <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a> (713,000).<sup id="cite_ref-Baily-Miguez-2003_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baily-Miguez-2003-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Other countries that received a more modest immigration flow (accounting for less than 10 percent of total European emigration to <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a>) were: Mexico (270,000), <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a> (126,000), <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> (90,000), Puerto Rico (62,000), <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> (30,000), and Paraguay (21,000).<sup id="cite_ref-Baily-Miguez-2003_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baily-Miguez-2003-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sánchez-Alonso_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sánchez-Alonso-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_emigration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_emigration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:272px;"><a href="/wiki/File:European_Ancestry_Large.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/European_Ancestry_Large.svg/270px-European_Ancestry_Large.svg.png" decoding="async" width="270" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/European_Ancestry_Large.svg/405px-European_Ancestry_Large.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/European_Ancestry_Large.svg/540px-European_Ancestry_Large.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="853" data-file-height="546" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:European_Ancestry_Large.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map showing areas of European settlement.</div></div></div>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Discovery" title="Age of Discovery">Age of Discovery</a>, different ethnic European communities began to emigrate out of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> with particular concentrations in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a>; particularly <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> where they came to constitute a European-descended majority population.<sup id="cite_ref-World_Civilizations:_Volume_II_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-World_Civilizations:_Volume_II-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BritDis47_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BritDis47-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Marsh254_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh254-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> 
It is important to note, however, that these statistics rely on identification with a European ethnic group in censuses, and as such are subjective (especially in the case of mixed origins).
Nations and regions outside of Europe with significant populations:<sup id="cite_ref-Ethnic_groups_by_country_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ethnic_groups_by_country-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_emigration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Total European population—approximately 300,000,000
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/1871_Canadian_Census" title="1871 Canadian Census">first Canadian census</a> in 1871, 98.5% chose a European origin with it slightly decreasing to 96.3% declared in 1971.<sup id="cite_ref-Ethnic_origins_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ethnic_origins-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-justice.gc.ca_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-justice.gc.ca-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Canada_2016_Census" class="mw-redirect" title="Canada 2016 Census">2016 census</a>, 19,683,320 or 53.0% self-identified with a European ethnic origin, the largest being of British Isles origins (11,211,850). Individually, they are <a href="/wiki/English_Canadians" title="English Canadians">English</a> (6,320,085), <a href="/wiki/French_Canadians" title="French Canadians">French</a> (4,680,820), <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Canadians" title="Scottish Canadians">Scottish</a> (4,799,005), <a href="/wiki/Irish_Canadians" title="Irish Canadians">Irish</a> (4,627,000), <a href="/wiki/German_Canadians" title="German Canadians">German</a> (3,322,405), <a href="/wiki/Italian_Canadians" title="Italian Canadians">Italian</a> (1,587,965).<sup id="cite_ref-Census_Profile,_2016_Census_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Census_Profile,_2016_Census-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> However, there is an undercount with 11,135,965 choosing "Canadian".<sup id="cite_ref-Census_Profile,_2016_Census_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Census_Profile,_2016_Census-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At the time of the first U.S. census in 1790, 80.7% of the <a href="/wiki/American_people" class="mw-redirect" title="American people">American people</a> self-identified as white, where it remained above that level before declining at the 1990 census. However, numerically it increased from 3.17 million (1790) to 199.6 million exactly two hundred years later.<sup id="cite_ref-USHispanicOrigin_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USHispanicOrigin-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Today, <a href="/wiki/European_Americans" title="European Americans">European Americans</a> are the largest panethnic group in the United States. The 2010 census data revealed that 72.4% of the population, or 223,800,000 people self-identified as white.<sup id="cite_ref-c2010_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-c2010-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mexicans_of_European_descent" title="Mexicans of European descent">European Mexican</a> - estimated by the government in 2010 as 47% of the population (56 million) using phenotypical traits (skin color) as the criteria.<sup id="cite_ref-Marzo_DiaIntElimDiscRacial_INACCSS_2017_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marzo_DiaIntElimDiscRacial_INACCSS_2017-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> If the criteria used is the presence of blond hair, it is 18%<sup id="cite_ref-journals.jstor.com_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-journals.jstor.com-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-journals.plos.org_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-journals.plos.org-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> - 23%.<sup id="cite_ref-scielo.org.mx_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scielo.org.mx-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Caribbean_and_South_America">Caribbean and South America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_emigration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Caribbean and South America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Argentina: 79% of the population or 38,900,000, may include an unknown percentage of mestizos and mulattos.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Other sources<sup id="cite_ref-WSARG_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WSARG-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> put 86.4% of the population. <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islanders" title="Falkland Islanders">Falkland Islanders</a>  mainly European of British descent—total population 3,140.
</p><p>Cubans of European origin reached a peak of 74.3% or 3,553,312 of the total population in 1943.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> Most recently, those self-identified as white made up 64.1% of the total population, according to the 2012 census.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> They are predominately the descendants of early <a href="/wiki/Spanish_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Spanish people">Spanish</a> settlers, along with other Europeans including the <a href="/wiki/English_people" title="English people">English</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italians" title="Italians">Italians</a> arriving later but in smaller numbers. However, after the mass exodus (particularly to the United States) resulting from the <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Revolution" title="Cuban Revolution">Cuban Revolution</a> in 1959, the number of white Cubans actually residing in Cuba diminished.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia_and_New_Zealand">Australia and New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_emigration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Australia and New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Total population of <a href="/wiki/Europeans_in_Oceania" title="Europeans in Oceania">Europeans in Oceania</a> is approximately 23,185,000 or approximately 22,818,000 excluding Hawaii.
</p><p>By 1947, Australia was overwhelmingly of British origin with 7,524,129 or 99.3% of the population declaring their race as European.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> As of <a href="/wiki/2016_Australian_census" title="2016 Australian census">2016</a>, the majority of Australians of <a href="/wiki/European_Australians" title="European Australians">European descent</a> are of <a href="/wiki/English_Australians" title="English Australians">English</a> 36.1%, <a href="/wiki/Irish_Australians" title="Irish Australians">Irish</a> 11.0%, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Australians" title="Scottish Australians">Scottish</a> 9.3%, <a href="/wiki/Italians" title="Italians">Italian</a> 4.6%, <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">German</a> 4.5%,  <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greek</a> 1.8% and <a href="/wiki/Dutch_people" title="Dutch people">Dutch</a> 1.6%. A large proportion —33.5%— chose to identify as ‘Australian’, however the census Bureau has stated that most of these are of old <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Celtic_Australians" title="Anglo-Celtic Australians">Anglo-Celtic</a> colonial stock.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> As at the 2016 census, it was estimated that around 58% of the Australian population were Anglo-Celtic Australians with 18% being of other European origins, a total of 76% for European ancestries as a whole.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Europeans historically (especially <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Celtic" title="Anglo-Celtic">Anglo-Celtic</a>) and presently are still the largest ethnic group in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>. Their proportion of the total New Zealand population has been decreasing gradually since the <a href="/wiki/1916_New_Zealand_census" title="1916 New Zealand census">1916 census</a> where they formed 95.1 percent.<sup id="cite_ref-Historical_and_statistical_survey_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Historical_and_statistical_survey-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/2013_New_Zealand_census" title="2013 New Zealand census">2013 official census</a> had 2,969,391 or 74.0% of the population were ethnic Europeans, with 2,727,009 people (68%) choosing the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_European" class="mw-redirect" title="New Zealand European">New Zealand European</a> option alone.<sup id="cite_ref-Census2013_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Census2013-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_emigration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/White_South_African" class="mw-redirect" title="White South African">South Africans of European descent</a> were 4,586,838 or 8.9% of South Africa's population in the <a href="/wiki/South_African_National_Census_of_2011" title="South African National Census of 2011">2011 census</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
They are predominantly descendants of <a href="/wiki/Dutch_people" title="Dutch people">Dutch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">German</a>, French <a href="/wiki/Huguenots" title="Huguenots">Huguenots</a>, <a href="/wiki/English_people" title="English people">English</a> and other European settlers.<sup id="cite_ref-khenr_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-khenr-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lvr_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lvr-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> Culturally and linguistically, they are divided into <a href="/wiki/Boers" class="mw-redirect" title="Boers">Boers</a>, who speak Afrikaans, and English-speaking groups.
The first national census in South Africa was held in 1911 and showed a percentage peak of 21.4% or 1,276,242.<sup id="cite_ref-Shimoni_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shimoni-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Afrikaans1_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Afrikaans1-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> The population increased to its peak at 5,044,000 in 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-ZAS_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ZAS-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> 
However, the number of white South African residents in their home country began gradually declining between 1990 and the mid-2000s as a result of increased <a href="/wiki/Emigration" title="Emigration">emigration</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ZAS_78-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ZAS-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Canary_Islanders" title="Canary Islanders">Canary Islanders</a> are the descendants of <a href="/wiki/Spaniards" title="Spaniards">Spaniards</a> who settled the Canary Islands. The Canarian people include long-tenured and new waves of Spanish immigrants, including <a href="/wiki/Andalucians" class="mw-redirect" title="Andalucians">Andalucians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galicians" title="Galicians">Galicians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Castilians" title="Castilians">Castilians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catalan_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Catalan people">Catalans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basques" title="Basques">Basques</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asturian_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Asturian people">Asturians</a> of Spain; and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_people" title="Portuguese people">Portuguese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italians" title="Italians">Italians</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_people" title="Dutch people">Dutch people</a> or <a href="/wiki/Flemings" class="mw-redirect" title="Flemings">Flemings</a>, and <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French people</a>. As of 2019, 72.1% or 1,553,078 Spaniards were Canary Islands-born with a further 15.5% born in mainland Spain. Many European people or those of <a href="/wiki/Isle%C3%B1o" title="Isleño">Isleño</a> (islander) origins have also moved to the Islands, such as those from Venezuela and Cuba. Presently there are 49,170 from Italy, Germany (25,619), United Kingdom (25,521), Romania, France and Portugal.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>: (<a href="/wiki/Russians" title="Russians">Russians</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Smaller_communities">Smaller communities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_emigration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Smaller communities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Small communities of European, white American and white Australian expatriates in the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> countries like <a href="/wiki/Bahrain" title="Bahrain">Bahrain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwait</a>, <a href="/wiki/Qatar" title="Qatar">Qatar</a> and the <a href="/wiki/UAE" class="mw-redirect" title="UAE">UAE</a>; and in Aramco compounds in <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>. Historically before 1970, small ethnic European (esp. Greek and Italian) enclaves were found in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> (<a href="/wiki/Greeks_in_Egypt" title="Greeks in Egypt">Greeks in Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italian_Egyptians" title="Italian Egyptians">Italian Egyptians</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> (<a href="/wiki/Greeks_in_Syria" title="Greeks in Syria">Greeks in Syria</a>).
</p>

Retrieved <span class="nowrap">14 September</span> 2013</span>. <q>The population of Europe entered its third and decisive stage in the early eighteenth century. Birthrates declined, but death rates also declined as the standard of living and advances in medical science provided for longer life spans. The population of Europe including Russia more than doubled from 188&#160;million in 1800 to 432&#160;million in 1900. From 1815 through 1932, sixty million people left Europe, primarily to "areas of European settlement," in North and South America, Australia, New Zealand and Siberia. These populations also multiplied rapidly in their new habitat; much more so than the populations of Africa and Asia. As a result, on the eve of World War I (1914), 38 percent of the world's total population was of European ancestry. This growth in population provided further impetus for European expansion, and became the driving force behind emigration. Rising populations put pressure on land, and land hunger and led to "land hunger." Millions of people went abroad in search of work or economic opportunity. The Irish, who left for America during the great Potato famine, were an extreme but not unique example. Ultimately, one third of all European migrants came from the British Isles between 1840 and 1920. <a href="/wiki/Italians" title="Italians">Italians</a> also migrated in large numbers because of poor economic conditions in their home country. German migration also was steady until industrial conditions in Germany improved when the wave of migration slowed. Less than one half of all migrants went to the United States, although it absorbed the largest number of European migrants. Others went to Asiatic Russia, Canada, Argentina, Brazil, Australia and New Zealand.</q></cite><span title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Abook&amp;rft.genre=unknown&amp;rft.btitle=European+Migration+and+Imperialism&amp;rft.pub=historydoctor.net&amp;rft_id=http%3A%2F%2Fhistorydoctor.net%2FAdvanced%2520Placement%2520European%2520History%2FNotes%2Feuropean_migration_and_imperiali.htm&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3AEuropean+emigration" class="Z3988"></span><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886058088"/></span>
