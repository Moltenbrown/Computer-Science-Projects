<p><b>Japanese philosophy</b> has historically been a fusion of both indigenous <a href="/wiki/Shinto" title="Shinto">Shinto</a> and continental religions, such as <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucianism</a>.  Formerly heavily influenced by both <a href="/wiki/Chinese_philosophy" title="Chinese philosophy">Chinese philosophy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indian_philosophy" title="Indian philosophy">Indian philosophy</a>, as with <a href="/wiki/Mitogaku" title="Mitogaku">Mitogaku</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zen" title="Zen">Zen</a>, much modern Japanese philosophy is now also influenced by <a href="/wiki/Western_philosophy" title="Western philosophy">Western philosophy</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_and_medieval_thought">Ancient and medieval thought</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ancient and medieval thought">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Buddhism_in_Japan" title="Buddhism in Japan">Buddhism in Japan</a></div>
<p>Before <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudalism</a> was firmly established in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a> occupied the mainstream of Japanese thought. The Buddhist culture introduced politically by <a href="/wiki/Prince_Sh%C5%8Dtoku" title="Prince Shōtoku">Prince Shōtoku</a> is completed as the "making a country safe" thought in the <a href="/wiki/Nara_period" title="Nara period">Nara period</a>. When the <a href="/wiki/Heian_period" title="Heian period">Heian period</a> (794–1185) began, in substitution for the "making a country safe thought", form of esoteric Buddhism collectively known as <i><a href="/wiki/Mikky%C5%8D" title="Mikkyō">mikkyō</a></i> became widespread. However, in the late noble era when pessimism was popular due to the "belief that Buddhism will decline during the <a href="/wiki/Three_Ages_of_Buddhism" title="Three Ages of Buddhism">latter days</a> of this world", the <a href="/wiki/Pure_Land" class="mw-redirect" title="Pure Land">Pure Land</a> movements spread out encouraging anticipation of a "future life" as a means to cope with desperation over "life in this world". During the <a href="/wiki/Kamakura_period" title="Kamakura period">Kamakura period</a> (1185–1333) when government dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Samurai" title="Samurai">samurai</a> class began, a “new” Buddhism for the newly-risen <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">class</a> (samurai) appears.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arrival_of_Buddhism_and_early_influence_in_Japan">Arrival of Buddhism and early influence in Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Arrival of Buddhism and early influence in Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Nanto_Rikush%C5%AB" class="mw-redirect" title="Nanto Rikushū">Nanto Rikushū</a></div>
<p>In ancient Japan, the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a> closely relates the national construction and the national centralization of <a href="/wiki/Political_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Political power">power</a>. <a href="/wiki/Prince_Sh%C5%8Dtoku" title="Prince Shōtoku">Prince Shōtoku</a> and the Soga family fought and overcame the Mononobe family, who had handled the ancient Japanese <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religion</a>, and elaborated a plan for national <a href="/wiki/Governance" title="Governance">governance</a> based on the unification of the <a href="/wiki/Legal_codes" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal codes">legal codes</a> system and Buddhism. While cooperating with the Soga family, Prince Shōtoku, who was the regent of the <a href="/wiki/Empress_Suiko" title="Empress Suiko">Empress Suiko</a>, showed a deep understanding in "foreign" Buddhism,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and planned to stabilize national <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a> through the use of Buddhism. The thought that national peace and security came through the power of Buddhism is called the "making a country safe" thought. In the <a href="/wiki/Nara_period" title="Nara period">Nara period</a>, in particular the times of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Sh%C5%8Dmu" title="Emperor Shōmu">Emperor Shōmu</a>, the Kokubun-ji temples and Kokubun-ni-ji temples were erected throughout the whole country and <a href="/wiki/T%C5%8Ddai-ji" title="Tōdai-ji">Tōdai-ji Temple</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Daibutsu" title="Daibutsu">Daibutsu</a> were erected in <a href="/wiki/Nara,_Nara" title="Nara, Nara">Nara</a>. The Buddhist policy of the state reached its apex during the Nara period, as evidenced by <a href="/wiki/Jianzhen" title="Jianzhen">Jianzhen</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang dynasty</a> bringing an imperial ordination platform to Todai-ji Temple,
</p><p>While Nara Buddhism followed only the "making a country safe" thought, Heian Buddhism brought not only national <a href="/wiki/Peace" title="Peace">peace</a> and <a href="/wiki/Security" title="Security">security</a> but also the personal worldly <a href="/wiki/Profit_(economics)" title="Profit (economics)">profit</a>. Because practitioners of Heian Buddhism frequently performed severe <a href="/wiki/Ascetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Ascetic">ascetic</a> practices, <a href="/wiki/Incantation" title="Incantation">incantations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prayer" title="Prayer">prayers</a> in the mountains, this Buddhism came to be called <a href="/wiki/Mikky%C5%8D" title="Mikkyō">mikkyō</a>. <a href="/wiki/K%C5%ABkai" title="Kūkai">Kūkai</a>, a Buddhist monk, learned Chinese <a href="/wiki/Esoteric_Buddhism" class="mw-redirect" title="Esoteric Buddhism">esoteric Buddhism</a> while on a diplomatic mission to the Chinese court, and combined Japanese Buddhism with Chinese esoteric Buddhist practices to form Japanese <a href="/wiki/Shingon_Buddhism" title="Shingon Buddhism">Shingon Buddhism</a>. <a href="/wiki/Saich%C5%8D" title="Saichō">Saichō</a>, a Buddhist monk who also journeyed to China, learned the practices of the Chinese <a href="/wiki/Tendai" title="Tendai">Tendai</a> sect and argued that the teachings of the <a href="/wiki/Lotus_Sutra" title="Lotus Sutra">Lotus Sutra</a> should be the core of Japanese Buddhism.
</p><p>By the late Heian era, the earthly focus of Heian Buddhism led Buddhist monks to declare a "sinful age" wherein the possibility of relief in this world was denied and therefore a trend of looking for reincarnation to the Buddhists' <a href="/wiki/Paradise" title="Paradise">paradise</a> after death arose. Additionally, the new thought that "Buddhism will decline during the latter days of this world" led to the rise of the <a href="/wiki/Pure_Land" class="mw-redirect" title="Pure Land">Pure Land</a> movement. This movement, spearheaded by <a href="/wiki/K%C5%ABya" title="Kūya">Kūya</a>, a follower of Pure Land Buddhism, preached faith to the <a href="/wiki/Amit%C4%81bha" title="Amitābha">Amitābha</a> and taught that all people could reach the Buddhist paradise, not just Buddhist monks.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kamakura_Buddhism">Kamakura Buddhism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Kamakura Buddhism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/J%C5%8Ddo-sh%C5%AB" title="Jōdo-shū">Jōdo</a> faith, which affected by the Jodo sect of the late Heian period, relies on salvation through the benevolence of <a href="/wiki/Amit%C4%81bha" title="Amitābha">Amitābha</a>, and is going to be relieved by its power. <a href="/wiki/H%C5%8Dnen" title="Hōnen">Hōnen</a>, who initiated the Jodo sect of Buddhism, abandoned other ascetic practices entirely. He preached his pupils to believe in Amitabha and to earnestly pray "<a href="/wiki/Nianfo" title="Nianfo">namu-amida-butsu</a>", and so they would go to the paradise. His pupil, <a href="/wiki/Shinran" title="Shinran">Shinran</a> who initiated <a href="/wiki/Pure_Land_Buddhism" title="Pure Land Buddhism">Pure Land Buddhism</a>, thoroughly carried out Honen's teaching and preached the absolute dependence. In addition, Shinran advocated that an object of the relief of the Amitabha was a criminal who was aware of a worldly and desirous criminal oneself. <a href="/wiki/Ippen" title="Ippen">Ippen</a>, who initiated the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jishu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jishu (page does not exist)">Jishu</a> sect, began "the chanting religious dance".
</p><p>As contrast with dependent <a href="/wiki/J%C5%8Dd%C5%8D" title="Jōdō">Jōdō</a> faith, <a href="/wiki/Zen" title="Zen">Zen</a> Buddhism attempts to be spiritually self-awakened by <a href="/wiki/Zazen" title="Zazen">Zen meditation</a>. <a href="/wiki/Eisai" title="Eisai">Eisai</a> learned <a href="/wiki/Rinzai" class="mw-redirect" title="Rinzai">Rinzai</a> sect in China. He gave pupils a difficult problem and he made them solving the problem, and so his pupils would be enlightened by themselves. Rinzai Zen was supported widely by the upper samurai class in the <a href="/wiki/Kamakura_period" title="Kamakura period">Kamakura period</a>. <a href="/wiki/D%C5%8Dgen" title="Dōgen">Dōgen</a> learned <a href="/wiki/S%C5%8Dt%C5%8D" title="Sōtō">Sōtō</a> sect in China.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Oppose to Eisai, he preached enlightenment by earnest sitting meditation (<a href="/wiki/Zazen" title="Zazen">zazen</a>). Soto Zen was supported by the local samurais.
</p><p>Most schools of <a href="/wiki/Nichiren_Buddhism" title="Nichiren Buddhism">Nichiren Buddhism</a> (Japanese: <span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">法華系仏教</span> <i lang="ja-Latn" title="Japanese-language romanization">Hokke-kei Bukkyō</i>) refer to the priest and teacher <a href="/wiki/Nichiren" title="Nichiren">Nichiren</a> as their founding father. In his teachings he underlined the, to his mind, supremacy of the <a href="/wiki/Lotus_Sutra" title="Lotus Sutra">Lotus Sutra</a>. He advocated the attainment of Buddhahood during one's lifetime and regarded his interpretation of the Buddhist teachings the correct form of practice for the Latter Day of the Law <a href="/wiki/Mapp%C5%8D" class="mw-redirect" title="Mappō">mappō</a>. One of his major treatises is the "Rissho Ankoku Ron" (On Establishing the Correct teaching for the Peace of the Land). The chanting of the <a href="/wiki/Mantra" title="Mantra">Mantra</a> "<a href="/wiki/Namu_My%C5%8Dh%C5%8D_Renge_Ky%C5%8D" title="Namu Myōhō Renge Kyō">Namu Myōhō Renge Kyō</a>" is to this day the central practice to almost all Nichiren Buddhist schools and organisations.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_modern_thought">Early modern thought</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early modern thought">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a></div>
<p>Whereas the ancient and medieval thought of Japan was tied closely to <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, the early modern thought of Japan was mainly <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucianism</a> or <a href="/wiki/Neo-Confucianism" title="Neo-Confucianism">Neo-Confucianism</a>, which was designated for official study of the <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_shogunate" title="Tokugawa shogunate">Tokugawa shogunate</a>. In addition, <a href="/wiki/Rationality" title="Rationality">rational</a> Confucianism stimulated <a href="/wiki/Kokugaku" title="Kokugaku">Kokugaku</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rangaku" title="Rangaku">Rangaku</a> and the non-official popular thought after the middle <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Confucianism">Confucianism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Confucianism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Neo-Confucianism_in_Japan" class="mw-redirect" title="Neo-Confucianism in Japan">Neo-Confucianism in Japan</a></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a>, <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucianism</a> was the authorised study. Various schools of <a href="/wiki/Neo-Confucianism" title="Neo-Confucianism">neo-Confucianism</a> were popular.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Zhu_Xi" title="Zhu Xi">Zhu Xi</a> school of neo-Confucianism respected <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">family</a>-like <a href="/wiki/Feudal" class="mw-redirect" title="Feudal">feudal</a> social position order. <a href="/wiki/Hayashi_Razan" title="Hayashi Razan">Hayashi Razan</a> assumed the Zhu Xi school of neo-Confucianism to be the theoretical basics of the <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_shogunate" title="Tokugawa shogunate">Tokugawa shogunate</a>. Through the principle of <a href="/wiki/Civilian" title="Civilian">civilian</a> <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yushima_Seid%C5%8D" title="Yushima Seidō">Yushima Seidō</a> dedicating to <a href="/wiki/Confucius" title="Confucius">Confucius</a> was established. By the <a href="/wiki/Kansei_Reforms" class="mw-redirect" title="Kansei Reforms">Kansei Reforms</a>, the Zhu Xi school of neo-Confucianism were still more strengthened and authorized by the Tokugawa shogunate. In addition, the thought of a school of the Zhu Xi school of neo-Confucianism gave big influence to the political movement advocating reverence for the <a href="/wiki/Emperor" title="Emperor">Emperor</a> and the expulsion of <a href="/wiki/Alien_(law)" title="Alien (law)">foreigners</a> of the late Tokugawa era.
</p><p>In contrast with the Zhu Xi school of neo-Confucianism, the <a href="/wiki/Wang_Yangming" title="Wang Yangming">Wang Yangming</a> school of neo-Confucianism respecting practical <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethics</a> consistently monitored and oppressed by the Tokugawa shogunate because of criticisms for the socio-political conditions under the Tokugawa shogunate.
</p><p>The third schools of neo-Confucianism took consideration into the real intentions of original texts by <a href="/wiki/Confucius" title="Confucius">Confucius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mencius" title="Mencius">Mencius</a>. <a href="/wiki/Yamaga_Sok%C5%8D" title="Yamaga Sokō">Yamaga Sokō</a> established his philosophy on Confucian ethics, and assumed the <a href="/wiki/Samurai" title="Samurai">samurai</a> to be the highest class. <a href="/wiki/It%C5%8D_Jinsai" title="Itō Jinsai">Itō Jinsai</a> paid attention to "<a href="/wiki/Ren_(Confucianism)" title="Ren (Confucianism)">ren</a>" of Confucius and he respected "ren" as the <a href="/wiki/Love" title="Love">love</a> for another person and "truth" as pure consideration. In addition, deriving from his substantial studies of ancient Chinese classics, <a href="/wiki/Ogy%C5%AB_Sorai" title="Ogyū Sorai">Ogyū Sorai</a> insisted that original Confucian spirit is to rule the <a href="/wiki/World" title="World">world</a> and to save a <a href="/wiki/Citizen" class="mw-redirect" title="Citizen">citizen</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kokugaku_and_Rangaku">Kokugaku and Rangaku</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Kokugaku and Rangaku">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Kokugaku" title="Kokugaku">Kokugaku</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rangaku" title="Rangaku">Rangaku</a></div>
<p>In the middle of the <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kokugaku" title="Kokugaku">Kokugaku</a>, the study of ancient Japanese thought and culture, became popular against foreign ideas such as <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a> or <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucianism</a>. By <a href="/wiki/Sakoku" title="Sakoku">Sakoku</a> policy of the Tokugawa shogunate, Edo intellectuals could not have any positive contact with Western civilization, and so <a href="/wiki/Rangaku" title="Rangaku">Rangaku</a>, Dutch learning, was the only window to the <a href="/wiki/West" title="West">West</a>.
</p><p>In the middle days of the Edo period, <a href="/wiki/Kokugaku" title="Kokugaku">Kokugaku</a> became popular while being influenced by positivist <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucianism</a> with <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalism</a> as a background. Kokugaku positively studied ancient Japanese thought and culture, including "<a href="/wiki/Kojiki" title="Kojiki">Kojiki</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Nihon_Shoki" title="Nihon Shoki">Nihon Shoki</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Man%27y%C5%8Dsh%C5%AB" title="Man&#39;yōshū">Man'yōshū</a>", and they aimed at excavating original moral <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a> of Japan which was different from Confucianism and <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>. <a href="/wiki/Kamo_no_Mabuchi" title="Kamo no Mabuchi">Kamo no Mabuchi</a> wrestled with the study of "Manyoshu" and called "<a href="/w/index.php?title=Masurao-buri&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Masurao-buri (page does not exist)">masurao-buri</a>" for <a href="/wiki/Masculine" class="mw-redirect" title="Masculine">masculine</a> and tolerant style, and he evaluated the collection as pure and simple. Through his study of the <i>Kojiki</i>, <a href="/wiki/Motoori_Norinaga" title="Motoori Norinaga">Motoori Norinaga</a> argued that the essence of the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_literature" title="Japanese literature">Japanese literature</a> came from "<a href="/wiki/Mono_no_aware" title="Mono no aware">mono no aware</a>" which was natural feelings to occur when you contacted with an object. He respected Japanese "Yamato spirit" instead of Chinese (Confucianism / Buddhism) "Kara spirit". According to him, Kokugaku should pursue Japanese old way of "<a href="/wiki/Shinto" title="Shinto">Shinto</a>". Through his study of Kokugaku, <a href="/wiki/Hirata_Atsutane" title="Hirata Atsutane">Hirata Atsutane</a> advocated nationalistic <a href="/wiki/State_Shinto" title="State Shinto">State Shinto</a>, the obedience to the <a href="/wiki/Emperor" title="Emperor">Emperor</a> and abolition of Confucianism and Buddhism. It was a driving force to the end of the Tokugawa shogunate and the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Restoration" title="Meiji Restoration">Meiji Restoration</a>.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Sakoku" title="Sakoku">Sakoku</a> period of the Edo period, there was no direct contact with the West, but, <a href="/wiki/Rangaku" title="Rangaku">Rangaku</a> became popular by encouraging importation of Western books translated in Chinese from China during the <a href="/wiki/Ky%C5%8Dh%C5%8D_Reforms" title="Kyōhō Reforms">Kyōhō Reforms</a>. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Maeno_Ryotaku&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Maeno Ryotaku (page does not exist)">Maeno Ryotaku</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sugita_Genpaku" title="Sugita Genpaku">Sugita Genpaku</a> translated Dutch "<a href="/wiki/Kaitai_Shinsho" title="Kaitai Shinsho">Tafel Anatomie</a>" into Japanese. Dutch learning unfolded to other Western studies such as British, French and American studies by the late Tokugawa era. The manner of "Japanese <a href="/wiki/Spirit" title="Spirit">spirit</a>, Western <a href="/wiki/Civilisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Civilisation">civilisation</a>" was completed by <a href="/wiki/Sakuma_Sh%C5%8Dzan" title="Sakuma Shōzan">Sakuma Shōzan</a>’s straightforward expression, "Eastern <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethics</a> and Western <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a>". Because <a href="/wiki/Takano_Ch%C5%8Dei" title="Takano Chōei">Takano Chōei</a> and <a href="/wiki/Watanabe_Kazan" title="Watanabe Kazan">Watanabe Kazan</a> of the person of Dutch learning criticized Sakoku strictly, they were oppressed by the Tokugawa shogunate.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_thought">Popular thought</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Popular thought">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a>, private schools were opened by <a href="/wiki/Samurai" title="Samurai">samurais</a>, <a href="/wiki/Merchant" title="Merchant">merchants</a> and scholars who played an active part. Their thoughts were criticisms for the dominant <a href="/wiki/Feudal" class="mw-redirect" title="Feudal">feudal</a> order.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ishida_Baigan" title="Ishida Baigan">Ishida Baigan</a> synthesized <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucianism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shinto" title="Shinto">Shinto</a>, and established practical philosophy for the masses. He recommended working hard at <a href="/wiki/Commerce" title="Commerce">commerce</a> as the effect by honesty and thrift. <a href="/wiki/Ando_Shoeki" title="Ando Shoeki">Ando Shoeki</a> called nature's world the ideal society where all human beings engaged in <a href="/wiki/Farming" class="mw-redirect" title="Farming">farming</a> and they lived self-sufficiently without artificiality. He criticized a <a href="/wiki/Lawful" class="mw-redirect" title="Lawful">lawful</a> society where there were feudal <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">class</a> discrimination and the difference between the rich and poor. <a href="/wiki/Ninomiya_Sontoku" title="Ninomiya Sontoku">Ninomiya Sontoku</a> insisted that people must repay the <a href="/wiki/Virtue" title="Virtue">virtues</a>, which supported their existence, with own virtue.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_Modern_thought">Late Modern thought</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Late Modern thought">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Japan" title="Empire of Japan">Empire of Japan</a></div>
<p>While the early modern Japanese thought developed in <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucianism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, English <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a> and French <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> were prevalent after the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Restoration" title="Meiji Restoration">Meiji Restoration</a> rapidly affected by Western thought. From the time of <a href="/wiki/First_Sino-Japanese_War" title="First Sino-Japanese War">Sino-</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russo-Japanese_War" title="Russo-Japanese War">Russo-Japanese Wars</a>, Japanese <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a> highly developed. <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a> developed and they tied to various social movements. In addition, <a href="/wiki/Nationalistic" class="mw-redirect" title="Nationalistic">nationalistic</a> thought and study were formed while being opposed to foreign study.
</p>
<h3><span id="The_Enlightenment_and_people.27s_rights"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Enlightenment_and_people's_rights">The Enlightenment and people's rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The Enlightenment and people&#039;s rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Restoration" title="Meiji Restoration">Meiji Restoration</a>, English and French <a href="/wiki/Civil_society" title="Civil society">civil society</a> was introduced, in particular, <a href="/wiki/Utilitarianism" title="Utilitarianism">utilitarianism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_Darwinism" title="Social Darwinism">social Darwinism</a> from England, and <a href="/wiki/Popular_sovereignty" title="Popular sovereignty">popular sovereignty</a> of <a href="/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Rousseau" title="Jean-Jacques Rousseau">Jean-Jacques Rousseau</a> from France.
</p><p>The thinkers of the early Meiji period advocated British Enlightenment derived from Western civil society. They attempted to criticise Japanese traditional <a href="/wiki/Authority" title="Authority">authority</a> and <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudalism</a>. However they were finally in harmony with the government and accepted the <a href="/wiki/Modernization" class="mw-redirect" title="Modernization">modernization</a> from the above without the radicalness. In 1873, <a href="/wiki/Mori_Arinori" title="Mori Arinori">Mori Arinori</a> formed <a href="/wiki/Meirokusha" title="Meirokusha">Meirokusha</a>. The people who gathered in this cultural association had much in common with points such as regarding practical learning as important, catching human characteristics practically and assuming the form of government that accepted the conditions of a country an ideal. Mori Arinori promoted national <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a> as Minister of Education. <a href="/wiki/Nishi_Amane" title="Nishi Amane">Nishi Amane</a> affirmed a human behaviour based on interest. <a href="/wiki/Kat%C5%8D_Hiroyuki" title="Katō Hiroyuki">Katō Hiroyuki</a> threw away <a href="/wiki/Natural_rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural rights">natural rights</a> under influence of <a href="/wiki/Social_Darwinism" title="Social Darwinism">social Darwinism</a>, and instead advocated the survival of the fittest.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Fukuzawa_Yukichi" title="Fukuzawa Yukichi">Fukuzawa Yukichi</a> who introduced British <a href="/wiki/Utilitarianism" title="Utilitarianism">utilitarianism</a> to Japan advocated the <a href="/wiki/Natural_rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural rights">natural rights</a> assumed that the <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> were given from the heaven. He considered the development of the <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilization</a> to be the development of the human spirit, and it was assumed that one's <a href="/wiki/Independence" title="Independence">independence</a> led to independence of one country.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Fukuzawa thought that there was the <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a> in "sake of the convenience", and its appearance should be suitable to the culture. He said that there is no single ideal form of government. In addition, he insisted that Japan should have gone into the continent externally against the <a href="/wiki/Great_Powers" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Powers">Great Powers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While members of <a href="/wiki/Meirokusha" title="Meirokusha">Meirokusha</a> finally advocated harmonization of the government and people, <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democratic</a> thinkers absorbed radical people's <a href="/wiki/Rights" title="Rights">rights</a> from France and they supported national resistance and revolution verbally against the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_oligarchy" title="Meiji oligarchy">Meiji oligarchy</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Satsuma_Rebellion" title="Satsuma Rebellion">Satsuma Rebellion</a>. In 1874, <a href="/wiki/Itagaki_Taisuke" title="Itagaki Taisuke">Itagaki Taisuke</a> introduced the establishment of the elected <a href="/wiki/Legislature" title="Legislature">legislature</a>. It spread nationwide as the <a href="/wiki/Freedom_and_People%27s_Rights_Movement" title="Freedom and People&#39;s Rights Movement">Freedom and People's Rights Movement</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ueki_Emori" title="Ueki Emori">Ueki Emori</a> helped Itagaki and he drew up a radical draft. Strongly influenced from <a href="/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Rousseau" title="Jean-Jacques Rousseau">Rousseau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nakae_Ch%C5%8Dmin" title="Nakae Chōmin">Nakae Chōmin</a> argued for people's <a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">sovereignty</a> and individual <a href="/wiki/Political_freedom" title="Political freedom">freedom</a>. However, concerning with Japanese situation, he pointed out the importance of <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_monarchy" class="mw-redirect" title="Parliamentary monarchy">parliamentary monarchy</a>. According to him, the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Constitution" title="Meiji Constitution">Imperial Constitution</a> should be gradually revised by the <a href="/wiki/Diet_of_Japan" class="mw-redirect" title="Diet of Japan">Diet</a>.
</p><p>From the late period of Meiji to the <a href="/wiki/Taish%C5%8D_era" class="mw-redirect" title="Taishō era">Taishō era</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democratic</a> trend spread as a background of bourgeois <a href="/wiki/Political_consciousness" title="Political consciousness">political consciousness</a>. Its current led to political movements for safeguarding the <a href="/wiki/Constitution" title="Constitution">Constitution</a> and for the <a href="/wiki/Direct_election" title="Direct election">popular election</a>. <a href="/wiki/Yoshino_Sakuz%C5%8D" class="mw-redirect" title="Yoshino Sakuzō">Yoshino Sakuzō</a> argued for party cabinet politics and popular election. He did not deeply pursue who was the sovereign but he insisted political goal aim for people's happiness and political decision aim for people's intention. <a href="/wiki/Minobe_Tatsukichi" class="mw-redirect" title="Minobe Tatsukichi">Minobe Tatsukichi</a> interpreted a sovereign as not an <a href="/wiki/Emperor" title="Emperor">emperor</a> but the <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a>. According to him, an emperor just only excises his <a href="/wiki/Political_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Political power">power</a> as the highest organ under the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Constitution" title="Meiji Constitution">Meiji Constitution</a>. Although his theory was widely acknowledged at first, he was politically suppressed by the military and the rightists afterwards.
</p><p>In 1911, <a href="/wiki/Hiratsuka_Raich%C5%8D" title="Hiratsuka Raichō">Hiratsuka Raichō</a> formed <a href="/wiki/Bluestockings_Journal" class="mw-redirect" title="Bluestockings Journal">Seitosha</a>. She asked for awakening of women's own and development of <a href="/wiki/Feminist_movement" title="Feminist movement">feminist movement</a>. While <a href="/wiki/Yosano_Akiko" title="Yosano Akiko">Yosano Akiko</a> denied <a href="/wiki/Gender" title="Gender">gender</a> difference, Raicho emphasised motherhood raising a child and she acknowledged the official aids for women to demonstrate their feminine ability. In 1920, Raicho formed a new association for women with <a href="/wiki/Ichikawa_Fusae" title="Ichikawa Fusae">Ichikawa Fusae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oku_Mumeo" title="Oku Mumeo">Oku Mumeo</a>. Soon after their activities were successful in women's participating at political address, the association were broken up by an internal split. Later, Ichikawa formed a new one and continued a movement for <a href="/wiki/Female_suffrage" class="mw-redirect" title="Female suffrage">female suffrage</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Christianity_and_socialism">Christianity and socialism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Christianity and socialism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Christianity_in_Japan" title="Christianity in Japan">Christianity in Japan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Socialist_thought_in_Imperial_Japan" title="Socialist thought in Imperial Japan">Socialist thought in Imperial Japan</a></div>
<p>It was <a href="/wiki/Christians" title="Christians">Christians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Socialist" class="mw-redirect" title="Socialist">socialists</a> who struggled with social contradictions derived from Japanese <a href="/wiki/Modernity" title="Modernity">modernity</a>. Christian social movements were active after the <a href="/wiki/First_Sino-Japanese_War" title="First Sino-Japanese War">Sino-</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russo-Japanese_War" title="Russo-Japanese War">Russo-Japanese Wars</a> which brought <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a> and its contradiction to Japanese society. Many Japanese socialists were influenced by <a href="/wiki/Christian_humanism" title="Christian humanism">Christian humanism</a>, and in that point they were deeply associated with <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>.
</p><p>Christianity, banned by the <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_shogunate" title="Tokugawa shogunate">Tokugawa shogunate</a>, influenced many Meiji intellectuals. <a href="/wiki/Uchimura_Kanz%C5%8D" title="Uchimura Kanzō">Uchimura Kanzō</a> developed "two Js" to unite <a href="/wiki/Bushido" title="Bushido">Bushido</a> and Christian spirit. He believed that his calling was to serve "Japan" and "Jesus". He argued for the <a href="/wiki/Nonchurch_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Nonchurch movement">nonchurch movement</a>. He challenged the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Rescript_on_Education" title="Imperial Rescript on Education">Imperial Rescript on Education</a> and spoke against the <a href="/wiki/Russo-Japanese_War" title="Russo-Japanese War">Russo-Japanese War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nitobe_Inaz%C5%8D" title="Nitobe Inazō">Nitobe Inazō</a> was a <a href="/wiki/Quaker" class="mw-redirect" title="Quaker">Quaker</a> and attempted to unite <a href="/wiki/Japanese_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese culture">Japanese culture</a> and Christianity. He introduced Japanese culture abroad and he became secretary-general of the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">League of Nations</a>. <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Hardy_Neesima" title="Joseph Hardy Neesima">Joseph Hardy Neesima</a> studied <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theology</a> abroad in the United States. He established <a href="/wiki/Doshisha_University" title="Doshisha University">Doshisha University</a> at Kyoto and he was engaged in Christian <a href="/wiki/Moral_character" title="Moral character">character building</a>.
</p><p>About the time of Sino- and Russo-Japanese wars, Japan succeeded in capitalization through the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a> as soon as <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a> spread against <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a>. However, the social movements were suppressed by the <a href="/wiki/Security_police" title="Security police">security police</a> law of 1900, and finally in the <a href="/wiki/High_Treason_Incident" title="High Treason Incident">High Treason Incident</a> of 1910 socialists were pressed by the military and the <a href="/wiki/Fascist" class="mw-redirect" title="Fascist">fascist</a> government&#160; ... <a href="/wiki/Kawakami_Hajime" class="mw-redirect" title="Kawakami Hajime">Kawakami Hajime</a> wrote articles about <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a> in a newspaper. He emphasized personal remodelling to solve poverty at first, however, later he became <a href="/wiki/Marxist" class="mw-redirect" title="Marxist">Marxist</a> and he argued for social remodelling by social compulsion. <a href="/wiki/K%C5%8Dtoku_Sh%C5%ABsui" title="Kōtoku Shūsui">Kōtoku Shūsui</a> originally attempted to realize socialism through the <a href="/wiki/Diet_of_Japan" class="mw-redirect" title="Diet of Japan">Diet</a>, however, he became a <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">unionist</a> and he argued for a direct action by a <a href="/wiki/General_strike" title="General strike">general strike</a>. He was executed as the mastermind of the high treason incident of 1910. <a href="/wiki/Osugi_Sakae" class="mw-redirect" title="Osugi Sakae">Osugi Sakae</a> argued for individual <a href="/wiki/Political_freedom" title="Political freedom">freedom</a> using the principles of <a href="/wiki/Anarchism" title="Anarchism">anarchism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">unionism</a>. He was seen as a threat by the government and was assassinated by <a href="/wiki/Military_police" title="Military police">military police</a> in the disorder following the <a href="/wiki/1923_Great_Kant%C5%8D_earthquake" title="1923 Great Kantō earthquake">1923 Great Kantō earthquake</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_development_of_Japanism">The development of Japanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: The development of Japanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Statism_in_Sh%C5%8Dwa_Japan" title="Statism in Shōwa Japan">Statism in Shōwa Japan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_School" title="Kyoto School">Kyoto School</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Age of Enlightenment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a> have influenced Japanese thought since the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Restoration" title="Meiji Restoration">Meiji Restoration</a>.
The emphasis on Japanese political <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a> and national <a href="/wiki/Tradition" title="Tradition">tradition</a> rose as a reaction against <a href="/wiki/Westernization" title="Westernization">westernization</a>. This trend has had an <a href="/wiki/Ideological" class="mw-redirect" title="Ideological">ideological</a> side of legitimizing <a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">imperialism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Militarism" title="Militarism">militarism</a>/<a href="/wiki/Fascism" title="Fascism">fascism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Tokutomi_Soh%C5%8D" title="Tokutomi Sohō">Tokutomi Sohō</a> published a magazine in which he argued for <a href="/wiki/Liberal_democracy" title="Liberal democracy">liberal democracy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Populism" title="Populism">populism</a> against Japanese <a href="/wiki/Westernization" title="Westernization">westernization</a>. However, he was disillusioned with the <a href="/wiki/Bourgeois" class="mw-redirect" title="Bourgeois">bourgeois</a> who should play a political part in ... <a href="/wiki/Kuga_Katsunan" title="Kuga Katsunan">Kuga Katsunan</a> regarded Japanese <a href="/wiki/Political_culture" title="Political culture">political culture</a> and national <a href="/wiki/Tradition" title="Tradition">tradition</a> very highly. He aimed for restoration and enhancement of national emotion; however, he was not a narrow-minded <a href="/wiki/Nationalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Nationalist">nationalist</a>. He criticized the military and argued for a <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_system" title="Parliamentary system">parliamentary system</a> of government and expansion of <a href="/wiki/Suffrage" title="Suffrage">suffrage</a>.
</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Restoration" title="Meiji Restoration">Meiji Restoration</a>, Japanese government protected <a href="/wiki/Shinto" title="Shinto">Shinto</a> and treated it well not as a special religion but as <a href="/wiki/State_Shinto" title="State Shinto">State Shinto</a>. The government closely related Shinto with the holy <a href="/wiki/Emperor" title="Emperor">emperor</a>, and they used Shinto as a tool for their state <a href="/wiki/Governance" title="Governance">governance</a>. State Shinto was clearly distinguished from private sects of Shinto <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religion</a>. It was a model of <a href="/wiki/Ideological" class="mw-redirect" title="Ideological">ideological</a> state governance to form State Shinto and to promulgate the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Rescript_on_Education" title="Imperial Rescript on Education">Imperial Rescript on Education</a>. Meiji <a href="/wiki/Statism" title="Statism">statism</a> attempted to restore national sovereignty and pursued <a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">imperialism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">colonialism</a> through <a href="/wiki/First_Sino-Japanese_War" title="First Sino-Japanese War">Sino-</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russo-Japanese_War" title="Russo-Japanese War">Russo-Japanese Wars</a>. However, its <a href="/wiki/Militaristic" class="mw-redirect" title="Militaristic">militaristic</a> trend developed to <a href="/wiki/Ultra-nationalism" class="mw-redirect" title="Ultra-nationalism">ultra-nationalism</a>. <a href="/wiki/Kita_Ikki" class="mw-redirect" title="Kita Ikki">Kita Ikki</a> advocated the exclusion of the <a href="/wiki/Zaibatsu" title="Zaibatsu">zaibatsu</a>, senior statesmen and <a href="/wiki/Political_parties" class="mw-redirect" title="Political parties">political parties</a> and the establishment of government for direct connection with the emperor and the people.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Yanagita_Kunio" class="mw-redirect" title="Yanagita Kunio">Yanagita Kunio</a> was at the forefront of study of Japanese <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a>. He named general public who are not political leaders and <a href="/wiki/Intellectual" title="Intellectual">intellectuals</a> as “jomin”. Other folklorists are <a href="/wiki/Minakata_Kumagusu" title="Minakata Kumagusu">Minakata Kumagusu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yanagi_Soetsu" class="mw-redirect" title="Yanagi Soetsu">Yanagi Muneyoshi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orikuchi_Shinobu" class="mw-redirect" title="Orikuchi Shinobu">Orikuchi Shinobu</a>.
</p><p>In pre-war Japan, <a href="/wiki/German_philosophy" title="German philosophy">German philosophy</a> was eagerly studied and introduced. However, from the late <a href="/wiki/Meiji_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Meiji period">Meiji</a> to <a href="/wiki/Taish%C5%8D_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Taishō period">Taishō period</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_School" title="Kyoto School">Kyoto School</a> attempted to harmonize Western thought with Eastern thought such as <a href="/wiki/Zen_Buddhism" class="mw-redirect" title="Zen Buddhism">Zen Buddhism</a>. <a href="/wiki/Nishida_Kitaro" class="mw-redirect" title="Nishida Kitaro">Nishida Kitaro</a> established an original thought by fusion of Zen and Western thought. His thought is called as Nishida philosophy. He insisted on pure <a href="/wiki/Experience" title="Experience">experience</a> in which there is no opposition between subjectivity and objectivity.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> His <a href="/wiki/Ontology" title="Ontology">ontology</a> derived from absolute nothingness. <a href="/wiki/Watsuji_Tetsuro" class="mw-redirect" title="Watsuji Tetsuro">Watsuji Tetsuro</a> criticized Western selfish <a href="/wiki/Individualism" title="Individualism">individualism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> His <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethics</a> says human beings are not isolated <a href="/wiki/Existence" title="Existence">existence</a> but related existence. He insisted that individual and social beings should be aware of their own individuality and social membership. He is also well known as his <i>Climate and Culture</i> in which he studied the relationship between natural environment and local lifestyle.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_Japanese_philosophy">Contemporary Japanese philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Contemporary Japanese philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After World War II, many academic philosophers have published books on Continental philosophy and American philosophy. Among those <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shozo_Omori&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Shozo Omori (page does not exist)">Shozo Omori</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wataru_Hiromatsu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Wataru Hiromatsu (page does not exist)">Wataru Hiromatsu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yasuo_Yuasa" title="Yasuo Yuasa">Yasuo Yuasa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Takaaki_Yoshimoto" title="Takaaki Yoshimoto">Takaaki Yoshimoto</a> created original works under the influence of <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phenomenology_(philosophy)" title="Phenomenology (philosophy)">phenomenology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Analytic_philosophy" title="Analytic philosophy">analytic philosophy</a>. Shozo Omori created a unique monist epistemology based on his concepts of "representation monism", "double depiction", and "language animism". Wataru Hiromatsu developed his theory of "multi-subjective ontological structure of the world". Yasuo Yuasa advanced a new theory of the body influenced by <a href="/wiki/Merleau-Ponty" class="mw-redirect" title="Merleau-Ponty">Merleau-Ponty</a> and the body image found in <a href="/wiki/Chinese_medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese medicine">Chinese medicine</a>. Takaaki Yoshimoto is famous for his "shared illusion theory" and various philosophical essays on Japanese culture. Today, such scholars as <a href="/wiki/Kojin_Karatani" title="Kojin Karatani">Kojin Karatani</a> (<a href="/wiki/Literary_theory" title="Literary theory">literary theory</a>), <a href="/wiki/Hitoshi_Nagai" title="Hitoshi Nagai">Hitoshi Nagai</a> (<a href="/wiki/Solipsism" title="Solipsism">solipsism</a>), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shigeki_Noya&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Shigeki Noya (page does not exist)">Shigeki Noya</a> (<a href="/wiki/Analytic_philosophy" title="Analytic philosophy">analytic philosophy</a>), <a href="/wiki/Masahiro_Morioka" title="Masahiro Morioka">Masahiro Morioka</a> (<a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_life" title="Philosophy of life">philosophy of life</a>), and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Motoyoshi_Irifuji&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Motoyoshi Irifuji (page does not exist)">Motoyoshi Irifuji</a> (<a href="/wiki/Analytic_philosophy" title="Analytic philosophy">analytic philosophy</a>) are considered to be characteristic philosophers in the Japanese academy.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
