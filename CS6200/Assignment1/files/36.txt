	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Ms.</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This article is about the title. For the magazine, see <a href="/wiki/Ms._(magazine)" title="Ms. (magazine)">Ms. (magazine)</a>. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/MS_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="MS (disambiguation)">MS (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p><i><b>Ms</b></i> or <i><b>Ms.</b></i> (normally <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;z&#39; in &#39;zoom&#39;">z</span></span>/</a></span></span>, but also <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="&#39;z&#39; in &#39;zoom&#39;">z</span></span>/</a></span></span>, or <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span></span>/</a></span></span> when unstressed)<sup id="cite_ref-OED_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> is an <a href="/wiki/English_honorific" class="mw-redirect" title="English honorific">English honorific</a> used with the last name or full name of a woman, intended as a default form of address for women regardless of marital status.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Like <i><a href="/wiki/Miss" title="Miss">Miss</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Mrs." title="Mrs.">Mrs</a>.</i>, the term <i>Ms.</i> has its origins in the female English title once used for all women, <i><a href="/wiki/Mistress_(form_of_address)" title="Mistress (form of address)">Mistress</a></i>. It originated in the 17th century and was revived into mainstream usage in the 20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-Zimmer_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zimmer-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In the UK and the majority of <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a> countries, a full stop (period) is usually not used with the title; in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> a period is usually used (see <a href="/wiki/Abbreviation#History" title="Abbreviation">Abbreviation</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-OED_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_development_and_revival_of_the_term">Historical development and revival of the term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ms.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historical development and revival of the term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Miss" title="Miss">Miss</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mrs." title="Mrs.">Mrs.</a>, both derived from the then formal Mistress, like Mister did not originally indicate marital status.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Ms was another acceptable abbreviation for Mistress, in England, in the 17th and 18th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-Buxton_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Buxton-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> During the 19th century, however, Mrs. and Miss came to be associated almost exclusively with marital status.<sup id="cite_ref-Luu_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Luu-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Ms. was popularized as an alternative in the 20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The earliest known proposal for the modern revival of Ms. as a title appeared in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Republican_(Springfield,_Massachusetts)" title="The Republican (Springfield, Massachusetts)">The Republican</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Springfield,_Massachusetts" title="Springfield, Massachusetts">Springfield, Massachusetts</a>, on November 10, 1901:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>There is a void in the English language which, with some diffidence, we undertake to fill. Every one has been put in an embarrassing position by ignorance of the status of some woman. To call a maiden Mrs is only a shade worse than to insult a matron with the inferior title Miss. Yet it is not always easy to know the facts...
</p><p>Now, clearly, what is needed is a more comprehensive term which does homage to the sex without expressing any views as to their domestic situation, and what could be simpler or more logical than the retention of what the two doubtful terms have in common. The abbreviation Ms is simple, it is easy to write, and the person concerned can translate it properly according to circumstances. For oral use it might be rendered as "Mizz," which would be a close parallel to the practice long universal in many bucolic regions, where a slurred Mis' does duty for Miss and Mrs alike.<sup id="cite_ref-Zimmer_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zimmer-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The term was again suggested as a convenience to writers of business letters by such publications as the <i>Bulletin of the <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Business_Communication" title="Association for Business Communication">American Business Writing Association</a></i> (1951) and <i>The Simplified Letter</i>, issued by the National Office Management Association (1952).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1961, <a href="/wiki/Sheila_Michaels" title="Sheila Michaels">Sheila Michaels</a> attempted to put the term into use when she saw what she thought was a typographical error on the address label of a copy of <i><a href="/wiki/News_%26_Letters" class="mw-redirect" title="News &amp; Letters">News &amp; Letters</a></i> sent to her roommate.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Michaels "was looking for a title for a woman who did not 'belong' to a man."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> She knew the separation of the now common terms <i>Miss</i> and <i>Mrs.</i> had derived from <i>Mistress</i>, but one could not suggest that women use the original title with its now louche connotations. Her efforts to promote use of a new honorific were at first ignored.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>In 1969, in a lull during a <a href="/wiki/WBAI" title="WBAI">WBAI</a>-radio interview with <i>The Feminists</i> group, Michaels suggested the use of <i>Ms.</i> A friend of <a href="/wiki/Gloria_Steinem" title="Gloria Steinem">Gloria Steinem</a> heard the interview and suggested it as a title for her new magazine. The magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Ms._(magazine)" title="Ms. (magazine)">Ms.</a></i> debuted on newsstands in January 1972, and its much-publicized name quickly led to widespread usage.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In February 1972, the US Government Printing Office approved using <i>Ms.</i> in official government documents.<sup id="cite_ref-&#39;70s_246_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-&#39;70s_246-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> In 1976 <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a> introduced a new <a href="/wiki/Superhero" title="Superhero">superhero</a> named <a href="/wiki/Ms._Marvel" title="Ms. Marvel">Ms. Marvel</a>, billing her as the "first feminist superhero".
</p><p>Even several public opponents of non-sexist language, such as <a href="/wiki/William_Safire" title="William Safire">William Safire</a>, were finally convinced that <i>Ms.</i> had earned a place in English by the case of <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">US Congresswoman</a> <a href="/wiki/Geraldine_Ferraro" title="Geraldine Ferraro">Geraldine A. Ferraro</a>. Ferraro, a United States vice-presidential candidate in 1984, was a married woman who used her birth surname professionally rather than her husband's (Zaccaro). Safire pointed out in 1984 that it would be equally incorrect to call her "Miss Ferraro" (as she was married) or "Mrs. Ferraro" (as her husband was not "Mr. Ferraro")—and that calling her "Mrs. Zaccaro" would confuse the reader.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ms.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Suggestions about how <i>Ms.</i> should be used, or whether it should be used at all, are varied.
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a></i> states in its style guide that <i>Ms</i> should only be used if a subject requests it herself and it "should not be used merely because we do not know whether the woman is Mrs or Miss".<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> On the other hand, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>, which restricts its use of honorific titles to <a href="/wiki/Leading_article" class="mw-redirect" title="Leading article">leading articles</a>, states in its style guide: "use Ms for women ... unless they have expressed a preference for Miss or Mrs".<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/BBC_Academy" title="BBC Academy">BBC Academy</a> style guide states, "In choosing between Miss, Mrs and Ms, try to find out what the person herself uses, and stick to that."<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> embraces the use of all three: Mrs., Miss, and Ms.,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> and will follow the individual’s preferences.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some etiquette writers and famous figures do not support the use of <i>Ms</i>, including Charles Kidd, the editor of <a href="/wiki/Debrett%27s" title="Debrett&#39;s">Debrett's</a> Peerage and Baronetage, who claims the usage is "not very helpful" and that he had been "brought up to address a married woman as Mrs John Smith, for example".<sup id="cite_ref-news.bbc.co.uk_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-news.bbc.co.uk-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Debrett%27s" title="Debrett&#39;s">Debrett's</a> itself, concerning the case of a married woman who chooses not to take her husband's name, states, "The ugly-sounding Ms is problematic. Although many women have assumed this bland epithet, it remains incorrect to use it when addressing a social letter."<sup id="cite_ref-telegraph.co.uk_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph.co.uk-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The former British <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a> <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">MP</a> <a href="/wiki/Ann_Widdecombe" title="Ann Widdecombe">Ann Widdecombe</a> has stated, "I can't see the point of Ms and I don't see it as an issue",<sup id="cite_ref-news.bbc.co.uk_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-news.bbc.co.uk-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> whilst author and journalist <a href="/wiki/Jessica_Fellowes" title="Jessica Fellowes">Jessica Fellowes</a> describes the title Ms as "ghastly".<sup id="cite_ref-telegraph.co.uk_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph.co.uk-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_English_Society" title="Queen&#39;s English Society">Queen's English Society</a> has criticised the use of Ms as "an abbreviation that is not short for anything", describing it as a "linguistic misfit [that] came about because certain women suddenly became sensitive about revealing their marital status".<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On the other hand, the default use of <i>Ms.</i>, especially for business purposes, is championed by some American sources, including <a href="/wiki/Judith_Martin" title="Judith Martin">Judith Martin</a> (<i>a.k.a.</i> Miss Manners).<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Concerning business, the Emily Post Institute states, "Ms. is the default form of address, unless you know positively that a woman wishes to be addressed as Mrs."<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> <i>The American Heritage Book of English Usage</i> states, "Using Ms. obviates the need for the guesswork involved in figuring out whether to address someone as Mrs. or Miss: you can’t go wrong with Ms. Whether the woman you are addressing is married or unmarried, has changed her name or not, Ms. is always correct."<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Canada, both Ms and Ms. with a period, are often used interchangeably.
</p>
