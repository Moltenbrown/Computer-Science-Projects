	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">French Canadians</h1>

<p><b>French Canadians</b> (also referred to as <b>Franco-Canadians</b> or <b>Canadiens</b>; <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Canadien français</i> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">[kanadjɑ̃ fʁɑ̃sɛ]</a></span> or <i lang="fr" title="French language text">Canadiennes françaises</i> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">[kanadjɛn fʁɑ̃sɛz]</a></span>) are an <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_group" title="Ethnic group">ethnic group</a> who trace their ancestry to <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a> colonists who settled in <a href="/wiki/Canada_(New_France)" title="Canada (New France)">Canada</a> from the 17th century onward. Today, people of French heritage make up the majority of <a href="/wiki/French_language_in_Canada" title="French language in Canada">native speakers of French in Canada</a>, who in turn account for about 22 per cent of the country's total population. The majority of French Canadians reside in <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, where they constitute the majority of the province's population, although French-Canadian and francophone minority communities exist in all other Canadian provinces and territories as well.  Besides the <i lang="fr" title="French language text"><a href="/wiki/Qu%C3%A9b%C3%A9cois_(word)" title="Québécois (word)">Québécois</a></i>, distinct French speaking ethnic groups in Canada include the <a href="/wiki/Acadians" title="Acadians">Acadians</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Maritime_Provinces" class="mw-redirect" title="Maritime Provinces">Maritime Provinces</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Brayon" title="Brayon">Brayons</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9tis_in_Canada" title="Métis in Canada">Métis</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Prairie_Provinces" class="mw-redirect" title="Prairie Provinces">Prairie Provinces</a>, among other smaller groups.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the mid-18th century, Canadian colonists born in French Canada expanded across <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and colonized various regions, cities, and towns;<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> the French Canadian settlers originated primarily from districts in the west of France, such as <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perche" title="Perche">Perche</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beauce,_France" title="Beauce, France">Beauce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maine_(province)" title="Maine (province)">Maine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anjou" title="Anjou">Anjou</a>, <a href="/wiki/Touraine" title="Touraine">Touraine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poitou" title="Poitou">Poitou</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aunis" title="Aunis">Aunis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Angoumois" title="Angoumois">Angoumois</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saintonge" title="Saintonge">Saintonge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gascony" title="Gascony">Gascony</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Today, French Canadians live across North America. Most French Canadians reside in Quebec, and are more commonly referred to as <i><a href="/wiki/French-speaking_Quebecer" title="French-speaking Quebecer">Quebecers</a></i> or <i lang="fr" title="French language text"><a href="/wiki/Qu%C3%A9b%C3%A9cois_people" title="Québécois people">Québécois</a></i>, although smaller communities exist throughout Canada and in the United States. Between 1840 and 1930, roughly 900,000 French Canadians emigrated to the United States, mostly to the <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> region.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Acadians" title="Acadians">Acadians</a> (<i>Acadiens</i>), who reside in <a href="/wiki/The_Maritimes" title="The Maritimes">the Maritimes</a>, may be included among the French Canadian group in linguistic contexts, but are considered a separate group from the French Canadians in a cultural sense due to their distinct history, much of which predates the admission of the Maritime Provinces to <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Confederation" title="Canadian Confederation">Canadian Confederation</a> in 1867.
</p><p>French Canadians (including those who are no longer French-speaking) constitute the second largest ethnic group in Canada, behind those of <a href="/wiki/English_Canadians" title="English Canadians">English</a> ancestry, and ahead of those of <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Canadians" title="Scottish Canadians">Scottish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irish_Canadians" title="Irish Canadians">Irish</a> heritage; there is nevertheless a distinction between those identifying as French Canadians and those simply identifying as French. In total, those whose ethnic origins are French Canadian, French, Québécois and Acadian number up to 11.9 million people or comprising 33.78% of the Canadian population.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Not all francophone Canadians are of French-Canadian descent or heritage, as the body of French language speakers in Canada also includes significant immigrant communities from other francophone countries such as <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a> or <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> — and not all French Canadians are francophone, as a significant number of people who have French Canadian ethnic roots are native English speakers.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=French_Canadians&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The French Canadians get their name from <i><a href="/wiki/Canada,_New_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Canada, New France">Canada</a></i>, the most developed and densely populated region of <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">New France</a> during the period of <a href="/wiki/French_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="French colonization of the Americas">French colonization</a> in the 17th and 18th centuries. The original use of the term <i>Canada</i> referred to the land area along the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lawrence_River" title="Saint Lawrence River">St. Lawrence River</a>, divided in three districts (<a href="/wiki/Quebec_City" title="Quebec City">Québec</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trois-Rivi%C3%A8res" title="Trois-Rivières">Trois-Rivières</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montréal</a>), as well as to the <i><a href="/wiki/Pays_d%27en_Haut" title="Pays d&#39;en Haut">Pays d'en Haut</a></i> (Upper Countries), a vast and thinly settled territorial dependence north and west of Montreal which covered the whole of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> area.
</p><p>From 1535 to the 1690s, the French word <i>Canadien</i> had referred to the <a href="/wiki/First_Nations" title="First Nations">First Nations</a> the French had encountered in the St. Lawrence River valley at <a href="/wiki/Stadacona" title="Stadacona">Stadacona</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hochelaga_(village)" title="Hochelaga (village)">Hochelaga</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> At the end of the 17th century, <i>Canadien</i> became an <a href="/wiki/Ethnonym" title="Ethnonym">ethnonym</a> distinguishing the inhabitants of Canada from those of France.  After World War I, English-Canadians appropriated the term "Canadian" and French-Canadians began identifying as <a href="/wiki/Qu%C3%A9b%C3%A9cois_people" title="Québécois people">Québécois</a> instead.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetics">Genetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=French_Canadians&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Genetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inbreeding">Inbreeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=French_Canadians&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Inbreeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>French Canadians of <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> are a classical example of founder population. Over 150 years of French colonization, between 1608 and 1760, an estimated 8,500 pioneers married and left at least one descendant on the territory.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Following the takeover of the colony by the British crown in 1760, immigration from France effectively stopped, but descendants of French settlers continued to grow in number mainly because of high fertility rate. Intermarriage occurred mostly with the deported <a href="/wiki/Acadians" title="Acadians">Acadians</a> and migrants coming from the British Isles. Since the 20th century, immigration in Quebec and mixing of French Canadians involve people from all over the world. While the French Canadians of Quebec today may be partly of other ancestries, the genetic contribution of the original French founders is predominant, explaining about 90% of regional gene pools, while Acadians (descended from other French settlers in eastern Canada) explain<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness"><span title="This information is too vague. (May 2019)">vague</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> 4% and British 2%, with Native American and other groups contributing less.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2011)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Identities">Identities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=French_Canadians&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Identities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>French Canadians living in Canada express their cultural identity using a number of terms. The <i>Ethnic Diversity Survey</i> of the 2006 Canadian census<sup id="cite_ref-Daily_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Daily-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EDS_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EDS-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-questions_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-questions-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> found that French-speaking Canadians identified their ethnicity most often as <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a>, French Canadians, <a href="/wiki/French-speaking_Quebecer" title="French-speaking Quebecer">Québécois</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Acadian" class="mw-redirect" title="Acadian">Acadian</a>. The latter three were grouped together by Jantzen (2006) as "French New World" ancestries because they originate in Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-Jantzen_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jantzen-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Jantzen (2006) distinguishes the English <i>Canadian</i>, meaning "someone whose family has been in Canada for multiple generations", and the French <i>Canadien</i>, used to refer to descendants of the original settlers of New France in the 17th and 18th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> "Canadien" was used to refer to the French-speaking residents of New France beginning in the last half of the 17th century. The English-speaking residents who arrived later from Great Britain were called "Anglais". This usage continued until <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Confederation" title="Canadian Confederation">Canadian Confederation</a> in 1867.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Confederation united several former British colonies into the Dominion of Canada, and from that time forward, the word "Canadian" has been used to describe both English-speaking and French-speaking citizens, wherever they live in the country.
</p><p>Those reporting "French New World" ancestries overwhelmingly had ancestors that went back at least four generations in Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Fourth generation Canadiens and Québécois showed considerable attachment to their ethno-cultural group, with 70% and 61%, respectively, reporting a strong sense of belonging.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The generational profile and strength of identity of French New World ancestries contrast with those of British or Canadian ancestries, which represent the largest ethnic identities in Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Although deeply rooted Canadians express a deep attachment to their ethnic identity, most English-speaking Canadians of British or Canadian ancestry generally cannot trace their ancestry as far back in Canada as French-speakers.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> As a result, their identification with their ethnicity is weaker: for example, only 50% of third generation "Canadians" strongly identify as such, bringing down the overall average.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The survey report notes that 80% of Canadians whose families had been in Canada for three or more generations reported "Canadian and provincial or regional ethnic identities". These identities include French New World ancestries such as "Québécois" (37% of Quebec population), "Acadian" (6% of Atlantic provinces).<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quebec">Quebec</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=French_Canadians&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Quebec">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/French-speaking_Quebecer" title="French-speaking Quebecer">French-speaking Quebecer</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Quebec_langues.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Quebec_langues.png/220px-Quebec_langues.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="249" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Quebec_langues.png/330px-Quebec_langues.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Quebec_langues.png/440px-Quebec_langues.png 2x" data-file-width="3311" data-file-height="3745" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Quebec_langues.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Languages in Quebec</div></div></div>
<p>Since the 1960s, French Canadians in Quebec have generally used <i>Québécois</i> (masculine) or <i>Québécoise</i> (feminine) to express their cultural and national identity, rather than <i>Canadien français</i> and <i>Canadienne française</i>. Francophones who self-identify as Québécois and do not have French-Canadian ancestry may not identify as "French Canadian" (<i>Canadien</i> or <i>Canadien français</i>). Those who do have French or French-Canadian ancestry, but who support <a href="/wiki/Quebec_sovereignty" class="mw-redirect" title="Quebec sovereignty">Quebec sovereignty</a>, often find <i>Canadien français</i> to be archaic or even pejorative. This is a reflection of the strong social, cultural, and political ties that most Quebecers of French-Canadian origin, who constitute a majority of <a href="/wiki/Francophone" class="mw-redirect" title="Francophone">francophone</a> Quebecers, maintain within Quebec. It has given Québécois an ambiguous meaning<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> which has often played out in <a href="/wiki/Qu%C3%A9b%C3%A9cois_nation_motion" title="Québécois nation motion">political issues</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> as all public institutions attached to the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Quebec" title="Government of Quebec">Government of Quebec</a> refer to all Quebec citizens, regardless of their language or their cultural heritage, as Québécois.
</p><p>Academic analysis of French Canadian culture has often focused on the degree to which the Quiet Revolution, particularly the shift in the social and cultural identity of the Québécois following the <a href="/wiki/Estates_General_of_French_Canada" title="Estates General of French Canada">Estates General of French Canada</a> of 1966 to 1969, did or did not create a "rupture" between the Québécois and other francophones elsewhere in Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elsewhere_in_Canada">Elsewhere in Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=French_Canadians&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Elsewhere in Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:StBonifaceCollege.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/StBonifaceCollege.jpg/170px-StBonifaceCollege.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/StBonifaceCollege.jpg/255px-StBonifaceCollege.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/StBonifaceCollege.jpg/340px-StBonifaceCollege.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1488" data-file-height="1984" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:StBonifaceCollege.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Universit%C3%A9_de_Saint-Boniface" title="Université de Saint-Boniface">Université de Saint-Boniface</a> in Manitoba</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Censusdivisions-ethnic.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Censusdivisions-ethnic.png/220px-Censusdivisions-ethnic.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="192" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Censusdivisions-ethnic.png/330px-Censusdivisions-ethnic.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Censusdivisions-ethnic.png/440px-Censusdivisions-ethnic.png 2x" data-file-width="1388" data-file-height="1214" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Censusdivisions-ethnic.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Major ethnicities in Canada</div></div></div>
<p>The emphasis on the French language and Quebec autonomy means that French-speakers across Canada may now self-identify as <i>québécois(e)</i>, <i>acadien(ne)</i>, or <i>Franco-canadien(ne)</i>, or as provincial linguistic minorities such as <i>Franco-manitobain(e)</i>, <i>Franco-ontarien(ne)</i> or <i>fransaskois(e)</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Education, health and social services are provided by provincial institutions, so that provincial identities are often used to identify French-language institutions:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Franco-Newfoundlander" title="Franco-Newfoundlander">Franco-Newfoundlanders</a>, province of <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland and Labrador</a>, also known as Terre-Neuvien(ne)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Franco-Ontarian" title="Franco-Ontarian">Franco-Ontarians</a>, province of <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, also referred to as Ontarien(ne)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Franco-Manitoban" title="Franco-Manitoban">Franco-Manitobans</a>, province of <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a>, also referred to as Manitobain(e)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fransaskois" title="Fransaskois">Fransaskois</a>, province of <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a>, also referred to Saskois(e)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Franco-Albertan" class="mw-redirect" title="Franco-Albertan">Franco-Albertans</a>, province of <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>, also referred to Albertain(e)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Franco-Columbian" title="Franco-Columbian">Franco-Columbians</a>, province of <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a> mostly live in the <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a> metro area; also referred to as Franco-Colombien(ne)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Franco-Yukonnais" title="Franco-Yukonnais">Franco-Yukonnais</a>, territory of <a href="/wiki/Yukon" title="Yukon">Yukon</a>, also referred to as Yukonais(e)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Franco-T%C3%A9nois" title="Franco-Ténois">Franco-Ténois</a>, territory of <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Territories" title="Northwest Territories">Northwest Territories</a>, also referred to as Ténois(e)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Franco-Nunavois" class="mw-redirect" title="Franco-Nunavois">Franco-Nunavois</a>, territory of <a href="/wiki/Nunavut" title="Nunavut">Nunavut</a>, also referred to as Nunavois(e)</li></ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Acadians" title="Acadians">Acadians</a> residing in the provinces of <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prince_Edward_Island" title="Prince Edward Island">Prince Edward Island</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a> represent a distinct francophone culture. This group's culture and history evolved separately from the French Canadian culture of Quebec, at a time when the Maritime Provinces were <i>not</i> part of what was referred to as Canada, and are consequently considered a distinct culture from French Canadians.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Brayon" title="Brayon">Brayons</a> in <a href="/wiki/Madawaska_County,_New_Brunswick" title="Madawaska County, New Brunswick">Madawaska County</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aroostook_County,_Maine" title="Aroostook County, Maine">Aroostook County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a> may be identified with either the Acadians or the Québécois, or considered a distinct group in their own right, by different sources.
</p><p>French Canadians outside Quebec are more likely to self-identify as "French Canadian". Identification with provincial groupings varies from province to province, with Franco-Ontarians, for example, using their provincial label far more frequently than Franco-Columbians do. Some identify <i>only</i> with the provincial groupings, explicitly rejecting "French Canadian" as an identity label.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=French_Canadians&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Americans" title="Canadian Americans">Canadian Americans</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_Americans" title="French Americans">French Americans</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:French1346.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/French1346.gif/220px-French1346.gif" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/French1346.gif/330px-French1346.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/French1346.gif/440px-French1346.gif 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="375" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:French1346.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Maps_of_American_ancestries" class="mw-redirect" title="Maps of American ancestries">Distribution</a> of <a href="/wiki/French_American" class="mw-redirect" title="French American">French Americans</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a></div></div></div>
<p>During the mid-18th century, French Canadian explorers and colonists colonized other parts of North America in what are today <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> (called <i>Louisianais</i>), <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, far northern <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Peninsula_of_Michigan" title="Upper Peninsula of Michigan">Upper Peninsula of Michigan</a> as well as around <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Illinois_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Illinois-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> They also founded such cities as <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a> and <a href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Louis, Missouri">St. Louis</a> and villages in the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Valley" class="mw-redirect" title="Mississippi Valley">Mississippi Valley</a>. French Canadians later emigrated in large numbers from Canada to the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> between the 1840s and the 1930s in search of economic opportunities in border communities and industrialized portions of <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Emigration_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emigration-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> French-Canadian communities in the United States remain along the <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> border in <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a>, as well as further south in <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>. There is also a significant community of French Canadians in <a href="/wiki/South_Florida" title="South Florida">South Florida</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Hollywood,_Florida" title="Hollywood, Florida">Hollywood, Florida</a>, especially during the winter months. The wealth of Catholic churches named after <a href="/wiki/Louis_IX_of_France" title="Louis IX of France">St. Louis</a> throughout New England is indicative of the French immigration to the area. They came to identify as <a href="/wiki/French_American" class="mw-redirect" title="French American">Franco-American</a>, especially those who were born American.
</p><p>Distinctions between French Canadian, natives of France, and other New World French identities is more blurred in the U.S. than in Canada, but those who identify as French Canadian or Franco American generally do not regard themselves as French. Rather, they identify culturally, historically, and ethnically with the culture that originated in Quebec that is differentiated from French culture. In <i>L'avenir du français aux États-Unis</i>, <a href="/wiki/Calvin_Veltman" title="Calvin Veltman">Calvin Veltman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beno%C3%AEt_Lacroix" title="Benoît Lacroix">Benoît Lacroix</a> found that since the French language has been so widely abandoned in the United States, the term "French Canadian" has taken on an ethnic rather than linguistic meaning.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>French Canadian identities are influenced by historical events that inform regional cultures. For example, in New England, the relatively recent immigration (19th/20th centuries) is informed by experiences of language oppression and an identification with certain occupations, such as the mill workers. In the Great Lakes, many French Canadians also identify as <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9tis" title="Métis">Métis</a> and trace their ancestry to the earliest <a href="/wiki/Voyageurs" title="Voyageurs">voyageurs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Habitants" title="Habitants">settlers</a>; many also have ancestry dating to the lumber era and often a mixture of the two groups.
</p><p>The main Franco-American regional identities are:
</p>
<ul><li>French Canadians:
<ul><li>French Canadians of the Great Lakes (including <a href="/wiki/Muskrat_French" title="Muskrat French">Muskrat French</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/New_England_French" title="New England French">New England French</a></li></ul></li>
<li>Creoles:
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Missouri_French" title="Missouri French">Missouri French</a> (and other people of French ancestry in the former <a href="/wiki/Illinois_Country" title="Illinois Country">Illinois Country</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Louisiana_Creole_people" title="Louisiana Creole people">Louisiana Creoles</a> (who speak <a href="/wiki/Colonial_French" class="mw-redirect" title="Colonial French">Colonial French</a>)</li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cajuns" title="Cajuns">Cajuns</a></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population">Population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=French_Canadians&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PlaceDArmes_by_Msteckiw.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/59/PlaceDArmes_by_Msteckiw.jpg/170px-PlaceDArmes_by_Msteckiw.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/59/PlaceDArmes_by_Msteckiw.jpg/255px-PlaceDArmes_by_Msteckiw.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/59/PlaceDArmes_by_Msteckiw.jpg/340px-PlaceDArmes_by_Msteckiw.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PlaceDArmes_by_Msteckiw.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Place d'Armes in Montreal, historic heart of French Canada</div></div></div>
<p>People who today claim some French-Canadian ancestry or heritage number some 7 million in Canada and 2.4 million people in the United States. (An additional 8.4 million Americans claim French ancestry; they are treated as a separate ethnic group by the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Census_Bureau" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Census Bureau">U.S. Census Bureau</a>.)
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_in_Canada">Distribution in Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=French_Canadians&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Distribution in Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Canada, 85% of French Canadians reside in <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> where they constitute the majority of the population in all regions except the far North. Most cities and villages in this province were built and settled by the French or French Canadians during the French colonial rule.
</p><p>There are various urban and small centres in Canada outside Quebec that have long-standing populations of French Canadians, going back to the late 19th century, due to <a href="/wiki/Interprovincial_migration_in_Canada" title="Interprovincial migration in Canada">interprovincial migration</a>. <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Ontario" title="Eastern Ontario">Eastern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ontario" title="Northern Ontario">Northern</a> <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> have large populations of francophones in communities such as <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">Ottawa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornwall,_Ontario" title="Cornwall, Ontario">Cornwall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hawkesbury,_Ontario" title="Hawkesbury, Ontario">Hawkesbury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greater_Sudbury" title="Greater Sudbury">Sudbury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Welland" title="Welland">Welland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Timmins" title="Timmins">Timmins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Windsor,_Ontario" title="Windsor, Ontario">Windsor</a>. Many also pioneered the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Prairies" title="Canadian Prairies">Canadian Prairies</a> in the late 18th century, founding the towns of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Boniface,_Manitoba" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Boniface, Manitoba">Saint Boniface, Manitoba</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Peace_Country" class="mw-redirect" title="Peace Country">Peace Country</a>, including the region of <a href="/wiki/Grande_Prairie" title="Grande Prairie">Grande Prairie</a>.
</p><p>The following table shows the population of Canada's that is of French ancestry. The data is from Statistics Canada.
</p><p>The French-speaking population have massively chosen the "Canadian" ("<i>Canadien</i><span style="padding-left:.15em;">"</span>) ethnic group since the government made it possible (1986), which has made the current statistics misleading. The term <i>Canadien</i> historically referred only to a French-speaker, though today it is used in French to describe any Canadian citizen.
</p><p>It is estimated that roughly 70–75% of Quebec's population descend from the French pioneers of the 17th and 18th century.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_in_the_United_States">Distribution in the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=French_Canadians&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Distribution in the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/French_language_in_the_United_States" title="French language in the United States">French language in the United States</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:French_in_the_United_States.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/French_in_the_United_States.png/170px-French_in_the_United_States.png" decoding="async" width="170" height="195" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/French_in_the_United_States.png/255px-French_in_the_United_States.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/French_in_the_United_States.png/340px-French_in_the_United_States.png 2x" data-file-width="1064" data-file-height="1222" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:French_in_the_United_States.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Distribution of <a href="/wiki/French_language_in_the_United_States" title="French language in the United States">French in the United States</a></div></div></div>
<p>In the United States, many cities were founded as colonial outposts of <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">New France</a> by French or French-Canadian explorers. They include <a href="/wiki/Mobile,_Alabama" title="Mobile, Alabama">Mobile (Alabama)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coeur_d%27Alene,_Idaho" title="Coeur d&#39;Alene, Idaho">Coeur d'Alene (Idaho)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vincennes,_Indiana" title="Vincennes, Indiana">Vincennes (Indiana)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belleville,_Illinois" title="Belleville, Illinois">Belleville (Illinois)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bourbonnais,_Illinois" title="Bourbonnais, Illinois">Bourbonnais (Illinois)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prairie_du_Rocher,_Illinois" title="Prairie du Rocher, Illinois">Prairie du Rocher (Illinois)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dubuque,_Iowa" title="Dubuque, Iowa">Dubuque (Iowa)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baton_Rouge,_Louisiana" title="Baton Rouge, Louisiana">Baton Rouge (Louisiana)</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans (Louisiana)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit (Michigan)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biloxi,_Mississippi" title="Biloxi, Mississippi">Biloxi (Mississippi)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Creve_Coeur,_Missouri" title="Creve Coeur, Missouri">Creve Coeur (Missouri)</a>, <a href="/wiki/St._Louis" title="St. Louis">St. Louis (Missouri)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh (Fort Duquesne, Pennsylvania)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Provo,_Utah" title="Provo, Utah">Provo (Utah)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_Bay,_Wisconsin" title="Green Bay, Wisconsin">Green Bay (Wisconsin)</a>, <a href="/wiki/La_Crosse,_Wisconsin" title="La Crosse, Wisconsin">La Crosse (Wisconsin)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee" title="Milwaukee">Milwaukee (Wisconsin)</a> or <a href="/wiki/Prairie_du_Chien,_Wisconsin" title="Prairie du Chien, Wisconsin">Prairie du Chien (Wisconsin)</a>.
</p><p>The majority of the French-Canadian population in the United States is found in the New England area, although there is also a large French-Canadian presence in <a href="/wiki/Plattsburgh,_New_York" class="mw-redirect" title="Plattsburgh, New York">Plattsburgh, New York</a>, across <a href="/wiki/Lake_Champlain" title="Lake Champlain">Lake Champlain</a> from <a href="/wiki/Burlington,_Vermont" title="Burlington, Vermont">Burlington, Vermont</a>. Quebec and Acadian emigrants settled in industrial cities like <a href="/wiki/Fitchburg,_Massachusetts" title="Fitchburg, Massachusetts">Fitchburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leominster,_Massachusetts" title="Leominster, Massachusetts">Leominster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lynn,_Massachusetts" title="Lynn, Massachusetts">Lynn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Worcester,_Massachusetts" title="Worcester, Massachusetts">Worcester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waltham,_Massachusetts" title="Waltham, Massachusetts">Waltham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lowell,_Massachusetts" title="Lowell, Massachusetts">Lowell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lawrence,_Massachusetts" title="Lawrence, Massachusetts">Lawrence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chicopee,_Massachusetts" title="Chicopee, Massachusetts">Chicopee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fall_River,_Massachusetts" title="Fall River, Massachusetts">Fall River</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_Bedford,_Massachusetts" title="New Bedford, Massachusetts">New Bedford</a> in <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>; <a href="/wiki/Woonsocket,_Rhode_Island" title="Woonsocket, Rhode Island">Woonsocket</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a>; <a href="/wiki/Manchester,_New_Hampshire" title="Manchester, New Hampshire">Manchester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nashua,_New_Hampshire" title="Nashua, New Hampshire">Nashua</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a>; <a href="/wiki/Bristol,_Connecticut" title="Bristol, Connecticut">Bristol</a> in <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>; throughout the state of <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a>, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Burlington,_Vermont" title="Burlington, Vermont">Burlington</a>, <a href="/wiki/St._Albans_(city),_Vermont" title="St. Albans (city), Vermont">St. Albans</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Barre,_Vermont_(city)" class="mw-redirect" title="Barre, Vermont (city)">Barre</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Biddeford,_Maine" title="Biddeford, Maine">Biddeford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lewiston,_Maine" title="Lewiston, Maine">Lewiston</a> in <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>. Smaller groups of French Canadians settled in the Midwest, notably in the states of <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>, Illinois, <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a>, Nebraska, Iowa, Missouri, and <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>. French-Canadians also settled in central North Dakota, largely in <a href="/wiki/Rolette_County,_North_Dakota" title="Rolette County, North Dakota">Rolette</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bottineau_County,_North_Dakota" title="Bottineau County, North Dakota">Bottineau</a> counties, and in South Dakota.
</p><p>Some <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9tis_people_(Canada)" class="mw-redirect" title="Métis people (Canada)">Metis</a> still speak <a href="/wiki/Michif" title="Michif">Michif</a>, a language influenced by French, and a mixture of other European and Native American tribal languages.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=French_Canadians&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=French_Canadians&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Canadian_French" title="Canadian French">Canadian French</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Arret.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Arret.jpg/170px-Arret.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="216" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Arret.jpg/255px-Arret.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Arret.jpg/340px-Arret.jpg 2x" data-file-width="456" data-file-height="580" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Arret.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Quebec <a href="/wiki/Stop_sign" title="Stop sign">stop sign</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Canadian_French" title="Canadian French">Canadian French</a> is an umbrella term for the distinct <a href="/wiki/Variety_(linguistics)" title="Variety (linguistics)">varieties</a> of French spoken by <a href="/wiki/Francophone" class="mw-redirect" title="Francophone">francophone</a> Canadians: <a href="/wiki/French-speaking_Quebecer" title="French-speaking Quebecer">Québécois</a> (<a href="/wiki/Quebec_French" title="Quebec French">Quebec French</a>), <a href="/wiki/Acadian_French" title="Acadian French">Acadian French</a>, <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9tis_French" title="Métis French">Métis French</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_French" title="Newfoundland French">Newfoundland French</a>. Unlike Acadian French and Newfoundland French, the French of Ontario, the Canadian West, and New England all originate from what is now Quebec French and do not constitute distinct varieties from it, though there are some regional differences. French Canadians may also speak either <a href="/wiki/Canadian_English" title="Canadian English">Canadian English</a> or <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a>, especially if they live in overwhelmingly English-speaking Canadian provinces or in the United States.
</p><p>In Quebec, about six million French Canadians are native French speakers. 599,225 (7.7% of population) are English-speaking, Anglophones or <a href="/wiki/English-speaking_Quebecer" class="mw-redirect" title="English-speaking Quebecer">English-speaking Quebecers</a>, and others are <a href="/wiki/Allophone_(Quebec)" class="mw-redirect" title="Allophone (Quebec)">Allophones</a> (literally "other-speakers", meaning, in practice, immigrants who speak neither French nor English at home). In the United States, assimilation to the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a> was more significant and very few Americans of French-Canadian ancestry or heritage speak French today.
</p><p>Six million of Canada's native French speakers, of all origins, are found in the province of Quebec, where they constitute the majority language group, and another one million are distributed throughout the rest of Canada. Roughly 31% of Canadian citizens are French-speaking and 25% are of French-Canadian descent. Not all French speakers are of French descent, and not all people of French-Canadian heritage are exclusively or primarily French-speaking.
</p><p>Francophones living in Canadian provinces other than Quebec have enjoyed <a href="/wiki/Minority_language" title="Minority language">minority language</a> rights under Canadian law since at least 1969, with the <a href="/wiki/Official_Languages_Act_(Canada)" title="Official Languages Act (Canada)">Official Languages Act</a>, and under the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="Canadian Constitution">Canadian Constitution</a> since 1982, protecting them from provincial governments that have historically been indifferent towards their presence. At the provincial level, <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a> formally designates French as a full <a href="/wiki/Official_language" title="Official language">official language</a>, while other provinces vary in the level of French language services they offer. All three of Canada's territories include French as an official language of the territory alongside English and local indigenous languages, although in practice French-language services are normally available only in the capital cities and not across the entire territory.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=French_Canadians&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ste_Anne_de_Beaupr%C3%A9,_Qu%C3%A9bec.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Ste_Anne_de_Beaupr%C3%A9%2C_Qu%C3%A9bec.jpg/170px-Ste_Anne_de_Beaupr%C3%A9%2C_Qu%C3%A9bec.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Ste_Anne_de_Beaupr%C3%A9%2C_Qu%C3%A9bec.jpg/255px-Ste_Anne_de_Beaupr%C3%A9%2C_Qu%C3%A9bec.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Ste_Anne_de_Beaupr%C3%A9%2C_Qu%C3%A9bec.jpg/340px-Ste_Anne_de_Beaupr%C3%A9%2C_Qu%C3%A9bec.jpg 2x" data-file-width="480" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ste_Anne_de_Beaupr%C3%A9,_Qu%C3%A9bec.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_Sainte-Anne-de-Beaupr%C3%A9" title="Basilica of Sainte-Anne-de-Beaupré">Basilica of Sainte-Anne-de-Beaupré</a>, Sainte-Anne-de-Beaupré, Quebec</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> is the predominant religion of French Canadians, with <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholicism">Roman Catholicism</a> the chief denomination. The kingdom of <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> forbade non-Catholic settlement in <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">New France</a> from 1629 onward and thus, almost all French settlers of <a href="/wiki/Canada,_New_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Canada, New France">Canada</a> were Catholic. In the United States, some families of French-Canadian origin have converted to <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a>. Until the 1960s, religion was a central component of French-Canadian national identity. The Church parish was the focal point of civic life in French-Canadian society, and religious orders ran French-Canadian schools, hospitals and orphanages and were very influential in everyday life in general. During the <a href="/wiki/Quiet_Revolution" title="Quiet Revolution">Quiet Revolution</a> of the 1960s, however, the practice of Catholicism dropped drastically.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Church attendance in <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> currently remains low. Rates of religious observance among French Canadians outside Quebec tend to vary by region, and by age. In general, however, those in Quebec are the least observant, while those in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States of America</a> and other places away from Quebec tend to be the most observant.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=French_Canadians&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>French Canadians have <a href="/wiki/Selective_breeding" title="Selective breeding">selectively bred</a> distinct livestock over the centuries, including <a href="/wiki/Canadienne_cattle" title="Canadienne cattle">cattle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canadian_horse" title="Canadian horse">horses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chantecler_chicken" title="Chantecler chicken">chickens</a>.
</p><p>Traditionally Canadiens had a <a href="/wiki/Subsistence_agriculture" title="Subsistence agriculture">subsistence agriculture</a> in Eastern Canada (Québec),<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> this subsistence agriculture slowly evolved in dairy farm during the end of the 19th century and the beginning of 20th century while retaining the subsistence side. By 1960 agriculture changed toward an industrial agriculture.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=French_Canadians&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Voyageur_canoe.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Voyageur_canoe.jpg/220px-Voyageur_canoe.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="110" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Voyageur_canoe.jpg/330px-Voyageur_canoe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Voyageur_canoe.jpg/440px-Voyageur_canoe.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="301" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Voyageur_canoe.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Voyageurs Passing a Waterfall</i></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a> were the first <a href="/wiki/European_ethnic_groups" class="mw-redirect" title="European ethnic groups">Europeans</a> to permanently colonize what is now <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, parts of Ontario, Acadia, and select areas of Western Canada, all in Canada (See <a href="/wiki/French_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="French colonization of the Americas">French colonization of the Americas</a>.) Their colonies of <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">New France</a> (also commonly called Canada) stretched across what today are the <a href="/wiki/Maritimes" class="mw-redirect" title="Maritimes">Maritime provinces</a>, southern Quebec and <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, as well as the entire <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> Valley.
</p><p>The first permanent European settlements in Canada were at <a href="/wiki/Habitation_at_Port-Royal" class="mw-redirect" title="Habitation at Port-Royal">Port Royal</a> in 1605 and <a href="/wiki/Quebec_City" title="Quebec City">Quebec City</a> in 1608 as <a href="/wiki/North_American_fur_trade" title="North American fur trade">fur trading posts</a>. The territories of New France were <a href="/wiki/Canada_(New_France)" title="Canada (New France)">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acadia" title="Acadia">Acadia</a> (later renamed <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_(New_France)" title="Louisiana (New France)">Louisiana</a>. The inhabitants of Canada called themselves the <i>Canadiens</i>, and came mostly from northwestern France.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The early inhabitants of Acadia, or <i>Acadiens</i>, came mostly but not exclusively from the Southwestern region of France. <i>Canadien</i> explorers and fur traders would come to be known as <i><a href="/wiki/Coureurs_des_bois" class="mw-redirect" title="Coureurs des bois">coureurs des bois</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Voyageurs" title="Voyageurs">voyageurs</a></i>, while those who settled on farms in Canada would come to be known as <i><a href="/wiki/Habitants" title="Habitants">habitants</a></i>. Many French Canadians are the descendants of the <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Daughters" title="King&#39;s Daughters">King's Daughters</a> (<i>filles du roi</i>) of this era. Many also are the descendants of mixed French and <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_peoples" title="Algonquian peoples">Algonquin</a> marriages (see also <a href="/wiki/Metis_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Metis people">Metis people</a>).
</p><p>During the mid-18th century, French explorers and <i>Canadiens</i> born in French Canada colonized other parts of North America in what are today the states of <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> (called <i>Louisianais</i>), <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vincennes,_Indiana" title="Vincennes, Indiana">Vincennes, Indiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louisville,_Kentucky" title="Louisville, Kentucky">Louisville, Kentucky</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Windsor-Detroit" class="mw-redirect" title="Windsor-Detroit">Windsor-Detroit</a> region and the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_prairies" class="mw-redirect" title="Canadian prairies">Canadian prairies</a> (primarily Southern <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a>).
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cornelius_Krieghoff_001.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Cornelius_Krieghoff_001.jpg/220px-Cornelius_Krieghoff_001.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="185" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Cornelius_Krieghoff_001.jpg/330px-Cornelius_Krieghoff_001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Cornelius_Krieghoff_001.jpg/440px-Cornelius_Krieghoff_001.jpg 2x" data-file-width="450" data-file-height="378" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cornelius_Krieghoff_001.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Habitants</i> by <a href="/wiki/Cornelius_Krieghoff" title="Cornelius Krieghoff">Cornelius Krieghoff</a> (1852)</div></div></div>
<p>After the 1760 British conquest of New France in the <a href="/wiki/French_and_Indian_War" title="French and Indian War">French and Indian War</a> (known as the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years&#39; War">Seven Years' War</a> in Canada), the French-Canadian population remained important in the life of the colonies.
</p><p>The British gained Acadia by the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Utrecht" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaty of Utrecht">Treaty of Utrecht</a> in 1713. It took the 1774 <a href="/wiki/Quebec_Act" title="Quebec Act">Quebec Act</a> for French Canadians to regain the French civil law system, and in 1791 French Canadians in <a href="/wiki/Lower_Canada" title="Lower Canada">Lower Canada</a> were introduced to the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">British parliamentary</a> system when an elected <a href="/wiki/Legislative_Assembly" class="mw-redirect" title="Legislative Assembly">Legislative Assembly</a> was created.
</p><p>The Legislative Assembly having no real power, the political situation degenerated into the <a href="/wiki/Lower_Canada_Rebellion" title="Lower Canada Rebellion">Lower Canada Rebellions</a> of 1837–1838, after which Lower Canada and <a href="/wiki/Upper_Canada" title="Upper Canada">Upper Canada</a> were unified. Some of the motivations for the union was to limit French-Canadian political power and at the same time transferring a large part of the Upper Canadian debt to the debt-free Lower Canada. After many decades of British immigration, the <i>Canadiens</i> became a minority in the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Canada" title="Province of Canada">Province of Canada</a> in the 1850s.
</p><p>French-Canadian contributions were essential in securing <a href="/wiki/Responsible_government" title="Responsible government">responsible government</a> for <a href="/wiki/The_Canadas" title="The Canadas">The Canadas</a> and in undertaking <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Confederation" title="Canadian Confederation">Canadian Confederation</a>. However, over the course of the late 19th and 20th centuries, French Canadians' discontent grew with their place in Canada because of a series of events, including the execution of <a href="/wiki/Louis_Riel" title="Louis Riel">Louis Riel</a>, the elimination of official bilingualism in <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a>, Canada's participation in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Boer_War" title="Second Boer War">Second Boer War</a>, <a href="/wiki/Regulation_17" title="Regulation 17">Regulation 17</a> which banned French-language schools in Ontario, the <a href="/wiki/Conscription_Crisis_of_1917" title="Conscription Crisis of 1917">Conscription Crisis of 1917</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Conscription_Crisis_of_1944" title="Conscription Crisis of 1944">Conscription Crisis of 1944</a>.
</p><p>Between the 1840s and the 1930s, some 900,000 French Canadians emigrated to the <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> region. About half of them returned home. The generations born in the United States would eventually come to see themselves as <a href="/wiki/French_American" class="mw-redirect" title="French American">Franco-Americans</a>. During the same period of time, numerous French Canadians also <a href="/wiki/Interprovincial_migration_in_Canada" title="Interprovincial migration in Canada">emigrated</a> and settled in Eastern and Northern <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>. The descendants of those Quebec immigrants constitute the bulk of today's <a href="/wiki/Franco-Ontarian" title="Franco-Ontarian">Franco-Ontarian</a> community.
</p><p>Since 1968, French has been one of Canada's two official languages. It is the sole official language of Quebec and one of the official languages of <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yukon" title="Yukon">Yukon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Territories" title="Northwest Territories">Northwest Territories</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nunavut" title="Nunavut">Nunavut</a>. The province of <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> has no official languages defined in law, although the provincial government provides French language services in many parts of the province under the <a href="/wiki/French_Language_Services_Act" title="French Language Services Act">French Language Services Act</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_usage">Modern usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=French_Canadians&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Modern usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In English usage, the terms for provincial subgroups, if used at all, are usually defined solely by province of residence, with all of the terms being strictly interchangeable with French Canadian. Although this remains the more common usage in English, it is considered outdated to many Canadians of French descent, especially in Quebec. Most francophone Canadians who use the provincial labels identify with their province of origin, even if it is not the province in which they currently reside; for example, a Québécois who moved to Manitoba would not normally change their own self-identification to Franco-Manitoban.
</p><p>Increasingly, provincial labels are used to stress the linguistic and cultural, as opposed to ethnic and religious, nature of French-speaking institutions and organizations. The term "French Canadian" is still used in historical and cultural contexts, or when it is necessary to refer to Canadians of French-Canadian heritage collectively, such as in the name and mandate of national organizations which serve francophone communities across Canada. Francophone Canadians of non-French-Canadian origin such as immigrants from francophone countries are not usually designated by the term "French Canadian" the more general term "francophones" is used for French-speaking Canadians across all ethnic origins.
</p>
