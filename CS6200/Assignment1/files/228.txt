	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Bridget</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p><b>Bridget</b> or <b>Brigid</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Goidelic_languages" title="Goidelic languages">Gaelic</a>/<a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a> female name derived from the noun <i>brígh</i>, meaning "power, strength, vigor, virtue".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> An alternate meaning of the name is "exalted one".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Its popularity, especially in Ireland, is largely related to the popularity of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Brigid" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Brigid">Saint Brigid of Kildare</a>, who was so popular in Ireland she was known as "Mary of the <a href="/wiki/Gaels" title="Gaels">Gael</a>". This saint took on many of the characteristics of the early Celtic goddess <a href="/wiki/Brigid" title="Brigid">Brigid</a>, who was the goddess of agriculture and healing and possibly also of poetry and fire. One of her epithets was "Brigid of the Holy Fire".<sup id="cite_ref-Todd_1998,_p._23_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Todd_1998,_p._23-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In German and Scandinavian countries, the popularity of the name spread due to <a href="/wiki/Bridget_of_Sweden" title="Bridget of Sweden">Saint Bridget of Sweden</a>. 
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish language</a>, the name is spelled <i>Brighid</i> or <i>Bríd</i> and is pronounced "breed" or "breej". In the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a> language, the name is spelled <i>Brìghde</i> and is pronounced "breej-eh" At one time the name was so popular for Irish girls that Bridey was used as a slang term for an Irish girl in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English-speaking</a> countries. Some Irish servant girls were called Biddie or Biddy by their employers even if that wasn't their real first name. It has been steadily used in the United States throughout the 20th century, though never among the top 100 most popular names for girls. It was most popular in the 1970s, when it ranked as the 153rd most popular name for girls born in that decade in the United States. In 2006, it was the 349th most popular female given name in the United States; in 2007 it fell to 357th place. It was ranked as the 367th most common name for girls and women in the United States in the 1990 census.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
