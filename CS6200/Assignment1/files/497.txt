<p><b>Inheritance</b> is the practice of passing on <a href="/wiki/Property" title="Property">property</a>, <a href="/wiki/Title_(property)" title="Title (property)">titles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Debt" title="Debt">debts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rights" title="Rights">rights</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Law_of_obligations" title="Law of obligations">obligations</a> upon the <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">death</a> of an <a href="/wiki/Individual" title="Individual">individual</a>. The rules of inheritance differ among societies and have changed over time.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inheritance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In law, an <i>heir</i> is a person who is entitled to receive a share of the <a href="/wiki/Decedent" class="mw-redirect" title="Decedent">deceased</a>'s (the person who died) property, subject to the rules of inheritance in the <a href="/wiki/Jurisdiction" title="Jurisdiction">jurisdiction</a> of which the deceased was a citizen or where the deceased (decedent) died or owned property at the time of death.
</p><p>The inheritance may be either under the terms of a <a href="/wiki/Will_(law)" class="mw-redirect" title="Will (law)">will</a> or by <a href="/wiki/Intestate" class="mw-redirect" title="Intestate">intestate</a> laws if the deceased had no will.  However, the will must comply with the laws of the jurisdiction at the time it was created or it will be declared invalid (for example, some states do not recognize <a href="/wiki/Holographic_will" title="Holographic will">holographic wills</a> as valid, or only in specific circumstances) and the intestate laws then apply.
</p><p>A person does not become an heir before the death of the deceased, since the exact identity of the persons entitled to inherit is determined only then. Members of ruling noble or royal houses who are expected to become heirs are called <a href="/wiki/Heir_apparent" title="Heir apparent">heirs apparent</a> if first in line and incapable of being displaced from inheriting by another claim; otherwise, they are <a href="/wiki/Heir_presumptive" title="Heir presumptive">heirs presumptive</a>. There is a further concept of joint inheritance, pending renunciation by all but one, which is called <a href="/wiki/Co-parceners" class="mw-redirect" title="Co-parceners">coparceny</a>.
</p><p>In modern law, the terms <i>inheritance</i> and <i>heir</i> refer exclusively to succession to property by descent from a deceased dying <a href="/wiki/Intestacy" title="Intestacy">intestate</a>. Takers in property succeeded to under a will are termed generally <i><a href="/wiki/Beneficiary" title="Beneficiary">beneficiaries</a>,</i> and specifically <i>devisees</i> for <a href="/wiki/Real_property" title="Real property">real property</a>, <i>bequestees</i> for <a href="/wiki/Personal_property" title="Personal property">personal property</a> (except money), or <i>legatees</i> for money.
</p><p>Except in some jurisdictions where a person cannot be legally disinherited (such as the United States state of <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, which allows disinheritance only under specifically enumerated circumstances), a person who would be an heir under intestate laws may be disinherited completely under the terms of a will (an example is that of the will of comedian <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Lewis" title="Jerry Lewis">Jerry Lewis</a>; his will specifically disinherited his six children by his first wife, and their descendants, leaving his entire estate to his second wife).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inheritance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Historical_inheritance_systems" title="Historical inheritance systems">Historical inheritance systems</a></div>
<p>Detailed <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropological</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociological</a> studies have been made about customs of patrilineal inheritance, where only male children can inherit. Some cultures also employ <a href="/wiki/Matrilineal" class="mw-redirect" title="Matrilineal">matrilineal</a> succession, where property can only pass along the female line, most commonly going to the sister's sons of the decedent; but also, in some societies, from the mother to her daughters. Some ancient societies and most modern states employ egalitarian inheritance, without discrimination based on gender and/or birth order.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jewish_laws">Jewish laws</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inheritance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Jewish laws">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The inheritance is patrilineal. The father —that is, the owner of the land— bequeaths only to his male descendants, so the Promised Land passes from one Jewish father to his sons.
</p><p>If there were no living sons and no descendants of any previously living sons, daughters inherit. In <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt0427.htm#1">Numbers 27:1-4</a>, the daughters of Zelophehad (Mahlah, Noa, Hoglah, Milcah, and Tirzah) of the tribe of Manasseh come to Moses and ask for their father's inheritance, as they have no brothers. The order of inheritance is set out in <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt0427.htm#7">Numbers 27:7-11</a>: a man's sons inherit first, daughters if no sons, brothers if he has no children, and so on.
</p><p>Later, in <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt0436.htm#1">Numbers 36</a>, some of the heads of the families of the tribe of Manasseh come to Moses and point out that, if a daughter inherits and then marries a man not from her paternal tribe, her land will pass from her birth-tribe's inheritance into her marriage-tribe's. So a further rule is laid down: if a daughter inherits land, she must marry someone within her father's tribe. (The daughters of Zelophehad marry the sons' of their father's brothers. There is no indication that this was not their choice.)
</p><p>The tractate <a href="/wiki/Baba_Bathra" class="mw-redirect" title="Baba Bathra">Baba Bathra</a>, written during late Antiquity in Babylon, deals extensively with issues of property ownership and inheritance according to Jewish Law. Other works of Rabbinical Law, such as the Hilkhot naḥalot&#160;: mi-sefer Mishneh Torah leha-Rambam,<sup id="cite_ref-chabad.org_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chabad.org-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Sefer_(Hebrew)" title="Sefer (Hebrew)">Sefer</a> ha-yerushot: ʻim yeter ha-mikhtavim be-divre ha-halakhah be-ʻAravit uve-ʻIvrit uve-Aramit<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> also deal with inheritance issues. The first, often abbreviated to <a href="/wiki/Mishneh_Torah" title="Mishneh Torah">Mishneh Torah</a>, was written by <a href="/wiki/Maimonides" title="Maimonides">Maimonides</a> and was very important in <a href="/wiki/Jewish" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> tradition.
</p><p>All these sources agree that the firstborn son is entitled to a double portion of his father's estate: <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt0521.htm#17">Deuteronomy 21:17</a>. This means that, for example, if a father left five sons, the firstborn receives a third of the estate and each of the other four receives a sixth. If he left nine sons, the firstborn receives a fifth and each of the other eight receive a tenth.<sup id="cite_ref-chabad.org_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chabad.org-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> If the eldest surviving son is not the firstborn son, he is not entitled to the double portion.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Philo_of_Alexandria" class="mw-redirect" title="Philo of Alexandria">Philo of Alexandria</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Josephus" title="Josephus">Josephus</a><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> also comment on the <a href="/wiki/Jewish" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> laws of inheritance, praising them above other law codes of their time. They also agreed that the firstborn son must receive a double portion of his father's estate.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Christian_laws">Christian laws</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inheritance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Christian laws">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> does not specifically mention anything about inheritance rights: the only story even mentioning inheritance is that of the <a href="/wiki/Prodigal_Son" class="mw-redirect" title="Prodigal Son">Prodigal Son</a>, but that involved the father voluntarily passing his estate to his two sons prior to his death; the younger son receiving his inheritance (1/3; the older son would have received 2/3 under then existing Jewish law) and squandering it.
</p><p>The topic is generally not discussed among doctrinal statements of various denominations or sects, leaving that to be a matter of secular concern.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Islamic_laws">Islamic laws</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inheritance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Islamic laws">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Islamic_inheritance_jurisprudence" title="Islamic inheritance jurisprudence">Islamic inheritance jurisprudence</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a> introduced a number of different rights and restrictions on matters of inheritance, including general improvements to the treatment of women and family life compared to the pre-Islamic societies that existed in the Arabian Peninsula at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-EI2_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EI2-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, the Quran introduced additional heirs that were not entitled to inheritance in pre-Islamic times, mentioning nine relatives specifically of which six were female and three were male. However, the inheritance rights of women remained inferior to those of men because in Islam someone always has a responsibility of looking after a woman's expenses. According to the Quran, for example, a son is entitled to twice as much inheritance as a daughter.<sup>[<a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a>&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus%3Atext%3A2002.02.0006%3Asura%3D4%3Averse%3D11">4:11</a>]</sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The Quran also presented efforts to fix the laws of inheritance, and thus forming a complete legal system. This development was in contrast to pre-Islamic societies where rules of inheritance varied considerably.<sup id="cite_ref-EI2_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EI2-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In addition to the above changes, the Quran imposed restrictions on <a href="/wiki/Testamentary" class="mw-redirect" title="Testamentary">testamentary</a> powers of a <a href="/wiki/Muslim" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> in disposing his or her property. 
The Quran contains only three verses that give specific details of inheritance and shares, in addition to few other verses dealing with testamentary. <sup>[<a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a>&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus%3Atext%3A2002.02.0006%3Asura%3D4%3Averse%3D11,12,176">4:11,12,176</a>]</sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> But this information was used as a starting point by Muslim jurists who expounded the laws of inheritance even further using <a href="/wiki/Hadith" title="Hadith">Hadith</a>, as well as methods of juristic reasoning like <a href="/wiki/Qiyas" title="Qiyas">Qiyas</a>. Nowadays, inheritance is considered an integral part of <a href="/wiki/Sharia_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Sharia law">Sharia law</a> and its application for Muslims is mandatory, though many peoples (see <a href="/wiki/Historical_inheritance_systems" title="Historical inheritance systems">Historical inheritance systems</a>), despite being Muslim, have other inheritance customs.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Inequality">Inequality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inheritance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Inequality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Inheritance_by_amount_and_distribution_received_and_action_taken_with_inheritances_in_Great_Britain_between_2008_and_2010.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Inheritance_by_amount_and_distribution_received_and_action_taken_with_inheritances_in_Great_Britain_between_2008_and_2010.png/220px-Inheritance_by_amount_and_distribution_received_and_action_taken_with_inheritances_in_Great_Britain_between_2008_and_2010.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="828" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Inheritance_by_amount_and_distribution_received_and_action_taken_with_inheritances_in_Great_Britain_between_2008_and_2010.png/330px-Inheritance_by_amount_and_distribution_received_and_action_taken_with_inheritances_in_Great_Britain_between_2008_and_2010.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Inheritance_by_amount_and_distribution_received_and_action_taken_with_inheritances_in_Great_Britain_between_2008_and_2010.png/440px-Inheritance_by_amount_and_distribution_received_and_action_taken_with_inheritances_in_Great_Britain_between_2008_and_2010.png 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="2633" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Inheritance_by_amount_and_distribution_received_and_action_taken_with_inheritances_in_Great_Britain_between_2008_and_2010.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Inheritance by amount and distribution received and action taken with inheritances in Great Britain between 2008 and 2010</div></div></div>
<p>The distribution of the inherited wealth has varied greatly among different cultures and legal traditions. In nations using <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(legal_system)" title="Civil law (legal system)">civil law</a>, for example, the right of children to inherit wealth from parents in pre-defined ratios is enshrined in law,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> as far back as the <a href="/wiki/Code_of_Hammurabi" title="Code of Hammurabi">Code of Hammurabi</a> (ca. 1750 BC).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  In the US State of Louisiana, the only US state to use <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Code" title="Napoleonic Code">Napoleonic Code</a> for state law, this system is known as "<a href="/wiki/Forced_heirship" title="Forced heirship">forced heirship</a>" which prohibits disinheritance of adult children except for a few narrowly-defined reasons that a parent is obligated to prove.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Other legal traditions, particularly in nations using <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a>, allow inheritances to be divided however one wishes, or to disinherit any child for any reason.
</p><p>In cases of unequal inheritance, the majority might receive little while only a small number inherit a larger amount, with the lesser amount given to the daughter in the family.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The amount of inheritance is often far less than the value of a business initially given to the son, especially when a son takes over a thriving multimillion-dollar business, yet the daughter is given the balance of the actual inheritance amounting to far less than the value of business that was initially given to the son. This is especially seen in old world cultures, but continues in many families to this day.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Arguments for eliminating the disparagement of inheritance inequality include the right to property and the merit of individual allocation of capital over government wealth confiscation and redistribution, but this does not resolve what some<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (January 2018)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> describe as the problem of unequal inheritance. In terms of inheritance inequality, some economists and sociologists focus on the inter generational transmission of income or wealth which is said to have a direct impact on one's mobility (or immobility) and class position in society. Nations differ on the political structure and policy options that govern the transfer of wealth.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to the American federal government statistics compiled by Mark Zandi in 1985, the average US inheritance was $39,000. In subsequent years, the overall amount of total annual inheritance more than doubled, reaching nearly $200 billion. By 2050, there will be an estimated $25 trillion inheritance transmitted across generations.<sup id="cite_ref-Marable_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marable-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some researchers have attributed this rise to the <a href="/wiki/Baby_boomer" class="mw-redirect" title="Baby boomer">baby boomer</a> generation. Historically, the baby boomers were the largest influx of children conceived after WW2. For this reason, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Shapiro" title="Thomas Shapiro">Thomas Shapiro</a> suggests that this generation "is in the midst of benefiting from the greatest inheritance of wealth in history".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Inherited wealth may help explain why many Americans who have become rich may have had a "substantial head start".<sup id="cite_ref-Salon-20140324_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salon-20140324-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ECO-20140318_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ECO-20140318-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In September 2012, according to the <a href="/wiki/Institute_for_Policy_Studies" title="Institute for Policy Studies">Institute for Policy Studies</a>, "over 60 percent" of the <a href="/wiki/Forbes_400" title="Forbes 400">Forbes richest 400 Americans</a> "grew up in substantial privilege", and often (but not always) received substantial inheritances.<sup id="cite_ref-OW-20120924_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OW-20120924-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The French economist <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Piketty" title="Thomas Piketty">Thomas Piketty</a> studied this phenomenon in his best-selling book <i><a href="/wiki/Capital_in_the_Twenty-First_Century" title="Capital in the Twenty-First Century">Capital in the Twenty-First Century</a></i>, published in 2013.
</p><p>Other research has shown that many inheritances, large or small, are rapidly squandered.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Similarly, analysis shows that over two-thirds of high-wealth families lose their wealth within two generations, and almost 80% of high-wealth parents "feel the next generation is not financially responsible enough to handle inheritance".<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_stratification">Social stratification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inheritance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Social stratification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It has been argued that inheritance plays a significant effect on social stratification. Inheritance is an integral component of family, economic, and legal institutions, and a basic mechanism of <a href="/wiki/Class_stratification" title="Class stratification">class stratification</a>. It also affects the <a href="/wiki/Distribution_of_wealth" title="Distribution of wealth">distribution of wealth</a> at the societal level.  The total cumulative effect of inheritance on stratification outcomes takes three forms, according to scholars who have examined the subject.
</p><p>The first form of inheritance is the inheritance of <a href="/wiki/Cultural_capital" title="Cultural capital">cultural capital</a> (i.e. linguistic styles, higher status social circles, and aesthetic preferences).<sup id="cite_ref-Edited_by_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Edited_by-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The second form of inheritance is through familial interventions in the form of <i>inter vivos</i> transfers (i.e. gifts between the living), especially at crucial junctures in the life courses.  Examples include during a child's milestone stages, such as going to college, getting married, getting a job, and purchasing a home.<sup id="cite_ref-Edited_by_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Edited_by-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The third form of inheritance is the transfers of bulk estates at the time of death of the testators, thus resulting in significant economic advantage accruing to children during their adult years.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The origin of the stability of inequalities is material (personal possessions one is able to obtain) and is also cultural, rooted either in varying child-rearing practices that are geared to socialization according to social class and economic position.  Child-rearing practices among those who inherit wealth may center around favoring some groups at the expense of others at the bottom of the <a href="/wiki/Social_hierarchy" class="mw-redirect" title="Social hierarchy">social hierarchy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sociological_and_economic_effects_of_inheritance_inequality">Sociological and economic effects of inheritance inequality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inheritance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sociological and economic effects of inheritance inequality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It is further argued that the degree to which economic status and inheritance is transmitted across generations determines one's life chances in society. Although many have linked one's social origins and educational attainment to life chances and opportunities, education cannot serve as the most influential predictor of <a href="/wiki/Economic_mobility" title="Economic mobility">economic mobility</a>.  In fact, children of well-off parents generally receive better schooling and benefit from material, cultural, and genetic inheritances.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Likewise, schooling attainment is often persistent across generations and families with higher amounts of inheritance are able to acquire and transmit higher amounts of human capital.  Lower amounts of human capital and inheritance can perpetuate inequality in the housing market and higher education.  Research reveals that inheritance plays an important role in the accumulation of housing wealth.  Those who receive an inheritance are more likely to own a home than those who do not regardless of the size of the inheritance.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Often, racial or religious minorities and individuals from socially disadvantaged backgrounds receive less inheritance and wealth.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  As a result, mixed races might be excluded in inheritance privilege and are more likely to rent homes or live in poorer neighborhoods, as well as achieve lower educational attainment compared with whites in America. Individuals with a substantial amount of wealth and inheritance often intermarry with others of the same social class to protect their wealth and ensure the continuous transmission of inheritance across generations; thus perpetuating a cycle of privilege.
</p><p>Nations with the highest <a href="/wiki/Income_inequality" class="mw-redirect" title="Income inequality">income</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wealth_inequalities" class="mw-redirect" title="Wealth inequalities">wealth inequalities</a> often have the highest rates of homicide and disease (such as obesity, diabetes, and hypertension).<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> A <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> article reveals that the U.S. is the world's wealthiest nation, but "ranks twenty-ninth in life expectancy, right behind Jordan and Bosnia".  This has been regarded as highly attributed to the significant gap of inheritance inequality in the country,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> although there are clearly other factors such as the affordability of <a href="/wiki/Healthcare" class="mw-redirect" title="Healthcare">healthcare</a>.
</p><p>When social and economic inequalities centered on inheritance are perpetuated by major social institutions such as family, education, religion, etc., these differing life opportunities are argued to be transmitted from each generation.  As a result, this inequality is believed to become part of the overall social structure.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dynastic_wealth">Dynastic wealth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inheritance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Dynastic wealth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dynastic wealth is monetary inheritance that is passed on to generations that didn't earn it.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Dynastic wealth is linked to the term <a href="/wiki/Plutocracy" title="Plutocracy">Plutocracy</a>. Much has been written about the rise and influence of dynastic wealth including the bestselling book Capital in the Twenty-First Century by the French economist <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Piketty" title="Thomas Piketty">Thomas Piketty</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup></p><p><a href="/wiki/Bill_Gates" title="Bill Gates">Bill Gates</a> uses the term in his article "Why Inequality Matters".<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxation">Taxation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inheritance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Taxation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Inheritance_tax" title="Inheritance tax">Inheritance tax</a></div>
<p>Many states have <a href="/wiki/Inheritance_tax" title="Inheritance tax">inheritance taxes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Death_duty" class="mw-redirect" title="Death duty">death duties</a>, under which a portion of any estate goes to the <a href="/wiki/Forms_of_government" class="mw-redirect" title="Forms of government">government</a>.
</p>
