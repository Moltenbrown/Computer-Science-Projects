<p><b><i lang="la" title="Latin language text">De architectura</i></b> (<i><b>On architecture</b></i>, published as <i><b>Ten Books on Architecture</b></i>) is a treatise on <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a> written by the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> <a href="/wiki/Architect" title="Architect">architect</a> and military engineer <a href="/wiki/Vitruvius" title="Vitruvius">Marcus Vitruvius Pollio</a> and dedicated to his patron, the emperor <a href="/wiki/Caesar_Augustus" class="mw-redirect" title="Caesar Augustus">Caesar Augustus</a>, as a guide for <a href="/wiki/Caesar_Augustus#Building_projects" class="mw-redirect" title="Caesar Augustus">building projects</a>. As the only treatise on architecture to survive from antiquity, it has been regarded since the Renaissance as the first book on architectural theory, as well as a major source on the canon of classical architecture.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It contains a variety of information on Greek and Roman buildings, as well as prescriptions for the planning and design of military camps, cities, and structures both large (aqueducts, buildings, baths, harbours) and small (machines, measuring devices, instruments).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  Since Vitruvius published before the development of cross vaulting, domes, concrete, and other innovations associated with Imperial Roman architecture, his ten books are not regarded as a source of information on these hallmarks of Roman building design and technology.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_and_contents">Origin and contents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_architectura&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin and contents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Probably written between 30 and 15 BC,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> it combines the knowledge and views of many antique writers, both Greek and Roman, not only on architecture but on the arts, natural history and building technology. Vitruvius cites many authorities throughout the text, often praising Greek architects for their development of temple building and the orders (Doric, Ionic and Corinthian), and providing key accounts of the origins of building in the primitive hut.
</p><p>Though often cited for his famous "triad" of characteristics associated with architecture—utilitas, firmitas and venustas (utility, strength and beauty)--the aesthetic principles that influenced later treatise writers were outlined in Book III. Derived partially from Latin rhetoric (through Cicero and Varro), Vitruvian terms for order, arrangement, proportion, and fitness for intended purposes have guided architects for centuries, and continue to do so today. The Roman author also gave advice on the qualifications of an architect (Book I) and on types of architectural drawing.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The ten books or scrolls are organized as follows:
</p><p><i>De architectura</i> – <i><a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Ten_Books_on_Architecture" class="extiw" title="s:Ten Books on Architecture">Ten Books on Architecture</a></i>
</p>
<div style="upper-roman"><ol><li><a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">Town planning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a> or <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineering" title="Civil engineering">civil engineering</a> in general, and the qualifications required of an <a href="/wiki/Architect" title="Architect">architect</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineer" title="Civil engineer">civil engineer</a></li><li><a href="/wiki/Building_material" title="Building material">Building materials</a></li><li><a href="/wiki/Roman_temple" title="Roman temple">Temples</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_architecture" class="mw-redirect" title="Orders of architecture">orders of architecture</a></li><li><i>continuation of book III</i></li><li><a href="/wiki/Roman_architecture" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman architecture">Civil buildings</a></li><li><a href="/wiki/Roman_villa" title="Roman villa">Domestic buildings</a></li><li><a href="/wiki/Pavement_(architecture)" title="Pavement (architecture)">Pavements</a> and decorative <a href="/wiki/Plaster" title="Plaster">plasterwork</a></li><li>Water supplies and <a href="/wiki/Roman_aqueduct" title="Roman aqueduct">aqueducts</a></li><li>Sciences influencing architecture – <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Measurement" title="Measurement">measurement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, <a href="/wiki/History_of_sundials" title="History of sundials">sundial</a></li><li>Use and construction of <a href="/wiki/Machines" class="mw-redirect" title="Machines">machines</a> – <a href="/wiki/Roman_siege_engines" title="Roman siege engines">Roman siege engines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_mill" class="mw-redirect" title="Water mill">water mills</a>, drainage machines, <a href="/wiki/Roman_technology" title="Roman technology">Roman technology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hoist_(device)" title="Hoist (device)">hoisting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pneumatics" title="Pneumatics">pneumatics</a></li></ol></div>
<p>Roman architects were skilled in engineering, art, and craftsmanship combined. Vitruvius was very much of this type, a fact reflected in <i>De architectura</i>. He covered a wide variety of subjects he saw as touching on architecture. This included many aspects that may seem irrelevant to modern eyes, ranging from mathematics to astronomy, meteorology, and medicine. In the Roman conception, architecture needed to take into account everything touching on the physical and intellectual life of man and his surroundings.
</p><p>Vitruvius, thus, deals with many theoretical issues concerning architecture. For instance, in Book II of <i>De architectura</i>, he advises architects working with bricks to familiarise themselves with pre-Socratic theories of matter so as to understand how their materials will behave. Book IX relates the abstract <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a> of <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> to the everyday work of the <a href="/wiki/Surveyor_(surveying)" class="mw-redirect" title="Surveyor (surveying)">surveyor</a>. <a href="/wiki/Astrology" title="Astrology">Astrology</a> is cited for its insights into the organisation of human life, while <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a> is required for the understanding of <a href="/wiki/Sundial" title="Sundial">sundials</a>. Likewise, Vitruvius cites <a href="/wiki/Ctesibius" title="Ctesibius">Ctesibius of Alexandria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Archimedes" title="Archimedes">Archimedes</a> for their inventions, <a href="/wiki/Aristoxenus" title="Aristoxenus">Aristoxenus</a> (<a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>'s apprentice) for music, <a href="/wiki/Agatharchus" title="Agatharchus">Agatharchus</a> for theatre, and <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Terentius_Varro" title="Marcus Terentius Varro">Varro</a> for architecture.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Buildings">Buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_architectura&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Greekhse1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Greekhse1.jpg/220px-Greekhse1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="125" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Greekhse1.jpg/330px-Greekhse1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Greekhse1.jpg/440px-Greekhse1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1060" data-file-height="603" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Greekhse1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Greek house plan by Vitruvius</div></div></div>
<p>Vitruvius sought to address the ethos of architecture, declaring that quality depends on the social relevance of the artist's work, not on the form or workmanship of the work itself. Perhaps the most famous declaration from <i>De architectura</i> is one still quoted by architects:  "Well building hath three conditions: firmness, commodity, and delight."  This quote is taken from Sir <a href="/wiki/Henry_Wotton" title="Henry Wotton">Henry Wotton</a>'s version of 1624, and is a plain and accurate translation of the passage in Vitruvius <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://penelope.uchicago.edu/Thayer/L/Roman/Texts/Vitruvius/1*.html#3.2">(I.iii.2)</a>: but English has changed since then, especially in regard to the word "commodity", and the tag is usually misunderstood. A modern interpretation of Wotton's English might render it thus: "The ideal building has three elements; it is sturdy, useful, and beautiful."
</p><p>Vitruvius also studied human proportions (Book III) and his <i>canones</i> were later encoded in a very famous drawing by <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a> (<i>Homo Vitruvianus</i>, "<a href="/wiki/Vitruvian_Man" title="Vitruvian Man">Vitruvian Man</a>").
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_technology">Roman technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_architectura&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Roman technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ballista.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Ballista.gif/220px-Ballista.gif" decoding="async" width="220" height="177" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/21/Ballista.gif 1.5x" data-file-width="288" data-file-height="232" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ballista.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Roman <a href="/wiki/Ballista" title="Ballista">Ballista</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pont_du_Gard_BLS.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Pont_du_Gard_BLS.jpg/220px-Pont_du_Gard_BLS.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="85" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Pont_du_Gard_BLS.jpg/330px-Pont_du_Gard_BLS.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Pont_du_Gard_BLS.jpg/440px-Pont_du_Gard_BLS.jpg 2x" data-file-width="12648" data-file-height="4882" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pont_du_Gard_BLS.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Pont_du_Gard" title="Pont du Gard">Pont du Gard</a> <a href="/wiki/Roman_aqueduct" title="Roman aqueduct">Roman aqueduct</a> in southern France.</div></div></div>
<p><i>De architectura</i> is important for its descriptions of many different machines used for engineering structures, such as hoists, <a href="/wiki/Crane_(machine)" title="Crane (machine)">cranes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pulleys" class="mw-redirect" title="Pulleys">pulleys</a>, as well as war machines such as <a href="/wiki/Catapult" title="Catapult">catapults</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ballistae" class="mw-redirect" title="Ballistae">ballistae</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Siege_engine" title="Siege engine">siege engines</a>.  Vitruvius also described the construction of sundials and <a href="/wiki/Water_clock" title="Water clock">water clocks</a>, and the use of an <a href="/wiki/Aeolipile" title="Aeolipile">aeolipile</a> (the first <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engine</a>) as an experiment to demonstrate the nature of atmospheric air movements (wind).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aqueducts_and_mills">Aqueducts and mills</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_architectura&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Aqueducts and mills">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Books VIII, IX, and X of <i>De architectura</i> form the basis of much of what is known about Roman technology, now augmented by archaeological studies of extant remains, such as the <a href="/wiki/Pont_du_Gard" title="Pont du Gard">Pont du Gard</a> in southern France.  Numerous such massive structures occur across the former empire, a testament to the power of <a href="/wiki/Roman_engineering" title="Roman engineering">Roman engineering</a>.  Vitruvius' description of Roman aqueduct construction is short, but mentions key details especially for the way they were surveyed, and the careful choice of materials needed.
</p><p>His book would have been of assistance to <a href="/wiki/Frontinus" title="Frontinus">Frontinus</a>, a general who was appointed in the late 1st century AD to administer the many <a href="/wiki/Aqueducts_of_Rome" class="mw-redirect" title="Aqueducts of Rome">aqueducts of Rome</a>. Frontinus wrote <i><a href="/wiki/De_aquaeductu" title="De aquaeductu">De aquaeductu</a></i>, the definitive treatise on 1st-century Roman aqueducts, and discovered a discrepancy between the intake and supply of water caused by illegal pipes inserted into the channels to divert the water. The Roman Empire went far in exploiting water power, as the set of no fewer than 16 <a href="/wiki/Water_mill" class="mw-redirect" title="Water mill">water mills</a> at <a href="/wiki/Barbegal" class="mw-redirect" title="Barbegal">Barbegal</a> in France demonstrates. The mills ground grain in a very efficient operation, and many other mills are now known, such as the much later <a href="/wiki/Hierapolis_sawmill" title="Hierapolis sawmill">Hierapolis sawmill</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Materials">Materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_architectura&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Vitruvius described many different construction materials used for a wide variety of different structures, as well as such details as <a href="/wiki/Stucco" title="Stucco">stucco</a> painting. <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">Cement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lime_(material)" title="Lime (material)">lime</a> received in-depth descriptions, the longevity of many Roman structures being mute testimony to their skill in building materials and design.
</p><p>Vitruvius advised that <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> should not be used to conduct drinking water, clay pipes being preferred.  He comes to this conclusion in Book VIII of <i>De architectura</i> after empirical observation of the apparent laborer illnesses in the <a href="/wiki/Plumbum" class="mw-redirect" title="Plumbum">plumbum</a> foundries of his time. In 1986, the United States banned the use of lead in plumbing due to lead poisoning's neurological damage. However, much of the water used by Rome and many other cities was very hard, and coated the inner surfaces of the pipes, so <a href="/wiki/Lead_poisoning" title="Lead poisoning">lead poisoning</a> was unlikely.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:VitruviusTenBooksMHMorgan1914p295.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/VitruviusTenBooksMHMorgan1914p295.gif/220px-VitruviusTenBooksMHMorgan1914p295.gif" decoding="async" width="220" height="88" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/81/VitruviusTenBooksMHMorgan1914p295.gif 1.5x" data-file-width="310" data-file-height="124" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:VitruviusTenBooksMHMorgan1914p295.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Design for an Archimedean water-screw</div></div></div>
<p>Vitruvius related the famous story about Archimedes and his detection of adulterated gold in a royal crown. When Archimedes realized the volume of the crown could be measured exactly by the displacement created in a bath of water, he ran into the street with the cry of <a href="/wiki/Eureka_(word)" title="Eureka (word)"><i>"Eureka!"</i></a>, and the discovery enabled him to compare the density of the crown with pure gold. He showed the crown had been alloyed with silver, and the king was defrauded.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dewatering_machines">Dewatering machines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_architectura&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dewatering machines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Archscrew2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Archscrew2.jpg/220px-Archscrew2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="283" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Archscrew2.jpg/330px-Archscrew2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Archscrew2.jpg/440px-Archscrew2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1526" data-file-height="1966" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Archscrew2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Drainage wheel from Rio Tinto mines</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:WaterwheelsSp.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/WaterwheelsSp.jpg/220px-WaterwheelsSp.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="216" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/WaterwheelsSp.jpg/330px-WaterwheelsSp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/WaterwheelsSp.jpg/440px-WaterwheelsSp.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1599" data-file-height="1571" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:WaterwheelsSp.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sequence of drainage wheels found in <a href="/wiki/Rio_Tinto_(river)" title="Rio Tinto (river)">Rio Tinto</a></div></div></div>
<p>Vitruvius described the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Archimedes%27_screw" title="Archimedes&#39; screw">Archimedes' screw</a> in Chapter 10, although did not mention Archimedes by name.  It was a device widely used for raising water to irrigate fields and dewater mines. Other lifting machines mentioned in <i>De architectura</i> include the endless chain of buckets and the <a href="/wiki/Reverse_overshot_water-wheel" title="Reverse overshot water-wheel">reverse overshot water-wheel</a>. Remains of the water wheels used for lifting water have been discovered in old mines such as those at <a href="/wiki/Rio_Tinto_(river)" title="Rio Tinto (river)">Rio Tinto</a> in Spain and <a href="/wiki/Dolaucothi" class="mw-redirect" title="Dolaucothi">Dolaucothi</a> in west Wales. One of the wheels from Rio Tinto is now in the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a>, and one from the latter in the <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="National Museum of Wales">National Museum of Wales</a>. The remains were discovered when these mines were reopened in modern mining attempts. They would have been used in a vertical sequence, with 16 such mills capable of raising water at least 96 feet (29&#160;m) above the water table. Each wheel would have been worked by a miner treading the device at the top of the wheel, by using cleats on the outer edge. That they were using such devices in mines clearly implies that they were entirely capable of using them as <a href="/wiki/Water_wheels" class="mw-redirect" title="Water wheels">water wheels</a> to develop power for a range of activities, not just for grinding wheat, but also probably for sawing timber, crushing ores, <a href="/wiki/Fulling" title="Fulling">fulling</a>, and so on.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Force_pump">Force pump</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_architectura&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Force pump">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ctesibius" title="Ctesibius">Ctesibius</a> is credited with the invention of the <a href="/wiki/Force_pump" class="mw-redirect" title="Force pump">force pump</a>, which Vitruvius described as being built from <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a> with valves to allow a head of water to be formed above the machine. The device is also described by <a href="/wiki/Hero_of_Alexandria" title="Hero of Alexandria">Hero of Alexandria</a> in his <i>Pneumatica</i>. The machine is operated by hand in moving a lever up and down. He mentioned its use for supplying fountains above a reservoir, although a more mundane use might be as a simple fire engine. One was found at <a href="/wiki/Roman_Silchester" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Silchester">Roman Silchester</a> or <a href="/wiki/Calleva_Atrebatum" title="Calleva Atrebatum">Calleva Atrebatum</a> in England, and another is on display at the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a>. Their functions are not described, but they are both made in bronze, just as Vitruvius specified.
</p><p>Vitruvius also mentioned the several <a href="/wiki/Automaton" title="Automaton">automatons</a> <a href="/wiki/Ctesibius" title="Ctesibius">Ctesibius</a> invented, and intended for amusement and pleasure rather than serving a useful function.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_heating">Central heating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_architectura&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Central heating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hypocaustum.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Hypocaustum.jpg/220px-Hypocaustum.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Hypocaustum.jpg/330px-Hypocaustum.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Hypocaustum.jpg/440px-Hypocaustum.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1536" data-file-height="1024" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hypocaustum.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ruins of the hypocaust under the floor of a Roman villa: The part under the <i><a href="/wiki/Exedra" title="Exedra">exedra</a></i> is covered.</div></div></div>
<p>Vitruvius described the many innovations made in building design to improve the living conditions of the inhabitants. Foremost among them is the development of the <a href="/wiki/Hypocaust" title="Hypocaust">hypocaust</a>, a type of <a href="/wiki/Central_heating" title="Central heating">central heating</a> where hot air developed by a fire was channelled under the floor and inside the walls of <a href="/wiki/Public_bath" class="mw-redirect" title="Public bath">public baths</a> and <a href="/wiki/Villa" title="Villa">villas</a>. He gave explicit instructions on how to design such buildings so <a href="/wiki/Fuel_efficiency" title="Fuel efficiency">fuel efficiency</a> is maximized; for example, the <i><a href="/wiki/Caldarium" title="Caldarium">caldarium</a></i> is next to the <i><a href="/wiki/Tepidarium" title="Tepidarium">tepidarium</a></i> followed by the <i><a href="/wiki/Frigidarium" title="Frigidarium">frigidarium</a></i>. He also advised using a type of regulator to control the heat in the hot rooms, a bronze disc set into the roof under a circular aperture, which could be raised or lowered by a pulley to adjust the ventilation. Although he did not suggest it himself, his dewatering devices such as the reverse overshot water-wheel likely were used in the larger baths to lift water to header tanks at the top of the larger <i>thermae</i>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Baths_of_Diocletian" title="Baths of Diocletian">Baths of Diocletian</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Baths_of_Caracalla" title="Baths of Caracalla">Baths of Caracalla</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Surveying_instruments">Surveying instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_architectura&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Surveying instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>That Vitruvius must have been well practised in surveying is shown by his descriptions of surveying instruments, especially the water level or <i><a href="/wiki/Chorobates" title="Chorobates">chorobates</a></i>, which he compared favourably with the <i><a href="/wiki/Groma_surveying" title="Groma surveying">groma</a></i>, a device using <a href="/wiki/Plumb_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Plumb line">plumb lines</a>. They were essential in all building operations, but especially in aqueduct construction, where a uniform gradient was important to provision of a regular supply of water without damage to the walls of the channel. He described the <i><a href="/wiki/Surveyor%27s_wheel" title="Surveyor&#39;s wheel">hodometer</a></i>, in essence a device for automatically measuring distances along roads, a machine essential for developing accurate itineraries, such as the <a href="/wiki/Peutinger_Table" class="mw-redirect" title="Peutinger Table">Peutinger Table</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sea_level_change">Sea level change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_architectura&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sea level change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Book IV Chapter 1 Subsection 4 of <i>De architectura</i> is a description of 13 <a href="/wiki/Athenian" class="mw-redirect" title="Athenian">Athenian</a> cities in <a href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" class="mw-redirect" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a>, "the land of <a href="/wiki/Caria" title="Caria">Caria</a>", in present-day Turkey. These cities are given as: <a href="/wiki/Ephesus" title="Ephesus">Ephesus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miletus" title="Miletus">Miletus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myus" title="Myus">Myus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Priene" title="Priene">Priene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samos" title="Samos">Samos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Teos" title="Teos">Teos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colophon_(city)" title="Colophon (city)">Colophon</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chius&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Chius (page does not exist)">Chius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erythrae" title="Erythrae">Erythrae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phocaea" title="Phocaea">Phocaea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clazomenae" class="mw-redirect" title="Clazomenae">Clazomenae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lebedos" class="mw-redirect" title="Lebedos">Lebedos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mytilene" title="Mytilene">Mytilene</a>, and later a 14th, <a href="/wiki/Smyrna" title="Smyrna">Smyrnaeans</a>. Myus, the third city, is described as being "long ago engulfed by the water, and its sacred rites and suffrage".  This sentence indicates, at the time of Vitruvius's writing, it was known that sea-level change and/or land <a href="/wiki/Subsidence" title="Subsidence">subsidence</a> occurred. The layout of these cities is in general from south to north so that it appears that where Myrus should be located is inland. If this is the case, then since the writing of <i>De architectura</i>, the region has experienced either <a href="/wiki/Soil_rebound" class="mw-redirect" title="Soil rebound">soil rebound</a> or a sea-level fall. Though not indicative of sea-level change, or speculation of such, during the later-empire many Roman ports suffered from what contemporary writers described as 'silting'. The constant need to dredge ports became a heavy burden on the treasury and some have speculated that this expense significantly contributed to the eventual collapse of the empire. Roman salt works in <a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a>, England, today are located at the five-metre contour, implying this was the coastline.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> These observations only indicate the extent of silting and soil rebound affecting coastline change since the writing of <i>De architectura</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Survival_and_rediscovery">Survival and rediscovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_architectura&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Survival and rediscovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vitruvius' work is one of many examples of Latin texts that owe their survival to the palace <i><a href="/wiki/Scriptorium" title="Scriptorium">scriptorium</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a> in the early 9th century. (This activity of finding and recopying classical <a href="/wiki/Manuscript" title="Manuscript">manuscripts</a> is part of what is called the <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_Renaissance" title="Carolingian Renaissance">Carolingian Renaissance</a>.) Many of the surviving manuscripts of Vitruvius' work derive from an existing manuscript that was written there, <a href="/wiki/British_Library" title="British Library">British Library</a> manuscript Harley 2767.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>These texts were not just copied, but also known at the court of Charlemagne, since his historian, bishop <a href="/wiki/Einhard" title="Einhard">Einhard</a>, asked the visiting English churchman <a href="/wiki/Alcuin" title="Alcuin">Alcuin</a> for explanations of some technical terms. In addition, a number of individuals are known to have read the text or have been indirectly influenced by it, including: Vussin, <a href="/wiki/Hrabanus_Maurus" class="mw-redirect" title="Hrabanus Maurus">Hrabanus Maurus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hermann_of_Reichenau" title="Hermann of Reichenau">Hermann of Reichenau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hugo_of_St._Victor" class="mw-redirect" title="Hugo of St. Victor">Hugo of St. Victor</a>, Gervase of Melkey, <a href="/wiki/William_of_Malmesbury" title="William of Malmesbury">William of Malmesbury</a>, Theoderich of St. Trond, <a href="/wiki/Peter_the_Deacon" title="Peter the Deacon">Petrus Diaconus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Albertus_Magnus" title="Albertus Magnus">Albertus Magnus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Villani" title="Filippo Villani">Filippo Villani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean_de_Montreuil" title="Jean de Montreuil">Jean de Montreuil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petrarch" title="Petrarch">Petrarch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boccaccio" class="mw-redirect" title="Boccaccio">Boccaccio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_de_Dondi" class="mw-redirect" title="Giovanni de Dondi">Giovanni de Dondi</a>, Domenico di Bandino, <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Acciaioli" title="Niccolò Acciaioli">Niccolò Acciaioli</a> bequeathed copy to the <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_San_Lorenzo,_Florence" class="mw-redirect" title="Basilica of San Lorenzo, Florence">Basilica of San Lorenzo, Florence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bernward_of_Hildesheim" title="Bernward of Hildesheim">Bernward of Hildesheim</a>, and <a href="/wiki/St._Thomas_Aquinas" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Thomas Aquinas">St. Thomas Aquinas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In 1244 the Dominican friar <a href="/wiki/Vincent_of_Beauvais" title="Vincent of Beauvais">Vincent of Beauvais</a> made a large number of references to <i>De architectura</i> in his compendium of all the knowledge of the Middle Ages <i>"Speculum maius"</i>.
</p><p>Many copies of <i>De architectura</i>, dating from the 8th to the 15th centuries, did exist in manuscript form during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> and 92 are still available in public collections, but they appear to have received little attention, possibly due to the obsolescence of many specialized Latin terms used by Vitruvius<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and the loss of most of the original 10 illustrations thought by some to be helpful in understanding parts of the text.
</p><p>Vitruvius' work was "rediscovered" in 1414 by the <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florentine</a> <a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">humanist</a> <a href="/wiki/Poggio_Bracciolini" title="Poggio Bracciolini">Poggio Bracciolini</a>, who found it in the Abbey of <a href="/wiki/St_Gallen" class="mw-redirect" title="St Gallen">St Gallen</a>, Switzerland. He publicized the manuscript to a receptive audience of <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> thinkers, just as interest in the classical cultural and scientific heritage was reviving.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:De_Architectura030.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/De_Architectura030.jpg/220px-De_Architectura030.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="313" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/De_Architectura030.jpg/330px-De_Architectura030.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/De_Architectura030.jpg/440px-De_Architectura030.jpg 2x" data-file-width="647" data-file-height="921" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:De_Architectura030.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Vitruvian_Man" title="Vitruvian Man">Vitruvian Man</a></i>, illustration in the edition of <i>De architectura</i> by Vitruvius; illustrated edition by Cesare Cesariano (1521)</div></div></div>
<p>The first printed edition (<i>editio princeps</i>), an <i><a href="/wiki/Incunabula" class="mw-redirect" title="Incunabula">incunabula</a></i> version,  was published by the <a href="/wiki/Verona" title="Verona">Veronese</a> scholar <a href="/wiki/Fra_Giovanni_Sulpitius" class="mw-redirect" title="Fra Giovanni Sulpitius">Fra Giovanni Sulpitius</a> in 1486 (with a second edition in 1495 or 1496), but none were illustrated. The <a href="/wiki/Dominican_friar" class="mw-redirect" title="Dominican friar">Dominican friar</a> <a href="/wiki/Fra_Giovanni_Giocondo" class="mw-redirect" title="Fra Giovanni Giocondo">Fra Giovanni Giocondo</a> produced the first version illustrated with <a href="/wiki/Woodcut" title="Woodcut">woodcuts</a> in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> in 1511. It had a thorough philosophical approach and superb illustrations.
</p><p>Translations into Italian were in circulation by the 1520s, the first in print being the translation with new illustrations by <a href="/wiki/Cesare_Cesariano" title="Cesare Cesariano">Cesare Cesariano</a>, a Milanese friend of the architect <a href="/wiki/Bramante" class="mw-redirect" title="Bramante">Bramante</a>, printed in <a href="/wiki/Como" title="Como">Como</a> in 1521. It was rapidly translated into other European languages&#160;&#8211;&#32;the first German version was published in 1528&#160;&#8211;&#32;and the first French versions followed in 1547 (but contained many mistakes). The first Spanish translation was published in 1582 by Miguel de Urrea and Juan Gracian. The most authoritative and influential edition was publicized in French in 1673 by <a href="/wiki/Claude_Perrault" title="Claude Perrault">Claude Perrault</a>, commissioned by <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Colbert" title="Jean-Baptiste Colbert">Jean-Baptiste Colbert</a> in 1664.
</p><p>The first English translation followed in 1692, although <a href="/wiki/John_Shute_(architect)" title="John Shute (architect)">John Shute</a> had drawn on the text as early as 1563 for his book <i>The First and Chief Grounds of Architecture</i>. The 1692 translation was an abridgment based on the French version of Claude Perrault.  English-speakers had to wait until 1771 for a full translation of the first five volumes and 1791 for the whole thing. Sir <a href="/wiki/Henry_Wotton" title="Henry Wotton">Henry Wotton</a>'s 1624 version <i>The Elements of Architecture</i> was more of a free adaptation than a literal translation, while a 1692 translation was much abbreviated. Thanks to the art of printing, Vitruvius' work had become a popular subject of <a href="/wiki/Hermeneutics" title="Hermeneutics">hermeneutics</a>, with highly detailed and interpretive illustrations, and became widely dispersed.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_architectura&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Da_Vinci_Vitruve_Luc_Viatour.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Da_Vinci_Vitruve_Luc_Viatour.jpg/220px-Da_Vinci_Vitruve_Luc_Viatour.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="299" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Da_Vinci_Vitruve_Luc_Viatour.jpg/330px-Da_Vinci_Vitruve_Luc_Viatour.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Da_Vinci_Vitruve_Luc_Viatour.jpg/440px-Da_Vinci_Vitruve_Luc_Viatour.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2258" data-file-height="3070" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Da_Vinci_Vitruve_Luc_Viatour.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Vitruvian_man" class="mw-redirect" title="Vitruvian man">Vitruvian man</a></i>, Leonardo da Vinci, (c. 1485-1490)</div></div></div>
<p>The rediscovery of Vitruvius' work had a profound influence on architects of the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>, prompting the rebirth of Classical architecture in subsequent centuries. Renaissance architects, such as <a href="/wiki/Niccoli" class="mw-redirect" title="Niccoli">Niccoli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Brunelleschi" title="Filippo Brunelleschi">Brunelleschi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leon_Battista_Alberti" title="Leon Battista Alberti">Leon Battista Alberti</a>, found in <i>De architectura</i> their rationale for raising their branch of knowledge to a <a href="/wiki/Scientific_discipline" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific discipline">scientific discipline</a> as well as emphasising the skills of the <a href="/wiki/Artisan" title="Artisan">artisan</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>'s  best known drawing, the <i><a href="/wiki/Vitruvian_man" class="mw-redirect" title="Vitruvian man">Vitruvian man</a></i>, is based on the concepts of proportion developed by Vitruvius.
</p><p>The English architect <a href="/wiki/Inigo_Jones" title="Inigo Jones">Inigo Jones</a> and the Frenchman <a href="/wiki/Salomon_de_Caus" title="Salomon de Caus">Salomon de Caus</a> were among the first to re-evaluate and implement those disciplines that Vitruvius considered a necessary element of architecture: arts and <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">sciences</a> based upon number and <a href="/wiki/Proportion_(architecture)" title="Proportion (architecture)">proportion (architecture)</a>. The 16th-century architect <a href="/wiki/Palladio" class="mw-redirect" title="Palladio">Palladio</a> considered Vitruvius his master and guide, and made some drawings based on Vitruvius' work before conceiving his own architectural precepts.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Astrolabe">Astrolabe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_architectura&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Astrolabe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The earliest evidence of use of the <a href="/wiki/Stereographic_projection" title="Stereographic projection">stereographic projection</a> in a machine is in <i>De architectura</i> which describes an anaphoric clock (it is presumed, a clepsydra or <a href="/wiki/Water_clock" title="Water clock">water clock</a>) in Alexandria. The clock had a rotating field of stars behind a wire frame indicating the hours of the day. The wire framework (the spider) and the star locations were constructed using the stereographic projection. Similar constructions dated from the 1st to 3rd centuries have been found in Salzburg and northeastern France, so such mechanisms were, it is presumed, fairly widespread among Romans.
</p>
