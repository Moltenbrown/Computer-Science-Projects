	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Hill</h1>

<p>A <b>hill</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Landform" title="Landform">landform</a> that extends above the surrounding terrain. It often has a distinct <a href="/wiki/Summit_(topography)" class="mw-redirect" title="Summit (topography)">summit</a>, although in areas with <a href="/wiki/Escarpment" title="Escarpment">scarp/dip topography</a> a <i>hill</i> may refer to the  particular section of flat terrain without a massive summit (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Box_Hill,_Surrey" title="Box Hill, Surrey">Box Hill, Surrey</a>).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Chocolate_Hills.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Chocolate_Hills.jpg/220px-Chocolate_Hills.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Chocolate_Hills.jpg/330px-Chocolate_Hills.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Chocolate_Hills.jpg/440px-Chocolate_Hills.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Chocolate_Hills.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Chocolate_Hills" title="Chocolate Hills">Chocolate Hills</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tuscan_views_at_sunset_(5790020702).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Tuscan_views_at_sunset_%285790020702%29.jpg/220px-Tuscan_views_at_sunset_%285790020702%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="115" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Tuscan_views_at_sunset_%285790020702%29.jpg/330px-Tuscan_views_at_sunset_%285790020702%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Tuscan_views_at_sunset_%285790020702%29.jpg/440px-Tuscan_views_at_sunset_%285790020702%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4288" data-file-height="2247" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tuscan_views_at_sunset_(5790020702).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hills in <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">Tuscany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a></div></div></div>
<p>The distinction between a hill and a <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountain</a> is unclear and largely subjective, but a hill is universally considered to be less tall and less <a href="/wiki/Grade_(slope)" title="Grade (slope)">steep</a> than a mountain. 
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, geographers historically regarded mountains as hills greater than 1,000 feet (304.8 meters) above sea level, which formed the basis of the plot of the 1995 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Englishman_Who_Went_Up_a_Hill_But_Came_Down_a_Mountain" class="mw-redirect" title="The Englishman Who Went Up a Hill But Came Down a Mountain">The Englishman Who Went Up a Hill But Came Down a Mountain</a></i>. In contrast, hillwalkers have tended to regard mountains as peaks 2,000 <a href="/wiki/Foot_(unit)" title="Foot (unit)">feet</a> (610&#160;<a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">m</a>) above sea level: the <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> also suggests a limit of 2,000 <a href="/wiki/Foot_(unit)" title="Foot (unit)">feet</a> (610&#160;<a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">m</a>) and Whittow<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> states "Some authorities regard eminences above 600&#160;m (1,969&#160;ft) as mountains, those below being referred to as hills." Today, a mountain is usually defined in the UK and Ireland as any summit at least 2,000 feet or 610 meters high,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-G4AW_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G4AW-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> while the official UK government's definition of a mountain is a summit of 600 meters (1,969 feet) or higher.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Some definitions include a <a href="/wiki/Topographical_prominence" class="mw-redirect" title="Topographical prominence">topographical prominence</a> requirement, typically 100 feet (30.5&#160;m) or 500 feet (152.4&#160;m).<sup id="cite_ref-G4AW_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G4AW-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In practice, mountains in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> are frequently referred to as "hills" no matter what their height, as reflected in names such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Cuillin" title="Cuillin">Cuillin</a> Hills</i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Torridon_Hills" title="Torridon Hills">Torridon Hills</a></i>. In Wales, the distinction is more a term of land use and appearance and has nothing to do with height. 
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rolling_Hills_Paranal.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Rolling_Hills_Paranal.jpg/220px-Rolling_Hills_Paranal.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Rolling_Hills_Paranal.jpg/330px-Rolling_Hills_Paranal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Rolling_Hills_Paranal.jpg/440px-Rolling_Hills_Paranal.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="1707" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rolling_Hills_Paranal.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rolling Hills <a href="/wiki/Paranal_Observatory" title="Paranal Observatory">Paranal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>For a while, the U.S. defined a mountain as being 1,000 feet (304.8&#160;m) or more tall. Any similar landform lower than this height was considered a hill. The United States Geological Survey (USGS), however, has concluded that these terms do not in fact have technical definitions in the U.S.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Great_Soviet_Encyclopedia" title="Great Soviet Encyclopedia">Great Soviet Encyclopedia</a> defined "hill" as an upland with a <a href="/wiki/Relative_height" class="mw-redirect" title="Relative height">relative height</a> up to 200&#160;m (660&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Hillock" title="Hillock">hillock</a> is a small hill. Other words include knoll and (in Scotland, Northern Ireland and northern England) its variant, knowe.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  Artificial hills may be referred to by a variety of technical names, including <a href="/wiki/Mound" title="Mound">mound</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tumulus" title="Tumulus">tumulus</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Judea_2_by_David_Shankbone.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Judea_2_by_David_Shankbone.jpg/220px-Judea_2_by_David_Shankbone.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Judea_2_by_David_Shankbone.jpg/330px-Judea_2_by_David_Shankbone.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Judea_2_by_David_Shankbone.jpg/440px-Judea_2_by_David_Shankbone.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Judea_2_by_David_Shankbone.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hills of the <a href="/wiki/Judean_Desert" class="mw-redirect" title="Judean Desert">Judean Desert</a></div></div></div>
<p>Hills may form through <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">geomorphic</a> <a href="/wiki/Phenomena" class="mw-redirect" title="Phenomena">phenomena</a>: <a href="/wiki/Geologic_fault" class="mw-redirect" title="Geologic fault">faulting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> of larger landforms such as mountains, and movement and deposition of <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a> by <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Moraine" title="Moraine">moraines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drumlin" title="Drumlin">drumlins</a> or by erosion exposing solid rock which then weathers down into a hill.) The rounded peaks of hills results from the <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">diffusive</a> movement of <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">regolith</a> covering the hill, a process known as <a href="/wiki/Downhill_creep" title="Downhill creep">downhill creep</a>.
</p><p>Various names used to describe types of hill, based on appearance and method of formation. Many such names originated in one geographical region to describe a type of hill formation peculiar to that region, though the names are often adopted by <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geologists</a> and used in a wider geographical context. These include:
</p>
<ul><li>Brae &#8211; Scottish term for a hillside or brow of a hill.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Drumlin" title="Drumlin">Drumlin</a> &#8211; an elongated whale-shaped hill formed by glacial action.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Butte" title="Butte">Butte</a> &#8211; an isolated hill with steep sides and a small flat top, formed by weathering.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Kuppe" title="Kuppe">Kuppe</a></i>  &#8211; a rounded hill or low mountain, typical of central <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tor_(rock_formation)" title="Tor (rock formation)">Tor</a> &#8211; a rock formation found on a hilltop; also used to refer to the hill, especially in <a href="/wiki/South_West_England" title="South West England">South West England</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Puy" title="Puy">Puy</a> &#8211; used especially in the <a href="/wiki/Auvergne_(region)" class="mw-redirect" title="Auvergne (region)">Auvergne</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, to describe a <a href="/wiki/Conical_hill" title="Conical hill">conical</a> <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic</a> hill.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pingo" title="Pingo">Pingo</a> &#8211; a mound of earth-covered ice found in the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_significance">Historical significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Clouds_over_hills.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Clouds_over_hills.jpg/220px-Clouds_over_hills.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Clouds_over_hills.jpg/330px-Clouds_over_hills.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Clouds_over_hills.jpg/440px-Clouds_over_hills.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="681" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Clouds_over_hills.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Clouds over hills in <a href="/wiki/Steptoe,_Washington" title="Steptoe, Washington">Steptoe, Washington</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mysore_1_06.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Mysore_1_06.jpg/220px-Mysore_1_06.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Mysore_1_06.jpg/330px-Mysore_1_06.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Mysore_1_06.jpg/440px-Mysore_1_06.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4160" data-file-height="3120" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mysore_1_06.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hill in <a href="/wiki/Mysore" title="Mysore">Mysore</a></div></div></div>
<p>Many settlements were originally built on hills, either to avoid floods (particularly if they were near a large body of water), or for defense (since they offer a good view of the surrounding land and require would-be attackers to fight uphill), or to avoid densely forested areas. For example, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Ancient Rome</a> was <a href="/wiki/Seven_hills_of_Rome" title="Seven hills of Rome">built on seven hills</a>, protecting it from invaders.
</p><p>Some settlements, particularly in the Middle East, are located on artificial hills consisting of debris (particularly <a href="/wiki/Mud_brick" class="mw-redirect" title="Mud brick">mud bricks</a>) that has accumulated over many generations. Such a location is known as a "<a href="/wiki/Tell_(archaeology)" title="Tell (archaeology)">tell</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In northern Europe, many ancient monuments are sited in heaps.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (December 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Some of these are defensive structures (such as the <a href="/wiki/Hill-forts" class="mw-redirect" title="Hill-forts">hill-forts</a> of the Iron Age), but others appear to have hardly any significance. In Britain, many churches at the tops of hills are thought to have been built on the sites of earlier pagan holy places. The <a href="/wiki/National_Cathedral" class="mw-redirect" title="National Cathedral">National Cathedral</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_DC" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington, DC">Washington, DC</a> has followed this tradition and was built on the highest hill in that city.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_significance">Military significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Military significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bunker_Hill_by_Pyle.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Bunker_Hill_by_Pyle.jpg/220px-Bunker_Hill_by_Pyle.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Bunker_Hill_by_Pyle.jpg/330px-Bunker_Hill_by_Pyle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Bunker_Hill_by_Pyle.jpg/440px-Bunker_Hill_by_Pyle.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1599" data-file-height="1060" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bunker_Hill_by_Pyle.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bunker_Hill" title="Battle of Bunker Hill">The Battle of Bunker Hill</a></div></div></div>
<p>Hills provide a major advantage to an army, giving them an elevated firing position and forcing an opposing army to charge uphill to attack them. They may also conceal forces behind them, allowing a force to lie in wait on the crest of a hill, using that crest for cover, and firing on unsuspecting attackers as they broach the hilltop. As a result, conventional military strategies often demand possession of high ground.
</p><p>Hills have been the sites of many noted battles, such as the first recorded military conflict in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> known as the battle of <a href="/wiki/Mons_Graupius" class="mw-redirect" title="Mons Graupius">Mons Graupius</a>. Modern conflicts include the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bunker_Hill" title="Battle of Bunker Hill">Battle of Bunker Hill</a> (which was actually fought on <a href="/wiki/Breed%27s_Hill" class="mw-redirect" title="Breed&#39;s Hill">Breed's Hill</a>) in the <a href="/wiki/American_War_of_Independence" class="mw-redirect" title="American War of Independence">American War of Independence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cemetery_Hill" title="Cemetery Hill">Cemetery Hill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Culp%27s_Hill" title="Culp&#39;s Hill">Culp's Hill</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Gettysburg" title="Battle of Gettysburg">Battle of Gettysburg</a>, the turning point of the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_San_Juan_Hill" title="Battle of San Juan Hill">Battle of San Juan Hill</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish–American War">Spanish–American War</a> won Americans control of <a href="/wiki/Santiago_de_Cuba" title="Santiago de Cuba">Santiago</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Alesia" title="Battle of Alesia">Battle of Alesia</a> was also fought from a hilltop <a href="/wiki/Fort" class="mw-redirect" title="Fort">fort</a>. Fighting on Mamayev Kurgan during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Stalingrad" title="Battle of Stalingrad">Battle of Stalingrad</a> and the Umurbrogol Pocket in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Peleliu" title="Battle of Peleliu">Battle of Peleliu</a> were also examples of bloody fighting for high ground. Another recent example is the <a href="/wiki/Kargil_War" title="Kargil War">Kargil War</a> between India and Pakistan. The <a href="/wiki/Great_Wall_of_China" title="Great Wall of China">Great Wall of China</a> is also an example of an advantage provider. It is built on mountain tops, and was meant to defend against invaders from the north, and among others, <a href="/wiki/Mongols" title="Mongols">Mongolians</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports_and_games">Sports and games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sports and games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Beinn_dearg_torridon.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Beinn_dearg_torridon.jpg/220px-Beinn_dearg_torridon.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Beinn_dearg_torridon.jpg/330px-Beinn_dearg_torridon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Beinn_dearg_torridon.jpg/440px-Beinn_dearg_torridon.jpg 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="384" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Beinn_dearg_torridon.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hillwalkers on <a href="/wiki/Beinn_Dearg_(Torridon)" title="Beinn Dearg (Torridon)">Beinn Dearg</a>, Scotland</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Golf_bunkers_Filton.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Golf_bunkers_Filton.jpg/220px-Golf_bunkers_Filton.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Golf_bunkers_Filton.jpg/330px-Golf_bunkers_Filton.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Golf_bunkers_Filton.jpg/440px-Golf_bunkers_Filton.jpg 2x" data-file-width="750" data-file-height="536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Golf_bunkers_Filton.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An example of a <a href="/wiki/Golf_course" title="Golf course">golf course</a> in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> that has hills</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hillwalking" class="mw-redirect" title="Hillwalking">Hillwalking</a> is a <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a> term for a form of <a href="/wiki/Hiking" title="Hiking">hiking</a> which involves the ascent of hills. The activity is usually distinguished from <a href="/wiki/Mountaineering" title="Mountaineering">mountaineering</a> as it does not involve ropes or technically difficult rock climbing, although the terms mountain and hill are often used interchangeably in Britain. Hillwalking is popular in hilly areas such as the English <a href="/wiki/Peak_District" title="Peak District">Peak District</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Scottish Highlands</a>. Many hills are categorized according to relative height or other criteria and feature on lists named after mountaineers, such as <a href="/wiki/Munro" title="Munro">Munros</a> (Scotland) and <a href="/wiki/Hill_lists_in_the_British_Isles#Wainwrights" class="mw-redirect" title="Hill lists in the British Isles">Wainwrights</a> (England). Specific activities such as "<a href="/wiki/Peak_bagging" title="Peak bagging">peak bagging</a>" (or "Munro bagging") involve climbing hills on these lists with the aim of eventually climbing every hill on the list.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cheese_rolling" class="mw-redirect" title="Cheese rolling">Cheese rolling</a> is an annual event in the <a href="/wiki/West_Country" title="West Country">West Country</a> of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> which involves rolling a wheel of <a href="/wiki/Cheese" title="Cheese">cheese</a> down a hill. Contestants stand at the top and chase the wheel of cheese to the bottom. The winner, the one who catches the cheese, gets to keep the wheel of cheese as a prize.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
