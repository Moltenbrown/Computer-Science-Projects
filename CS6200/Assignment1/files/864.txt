<p><b>Decius</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;d&#39; in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span><span title="/iː/: &#39;ee&#39; in &#39;fleece&#39;">iː</span><span title="/ʃ/: &#39;sh&#39; in &#39;shy&#39;">ʃ</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span></span>,<span class="wrap"> </span><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;d&#39; in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span><span title="/ɛ/: &#39;e&#39; in &#39;dress&#39;">ɛ</span><span title="/ʃ/: &#39;sh&#39; in &#39;shy&#39;">ʃ</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <i lang="la">Caesar Gaius Messius Quintus Trajanus Decius Augustus</i>;<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> c. 201&#160;&#8211;&#32;June 251), also known as <b>Trajan Decius</b>, was <a href="/wiki/Roman_Emperor" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Emperor">Roman Emperor</a> from 249 to 251.
</p><p>A distinguished politician during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Philippus_Arabus" class="mw-redirect" title="Philippus Arabus">Philippus Arabus</a>, Decius was proclaimed emperor by his troops after successfully putting down a rebellion in <a href="/wiki/Moesia" title="Moesia">Moesia</a>. In 249, he defeated and killed Philip near <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Verona_(249)" title="Battle of Verona (249)">Verona</a> and was recognized as emperor by the Senate afterwards. During his reign, he attempted to strengthen the Roman state and its religion, leading to the <a href="/wiki/Decian_persecution" title="Decian persecution">Decian persecution</a>, where a number of prominent Christians (including <a href="/wiki/Pope_Fabian" title="Pope Fabian">Pope Fabian</a>) were put to death.
</p><p>In the last year of his reign, Decius co-ruled with his son <a href="/wiki/Herennius_Etruscus" title="Herennius Etruscus">Herennius Etruscus</a>, until they were both killed by the <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Goths</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Abritus" title="Battle of Abritus">Battle of Abritus</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_rise_to_power">Early life and rise to power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and rise to power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Trajan_Decius_Ant.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Trajan_Decius_Ant.jpg/200px-Trajan_Decius_Ant.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="194" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Trajan_Decius_Ant.jpg/300px-Trajan_Decius_Ant.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Trajan_Decius_Ant.jpg/400px-Trajan_Decius_Ant.jpg 2x" data-file-width="507" data-file-height="493" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Trajan_Decius_Ant.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Antoninianus" title="Antoninianus">Antoninianus</a> of Trajan Decius. Inscription: IMP. C. M. Q. TRAIANVS DECIVS AVG.</div></div></div>
<p>Decius, who was born at <a href="/wiki/Budalia" class="mw-redirect" title="Budalia">Budalia</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Decius:_249_-_251_AD_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Decius:_249_-_251_AD-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Anc.Rome_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anc.Rome-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> near <a href="/wiki/Sirmium" title="Sirmium">Sirmium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Pannonia_Inferior" title="Pannonia Inferior">Pannonia Inferior</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Martinci" title="Martinci">Martinci</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sremska_Mitrovica" title="Sremska Mitrovica">Sremska Mitrovica</a> in <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>), was one of the first among a long succession of future Roman Emperors to originate from the Danube provinces, often simply called Illyricum.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Unlike some of his immediate imperial predecessors such as <a href="/wiki/Philip_the_Arab" title="Philip the Arab">Philip the Arab</a> or <a href="/wiki/Maximinus_I_(Thrax)" class="mw-redirect" title="Maximinus I (Thrax)">Maximinus</a> who did not have extensive administrative experience before assuming the throne, Decius was a distinguished <a href="/wiki/Roman_senate" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman senate">senator</a> who had served as suffect <a href="/wiki/Roman_consul" title="Roman consul">consul</a> in 232, had been governor of <a href="/wiki/Moesia" title="Moesia">Moesia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germania_Inferior" title="Germania Inferior">Germania Inferior</a> soon afterwards, served as governor of <a href="/wiki/Hispania_Tarraconensis" title="Hispania Tarraconensis">Hispania Tarraconensis</a> between 235–238, and was <a href="/wiki/Urban_prefect" class="mw-redirect" title="Urban prefect">urban prefect</a> of Rome during the early reign of Emperor Philip the Arab (Marcus Iulius Phillippus).<sup id="cite_ref-Scarre169_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scarre169-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Around 245, <a href="/wiki/Philip_the_Arab" title="Philip the Arab">Philip</a> entrusted Decius with an important command on the Danube.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> By the end of 248 or 249, Decius was sent to quell the revolt of <a href="/wiki/Pacatianus" title="Pacatianus">Pacatianus</a> and his troops in Moesia and Pannonia; some modern historians see this rebellion as a reflection of emerging Balkan separatism.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> After the collapse of the revolt, Decius let the troops proclaim him Emperor. Philip advanced against him and was killed at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Verona_(249)" title="Battle of Verona (249)">Verona</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, in September 249.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Roman_Senate" title="Roman Senate">Senate</a> then recognized Decius as Emperor, giving him the attribute <i>Traianus</i> in reference to the emperor <a href="/wiki/Trajan" title="Trajan">Trajan</a>. According to the Byzantine historian <a href="/wiki/Zosimus" title="Zosimus">Zosimus</a>, Decius was clothed in purple and forced to undertake the [burdens of] government, despite his reluctance and unwillingness.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_and_monumental_initiatives">Political and monumental initiatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Political and monumental initiatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Decius' political program was focused on the restoration of the strength of the State, both militarily opposing the external threats, and restoring the public <a href="/wiki/Piety" title="Piety">piety</a> with a program of renovation of the <a href="/wiki/State_religion" title="State religion">State religion</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reviving_the_censorship">Reviving the censorship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reviving the censorship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Either as a concession to the Senate, or perhaps with the idea of improving public morality, Decius endeavoured to revive the separate office and authority of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_censor" title="Roman censor">censor</a>. The choice was left to the Senate, who unanimously selected <a href="/wiki/Valerian_(Roman_emperor)" class="mw-redirect" title="Valerian (Roman emperor)">Valerian</a> (the future emperor). But Valerian, well aware of the dangers and difficulties attached to the office at such a time, declined the responsibility. The invasion of the <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Goths</a> and Decius' death put an end to the abortive attempt.<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Baths_of_Decius">The Baths of Decius</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Baths of Decius">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During his reign, he proceeded with several building projects in Rome, "including the Thermae Decianae or <a href="/wiki/Baths_of_Decius" title="Baths of Decius">Baths of Decius</a> on the Aventine", which was completed in 252 and survived through to the 16th century; Decius also repaired the Colosseum, which had been damaged by lightning strikes.<sup id="cite_ref-Scarre169_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scarre169-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Persecution_of_Christians">Persecution of Christians</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Persecution of Christians">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Decian_persecution" title="Decian persecution">Decian persecution</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Persecution_of_Christians_in_the_Roman_Empire#Under_Decius" title="Persecution of Christians in the Roman Empire">Persecution of Christians in the Roman Empire §&#160;Under Decius</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Byzantine_icon_St-Mercurius_1295.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Byzantine_icon_St-Mercurius_1295.jpg/170px-Byzantine_icon_St-Mercurius_1295.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="377" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Byzantine_icon_St-Mercurius_1295.jpg/255px-Byzantine_icon_St-Mercurius_1295.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Byzantine_icon_St-Mercurius_1295.jpg/340px-Byzantine_icon_St-Mercurius_1295.jpg 2x" data-file-width="525" data-file-height="1164" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Byzantine_icon_St-Mercurius_1295.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_art" title="Byzantine art">Byzantine fresco</a> of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Mercurius" title="Saint Mercurius">Saint Mercurius</a> (a Christian victim of the <a href="/wiki/Decian_persecution" title="Decian persecution">Decian persecution</a>), dated 1295, from <a href="/wiki/Ohrid" title="Ohrid">Ohrid</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Macedonia" title="North Macedonia">North Macedonia</a></div></div></div>
<p>In January 250, Decius is said to have issued one of the most remarkable Roman imperial edicts. From the numerous surviving texts from Egypt, recording the act of sacrifice, it appears that the edict itself was fairly clear:<sup id="cite_ref-Potter241_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Potter241-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>All the inhabitants of the empire were required to sacrifice before the magistrates of their community 'for the safety of the empire' by a certain day (the date would vary from place to place and the order may have been that the sacrifice had to be completed within a specified period after a community received the edict). When they sacrificed they would obtain a certificate (libellus) recording the fact that they had complied with the order.<sup id="cite_ref-Decius:_249_-_251_AD_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Decius:_249_-_251_AD-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> That is, the certificate would testify the sacrificant's loyalty to the ancestral gods and to the consumption of sacrificial food and drink as well as the names of the officials who were overseeing the sacrifice.<sup id="cite_ref-Potter241_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Potter241-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>According to D. S. Potter, Decius did not try to impose the superiority of the Roman pantheon over any other gods. It is very probable that the edict was an attempt to legitimize his position and to respond to a general unease provoked by the passing of the Roman millennium.<sup id="cite_ref-Potter243_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Potter243-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> While Decius himself may have intended the edict as a way to reaffirm his conservative vision of the Pax Romana and to reassure Rome's citizens that the empire was still secure, it nevertheless sparked a "terrible crisis of authority as various Christian bishops and their flocks reacted to it in different ways."<sup id="cite_ref-Decius:_249_-_251_AD_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Decius:_249_-_251_AD-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Measures were first taken demanding that the <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishops</a> and officers of the church make a sacrifice for the Emperor. The sacrifice was "on behalf of" (Latin <i>pro</i>) the Emperor, not <i>to</i> the Emperor, since a living Emperor was not considered <a href="/wiki/Divus" class="mw-redirect" title="Divus">divine</a>. Certificates were issued to those who satisfied the commissioners during the persecution of Christians under Decius. Forty-six such certificates have been published, all dating from 250, four of them from <a href="/wiki/Oxyrhynchus" title="Oxyrhynchus">Oxyrhynchus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Anyone, including Christian followers, who refused to offer a sacrifice for the Emperor and the Empire's well-being by a specified date risked torture and execution.<sup id="cite_ref-Scarre170_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scarre170-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> A number of prominent Christians did, in fact, refuse to make a sacrifice and were killed in the process, including <a href="/wiki/Pope_Fabian" title="Pope Fabian">Pope Fabian</a> himself in 250, and "anti-Christian feeling[s] led to killings at Carthage and Alexandria."<sup id="cite_ref-Scarre170_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scarre170-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In reality, however, towards the end of the second year of Decius' reign, "the ferocity of the [anti-Christian] persecution had eased off, and the earlier tradition of tolerance had begun to reassert itself."<sup id="cite_ref-Scarre170_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scarre170-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The Christian church, despite no indication in the surviving texts that the edict targeted any specific group, never forgot the reign of Decius whom they labelled as that "fierce tyrant".<sup id="cite_ref-Scarre170_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scarre170-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At this time, there was a second outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/Antonine_Plague" title="Antonine Plague">Antonine Plague</a>, which at its height from 251 to 266, took the lives of 5,000 daily in Rome. This outbreak is referred to as the "<a href="/wiki/Plague_of_Cyprian" title="Plague of Cyprian">Plague of Cyprian</a>" (<a href="/wiki/Cyprian" title="Cyprian">Cyprian</a> was the bishop of <a href="/wiki/Carthage" title="Carthage">Carthage</a>, where both the plague and the <a href="/wiki/Persecution_of_Christians" title="Persecution of Christians">persecution of Christians</a> were especially severe). Cyprian's biographer <a href="/wiki/Pontius_of_Carthage" title="Pontius of Carthage">Pontius</a> gave a vivid picture of the demoralizing effects of the plague<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and Cyprian moralized the event in his essay <i>De mortalitate</i>. In Carthage, the "Decian persecution", unleashed at the onset of the plague, sought out Christian scapegoats. Decius' edicts were renewed under Valerian in 253 and repealed under his son, <a href="/wiki/Gallienus" title="Gallienus">Gallienus</a>, in 260-1.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fighting_the_Goths_and_death">Fighting the Goths and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fighting the Goths and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Battle_of_Abritus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Battle_of_Abritus.jpg/220px-Battle_of_Abritus.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="232" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Battle_of_Abritus.jpg/330px-Battle_of_Abritus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Battle_of_Abritus.jpg/440px-Battle_of_Abritus.jpg 2x" data-file-width="816" data-file-height="859" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Battle_of_Abritus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Gothic Invasions of 250-251 AD</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Goths_enter_the_Balkans">The Goths enter the Balkans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Goths enter the Balkans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Barbarian" title="Barbarian">barbarian</a> incursions into the Empire were becoming more and more daring and frequent whereas the Empire was facing a serious economic crisis in Decius' time. During his brief reign, Decius engaged in important operations against the <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Goths</a>, who crossed the Danube to raid districts of Moesia and <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thrace</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> This is the first considerable occasion that the Goths — who would later come to play such an important role — appear in the historical record. The Goths under King <a href="/wiki/Cniva" title="Cniva">Cniva</a> were <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Nicopolis_ad_Istrum" title="Battle of Nicopolis ad Istrum">surprised</a> by the emperor while besieging <a href="/wiki/Nikopol,_Bulgaria" title="Nikopol, Bulgaria">Nicopolis</a> on the Danube; the Goths fled through the difficult terrain of the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a>, but then doubled back and surprised the Romans near <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Beroe" title="Battle of Beroe">Beroë</a> (modern <a href="/wiki/Stara_Zagora" title="Stara Zagora">Stara Zagora</a>), sacking their camp and dispersing the Roman troops. The Goths then moved to <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Philippopolis_(250)" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Philippopolis (250)">attack <i>Philippopolis</i></a> (modern <a href="/wiki/Plovdiv" title="Plovdiv">Plovdiv</a>), which fell into their hands.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  The governor of Thrace, <a href="/wiki/Titus_Julius_Priscus" title="Titus Julius Priscus">Titus Julius Priscus</a>, declared himself Emperor under Gothic protection in opposition to Decius but Priscus's challenge was rendered moot when he was killed soon afterwards.<sup id="cite_ref-Scarre169_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scarre169-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Then the invaders began returning to their homeland, laden with booty and captives, among them many of senatorial rank.<sup id="cite_ref-Wolfram46_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wolfram46-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Battle_of_Abritus">Battle of Abritus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Battle of Abritus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Abritus" title="Battle of Abritus">Battle of Abritus</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Herennius_Etruscus_Coin_.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Herennius_Etruscus_Coin_.jpg/220px-Herennius_Etruscus_Coin_.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="104" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Herennius_Etruscus_Coin_.jpg/330px-Herennius_Etruscus_Coin_.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Herennius_Etruscus_Coin_.jpg/440px-Herennius_Etruscus_Coin_.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1300" data-file-height="616" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Herennius_Etruscus_Coin_.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Coin of Herennius Etruscus. Inscription: HER. ETR. MES. DECIVS NOB. C. / CONCORDIA AVG. F</div></div></div>
<p>In the meantime, Decius had returned with his re-organized army, accompanied by his son Herennius Etruscus and the general <a href="/wiki/Trebonianus_Gallus" title="Trebonianus Gallus">Trebonianus Gallus</a>, intending to defeat the invaders and recover the booty. The final engagement, the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Abritus" title="Battle of Abritus">battle of Abritus</a>, in which the Goths fought with the courage of despair, under the command of Cniva, took place during the second week of June 251 on swampy ground in the <a href="/wiki/Ludogorie" title="Ludogorie">Ludogorie</a>  (region in northeastern Bulgaria which merges with Dobruja plateau and the Danube Plain to the north) near the small settlement of Abritus<sup id="cite_ref-Anc.Rome_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anc.Rome-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  or <i>Forum Terebronii</i> (modern <a href="/wiki/Razgrad" title="Razgrad">Razgrad</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jordanes" title="Jordanes">Jordanes</a> records that Decius' son <a href="/wiki/Herennius_Etruscus" title="Herennius Etruscus">Herennius Etruscus</a> was killed by an arrow early in the battle, and to cheer his men Decius exclaimed, "Let no one mourn; the death of one soldier is not a great loss to the republic." Nevertheless, Decius' army was entangled in the swamp and annihilated in this battle, while he himself was killed on the field of battle.<sup id="cite_ref-Decius:_249_-_251_AD_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Decius:_249_-_251_AD-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> As the historian <a href="/wiki/Aurelius_Victor" title="Aurelius Victor">Aurelius Victor</a> relates:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The Decii (i.e., <i>Decius</i> and his son), while pursuing the barbarians across the Danube, died through treachery at Abritus after reigning two years. ... Very many report that the son had fallen in battle while pressing an attack too boldly; that the father however, has strenuously asserted that the loss of one soldier seemed to him too little to matter. And so he resumed the war and died in a similar manner while fighting vigorously.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>One literary tradition claims that Decius was betrayed by his successor, Trebonianus Gallus, who was involved in a secret alliance with the Goths, but this cannot be substantiated and was most likely a later invention since Gallus felt compelled to adopt Decius' younger son, Gaius Valens Hostilianus, as joint emperor even though the latter was too young to rule in his own right.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> It is also unlikely that the shattered Roman legions would proclaim as emperor a traitor who was responsible for the loss of so many soldiers from their ranks.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Decius was the first Roman Emperor to die in battle against a foreign enemy.<sup id="cite_ref-Scarre170_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scarre170-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>In the <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Iran" title="Culture of Iran">Iranian culture</a>, the "Age of Decius" (<a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>: عهد دقیانوس, <i>ahd e</i> <i>deciānus</i>) refers to the very old times. When something is old and outdated, people would say "This belongs to the Age of Decius."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
