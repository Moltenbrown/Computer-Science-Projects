<p><b>Generation name</b>, variously <b>zibei</b> or <b>banci</b>, is one of the characters in a traditional <a href="/wiki/Chinese_given_name" title="Chinese given name">Chinese given name</a>, and is so called because each member of a generation (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Sibling" title="Sibling">siblings</a> and paternal <a href="/wiki/Cousin" title="Cousin">cousins</a> of the same generation) share that character.
</p><p>Where used, generation names were usually given only to males,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> although this varies from <a href="/wiki/Lineage_(anthropology)" title="Lineage (anthropology)">lineage</a> to lineage and has changed over time.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Generation_poem">Generation poem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Generation_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Generation poem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The sequence of generation names is typically prescribed and kept in record by a <b>generation poem</b> (<a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hant">班次聯</span> <i lang="zh-Latn" title="Pinyin transliteration">bāncì lián</i> or <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hant">派字歌</span> <i lang="zh-Latn" title="Pinyin transliteration">pàizì gē</i>) specific to each lineage. While it may have a <a href="/wiki/Mnemonic_device" class="mw-redirect" title="Mnemonic device">mnemonic function</a>, these poems can vary in length from around a dozen characters to hundreds of characters. Each successive character becomes the generation name for successive generations.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> After the last character of the poem is reached, the poem is usually recycled though occasionally it may be extended.
</p><p>Generation poems were usually composed by a committee of family elders whenever a new lineage was established through geographical emigration or social elevation. Thus families sharing a common generation poem are considered to also share a common ancestor and have originated from a common geographical location.
</p><p>Important examples are the generation poems of the descendants of the <a href="/wiki/Four_Sages" title="Four Sages">Four Sages</a> (<a href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E5%9B%9B%E6%B0%8F" class="extiw" title="zh:四氏">四氏</a>), <a href="/wiki/Confucius" title="Confucius">Confucius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mencius" title="Mencius">Mencius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yan_Hui" title="Yan Hui">Yan Hui</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zengzi" title="Zengzi">Zengzi</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kong_(surname)" title="Kong (surname)">Kong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meng_(surname)" title="Meng (surname)">Meng</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yan_(surname)" title="Yan (surname)">Yan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zeng" title="Zeng">Zeng</a> families. During the <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming dynasty</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Jianwen_Emperor" title="Jianwen Emperor">Emperor Jianwen</a> respected <a href="/wiki/Confucius" title="Confucius">Confucius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mencius" title="Mencius">Mencius</a> so much that he honored their families with generation poems. These generation poems were extended with the permission of the <a href="/wiki/Chongzhen_Emperor" title="Chongzhen Emperor">Chongzhen Emperor</a> of the Ming dynasty, the <a href="/wiki/Tongzhi_Emperor" title="Tongzhi Emperor">Tongzhi Emperor</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Qing dynasty</a>, and the Ministry of Interior of the <a href="/wiki/Beiyang_government" title="Beiyang government">Beiyang government</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="poem">
<p>希言公彥承，宏聞貞尚衍；<br />
興毓傳繼廣，昭憲慶繁祥；<br />
令德維垂佑，欽紹念顯揚；<br />
建道敦安定，懋修肇彝常；<br />
裕文煥景瑞，永錫世緒昌。
</p>
</div>
<p>The generation poem used by the <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">Song dynasty</a> <a href="/wiki/House_of_Zhao" title="House of Zhao">House of Zhao</a> was "若夫，元德允克、令德宜崇、師古希孟、時順光宗、良友彥士、登汝必公、不惟世子、與善之從、伯仲叔季、承嗣由同。"<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-梁永樂、趙公梃2014_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-梁永樂、趙公梃2014-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Chaffee1999_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chaffee1999-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The 42 characters were split into three groups of 14 for the offspring of Song Taizu and his two brothers.<sup id="cite_ref-Lee2004_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lee2004-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another notable generation poem is the <a href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_dynasty" title="Nguyễn dynasty">Nguyễn dynasty</a>'s <i><a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/vi:%C4%90%E1%BA%BF_h%E1%BB%87_thi" class="extiw" title="s:vi:Đế hệ thi">Đế hệ thi</a></i> (帝係詩 ‘Poem of the Generations of the Imperial Family’), created by Emperor <a href="/wiki/Minh_M%E1%BA%A1ng" title="Minh Mạng">Minh Mạng</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Practice">Practice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Generation_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Practice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Generation names may be the first or second character in a given name. Normally this position is consistent for the associated lineage. However some lineages alternate its position from generation to generation. This is quite common for <a href="/wiki/Korean_name" title="Korean name">Korean names</a>. Sometimes lineages will also share the same <a href="/wiki/Radical_(Chinese)" class="mw-redirect" title="Radical (Chinese)">radical</a> in the non-generation name.
</p><p>A related, but uncommon, custom is the practice of giving two children characters from a phrase. In Chinese, most words are composed of two or more words. For example, by taking apart the word <i>jiàn-kāng</i> <span lang="zh-Hant"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%81%A5%E5%BA%B7" class="extiw" title="wikt:健康">健康</a></span> (‘healthy’), the Wang family might name one son Wáng Jiàn (王健) and the other Wáng Kāng (王康). Another example would be <i>měi-lì</i> <span lang="zh-Hant"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%BE%8E%E4%B8%BD" class="extiw" title="wikt:美丽">美丽</a></span> (‘beautiful’). Daughters of the Zhous might be names Zhōu Měi (周美) and Zhōu Lì (周丽).
</p><p>Besides the <a href="/wiki/Han_Chinese" title="Han Chinese">Han</a> majority, the Muslim <a href="/wiki/Hui_people" title="Hui people">Hui Chinese people</a><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;N.B. 1&#93;</a></sup> also widely employed generation names, which they call <i>lunzi paibie</i>;  for instance, in the <a href="/wiki/Nasr_al-Din_(Yunnan)" title="Nasr al-Din (Yunnan)">Na family</a>, the five most recent generations used the characters Wan, Yu, Zhang, Dian, and Hong.  This practice is slowly fading since the government began keeping public records of genealogy.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Affiliation_character">Affiliation character</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Generation_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Affiliation character">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In place of a biological generation, the character could be used as an indicator of seniority and peer groups in  religious lineages. Thus, in the lay <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Buddhism" title="Chinese Buddhism">Buddhist</a> circles of Song and Yuan times, it could be Dào (<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%81%93" class="extiw" title="wikt:道">道</a> ‘<a href="/wiki/Dharma#Buddhism" title="Dharma">Dharma</a>’), Zhì (<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%99%BA" class="extiw" title="wikt:智">智</a> ‘<a href="/wiki/Praj%C3%B1%C4%81_(Buddhism)" title="Prajñā (Buddhism)">Prajñā, wisdom</a>’), Yuán (<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%9C%86" class="extiw" title="wikt:圆">圆</a> ‘Complete, All-embracing’<sup id="cite_ref-Soothill_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Soothill-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;N.B. 2&#93;</a></sup>), Pǔ (<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%99%AE" class="extiw" title="wikt:普">普</a> ‘universal’<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;N.B. 3&#93;</a></sup>), Jué (<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%A7%89" class="extiw" title="wikt:觉">觉</a> ‘<a href="/wiki/Bodhi" class="mw-redirect" title="Bodhi">Bodhi</a>, Enlightenment’), Shàn (<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%96%84" class="extiw" title="wikt:善">善</a> ‘Skillful, Virtuous’<sup id="cite_ref-Muller_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Muller-13">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>). The characters demonstrated belonging to a devotionalist group with a social status close to the family one. The affiliation character Miào (<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A6%99" class="extiw" title="wikt:妙">妙</a> ‘Profound, Marvelous’) usually was used by women, relating them to <a href="/wiki/Guanyin" title="Guanyin">Guanyin</a>, as <a href="/wiki/Guanyin#Legend_of_Miao_Shan" title="Guanyin">Miàoshàn (妙善)</a> was her name at birth.
</p><p>In a same way, taking the monastic vows meant the break with the family lineage, which was shown by application of the surname Shì (<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%87%8A" class="extiw" title="wikt:释">释</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thich" title="Thich">Thích</a> in Vietnam) in one's <a href="/wiki/Dharma_name" title="Dharma name">Dharma name</a>, the first character of <a href="/wiki/Gautama_Buddha" title="Gautama Buddha">Gautama Buddha</a>'s title in Chinese, <i>Shìjiāmóuní</i> (释迦牟尼, ‘<i>Śākyamuni</i>, literally "Sage of the Śakyas"‘).
</p>
