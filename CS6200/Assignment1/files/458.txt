<p>In the language of <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">kinship</a>, a <b>niece</b> is a daughter of a person's <a href="/wiki/Sibling" title="Sibling">sibling</a>, and a <b>nephew</b> is a son of a person's <a href="/wiki/Sibling" title="Sibling">sibling</a>. <a href="/wiki/Converse_relation" title="Converse relation">Conversely</a>, that person is the <a href="/wiki/Aunt" title="Aunt">aunt</a> or <a href="/wiki/Uncle" title="Uncle">uncle</a> of their <b>nibling</b> (niece or nephew). The relationship of aunt/uncle to niece/nephew is an example of <a href="/wiki/Second-degree_relative" title="Second-degree relative">second-degree relatives</a>, meaning that their <a href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_relationship" title="Coefficient of relationship">coefficient of relationship</a> is 25%. A half-niece is a daughter of a person's half-sibling, half-nephew is a son of a person's half-sibling and they are third-degree relative with 12.5% genetic overlap.
</p><p>A <b>niece-in-law</b> is the wife of one's nephew or the niece of one's spouse. A <b>co-niece-in-law</b> is the wife of one's nephew of one's spouse.
</p><p>The <b>nephew-in-law</b> is the husband of one's niece or the nephew of one's spouse. The <b>co-nephew-in-law</b> is the husband of one's niece of one's spouse.
</p><p>The <b>sororal niece</b> is the daughter of one's sister, the <b>sororal nephew</b> is the son of one's sister, the <b>fraternal niece</b> is the daughter of one's brother and the <b>fraternal nephew</b> is the son of one's brother.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lexicology">Lexicology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niece_and_nephew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Lexicology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word nephew is derived from the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> word <i>neveu</i> which is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin languages">Latin</a> <i>nepotem</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The term <i>nepotism</i>, meaning familial loyalty, is derived from this Latin term.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <i>Niece</i> entered <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> word <i>nece</i>, which also derives from Latin <i>nepotem</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The word <i>nibling</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">neologism</a> suggested by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Martin_(linguist)" title="Samuel Martin (linguist)">Samuel Martin</a> in 1951 as a cover term for "nephew or niece"; it is not common outside of specialist literature.<sup id="cite_ref-nibling_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nibling-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Sometimes in discussions involving analytic material or in abstract literature, terms such as <i>male nibling</i> and <i>female nibling</i> are preferred to describe nephews and nieces respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Terms such as <i>nibling</i> are also sometimes viewed as a gender-neutral alternative to terms which may be viewed as perpetuating the overgenderization of the English language.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niece_and_nephew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="box-Cleanup_rewrite plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Crystal_Clear_app_kedit.svg" class="image"><img alt="Crystal Clear app kedit.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Crystal_Clear_app_kedit.svg/40px-Crystal_Clear_app_kedit.svg.png" decoding="async" width="40" height="40" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Crystal_Clear_app_kedit.svg/60px-Crystal_Clear_app_kedit.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Crystal_Clear_app_kedit.svg/80px-Crystal_Clear_app_kedit.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="128" data-file-height="128" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This article may need to be <b>rewritten</b> to comply with Wikipedia's <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style">quality standards</a>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Niece_and_nephew&amp;action=edit">You can help</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Talk:Niece_and_nephew" title="Talk:Niece and nephew">discussion page</a> may contain suggestions.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">September 2016</span>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Historically, a nephew was the logical recipient of his uncle's <a href="/wiki/Inheritance" title="Inheritance">inheritance</a> if the latter did not have a son or daughter, although in some northern Bangladeshi societies, a nephew takes precedence over a daughter.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> This also happened in segments of medieval English law, where nephews were at times favored over daughters.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In social environments that lacked a stable home or environments such as refugee situations, uncles and fathers would equally be assigned responsibility for their sons and nephews.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Among parents, some cultures have assigned equal status in their social status to daughters and nieces. This is, for instance, the case in Indian communities in <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and the Thai <a href="/wiki/Nakhon_Phanom_Province" title="Nakhon Phanom Province">Nakhon Phanom Province</a>, where the transfer of cultural knowledge such as weaving was distributed equally among daughters, nieces and nieces-in-law by the Tai So community,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and some <a href="/wiki/Garifuna_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Garifuna people">Garifuna people</a> that would transmit languages to their nieces.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In some proselytizing communities the term <i>niece</i> was informally extended to include non-related younger female community members as a form of endearment.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Among some tribes in Manus Province of Papua New Guinea, women's roles as sisters, daughters and nieces may have taken precedence over their marital status in social importance.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In some cultures and family traditions, it is common to refer to one's <a href="/wiki/First_cousin_once-removed" class="mw-redirect" title="First cousin once-removed">first cousin once-removed</a> (the child of one's <a href="/wiki/Cousin" title="Cousin">cousin</a>), as a niece or nephew. In archaic terminology, a maternal nephew is called a <i>sister-son</i>, emphasizing the importance as a person's nearest male relative should he have no brothers or sons of his own.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Sister-son is used to describe some knights who are nephews to <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a> and is imitated by <a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">J. R. R. Tolkien</a>, especially in lists of Kings of Rohan or dwarves where the sister-son is also heir. <i>Sister-daughter</i> is a less common parallel term for niece.
</p><p>The terms <b>cousin-niece</b> and <b>cousin-nephew</b> correspond to those of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cousin-aunt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cousin-aunt (page does not exist)">cousin-aunt</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cousin-uncle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cousin-uncle (page does not exist)">cousin-uncle</a>, expressing a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fifth-degree_relative&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Fifth-degree relative (page does not exist)">fifth-degree relationship</a>. For (poorly standardized) terminology such as "cousin-uncle", "cousin-aunt", "cousin-nephew" and "cousin-niece", see <a href="/wiki/First_cousins_once_removed" class="mw-redirect" title="First cousins once removed">first cousins once removed</a>.
</p><p>For (poorly standardized) terminology such as "great-cousin-uncle", "great-cousin-aunt", "great-cousin-nephew" and "great-cousin-niece", see <a href="/wiki/First_cousins_twice_removed" class="mw-redirect" title="First cousins twice removed">first cousins twice removed</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grandniece_and_grandnephew">Grandniece and grandnephew</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niece_and_nephew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Grandniece and grandnephew">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The terms <b>grandniece</b> and <b>grandnephew</b> (or <b>great-niece</b> and <b>great-nephew</b>) correspond to those of <a href="/wiki/Grandaunt" class="mw-redirect" title="Grandaunt">grandaunt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Granduncle" class="mw-redirect" title="Granduncle">granduncle</a> (or <b>great-aunt</b> and <b>great-uncle</b>), expressing a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fourth-degree_relative&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Fourth-degree relative (page does not exist)">fourth-degree relationship</a>.
</p><p>A <b>grandniece</b>/<b>great-niece</b> is the granddaughter of one's sibling. A <b>half-grandniece</b>/<b>half-great-niece</b> is the granddaughter of one's half-sibling. A <b>grandniece-in-law</b>/<b>great-niece-in-law</b> is the wife of one's grandnephew/great-nephew or the grandniece/great-niece of one's spouse. A <b>co-grandniece-in-law</b>/<b>co-great-niece-in-law</b> is the wife of one's grandnephew/great-nephew of one's spouse.
</p><p>A <b>grandnephew</b>/<b>great-nephew</b> is the grandson of one's sibling. A <b>half-grandnephew</b>/<b>half-great-nephew</b> is the grandson of one's half-sibling. A <b>grandnephew-in-law</b>/<b>great-nephew-in-law</b> is the husband of one's grandniece/great-niece or the grandnephew/great-nephew of one's spouse. A <b>co-grandnephew-in-law</b>/<b>co-great-nephew-in-law</b> is the husband of one's grandniece/great-niece of one's spouse.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Great-grandniece_and_great-grandnephew">Great-grandniece and great-grandnephew</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niece_and_nephew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Great-grandniece and great-grandnephew">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The terms <b>great-grandniece</b> and <b>great-grandnephew</b> (or <b>great-great-niece</b> and <b>great-great-nephew</b>) correspond to those of <a href="/wiki/Great-grandaunt" class="mw-redirect" title="Great-grandaunt">great-grandaunt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Great-granduncle" class="mw-redirect" title="Great-granduncle">great-granduncle</a> (or <b>great-great-aunt</b> and <b>great-great-uncle</b>), expressing a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fifth-degree_relative&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Fifth-degree relative (page does not exist)">fifth-degree relationship</a>.
</p><p>A <b>great-grandniece</b>/<b>great-great-niece</b> is the great-granddaughter of one's sibling. A <b>half-great-grandniece</b>/<b>half-great-great-niece</b> is the great-granddaughter of one's half-sibling. A <b>great-grandniece-in-law</b>/<b>great-great-niece-in-law</b> is the wife of one's great-grandnephew/great-great-nephew or the great-grandniece/great-great-niece of one's spouse. A <b>co-great-grandniece-in-law</b>/<b>co-great-great-niece-in-law</b> is the wife of one's great-grandnephew/great-great-nephew of one's spouse.
</p><p>A <b>great-grandnephew</b>/<b>great-great-nephew</b> is the great-grandson of one's sibling. A <b>half-great-grandnephew</b>/<b>half-great-great-nephew</b> is the great-grandson of one's half-sibling. A <b>great-grandnephew-in-law</b>/<b>great-great-nephew-in-law</b> is the husband of one's great-grandniece/great-great-niece or the great-grandnephew/great-great-nephew of one's spouse. A <b>co-great-grandnephew-in-law</b>/<b>co-great-great-nephew-in-law</b> is the husband of one's great-grandniece/great-great-niece of one's spouse.
</p>
