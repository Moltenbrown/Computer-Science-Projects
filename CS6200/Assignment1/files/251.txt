	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Mystery play</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mystery_Play_Metz.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/05/Mystery_Play_Metz.jpg/220px-Mystery_Play_Metz.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/05/Mystery_Play_Metz.jpg/330px-Mystery_Play_Metz.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/05/Mystery_Play_Metz.jpg/440px-Mystery_Play_Metz.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="450" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mystery_Play_Metz.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Depiction of a performance of the Mystery Play of <a href="/wiki/Clement_of_Metz" class="mw-redirect" title="Clement of Metz">Saint Clement</a> in <a href="/wiki/Metz" title="Metz">Metz</a> during the Middle Ages.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Mystery plays</b> and <b>miracle plays</b> (they are distinguished as two different forms although the terms are often used interchangeably<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>) are among the earliest formally developed <a href="/wiki/Play_(theatre)" title="Play (theatre)">plays</a> in <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval Europe">medieval Europe</a>. Medieval mystery plays focused on the representation of <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> stories in <a href="/wiki/Church_(building)" title="Church (building)">churches</a> as <a href="/wiki/Tableau_vivant" title="Tableau vivant">tableaux</a> with accompanying <a href="/wiki/Antiphon" title="Antiphon">antiphonal</a> song. They told of subjects such as the Creation, Adam and Eve, the murder of Abel, and the <a href="/wiki/Last_Judgment" title="Last Judgment">Last Judgment</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Often they were performed together in cycles which could last for days.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The name derives from <i><a href="/wiki/Sacred_Mysteries" class="mw-redirect" title="Sacred Mysteries">mystery</a></i> used in its sense of <i><a href="/wiki/Miracle" title="Miracle">miracle</a>,</i><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> but an occasionally quoted derivation is from <i>ministerium</i>, meaning <i><a href="/wiki/Craft" title="Craft">craft</a></i>, and so the 'mysteries' or plays performed by the <a href="/wiki/Craft_guilds" class="mw-redirect" title="Craft guilds">craft guilds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mystery_play&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mystery_play_001.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Mystery_play_001.jpg/220px-Mystery_play_001.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="264" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Mystery_play_001.jpg/330px-Mystery_play_001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Mystery_play_001.jpg/440px-Mystery_play_001.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1709" data-file-height="2047" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mystery_play_001.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mystery play, <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a>, 15th century</div></div></div>
<p>As early as the fifth century living tableaux were introduced into sacred services.<sup id="cite_ref-bellinger_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bellinger-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
The plays originated as simple <i>tropes</i>, verbal embellishments of liturgical texts, and slowly became more elaborate. At an early period chants from the service of the day were added to the prose dialogue. As these liturgical dramas increased in popularity, vernacular forms emerged, as travelling companies of actors and theatrical productions organized by local communities became more common in the later Middle Ages.
</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Quem_quaeritis%3F" title="Quem quaeritis?">Quem quaeritis?</a></i> is the best known early form of the dramas, a dramatised liturgical dialogue between the angel at the tomb of Christ and the women who are seeking his body.<sup id="cite_ref-bellinger_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bellinger-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> These primitive forms were later elaborated with dialogue and dramatic action. Eventually the dramas moved from church to the exterior - the churchyard and the public marketplace. These early performances were given in Latin, and were preceded by a vernacular prologue spoken by a herald who gave a synopsis of the events. The writers and directors of the earliest plays were probably monks. Religious drama flourished from about the ninth century to the sixteenth.
</p><p>In 1210, suspicious of the growing popularity of miracle plays, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Innocent_III" title="Pope Innocent III">Pope Innocent III</a> issued a <a href="/wiki/Papal_bull" title="Papal bull">papal edict</a> forbidding clergy from acting on a public stage. This had the effect of transferring the organization of the dramas to town guilds, after which several changes followed. Vernacular texts replaced Latin, and non-Biblical passages were added along with comic scenes, for example in the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Second_Shepherds%27_Play" title="The Second Shepherds&#39; Play">Secunda Pastorum</a></i> of the <a href="/wiki/Wakefield_Cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Wakefield Cycle">Wakefield Cycle</a>. Acting and characterization became more elaborate.
</p><p>These vernacular religious performances were, in some of the larger cities in England such as <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a>, performed and produced by <a href="/wiki/Guild" title="Guild">guilds</a>, with each guild taking responsibility for a particular piece of scriptural history. From the guild control originated the term mystery play or mysteries, from the Latin <i>ministerium</i> meaning "occupation" (i.e. that of the guilds). The genre was again banned, following the <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">Reformation</a> and the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> in 1534.
</p><p>An example of a mystery play was the Easter Trope performed in the 10th Century. It was also called Quem Quaeritis? (Whom do you seek?)
</p><p>The mystery play developed, in some places, into a series of plays dealing with all the major events in the Christian calendar, from the Creation to the Day of Judgment. By the end of the 15th century, the practice of acting these plays in cycles on festival days was established in several parts of Europe. Sometimes, each play was performed on a decorated pageant cart that moved about the city to allow different crowds to watch each play as well as provided actors with a dressing room as well as a stage<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The entire cycle could take up to twenty hours to perform and could be spread over a number of days. Taken as a whole, these are referred to as <i>Corpus Christi cycles</i>. These cycles were often performed during the <a href="/wiki/Feast_of_Corpus_Christi" class="mw-redirect" title="Feast of Corpus Christi">Feast of Corpus Christi</a> and their overall design drew attention to Christ's life and his redemption for all of mankind.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The plays were performed by a combination of professionals and amateurs and were written in highly elaborate stanza forms; they were often marked by the extravagance of the sets and 'special effects', but could also be stark and intimate. The variety of theatrical and poetic styles, even in a single cycle of plays, could be remarkable.
</p><p>Mystery Plays origin comes from the Latin word "mysterium" which translates craft or handicraft
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="English_mystery_plays">English mystery plays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mystery_play&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: English mystery plays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:04b266_adamandeve.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/04b266_adamandeve.jpg/220px-04b266_adamandeve.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/04b266_adamandeve.jpg/330px-04b266_adamandeve.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/04b266_adamandeve.jpg/440px-04b266_adamandeve.jpg 2x" data-file-width="768" data-file-height="1024" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:04b266_adamandeve.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Actors portraying <a href="/wiki/Adam_and_Eve" title="Adam and Eve">Adam and Eve</a> are expelled from paradise in a performance by the Players of St Peter</div></div></div>
<p>There are four complete or nearly complete extant English biblical collections of plays;<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> although these collections are sometimes referred to as "cycles," it is now believed that this term may attribute to these collections more coherence than they in fact possess. The most complete is the <i><a href="/wiki/York_Mystery_Plays" title="York Mystery Plays">York cycle</a></i> of forty-eight pageants; there are also the <i><a href="/wiki/Wakefield_Cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Wakefield Cycle">Towneley plays</a></i> of thirty-two pageants, once thought to have been a true 'cycle' of plays acted at <a href="/wiki/Wakefield" title="Wakefield">Wakefield</a>; the <a href="/wiki/N-Town_Plays" title="N-Town Plays">Ludus Coventriae</a>, now generally agreed to be a redacted compilation of at least three older, unrelated plays, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Chester_Mystery_Plays" title="Chester Mystery Plays">Chester cycle</a></i> of twenty-four pageants, now generally agreed to be an Elizabethan reconstruction of older medieval traditions. Also extant are two pageants from a New Testament <a href="/wiki/Coventry_Mystery_Plays" title="Coventry Mystery Plays">cycle acted at Coventry</a> and one pageant each from Norwich and Newcastle upon Tyne. Additionally, a fifteenth-century play of the life of <a href="/wiki/Mary_Magdalene" title="Mary Magdalene">Mary Magdalene</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Brome_Abraham_and_Isaac" class="mw-redirect" title="The Brome Abraham and Isaac">The Brome Abraham and Isaac</a></i> and a sixteenth-century play of the <i>Conversion of <a href="/wiki/Paul_of_Tarsus" class="mw-redirect" title="Paul of Tarsus">Saint Paul</a></i> exist, all hailing from <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglia</a>. Besides the <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> drama, there are three surviving plays in <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a> known as the <a href="/wiki/Ordinalia" title="Ordinalia">Ordinalia</a>, and several cyclical plays survive from continental Europe.
</p><p>These biblical plays differ widely in content. Most contain episodes such as the <i>Fall of Lucifer</i>, the <i>Creation and Fall of Man</i>, <i>Cain and Abel</i>, <i>Noah and the Flood</i>, <i>Abraham and Isaac</i>, the <i>Nativity</i>, the <i>Raising of Lazarus</i>, the <i>Passion</i>, and the <i>Resurrection</i>. Other pageants included the story of <i>Moses</i>, the <i>Procession of the Prophets</i>, <i>Christ's Baptism</i>, the <i>Temptation in the Wilderness</i>, and the <i>Assumption and Coronation of the Virgin</i>. In given cycles, the plays came to be sponsored by the newly emerging Medieval <a href="/wiki/Craft_guild" class="mw-redirect" title="Craft guild">craft guilds</a>. The York <a href="/wiki/Mercery" title="Mercery">mercers</a>, for example, sponsored the <i>Doomsday</i> pageant. Other guilds presented scenes appropriate to their trade: the building of the <a href="/wiki/Noah%27s_Ark" title="Noah&#39;s Ark">Ark</a> from the carpenters' guild; the <a href="/wiki/Feeding_the_multitude" title="Feeding the multitude">five loaves and fishes</a> miracle from the bakers; and the <a href="/wiki/Visit_of_the_Magi" class="mw-redirect" title="Visit of the Magi">visit of the Magi</a>, with their offerings of gold, frankincense and myrrh, from the goldsmiths.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The guild associations are not, however, to be understood as the method of production for all towns. While the Chester pageants are associated with guilds, there is no indication that the N-Town plays are either associated with guilds or performed on <a href="/wiki/Pageant_wagon" title="Pageant wagon">pageant wagons</a>. Perhaps the most famous of the mystery plays, at least to modern readers and audiences, are those of Wakefield. Unfortunately, we cannot know whether the plays of the Towneley manuscript are actually the plays performed at Wakefield but a reference in the <i>Second Shepherds' Play</i> to <a href="/wiki/Horbury" title="Horbury">Horbery</a> Shrogys <sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> is strongly suggestive. In "The London Burial Grounds" by Mrs Basil Holmes (1897), the author claims that the Holy Priory Church, next to <a href="/wiki/St_Katherine_Cree" class="mw-redirect" title="St Katherine Cree">St Katherine Cree</a> on Leadenhall Street, London was the location of miracle plays from the tenth to the sixteenth century. <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Bonner" title="Edmund Bonner">Edmund Bonner</a>, Bishop of London (c 1500 - 1569) stopped this in 1542.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The most famous plays of the Towneley collection are attributed to the Wakefield Master, an anonymous playwright who wrote in the fifteenth century. The epithet "Wakefield Master" was first applied to this individual by the literary historian Gayley. The Wakefield Master gets his name from the geographic location where he lived, the market-town of Wakefield in Yorkshire. He may have been a highly educated cleric there, or possibly a friar from a nearby monastery at Woodkirk, four miles north of Wakefield. It was once thought that this anonymous author wrote a series of 32 plays (each averaging about 384 lines) called the Towneley Cycle. The Master's contributions to this collection are still much debated, and some scholars believe he may have written fewer than ten of them. These works appear in a single manuscript, currently found in the Huntington Library of California. It shows signs of Protestant editing &#8212; references to the Pope and the sacraments are crossed out, for instance. Likewise, twelve manuscript leaves were ripped out between the two final plays because of Catholic references. This evidence strongly suggests the play was still being read and performed as late as 1520, perhaps as late in Renaissance as the final years of King Henry VIII's reign.
</p><p>The best known pageant in the Towneley manuscript is <i>The Second Shepherds' Pageant</i>, a burlesque of the Nativity featuring Mak the sheep stealer and his wife Gill, which more or less explicitly compares a stolen lamb to the Saviour of mankind. The <i><a href="/wiki/Harrowing_of_Hell" title="Harrowing of Hell">Harrowing of Hell</a></i>, derived from the apocryphal <i><a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Pilate" class="mw-redirect" title="Acts of Pilate">Acts of Pilate</a></i>, was a popular part of the York and Wakefield cycles.
</p><p>The dramas of the <a href="/wiki/Elizabethan_theatre" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabethan theatre">Elizabethan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jacobean_era" title="Jacobean era">Jacobean</a> periods were developed out of mystery plays.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish_mystery_plays">Spanish mystery plays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mystery_play&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Spanish mystery plays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Misteri_d%27Elx" class="mw-redirect" title="Misteri d&#39;Elx">Misteri d'Elx</a> (in English, the <a href="/wiki/Elx_Mystery_Play" class="mw-redirect" title="Elx Mystery Play">Elx Mystery Play</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mystery_Play_of_Elx" class="mw-redirect" title="Mystery Play of Elx">Mystery Play of Elx</a>) is a liturgical drama dating from the Middle Ages which has been enacted and celebrated every year without any known interruptions. Commemorating the Assumption of Mary, it is played on every 14 and 15 August in the Basilica de Santa María in the city of <a href="/wiki/Elx" class="mw-redirect" title="Elx">Elx</a> (also known as <a href="/wiki/Elche" title="Elche">Elche</a>). The prohibition of theatrical plays in churches by the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Trent" title="Council of Trent">Council of Trent</a> eventually threatened to interrupt the yearly performance of the Misteri, but in 1632 <a href="/wiki/Pope_Urban_VIII" title="Pope Urban VIII">Pope Urban VIII</a> issued a special permit for its continuation. In 2001, UNESCO declared it one of the Masterpieces of the Oral and Intangible Heritage of Humanity.
</p><p>The oldest liturgical drama (12th century) written already in old Spanish language was a <a href="/wiki/Codex" title="Codex">codex</a> found in the library of the <a href="/wiki/Toledo_Cathedral" title="Toledo Cathedral">Toledo Cathedral</a>. The Auto de los Reyes Magos belongs to the Christmas cycle. It is a play about the <a href="/wiki/Biblical_Magi" title="Biblical Magi">Biblical Magi</a>, three wise men from the East who followed a star and visited the baby Jesus in <a href="/wiki/Bethlehem" title="Bethlehem">Bethlehem</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> It is believed to have been based on an earlier liturgical Latin play written in France.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Miracle_play">Miracle play</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mystery_play&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Miracle play">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Miracle plays, or <i>Saint's plays,</i> are now distinguished from mystery plays as they specifically re-enacted miraculous interventions by the <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saints</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/St._Nicholas" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Nicholas">St. Nicholas</a> or <a href="/wiki/St._Mary" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Mary">St. Mary</a>, into the lives of ordinary people, rather than biblical events;<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> however both of these terms are more commonly used by modern scholars than they were by medieval people, who used a wide variety of terminology to refer to their dramatic performances. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Chambers_(journalist)" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Chambers (journalist)">Robert Chambers</a>, writing in the 19th century, notes that "especially in England, miracle [came] to stand for religious play in general".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish language</a> miracle plays were performed in <a href="/wiki/Plain-an-gwarry_(theatre)" class="mw-redirect" title="Plain-an-gwarry (theatre)">plain-an-gwarrys</a>. To capture the attention of the audience, "the plays were often noisy, bawdy and entertaining".<sup id="cite_ref-HC_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HC-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_performances">Modern performances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mystery_play&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Modern performances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mystery Plays are still produced regularly throughout the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. The local cycles were revived in both <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chester" title="Chester">Chester</a> in 1951 as part of the <a href="/wiki/Festival_of_Britain" title="Festival of Britain">Festival of Britain</a>, and are still performed by the local guilds.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The N-Town cycle was revived in 1978 as the <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_mystery_plays" class="mw-redirect" title="Lincoln mystery plays">Lincoln mystery plays</a>,
<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> and in 1994 the <a href="/wiki/Lichfield" title="Lichfield">Lichfield</a> Mysteries were revived (now the biggest community theatre event in the United Kingdom).<sup id="cite_ref-lichmyst_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lichmyst-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1977 the <a href="/wiki/Royal_National_Theatre" title="Royal National Theatre">National Theatre</a> commissioned <a href="/wiki/Tony_Harrison" title="Tony Harrison">Tony Harrison</a> to create <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mysteries_(play)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Mysteries (play)">The Mysteries</a></i>, a re-working of the <a href="/wiki/Wakefield_Cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Wakefield Cycle">Wakefield Cycle</a> and others.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> It was revived in 1985 (whereupon the production was filmed for <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">Channel 4 Television</a>), and again as a part of the theatre's millennium celebration in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The productions won <a href="/wiki/Bill_Bryden" title="Bill Bryden">Bill Bryden</a> the "Best Director" title in both the <a href="/wiki/Evening_Standard_Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="Evening Standard Awards">Evening Standard Theatre Awards</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Olivier_Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="Olivier Awards">Olivier Awards</a> for 1985, the year the three plays first appeared together in performance at the <a href="/wiki/Lyceum_Theatre,_London" title="Lyceum Theatre, London">Lyceum Theatre</a>. An adaptation of Harrison's play was staged at <a href="/wiki/Shakespeare%27s_Globe" title="Shakespeare&#39;s Globe">Shakespeare's Globe</a> in 2011 as <i>The Globe Mysteries</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2001, the <a href="/wiki/Isango_Ensemble" title="Isango Ensemble">Isango Ensemble</a> produced an African version of the Chester Cycle at the <a href="/wiki/Garrick_Theatre" title="Garrick Theatre">Garrick Theatre</a> in London as <i>The Mysteries – Yiimimangaliso</i>, performing in a combination of the <a href="/wiki/Xhosa_language" title="Xhosa language">Xhosa language</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Zulu_language" title="Zulu language">Zulu language</a>, English, <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Afrikaans" title="Afrikaans">Afrikaans</a>. They revived an adapted version of the production at Shakespeare's Globe in 2015 as <i>The Mysteries</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In 2004, two mystery plays (one focusing on the Creation and the other on the Passion) were performed at Canterbury Cathedral, with actor <a href="/wiki/Edward_Woodward" title="Edward Woodward">Edward Woodward</a> in the role of God. The large cast also included <a href="/wiki/Daniel_MacPherson" title="Daniel MacPherson">Daniel MacPherson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_James_Longley" title="Thomas James Longley">Thomas James Longley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_McManners" title="Joseph McManners">Joseph McManners</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
