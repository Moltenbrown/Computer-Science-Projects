<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">Kinship</a></div>
<p><b>Sudanese kinship</b>, also referred to as the <b>descriptive system</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Kinship_and_descent" class="mw-redirect" title="Kinship and descent">kinship</a> system used to define <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">family</a>. Identified by <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Henry_Morgan" class="mw-redirect" title="Lewis Henry Morgan">Lewis Henry Morgan</a> in his 1871 work <i>Systems of Consanguinity and Affinity of the Human Family</i>, the Sudanese system is one of the six major kinship systems (<a href="/wiki/Eskimo_kinship" title="Eskimo kinship">Eskimo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_kinship" title="Hawaiian kinship">Hawaiian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iroquois_kinship" title="Iroquois kinship">Iroquois</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crow_kinship" title="Crow kinship">Crow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Omaha_kinship" title="Omaha kinship">Omaha</a> and Sudanese).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Sudanese kinship system is the most complicated of all kinship systems.  It maintains a separate designation for almost every one of Ego's kin, based on their distance from Ego, their relation, and their gender. Ego's father is distinguished from Ego's father's brother and from Ego's mother's brother. Ego's mother is similarly distinguished from Ego's mother's sister and from Ego's father's sister.  For cousins, there are eight possible terms.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sudanese_kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The system is named for the peoples of <a href="/wiki/South_Sudan" title="South Sudan">South Sudan</a>.  The Sudanese kinship system also existed in ancient Latin-speaking<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and Anglo-Saxon<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> cultures.  It exists today among present day-<a href="/wiki/Arab" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab">Arab</a> and Turkish<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> cultures. It tends to co-occur with <a href="/wiki/Patrilineal" class="mw-redirect" title="Patrilineal">patrilineal</a> descent, and it is often said to be common in complex and stratified cultures.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Variants</b>
</p><p>Balkan kinships such as <a href="/wiki/Bulgarians" title="Bulgarians">Bulgarian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serbs" title="Serbs">Serbian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bosniak" class="mw-redirect" title="Bosniak">Bosniak</a> follow this system for different <a href="/wiki/Patrilinear" class="mw-redirect" title="Patrilinear">patrilinear</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matrilinear" class="mw-redirect" title="Matrilinear">matrilinear</a> uncles but collapse mother's sister and father's sister into the same term of "aunt" and <a href="/wiki/Croatian_language" title="Croatian language">Croatian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Macedonian_language" title="Macedonian language">Macedonian</a> further collapse the offspring of the uncles into one term.
</p><p>On the opposite side, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_kinship" title="Chinese kinship">Chinese</a> adds an additional dimension of relative age. Ego's older siblings are distinguished from younger, as are those of Ego's parents. One must specify whether older (e.g.  <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">Mandarin</a> 哥哥 <i>gēge</i>) or younger (e.g. Mandarin 弟弟 <i>dìdi</i>). Similarly, a term for "uncle" or (in at least in some <a href="/wiki/Varieties_of_Chinese" title="Varieties of Chinese">varieties of Chinese</a>, including Mandarin) even "father's brother" does not exist without circumlocution; the speaker must either specify "father's older brother" (e.g. Mandarin 伯伯 <i>bóbo</i>) or "father's younger brother" (e.g. Mandarin 叔叔 <i>shūshu</i>). This applies to both uncles and aunts, both patrilinear and matrilinear.
</p>
