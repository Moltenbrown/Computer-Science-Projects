<p><b>Aristides</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/Àå/: secondary stress follows">Àå</span><span title="/√¶/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;bad&#39;">√¶</span><span title="&#39;r&#39; in &#39;rye&#39;">r</span><span title="/…ô/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">…ô</span><span title="/Àà/: primary stress follows">Àà</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span><span title="/a…™/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;tide&#39;">a…™</span><span title="&#39;d&#39; in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span><span title="/iÀê/: &#39;ee&#39; in &#39;fleece&#39;">iÀê</span><span title="&#39;z&#39; in &#39;zoom&#39;">z</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">·ºàœÅŒπœÉœÑŒµŒØŒ¥Œ∑œÇ</span>, <i>Aristeides</i>; 530‚Äì468 BC) was an <a href="/wiki/Classical_Athens" title="Classical Athens">ancient Athenian</a> <a href="/wiki/Politician" title="Politician">statesman</a>.  Nicknamed "the Just", he flourished in the early quarter of Athens' <a href="/wiki/Classical_Greece" title="Classical Greece">Classical period</a> and is remembered for his generalship in the <a href="/wiki/Greco-Persian_Wars" title="Greco-Persian Wars">Persian War</a>.  The ancient historian <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a> cited him as "the best and most honourable man in Athens",<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and he received similarly reverent treatment in <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>'s Socratic dialogues.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aristides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aristides_and_the_Citizens.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Aristides_and_the_Citizens.jpg/220px-Aristides_and_the_Citizens.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="308" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Aristides_and_the_Citizens.jpg/330px-Aristides_and_the_Citizens.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Aristides_and_the_Citizens.jpg/440px-Aristides_and_the_Citizens.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1557" data-file-height="2177" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aristides_and_the_Citizens.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aristides and the citizens</div></div></div>
<p>Aristides was the son of Lysimachus, and a member of a family of moderate fortune. Of his early life, it is only told that he became a follower of the statesman <a href="/wiki/Cleisthenes" title="Cleisthenes">Cleisthenes</a> and sided with the aristocratic party in Athenian politics. He first came to notice as <i><a href="/wiki/Strategos" title="Strategos">strategos</a></i> in command of his native tribe <a href="/wiki/Antiochis_(tribe)" title="Antiochis (tribe)">Antiochis</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Marathon" title="Battle of Marathon">Battle of Marathon</a>, and it was no doubt in consequence of the distinction which he then achieved that he was elected <i><a href="/wiki/Archon_eponymos" class="mw-redirect" title="Archon eponymos">archon eponymos</a></i> for the ensuing year (489‚Äì488). Pursuing a conservative policy to maintain Athens as a land power, he was one of the chief opponents of the naval policy proposed by <a href="/wiki/Themistocles" title="Themistocles">Themistocles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a>, the rivalry between Aristides and Themistocles began in their youth, when they competed for the <a href="/wiki/Paederasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Paederasty">love</a> of a beautiful boy called Stesila√ºs from <a href="/wiki/Ceos" class="mw-redirect" title="Ceos">Ceos</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The conflict between the two leaders ended in the <a href="/wiki/Ostracism" title="Ostracism">ostracism</a> of Aristides at a date variously given between 485 and 482. It is said that, on this occasion, an illiterate voter who did not recognise Aristides approached the statesman and requested that he write the name of Aristides on his <a href="/wiki/Ostracon" title="Ostracon">voting shard</a> to ostracize him. The latter asked if Aristides had wronged him. "No," was the reply, "and I do not even know him, but it irritates me to hear him everywhere called 'the Just'."<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  Aristides then wrote his own name on the ballot.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Early in 480, Aristides profited by the decree recalling exiles to help in the defence of Athens against Persian invaders, and was elected <i>strategos</i> for the year 480‚Äì479. In the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Salamis" title="Battle of Salamis">Battle of Salamis</a>, he gave loyal support to Themistocles, and crowned the victory by landing Athenian infantry on the island of <a href="/wiki/Psyttaleia" title="Psyttaleia">Psyttaleia</a> and annihilating the Persian garrison stationed there.<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aristides_and_Alexander_479_BCE.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Aristides_and_Alexander_479_BCE.jpg/220px-Aristides_and_Alexander_479_BCE.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="167" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Aristides_and_Alexander_479_BCE.jpg/330px-Aristides_and_Alexander_479_BCE.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Aristides_and_Alexander_479_BCE.jpg/440px-Aristides_and_Alexander_479_BCE.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1680" data-file-height="1274" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aristides_and_Alexander_479_BCE.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aristides warned by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_I_of_Macedon" title="Alexander I of Macedon">Alexander I of Macedon</a> of the impending Persian attack at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Plataea" title="Battle of Plataea">Battle of Plataea</a>, 479 BC.</div></div></div>
<p>In 479, he was re-elected <i>strategos</i>, and given special powers as commander of the Athenian forces at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Plataea" title="Battle of Plataea">Battle of Plataea</a>; he is also said to have suppressed a conspiracy among some in the army. He so won the confidence of the <a href="/wiki/Ionia" title="Ionia">Ionian</a> allies that, after revolting from the <a href="/wiki/Sparta" title="Sparta">Spartan</a> admiral <a href="/wiki/Pausanias_(admiral)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pausanias (admiral)">Pausanias</a>, they gave him the chief command and left him with absolute discretion in fixing the contributions of the newly formed confederacy, the <a href="/wiki/Delian_League" title="Delian League">Delian League</a>. His assessment was universally accepted as equitable, and continued as the basis of taxation for the greater part of the league's duration. He continued to hold a predominant position in Athens. At first he seems to have remained on good terms with Themistocles, whom he is said to have helped in outwitting the Spartans over the rebuilding of the walls of Athens.<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He is said by some authorities to have died at Athens, by others on a journey to the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>. The date of his death is given by Nepos as 468. He lived to witness the ostracism of Themistocles, towards whom he always displayed generosity, but he died before the rise of <a href="/wiki/Pericles" title="Pericles">Pericles</a>. His estate seems to have suffered severely from the Persian invasions, for apparently he did not leave enough money to defray the expenses of his burial, and it is known that his descendants even in the 4th century received state pensions.<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Authorities">Authorities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aristides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Authorities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Answer_of_Aristides_to_the_ambassadors_of_Mardonius.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Answer_of_Aristides_to_the_ambassadors_of_Mardonius.jpg/220px-Answer_of_Aristides_to_the_ambassadors_of_Mardonius.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="160" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Answer_of_Aristides_to_the_ambassadors_of_Mardonius.jpg/330px-Answer_of_Aristides_to_the_ambassadors_of_Mardonius.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Answer_of_Aristides_to_the_ambassadors_of_Mardonius.jpg/440px-Answer_of_Aristides_to_the_ambassadors_of_Mardonius.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1682" data-file-height="1224" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Answer_of_Aristides_to_the_ambassadors_of_Mardonius.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Answer of Aristides to the ambassadors of <a href="/wiki/Mardonius_(general)" title="Mardonius (general)">Mardonius</a>.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a> is not the only ancient author to evaluate Aristides' life. He is also the subject of one of <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Parallel_Lives" title="Parallel Lives">Parallel Lives</a></i>, although Plutarch, writing during the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, was several centuries removed. Aristides is praised by <a href="/wiki/Socrates" title="Socrates">Socrates</a> in <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>'s dialogues <i><a href="/wiki/Gorgias_(dialogue)" title="Gorgias (dialogue)">Gorgias</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Meno" title="Meno">Meno</a></i> as an exceptional instance of good leadership.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Plato's dialogue <i><a href="/wiki/Theaetetus_(dialogue)" title="Theaetetus (dialogue)">Theaetetus</a></i>, Socrates refers to Aristides, the grandson of the famous Aristides, less positively, bringing him as an example of a student who leaves his care too soon and realizes later that he is a fool.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aristides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">
