<p>The <b>Far East </b> is a geographical term in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> that usually refers to <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Asia" title="Northeast Asia">Northeast Asia</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Far_East" title="Russian Far East">Russian Far East</a> (part of <a href="/wiki/North_Asia" title="North Asia">North Asia</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> is sometimes also included for economic and cultural reasons.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The term "Far East" came into use in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> geopolitical discourse in the 12th century, denoting the Far East as the "farthest" of the three "easts", beyond the <a href="/wiki/Near_East" title="Near East">Near East</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>. Likewise, in <a href="/wiki/Qing_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Qing Dynasty">Qing Dynasty</a> of the 19th and early 20th centuries the term "<a href="/wiki/Far_West_(Taixi)" title="Far West (Taixi)">Tàixī</a> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">泰西</span>)" – i.e. anything further west than the <a href="/wiki/Arab_world" title="Arab world">Arab world</a> – was used to refer to the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western</a> countries.
</p><p>Since the 1960s, East Asia has become the most common term for the region in international <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">mass media</a> outlets.<sup id="cite_ref-Economist_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Economist-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Reischauer_1960_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reischauer_1960-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popularization">Popularization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Far_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Popularization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prior to the colonial era, "Far East" referred to anything further east than the Middle East. In the 16th century, <a href="/wiki/King_John_III_of_Portugal" class="mw-redirect" title="King John III of Portugal">King John III of Portugal</a> called <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> a "rich and interesting country in the Far East<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> (<i>Extremo Oriente</i>)." The term was popularized during the period of the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> as a blanket term for lands to the east of <a href="/wiki/British_Raj" title="British Raj">British India</a>.
</p><p>In pre-<a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> European geopolitics, the <i><a href="/wiki/Near_East" title="Near East">Near East</a></i> referred to the relatively nearby lands of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a></i> denoted northwestern <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>, and the <i>Far East</i> meant countries along the western <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> and eastern <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>. Many European languages have analogous terms, such as the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> (<i><i lang="fr" title="French language text"><a href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extr%C3%AAme-Orient" class="extiw" title="fr:Extrême-Orient">Extrême-Orient</a></i></i>), <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> (<i><i lang="es" title="Spanish language text"><a href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lejano_Oriente" class="extiw" title="es:Lejano Oriente">Lejano Oriente</a></i></i>), <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a> (<i><i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text"><a href="https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extremo_Oriente" class="extiw" title="pt:Extremo Oriente">Extremo Oriente</a></i></i>), <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> (<i><i lang="de" title="German language text"><a <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_as_well_as_geographic_meaning">Cultural as well as geographic meaning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Far_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cultural as well as geographic meaning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Significantly, the term evokes cultural as well as geographic separation; the <i>Far East</i> is not just geographically distant, but also culturally exotic. It never refers, for instance, to the culturally Western nations of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, which lie even farther to the east of Europe than East Asia itself. This combination of cultural and geographic subjectivity was well illustrated in 1939 by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Menzies" title="Robert Menzies">Robert Menzies</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Australia" title="Prime Minister of Australia">Prime Minister of Australia</a>. Reflecting on his country's geopolitical concerns with the onset of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">war</a>, Menzies commented that: 
</p>
<blockquote><p> The problems of the Pacific are different. What Great Britain calls the Far East is to us the Near North.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> </p></blockquote>
<p><i>Far East</i> in its usual sense is comparable to terms such as <i>the <a href="/wiki/Orient" title="Orient">Orient</a></i>, which means <a href="/wiki/East" title="East">East</a>; the <i><a href="/wiki/Eastern_world" title="Eastern world">Eastern world</a></i>; or simply the <i>East</i>. <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Far_East" title="Russian Far East">Russian Far East</a>, and occasionally <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> might be included in the Far East to some extent.
</p><p>Concerning the term, John K. Fairbank and Edwin O. Reischauer, professors of East Asian Studies at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, wrote (in <i>East Asia: The Great Tradition</i>): "When Europeans traveled far to the east to reach <a href="/wiki/Cathay" title="Cathay">Cathay</a>, Japan and the Indies, they naturally gave those distant regions the general name 'Far East.' Americans who reached China, Japan and Southeast Asia by sail and steam across the Pacific could, with equal logic, have called that area the 'Far West.' For the people who live in that part of the world, however, it is neither 'East' nor 'West' and certainly not 'Far.' A more generally acceptable term for the area is 'East Asia,' which is geographically more precise and does not imply the outdated notion that Europe is the center of the civilized world."<sup id="cite_ref-Reischauer_1960_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reischauer_1960-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Today, the term remains in the names of some longstanding institutions, including the <a href="/wiki/Far_Eastern_Federal_University" title="Far Eastern Federal University">Far Eastern Federal University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vladivostok" title="Vladivostok">Vladivostok</a>, <a href="/wiki/Far_Eastern_University" title="Far Eastern University">Far Eastern University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Far_East_University_(Korea)" title="Far East University (Korea)">Far East University</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>.  Furthermore, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> have historically used <i>Far East</i> for several military units and commands in the region; the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Far_East_Fleet" class="mw-redirect" title="Far East Fleet">Far East Fleet</a>, for instance.
</p>
