	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">History of the Mediterranean region</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Antonio_Millo_Bacino_del_Mediterraneo.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Antonio_Millo_Bacino_del_Mediterraneo.jpg/300px-Antonio_Millo_Bacino_del_Mediterraneo.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="208" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Antonio_Millo_Bacino_del_Mediterraneo.jpg/450px-Antonio_Millo_Bacino_del_Mediterraneo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Antonio_Millo_Bacino_del_Mediterraneo.jpg/600px-Antonio_Millo_Bacino_del_Mediterraneo.jpg 2x" data-file-width="601" data-file-height="416" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Antonio_Millo_Bacino_del_Mediterraneo.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bacino del Mediterraneo, dall’Atlante manoscritto del 1582–1584 ca. <a href="/wiki/Biblioteca_Nazionale_Centrale_di_Roma" title="Biblioteca Nazionale Centrale di Roma">Biblioteca Nazionale Centrale Vittorio Emanuele II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> (cart. naut. 2 – cart. naut 6/1-2).</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> was the central superhighway of transport, trade and cultural exchange between diverse peoples encompassing three continents:<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Western_Asia" title="Western Asia">Western Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Europe" title="Southern Europe">Southern Europe</a>. The history of the cultures and people of the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Basin" title="Mediterranean Basin">Mediterranean Basin</a> is important for understanding the origin and development of the <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Egyptian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canaanites" class="mw-redirect" title="Canaanites">Canaanite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">Phoenician</a>, <a href="/wiki/History_of_ancient_Israel_and_Judah" title="History of ancient Israel and Judah">Hebrew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carthage" title="Carthage">Carthaginian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Greek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Persian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Thrace" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Thrace">Thracian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Etruscan_civilization" title="Etruscan civilization">Etruscan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iberian_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Iberian culture">Iberian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_Empire" title="Bulgarian Empire">Bulgarian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arab_Civilization" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab Civilization">Arab</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berbers" title="Berbers">Berbers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muslim_world" title="Muslim world">Islamic cultures</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Mediterranean_region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Near_East" title="Ancient Near East">Ancient Near East</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Fertile_Crescent" title="Fertile Crescent">Fertile Crescent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cradle_of_civilization" title="Cradle of civilization">Cradle of civilization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egypt</a>, and <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Levant" class="mw-redirect" title="History of the Levant">History of the Levant</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_of_fertile_crescent.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Map_of_fertile_crescent.svg/250px-Map_of_fertile_crescent.svg.png" decoding="async" width="250" height="307" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Map_of_fertile_crescent.svg/375px-Map_of_fertile_crescent.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Map_of_fertile_crescent.svg/500px-Map_of_fertile_crescent.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1987" data-file-height="2441" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_of_fertile_crescent.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Fertile_Crescent" title="Fertile Crescent">Fertile Crescent</a> in the 2nd millennium BC.</div></div></div>
<p>There is evidence of stone tools on <a href="/wiki/History_of_Crete#Prehistoric_Crete" title="History of Crete">Crete</a>, 130,000 years BC,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> which proves that early man was capable of using boats to reach the island.
</p><p>The cultural stage of <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilization</a> (organised society structured around urban centers) first arises in Southwest Asia, as an extension of the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> trend, from as early as the <a href="/wiki/8th_millennium_BC" title="8th millennium BC">8th millennium BC</a>, of proto-urban centers such as <a href="/wiki/%C3%87atal_H%C3%BCy%C3%BCk" class="mw-redirect" title="Çatal Hüyük">Çatal Hüyük</a>. Urban civilizations proper begin to emerge in the <a href="/wiki/Chalcolithic" title="Chalcolithic">Chalcolithic</a>, in 5th to 4th millennium <a href="/wiki/Pre-dynastic_Egypt" class="mw-redirect" title="Pre-dynastic Egypt">Egypt</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Ubaid_period" title="Ubaid period">Mesopotamia</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> arises in this region during the final centuries of the 4th millennium. The urban civilizations of the <a href="/wiki/Fertile_Crescent" title="Fertile Crescent">Fertile Crescent</a> now have <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_writing" class="mw-redirect" title="Bronze Age writing">writing systems</a> and develop <a href="/wiki/Bureaucracy" title="Bureaucracy">bureaucracy</a>, by the mid-3rd millennium leading to the development of the earliest <a href="/wiki/Empire#Early_empires" title="Empire">Empires</a>. In the 2nd millennium, the eastern coastlines of the Mediterranean are dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Hittite_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Hittite Empire">Hittite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Egyptian Empire">Egyptian</a> empires, competing for control over the city states in the Levant (<a href="/wiki/Canaan" title="Canaan">Canaan</a>).
</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_collapse" class="mw-redirect" title="Bronze Age collapse">Bronze Age collapse</a></i> is the transition from the <a href="/wiki/Late_Bronze_Age" class="mw-redirect" title="Late Bronze Age">Late Bronze Age</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Early_Iron_Age" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Iron Age">Early Iron Age</a>, expressed by the collapse of <a href="/wiki/Palace_economy" title="Palace economy">palace economies</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Aegean_civilization" title="Aegean civilization">Aegean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>, which were replaced after a hiatus by the isolated village cultures of the <a href="/wiki/Dark_Ages_in_history" class="mw-redirect" title="Dark Ages in history">Dark Age period in history</a> of the ancient Near East. Some have gone so far as to call the catalyst that ended the Bronze Age a "catastrophe".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The Bronze Age collapse may be seen in the context of a technological history that saw the slow, comparatively continuous spread of iron-working technology in the region, beginning with precocious iron-working in what is now <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> in the 13th and 12th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The cultural collapse of the <a href="/wiki/Mycenaean_Greece" title="Mycenaean Greece">Mycenaean kingdoms</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hittites" title="Hittites">Hittite Empire</a> in <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Egyptian Empire</a> in <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, the scission of long-distance <a href="/wiki/Trade_route" title="Trade route">trade</a> contacts and sudden eclipse of literacy occurred between 1206 and 1150 BC. In the first phase of this period, almost every city between <a href="/wiki/Troy" title="Troy">Troy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gaza_City" title="Gaza City">Gaza</a> was violently destroyed, and often left unoccupied thereafter (for example, <a href="/wiki/Hattusas" class="mw-redirect" title="Hattusas">Hattusas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mycenae" title="Mycenae">Mycenae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ugarit" title="Ugarit">Ugarit</a>). The gradual end of the Dark Age that ensued saw the rise of settled <a href="/wiki/Neo-Hittite" class="mw-redirect" title="Neo-Hittite">Neo-Hittite</a> <a href="/wiki/Aramaean" class="mw-redirect" title="Aramaean">Aramaean</a> kingdoms of the mid-10th century BC, and the rise of the <a href="/wiki/Neo-Assyrian_Empire" title="Neo-Assyrian Empire">Neo-Assyrian Empire</a>.
</p><p>While the cultural advances during the Bronze Age had mostly been confined to the eastern parts of the Mediterranean, with the Iron Age, the entire coastal region surrounding the Mediterranean now becomes involved, significantly due to the <a href="/wiki/Phoenicians" class="mw-redirect" title="Phoenicians">Phoenician</a> expansion from the Levant, beginning in ca. the 12th century. <a href="/wiki/Fernand_Braudel" title="Fernand Braudel">Fernand Braudel</a> remarked in <i>The Perspective of the World</i> that Phoenicia was an early example of a "world-economy" surrounded by empires. The high point of Phoenician culture and sea power is usually placed ca. 1200–800&#160;BC. Many of the most important Phoenician settlements had been established long before this: <a href="/wiki/Byblos" title="Byblos">Byblos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tyre_(Lebanon)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tyre (Lebanon)">Tyre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sidon" title="Sidon">Sidon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Simyra" class="mw-redirect" title="Simyra">Simyra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arwad" title="Arwad">Arwad</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Beirut" title="Beirut">Berytus</a>, all appear in the <a href="/wiki/Amarna_tablets" class="mw-redirect" title="Amarna tablets">Amarna tablets</a>.
</p><p>The Phoenicians and the Assyrians transported elements of the Late Bronze Age culture of the Near East to Iron Age <a href="/wiki/Archaic_Greece" title="Archaic Greece">Greece</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age_Italy" class="mw-redirect" title="Iron Age Italy">Italy</a>, but also further afield to <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Libya" title="Ancient Libya">Northwestern Africa</a> and to <a href="/wiki/Tartessos" title="Tartessos">Iberia</a>, initiating the beginning of Mediterranean history now known as <a href="/wiki/Classical_Antiquity" class="mw-redirect" title="Classical Antiquity">Classical Antiquity</a>.
They notably spread <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_alphabet" title="History of the alphabet">alphabetic writing</a>, which would become the hallmark of the Mediterranean civilizations of the Iron Age, in contrast to the <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_cuneiform" class="mw-redirect" title="Assyrian cuneiform">cuneiform</a> writing of Assyria and the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_bronze_inscriptions" title="Chinese bronze inscriptions">logographic</a> system in the Far East (and later the <a href="/wiki/Abugida" title="Abugida">abugida</a> systems of India).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_antiquity">Classical antiquity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Mediterranean_region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classical antiquity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Greek_Colonization_Archaic_Period.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Greek_Colonization_Archaic_Period.svg/250px-Greek_Colonization_Archaic_Period.svg.png" decoding="async" width="250" height="142" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Greek_Colonization_Archaic_Period.svg/375px-Greek_Colonization_Archaic_Period.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Greek_Colonization_Archaic_Period.svg/500px-Greek_Colonization_Archaic_Period.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="855" data-file-height="485" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Greek_Colonization_Archaic_Period.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Magna_Graecia" title="Magna Graecia">Greek colonies</a> during the 8th and 7th centuries BC.</div></div></div>
<p>Two of the most notable Mediterranean civilizations in <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">classical antiquity</a> were the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Greek</a> <a href="/wiki/City_state" class="mw-redirect" title="City state">city states</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">Phoenicians</a>. The Greeks expanded throughout the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> and south through the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a>. The Phoenicians spread through the western Mediterranean reaching <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>. From the 6th century BC up to including the 5th century BC, many of the significant Mediterranean peoples were under Persian rule, making them dominate the Mediterranean during these years. Both the Phoenicians and some of the <a href="/wiki/Greek_city_states" class="mw-redirect" title="Greek city states">Greek city states</a> in Asia Minor provided the naval forces of the <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Achaemenid</a> <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Persian Empire</a>. Persian dominance ended after the <a href="/wiki/Greco-Persian_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Greco-Persian War">Greco-Persian War</a> in the 5th century BC and Persia was crippled by Macedonia in the 4th century BC.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Persian_period">Persian period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Mediterranean_region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Persian period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Achaemenid Empire</a></div>
<p>From the 6th century BC up to including the first half of the 4th century BC, many of the significant Mediterranean peoples came under <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Achaemenid Persian</a> rule, making them dominate the Mediterranean during all these years. The empire, founded by <a href="/wiki/Cyrus_the_Great" title="Cyrus the Great">Cyrus the Great</a>, would include <a href="/wiki/Macedonia_(ancient_kingdom)" title="Macedonia (ancient kingdom)">Macedonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thrace</a>, modern day <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>, Egypt, <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>, the Phoenician lands, the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a>, and many other basin regions of the Mediterranean later on.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Darius_the_Great" title="Darius the Great">Darius the Great</a> (Darius I) is to be credited as the first Achaemenid king to invest in a Persian fleet.<sup id="cite_ref-navy_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-navy-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Even by then no true "imperial navy" had existed either in Greece or Egypt. Persia would become the first empire, under Darius, to inaugurate and deploy the first regular imperial navy.<sup id="cite_ref-navy_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-navy-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Both the <a href="/wiki/Phoenicians" class="mw-redirect" title="Phoenicians">Phoenicians</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greeks</a> provided the bulk of the naval forces of the <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Achaemenid</a> Persian Empire, alongside the <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cypriots</a> and Egyptians.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Full Persian dominance in the Mediterranean ended after the <a href="/wiki/Greco-Persian_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Greco-Persian War">Greco-Persian War</a> in the 5th century BC, and Persia eventually lost all her influence in the Mediterranean in the late 4th century BC following Alexander's conquests.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hellenistic_period">Hellenistic period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Mediterranean_region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hellenistic period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">Hellenistic period</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_civilization" class="mw-redirect" title="Hellenistic civilization">Hellenistic civilization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Carthage" title="Ancient Carthage">Ancient Carthage</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Europe_map_220BC.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Europe_map_220BC.PNG/250px-Europe_map_220BC.PNG" decoding="async" width="250" height="239" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Europe_map_220BC.PNG/375px-Europe_map_220BC.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Europe_map_220BC.PNG/500px-Europe_map_220BC.PNG 2x" data-file-width="1143" data-file-height="1091" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Europe_map_220BC.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Mediterranean region in 220 BC.</div></div></div>
<p>In the northernmost part of ancient Greece, in the ancient kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Macedonia_(ancient_kingdom)" title="Macedonia (ancient kingdom)">Macedonia</a>, technological and organizational skills were forged with a long history of <a href="/wiki/Cavalry_warfare" class="mw-redirect" title="Cavalry warfare">cavalry warfare</a>. The <i>hetairoi</i> (<a href="/wiki/Companion_cavalry" title="Companion cavalry">Companion cavalry</a>) was considered the strongest of their time.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Under <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a>, this force turned east, and in a series of decisive battles, it routed the Persian forces and took over as the dominant empire of the Mediterranean. Their Macedonia empire included present-day Greece, Bulgaria, Egypt, the Phoenician lands and many other basin regions of the Mediterranean and Asia Minor.
</p><p>The major centres of the Mediterranean at the time became part of Alexander's empire as a result. His empire quickly disintegrated, and the Middle East, Egypt, and Greece were soon again independent. Alexander's conquests spread Greek knowledge and ideas throughout the region.
</p>
<h3><span id="Roman.E2.80.93Carthaginian_rivalry"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman–Carthaginian_rivalry">Roman–Carthaginian rivalry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Mediterranean_region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Roman–Carthaginian rivalry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Punic_Wars" title="Punic Wars">Punic Wars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Carthage" title="Ancient Carthage">Ancient Carthage</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carthage" title="Carthage">Carthage</a></div>
<p>These eastern powers soon began to be overshadowed by those farther west. In North Africa, the former Phoenician colony of <a href="/wiki/Carthage" title="Carthage">Carthage</a> rose to dominate its surroundings with an empire that contained many of the former Phoenician holdings. However, it was a city on the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Peninsula" title="Italian Peninsula">Italian Peninsula</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, that would eventually dominate the entire Mediterranean basin. Spreading first through Italy, Rome defeated Carthage in the <a href="/wiki/Punic_Wars" title="Punic Wars">Punic Wars</a>, despite <a href="/wiki/Hannibal" title="Hannibal">Hannibal</a>'s famous efforts against Rome in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Punic_War" title="Second Punic War">Second Punic War</a>.
</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/Third_Punic_War" title="Third Punic War">Third Punic War</a>, Rome then became the leading force in the Mediterranean region. The Romans soon spread east, taking Greece, and the Greek heritage played an important role in the Roman Empire. By this point the coastal trading cultures were thoroughly dominant over the inland river valleys that had once been the heart of the great powers. Egyptian power moved from the Nile cities to the coastal ones, especially <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a>. Mesopotamia became a fringe border region between the Roman Empire and the Persians.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_Mare_Nostrum">Roman <i>Mare Nostrum</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Mediterranean_region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Roman Mare Nostrum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Roman_Empire_ca_400_AD.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/The_Roman_Empire_ca_400_AD.png/250px-The_Roman_Empire_ca_400_AD.png" decoding="async" width="250" height="155" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/The_Roman_Empire_ca_400_AD.png/375px-The_Roman_Empire_ca_400_AD.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/The_Roman_Empire_ca_400_AD.png/500px-The_Roman_Empire_ca_400_AD.png 2x" data-file-width="4339" data-file-height="2689" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Roman_Empire_ca_400_AD.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <i>Mare nostrum</i>, surrounded by <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> territory in c. 400 AD.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Mare_Nostrum" title="Mare Nostrum">Mare Nostrum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a></div>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a> founded the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, the Mediterranean sea began to be called <i><a href="/wiki/Mare_Nostrum" title="Mare Nostrum">Mare Nostrum</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: "Our Sea") by the Romans. Their empire was centered on this sea and all the area was full of commerce and naval development. For the first time in history, an entire sea (the Mediterranean) was free of piracy. For several centuries, the Mediterranean was a "Roman Lake", surrounded on all sides by the empire.
</p><p>The empire began to crumble, however, in the fifth century and <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> collapsed after 476 CE.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Byzantine_times">Byzantine times</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Mediterranean_region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Byzantine times">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a></div>
<p>The Eastern Roman or <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Byzantine empire">Byzantine empire</a> began its domination of the Levant during its <a href="/wiki/Byzantine-Sassanid_Wars" class="mw-redirect" title="Byzantine-Sassanid Wars">wars with neighbouring Sassanid Persia</a>. The century-long rule saw a general decline in economy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The Byzantines lost territory on Mediterranean land regularly, but remained superior in the Mediterranean sea for centuries of the empires' span. In the first quarter of the 7th century AD, the Byzantines lost swaths of the Mediterranean region to Sassanid Persia during the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine%E2%80%93Sasanian_War_of_602%E2%80%93628" title="Byzantine–Sasanian War of 602–628">Byzantine–Sasanian War of 602–628</a>, though regained the lost territories by the end of the war. Ultimately, Byzantine domination in the region was forever finished by the Arabs and later the Turks, who came into the scene.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Mediterranean_region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Islamic_Golden_Age">Islamic Golden Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Mediterranean_region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Islamic Golden Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Golden_Age" title="Islamic Golden Age">Islamic Golden Age</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muslim_conquests" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim conquests">Muslim conquests</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caliphate" title="Caliphate">Caliphate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a>, and <a href="/wiki/First_Bulgarian_Empire" title="First Bulgarian Empire">First Bulgarian Empire</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_of_expansion_of_Caliphate.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Map_of_expansion_of_Caliphate.svg/250px-Map_of_expansion_of_Caliphate.svg.png" decoding="async" width="250" height="115" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Map_of_expansion_of_Caliphate.svg/375px-Map_of_expansion_of_Caliphate.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Map_of_expansion_of_Caliphate.svg/500px-Map_of_expansion_of_Caliphate.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="900" data-file-height="413" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_of_expansion_of_Caliphate.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The expansion of the <a href="/wiki/Caliphate" title="Caliphate">Caliphate</a> in the Mediterranean region from 622 to 750 AD. <div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #a1584e; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;Expansion under <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a>, 622–632</div> <div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #ef9070; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;Expansion during the <a href="/wiki/Rashidun_Caliphate" title="Rashidun Caliphate">Rashidun Caliphate</a>, 632–661</div> <div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; <p>Another power was rising in the east, that of <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>, whilst the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine</a> Roman and <a href="/wiki/Sassanid_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Sassanid Empire">Sassanid</a> Persian empires were both weakened by centuries of stalemate warfare during the <a href="/wiki/Roman%E2%80%93Persian_Wars" title="Roman–Persian Wars">Roman–Persian Wars</a>. In a series of rapid <a href="/wiki/Muslim_conquests" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim conquests">Muslim conquests</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Rashidun_army" title="Rashidun army">Arab armies</a>, motivated by Islam and led by the <a href="/wiki/Caliph" class="mw-redirect" title="Caliph">Caliphs</a> and skilled military commanders such as <a href="/wiki/Khalid_ibn_al-Walid" title="Khalid ibn al-Walid">Khalid ibn al-Walid</a>, swept through most of the Middle East; <a href="/wiki/Byzantine-Arab_Wars" class="mw-redirect" title="Byzantine-Arab Wars">reducing Byzantine lands</a> by more than half and completely <a href="/wiki/Islamic_conquest_of_Persia" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic conquest of Persia">engulfing the Persian lands</a>.
</p><p>The Arab invasions disrupted the trade relations between Western and Eastern Europe while cutting the trade route with Oriental lands. This however had the indirect effect of promoting the trade across the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a>. The export of grains from <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> was re-routed towards the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_world" title="Eastern world">Eastern world</a>. Oriental goods like silk and spices were carried from Egypt to ports like <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Venice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> by sailors and Jewish merchants. The <a href="/wiki/Viking_expansion" title="Viking expansion">Viking raids</a> further disrupted the trade in western Europe and brought it to a halt. However, the <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norsemen</a> developed the trade from Norway to the <a href="/wiki/White_Sea" title="White Sea">White Sea</a>, while also trading in luxury goods from <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">Spain</a> and the Mediterranean. The <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire_under_the_Isaurian_dynasty" title="Byzantine Empire under the Isaurian dynasty">Byzantines in the mid-8th century</a> retook control of the area around the north-eastern part of the Mediterranean. Venetian ships from the 9th century armed themselves to counter the harassment by Arabs while concentrating trade of oriental goods at Venice.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>, the Muslim <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Constantinople_(718)" class="mw-redirect" title="Siege of Constantinople (718)">expansion was blocked</a> by the still capable Byzantines with the help of the <a href="/wiki/First_Bulgarian_Empire" title="First Bulgarian Empire">Bulgarians</a>. The Byzantine provinces of <a href="/wiki/Muslim_conquest_of_Syria" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim conquest of Syria">Roman Syria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_conquest_of_North_Africa" class="mw-redirect" title="Umayyad conquest of North Africa">North Africa</a>, and Sicily, however, could not mount such a resistance, and the Muslim conquerors swept through those regions. At the far west, they crossed the sea taking <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_conquest_of_Hispania" title="Umayyad conquest of Hispania">Visigothic Hispania</a> before being <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tours" title="Battle of Tours">halted in southern France</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a>. At its greatest extent, the <a href="/wiki/Caliphate" title="Caliphate">Arab Empire</a> controlled 3/4 of the Mediterranean region, the only other empire besides the Roman Empire to control most of the Mediterranean Sea.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Much of North Africa became a peripheral area to the main Muslim centers in the Middle East, but <a href="/wiki/Al_Andalus" class="mw-redirect" title="Al Andalus">Al Andalus</a> and Morocco soon broke from this distant control and became highly advanced societies in their own right.
</p><p>Between 831 and 1071, the <a href="/wiki/Emirate_of_Sicily" title="Emirate of Sicily">Emirate of Sicily</a> was one of the major centres of Islamic culture in the Mediterranean. After its conquest by the Christian <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Normans</a>, the island developed its own distinct culture with the fusion of Latin and Byzantine influences. Palermo remained a leading artistic and commercial centre of the Mediterranean well into the Middle Ages.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Byzantine-Arab_naval_struggle.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Byzantine-Arab_naval_struggle.svg/250px-Byzantine-Arab_naval_struggle.svg.png" decoding="async" width="250" height="128" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Byzantine-Arab_naval_struggle.svg/375px-Byzantine-Arab_naval_struggle.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Byzantine-Arab_naval_struggle.svg/500px-Byzantine-Arab_naval_struggle.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1914" data-file-height="977" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Byzantine-Arab_naval_struggle.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of the main Byzantine-Muslim <a href="/wiki/Arab%E2%80%93Byzantine_wars" title="Arab–Byzantine wars">naval operations and battles</a> in the Mediterranean, 7th to 11th centuries</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Fatimids" class="mw-redirect" title="Fatimids">Fatimids</a> maintained trade relations with the <a href="/wiki/Italian_city-states" title="Italian city-states">Italian city-states</a> like <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Amalfi" title="Duchy of Amalfi">Amalfi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Genoa" title="Republic of Genoa">Genoa</a> before the Crusades, according to the <a href="/wiki/Cairo_Geniza" title="Cairo Geniza">Cairo Geniza</a> documents. A document dated 996 mentions Amalfian merchants living in <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a>. Another letter states that the Genoese had traded with <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a>. The caliph <a href="/wiki/Ma%27ad_al-Mustansir_Billah" class="mw-redirect" title="Ma&#39;ad al-Mustansir Billah">al-Mustansir</a> had allowed Amalfian merchants to reside in <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a> about 1060 in place of the Latin <a href="/wiki/Hospice" title="Hospice">hospice</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Europe was reviving, however, as more organized and centralized states began to form in the later <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_of_the_12th_century" title="Renaissance of the 12th century">Renaissance of the 12th century</a>.  Motivated by religion and dreams of conquest, the kings of Europe launched a number of <a href="/wiki/The_Crusades" class="mw-redirect" title="The Crusades">Crusades</a> to try to roll back Muslim power and retake the <a href="/wiki/Holy_land" class="mw-redirect" title="Holy land">holy land</a>.  The Crusades were unsuccessful in this goal, but they were far more effective in weakening the already tottering Byzantine Empire that began to lose increasing amounts of territory to the <a href="/wiki/Seljuk_Turks" class="mw-redirect" title="Seljuk Turks">Seljuk Turks</a> and later to the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Turks" title="Ottoman Turks">Ottoman Turks</a>.  They also rearranged the balance of power in the Muslim world as Egypt once again emerged as a major power in the eastern Mediterranean.
</p><p>The Crusades led to flourishing of trade between Europe and the <i><a href="/wiki/Outremer" class="mw-redirect" title="Outremer">outremer</a></i> region.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Genoa, Venica and <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Pisa" title="Republic of Pisa">Pisa</a> created colonies in regions controlled by the Crusaders and came to control the trade with the Orient. These colonies also allowed them to trade with the Eastern world. Though the fall of the Crusader states and attempts at banning of trade relations with Muslim states by the Popes temporarily disrupted the trade with the Orient, it however continued.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Slavery">Slavery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Mediterranean_region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Slavery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Slavery was a strategic and very important part of all Mediterranean societies during the Middle Ages. The threat of becoming a slave was a constant fear for peasants, fishermen and merchants. Those with money or who had financial backing only feared the lack of support, should they be threatened with abduction for ransom.
</p><p>There were several things which could happen to people in the Mediterranean region of the Middle Ages:
</p>
<ol><li>When <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/corsair" class="extiw" title="wikt:corsair">Corsairs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Piracy" title="Piracy">pirate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barbary_corsairs" class="mw-redirect" title="Barbary corsairs">Barbary corsairs</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_corsairs" title="French corsairs">French corsairs</a> or <a href="/wiki/Commerce_raiders" class="mw-redirect" title="Commerce raiders">commerce raiders</a> plied their trade, a peasant, fisherman or coastal villager, who had no financial backing, could be abducted or sold to slave traders, or adversaries, who made large profits on an international market;</li>
<li>If the captive was wealthy or had influential supporters, the captive could be ransomed. This would be the most advantageous plan, since the money exchange was immediate and direct, not long and drawn out as in the slave market business;</li>
<li>The captive could be used immediately by the corsair for labour on the ship rather than traded. In battles during this era, prisoners of war were often captured and used as slaves.</li></ol>
<p>Emperors would take large numbers of prisoners, parade them through the capital, hold feasts in honour of their capture and parade diplomats in front of them as a display of victory.<sup id="cite_ref-Europe_and_the_Islamic_World:_A_History_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Europe_and_the_Islamic_World:_A_History-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_Middle_Ages">Late Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Mediterranean_region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Late Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Genoa" title="Republic of Genoa">Republic of Genoa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Republic of Venice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ragusa" title="Republic of Ragusa">Republic of Ragusa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Aragon" title="Crown of Aragon">Crown of Aragon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">Crown of Castile</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Republik_Venedig_Handelswege01.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Republik_Venedig_Handelswege01.png/250px-Republik_Venedig_Handelswege01.png" decoding="async" width="250" height="133" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Republik_Venedig_Handelswege01.png/375px-Republik_Venedig_Handelswege01.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Republik_Venedig_Handelswege01.png/500px-Republik_Venedig_Handelswege01.png 2x" data-file-width="943" data-file-height="501" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Republik_Venedig_Handelswege01.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Genoa" title="Republic of Genoa">Genoese</a> (red) and <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Venetian</a> (green) maritime trade routes in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The Italian "Repubbliche Marinare" (Maritime Republics) of <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Venice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Genoa" title="Republic of Genoa">Genoa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Amalfi" class="mw-redirect" title="Republic of Amalfi">Amalfi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Pisa" title="Republic of Pisa">Pisa</a> developed their own "empires" in the Mediterranean shores. The Islamic states had never been major naval powers, and trade from the east to Europe was soon in the hands of Italian traders, especially the Genoese and the Venetians, who profited immensely from it. The <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Pisa" title="Republic of Pisa">Republic of Pisa</a> and later the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ragusa" title="Republic of Ragusa">Republic of Ragusa</a> used diplomacy to further trade and maintained a libertarian approach in civil matters to further sentiment in its inhabitants.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">republic of Venice</a> got to dominate the eastern mediterranean shores after the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Crusade" title="Fourth Crusade">Fourth Crusade</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBanaji200762-63_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBanaji200762-63-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Between 1275 and 1344 a struggle for the control of the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Gibraltar" title="Strait of Gibraltar">Strait of Gibraltar</a> took place. Featuring the <a href="/wiki/Marinid_Sultanate" title="Marinid Sultanate">Marinid Sultanate</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Nasrid_Kingdom_of_Granada" class="mw-redirect" title="Nasrid Kingdom of Granada">Nasrid Kingdom of Granada</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">Crown of Castile</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Arag%C3%B3n" class="mw-redirect" title="Crown of Aragón">Crown of Aragón</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Portugal" title="Kingdom of Portugal">Kingdom of Portugal</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Genoa" title="Republic of Genoa">Republic of Genoa</a>, it was characterized by changing alliances between the main actors.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELópez1996–1997405_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELópez1996–1997405-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The iberian cities of <a href="/wiki/Tarifa" title="Tarifa">Tarifa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ceuta" title="Ceuta">Ceuta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algeciras" title="Algeciras">Algeciras</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ronda" title="Ronda">Ronda</a> and the African port of <a href="/wiki/Ceuta" title="Ceuta">Ceuta</a> were at stake.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELópez1996–1997405_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELópez1996–1997405-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
The Western Mediterranean sea was dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Aragon" title="Crown of Aragon">Crown of Aragon</a>: thanks to their possessions of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Sicily" title="Kingdom of Sicily">Sicily</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Naples" title="Kingdom of Naples">Kingdom of Naples</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Sardinia" title="Kingdom of Sardinia">Kingdom of Sardinia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">Balearic Islands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Athens" title="Duchy of Athens">Duchy of Athens</a> the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Neopatria" class="mw-redirect" title="Duchy of Neopatria">Duchy of Neopatria</a>, and several northern African cities.
</p><p>In 1347 the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a> spread from <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> across the mediterranean basin.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESola200646_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESola200646-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ottoman power continued to grow, and in 1453, the Byzantine Empire was extinguished with the <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Constantinople" title="Fall of Constantinople">fall of Constantinople</a>.  The Ottomans already controlled Greece, <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a> and much of the Balkans and soon also began to spread through North Africa. North Africa had grown wealthy from the trade across the <a href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert" class="mw-redirect" title="Sahara Desert">Sahara Desert</a>, but the <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a>, who, along with other Christian powers, had been engaged in a long campaign to evict the Muslims from Iberia, had found a method to circumvent this trade by trading directly with <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a>.  This was enabled by a new type of ships, the <a href="/wiki/Caravel" title="Caravel">caravel</a>, that made trade in the rough Atlantic waters profitable for the first time.  The reduction in the Saharan trade weakened North Africa, and made them an easy target for the Ottomans.
</p><p>Ceuta was ultimately taken by the Kingdom of Portugal in 1415, searching to undermine Castilian, Aragonese, and Genoese interests in the area.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBanaji200749;_63–64_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBanaji200749;_63–64-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the Middle Ages, rival Christian and Muslim kingdoms forbade the trade of particular goods to enemy kingdoms including weaponry and other contraband items. The popes forbade the export of these commodities to the Islamic world. The Ottomans too forbade the export of weapons and other strategic items, declaring them <i>memnu eşya</i> or <i>memnu olan</i> to Christian states even in peace treaties, however friendly states could import some of the prohibited goods through <a href="/wiki/Capitulations_of_the_Ottoman_Empire" title="Capitulations of the Ottoman Empire">capitulations</a>. Despite these prohibitions, trade of contraband occurred on both sides. The European merchants traded in illegal goods with Muslims. The Ottomans were unable to suppress the trade with smuggling being undertaken mainly in the winter when the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Navy" title="Ottoman Navy">Ottoman Navy</a> stationed at the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Arsenal" title="Imperial Arsenal">Istanbul Arsenal</a> was unable to stop Ottoman and non-Ottoman vessels from indulging in the trade.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_era">Modern era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Mediterranean_region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Modern era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Empire" title="Portuguese Empire">Portuguese Empire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish Empire</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Italian_Empire" title="Italian Empire">Italian Empire</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:OttomanEmpireIn1683.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/OttomanEmpireIn1683.png/250px-OttomanEmpireIn1683.png" decoding="async" width="250" height="235" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/OttomanEmpireIn1683.png/375px-OttomanEmpireIn1683.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/OttomanEmpireIn1683.png/500px-OttomanEmpireIn1683.png 2x" data-file-width="1966" data-file-height="1850" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:OttomanEmpireIn1683.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> territories acquired between 1300 and 1683.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ItalianMareNostrum.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/ItalianMareNostrum.jpg/250px-ItalianMareNostrum.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="126" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/ItalianMareNostrum.jpg/375px-ItalianMareNostrum.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/ItalianMareNostrum.jpg/500px-ItalianMareNostrum.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1254" data-file-height="630" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ItalianMareNostrum.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Greatest extent of Italian control of the Mediterranean littoral and seas (within green line &amp; dots) in summer/fall 1942. Allied-controlled areas in red.</div></div></div>
<p>The growing naval prowess of the European powers confronted further rapid Ottoman expansion in the region when the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Lepanto_(1571)" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Lepanto (1571)">Battle of Lepanto</a> checked the power of the Ottoman navy. However, as <a href="/wiki/Braudel" class="mw-redirect" title="Braudel">Braudel</a> argued forcefully, this only slowed the Ottoman expansion instead of ending it. The prized island of <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a> became Ottoman in 1571. The last resistance in <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a> ended in 1574 and almost a generation long siege in <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a> pushed Venetians out of this strategic island in 1669.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Balance_of_power_in_international_relations" class="mw-redirect" title="Balance of power in international relations">balance of power</a> was then established between <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> until the 18th century, each dominating their respective half of Mediterranean, reducing Italian navies as naval powers increasingly more irrelevant. Furthermore, the Ottoman Empire had succeeded in their objective of extending Muslim rule across the North African coast.
</p><p>The development of long range seafaring had an influence upon the entire Mediterranean. While once all trade from the east had passed through the region, the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_discoveries" title="Portuguese discoveries">circumnavigation of Africa</a> allowed <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spice" title="Spice">spices</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dye" title="Dye">dyes</a> to be imported directly to the Atlantic ports of western Europe.  The Americas were also a source of extreme wealth to the western powers, from which some of the Mediterranean states were largely cut off.
</p><p>The base of European power thus shifted northward and the once wealthy <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> became a peripheral area dominated by foreigners.  The Ottoman Empire also began a slow decline that saw its North African possessions gain de facto independence and its European holdings gradually reduced by the increasing power of Austria and Russia.
</p><p>By the nineteenth century the European States were vastly more powerful, and began to colonize North Africa.  France spread its power south by taking <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a> in 1830 and later <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>. Britain gained control of <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> in 1882. <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy" title="Kingdom of Italy">Italy</a> conquered <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a> from the Ottomans in 1911. Greece <a href="/wiki/Greek_War_of_Independence" title="Greek War of Independence">achieved independence in 1832</a>. The Ottoman Empire finally collapsed in the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a>, and its holdings were carved up among France and Britain. The <a href="/wiki/Rump_state" title="Rump state">rump state</a> of the wider Ottoman Empire became the independent state of <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> in 1923. Yugoslavia was created from the former Austro-Hungarian empire at the end of the First World War.
</p><p>During the first half of the twentieth century the Mediterranean was at the center of the expansion of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy" title="Kingdom of Italy">Kingdom of Italy</a>, and was one of the main areas of battle during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Axis_powers_of_World_War_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Axis powers of World War II">Axis</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allies</a>. Post-world war period was marked by increasing activity in the Eastern Mediterranean, where naval actions formed part of ongoing Arab–Israeli conflict and Turkey had <a href="/wiki/Turkish_invasion_of_Cyprus" title="Turkish invasion of Cyprus">occupied the northern part of Cyprus</a>. Cold War tensions split the Mediterranean into pro-American and pro-Soviet factions, with Turkey, Greece, Spain, Italy and France being <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> members. Syria was socialist and a pro-Soviet regime, offering the Soviets <a href="/wiki/Tartus" title="Tartus">a port for their navy</a> from an agreement in 1971. Yugoslavia was Communist but in neither the Soviet nor American camps. Egypt tilted towards the Soviets during the time of <a href="/wiki/Nasser" class="mw-redirect" title="Nasser">Nasser</a> but then turned towards American influence during the time of <a href="/wiki/Sadat" title="Sadat">Sadat</a>. Israel and Egypt both received massive American military aid. American naval power made the Mediterranean a base for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Sixth_Fleet" title="United States Sixth Fleet">United States Sixth Fleet</a> during the Cold war.
</p><p>Today, the Mediterranean Sea is the southern border of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> and represents one of the largest area by Trade in the World. The Maltese prime minister described the Mediterranean sea as a "cemetery" due to the large amounts of migrants who drown there.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Following the <a href="/wiki/2013_Lampedusa_migrant_shipwreck" title="2013 Lampedusa migrant shipwreck">2013 Lampedusa migrant shipwreck</a>, the Italian government, has decided to strengthen the national system for the patrolling of the Mediterranean Sea by authorizing <a href="/wiki/Operation_Mare_Nostrum" title="Operation Mare Nostrum">Operation Mare Nostrum</a>, a military and humanitarian operation in order to rescue the migrants and arrest the traffickers of immigrants.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
