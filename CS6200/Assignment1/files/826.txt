<p>The  history of <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a> (Asia Minor)  can be roughly subdivided into 
<a href="/wiki/Prehistory_of_Anatolia" title="Prehistory of Anatolia">prehistory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Near_East" title="Ancient Near East">Ancient Near East</a> (Bronze Age and Early Iron Age), <a href="/wiki/Classical_Anatolia" title="Classical Anatolia">Classical Anatolia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_Anatolia" class="mw-redirect" title="Hellenistic Anatolia">Hellenistic Anatolia</a>, 
<a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Anatolia" title="Byzantine Anatolia">Byzantine Anatolia</a>, the age of the <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">Crusades</a> followed by the gradual <a href="/wiki/Seljuk_Empire" title="Seljuk Empire">Seljuk</a>/<a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a> conquest in the 13th to 14th centuries, <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Anatolia" class="mw-redirect" title="Ottoman Anatolia">Ottoman Anatolia</a> (14th to 19th centuries) and the modern <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Republic_of_Turkey" title="History of the Republic of Turkey">history of the Republic of Turkey</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistory">Prehistory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Prehistory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Prehistory_of_Anatolia" title="Prehistory of Anatolia">Prehistory of Anatolia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Anatolians" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Anatolians">Ancient Anatolians</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Regions_of_Anatolia" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Regions of Anatolia">Ancient Regions of Anatolia</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleolithic">Paleolithic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Paleolithic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Karain.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Karain.JPG/250px-Karain.JPG" decoding="async" width="250" height="162" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Karain.JPG/375px-Karain.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Karain.JPG/500px-Karain.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1918" data-file-height="1242" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Karain.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Karain_Cave" title="Karain Cave">Karain Cave</a> is a Paleolithic archaeological site located at Yağca village 27 km northwest of <a href="/wiki/Antalya" title="Antalya">Antalya city</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_region" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean region">Mediterranean region</a> of Turkey</div></div></div>
<p>In 2014 a stone tool was found in the <a href="/wiki/Gediz_River" title="Gediz River">Gediz River</a> that was dated with certainty to 1.2 million years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The 27,000 years old homo sapiens footprints of <a href="/wiki/Kula,_Manisa" title="Kula, Manisa">Kula</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Karain_Cave" title="Karain Cave">Karain Cave</a> are samples for human existence in Anatolia, in this period.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neolithic">Neolithic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Neolithic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:G%C3%B6bekli_Tepe,_Urfa.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/G%C3%B6bekli_Tepe%2C_Urfa.jpg/250px-G%C3%B6bekli_Tepe%2C_Urfa.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/G%C3%B6bekli_Tepe%2C_Urfa.jpg/375px-G%C3%B6bekli_Tepe%2C_Urfa.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/G%C3%B6bekli_Tepe%2C_Urfa.jpg/500px-G%C3%B6bekli_Tepe%2C_Urfa.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4288" data-file-height="2848" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:G%C3%B6bekli_Tepe,_Urfa.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6bekli_Tepe" title="Göbekli Tepe">Göbekli Tepe</a>, Şanlıurfa.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Museum_of_Anatolian_Civilizations003.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Museum_of_Anatolian_Civilizations003.jpg/250px-Museum_of_Anatolian_Civilizations003.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="142" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Museum_of_Anatolian_Civilizations003.jpg/375px-Museum_of_Anatolian_Civilizations003.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Museum_of_Anatolian_Civilizations003.jpg/500px-Museum_of_Anatolian_Civilizations003.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1420" data-file-height="807" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Museum_of_Anatolian_Civilizations003.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Wall painting of a bull, deer and man from <a href="/wiki/%C3%87atalh%C3%B6y%C3%BCk" title="Çatalhöyük">Çatalhöyük</a>; 6th millennium BC; reconstruction in their original positions of the bull's heads and the human relief-figure; <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Anatolian_Civilizations" title="Museum of Anatolian Civilizations">Museum of Anatolian Civilizations</a>, Ankara.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Anatolian_hypothesis" title="Anatolian hypothesis">Anatolian hypothesis</a></div>
<p>Because of its strategic location at the intersection of Asia and Europe, Anatolia has been the center of several <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilizations</a> since <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric" class="mw-redirect" title="Prehistoric">prehistoric</a> times. <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> settlements include <a href="/wiki/%C3%87atalh%C3%B6y%C3%BCk" title="Çatalhöyük">Çatalhöyük</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%87ay%C3%B6n%C3%BC" title="Çayönü">Çayönü</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neval%C4%B1_%C3%87ori" title="Nevalı Çori">Nevalı Çori</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hac%C4%B1lar" title="Hacılar">Hacılar</a>, <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6bekli_Tepe" title="Göbekli Tepe">Göbekli Tepe</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mersin" title="Mersin">Mersin</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bronze_Age">Bronze Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bronze Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Bronze_Age">Early Bronze Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early Bronze Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1900_BC_Near_East_mass_migration" class="mw-redirect" title="1900 BC Near East mass migration">1900 BC Near East mass migration</a></div>
<p>By this time, bronze metallurgy spread to Anatolia from the Transcaucasian <a href="/wiki/Kura-Araxes_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Kura-Araxes culture">Kura-Araxes culture</a> in the late 4th millennium BCE. Anatolia remained fully in the prehistoric period until it entered the sphere of influence of the <a href="/wiki/Akkadian_Empire" title="Akkadian Empire">Akkadian Empire</a> in the 24th century BCE under <a href="/wiki/Sargon_I" title="Sargon I">Sargon I</a>. The interest of Akkad in the region as far as it is known was for exporting various materials for manufacturing.<sup id="cite_ref-Freeman_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Freeman-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> While Anatolia was well endowed with copper ores, there is no trace as yet of substantial workings of the <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> required to make bronze in Bronze-Age Anatolia.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Akkad_(city)" title="Akkad (city)">Akkad</a> suffered problematic climate changes in <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>, as well as a reduction in available manpower that affected trade. This led to the fall of the <a href="/wiki/Akkadian_Empire" title="Akkadian Empire">Akkadians</a> around 2150 BCE at the hands of the <a href="/wiki/Gutians" class="mw-redirect" title="Gutians">Gutians</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Bronze_Age">Middle Bronze Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Middle Bronze Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Old_Assyrian_Empire" title="Old Assyrian Empire">Old Assyrian Empire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hattians" title="Hattians">Hattians</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Old_Assyrian_Empire" title="Old Assyrian Empire">Old Assyrian Empire</a> claimed the resources for themselves after the <a href="/wiki/Gutians" class="mw-redirect" title="Gutians">Gutians</a> were vanquished, notably silver. One of the numerous <a href="/wiki/Assyria" title="Assyria">Assyrian</a> <a href="/wiki/Cuneiform" title="Cuneiform">cuneiform</a> records found in Anatolia at <a href="/wiki/Kanesh" class="mw-redirect" title="Kanesh">Kanesh</a> uses an advanced system of trading computations and credit lines.<sup id="cite_ref-Freeman_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Freeman-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Hittite_Old_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Hittite Old Kingdom">Hittite Old Kingdom</a> emerges towards the close of the Middle Bronze Age, conquering <a href="/wiki/Hattusa" title="Hattusa">Hattusa</a> under <a href="/wiki/Hattusili_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Hattusili I">Hattusili I</a> (17th century BCE).
</p><p>The Anatolian Middle Bronze Age influenced the <a href="/wiki/Minoan_civilization" title="Minoan civilization">Minoan</a> culture on <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a> as evidenced by <a href="/wiki/Archaeological" class="mw-redirect" title="Archaeological">archaeological</a> recovery at <a href="/wiki/Knossos" title="Knossos">Knossos</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_Bronze_Age">Late Bronze Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Late Bronze Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Hittites" class="mw-redirect" title="History of the Hittites">History of the Hittites</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Assuwa" title="Assuwa">Assuwa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ahhiyawa" class="mw-redirect" title="Ahhiyawa">Ahhiyawa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Troy_VII" title="Troy VII">Troy VII</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ProcessionBogazkale.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/ProcessionBogazkale.JPG/200px-ProcessionBogazkale.JPG" decoding="async" width="200" height="161" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/ProcessionBogazkale.JPG/300px-ProcessionBogazkale.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/74/ProcessionBogazkale.JPG 2x" data-file-width="304" data-file-height="244" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ProcessionBogazkale.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A drawing of an early <a href="/wiki/Cuneiform" title="Cuneiform">cuneiform</a> carving of a procession by <a href="/wiki/Hittites" title="Hittites">Hittites</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bo%C4%9Fazkale" title="Boğazkale">Boğazkale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The Hittite Empire was at its height in the 14th century BCE, encompassing central Anatolia, north-western <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> as far as <a href="/wiki/Ugarit" title="Ugarit">Ugarit</a>, and upper Mesopotamia. <a href="/wiki/Kizzuwatna" title="Kizzuwatna">Kizzuwatna</a> in southern Anatolia controlled the region separating <a href="/wiki/Hittite_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Hittite Empire">Hatti</a> from <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>, thereby greatly affecting trade routes. The peace was kept in accordance with both empires through treaties that established boundaries of control. It was not until the reign of the Hittite king <a href="/wiki/Suppiluliumas" class="mw-redirect" title="Suppiluliumas">Suppiluliumas</a> that <a href="/wiki/Kizzuwatna" title="Kizzuwatna">Kizzuwatna</a> was taken over fully, although the <a href="/wiki/Hittites" title="Hittites">Hittites</a> still preserved their cultural accomplishments in <a href="/wiki/Kummanni" title="Kummanni">Kummanni</a> (now Şar, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>) and Lazawantiya, north of <a href="/wiki/Cilicia" title="Cilicia">Cilicia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the 1180s BCE, amid general turmoil in the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a> associated with the sudden arrival of the <a href="/wiki/Sea_Peoples" title="Sea Peoples">Sea Peoples</a>, the empire disintegrated into several independent "<a href="/wiki/Neo-Hittite" class="mw-redirect" title="Neo-Hittite">Neo-Hittite</a>" city-states, some of which survived until as late as the 8th century BCE. The history of the <a href="/wiki/Hittites" title="Hittites">Hittite</a> civilization is known mostly from <a href="/wiki/Cuneiform_(script)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cuneiform (script)">cuneiform</a> texts found in the area of their empire, and from diplomatic and commercial correspondence found in various archives in Egypt and the Middle East.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Iron_Age">Iron Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Iron Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Prehistory_of_Anatolia#Iron_Age" title="Prehistory of Anatolia">Prehistory_of_Anatolia §&#160;Iron_Age</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Phrygia" title="Phrygia">Phrygia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neo-Hittite" class="mw-redirect" title="Neo-Hittite">Neo-Hittite</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Urartu" title="Urartu">Urartu</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_of_Assyria.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Map_of_Assyria.png/250px-Map_of_Assyria.png" decoding="async" width="250" height="172" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Map_of_Assyria.png/375px-Map_of_Assyria.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Map_of_Assyria.png/500px-Map_of_Assyria.png 2x" data-file-width="1983" data-file-height="1363" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_of_Assyria.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Phrygia at the height of its power and Assyria, 9th-7th century BCE.</div></div></div>
<p>Beginning with the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_collapse" class="mw-redirect" title="Bronze Age collapse">Bronze Age collapse</a> at the end of the 2nd millennium BC, the west coast of Anatolia was settled by <a href="/wiki/Ionian_Greeks" class="mw-redirect" title="Ionian Greeks">Ionian Greeks</a>, usurping the related but earlier <a href="/wiki/Mycenaean_Greeks" class="mw-redirect" title="Mycenaean Greeks">Mycenaean Greeks</a>. Over several centuries, numerous Ancient Greek <a href="/wiki/City-state" title="City-state">city-states</a> were established on the coasts of Anatolia. Greeks started Western philosophy on the western coast of Anatolia (<a href="/wiki/Pre-Socratic_philosophy" title="Pre-Socratic philosophy">Pre-Socratic philosophy</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Phrygia" title="Phrygia">Phrygian</a> Kingdom essentially came into being after the fragmentation of the <a href="/wiki/Hittite_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Hittite Empire">Hittite Empire</a> during the 12th century BCE, and existed independently until the 7th century BCE. Possibly from the region of <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thrace</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Phrygians" title="Phrygians">Phrygians</a> eventually established their capital of <a href="/wiki/Gordium" title="Gordium">Gordium</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Yaz%C4%B1l%C4%B1kaya" title="Yazılıkaya">Yazılıkaya</a>). Known as <a href="/wiki/Mushki" title="Mushki">Mushki</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Assyrians" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Assyrians">Assyrians</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Phrygia" title="Phrygia">Phrygian</a> people lacked central control in their style of government, and yet established an extensive network of roads. They also held tightly onto a lot of the <a href="/wiki/Hittites" title="Hittites">Hittite</a> facets of culture and adapted them over time.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Shrouded in myth and legend<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Writing_better_articles#Tone" title="Wikipedia:Writing better articles"><span title="The tone or style of material in the vicinity of this tag may not be appropriate for Wikipedia. (February 2015)">tone</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> promulgated by ancient Greek and Roman writers is <a href="/wiki/King_Midas" class="mw-redirect" title="King Midas">King Midas</a>, the last king of the <a href="/wiki/Phrygia" title="Phrygia">Phrygian</a> Kingdom. The mythology of <a href="/wiki/King_Midas" class="mw-redirect" title="King Midas">Midas</a> revolves around his ability to turn objects to gold by mere touch, as granted by <a href="/wiki/Dionysos" class="mw-redirect" title="Dionysos">Dionysos</a>, and his unfortunate encounter with <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a> from which his ears are turned into the ears of a donkey. The historical record of <a href="/wiki/King_Midas" class="mw-redirect" title="King Midas">Midas</a> shows that he lived approximately between 740 and 696 BCE, and represented <a href="/wiki/Phrygia" title="Phrygia">Phrygia</a> as a great king. Most historians now consider him to be King Mita of the <a href="/wiki/Mushki" title="Mushki">Mushkis</a> as noted in <a href="/wiki/Assyria" title="Assyria">Assyrian</a> accounts. The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Assyrians" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Assyrians">Assyrians</a> thought of Mita as a dangerous foe, for <a href="/wiki/Sargon_II" title="Sargon II">Sargon II</a>, their ruler at the time, was quite happy to negotiate a peace treaty in 709 BCE. This treaty had no effect on the advancing <a href="/wiki/Cimmerians" title="Cimmerians">Cimmerians</a>, who streamed into Phrygia and led to the downfall and suicide of <a href="/wiki/King_Midas" class="mw-redirect" title="King Midas">King Midas</a> in 696 BCE.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Maeonia_and_the_Lydian_Kingdom">Maeonia and the Lydian Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Maeonia and the Lydian Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lydia" title="Lydia">Lydia</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:166px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Moneycroesus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/94/Moneycroesus.jpg" decoding="async" width="164" height="132" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="164" data-file-height="132" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Moneycroesus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lydian electrum coin, depicting a lion and bull.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:15th_century_map_of_Turkey_region.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/15th_century_map_of_Turkey_region.jpg/250px-15th_century_map_of_Turkey_region.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/15th_century_map_of_Turkey_region.jpg/375px-15th_century_map_of_Turkey_region.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/15th_century_map_of_Turkey_region.jpg/500px-15th_century_map_of_Turkey_region.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:15th_century_map_of_Turkey_region.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Photo of a 15th-century map showing Lydia.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lydia" title="Lydia">Lydia</a>, or Maeonia as it was called before 687 BCE, was a major part of the history of western Anatolia, beginning with the Atyad dynasty, who first appeared around 1300 BCE. The succeeding dynasty, the Heraclids, managed to rule successively from 1185-687 BCE despite a growing presence of <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Greece" title="Culture of Greece">Greek</a> influences along the Mediterranean coast. As Greek cities such as <a href="/wiki/Smyrna" title="Smyrna">Smyrna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colophon_(city)" title="Colophon (city)">Colophon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ephesus" title="Ephesus">Ephesus</a> rose, the <a href="/wiki/Heraclids" class="mw-redirect" title="Heraclids">Heraclids</a> became weaker and weaker. The last king, <a href="/wiki/Candaules" title="Candaules">Candaules</a>, was murdered by his friend and lance-bearer named <a href="/wiki/Gyges_of_Lydia" title="Gyges of Lydia">Gyges</a>, and he took over as ruler. <a href="/wiki/Gyges_of_Lydia" title="Gyges of Lydia">Gyges</a> waged war against the intruding Greeks, and soon faced by a grave problem as the <a href="/wiki/Cimmerians" title="Cimmerians">Cimmerians</a> began to pillage outlying cities within the kingdom. It was this wave of attacks that led to the incorporation of the formerly independent <a href="/wiki/Phrygia" title="Phrygia">Phrygia</a> and its capital <a href="/wiki/Gordium" title="Gordium">Gordium</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Lydia" title="Lydia">Lydian</a> domain. It was until the successive rules of <a href="/wiki/Sadyattes" title="Sadyattes">Sadyattes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alyattes_of_Lydia" title="Alyattes of Lydia">Alyattes</a>, ending in 560 BCE, that the attacks of the <a href="/wiki/Cimmerians" title="Cimmerians">Cimmerians</a> ended for good. Under the reign of the last <a href="/wiki/Lydia" title="Lydia">Lydian</a> king <a href="/wiki/Croesus" title="Croesus">Croesus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Persia" class="mw-redirect" title="Persia">Persia</a> was invaded first at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Pteria" title="Battle of Pteria">Battle of Pteria</a> ending without a victor. Progressing deeper into <a href="/wiki/Persia" class="mw-redirect" title="Persia">Persia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Croesus" title="Croesus">Croesus</a> was thoroughly defeated in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Thymbra" title="Battle of Thymbra">Battle of Thymbra</a> at the hands of the <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Persian</a> <a href="/wiki/Cyrus_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Cyrus II">Cyrus II</a> in 546 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_Antiquity">Classical Antiquity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Classical Antiquity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Classical_Anatolia" title="Classical Anatolia">Classical Anatolia</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Thracians" title="Thracians">Thracians</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Balkans#Antiquity" title="History of the Balkans">History of the Balkans §&#160;Antiquity</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Achaemenid_Empire">Achaemenid Empire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Achaemenid Empire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Achaemenid Empire</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Moneta_di_hekatomnos,_392-377_ac..JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Moneta_di_hekatomnos%2C_392-377_ac..JPG/250px-Moneta_di_hekatomnos%2C_392-377_ac..JPG" decoding="async" width="250" height="206" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Moneta_di_hekatomnos%2C_392-377_ac..JPG/375px-Moneta_di_hekatomnos%2C_392-377_ac..JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Moneta_di_hekatomnos%2C_392-377_ac..JPG/500px-Moneta_di_hekatomnos%2C_392-377_ac..JPG 2x" data-file-width="1743" data-file-height="1437" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Moneta_di_hekatomnos,_392-377_ac..JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Hecatomnus" title="Hecatomnus">Hecatomnus</a> coin, Bodrum Museum of Underwater Archaeology, <a href="/wiki/Bodrum" title="Bodrum">Bodrum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:RuinsofSardis.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/RuinsofSardis.jpg/200px-RuinsofSardis.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/RuinsofSardis.jpg/300px-RuinsofSardis.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/RuinsofSardis.jpg/400px-RuinsofSardis.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="960" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:RuinsofSardis.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The archaeological site of <a href="/wiki/Sardis" title="Sardis">Sardis</a>, today known as Sart in <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ruins_of_the_Mausoleum_at_Halicarnassus,_one_of_the_Seven_Wonders_of_the_Ancient_World.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Ruins_of_the_Mausoleum_at_Halicarnassus%2C_one_of_the_Seven_Wonders_of_the_Ancient_World.jpg/200px-Ruins_of_the_Mausoleum_at_Halicarnassus%2C_one_of_the_Seven_Wonders_of_the_Ancient_World.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Ruins_of_the_Mausoleum_at_Halicarnassus%2C_one_of_the_Seven_Wonders_of_the_Ancient_World.jpg/300px-Ruins_of_the_Mausoleum_at_Halicarnassus%2C_one_of_the_Seven_Wonders_of_the_Ancient_World.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Ruins_of_the_Mausoleum_at_Halicarnassus%2C_one_of_the_Seven_Wonders_of_the_Ancient_World.jpg/400px-Ruins_of_the_Mausoleum_at_Halicarnassus%2C_one_of_the_Seven_Wonders_of_the_Ancient_World.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="960" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ruins_of_the_Mausoleum_at_Halicarnassus,_one_of_the_Seven_Wonders_of_the_Ancient_World.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The ruins of the <a href="/wiki/Mausoleum_at_Halicarnassus" title="Mausoleum at Halicarnassus">Mausoleum at Halicarnassus</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bodrum" title="Bodrum">Bodrum</a>, one of the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Wonders_of_the_Ancient_World" title="Seven Wonders of the Ancient World">Seven Wonders of the Ancient World</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>By 550 BCE, the <a href="/wiki/Median_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Median Empire">Median Empire</a>, which had existed for barely a hundred years, was suddenly torn apart by a Persian rebellion.  As <a href="/wiki/Lydia" title="Lydia">Lydia</a>'s king, <a href="/wiki/Croesus" title="Croesus">Croesus</a> had a large amount of wealth which to draw from, and he used it to go on the offensive against the <a href="/wiki/Persia" class="mw-redirect" title="Persia">Persian</a> king <a href="/wiki/Cyrus_the_Great" title="Cyrus the Great">Cyrus the Great</a>. In the end, <a href="/wiki/Croesus" title="Croesus">Croesus</a> was thrust back west and Cyrus burned the Lydian capital <a href="/wiki/Sardis" title="Sardis">Sardis</a>, taking control of Lydia in 546 BCE.<sup id="cite_ref-Botsford_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Botsford-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The remaining kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Ionia" title="Ionia">Ionia</a> and several cities of Lydia still refused to fall under <a href="/wiki/Persia" class="mw-redirect" title="Persia">Persian</a> domination, and prepared defenses to fight them and sending for aid from <a href="/wiki/Sparta" title="Sparta">Sparta</a>. Since no aid was promised except for a warning to Cyrus from their emissary, eventually their stance was abandoned and they submitted, or they fled as in citizens from <a href="/wiki/Phocaea" title="Phocaea">Phocaea</a> to <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a> or citizens from <a href="/wiki/Teos" title="Teos">Teos</a> to <a href="/wiki/Abdera,_Thrace" title="Abdera, Thrace">Abdera</a> in <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thrace</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Botsford2_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Botsford2-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid" class="mw-redirect" title="Achaemenid">Achaemenid</a> Persian Empire, thus founded by Cyrus the Great, continued its expansion under the <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Persia</a> king <a href="/wiki/Darius_the_Great" title="Darius the Great">Darius the Great</a>, in which the <a href="/wiki/Satrap" title="Satrap">satrap</a> system of local governors continued to be used and upgraded and other governmental upgrades were carried out. A revolt by <a href="/wiki/Naxos_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="Naxos Island">Naxos</a> in 502 BCE prompted <a href="/wiki/Aristagoras" title="Aristagoras">Aristagoras</a> of <a href="/wiki/Miletus" title="Miletus">Miletus</a> to devise a grandiose plan by which he would give a share of <a href="/wiki/Naxos_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="Naxos Island">Naxos</a>'s wealth to <a href="/wiki/Artaphernes" title="Artaphernes">Artaphernes</a>, satrap of Lydia, in return for his aid in quashing the revolt. The failure of Aristagoras in fulfilling his promise of rewards and his conduct disturbed the Persians, so much so that he resorted to convincing his fellow Ionians to revolt against the Persians. This revolt, known as the <a href="/wiki/Ionian_Revolt" title="Ionian Revolt">Ionian Revolt</a>, spread across Anatolia, and with <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athenian</a> aid, Aristagoras held firm for a time, despite the loss in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Ephesus_(498_BC)" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Ephesus (498 BC)">Battle of Ephesus</a>. The burning of <a href="/wiki/Sardis" title="Sardis">Sardis</a> in 498 BCE enraged <a href="/wiki/Darius_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Darius I">Darius</a> so much that he swore revenge upon <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>. This event brought down the hammer upon Aristagoras as the Persian army swept through Ionia, re-taking city by city. It was the eventual <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Lade" title="Battle of Lade">Battle of Lade</a> outside <a href="/wiki/Miletus" title="Miletus">Miletus</a> in 494 BCE that put an end to the <a href="/wiki/Ionian_Revolt" title="Ionian Revolt">Ionian Revolt</a> once and for all.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Persian Empire</a> had official control of the Carians as a satrap, the appointed local ruler <a href="/wiki/Hecatomnus" title="Hecatomnus">Hecatomnus</a> took advantage of his position. He gained for his family an autonomous hand in control of the province by providing the <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Persians</a> with regular tribute, avoiding the look of deception. His son <a href="/wiki/Mausolus" title="Mausolus">Mausolus</a> continued in this manner, and expanded upon the groundwork laid by his father. He first removed the official capital of the satrap from <a href="/wiki/Mylasa" class="mw-redirect" title="Mylasa">Mylasa</a> to <a href="/wiki/Halicarnassus" title="Halicarnassus">Halicarnassus</a>, gaining a strategic naval advantage as the new capital was on the ocean. On this land he built a strong fortress and a works by which he could build up a strong navy. He shrewdly used this power to guarantee protection for the citizens of <a href="/wiki/Chios" title="Chios">Chios</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kos" title="Kos">Kos</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rhodes" title="Rhodes">Rhodes</a> as they proclaimed independence from <a href="/wiki/Athenian" class="mw-redirect" title="Athenian">Athenian</a> Greece. <a href="/wiki/Mausolus" title="Mausolus">Mausolus</a> did not live to see his plans realized fully, and his position went to his widow <a href="/wiki/Artemisia_II_of_Caria" title="Artemisia II of Caria">Artemisia</a>. The local control over Caria remained in <a href="/wiki/Hecatomnus" title="Hecatomnus">Hecatomnus</a>'s family for another 20 years before the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bury_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bury-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Alexander_the_Great">Alexander the Great</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Alexander the Great">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 336 BCE, King <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Macedon" title="Philip II of Macedon">Philip of Macedon</a> was unexpectedly killed, making his son <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander</a> the new ruler of <a href="/wiki/Macedon" class="mw-redirect" title="Macedon">Macedon</a> as he was very popular. He immediately went to work, raising a force large enough to go up against the <a href="/wiki/Persia" class="mw-redirect" title="Persia">Persians</a>, gathering a navy large enough to counter any threats by their powerful navy. Landing on the shores of Anatolia near <a href="/wiki/Sestos" title="Sestos">Sestos</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Gallipoli" title="Gallipoli">Gallipoli</a> in 334 BCE, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander</a> first faced the <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Persian</a> army in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Granicus" title="Battle of the Granicus">Battle of the Granicus</a>, in which the <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Persians</a> were effectively routed. Using the victory as a springboard for success, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander</a> turned his attention to the rest of the western coast, liberating <a href="/wiki/Lydia" title="Lydia">Lydia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ionia" title="Ionia">Ionia</a> in quick succession. The eventual fall of <a href="/wiki/Miletus" title="Miletus">Miletus</a> led to the brilliant strategy by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander</a> to defeat the <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Persian</a> navy by taking every city along the Mediterranean instead of initiating a very high-risk battle on the sea. By reducing this threat, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander</a> turned inland, rolling through <a href="/wiki/Phyrgia" class="mw-redirect" title="Phyrgia">Phyrgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cappadocia" title="Cappadocia">Cappadocia</a>, and finally <a href="/wiki/Cilicia" title="Cilicia">Cilicia</a>, before reaching <a href="/wiki/Nur_Mountains" title="Nur Mountains">Mount Amanus</a>. Scouts for  <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander</a> found the <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Persian</a> army, under its king <a href="/wiki/Darius_III" title="Darius III">Darius III</a>, advancing through the plains of <a href="/wiki/Issus_(town)" class="mw-redirect" title="Issus (town)">Issus</a> in search of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander</a>. At this moment, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander</a> realized that the terrain favored his smaller army, and the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Issus" title="Battle of Issus">Battle of Issus</a> began. <a href="/wiki/Darius_III" title="Darius III">Darius's</a> army was effectively squeezed by the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Macedonians" title="Ancient Macedonians">Macedonians</a>, leading to not only an embarrassing defeat for <a href="/wiki/Darius_III" title="Darius III">Darius</a>, but that he fled back across the <a href="/wiki/Euphrates" title="Euphrates">Euphrates</a> river, leaving the rest of his family in <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander's</a> hands. Thus, Anatolia was freed from the <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Persian</a> yoke for good.<sup id="cite_ref-Bury2_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bury2-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Wars_of_the_Diadochi_and_division_of_Alexander.27s_empire"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Wars_of_the_Diadochi_and_division_of_Alexander's_empire">Wars of the Diadochi and division of Alexander's empire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Wars of the Diadochi and division of Alexander&#039;s empire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Diadochi" title="Diadochi">Diadochi</a></div>
<p>In June 323 BCE, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander</a> died suddenly, leaving a power vacuum in <a href="/wiki/Macedon" class="mw-redirect" title="Macedon">Macedon</a>, putting all he had worked for at risk. Being that his half-brother <a href="/wiki/Philip_III_of_Macedon" title="Philip III of Macedon">Arrhidaeus</a> was unable to rule effectively due to a serious disability, a succession of wars over the rights to his conquests were fought known as the Wars of the <a href="/wiki/Diadochi" title="Diadochi">Diadochi</a>. <a href="/wiki/Perdiccas" title="Perdiccas">Perdiccas</a>, a high-ranking officer of the cavalry, and later <a href="/wiki/Antigonus_I_Monophthalmus" title="Antigonus I Monophthalmus">Antigonus</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Phrygia" title="Phrygia">Phrygian</a> <a href="/wiki/Satrap" title="Satrap">satrap</a>, prevailed over the other contenders of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander's</a> empire in Asia for a time.<sup id="cite_ref-Freeman2_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Freeman2-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ptolemy_I_Soter" title="Ptolemy I Soter">Ptolemy</a>, the governor of Egypt, <a href="/wiki/Lysimachus" title="Lysimachus">Lysimachus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Seleucus_I_Nicator" title="Seleucus I Nicator">Seleucus</a>, strong leaders of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander's</a>, consolidated their positions after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Ipsus" title="Battle of Ipsus">Battle of Ipsus</a>, in which their common rival <a href="/wiki/Antigonus_I_Monophthalmus" title="Antigonus I Monophthalmus">Antigonus</a> was defeated. The former empire of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander</a> was divided as such: <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a> gained territory in southern <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>, much of <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> and the  <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a>, which combined to form the <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Ptolemaic Empire">Ptolemaic Empire</a>; <a href="/wiki/Lysimachus" title="Lysimachus">Lysimachus</a> controlled western <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thrace</a>, while Seleucus claimed the rest of <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Seleucid_Empire" title="Seleucid Empire">Seleucid Empire</a>. Only the kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Pontus" title="Kingdom of Pontus">Pontus</a> under <a href="/wiki/Mithridates_I_of_Pontus" title="Mithridates I of Pontus">Mithridates I</a> managed to gain their independence in <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a> due to the fact that Antigonus had been a common enemy.<sup id="cite_ref-Rawlinson_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rawlinson-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Seleucid_Empire">Seleucid Empire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Seleucid Empire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Seleucid_Empire" title="Seleucid Empire">Seleucid Empire</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Seleucusnicatorcoin.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Seleucusnicatorcoin.jpg/250px-Seleucusnicatorcoin.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="123" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/ae/Seleucusnicatorcoin.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="254" data-file-height="125" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Seleucusnicatorcoin.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Seleucus I Nicator, namesake of the Seleucid Empire</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Seleucus_I_Nicator" title="Seleucus I Nicator">Seleucus I Nicator</a> first created a capital city over the span of 12 years (299 BCE-287 BCE) worthy of his personage, <a href="/wiki/Antioch" title="Antioch">Antioch</a>, named after his father <a href="/wiki/Antiochus_(father_of_Seleucus_I_Nicator)" title="Antiochus (father of Seleucus I Nicator)">Antiochus</a>. He concentrated also on creating a large standing army, and also divided his empire into 72 <a href="/wiki/Satrapy" class="mw-redirect" title="Satrapy">satrapies</a> for easier administration. After a peaceful beginning, a rift occurred between <a href="/wiki/Lysimachus" title="Lysimachus">Lysimachus</a> and Seleucus that led to open warfare in 281 BCE. Even though Seleucus had managed to defeat his former friend and gain his territory at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Corupedium" title="Battle of Corupedium">Battle of Corupedium</a>, it cost him his life as he was assassinated by <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy_Keraunos" title="Ptolemy Keraunos">Ptolemy Keraunos</a>, future king of <a href="/wiki/Macedon" class="mw-redirect" title="Macedon">Macedon</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Lysimachia" title="Lysimachia">Lysimachia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rawlinson2_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rawlinson2-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the death of Seleucus, the empire he left faced many trials, both from internal and external forces. <a href="/wiki/Antiochus_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Antiochus I">Antiochus I</a> fought off an attack from the <a href="/wiki/Gauls" title="Gauls">Gauls</a> successfully, but could not defeat the King of <a href="/wiki/Pergamon" title="Pergamon">Pergamon</a> <a href="/wiki/Eumenes_I" title="Eumenes I">Eumenes I</a> in 262 BCE, guaranteeing <a href="/wiki/Pergamon" title="Pergamon">Pergamon</a>'s independence.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Antiochus_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Antiochus II">Antiochus II</a> named Theos, or "divine", was poisoned by his first wife, who in turn poisoned <a href="/wiki/Berenice_Phernophorus" class="mw-redirect" title="Berenice Phernophorus">Berenice Phernophorus</a>, second wife of <a href="/wiki/Antiochus_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Antiochus II">Antiochus</a> and the daughter of <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy_III_Euergetes" title="Ptolemy III Euergetes">Ptolemy III Euergetes</a>. <a href="/wiki/Antiochus_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Antiochus II">Antiochus II</a>'s son from his first wife, <a href="/wiki/Seleucus_II_Callinicus" title="Seleucus II Callinicus">Seleucus II Callinicus</a>, ended up as ruler of the <a href="/wiki/Seleucid_Empire" title="Seleucid Empire">Seleucids</a> after this tragedy. These turn of events made Ptolemy III very angry, and led to the invasion of the empire (the <a href="/wiki/Syrian_Wars" title="Syrian Wars">Third Syrian War</a>) in 246 BCE. This invasion leads to victory for Ptolemy III at <a href="/wiki/Antioch" title="Antioch">Antioch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seleucia" title="Seleucia">Seleucia</a>, and he grants the lands of <a href="/wiki/Phrygia" title="Phrygia">Phrygia</a> to <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Pontus" title="Kingdom of Pontus">Pontus</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Mithridates_II_of_Pontus" title="Mithridates II of Pontus">Mithridates II</a> in 245 BCE as a wedding gift.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Parthia_and_Pergamon_before_200_BCE">Parthia and Pergamon before 200 BCE</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Parthia and Pergamon before 200 BCE">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dying_gaul.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Dying_gaul.jpg/250px-Dying_gaul.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="203" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Dying_gaul.jpg/375px-Dying_gaul.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Dying_gaul.jpg/500px-Dying_gaul.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1458" data-file-height="1186" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dying_gaul.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <i>"Dying Gaul"</i> representing the defeat of the <a href="/wiki/Galatians_(people)" title="Galatians (people)">Galatians</a> by <a href="/wiki/Attalus_I" title="Attalus I">Attalus I</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Events in the east showed the fragile nature of the <a href="/wiki/Seleucid_Empire" title="Seleucid Empire">Seleucids</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Bactria" title="Bactria">Bactrian</a>-inspired revolt in <a href="/wiki/Parthia" title="Parthia">Parthia</a> begun by its <a href="/wiki/Satrap" title="Satrap">satrap</a> <a href="/wiki/Andragoras_(3rd_century_BC)" class="mw-redirect" title="Andragoras (3rd century BC)">Andragoras</a> in 245 BCE led to the loss of territory bordering <a href="/wiki/Persia" class="mw-redirect" title="Persia">Persia</a>. This was coupled with an unexpected invasion of northern <a href="/wiki/Parthia" title="Parthia">Parthia</a> by the nomadic <a href="/wiki/Parni" title="Parni">Parni</a> in 238 BCE and a subsequent occupation of the whole of <a href="/wiki/Parthia" title="Parthia">Parthia</a> by one of their leaders, <a href="/wiki/Tiridates_I_of_Parthia" title="Tiridates I of Parthia">Tiridates</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Antiochus_II_Theos" title="Antiochus II Theos">Antiochus II Theos</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Seleucids" class="mw-redirect" title="Seleucids">Seleucids</a> failed to end the rebellion, and therefore a new kingdom was created, the <a href="/wiki/Parthian_Empire" title="Parthian Empire">Parthian Empire</a>, under Tiridates's brother <a href="/wiki/Arsaces_I_of_Parthia" title="Arsaces I of Parthia">Arsaces I</a>. Parthia extended to the <a href="/wiki/Euphrates_river" class="mw-redirect" title="Euphrates river">Euphrates river</a> at the height of its power.<sup id="cite_ref-Rawlinson3_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rawlinson3-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Pergamon" title="Pergamon">Pergamon</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Attalid_dynasty" title="Attalid dynasty">Attalid dynasty</a> was an independent kingdom established after the rule of <a href="/wiki/Philetaerus" title="Philetaerus">Philetaerus</a> by his nephew <a href="/wiki/Eumenes_I" title="Eumenes I">Eumenes I</a>. Eumenes enlarged Pergamon to include parts of <a href="/wiki/Mysia" title="Mysia">Mysia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aeolis" title="Aeolis">Aeolis</a>, and held tightly onto the ports of Elaia and <a href="/wiki/Pitane_(Aeolis)" title="Pitane (Aeolis)">Pitane</a>. <a href="/wiki/Attalus_I" title="Attalus I">Attalus I</a>, successor of <a href="/wiki/Eumenes_I" title="Eumenes I">Eumenes I</a>, remained active outside of the boundaries of Pergamon. He refused protection payment to the <a href="/wiki/Galatia" title="Galatia">Galatians</a> and won a fight against them in 230 BCE, and then defeated <a href="/wiki/Antiochus_Hierax" title="Antiochus Hierax">Antiochus Hierax</a> three years later in order to secure nominal control over Anatolia under the <a href="/wiki/Seleucids" class="mw-redirect" title="Seleucids">Seleucids</a>. The victory was not to last as <a href="/wiki/Seleucus_III" class="mw-redirect" title="Seleucus III">Seleucus III</a> reestablished control of his empire, but Attalus was allowed to retain control of former territories of Pergamon.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The dealings with <a href="/wiki/Attalus_I" title="Attalus I">Attalus</a> proved to be the last time the <a href="/wiki/Seleucids" class="mw-redirect" title="Seleucids">Seleucids</a> had any meaningful success in Anatolia as the Roman Empire lay on the horizon. After that victory, <a href="/wiki/Seleucus_III" class="mw-redirect" title="Seleucus III">Seleucus's</a> heirs would never again expand their empire.<sup id="cite_ref-Freeman2_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Freeman2-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_period">Roman period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Roman period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:RomanPowerAsiaMinor188BCE.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/RomanPowerAsiaMinor188BCE.JPG/250px-RomanPowerAsiaMinor188BCE.JPG" decoding="async" width="250" height="138" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/RomanPowerAsiaMinor188BCE.JPG/375px-RomanPowerAsiaMinor188BCE.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/RomanPowerAsiaMinor188BCE.JPG/500px-RomanPowerAsiaMinor188BCE.JPG 2x" data-file-width="763" data-file-height="420" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:RomanPowerAsiaMinor188BCE.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Apamea" title="Treaty of Apamea">Treaty of Apamea</a> in 188 BCE.</div></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_intervention_in_Anatolia">Roman intervention in Anatolia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Roman intervention in Anatolia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Second_Punic_War" title="Second Punic War">Second Punic War</a>, Rome had suffered in Spain, Africa, and Italy because of the impressive strategies of <a href="/wiki/Hannibal" title="Hannibal">Hannibal</a>, the famous <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Carthage" title="Ancient Carthage">Carthaginian</a> general. When Hannibal entered into an alliance with <a href="/wiki/Philip_V_of_Macedon" title="Philip V of Macedon">Philip V of Macedon</a> in 215 BCE, Rome used a small naval force with the <a href="/wiki/Aetolian_League" title="Aetolian League">Aetolian League</a> to help ward off Hannibal in the east and to prevent <a href="/wiki/Macedon" class="mw-redirect" title="Macedon">Macedonian</a> expansion in western Anatolia. <a href="/wiki/Attalus_I" title="Attalus I">Attalus I</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pergamon" title="Pergamon">Pergamon</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Rhodes" title="Rhodes">Rhodes</a>, traveled to Rome and helped convince the Romans that war against <a href="/wiki/Macedon" class="mw-redirect" title="Macedon">Macedon</a> was supremely necessary. The Roman general <a href="/wiki/Titus_Quinctius_Flamininus" title="Titus Quinctius Flamininus">Titus Quinctius Flamininus</a> not only soundly defeated <a href="/wiki/Philip_V_of_Macedon" title="Philip V of Macedon">Philip's</a> army in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cynoscephalae" title="Battle of Cynoscephalae">Battle of Cynoscephalae</a> in 197 BCE, but also brought further hope to the <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greeks</a> when he said that an autonomous Greece and Greek cities in Anatolia was what Rome desired.<sup id="cite_ref-Freeman_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Freeman-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the period just after Rome's victory, the <a href="/wiki/Aetolian_League" title="Aetolian League">Aetolian League</a> desired some of the spoils left in the wake of <a href="/wiki/Philip_V_of_Macedon" title="Philip V of Macedon">Philip's</a> defeat, and requested a shared expedition with <a href="/wiki/Antiochus_III" class="mw-redirect" title="Antiochus III">Antiochus III</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Seleucids" class="mw-redirect" title="Seleucids">Seleucids</a> to obtain it. Despite warnings by Rome, Antiochus left <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thrace</a> and ventured into Greece, deciding to ally himself with the <a href="/wiki/Aetolian_League" title="Aetolian League">League</a>. This was intolerable for Rome, and they soundly defeated him in <a href="/wiki/Thessaly" title="Thessaly">Thessaly</a> at <a href="/wiki/Thermopylae" title="Thermopylae">Thermopylae</a> before Antiochus retreated to <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a> near <a href="/wiki/Sardis" title="Sardis">Sardis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Freeman_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Freeman-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Combining forces with the Romans, <a href="/wiki/Eumenes_II" title="Eumenes II">Eumenes II</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pergamon" title="Pergamon">Pergamon</a> met Antiochus in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Magnesia" title="Battle of Magnesia">Battle of Magnesia</a> in 189 BCE. There Antiochus was thrashed by an intensive cavalry charge by the Romans and an outflanking maneuver by Eumenes.
</p><p>Because of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Apamea" title="Treaty of Apamea">Treaty of Apamea</a> the very next year, <a href="/wiki/Pergamon" title="Pergamon">Pergamon</a> was granted all of the <a href="/wiki/Seleucid" class="mw-redirect" title="Seleucid">Seleucid</a> lands north of the <a href="/wiki/Taurus_mountains" class="mw-redirect" title="Taurus mountains">Taurus mountains</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rhodes" title="Rhodes">Rhodes</a> was given all that remained. This seemingly great reward would be the downfall of Eumenes as an effective ruler, for after Pergamon defeated <a href="/wiki/Prusias_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Prusias I">Prusias I</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bithynia" title="Bithynia">Bithynia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pharnaces_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Pharnaces I">Pharnaces I</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Pontus" title="Kingdom of Pontus">Pontus</a>, he delved too deeply into Roman affairs and the Roman senate became alarmed. When Eumenes put down an invasion by the <a href="/wiki/Galatia" title="Galatia">Galatians</a> in 184 BCE, Rome countered his victory by freeing them, providing a heavy indicator that the scope of <a href="/wiki/Pergamon" title="Pergamon">Pergamon</a>'s rule was now stunted.<sup id="cite_ref-OCD_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OCD-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:RomanPowerAsiaMinor90BCE.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/RomanPowerAsiaMinor90BCE.JPG/250px-RomanPowerAsiaMinor90BCE.JPG" decoding="async" width="250" height="138" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/RomanPowerAsiaMinor90BCE.JPG/375px-RomanPowerAsiaMinor90BCE.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/RomanPowerAsiaMinor90BCE.JPG/500px-RomanPowerAsiaMinor90BCE.JPG 2x" data-file-width="762" data-file-height="420" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:RomanPowerAsiaMinor90BCE.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a> before the <a href="/wiki/Mithridatic_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Mithridatic War">Mithridatic War</a>, 90 BCE.</div></div></div>
<p>The interior of Anatolia had been relatively stable despite occasional incursions by the <a href="/wiki/Galatia" title="Galatia">Galatians</a> until the rise of the kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Pontus" title="Kingdom of Pontus">Pontus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cappadocia" title="Cappadocia">Cappadocia</a> in the 2nd century BCE. <a href="/wiki/Cappadocia" title="Cappadocia">Cappadocia</a> under <a href="/wiki/Ariarathes_IV" class="mw-redirect" title="Ariarathes IV">Ariarathes IV</a> initially was allied with the <a href="/wiki/Seleucids" class="mw-redirect" title="Seleucids">Seleucids</a> in their war against Rome, but he soon changed his mind and repaired relations with them by marriage and his conduct. His son, <a href="/wiki/Ariarathes_V" class="mw-redirect" title="Ariarathes V">Ariarathes V Philopator</a>, continued his father's policy of allying with Rome and even joined with them in battle against <a href="/wiki/Prusias_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Prusias I">Prusias I</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bithynia" title="Bithynia">Bithynia</a> when he died in 131 BCE. <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Pontus" title="Kingdom of Pontus">Pontus</a> had been an independent kingdom since the rule of <a href="/wiki/Mithridates_I_of_Pontus" title="Mithridates I of Pontus">Mithridates</a> when the threat of <a href="/wiki/Macedon" class="mw-redirect" title="Macedon">Macedon</a> had been removed. Despite several attempts by the <a href="/wiki/Seleucid_Empire" title="Seleucid Empire">Seleucid Empire</a> to defeat <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Pontus" title="Kingdom of Pontus">Pontus</a>, independence was maintained. When Rome became involved in Anatolian affairs under <a href="/wiki/Pharnaces_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Pharnaces I">Pharnaces I</a>, an alliance was formed that guaranteed protection for the kingdom. The other major kingdom in Anatolia, <a href="/wiki/Bithynia" title="Bithynia">Bithynia</a>, established by <a href="/wiki/Nicomedes_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Nicomedes I">Nicomedes I</a> at <a href="/wiki/Nicomedia" title="Nicomedia">Nicomedia</a>, always maintained good relations with Rome. Even under the hated <a href="/wiki/Prusias_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Prusias II">Prusias II</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bithynia" title="Bithynia">Bithynia</a> when that relationship was strained it did not cause much trouble.<sup id="cite_ref-Rawlinson2_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rawlinson2-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The rule of Rome in Anatolia was unlike any other part of their empire because of their light hand with regards to government and organization. Controlling unstable elements within the region was made simpler by the bequeathal of <a href="/wiki/Pergamon" title="Pergamon">Pergamon</a> to the Romans by its last king, <a href="/wiki/Attalus_III" title="Attalus III">Attalus III</a> in 133 BCE. The new territory was named the province of Asia by Roman <a href="/wiki/Consul" title="Consul">consul</a> <a href="/wiki/Manius_Aquillius_(consul_129_BC)" title="Manius Aquillius (consul 129 BC)">Manius Aquillius the Elder</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-OCD_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OCD-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Mithridatic_Wars">The Mithridatic Wars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: The Mithridatic Wars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mithridatic_Wars" title="Mithridatic Wars">Mithridatic Wars</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:RomanPowerAsiaMinor63BCE.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/RomanPowerAsiaMinor63BCE.JPG/250px-RomanPowerAsiaMinor63BCE.JPG" decoding="async" width="250" height="137" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/RomanPowerAsiaMinor63BCE.JPG/375px-RomanPowerAsiaMinor63BCE.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/RomanPowerAsiaMinor63BCE.JPG/500px-RomanPowerAsiaMinor63BCE.JPG 2x" data-file-width="763" data-file-height="418" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:RomanPowerAsiaMinor63BCE.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a> as divided by <a href="/wiki/Pompey" title="Pompey">Pompey</a>, 63 BCE.</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Mithridatic_Wars" title="Mithridatic Wars">Mithridatic Wars</a> were precluded by infighting that drew Rome into a war against <a href="/wiki/Italian_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian people">Italian</a> rebels known as the <a href="/wiki/Social_War_(91%E2%80%9388_BC)" title="Social War (91–88 BC)">Social War</a> in 90 BCE. <a href="/wiki/Mithridates_VI" class="mw-redirect" title="Mithridates VI">Mithridates VI</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Pontus" title="Kingdom of Pontus">Pontus</a> decided that it was time to strike in Anatolia while Rome was occupied, overrunning  <a href="/wiki/Bithynia" title="Bithynia">Bithynia</a>. Though he withdrew when this was demanded of him by Rome he did not agree to all Romes demands. As a result, Rome encouraged Bithynia to attack Pontus but Bithynia was defeated.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Mithridates then marched into the Roman province of Asia, where he persuaded <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greeks</a> to slaughter as many <a href="/wiki/Italians" title="Italians">Italians</a> as possible (the <a href="/wiki/Asiatic_Vespers" title="Asiatic Vespers">Asiatic Vespers</a>). Despite a power struggle within Rome itself, consul <a href="/wiki/Cornelius_Sulla" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornelius Sulla">Cornelius Sulla</a> went to Anatolia to defeat the <a href="/wiki/Pontic_Greeks" title="Pontic Greeks">Pontian</a> king. <a href="/wiki/Cornelius_Sulla" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornelius Sulla">Sulla</a> defeated him thoroughly in and left <a href="/wiki/Mithridates_VI" class="mw-redirect" title="Mithridates VI">Mithridates</a> with only <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Pontus" title="Kingdom of Pontus">Pontus</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Dardanos" title="Treaty of Dardanos">Treaty of Dardanos</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Freeman_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Freeman-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 74 BCE, another Anatolian kingdom passed under Roman control as <a href="/wiki/Nicomedes_IV" class="mw-redirect" title="Nicomedes IV">Nicomedes IV</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bithynia" title="Bithynia">Bithynia</a> instructed it to be done after his death. Making <a href="/wiki/Bithynia" title="Bithynia">Bithynia</a> a Roman province soon after roused <a href="/wiki/Mithridates_VI" class="mw-redirect" title="Mithridates VI">Mithridates VI</a> to once again go after more territory, and he invaded it in the same year. Rome this time sent consul <a href="/wiki/Lucullus" title="Lucullus">Lucius Licinius Lucullus</a> to take back control of the province. The expedition proved to be very positive as <a href="/wiki/Mithridates_VI" class="mw-redirect" title="Mithridates VI">Mithridates</a> was driven back into the mountains.<sup id="cite_ref-Freeman_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Freeman-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The failure of <a href="/wiki/Lucullus" title="Lucullus">Lucius Licinius Lucullus</a> to rid Rome once and for all of <a href="/wiki/Mithridates_VI" class="mw-redirect" title="Mithridates VI">Mithridates</a> brought a lot of opposition back at home, some fueled by the great Roman consul <a href="/wiki/Pompey" title="Pompey">Pompey</a>. A threat by pirates on the Roman food supply in the <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Sea" title="Aegean Sea">Aegean Sea</a> brought <a href="/wiki/Pompey" title="Pompey">Pompey</a> once again to the forefront of Roman politics, and he drove them back to <a href="/wiki/Cilicia" title="Cilicia">Cilicia</a>. The powers granted <a href="/wiki/Pompey" title="Pompey">Pompey</a> after this success allowed him to not only throw back <a href="/wiki/Mithridates_VI" class="mw-redirect" title="Mithridates VI">Mithridates</a> all the way to the <a href="/wiki/Bosphorus" class="mw-redirect" title="Bosphorus">Bosphorus</a>, but made neighboring <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a> a <a href="/wiki/Patronage_in_ancient_Rome" title="Patronage in ancient Rome">client kingdom</a>. In the end, <a href="/wiki/Mithridates_VI" class="mw-redirect" title="Mithridates VI">Mithridates</a> committed suicide in 63 BCE, and therefore allowed Rome to add <a href="/wiki/Pontus_(region)" title="Pontus (region)">Pontus</a> as a protectorate along with <a href="/wiki/Cilicia" title="Cilicia">Cilicia</a> as a Roman province.<sup id="cite_ref-Freeman_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Freeman-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> This left only <a href="/wiki/Galatia" title="Galatia">Galatia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pisidia" title="Pisidia">Pisidia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cappadocia" title="Cappadocia">Cappadocia</a>, all ruled by <a href="/wiki/Amyntas_of_Galatia" title="Amyntas of Galatia">Amyntas</a> in whole, as the last remaining kingdom not under a protectorate or provincial status. However, in 25 BCE, <a href="/wiki/Amyntas_of_Galatia" title="Amyntas of Galatia">Amyntas</a> died while pursuing enemies in the <a href="/wiki/Taurus_mountains" class="mw-redirect" title="Taurus mountains">Taurus mountains</a>, and Rome claimed his lands as a province, leaving Anatolia completely in Roman hands.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Christianity_in_Anatolia_during_Roman_times">Christianity in Anatolia during Roman times</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Christianity in Anatolia during Roman times">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Christianity" title="History of Christianity">History of Christianity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Early_Christianity" title="Early Christianity">Early Christianity</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Early_centers_of_Christianity#Western_Anatolia" title="Early centers of Christianity">Early centers of Christianity §&#160;Western Anatolia</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_Judaism" title="Hellenistic Judaism">Hellenistic Judaism</a></div>
<p>Jewish influences in Anatolia were changing the religious makeup of the region as Rome consolidated its power. In about 210 BCE, <a href="/wiki/Antiochus_III" class="mw-redirect" title="Antiochus III">Antiochus III</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Seleucid_Empire" title="Seleucid Empire">Seleucid Empire</a> relocated 2,000 families of Jews from <a href="/wiki/Babylonia" title="Babylonia">Babylonia</a> to <a href="/wiki/Lydia" title="Lydia">Lydia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phrygia" title="Phrygia">Phrygia</a>, and this kind of migration continued throughout the remainder of the Empire's existence. Additional clues to the size of the Jewish influence in the area were provided by <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a>, who noted that a fellow Roman governor had halted the tribute sent to <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a> by Jews in 66 BCE, and the record of <a href="/wiki/Ephesus" title="Ephesus">Ephesus</a>, where the people urged <a href="/wiki/Agrippina_the_Younger" title="Agrippina the Younger">Agrippina</a> to expel <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a> because they were not active in their religious activities.<sup id="cite_ref-Ramsay_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ramsay-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The blossoming religious following of Christianity was evident in Anatolia during the beginning of the 1st century. The letters of <a href="/wiki/Paul_the_Apostle" title="Paul the Apostle">St. Paul</a> in the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> reflect this growth, particularly in his home province of Asia. From his home in <a href="/wiki/Ephesus" title="Ephesus">Ephesus</a> from 54 AD to 56 AD he noted that "all they which dwelt in Asia heard the word" and verified the existence of a church in <a href="/wiki/Colossae" title="Colossae">Colossae</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Troad" title="Troad">Troas</a>. Later he received letters from <a href="/wiki/Magnesia_ad_Sipylum" title="Magnesia ad Sipylum">Magnesia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tralleis" class="mw-redirect" title="Tralleis">Tralleis</a>, both of which already had churches, bishops, and official representatives who supported <a href="/wiki/Ignatius_of_Antioch" title="Ignatius of Antioch">Ignatius of Antioch</a>. After the references to these institutions by <a href="/wiki/Paul_the_Apostle" title="Paul the Apostle">St. Paul</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation" title="Book of Revelation">Book of Revelation</a> mentions the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Churches_of_Asia" class="mw-redirect" title="Seven Churches of Asia">Seven Churches of Asia</a>: <a href="/wiki/Ephesus" title="Ephesus">Ephesus</a>, Magnesia, <a href="/wiki/Thyatira" title="Thyatira">Thyatira</a>, <a href="/wiki/Smyrna" title="Smyrna">Smyrna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ala%C5%9Fehir" title="Alaşehir">Philadelphia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pergamon" title="Pergamon">Pergamon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Laodicea_on_the_Lycus" title="Laodicea on the Lycus">Laodicea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ramsay2_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ramsay2-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Even other non-Christians started to take notice of the new religion. In 112 the Roman governor in <a href="/wiki/Bithynia" title="Bithynia">Bithynia</a> writes to the Roman emperor <a href="/wiki/Trajan" title="Trajan">Trajan</a> that so many different people are flocking to Christianity, leaving the temples vacated.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Anatolia_before_the_4th_century:_Peace_and_the_Goths">Anatolia before the 4th century: Peace and the Goths</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Anatolia before the 4th century: Peace and the Goths">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aureus_Valerian-RIC_0034-transparent.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Aureus_Valerian-RIC_0034-transparent.png/200px-Aureus_Valerian-RIC_0034-transparent.png" decoding="async" width="200" height="96" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Aureus_Valerian-RIC_0034-transparent.png/300px-Aureus_Valerian-RIC_0034-transparent.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Aureus_Valerian-RIC_0034-transparent.png/400px-Aureus_Valerian-RIC_0034-transparent.png 2x" data-file-width="416" data-file-height="200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aureus_Valerian-RIC_0034-transparent.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aureus of emperor Valerian.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:GateOfAugustus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/GateOfAugustus.jpg/250px-GateOfAugustus.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/GateOfAugustus.jpg/375px-GateOfAugustus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/GateOfAugustus.jpg/500px-GateOfAugustus.jpg 2x" data-file-width="819" data-file-height="544" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:GateOfAugustus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Gate of <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ephesus" title="Ephesus">Ephesus</a>. <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> was built to honor the Emperor Augustus and his family.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Biblioteca_de_Celso_-_02.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Biblioteca_de_Celso_-_02.jpg/250px-Biblioteca_de_Celso_-_02.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Biblioteca_de_Celso_-_02.jpg/375px-Biblioteca_de_Celso_-_02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Biblioteca_de_Celso_-_02.jpg/500px-Biblioteca_de_Celso_-_02.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3630" data-file-height="2723" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Biblioteca_de_Celso_-_02.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Library of Celsus, <a href="/wiki/Ephesus" title="Ephesus">Ephesus</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>From the rule of <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a> onwards until that of <a href="/wiki/Constantine_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Constantine I">Constantine I</a>, Anatolia enjoyed relative peace that allowed itself to grow as a region. The emperor <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a> removed all debts owed to the Roman Empire by the provinces and protectorates there, making advanced progress possible. Roads were built to connect the larger cities in order to improve trade and transportation, and the abundance of high outputs in agricultural pursuits made more money for everyone involved. Settlement was encouraged, and local governors did not place a heavy burden upon the people with regards to taxation. The wealth gained from the peace and prosperity prevented great tragedy as powerful earthquakes tore through the region, and help was given from the Roman government and other parties. Through it all was produced some of the most respected scientific men of that period- the philosopher <a href="/wiki/Dio_Chrysostom" title="Dio Chrysostom">Dio</a> of Bithynia, the medical mind of <a href="/wiki/Galen" title="Galen">Galen</a> from <a href="/wiki/Pergamon" title="Pergamon">Pergamon</a>, and the historians <a href="/wiki/Memnon_of_Heraclea" title="Memnon of Heraclea">Memnon of Heraclea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cassius_Dio" title="Cassius Dio">Cassius Dio</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nicaea_(city)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nicaea (city)">Nicaea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By the middle of the 3rd century, everything that had been built by peace was being threatened by a new enemy, the Goths. As the inroads to central Europe through Macedonia, Italy, and Germania were all defended successfully by the Romans, the Goths found Anatolia to be irresistible due to its wealth and deteriorating defenses. Using a captured fleet of ships from the Bosphorus and flat-bottomed boats to cross the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>, they sailed in 256 around the eastern shores, landing in the coastal city of <a href="/wiki/Trabzon" title="Trabzon">Trebizond</a>. What ensued was a huge embarrassment for <a href="/wiki/Pontus_(region)" title="Pontus (region)">Pontus</a> — the wealth of the city was absconded, a larger number of ships were confiscated, and they entered the interior without much to turn them back. A second invasion of Anatolia through <a href="/wiki/Bithynia" title="Bithynia">Bithynia</a> brought even more terror inland and wanton destruction. The Goths entered <a href="/wiki/Chalcedon" title="Chalcedon">Chalcedon</a> and used it as a base by which to expand their operations, sacking <a href="/wiki/Nicomedia" title="Nicomedia">Nicomedia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cius" title="Cius">Prusa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Apamea_Myrlea" title="Apamea Myrlea">Apamea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cius" title="Cius">Cius</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nice" title="Nice">Nice</a> in turn. Only the turn of the weather during a fall season kept them from doing any more harm to those outside the realm of the province. The Goths managed a third attack upon not only the coastline of western Anatolia, but in Greece and Italy as well. Despite the Romans under their emperor <a href="/wiki/Valerian_(emperor)" title="Valerian (emperor)">Valerian</a> finally turning them away, it did not stop the Goths from first destroying the Temple of Diana in <a href="/wiki/Ephesus" title="Ephesus">Ephesus</a> and the city itself in 263.<sup id="cite_ref-Gibbon_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbon-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Byzantine_Anatolia">Byzantine Anatolia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Byzantine Anatolia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Anatolia" title="Byzantine Anatolia">Byzantine Anatolia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Creation_of_the_Byzantine_Empire">Creation of the Byzantine Empire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Creation of the Byzantine Empire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire#Early_history" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire §&#160;Early history</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NicaeaConstantine.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/NicaeaConstantine.jpg/200px-NicaeaConstantine.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="260" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/68/NicaeaConstantine.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="288" data-file-height="375" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NicaeaConstantine.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An icon representing Constantine as a saint and others in <a href="/wiki/Nicaea_(city)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nicaea (city)">Nicaea</a> in 325, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Nicaean_Creed" class="mw-redirect" title="Nicaean Creed">Nicaean Creed</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:COUNCIL_OF_NICEA_Fresco_in_the_Sistine_Salon_Vatican_t.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/COUNCIL_OF_NICEA_Fresco_in_the_Sistine_Salon_Vatican_t.jpg/250px-COUNCIL_OF_NICEA_Fresco_in_the_Sistine_Salon_Vatican_t.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="111" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/COUNCIL_OF_NICEA_Fresco_in_the_Sistine_Salon_Vatican_t.jpg/375px-COUNCIL_OF_NICEA_Fresco_in_the_Sistine_Salon_Vatican_t.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/COUNCIL_OF_NICEA_Fresco_in_the_Sistine_Salon_Vatican_t.jpg/500px-COUNCIL_OF_NICEA_Fresco_in_the_Sistine_Salon_Vatican_t.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="356" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:COUNCIL_OF_NICEA_Fresco_in_the_Sistine_Salon_Vatican_t.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fresco depicting the First Council of Nicaea.</div></div></div>
<p>The constant instability of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> as a whole gradually made it more and more difficult to control. Upon the ascension of the emperor Constantine in 330, he made a bold decision by removing himself from Rome and into a new capital. Located in the old city of <a href="/wiki/Byzantium" title="Byzantium">Byzantium</a>, now known as <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> after the emperor, it was strengthened and improved in order to assure more than adequate defense of the whole region. What added to the prestige of the city was Constantine's favor of <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>. He allowed bishops and other religious figures to aid in the government of the empire, and he personally intervened in the <a href="/wiki/First_Council_of_Nicaea" title="First Council of Nicaea">First Council of Nicaea</a> to prove his sincerity.
</p><p>The next forty years after the death of Constantine in 337 saw a power struggle amongst his descendants for control of the empire. His three sons, <a href="/wiki/Constantine_II_(emperor)" title="Constantine II (emperor)">Constantine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Constans" title="Constans">Constans</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Constantius_II" title="Constantius II">Constantius</a> were unable to coexist peacefully under a joint rule, and they eventually resorted to violent means to end the arrangement. A short time after taking power, a purge of a majority of their relations began and the blood of Constantine's progeny flowed. Eventually Constans came after and killed Constantine II near <a href="/wiki/Aquileia" title="Aquileia">Aquileia</a>, but was soon removed and himself murdered by his own army. This left Constantius II as the sole emperor of the Byzantines, but even this would not last. Despite supporting his cousin <a href="/wiki/Julian_the_Apostate" class="mw-redirect" title="Julian the Apostate">Julian</a> as commander of the armies in <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a>, events soon forced Julian to ignore Constantine's orders to move eastward with his armies and to head straight for Constantinople to claim the imperial purple. The death of Constantius II in <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(city)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tarsus (city)">Tarsus</a> resulted in a bloodless transfer of power in 361. Julian did not survive but a scant year and a half thanks to a Persian spear, but during that time he tried to revert what progress <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> had made after the founding of the empire. Even on his deathbed he was supposed to have said <i>"Thou hast conquered, Galilean."</i>, a reference to Christianity besting him.<sup id="cite_ref-Norwich_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Norwich-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The threat of barbarian invasion and its effects upon the Roman Empire in the west carried over into the east. After a short rule by the emperor <a href="/wiki/Jovian_(Emperor)" class="mw-redirect" title="Jovian (Emperor)">Jovian</a> and a joint rule of both empires by <a href="/wiki/Valentinian_II" title="Valentinian II">Valentinian II</a> in the west and <a href="/wiki/Valens" title="Valens">Valens</a> in the east, the young emperor <a href="/wiki/Gratian" title="Gratian">Gratian</a> made what was to be a very fortunate decision. He chose the favored general <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_I" title="Theodosius I">Theodosius I</a> to rule with his as a co-emperor, granting him authority over all of the domains of the Byzantine empire in 379. This proved to be a wise decision with regards to the survival of his newly obtained dominion, for he immediately set about healing the religious rifts that had emerged during the insecurity of past years. The practice of <a href="/wiki/Arianism" title="Arianism">Arianism</a> and pagan rites were abolished, and the standards set by <a href="/wiki/Constantine_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Constantine I">Constantine</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nicaea_(city)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nicaea (city)">Nicaea</a> were restored by law. By 395, the year in which the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> was officially divided in half and the aptly named <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_the_Great" class="mw-redirect" title="Theodosius the Great">Theodosius the Great</a> died, the east was so strong that it could now be considered an equal.<sup id="cite_ref-Gibbon2_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbon2-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Byzantine_Empire">The Byzantine Empire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: The Byzantine Empire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bizansist_touchup.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Bizansist_touchup.jpg/250px-Bizansist_touchup.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="96" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Bizansist_touchup.jpg/375px-Bizansist_touchup.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Bizansist_touchup.jpg/500px-Bizansist_touchup.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2516" data-file-height="970" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bizansist_touchup.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> in Byzantine times</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aya_sofya.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Aya_sofya.jpg/250px-Aya_sofya.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="168" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Aya_sofya.jpg/375px-Aya_sofya.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Aya_sofya.jpg/500px-Aya_sofya.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="688" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aya_sofya.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>As a symbol and expression of the universal prestige of the Patriarchate of <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>, <a href="/wiki/Justinian_I" title="Justinian I">Justinian I</a> built the Church of the Holy Wisdom of God, <a href="/wiki/Hagia_Sophia" title="Hagia Sophia">Hagia Sophia</a>, which was completed in the short period of four and a half years (532–537).</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Justinian_I" title="Justinian I">Justinian I</a></div>
<p>The Byzantine Empire was the predominantly Greek-speaking continuation of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> during <a href="/wiki/Late_Antiquity" class="mw-redirect" title="Late Antiquity">Late Antiquity</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. Its capital city was <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> (modern-day <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>), originally known as <a href="/wiki/Byzantium" title="Byzantium">Byzantium</a>. Initially the eastern half of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> (often called the Eastern Roman Empire in this context), it survived the 5th century fragmentation and <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_the_Western_Roman_Empire" title="Fall of the Western Roman Empire">fall of the Western Roman Empire</a> and continued to exist for an additional thousand years until it fell to the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Turks" title="Ottoman Turks">Ottoman Turks</a> in 1453.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Persian_intervention">Persian intervention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Persian intervention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire_under_the_Heraclian_dynasty" title="Byzantine Empire under the Heraclian dynasty">Byzantine Empire under the Heraclian dynasty</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sasanian_Empire" title="Sasanian Empire">Sasanian Empire</a></div>
<p>The Sassanid Persians, after having fought centuries of wars against the Byzantines and at their peak sieged <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Constantinople_(626)" title="Siege of Constantinople (626)">Constantinople</a> together with the Avars, paved the way for a new threat to enter onto the scene; the Arabs.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arab_conquests_and_threats">Arab conquests and threats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Arab conquests and threats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Arab%E2%80%93Byzantine_Wars" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab–Byzantine Wars">Arab–Byzantine Wars</a></div>
<p>Arab attacks throughout the empire reduced significantly the territory once held under <a href="/wiki/Justinian" class="mw-redirect" title="Justinian">Justinian</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Crusades_and_their_effects">The Crusades and their effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: The Crusades and their effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire#Komnenian_dynasty_and_the_Crusades" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire §&#160;Komnenian dynasty and the Crusades</a></div>
<p>The four crusades that involved the Byzantines severely weakened their power, and led to a disunity that would never be successfully restored.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breakaway_successor_states_and_the_fall">Breakaway successor states and the fall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Breakaway successor states and the fall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The newly forming states of the Turks gradually squeezed the empire so much that it was only a matter of time before Constantinople was taken in 1453.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Seljuks_and_Anatolian_beyliks">The Seljuks and Anatolian beyliks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: The Seljuks and Anatolian beyliks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Turkey" title="History of Turkey">History of Turkey</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Seljuq_dynasty" title="Seljuq dynasty">Seljuq dynasty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seljuk_Empire" title="Seljuk Empire">Seljuk Empire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sultanate_of_R%C3%BBm" class="mw-redirect" title="Sultanate of Rûm">Sultanate of Rûm</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anatolian_beyliks" title="Anatolian beyliks">Anatolian beyliks</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Turkic_migration" title="Turkic migration">Turkic migration</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Turkic_peoples" class="mw-redirect" title="History of the Turkic peoples">History of the Turkic peoples</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Europe_map_1092.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Europe_map_1092.PNG/250px-Europe_map_1092.PNG" decoding="async" width="250" height="183" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Europe_map_1092.PNG/375px-Europe_map_1092.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Europe_map_1092.PNG/500px-Europe_map_1092.PNG 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="585" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Europe_map_1092.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Great Seljuk Empire (dark green) and neighboring states, circa 1090</div></div></div>
<p>Before the Turkic settlement, the local population of <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a> had reached an estimated level of 12 to 14 million people during the late Roman Period.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Turkic_migration" title="Turkic migration">migration</a> of Turks to the country of modern Turkey occurred during the main <a href="/wiki/Turkic_migration" title="Turkic migration">Turkic migration</a> across most of <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> and into <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> which was between the 6th and 11th centuries. Mainly Turkic people living in the <a href="/wiki/Seljuk_Empire" title="Seljuk Empire">Seljuk Empire</a> arrived in Turkey during the eleventh century. The Seljuks proceeded to gradually conquer the Anatolian part of the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:131_Bataille_de_Malazgirt.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/131_Bataille_de_Malazgirt.jpg/200px-131_Bataille_de_Malazgirt.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="212" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/131_Bataille_de_Malazgirt.jpg/300px-131_Bataille_de_Malazgirt.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/131_Bataille_de_Malazgirt.jpg/400px-131_Bataille_de_Malazgirt.jpg 2x" data-file-width="415" data-file-height="439" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:131_Bataille_de_Malazgirt.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Manzikert" title="Battle of Manzikert">Battle of Manzikert</a></div></div></div>
<p>The <i>House of Seljuk</i> was a branch of the <i>Kınık</i> <a href="/wiki/Oghuz_Turks" title="Oghuz Turks">Oğuz Turks</a> who resided on the periphery of the <a href="/wiki/Muslim_history#Abbasids_-_&quot;Islamic_Golden_Age&quot;" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim history">Muslim world</a>, north of the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aral_Sea" title="Aral Sea">Aral Seas</a> in the Yabghu <a href="/wiki/Khagan" title="Khagan">Khaganate</a> of the Oğuz confederacy<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> in the 10th century. In the 11th century, the Turkic people living in the Seljuk Empire started migrating from their ancestral homelands towards east of Anatolia, which eventually became a new homeland of Oğuz Turkic tribes following the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Manzikert" title="Battle of Manzikert">Battle of Manzikert</a> on August 26, 1071.
</p><p>The victory of the Seljuks gave rise to the <a href="/wiki/Seljuk_Sultanate_of_Rum" class="mw-redirect" title="Seljuk Sultanate of Rum">Seljuk Sultanate of Rum</a>, a separate branch of the larger <a href="/wiki/Seljuk_Empire" title="Seljuk Empire">Seljuk Empire</a><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> and to some Turkish principalities (beyliks), mostly situated towards the east which were vassals of or at war with Seljuk Sultanate of Rum.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mongol_invasion">Mongol invasion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Anatolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Mongol invasion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Mongol_conquest_of_Anatolia" class="mw-redirect" title="Mongol conquest of Anatolia">Mongol conquest of Anatolia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ilkhanate" title="Ilkhanate">Ilkhanate</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:IlkhanidHorseArcher.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/IlkhanidHorseArcher.jpg/250px-IlkhanidHorseArcher.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="194" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/IlkhanidHorseArcher.jpg/375px-IlkhanidHorseArcher.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b5/IlkhanidHorseArcher.jpg 2x" data-file-width="450" data-file-height="350" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:IlkhanidHorseArcher.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Ilkanid horse archer in the 13th century</div></div></div>
<p>On June 26, 1243, the Seljuk armies were defeated by the <a href="/wiki/Mongol_Empire" title="Mongol Empire">Mongols</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kosedag" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Kosedag">Battle of Kosedag</a>, and the Seljuk Sultanate of Rûm became a vassal of the Mongols.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> This caused the Seljuks to lose their power. <a href="/wiki/Hulegu_Khan" class="mw-redirect" title="Hulegu Khan">Hulegu Khan</a>, grandson of <a href="/wiki/Genghis_Khan" title="Genghis Khan">Genghis Khan</a> founded the Ilkhanate in the southwestern part of the Mongol Empire. The Ilkhanate ruled Anatolia through Mongol military governors. The last Seljuk sultan <a href="/wiki/Mesud_II" title="Mesud II">Mesud II</a>, died in 1308. The Mongol invasion of Transoxiana, Iran, Azerbaijan and Anatolia caused Turkomens to move to Western Anatolia.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The Turkomens founded some Anatolian principalities (beyliks) under the Mongol dominion in Turkey.<sup id="cite_ref-beyliks_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beyliks-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The most powerful beyliks were the <a href="/wiki/Karamanids" title="Karamanids">Karamanids</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Germiyanids" title="Germiyanids">Germiyanids</a> in the central area. Along the Aegean coast, from north to south, stretched <a href="/wiki/Karasids" title="Karasids">Karasids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sarukhanids" title="Sarukhanids">Sarukhanids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aydinids" class="mw-redirect" title="Aydinids">Aydinids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mente%C5%9Fe_(beylik)" class="mw-redirect" title="Menteşe (beylik)">Menteşe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beylik_of_Teke" title="Beylik of Teke">Teke</a> principalities. The <a href="/wiki/Jandarids" class="mw-redirect" title="Jandarids">Jandarids</a> (later called <a href="/wiki/Isfendiyarids" title="Isfendiyarids">Isfendiyarids</a>) controlled the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> region round <a href="/wiki/Kastamonu" title="Kastamonu">Kastamonu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sinop,_Turkey" title="Sinop, Turkey">Sinop</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> The Beylik of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a> Dynasty was situated in the northwest of Anatolia, around <a href="/wiki/S%C3%B6%C4%9F%C3%BCt" title="Söğüt">Söğüt</a>, and it was a small and insignificant state at that time. The Ottoman beylik would, however, evolve into the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> over the next 200 years, expanding throughout the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ottomans_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ottomans-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
