<p><b>Agape</b> (<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <span lang="grc"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%80%CE%B3%CE%AC%CF%80%CE%B7#Ancient_Greek" class="extiw" title="wikt:ἀγάπη">ἀγάπη</a></span>, <i>agapē</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greco</a>-<a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> term referring to love, "the highest form of love, charity" and "the love of God for man and of man for God".<sup id="cite_ref-LiddellScott2010_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LiddellScott2010-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The word is not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Philia" title="Philia">philia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brotherly_love_(philosophy)" title="Brotherly love (philosophy)">brotherly love</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Philautia" class="mw-redirect" title="Philautia">philautia</a>, self-love, as it embraces a universal, unconditional love that transcends and persists regardless of circumstance. It goes beyond just the emotions to the extent of seeking the best for others. The noun form first occurs in the <a href="/wiki/Septuagint" title="Septuagint">Septuagint</a>, but the verb form goes as far back as <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>, translated literally as <a href="/wiki/Affection" title="Affection">affection</a>, as in "greet with affection" and "show affection for the dead".<sup id="cite_ref-LiddellScott1871_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LiddellScott1871-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Other ancient authors have used forms of the word to denote love of a spouse or family, or affection for a particular activity, in contrast to <a href="/wiki/Eros_(concept)" title="Eros (concept)">eros</a> (an affection of a sexual nature).
</p><p>Within Christianity, <i>agape</i> is considered to be the <a href="/wiki/Love_of_God" title="Love of God">love originating from God</a> or <a href="/wiki/Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Christ">Christ</a> for humankind <sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>, it refers to the covenant love of God for humans, as well as the human reciprocal love for God; the term necessarily extends to the love of one's fellow man.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Some contemporary writers have sought to extend the use of <i>agape</i> into non-religious contexts.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The concept of <i>agape</i> has been widely examined within its Christian context.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> It has also been considered in the contexts of other religions,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Religious_ethics" class="mw-redirect" title="Religious ethics">religious ethics</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and science.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_uses">Early uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are few instances of the word agape in polytheistic Greek literature. <a href="/wiki/Bauer%27s_Lexicon" title="Bauer&#39;s Lexicon">Bauer's Lexicon</a> mentions a sepulchral inscription, most likely to honor a polytheistic army officer held in "high esteem" by his country.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Christianity">Christianity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Christianity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Agape_feast_03.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Agape_feast_03.jpg/220px-Agape_feast_03.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Agape_feast_03.jpg/330px-Agape_feast_03.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Agape_feast_03.jpg/440px-Agape_feast_03.jpg 2x" data-file-width="770" data-file-height="555" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Agape_feast_03.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">Fresco</a> of a female figure holding a chalice at an early Christian <a href="/wiki/Agape_feast" title="Agape feast">Agape feast</a>. <a href="/wiki/Catacombs_of_Marcellinus_and_Peter" title="Catacombs of Marcellinus and Peter">Catacomb</a> of Saints <a href="/wiki/Marcellinus_and_Peter" title="Marcellinus and Peter">Marcellinus and Peter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Via_Labicana" title="Via Labicana">Via Labicana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a></div></div></div><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/1_Corinthians_13" title="1 Corinthians 13">1 Corinthians 13</a></div>
<p>A journalist in <a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)"><i>Time</i> magazine</a>  describes <a href="/wiki/John_3:16" title="John 3:16">John 3:16</a> as "one of the most famous and well-known Bible verses. It has been called the 'Gospel in a nutshell' because it is considered a summary of the central doctrines of <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>."
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>For God so loved the world, that he gave his only begotten Son, that whosoever believeth in him should not perish, but have everlasting life.</p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8201;<cite>John 3:16, <i><a href="/wiki/King_James_Version" title="King James Version">KJV</a></i></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>The word <i>agape</i> received a broader usage under later Christian writers as the word that specifically denoted Christian love or <a href="/wiki/Charity_(virtue)" title="Charity (virtue)">charity</a> (<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/1_Corinthians#13:1" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/1 Corinthians">1 Corinthians 13:1–8</a>), or even <a href="/wiki/God_in_Christianity" title="God in Christianity">God</a> himself. The expression "God is love" (ὁ θεὸς ἀγάπη ἐστίν) occurs twice in the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>: <a href="/wiki/First_Epistle_of_John" title="First Epistle of John">1 John</a> <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=1+John+4%3A8%2C16&amp;version=KJV%3BSBLGNT">4:8,16</a></span>. <i>Agape</i> was also used by the early Christians to refer to the self-sacrificing love of God for humanity, which they were committed to reciprocating and practicing towards God and among one another (see <i><a href="/wiki/Kenosis" title="Kenosis">kenosis</a></i>).
</p><p><i>Agape</i> has been expounded on by many Christian writers in a specifically Christian context. C. S. Lewis uses <i>agape</i> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Four_Loves" title="The Four Loves">The Four Loves</a></i> to describe what he believes is the highest level of love known to humanity: a selfless love that is passionately committed to the well-being of others.<sup id="cite_ref-Kreeft_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kreeft-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Christian use of the term comes directly from the canonical <a href="/wiki/Gospel" title="Gospel">Gospels</a>' accounts of the teachings of <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a>. When asked what was the <a href="/wiki/Great_Commandment" title="Great Commandment">great commandment</a>, "Jesus said unto him, Thou shalt love the Lord thy God with all thy heart, and with all thy soul, and with all thy mind. This is the first and great commandment. And the second <i>is</i> like unto it, Thou shalt love thy neighbour as thyself. On these two commandments hang all the law and the prophets." (<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Matthew#22:37" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Matthew">Matthew 22:37-40</a>) In <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a>, the first "love the L<span class="smallcaps" style="font-size:83%">ORD</span> thy God" is part of the <a href="/wiki/Shema_Yisrael" title="Shema Yisrael">Shema</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bloch-SermonOnTheMount.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Bloch-SermonOnTheMount.jpg/280px-Bloch-SermonOnTheMount.jpg" decoding="async" width="280" height="314" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Bloch-SermonOnTheMount.jpg/420px-Bloch-SermonOnTheMount.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Bloch-SermonOnTheMount.jpg/560px-Bloch-SermonOnTheMount.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1377" data-file-height="1545" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bloch-SermonOnTheMount.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>The Sermon on the Mount</i>, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Bloch" title="Carl Bloch">Carl Bloch</a>, 1877</div></div></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Sermon_on_the_Mount" title="Sermon on the Mount">Sermon on the Mount</a>, Jesus said:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>You have heard that it was said, 'You shall love (<i>agapēseis</i>) your neighbor and hate your enemy.' But I say to you, Love (<i>agapāte</i>) your enemies and pray for those who persecute you, so that you may be sons of your Father who is in heaven; for he makes his sun rise on the evil and on the good, and sends rain on the just and on the unjust. For if you love those who love you, what reward have you?</p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8201;<cite>Matthew 5:43-46, <a href="/wiki/Revised_Standard_Version" title="Revised Standard Version">RSV</a></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tertullian" title="Tertullian">Tertullian</a> remarks in his 2nd century defense of Christians that Christian love attracted pagan notice: "What marks us in the eyes of our enemies is our loving kindness. 'Only look,' they say, 'look how they love one another' " (<i><a href="/wiki/Apologetics" title="Apologetics">Apology</a></i> 39).
</p><p>Anglican theologian <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Chase_Quick" title="Oliver Chase Quick">O.C. Quick</a> writes that this <i>agape</i> within human experience is "a very partial and rudimentary realization," and that "in its pure form it is essentially divine." 
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>If we could imagine the love of one who loves men purely for their own sake, and not because of any need or desire of his own, purely desires their good, and yet loves them wholly, not for what at this moment they are, but for what he knows he can make of them because he made them, then we should have in our minds some true image of the love of the Father and Creator of mankind.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>, the word <i>agape</i> is often used to describe God's love. However, other forms of the word are used in a negative context, such as the various forms of the verb <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%80%CE%B3%CE%B1%CF%80%CE%AC%CF%89" class="extiw" title="wikt:ἀγαπάω">agapaō</a></i>. Examples include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/2_Timothy#4:10" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/2 Timothy">2 Timothy 4:10</a>— "for Demas hath forsaken me, having loved [<i>agapēsas</i>] this present world...".</li>
<li><a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/John#12:43" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/John">John 12:43</a>— "For they loved [<i>ēgapēsan</i>] the praise of men more than the praise of God."</li>
<li><a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/John#3:19" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/John">John 3:19</a>— "And this is the condemnation, that light is come into the world, and men loved [<i>ēgapēsan</i>] darkness rather than light, because their deeds were evil."</li></ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Karl_Barth" title="Karl Barth">Karl Barth</a> distinguishes <i>agape</i> from <i><a href="/wiki/Eros_(concept)" title="Eros (concept)">eros</a></i> on the basis of its origin and unconditional character. In <i>agape</i>, humanity does not merely express its nature, but transcends it. <i>Agape</i> identifies with the interests of the neighbor "in utter independence of the question of his attractiveness" and with no expectation of reciprocity.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Meal">Meal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Meal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Agape_feast" title="Agape feast">Agape feast</a></div>
<p>The word <i>agape</i> is used in its plural form (<i>agapai</i>) in the New Testament to describe a meal or feast eaten by early Christians, as in <a href="/wiki/Epistle_of_Jude" title="Epistle of Jude">Jude</a> <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Jude#1:1" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Jude">1:12</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second_Epistle_of_Peter" title="Second Epistle of Peter">2nd Peter</a> <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/2_Peter#2:13" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/2 Peter">2:13</a>.
</p>
