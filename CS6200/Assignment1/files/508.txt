<p><b>Mother's Day in the United States</b> is an annual holiday celebrated on the second Sunday in May. Mother's Day recognizes mothers, motherhood and maternal bonds in general, as well as the positive contributions that they make to society. It was established by Anna Jarvis, with the first official Mother's Day celebrated at St. Andrew's Methodist Church in <a href="/wiki/Grafton,_West_Virginia" title="Grafton, West Virginia">Grafton, West Virginia</a>, on May 10, 1908.<sup id="cite_ref-O&#39;Reilly2010_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-O&#39;Reilly2010-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In the United States, Mother's Day complements similar celebrations honoring family members, such as <a href="/wiki/Father%27s_Day_(United_States)" title="Father&#39;s Day (United States)">Father's Day</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siblings_Day" title="Siblings Day">Siblings Day</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grandparents_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="Grandparents Day">Grandparents Day</a>.
</p><p>Internationally, there are a large variety of Mother's Day celebrations with different origins and traditions, some now also having been influenced by this more recent American tradition. For the international celebration, see <a href="/wiki/Mother%27s_Day" title="Mother&#39;s Day">Mother's Day</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mother%27s_Day_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_attempts_to_establish_a_holiday">First attempts to establish a holiday</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mother%27s_Day_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: First attempts to establish a holiday">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the 19th century, women's peace groups in the United States tried to establish holidays and regular activities in favor of peace and against war. A common early activity was the meeting of groups of mothers whose sons had fought or died on opposite sides of the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1868, <a href="/wiki/Ann_Jarvis" title="Ann Jarvis">Ann Jarvis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anna_Jarvis" title="Anna Jarvis">Anna Jarvis</a>'s mother, organised a committee to establish a "Mother's Friendship Day", the purpose of which was "to reunite families that had been divided during the Civil War." Ann Jarvis, who had previously organized Mother's Day Work Clubs to improve sanitation and health for both Union and Confederate encampments undergoing a typhoid outbreak, wanted to expand these into an annual memorial for mothers, but she died in 1905 before the annual celebration was established.<sup id="cite_ref-virgina_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-virgina-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Her daughter, who became almost obsessed with her, would continue her mother's efforts.
</p><p>There were several limited observances in the 1870s and the 1880s but none achieved resonance beyond the local level.<sup id="cite_ref-virgina_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-virgina-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> At the time, Protestant schools in the United States already held many celebrations and observations such as <a href="/wiki/Children%27s_Day" title="Children&#39;s Day">Children's Day</a>, Temperance Sunday, Roll Call Day, Decision Day, <a href="/wiki/Missionary_Day" title="Missionary Day">Missionary Day</a> and others.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In New York City, <a href="/wiki/Julia_Ward_Howe" title="Julia Ward Howe">Julia Ward Howe</a> led a "Mother's Day for Peace" anti-war observance on June 2, 1872,<sup id="cite_ref-virgina_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-virgina-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Leigh,_p._252_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leigh,_p._252-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> which was accompanied by a "Appeal to womanhood throughout the world" (nowadays known as <a href="/wiki/Mother%27s_Day_Proclamation" title="Mother&#39;s Day Proclamation">Mother's Day Proclamation</a>). The observance continued in Boston for about 10 years under Howe's personal sponsorship, then died out.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In these celebrations, mothers all around the world would work towards world peace.<sup id="cite_ref-Leigh,_p._252_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leigh,_p._252-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Several years later a Mother's Day observance on May 13, 1877 was held in <a href="/wiki/Albion,_Michigan" title="Albion, Michigan">Albion, Michigan</a> over a dispute related to the <a href="/wiki/Temperance_movement" title="Temperance movement">temperance movement</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> According to local legend, Albion pioneer Juliet Calhoun Blakeley stepped up to complete the sermon of the Rev. Myron Daughterty who was distraught because an anti-temperance group had forced his son and two other <a href="/wiki/Temperance_movement" title="Temperance movement">temperance</a> advocates at gunpoint to spend the night in a saloon and <a href="/wiki/Involuntary_intoxication" class="mw-redirect" title="Involuntary intoxication">become publicly drunk</a>. From the pulpit Blakeley called on other mothers to join her. Blakeley's two sons, both traveling salesmen, were so moved that they vowed to return each year to pay tribute to her and embarked on a campaign to urge their business contacts to do likewise. At their urging, in the early 1880s, the <a href="/wiki/Methodist_Episcopal_Church" title="Methodist Episcopal Church">Methodist Episcopal Church</a> in Albion set aside the second Sunday in May to recognize the special contributions of mothers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Frank_E._Hering" title="Frank E. Hering">Frank E. Hering</a>, alumnus and administrator at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Notre_Dame" title="University of Notre Dame">University of Notre Dame</a> and President of the <a href="/wiki/Fraternal_Order_of_Eagles" title="Fraternal Order of Eagles">Fraternal Order of Eagles</a>, made a plea for "setting aside one day in the year as a nationwide memorial to the memories of Mothers and motherhood" in 1904.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> After observing a class of Notre Dame students sending home <a href="/wiki/Postcard" title="Postcard">penny postcards</a> to their mothers, Hering went on to be a vocal advocate for a national Mother's Day for the next decade.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> As Hering stated in a 1941 issue of <i><a href="/wiki/Scholastic_(Notre_Dame_publication)" title="Scholastic (Notre Dame publication)">Scholastic</a></i>: "Throughout history the great men of the world have given their credit for their achievements to their mothers.  [The] Holy Church recognizes this, as does Notre Dame especially, and Our Lady who watches over our great institution."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Establishment_of_holiday">Establishment of holiday</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mother%27s_Day_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Establishment of holiday">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In its present form, Mother's Day was established by Anna Jarvis with the help of Philadelphia merchant <a href="/wiki/John_Wanamaker" title="John Wanamaker">John Wanamaker</a> following the death of her mother, Ann Jarvis, on May 9, 1905. Jarvis never mentioned Howe or <a href="/wiki/Mothering_Sunday" title="Mothering Sunday">Mothering Sunday</a>, and she never mentioned any connection to the Protestant school celebrations, always claiming that the creation of Mother's Day was hers alone.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A small service was held on May 12, 1907 in the Andrew's Methodist Episcopal Church in <a href="/wiki/Grafton,_West_Virginia" title="Grafton, West Virginia">Grafton, West Virginia</a>, where Anna's mother had been teaching <a href="/wiki/Sunday_school" title="Sunday school">Sunday school</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-virgina_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-virgina-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The first "official" service was on May 10, 1908, in the same church, accompanied by a larger ceremony in the Wanamaker Auditorium in the <a href="/wiki/Wanamaker%27s" title="Wanamaker&#39;s">Wanamaker's</a> store in Philadelphia.<sup id="cite_ref-virgina_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-virgina-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The next year the day was reported to be widely celebrated in New York.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Jarvis then campaigned to establish Mother's Day first as a U.S. national holiday and then later as an international holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The holiday was declared officially by the state of West Virginia in 1910, and the rest of the states followed quickly.<sup id="cite_ref-virgina_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-virgina-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On May 10, 1913, the <a href="/wiki/U.S._House_of_Representatives" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. House of Representatives">U.S. House of Representatives</a> passed a resolution calling on all federal government officials (from the president down) to wear a white carnation the following day in observance of Mother's Day.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> On May 8, 1914, the U.S. Congress passed a law designating the second Sunday in May as Mother's Day and requesting a proclamation. The next day, President <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">Woodrow Wilson</a> issued a proclamation declaring the first national Mother's Day<sup id="cite_ref-rice_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rice-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9may_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9may-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> as a day for American citizens to show the flag in honor of those mothers whose sons had died in war.<sup id="cite_ref-rice_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rice-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>  In 1934, U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> approved a stamp commemorating the holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2008, the <a href="/wiki/U.S._House_of_Representatives" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. House of Representatives">U.S. House of Representatives</a> voted twice on a resolution commemorating Mother's Day,<sup id="cite_ref-vote274_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vote274-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-vote275_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vote275-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> the first one being passed without a dissenting vote (21 members not voting).<sup id="cite_ref-vote274_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vote274-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>  The Grafton church, where the first celebration was held, is now the <a href="/wiki/International_Mother%27s_Day_Shrine" title="International Mother&#39;s Day Shrine">International Mother's Day Shrine</a> and is a <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Landmark" title="National Historic Landmark">National Historic Landmark</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nps_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nps-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spelling">Spelling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mother%27s_Day_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Spelling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1912 Anna Jarvis trademarked the phrases "Second Sunday in May" and "Mother's Day", and created the Mother's Day International Association.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> She specifically noted that "Mother's" should "be a singular possessive, for each family to honor its own mother, not a plural possessive commemorating all mothers in the world."<sup id="cite_ref-Taylor-vancouversun_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor-vancouversun-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> This is also the spelling used by U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">Woodrow Wilson</a> in his 1914 presidential proclamation, by the U.S. Congress in relevant bills,<sup id="cite_ref-vote274_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vote274-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-vote275_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vote275-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> and by various U.S. presidents in their proclamations concerning Mother's Day.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditions">Traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mother%27s_Day_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Traditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Traditions on Mother's Day include <a href="/wiki/Church_service" title="Church service">churchgoing</a>, the distribution of <a href="/wiki/Carnations" class="mw-redirect" title="Carnations">carnations</a>, and family dinners.<sup id="cite_ref-Churchgoing_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Churchgoing-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Mother’s Day is the third largest holiday in the United States for sending cards. According to the greeting card industry, it is estimated that more than 50 percent of American households send greeting cards on this holiday. The holiday has been heavily commercialized by advertisers and retailers, and has been criticized by some as a <a href="/wiki/Hallmark_Holiday" class="mw-redirect" title="Hallmark Holiday">Hallmark Holiday</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carnations">Carnations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mother%27s_Day_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Carnations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dianthus_caryophyllus" title="Dianthus caryophyllus">Carnations</a> have come to represent Mother's Day since Anna Jarvis delivered 500 of them at the first celebration in 1908.<sup id="cite_ref-virgina_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-virgina-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9may_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9may-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nps_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nps-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Many religious services held later adopted the custom of giving away carnations.<sup id="cite_ref-virgina_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-virgina-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> This also started the custom of wearing a carnation on Mother's Day. The founder, Anna Jarvis, chose the carnation because it was the favorite flower of her mother.<sup id="cite_ref-leigh_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leigh-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> In part due to the shortage of white carnations, and in part due to the efforts to expand the sales of more types of flowers in Mother's Day, florists invented the idea of wearing a pink carnation if your mother was living, or a white one if she was dead; this was tirelessly promoted until it made its way into the popular observations at churches.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Other less traditional flower options may include roses, a live blooming plant, flower leis, or a bouquet of a variety of different flowers.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercialization">Commercialization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mother%27s_Day_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Commercialization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The commercialization of the American holiday began very early, and only nine years after the first official Mother's Day it had become so rampant that Anna Jarvis herself became a major opponent of what the holiday had become,<sup id="cite_ref-Taylor-vancouversun_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor-vancouversun-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-msnbc_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-msnbc-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> spending all her inheritance and the rest of her life fighting what she saw as an abuse of the celebration.<sup id="cite_ref-Taylor-vancouversun_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor-vancouversun-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> She decried the practice of purchasing greeting cards, which she saw as a sign of being too lazy to write a personal letter. She was arrested in 1948 for disturbing the peace while protesting against the commercialization of Mother's Day, and she finally said that she "...wished she would have never started the day because it became so out of control ..."<sup id="cite_ref-msnbc_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-msnbc-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> She died later that year.
</p><p>Mother's Day is now one of the most commercially successful American occasions, having become the most popular day of the year to dine out at a restaurant in the United States<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> and generating a significant portion of the U.S. jewelry industry's annual revenue, from custom gifts like mother's rings.<sup id="cite_ref-Helzberg_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Helzberg-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Americans spend approximately $2.6 billion on flowers, $1.53 billion on pampering gifts—like spa treatments—and another $68 million on greeting cards.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Commercialization has ensured that the holiday has continued, while other holidays from the same time, such as <a href="/wiki/Children%27s_Day" title="Children&#39;s Day">Children's Day</a> and Temperance Sunday,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> are no longer celebrated.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mother%27s_Day_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 2005 to 2013, the <a href="/wiki/Southern_500" class="mw-redirect" title="Southern 500">Southern 500</a> auto race at <a href="/wiki/Darlington_Raceway" title="Darlington Raceway">Darlington Raceway</a> was held on Saturday of Mother's Day weekend. From 2014 to 2019, the <a href="/wiki/NASCAR_Cup_Series" title="NASCAR Cup Series">NASCAR Cup Series</a> hosted the <a href="/wiki/Digital_Ally_400" title="Digital Ally 400">Digital Ally 400</a> at <a href="/wiki/Kansas_Speedway" title="Kansas Speedway">Kansas Speedway</a> on Saturday of Mother's Day weekend. Starting in 2020, the <a href="/wiki/STP_500" title="STP 500">STP 500</a> at <a href="/wiki/Martinsville_Speedway" title="Martinsville Speedway">Martinsville Speedway</a> will take place on the Saturday of Mother's Day weekend. Also, the <a href="/wiki/IndyCar_Grand_Prix" title="IndyCar Grand Prix">IndyCar Grand Prix</a> has been held on Saturday of Mother's Day weekend since 2014.
</p><p>From 2007–2018, <a href="/wiki/The_Players_Championship" title="The Players Championship">The Players Championship</a> men's golf tournament was held on Mother's Day weekend, except in 2011 and 2016 when May 1 fell on a Sunday. In those cases, it was held the next weekend. Previously, the <a href="/wiki/Kingsmill_Championship" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingsmill Championship">Kingsmill Championship</a> women's golf tournament was played on Mother's Day weekend from 2004 to 2009.
</p><p>Professional sports players often wear pink clothing accessories on Mother's Day weekend.
</p>
