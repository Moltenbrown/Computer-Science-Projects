	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Stepfather</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Step-dad" redirects here. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Stepfather_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Stepfather (disambiguation)">Stepfather (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>A <b>stepfather</b> or <b>stepdad</b> is a non-biological man married to one's parent. 
</p><p>A stepfather-in-law is a stepfather of one's spouse. 
</p><p>The children of the stepfather-in-law would be the person's stepbrothers-in-law and stepsisters-in-law.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stepfather&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Though less common in literature than evil stepmothers, there are also cases of evil <i>stepfathers</i>, such as in the fairy tales <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Gold-Bearded_Man&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="The Gold-Bearded Man (page does not exist)">The Gold-Bearded Man</a></i> (in a plot usually featuring a cruel father) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Little_Bull-Calf" title="The Little Bull-Calf">The Little Bull-Calf</a></i>. One type of such tale features a defeated villain who insists on marrying the hero's mother and makes her help him trick the hero and so defeat him. Such tales include <i><a href="/wiki/The_Prince_and_the_Princess_in_the_Forest" title="The Prince and the Princess in the Forest">The Prince and the Princess in the Forest</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Blue_Belt" title="The Blue Belt">The Blue Belt</a></i>, although the tales of this type can also feature a different female relation, such as the stepsister in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Three_Princes_and_their_Beasts" title="The Three Princes and their Beasts">The Three Princes and their Beasts</a></i>.
</p><p>In fiction, evil stepfathers include Claudius in <i><a href="/wiki/Hamlet" title="Hamlet">Hamlet</a></i> (though his role as uncle is more emphasized), Walter Parks Thatcher in <i><a href="/wiki/Citizen_Kane" title="Citizen Kane">Citizen Kane</a></i> (though this is debatable), Murdstone in <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/David_Copperfield_(novel)" class="mw-redirect" title="David Copperfield (novel)">David Copperfield</a></i>, the King from the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Radio_Flyer_(film)" title="Radio Flyer (film)">Radio Flyer</a></i>, and Gozaburo Kaiba (who adopted Seto and Mokuba Kaiba) from <i><a href="/wiki/Yu-Gi-Oh!" title="Yu-Gi-Oh!">Yu-Gi-Oh!</a></i>, as well as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Stepfather_(1987_film)" title="The Stepfather (1987 film)">The Stepfather</a></i>, films. The film <i><a href="/wiki/Sucker_Punch_(2011_film)" title="Sucker Punch (2011 film)">Sucker Punch</a></i> features a sexually abusive stepfather.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In his opera <i><a href="/wiki/La_Cenerentola" title="La Cenerentola">La Cenerentola</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Gioacchino_Rossini" class="mw-redirect" title="Gioacchino Rossini">Gioacchino Rossini</a> inverted the tale of <i>Cinderella</i> to have her oppressed by her stepfather. His motive is made explicit, in that providing a <a href="/wiki/Dowry" title="Dowry">dowry</a> to Cenerentola would cut into what he can give to his own daughters.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> An analogous male figure may also appear as a wicked uncle; like the stepmother, the father's brother may covet the child's inheritance for his own children, and so maltreat his nephews or nieces. Modern films, however, seem to cast stepfathers in a somewhat kinder light, implying honorable men who marry divorced women or single mothers make good stepfathers.
</p>
