	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Parent</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Parent_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Parent (disambiguation)">Parent (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>A <b>parent</b>  is a caregiver of the <a href="/wiki/Offspring" title="Offspring">offspring</a> in their own <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a>, a parent is the caretaker of a <a href="/wiki/Child" title="Child">child</a> (where "child" refers to offspring, not necessarily age). A <i>biological parent</i> is a person whose <a href="/wiki/Gamete" title="Gamete">gamete</a> resulted in a child, a male through the sperm, and a female through the ovum. Biological parents are first-degree relatives and have 50% genetic meet. A <a href="/wiki/Female" title="Female">female</a> can also become a parent through <a href="/wiki/Surrogacy" title="Surrogacy">surrogacy</a>. Some parents may be <a href="/wiki/Adoption" title="Adoption">adoptive</a> parents, who nurture and raise an offspring, but are not biologically related to the child. <a href="/wiki/Orphan" title="Orphan">Orphans</a> without adoptive parents can be raised by their <a href="/wiki/Grandparent" title="Grandparent">grandparents</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Family_member" class="mw-redirect" title="Family member">family members</a>.
</p><p>A parent can also be elaborated as an <a href="/wiki/Ancestor" title="Ancestor">ancestor</a> removed one <a href="/wiki/Generation" title="Generation">generation</a>. With recent medical advances, it is possible to have more than two biological parents.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Examples of <a href="/wiki/Third-party_reproduction" title="Third-party reproduction">third biological parents</a> include instances involving surrogacy or a third person who has provided DNA samples during an assisted reproductive procedure that has altered the recipients' genetic material.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The most common types of parents are mothers, fathers, step-parents, and grandparents. A mother is, "a woman in relation to a child or children to whom she has given birth."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The extent to which it is socially acceptable for a parent to be involved in their offspring's life varies from culture to culture, however one that exhibits too little involvement is sometimes said to exhibit <a href="/wiki/Child_neglect" title="Child neglect">child neglect</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> while one that is too involved is sometimes said to be <a href="/wiki/Overprotective" class="mw-redirect" title="Overprotective">overprotective</a>, cosseting, <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/nosy" class="extiw" title="wikt:nosy">nosey</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Intrusiveness" title="Intrusiveness">intrusive</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological">Biological</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Biological">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An individual's biological parents are the persons from whom the individual inherits his or her <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a>. The term is generally only used if there is a need to distinguish an individual's parents from their biological parents,  For example, an individual whose father has remarried may call the father's new wife their <a href="/wiki/Stepmother" title="Stepmother">stepmother</a> and continue to refer to their mother normally, though someone who has had little or no contact with their biological mother may address their <a href="/wiki/Foster_parent" class="mw-redirect" title="Foster parent">foster parent</a> as their mother, and their biological mother as such, or perhaps by her first name.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mother">Mother</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mother">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mother" title="Mother">Mother</a></div>
<p>A mother is a female who has a maternal connection with another individual, whether arising from <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">conception</a>, by giving <a href="/wiki/Childbirth" title="Childbirth">birth</a> to, or <a href="/wiki/Parenting" title="Parenting">raising</a> the individual in the role of a parent.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> More than one female may have such connections with an individual. Because of the complexity and differences of a mother's social, cultural, and religious definitions and roles, it is challenging to define a mother to suit a universally accepted definition. The utilization of a surrogate mother may result in explication of there being two biological mothers.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Father">Father</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Father">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Father" title="Father">Father</a></div>
<p>A father is a male parent of any type of offspring.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> It may be the person who shares in the raising of a child or who has provided the biological material, the <a href="/wiki/Sperm" title="Sperm">sperm</a>, which results in the birth of the child.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grandparent">Grandparent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Grandparent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Grandparent" title="Grandparent">Grandparent</a></div>
<p>Grandparents are the parents of a person's own parent, whether that be a father or a mother. Every sexually reproducing creature who is not a <a href="/wiki/Genetic_chimera" class="mw-redirect" title="Genetic chimera">genetic chimera</a> has a maximum of four <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genetic</a> grandparents, eight genetic great-grandparents, sixteen genetic great-great-grandparents and so on. Rarely, such as in the case of sibling or half-sibling <a href="/wiki/Incest" title="Incest">incest</a>, these <a href="/wiki/Pedigree_collapse" title="Pedigree collapse">numbers are lower</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paternity_issues">Paternity issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Paternity issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/DNA_paternity_testing" title="DNA paternity testing">DNA paternity testing</a></div>
<p>A paternity test is conducted to prove paternity, that is, whether a male is the biological father of another individual. This may be relevant in view of <a href="/wiki/Fathers%27_rights_movement" title="Fathers&#39; rights movement">rights</a> and duties of the father. Similarly, a maternity test can be carried out. This is less common, because at least during <a href="/wiki/Childbirth" title="Childbirth">childbirth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnancy</a>, except in the case of a pregnancy involving <a href="/wiki/Embryo_transfer" title="Embryo transfer">embryo transfer</a> or <a href="/wiki/Egg_donation" title="Egg donation">egg donation</a>, it is obvious who the <a href="/wiki/Mother" title="Mother">mother</a> is. However, it is used in a number of events such as legal battles where a person's maternity is challenged, where the mother is uncertain because she has not seen her child for an extended period of time, or where deceased persons need to be identified.
</p><p>Although not constituting completely reliable evidence, several <a href="/wiki/List_of_Mendelian_traits_in_humans" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Mendelian traits in humans">congenital traits</a> such as attached <a href="/wiki/Earlobe" title="Earlobe">earlobes</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Widow%27s_peak" title="Widow&#39;s peak">widow's peak</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/Cleft_chin" class="mw-redirect" title="Cleft chin">cleft chin</a>, may serve as tentative indicators of (non-) parenthood as they are readily observable and inherited via <a href="/wiki/Dominance_relationship" class="mw-redirect" title="Dominance relationship">autosomal-dominant</a> genes.
</p><p>A more reliable way to ascertain parenthood is via DNA analysis (known as <a href="/wiki/Genetic_fingerprinting" class="mw-redirect" title="Genetic fingerprinting">genetic fingerprinting</a> of individuals, although older methods have included <a href="/wiki/Blood_type" title="Blood type">ABO blood group typing</a>, analysis of various other <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a>, or using <a href="/wiki/Human_leukocyte_antigen" title="Human leukocyte antigen">human leukocyte antigens</a>. The current techniques for paternity testing are using <a href="/wiki/Polymerase_chain_reaction" title="Polymerase chain reaction">polymerase chain reaction</a> (PCR) and <a href="/wiki/Restriction_fragment_length_polymorphism" title="Restriction fragment length polymorphism">restriction fragment length polymorphism</a> (RFLP). For the most part, however, genetic fingerprinting has all but taken over all the other forms of testing.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Roles_and_responsibilities">Roles and responsibilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Roles and responsibilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Guardianship">Guardianship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Guardianship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Legal_guardian" title="Legal guardian">Legal guardian</a></div>
<p>A legal guardian is a person who has the legal authority (and the corresponding duty) to care for the personal and property interests of another person, called a ward. Guardians are typically used in three situations: guardianship for an incapacitated senior (due to old age or infirmity), guardianship for a minor, and guardianship for developmentally disabled adults.
</p><p>Most countries and states have laws that provide that the parents of a minor child are the legal guardians of that child, and that the parents can designate who shall become the child's legal guardian in the event of death, subject to the approval of the court. Some jurisdictions allow a parent of a child to exercise the authority of a legal guardian without a formal court appointment. In such circumstances the parent acting in that capacity is called the natural guardian of that parent's child.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parenting">Parenting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Parenting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Parenting" title="Parenting">Parenting</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Parenting" title="Parenting">Parenting</a> or child rearing is the process of promoting and supporting the <a href="/wiki/Physical_fitness" title="Physical fitness">physical</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emotion" title="Emotion">emotional</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_development" class="mw-redirect" title="Social development">social</a>, financial, and <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_development" class="mw-redirect" title="Intellectual development">intellectual development</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Child" title="Child">child</a> from <a href="/wiki/Infant" title="Infant">infancy</a> to <a href="/wiki/Adult" title="Adult">adulthood</a>. Parenting refers to the aspects of raising a child aside from the biological relationship.<sup id="cite_ref-Davies_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davies-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gender_and_gender_mix">Gender and gender mix</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Gender and gender mix">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A child has at least one biological <a href="/wiki/Father" title="Father">father</a> and at least one biological <a href="/wiki/Mother" title="Mother">mother</a>, but not every <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">family</a> is a traditional <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_family" title="Nuclear family">nuclear family</a>. There are many variants, such as <a href="/wiki/Adoption" title="Adoption">adoption</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shared_parenting" title="Shared parenting">shared parenting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stepfamily" title="Stepfamily">stepfamilies</a>, and <a href="/wiki/LGBT_parenting" title="LGBT parenting">LGBT parenting</a>, over which there has been controversy.
</p><p>The social science literature rejects the notion that there is an optimal gender mix of parents or that children and adolescents with <a href="/wiki/LGBT_parenting" title="LGBT parenting">same-sex parents</a> suffer any developmental disadvantages compared with those with two opposite-sex parents.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-apsp_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-apsp-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The professionals and the major associations now agree there is a well-established and accepted consensus in the field that there is no optimal gender combination of parents.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The family studies literature indicates that it is family processes (such as the quality of parenting and relationships within the family) that contribute to determining children's well-being and "outcomes," rather than family structures, per se, such as the number, gender, sexuality and co-habitation status of parents.<sup id="cite_ref-apsp_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-apsp-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span id="Parent.E2.80.93offspring_conflict"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Parent–offspring_conflict">Parent–offspring conflict</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Parent–offspring conflict">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Parent%E2%80%93offspring_conflict" title="Parent–offspring conflict">Parent–offspring conflict</a></div>
<p>An offspring who hates their father is called a misopater, one that hates their mother is a misomater, while a parent that hates their offspring is a misopedist.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Parent–offspring conflict describes the <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_conflict" class="mw-redirect" title="Evolutionary conflict">evolutionary conflict</a> arising from differences in optimal <a href="/wiki/Fitness_(biology)" title="Fitness (biology)">fitness</a> of parents and their <a href="/wiki/Offspring" title="Offspring">offspring</a>. While parents tend to maximize the number of offspring, the offspring can increase their fitness by getting a greater share of <a href="/wiki/Parental_investment" title="Parental investment">parental investment</a> often by competing with their <a href="/wiki/Sibling" title="Sibling">siblings</a>. The theory was proposed by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Trivers" title="Robert Trivers">Robert Trivers</a> in 1974 and extends the more general <a href="/wiki/Gene-centered_view_of_evolution" title="Gene-centered view of evolution">selfish gene theory</a> and has been used to explain many observed biological phenomena.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> For example, in some <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> species, although parents often lay two eggs and attempt to raise two or more young, the strongest fledgling takes a greater share of the food brought by parents and will often kill the weaker sibling, an act known as <a href="/wiki/Siblicide" title="Siblicide">siblicide</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Empathy">Empathy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Empathy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>David Haig has argued that human <a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">fetal</a> genes would be selected to draw more resources from the mother than it would be optimal for the mother to give, a hypothesis that has received empirical support. The <a href="/wiki/Placenta" title="Placenta">placenta</a>, for example, secretes allocrine <a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">hormones</a> that decrease the sensitivity of the mother to <a href="/wiki/Insulin" title="Insulin">insulin</a> and thus make a larger supply of blood sugar available to the fetus. The mother responds by increasing the level of insulin in her bloodstream, the placenta has insulin receptors that stimulate the production of insulin-degrading <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> which counteract this effect.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Having_children_and_happiness">Having children and happiness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Having children and happiness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Europe, parents are generally happier than non-parents. In women, happiness increases after the first child, but having higher-order children is not associated with further increased well-being. Happiness seems to increase most in the year before and after the first childbirth.<sup id="cite_ref-balbo2013_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-balbo2013-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
