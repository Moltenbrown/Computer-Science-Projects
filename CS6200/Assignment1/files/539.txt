<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">Kinship</a></div>
<p><b>Eskimo kinship</b> or <b>Inuit kinship</b> is a category of <a href="/wiki/Kinship_and_descent" class="mw-redirect" title="Kinship and descent">kinship</a> used to define <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">family</a> organization in <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a>. Identified by <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Henry_Morgan" class="mw-redirect" title="Lewis Henry Morgan">Lewis Henry Morgan</a> in his 1871 work <i>Systems of Consanguinity and Affinity of the Human Family</i>, the Eskimo system was one of six major kinship systems (Eskimo, <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_kinship" title="Hawaiian kinship">Hawaiian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iroquois_kinship" title="Iroquois kinship">Iroquois</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crow_kinship" title="Crow kinship">Crow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Omaha_kinship" title="Omaha kinship">Omaha</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sudanese_kinship" title="Sudanese kinship">Sudanese</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Joint_family">Joint family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eskimo_kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Joint family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Joint_family" class="mw-redirect" title="Joint family">joint family</a> system places no distinction between patrilineal and matrilineal relatives, instead, it focuses on differences in kinship distance (the closer the relative is, the more distinctions are made). The system emphasizes the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_family" title="Nuclear family">nuclear family</a>, identifying directly only the mother, father, brother, and sister.  All other relatives are grouped together into categories. It uses both <a href="/wiki/Classificatory_kinship" title="Classificatory kinship">classificatory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Descriptive" class="mw-redirect" title="Descriptive">descriptive</a> terms, differentiating between gender, generation, lineal relatives (relatives in the direct line of descent), and collateral relatives (blood relatives not in the direct line of descent). The Eskimo system is defined by its "cognatic" or "bilateral" emphasis - no distinction is made between patrilineal and matrilineal relatives.
</p><p>Parental siblings are distinguished only by their sex (Aunt, Uncle). All children of these individuals are lumped together regardless of sex (Cousins).  Unlike the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_kinship" title="Hawaiian kinship">Hawaiian system</a>, Ego's parents are clearly distinguished from their siblings.
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a href="/wiki/File:Eskimo-kinship-chart.svg" class="image" title="Graphic of the Eskimo kinship system"><img alt="Graphic of the Eskimo kinship system" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Eskimo-kinship-chart.svg/700px-Eskimo-kinship-chart.svg.png" decoding="async" width="700" height="230" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Eskimo-kinship-chart.svg/1050px-Eskimo-kinship-chart.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Eskimo-kinship-chart.svg/1400px-Eskimo-kinship-chart.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="900" data-file-height="296" /></a></div></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eskimo_kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Eskimo system is relatively common among the world's kinship systems, at about 10% of the world's societies.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It is now common in most Western societies (such as those of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> or <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>). In addition, it is found among a small number of <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">food-foraging</a> peoples such as the <a href="/wiki/!Kung_people" class="mw-redirect" title="!Kung people">!Kung</a> tribe of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and the Eskimos/Inuit for whom it is named.
</p><p>The system is widely used in non-unilineal societies, where the dominant relatives are the immediate family. In most Western societies, the nuclear family represents an independent social and economic group, which has caused the emphasis on the immediate kinship. The tendency of families in Western societies to live apart also reinforces this.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eskimo_kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Native_American_name_controversy" title="Native American name controversy">Native American name controversy</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Eskimo#terminology" title="Eskimo">The term "Eskimo"</a> is considered pejorative in Canada, and has been replaced there by <a href="/wiki/Inuit#terminology" title="Inuit">the term "Inuit"</a>. The former remains in use in Alaska, though less so than in past decades. In Canada, the term "Inuit kinship" is therefore widely used instead of "Eskimo kinship".
</p>
