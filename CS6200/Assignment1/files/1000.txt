<p><b>Diego Rodríguez de Silva y Velázquez</b><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> (baptized June 6, 1599&#160;&#8211;&#32;August 6, 1660) was a <a href="/wiki/Spaniards" title="Spaniards">Spanish</a> painter, the leading artist in the <a href="/wiki/Noble_court" class="mw-redirect" title="Noble court">court</a> of King <a href="/wiki/Philip_IV_of_Spain" title="Philip IV of Spain">Philip IV</a>, and one of the most important painters of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Golden_Age" title="Spanish Golden Age">Spanish Golden Age</a>. He was an individualistic artist of the contemporary <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> period. He began to paint in a precise <a href="/wiki/Tenebrism" title="Tenebrism">tenebrist</a> style,  later developing a freer manner characterized by bold brushwork. In addition to numerous renditions of scenes of historical and cultural significance, he painted scores of <a href="/wiki/Portrait_painting" title="Portrait painting">portraits</a> of the Spanish royal family and commoners, culminating in his masterpiece <i><a href="/wiki/Las_Meninas" title="Las Meninas">Las Meninas</a></i> (1656).
</p><p>Velázquez's artwork became a model for 19th century <a href="/wiki/Realism_(art_movement)" title="Realism (art movement)">realist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Impressionism" title="Impressionism">impressionist</a> painters. Since then, famous modern artists, including <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">Pablo Picasso</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Dal%C3%AD" title="Salvador Dalí">Salvador Dalí</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francis_Bacon_(artist)" title="Francis Bacon (artist)">Francis Bacon</a>, have paid tribute to Velázquez by recreating several of his most famous works.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Casa_natal_de_Velazquez.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Casa_natal_de_Velazquez.jpg/170px-Casa_natal_de_Velazquez.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="205" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Casa_natal_de_Velazquez.jpg/255px-Casa_natal_de_Velazquez.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Casa_natal_de_Velazquez.jpg/340px-Casa_natal_de_Velazquez.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="3124" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Casa_natal_de_Velazquez.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Birthplace of Velázquez in <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">Seville</a></div></div></div>
<p>Velázquez was born in <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">Seville</a>, Spain, the first child of João Rodrigues de Silva, a notary, and Jerónima Velázquez. He was baptized at the church of St. Peter in Seville on Sunday, June 6, 1599.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The baptism most likely occurred a few days or weeks after his birth. His paternal grandparents, Diogo da Silva and Maria Rodrigues, were Portuguese and had moved to Seville decades earlier. When Velázquez was offered knighthood in 1658 he claimed descent from the lesser nobility in order to qualify; in fact, however, his grandparents were tradespeople, and possibly Jewish <i><a href="/wiki/Conversos" class="mw-redirect" title="Conversos">conversos</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> As was customary in <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">Andalusia</a>, Velázquez usually used his mother's surname.<sup id="cite_ref-Harris_9_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harris_9-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Raised in modest circumstances, he showed an early gift for art, and was apprenticed to <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Pacheco" title="Francisco Pacheco">Francisco Pacheco</a>, an artist and teacher in Seville. An early-18th-century biographer, Antonio Palomino, said Velázquez studied for a short time under <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Herrera_the_Elder" title="Francisco Herrera the Elder">Francisco de Herrera</a> before beginning his apprenticeship under Pacheco, but this is undocumented. A contract signed on September 17, 1611, formalized a six-year apprenticeship with Pacheco backdated to December 1610,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and it has been suggested that Herrera may have substituted for a traveling Pacheco between December 1610 and September 1611.<sup id="cite_ref-Harris_9_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harris_9-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Though considered a dull and undistinguished painter, Pacheco sometimes expressed a simple, direct realism although his work remained essentially <a href="/wiki/Mannerism" title="Mannerism">Mannerist</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  As a teacher, he was highly learned and encouraged his students' intellectual development. In Pacheco's school Velázquez studied the classics, was trained in proportion and perspective, and witnessed the trends in the literary and artistic circles of Seville.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:VEL%C3%81ZQUEZ_-_Vieja_friendo_huevos_(National_Galleries_of_Scotland,_1618._%C3%93leo_sobre_lienzo,_100.5_x_119.5_cm).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/VEL%C3%81ZQUEZ_-_Vieja_friendo_huevos_%28National_Galleries_of_Scotland%2C_1618._%C3%93leo_sobre_lienzo%2C_100.5_x_119.5_cm%29.jpg/220px-VEL%C3%81ZQUEZ_-_Vieja_friendo_huevos_%28National_Galleries_of_Scotland%2C_1618._%C3%93leo_sobre_lienzo%2C_100.5_x_119.5_cm%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="187" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/VEL%C3%81ZQUEZ_-_Vieja_friendo_huevos_%28National_Galleries_of_Scotland%2C_1618._%C3%93leo_sobre_lienzo%2C_100.5_x_119.5_cm%29.jpg/330px-VEL%C3%81ZQUEZ_-_Vieja_friendo_huevos_%28National_Galleries_of_Scotland%2C_1618._%C3%93leo_sobre_lienzo%2C_100.5_x_119.5_cm%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/VEL%C3%81ZQUEZ_-_Vieja_friendo_huevos_%28National_Galleries_of_Scotland%2C_1618._%C3%93leo_sobre_lienzo%2C_100.5_x_119.5_cm%29.jpg/440px-VEL%C3%81ZQUEZ_-_Vieja_friendo_huevos_%28National_Galleries_of_Scotland%2C_1618._%C3%93leo_sobre_lienzo%2C_100.5_x_119.5_cm%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3998" data-file-height="3407" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:VEL%C3%81ZQUEZ_-_Vieja_friendo_huevos_(National_Galleries_of_Scotland,_1618._%C3%93leo_sobre_lienzo,_100.5_x_119.5_cm).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Vieja friendo huevos</i> (1618, English: <i><a href="/wiki/Old_Woman_Cooking_Eggs" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Woman Cooking Eggs">Old Woman Frying Eggs</a></i>). <a href="/wiki/National_Gallery_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="National Gallery of Scotland">National Gallery of Scotland</a>, Edinburgh</div></div></div>
<p>On April 23, 1618, Velázquez married Juana Pacheco (June 1, 1602&#160;&#8211;&#32;August 10, 1660), the daughter of his teacher. She had two daughters. The elder, Francisca de Silva Velázquez y Pacheco (1619–1658), married painter <a href="/wiki/Juan_Bautista_Mart%C3%ADnez_del_Mazo" title="Juan Bautista Martínez del Mazo">Juan Bautista Martínez del Mazo</a> at the Church of Santiago in Madrid on August 21, 1633; the younger, Ignacia de Silva Velázquez y Pacheco, born in 1621, died in infancy.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Velázquez's earliest works are <a href="/wiki/Bodeg%C3%B3n" title="Bodegón">bodegones</a> (kitchen scenes with prominent <a href="/wiki/Still-life" class="mw-redirect" title="Still-life">still-life</a>), He was one of the first Spanish artists to paint such scenes, and his <i>Old Woman Frying Eggs</i> (1618) demonstrates the young artist's unusual skill in realistic depiction.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The realism and dramatic lighting of this work may have been influenced by <a href="/wiki/Caravaggio" title="Caravaggio">Caravaggio</a>'s work—which Velázquez could have seen second-hand, in copies—and by the polychrome sculpture in Sevillian churches.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Two of his bodegones, <i>Kitchen Scene with Christ in the House of Martha</i> (1618) and <i>Kitchen Scene with Christ at Emmaus</i> (c. 1618), feature religious scenes in the background, painted in a way that creates ambiguity as to whether the religious scene is a painting on the wall, a representation of the thoughts of the kitchen maid in the foreground, or an actual incident seen through a window.<sup id="cite_ref-Sánchez_Grove_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sánchez_Grove-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>  <i>The Virgin of the Immaculate Conception</i> (1618–19) follows a formula used by Pacheco, but replaces the idealized facial type and smoothly finished surfaces of his teacher with the face of a local girl and varied brushwork.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> His other religious works include <i><a href="/wiki/Adoration_of_the_Magi_(Vel%C3%A1zquez)" title="Adoration of the Magi (Velázquez)">The Adoration of the Magi</a></i> (1619) and <i>Saint John the Evangelist on the Island of Patmos</i> (1618–19), both of which begin to express his more pointed and careful realism.
</p><p>Also from this period are the portrait of <i>Sor Jerónima de la Fuente</i> (1620) – Velázquez's first full-length portrait<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> – and the genre <i>The Water Seller of Seville</i> (1618–1622). <i>The Water Seller of Seville</i> has been termed "the peak of Velázquez's <i>bodegones</i>" and is admired for its virtuoso rendering of volumes and textures as well as for its enigmatic gravitas.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="To_Madrid_.28early_period.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="To_Madrid_(early_period)">To Madrid (early period)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: To Madrid (early period)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Velazquez1632philipIV.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Velazquez1632philipIV.jpg/170px-Velazquez1632philipIV.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="302" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Velazquez1632philipIV.jpg/255px-Velazquez1632philipIV.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/79/Velazquez1632philipIV.jpg 2x" data-file-width="327" data-file-height="580" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Velazquez1632philipIV.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Philip_IV_in_Brown_and_Silver" title="Philip IV in Brown and Silver">Philip IV in Brown and Silver</a></i>, 1632</div></div></div>
<p>Velázquez had established his reputation in Seville by the early 1620s. He traveled to Madrid in April 1622, with letters of introduction to Don Juan de Fonseca, chaplain to the King. Velázquez was not allowed to paint the new king, <a href="/wiki/Philip_IV_of_Spain" title="Philip IV of Spain">Philip IV</a>, but portrayed the poet <a href="/wiki/Luis_de_G%C3%B3ngora" title="Luis de Góngora">Luis de Góngora</a> at the request of Pacheco.<sup id="cite_ref-Carr_245_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carr_245-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The portrait showed Góngora crowned with a laurel wreath, which Velázquez later painted over.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> He returned to Seville in January 1623 and remained there until August.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In December 1622, <a href="/wiki/Rodrigo_de_Villandrando_(painter)" title="Rodrigo de Villandrando (painter)">Rodrigo de Villandrando</a>, the king's favorite court painter, died.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Velázquez received a command to come to the court from the <a href="/wiki/Count-Duke_of_Olivares" class="mw-redirect" title="Count-Duke of Olivares">Count-Duke of Olivares</a>, the powerful minister of Philip IV. He was offered 50 <a href="/wiki/Ducat" title="Ducat">ducats</a> (175 g of gold) to defray his expenses, and he was accompanied by his father-in-law. Fonseca lodged the young painter in his home and sat for a portrait, which, when completed, was conveyed to the royal palace.<sup id="cite_ref-Carr_245_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carr_245-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> A portrait of the king was commissioned, and on August 30, 1623, Philip IV sat for Velázquez.<sup id="cite_ref-Carr_245_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carr_245-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The portrait pleased the king, and Olivares commanded Velázquez to move to Madrid, promising that no other painter would ever paint Philip's portrait and all other portraits of the king would be withdrawn from circulation.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>  In the following year, 1624, he received 300 ducats from the king to pay the cost of moving his family to Madrid, which became his home for the remainder of his life.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_El_Triunfo_de_Baco_o_Los_Borrachos_(Museo_del_Prado,_1628-29).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_El_Triunfo_de_Baco_o_Los_Borrachos_%28Museo_del_Prado%2C_1628-29%29.jpg/220px-Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_El_Triunfo_de_Baco_o_Los_Borrachos_%28Museo_del_Prado%2C_1628-29%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="160" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_El_Triunfo_de_Baco_o_Los_Borrachos_%28Museo_del_Prado%2C_1628-29%29.jpg/330px-Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_El_Triunfo_de_Baco_o_Los_Borrachos_%28Museo_del_Prado%2C_1628-29%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_El_Triunfo_de_Baco_o_Los_Borrachos_%28Museo_del_Prado%2C_1628-29%29.jpg/440px-Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_El_Triunfo_de_Baco_o_Los_Borrachos_%28Museo_del_Prado%2C_1628-29%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1900" data-file-height="1386" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_El_Triunfo_de_Baco_o_Los_Borrachos_(Museo_del_Prado,_1628-29).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/The_Triumph_of_Bacchus" title="The Triumph of Bacchus">El Triunfo de Baco</a></i> or <i>Los Borrachos</i> 1629 (English: <i>The Triumph of Bacchus/The Drunks</i>)</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez_030.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez_030.jpg/220px-Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez_030.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="282" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez_030.jpg/330px-Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez_030.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez_030.jpg/440px-Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez_030.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1178" data-file-height="1508" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez_030.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Portrait of the Infanta <a href="/wiki/Maria_Theresa_of_Spain" title="Maria Theresa of Spain">Maria Theresa</a></i>, Philip IV's daughter with <a href="/wiki/Elisabeth_of_France_(1602%E2%80%931644)" title="Elisabeth of France (1602–1644)">Elisabeth of France</a></div></div></div>
<p>Velázquez secured admission to the royal service with a salary of 20 ducats per month, lodgings and payment for the pictures he might paint. His portrait of Philip was exhibited on the steps of San Felipe and received with enthusiasm. It is now lost (as is the portrait of Fonseca).<sup id="cite_ref-Harris_12_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harris_12-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Museo_del_Prado" title="Museo del Prado">Museo del Prado</a>, however, has two of Velázquez's portraits of the king (nos. 1070 and 1071) in which the severity of the Seville period has disappeared and the tones are more delicate. The modeling is firm, recalling that of <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Mor" class="mw-redirect" title="Antonio Mor">Antonio Mor</a>, the Dutch portrait painter of <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Philip II</a>, who exercised a considerable influence on the Spanish school.  Velázquez depicts Philip wearing the <i><a href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/golilla" class="extiw" title="es:golilla">golilla</a></i>, a stiff linen collar projecting at right angles from the neck. The <i>golilla</i> replaced the earlier court fashion of elaborate ruffed collars as part of Philip's dress reform laws during a period of economic crisis.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Prince of Wales (afterwards <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a>) arrived at the court of Spain in 1623. Records indicate that he sat for Velázquez, but the picture is now lost.<sup id="cite_ref-Harris_12_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harris_12-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1627, Philip set a competition for the best painters of Spain with the subject to be the expulsion of the <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moors</a>. Velázquez won. Recorded descriptions of his painting (destroyed in a fire at the palace in 1734)<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> say it depicted <a href="/wiki/Philip_III_of_Spain" title="Philip III of Spain">Philip III</a> pointing with his baton to a crowd of men and women being led away by soldiers, while the <a href="/wiki/Allegory_of_Hispania" title="Allegory of Hispania">female personification of Spain</a> sits in calm repose. Velázquez was appointed gentleman usher as reward. Later he also received a daily allowance of 12 <a href="/wiki/Spanish_real" title="Spanish real">réis</a>, the same amount allotted to the court barbers, and 90 ducats a year for dress.
</p><p>In September 1628, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Paul_Rubens" title="Peter Paul Rubens">Peter Paul Rubens</a> was positioned in Madrid as an emissary from the Infanta Isabella, and Velázquez accompanied him to view the <a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titians</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Escorial" class="mw-redirect" title="Escorial">Escorial</a>. Rubens, who demonstrated his brilliance as painter and courtier during the seven months of the diplomatic mission, had a high opinion of Velázquez but had no significant influence on his painting. He did, however, galvanize Velázquez's desire to see Italy and the works of the great Italian masters.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1629, Velázquez received 100 ducats for the picture of <a href="/wiki/Dionysus" title="Dionysus">Bacchus</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/The_Triumph_of_Bacchus" title="The Triumph of Bacchus">The Triumph of Bacchus</a></i>), also called <i>Los Borrachos</i> (The Drunks), a painting of a group of men in contemporary dress paying homage to a half-naked ivy-crowned young man seated on a wine barrel. Velázquez's first mythological painting,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> it has been interpreted variously as a depiction of a theatrical performance, as a parody, or as a symbolic representation of peasants asking the god of wine to give them relief from their sorrows.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The style shows the naturalism of Velázquez's early works slightly touched by the influence of Titian and Rubens.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Italian_period">Italian period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Italian period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1629, Velázquez was given permission to spend a year and a half in Italy. Though this first visit is recognized as a crucial chapter in the development of his style—and in the history of Spanish Royal Patronage, since Philip IV sponsored his trip—few details and specifics are known of what the painter saw, whom he met, how he was perceived and what innovations he hoped to introduce into his painting.
</p><p>He traveled to Venice, Ferrara, Cento, Loreto, Bologna, and Rome.<sup id="cite_ref-Sánchez_Grove_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sánchez_Grove-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In 1630 he visited Naples to paint the portrait of <a href="/wiki/Maria_Anna_of_Spain" title="Maria Anna of Spain">Maria Anna of Spain</a>, and there he probably met <a href="/wiki/Jusepe_de_Ribera" title="Jusepe de Ribera">Ribera</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sánchez_Grove_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sánchez_Grove-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The major works from his first Italian period are <i>Joseph's Bloody Coat brought to Jacob</i> (1629–30) and <i><a href="/wiki/Apollo_in_the_Forge_of_Vulcan" title="Apollo in the Forge of Vulcan">Apollo in the Forge of Vulcan</a></i> (1630), both of which reveal his ambition to rival the Italians as a history painter in the grand manner.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Return_to_Madrid_.28middle_period.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Return_to_Madrid_(middle_period)">Return to Madrid (middle period)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Return to Madrid (middle period)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_de_Breda_o_Las_Lanzas_(Museo_del_Prado,_1634-35).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_de_Breda_o_Las_Lanzas_%28Museo_del_Prado%2C_1634-35%29.jpg/220px-Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_de_Breda_o_Las_Lanzas_%28Museo_del_Prado%2C_1634-35%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="181" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_de_Breda_o_Las_Lanzas_%28Museo_del_Prado%2C_1634-35%29.jpg/330px-Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_de_Breda_o_Las_Lanzas_%28Museo_del_Prado%2C_1634-35%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_de_Breda_o_Las_Lanzas_%28Museo_del_Prado%2C_1634-35%29.jpg/440px-Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_de_Breda_o_Las_Lanzas_%28Museo_del_Prado%2C_1634-35%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2870" data-file-height="2362" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_de_Breda_o_Las_Lanzas_(Museo_del_Prado,_1634-35).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/La_rendici%C3%B3n_de_Breda" class="mw-redirect" title="La rendición de Breda">La rendición de Breda</a></i> (1634–1635) was inspired by Velázquez's first visit to Italy, in which he accompanied <a href="/wiki/Ambrogio_Spinola,_marqu%C3%A9s_de_los_Balbases" class="mw-redirect" title="Ambrogio Spinola, marqués de los Balbases">Ambrogio Spinola</a>, who conquered the Dutch city of Breda a few years prior. It depicts a transfer of the key to the city from the Dutch to the Spanish army during the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Breda_(1624)" title="Siege of Breda (1624)">Siege of Breda</a>. It is considered one of the best of Velázquez's paintings.</div></div></div>
<p>Velázquez returned to Madrid in January 1631.<sup id="cite_ref-Sánchez_Grove_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sánchez_Grove-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> That year he completed the first of his many portraits of the young prince, beginning with <i><a href="/wiki/Prince_Balthasar_Charles_with_a_Dwarf" title="Prince Balthasar Charles with a Dwarf">Prince Balthasar Charles with a Dwarf</a></i>, (Boston, Museum of Fine Arts).<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> ln portraits such as <i><a href="/wiki/Equestrian_portrait_of_prince_Balthasar_Charles" class="mw-redirect" title="Equestrian portrait of prince Balthasar Charles">Equestrian portrait of prince Balthasar Charles</a></i> (1635), Velázquez depicts the prince looking dignified and lordly, or in the dress of a field marshal on his prancing steed. In one version, the scene is in the <a href="/wiki/Riding_school" class="mw-redirect" title="Riding school">riding school</a> of the palace, the king and queen looking on from a balcony, while Olivares attends as master of the horse to the prince.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>To decorate the king's new palace, the Palacio del Buen Retiro, Velázquez painted equestrian portraits of the royal family.<sup id="cite_ref-Sánchez_Grove_18-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sánchez_Grove-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In <i>Philip IV on Horseback</i> (1634–35), the king is represented in profile in an image of imperturbable majesty, demonstrating expert horsemanship by executing an effortless <a href="/wiki/Levade" class="mw-redirect" title="Levade">levade</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Carr_38_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carr_38-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The large <i><a href="/wiki/The_Surrender_of_Breda" title="The Surrender of Breda">The Surrender of Breda</a></i> (1634–35), also painted for the Palacio, is Velázquez's only extant painting depicting contemporary history.<sup id="cite_ref-Carr_38_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carr_38-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Its symbolic treatment of a Spanish military victory over the Dutch eschews the rhetoric of conquest and superiority that is typical in such scenes, in which a general on horseback looks down on his vanquished, kneeling opponent. Instead, Velázquez shows the Spanish general standing before his Dutch counterpart as an equal, and extending to him a hand of consolation.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The influential minister Olivares was the early patron of the painter.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> His impassive, saturnine face is familiar to us from the many portraits painted by Velázquez. Two are notable: one is full-length, stately and dignified, in which he wears the green cross of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Alcantara" class="mw-redirect" title="Order of Alcantara">order of Alcantara</a> and holds a wand, the badge of his office as master of the horse; in the other, <i>The Count-Duke of Olivares on Horseback</i> (c. 1635), he is flatteringly represented as a field marshal during action. In these portraits, Velázquez well repaid the debt of gratitude that he owed to his first patron, whom Velázquez stood by during Olivares's fall from power, thus exposing himself to the great risk of the anger of the jealous Philip.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The king, however, showed no sign of malice towards his favorite painter.
</p><p>The sculptor <a href="/wiki/Juan_Mart%C3%ADnez_Monta%C3%B1%C3%A9s" title="Juan Martínez Montañés">Juan Martínez Montañés</a> modeled a statue on one of Velázquez's equestrian portraits of the king (painted in 1636; now lost) which was cast in bronze by the <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florentine</a> sculptor <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Tacca" title="Pietro Tacca">Pietro Tacca</a> and now stands in the <a href="/wiki/Plaza_de_Oriente" title="Plaza de Oriente">Plaza de Oriente</a> in Madrid.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Velázquez was in close attendance to Philip, and accompanied him to <a href="/wiki/Aragon" title="Aragon">Aragon</a> in 1644, where the artist painted a portrait of the monarch in the costume as he reviewed his troops in <a href="/wiki/Fraga" title="Fraga">Fraga</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Velázquez's paintings of <i>Aesop</i> and <i>Menippus</i> (both c. 1636–1638) portray ancient writers in the guise of portraits of beggars.<sup id="cite_ref-Sánchez_Grove_18-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sánchez_Grove-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Mars_Resting" title="Mars Resting">Mars Resting</a></i> (c. 1638) is both a depiction of a mythological figure and a portrait of a weary-looking, middle-aged man posing as Mars.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The model is painted with attention to his individuality, while his unkempt, oversized mustache is a faintly comic incongruity.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> The equivocal image has been interpreted in various ways: Javier Portús describes it as a "reflection on reality, representation, and the artistic vision", while Alfonso E. Pérez Sánchez says it "has also been seen as a melancholy meditation on the arms of Spain in decline".<sup id="cite_ref-Sánchez_Grove_18-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sánchez_Grove-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Had it not been for his royal appointment,  which enabled Velázquez to escape the censorship of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Inquisition" title="Spanish Inquisition">Inquisition</a>, he would not have been able to release his <i>La Venus del espejo</i> (c. 1644–1648, English: <i><a href="/wiki/Rokeby_Venus" title="Rokeby Venus">Venus at her Mirror</a></i>) also known as <i>The Rokeby Venus</i>. It is the first known female <a href="/wiki/Nude" class="mw-redirect" title="Nude">nude</a> painted by a Spanish artist,<sup id="cite_ref-Sánchez_Grove_18-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sánchez_Grove-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and the only surviving female nude by Velázquez.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Portraiture">Portraiture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Portraiture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_Portrait_of_a_Lady_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_Portrait_of_a_Lady_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/170px-Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_Portrait_of_a_Lady_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="209" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_Portrait_of_a_Lady_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/255px-Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_Portrait_of_a_Lady_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_Portrait_of_a_Lady_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/340px-Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_Portrait_of_a_Lady_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="4922" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_Portrait_of_a_Lady_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Lady from court</i>, c. 1635</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pablo_de_Valladolid,_por_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Pablo_de_Valladolid%2C_por_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg/170px-Pablo_de_Valladolid%2C_por_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="291" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Pablo_de_Valladolid%2C_por_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg/255px-Pablo_de_Valladolid%2C_por_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Pablo_de_Valladolid%2C_por_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg/340px-Pablo_de_Valladolid%2C_por_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1120" data-file-height="1920" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pablo_de_Valladolid,_por_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Portrait_of_Pablo_de_Valladolid" title="Portrait of Pablo de Valladolid">Portrait of Pablo de Valladolid</a></i>, 1635, a court fool of <a href="/wiki/Philip_IV_of_Spain" title="Philip IV of Spain">Philip IV</a></div></div></div>
<p>Besides the many portraits of Philip by Velázquez—thirty-four by one count<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>—he painted portraits of other members of the royal family: Philip's first wife, <a href="/wiki/Elisabeth_of_Bourbon" class="mw-redirect" title="Elisabeth of Bourbon">Elisabeth of Bourbon</a>, and her children, especially her eldest son, Don Baltasar Carlos, whom Velázquez first depicted at about two years of age. Cavaliers, soldiers, churchmen, and the poet <a href="/wiki/Francisco_de_Quevedo" title="Francisco de Quevedo">Francisco de Quevedo</a> (now at <a href="/wiki/Apsley_House" title="Apsley House">Apsley House</a>), sat for Velázquez.
</p><p>Velázquez also painted several buffoons and dwarfs in Philip's court, whom he depicted sympathetically and with respect for their individuality, as in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Jester_Don_Diego_de_Acedo" title="The Jester Don Diego de Acedo">The Jester Don Diego de Acedo</a></i> (1644), whose intelligent face and huge folio with ink-bottle and pen by his side show him to be a wise and well-educated man.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> <i>Pablo de Valladolid</i> (1635), a buffoon evidently acting a part, and <i>The Buffoon of Coria</i> (1639) belong to this middle period.
</p><p>As court painter, Velázquez had fewer commissions for religious works than any of his contemporaries.<sup id="cite_ref-Carr_36_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carr_36-47">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Christ_Crucified_(Vel%C3%A1zquez)" title="Christ Crucified (Velázquez)">Christ Crucified</a></i> (1632), painted for the Convent of San Plácido in Madrid, depicts Christ immediately after death. The Savior's head hangs on his breast and a mass of dark tangled hair conceals part of the face, visually reinforcing the idea of death.<sup id="cite_ref-Carr_36_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carr_36-47">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> The figure is presented alone before a dark background. 
</p><p>Velázquez's son-in-law <a href="/wiki/Juan_Bautista_Mart%C3%ADnez_del_Mazo" title="Juan Bautista Martínez del Mazo">Juan Bautista Martínez del Mazo</a> had succeeded him as usher in 1634,<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> and Mazo himself had received a steady promotion in the royal household. Mazo received a pension of 500 <a href="/wiki/Ducado" class="mw-redirect" title="Ducado">ducats</a> in 1640, increased to 700 in 1648, for portraits painted and to be painted, and was appointed inspector of works in the palace in 1647.
</p><p>Philip now entrusted Velázquez with the mission of procuring paintings and sculpture for the royal collection. Rich in pictures, Spain was weak in statuary, and Velázquez was commissioned once again to proceed to Italy to make purchases.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_visit_to_Italy">Second visit to Italy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Second visit to Italy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Retrato_del_Papa_Inocencio_X._Roma,_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Retrato_del_Papa_Inocencio_X._Roma%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg/170px-Retrato_del_Papa_Inocencio_X._Roma%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="217" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Retrato_del_Papa_Inocencio_X._Roma%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg/255px-Retrato_del_Papa_Inocencio_X._Roma%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Retrato_del_Papa_Inocencio_X._Roma%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg/340px-Retrato_del_Papa_Inocencio_X._Roma%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4545" data-file-height="5799" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Retrato_del_Papa_Inocencio_X._Roma,_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Portrait_of_Pope_Innocent_X" class="mw-redirect" title="Portrait of Pope Innocent X">Portrait of Pope Innocent X</a></i>, 1650</div></div></div>
<p>When he set out in 1649, he was accompanied by his assistant <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_Pareja" title="Juan de Pareja">Juan de Pareja</a> who at this point in time was a slave and who had been trained in painting by Velázquez.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Velázquez sailed from <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1laga" title="Málaga">Málaga</a>, landed at <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a>, and proceeded from Milan to Venice, buying paintings of <a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tintoretto" title="Tintoretto">Tintoretto</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Veronese" title="Paolo Veronese">Veronese</a> as he went.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> At <a href="/wiki/Modena" title="Modena">Modena</a> he was received with much favor by the duke, and here he painted the portrait of the duke at the Modena gallery and two portraits that now adorn the <a href="/wiki/Dresden" title="Dresden">Dresden</a> gallery, for these paintings came from the Modena sale of 1746.
</p><p>Those works presage the advent of the painter's third and latest manner, a noble example of which is the great portrait of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Innocent_X" title="Pope Innocent X">Pope Innocent X</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Doria_Pamphilj_Gallery" title="Doria Pamphilj Gallery">Doria Pamphilj Gallery</a> in Rome, where Velázquez now proceeded. There he was received with marked favor by the Pope, who presented him with a medal and golden chain. Velázquez took a copy of the portrait—which Sir <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Reynolds" title="Joshua Reynolds">Joshua Reynolds</a> thought was the finest picture in Rome—with him to Spain. Several copies of it exist in different galleries, some of them possibly studies for the original or replicas painted for Philip. Velázquez, in this work, had now reached the <i>manera abreviada</i>, a term coined by contemporary Spaniards for this bolder, sharper style. The portrait shows such ruthlessness in Innocent's expression that some in the <a href="/wiki/Vatican_City" title="Vatican City">Vatican</a> feared that it would be seen unfavorably by the Pope; in fact Innocent was pleased with the work, and hung it in his official visitor's waiting room.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Retrato_de_Juan_Pareja,_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Retrato_de_Juan_Pareja%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg/170px-Retrato_de_Juan_Pareja%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="198" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Retrato_de_Juan_Pareja%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg/255px-Retrato_de_Juan_Pareja%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Retrato_de_Juan_Pareja%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg/340px-Retrato_de_Juan_Pareja%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3363" data-file-height="3908" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Retrato_de_Juan_Pareja,_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Portrait_of_Juan_de_Pareja" title="Portrait of Juan de Pareja">Portrait of Juan de Pareja</a></i> (c. 1650)</div></div></div>
<p>In 1650 in Rome Velázquez also painted a <i><a href="/wiki/Portrait_of_Juan_de_Pareja" title="Portrait of Juan de Pareja">portrait of Juan de Pareja</a></i>, now in the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art" title="Metropolitan Museum of Art">Metropolitan Museum of Art</a> in New York City, USA. This portrait procured his election into the <a href="/wiki/Accademia_di_San_Luca" title="Accademia di San Luca">Accademia di San Luca</a>. Purportedly Velázquez created this portrait as a warm-up of his skills before his portrait of the Pope. It captures in great detail Pareja's countenance and his somewhat worn and patched clothing with an economic use of brushwork. In November 1650, Juan de Pareja was freed by Velázquez.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>To this period also belong two small landscape paintings both titled <i><a href="/wiki/View_of_the_Garden_of_the_Villa_Medici" title="View of the Garden of the Villa Medici">View of the Garden of the Villa Medici</a></i>. As landscapes apparently painted directly from nature, they were exceptional for their time, and reveal Velázquez's close study of light at different times of day.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As part of his mission to procure decorations for the <i>Room of Mirrors</i> at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Alcazar_of_Madrid" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Alcazar of Madrid">Royal Alcazar of Madrid</a>, Velázquez commissioned <a href="/w/index.php?title=Matteo_Bonuccelli&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Matteo Bonuccelli (page does not exist)">Matteo Bonuccelli</a> to cast twelve bronze copies of the <a href="/wiki/Medici_lions" title="Medici lions">Medici lions</a>. The copies are now in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Palace_of_Madrid" title="Royal Palace of Madrid">Royal Palace of Madrid</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Museo_del_Prado" title="Museo del Prado">Museo del Prado</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During his time in Rome, Velázquez fathered a natural son, Antonio, whom he is not known ever to have seen.<sup id="cite_ref-Carr_247_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carr_247-55">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Return_to_Spain_and_later_career">Return to Spain and later career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Return to Spain and later career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From February 1650, Philip repeatedly sought Velázquez's return to Spain.<sup id="cite_ref-Carr_247_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carr_247-55">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> Accordingly, after visiting <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a>—where he saw his old friend Jose Ribera—and Venice, Velázquez returned to Spain via <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a> in 1651, taking with him many pictures and 300 pieces of statuary, which afterwards were arranged and catalogued for the king. Undraped sculpture was, however, abhorrent to the Spanish Church, and after Philip's death these works gradually disappeared.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
<a href="/wiki/Elisabeth_of_France_(1602%E2%80%931644)" title="Elisabeth of France (1602–1644)">Elisabeth of France</a> had died in 1644, and the king had married <a href="/wiki/Mariana_of_Austria" title="Mariana of Austria">Mariana of Austria</a>, whom Velázquez now painted in many attitudes. In 1652 he was specially chosen by the king to fill the high office of <i>aposentador mayor</i>, which imposed on him the duty of looking after the quarters occupied by the court—a responsible function which was no <a href="/wiki/Sinecure" title="Sinecure">sinecure</a> and one which interfered with the exercise of his art.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Yet far from indicating any decline, his works of this period are amongst the highest examples of his style.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Las_Meninas"><i>Las Meninas</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Las Meninas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Las_Meninas" title="Las Meninas">Las Meninas</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Las_Meninas,_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez,_from_Prado_in_Google_Earth.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Las_Meninas%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez%2C_from_Prado_in_Google_Earth.jpg/220px-Las_Meninas%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez%2C_from_Prado_in_Google_Earth.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="253" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Las_Meninas%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez%2C_from_Prado_in_Google_Earth.jpg/330px-Las_Meninas%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez%2C_from_Prado_in_Google_Earth.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Las_Meninas%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez%2C_from_Prado_in_Google_Earth.jpg/440px-Las_Meninas%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez%2C_from_Prado_in_Google_Earth.jpg 2x" data-file-width="26065" data-file-height="30000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Las_Meninas,_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez,_from_Prado_in_Google_Earth.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Las_Meninas" title="Las Meninas">Las Meninas</a></i> (1656)</div></div></div>
<p>One of the <i><a href="/wiki/Infanta" class="mw-redirect" title="Infanta">infantas</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Theresa_of_Spain" title="Margaret Theresa of Spain">Margaret Theresa</a>, the eldest daughter of the new Queen, appears to be the subject of <i><a href="/wiki/Las_Meninas" title="Las Meninas">Las Meninas</a></i> (1656, English: <i>The Maids of Honour</i>), Velázquez's <a href="/wiki/Masterpiece" title="Masterpiece">magnum opus</a>. 
Created four years before his death, it serves as an outstanding example of European <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">baroque</a> art. <a href="/wiki/Luca_Giordano" title="Luca Giordano">Luca Giordano</a>, a contemporary Italian painter, referred to it as the "theology of painting",<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> and in the eighteenth century the Englishman <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Lawrence_(painter)" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Lawrence (painter)">Thomas Lawrence</a> cited it as the "philosophy of art". However, it is unclear as to who or what is the true subject of the picture.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> Is it the royal daughter, or perhaps the painter himself? The king and queen are seen reflected in a mirror on the back wall, but the source of the reflection is a mystery: are the royal pair standing in the viewer's space, or does the mirror reflect the painting on which Velázquez is working? <a href="#References">Dale Brown</a> says Velázquez may have conceived the faded image of the king and queen on the back wall as a foreshadowing of the fall of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish Empire</a> that was to gain momentum following Philip's death.
</p><p>In the 1966 book <i>Les Mots et Les Choses</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/The_Order_of_Things" title="The Order of Things">The Order of Things</a></i>), philosopher <a href="/wiki/Michel_Foucault" title="Michel Foucault">Michel Foucault</a> devotes the opening chapter to a detailed analysis of <i>Las Meninas</i>. He describes the ways in which the painting problematizes issues of representation through its use of mirrors, screens, and the subsequent oscillations that occur between the image's interior, surface, and exterior.
</p><p>It is said the king painted the honorary <i><a href="/wiki/Cross_of_Saint_James" title="Cross of Saint James">Cross of Saint James</a></i> of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Santiago" title="Order of Santiago">Order of Santiago</a> on the breast of the painter as it appears today on the canvas. However, Velázquez did not receive this honor of <a href="/wiki/Knighthood" class="mw-redirect" title="Knighthood">knighthood</a> until three years after execution of this painting. Even the King of Spain could not make his favorite a belted knight without the consent of the commission established to inquire into the <a href="/wiki/Cleanliness_of_blood" class="mw-redirect" title="Cleanliness of blood">purity of his lineage</a>. The aim of these inquiries would be to prevent the appointment to positions of anyone found to have even a taint of heresy in their lineage—that is, a trace of Jewish or Moorish blood or contamination by trade or commerce in either side of the family for many generations. The records of this commission have been found among the archives of the <i>Order of Santiago</i>. Velázquez was awarded the honor in 1659. His occupation as plebeian and tradesman was justified because, as painter to the king, he was evidently not involved in the practice of "selling" pictures.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_years">Final years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Final years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Velazquez_figurando.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Velazquez_figurando.jpg/220px-Velazquez_figurando.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="251" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Velazquez_figurando.jpg/330px-Velazquez_figurando.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Velazquez_figurando.jpg/440px-Velazquez_figurando.jpg 2x" data-file-width="729" data-file-height="832" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Velazquez_figurando.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Detail of <i><a href="/wiki/Las_Meninas" title="Las Meninas">Las Meninas</a></i> (Velázquez's self-portrait)</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Diego_Rodriguez_de_Silva_y_Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_Infanta_Margarita_Teresa_in_a_Blue_Dress_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Diego_Rodriguez_de_Silva_y_Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_Infanta_Margarita_Teresa_in_a_Blue_Dress_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/220px-Diego_Rodriguez_de_Silva_y_Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_Infanta_Margarita_Teresa_in_a_Blue_Dress_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="262" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Diego_Rodriguez_de_Silva_y_Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_Infanta_Margarita_Teresa_in_a_Blue_Dress_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/330px-Diego_Rodriguez_de_Silva_y_Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_Infanta_Margarita_Teresa_in_a_Blue_Dress_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Diego_Rodriguez_de_Silva_y_Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_Infanta_Margarita_Teresa_in_a_Blue_Dress_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/440px-Diego_Rodriguez_de_Silva_y_Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_Infanta_Margarita_Teresa_in_a_Blue_Dress_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5360" data-file-height="6374" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Diego_Rodriguez_de_Silva_y_Vel%C3%A1zquez_-_Infanta_Margarita_Teresa_in_a_Blue_Dress_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Portrait of the eight-year-old <i>Infanta Margarita Teresa in a Blue Dress</i> (1659)</div></div></div>
<p>There were essentially only two patrons of art in Spain—the church and the art-loving king and court. <a href="/wiki/Bartolom%C3%A9_Esteban_Murillo" title="Bartolomé Esteban Murillo">Bartolomé Esteban Murillo</a>, who toiled for a rich and powerful church, left little means to pay for his burial, while Velázquez lived and died in the enjoyment of a good salary and pension.
</p><p>One of his final works was <a href="/wiki/Las_Hilanderas_(Vel%C3%A1zquez)" title="Las Hilanderas (Velázquez)"><i>Las hilanderas</i> (<i>The Spinners</i>)</a>, painted circa 1657, a depiction of Ovid's Fable of <a href="/wiki/Arachne" title="Arachne">Arachne</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sánchez_Grove_18-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sánchez_Grove-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The tapestry in the background is based on <a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titian</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rape_of_Europa_(Titian)" title="The Rape of Europa (Titian)">The Rape of Europa</a></i>, or, more probably, the copy that <a href="/wiki/Peter_Paul_Rubens" title="Peter Paul Rubens">Rubens</a> painted in <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> It is full of light, air and movement, featuring vibrant colors and careful handling. <a href="/wiki/Anton_Raphael_Mengs" title="Anton Raphael Mengs">Anton Raphael Mengs</a> said this work seemed to have been painted not by the hand but by the pure force of will. It displays a concentration of all the art-knowledge Velázquez had gathered during his long artistic career of more than forty years. The scheme is simple—a confluence of varied and blended red, bluish-green, gray and black.
</p><p>Velázquez's final portraits of the royal children are among his finest works and in the <i><a href="/wiki/Infanta_Margarita_Teresa_in_a_Blue_Dress" title="Infanta Margarita Teresa in a Blue Dress">Infanta Margarita Teresa in a Blue Dress</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-bilddatenbank.khm.at_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bilddatenbank.khm.at-61">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> the painter's personal style reached its high-point: shimmering spots of color on wide painting surfaces produce an almost impressionistic effect – the viewer must stand at a suitable distance to get the impression of complete, three-dimensional spatiality.
</p><p>His only surviving portrait of the delicate and sickly Prince Felipe Prospero<sup id="cite_ref-Kunsthistorisches_Museum_1661_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kunsthistorisches_Museum_1661-62">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> is remarkable for its combination of the sweet features of the child prince and his dog with a subtle sense of gloom. The hope that was placed at that time in the sole heir to the Spanish crown is reflected in the depiction: fresh red and white stand in contrast to late autumnal, morbid colors. A small dog with wide eyes looks at the viewer as if questioningly, and the largely pale background hints at a gloomy fate: the little prince was barely four years old when he died. As in all of the artist's late paintings, the handling of the colors is extraordinarily fluid and vibrant.
</p><p>In 1660 a peace treaty between France and Spain was consummated by the marriage of <a href="/wiki/Maria_Theresa_of_Spain" title="Maria Theresa of Spain">Maria Theresa</a> with <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">Louis XIV</a>, and the ceremony took place on the Island of Pheasants, a small swampy island in the <a href="/wiki/Bidassoa" class="mw-redirect" title="Bidassoa">Bidassoa</a>. Velázquez was charged with the decoration of the Spanish pavilion and with the entire scenic display. He attracted much attention from the nobility of his bearing and the splendor of his costume. On June 26 he returned to Madrid, and on July 31 he was stricken with fever. Feeling his end approaching, he signed his will, appointing as his sole executors his wife and his firm friend named Fuensalida, keeper of the royal records. He died on August 6, 1660. He was buried in the Fuensalida vault of the church of <a href="/wiki/Saint_John_the_Baptist" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint John the Baptist">San Juan Bautista</a>, and within eight days his wife Juana was buried beside him. This church was destroyed by the French around 1809, so his place of interment is now unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There was much difficulty in adjusting the tangled accounts outstanding between Velázquez and the treasury, and it was not until 1666, after the death of King Philip, that they were finally settled.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style_and_technique">Style and technique</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Style and technique">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is canonical to divide Velázquez's career by his two visits to Italy.  He rarely signed his pictures, and the royal archives give the dates of only his most important works. Internal evidence and history pertaining to his portraits supply the rest to a certain extent.
</p><p>Although acquainted with all the Italian schools and a friend of the foremost painters of his day, Velázquez was strong enough to withstand external influences and work out for himself the development of his own nature and his own principles of art. He rejected the pomp that characterized the portraiture of other European courts, and instead brought an even greater reserve to the understated formula for Habsburg portraiture established by Titian, <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Mor" class="mw-redirect" title="Antonio Mor">Antonio Mor</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alonso_S%C3%A1nchez_Coello" title="Alonso Sánchez Coello">Alonso Sánchez Coello</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> He is known for using a rather limited palette, but he mixed the available paints with great skill to achieve varying hues.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> His <a href="/wiki/Pigments" class="mw-redirect" title="Pigments">pigments</a> were not significantly different from those of his contemporaries and he mainly employed <a href="/wiki/Azurite" title="Azurite">azurite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Smalt" class="mw-redirect" title="Smalt">smalt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vermilion" title="Vermilion">vermilion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carmine" title="Carmine">red lake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lead-tin-yellow" title="Lead-tin-yellow">lead-tin-yellow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ochres" class="mw-redirect" title="Ochres">ochres</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> His early works were painted on canvases prepared with a red-brown ground. He adopted the use of light-gray grounds during his first trip to Italy, and continued using them for the rest of his life.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> The change resulted in paintings with greater luminosity and a generally cool, silvery range of color.<sup id="cite_ref-Carr_2006_p79_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carr_2006_p79-68">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Few drawings are securely attributed to Velázquez.<sup id="cite_ref-McKim-Smith_Dec_1979_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McKim-Smith_Dec_1979-69">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> Although preparatory drawings for some of his paintings exist, his method was to paint directly from life, and x-rays of his paintings reveal that he frequently made changes in his composition as a painting progressed.<sup id="cite_ref-McKim-Smith_Dec_1979_69-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McKim-Smith_Dec_1979-69">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Velázquez was not prolific; he is estimated to have produced between only 110 and 120 known canvases.<sup id="cite_ref-vogel_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vogel-70">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> He produced no etchings or engravings, and only a few drawings are attributed to him.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Velázquez is the most influential figure in the history of Spanish portraiture.<sup id="cite_ref-Portus_57_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Portus_57-72">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> In the decades after his death, Spanish court painters such as his son-in-law <a href="/wiki/Juan_Bautista_Martinez_del_Mazo" class="mw-redirect" title="Juan Bautista Martinez del Mazo">Juan Bautista Martinez del Mazo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Juan_Carre%C3%B1o_de_Miranda" title="Juan Carreño de Miranda">Juan Carreño de Miranda</a> took inspiration from his work.<sup id="cite_ref-Portus_57_72-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Portus_57-72">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> Mazo closely mimicked his style and many paintings and copies by Mazo were formerly attributed to Velázquez.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> Velázquez had few immediate followers, however. In the eighteenth century, Spanish court portraiture was dominated by artists of foreign birth and training, and Velázquez's reputation languished. In the final decades of the century, his importance was increasingly recognized by intellectuals close to the Spanish court—an essay published In 1781 by <a href="/wiki/Gaspar_Melchor_de_Jovellanos" title="Gaspar Melchor de Jovellanos">Gaspar Melchor de Jovellanos</a> said of Velázquez that "when he died, the glory of Painting in Spain died with him."<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> In 1778, <a href="/wiki/Goya" class="mw-redirect" title="Goya">Goya</a> made a set of etchings after paintings by Velázquez, as part of a project by the <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Mo%C3%B1ino,_1st_Count_of_Floridablanca" title="José Moñino, 1st Count of Floridablanca">Count of Floridablanca</a> to produce prints of paintings in the Royal Collection.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> Goya's free copies reveal a searching engagement with the older master's work, which remained a model for Goya for the rest of his career.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Until the nineteenth century, little was known outside of Spain of Velázquez's work.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> His paintings mostly escaped being stolen by the French marshals during the <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_War" title="Peninsular War">Peninsular War</a>. In 1828, Sir <a href="/wiki/David_Wilkie_(artist)" title="David Wilkie (artist)">David Wilkie</a> wrote from Madrid that he felt himself in the presence of a new power in art as he looked at the works of Velázquez, and at the same time found a wonderful affinity between this artist and the British school of portrait painters, especially <a href="/wiki/Henry_Raeburn" title="Henry Raeburn">Henry Raeburn</a>. He was struck by the modern impression pervading Velázquez's work in both landscape and portraiture.
</p><p>Velázquez is often cited as a key influence on the art of <a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Manet" title="Édouard Manet">Édouard Manet</a>, who is often considered the bridge between realism and <a href="/wiki/Impressionism" title="Impressionism">impressionism</a>. Calling Velázquez the "painter of painters",<sup id="cite_ref-Carr_2006_p79_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carr_2006_p79-68">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> Manet admired the immediacy and vivid brushwork of Velázquez's work, and built upon Velázquez's motifs in his own art.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> In the  late nineteenth century, artists such as <a href="/wiki/James_McNeill_Whistler" class="mw-redirect" title="James McNeill Whistler">James McNeill Whistler</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Singer_Sargent" title="John Singer Sargent">John Singer Sargent</a> were strongly influenced by Velázquez.<sup id="cite_ref-Sánchez_Grove_18-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sánchez_Grove-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_recreations_of_classics">Modern recreations of classics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Modern recreations of classics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The respect with which twentieth century painters regard Velázquez's work attests to its continuing importance. Pablo Picasso paid homage to Velázquez in 1957 when he recreated <i>Las Meninas</i> in 44 variations, in his characteristic style.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> Although Picasso was concerned that his reinterpretations of Velázquez's painting would be seen merely as copies rather than unique representations, the enormous works—including the largest he had produced since <i>Guernica</i> in 1937—obtained a position of importance in the canon of Spanish art.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Salvador_Dal%C3%AD" title="Salvador Dalí">Salvador Dalí</a>, as with Picasso in anticipation of the tercentennial of Velázquez's death, created in 1958 a work entitled <i> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vel%C3%A1zquez_Painting_the_Infanta_Margarita_With_the_Lights_and_Shadows_of_His_Own_Glory&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Velázquez Painting the Infanta Margarita With the Lights and Shadows of His Own Glory (page does not exist)">Velázquez Painting the Infanta Margarita With the Lights and Shadows of His Own Glory</a></i>. The color scheme shows Dalí's serious tribute to Velázquez; the work also functioned, as in Picasso's case, as a vehicle for the presentation of newer theories in art and thought—nuclear mysticism, in Dalí's case.
</p><p>The Anglo-Irish painter <a href="/wiki/Francis_Bacon_(artist)" title="Francis Bacon (artist)">Francis Bacon</a> found Velázquez's portrait of Pope Innocent X to be "one of the greatest portraits ever".<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> He created several <a href="/wiki/Expressionism" title="Expressionism">expressionist</a> variations of this piece in the 1950s; however, Bacon's paintings presented a more gruesome image of Innocent. One such famous variation, entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Figure_with_Meat" title="Figure with Meat">Figure with Meat</a></i> (1954), shows the pope between two halves of a bisected cow.
</p>
<h3><span id="Recent_rediscoveries_of_Vel.C3.A1zquez_originals"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_rediscoveries_of_Velázquez_originals">Recent rediscoveries of Velázquez originals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Recent rediscoveries of Velázquez originals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2009, the <i><a href="/wiki/Portrait_of_a_Man_(Vel%C3%A1zquez)" title="Portrait of a Man (Velázquez)">Portrait of a Man</a></i> in the collection of the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art" title="Metropolitan Museum of Art">Metropolitan Museum of Art</a>, which had long been associated with the followers of Velázquez' style of painting, was cleaned and restored. It was found to be by Velázquez himself, and the features of the man match those of a figure in the painting "the Surrender of Breda". The newly cleaned canvas may therefore be a study for that painting. Although the attribution to Velázquez is regarded as certain, the identity of the sitter is still open to question. Some art historians regard this new study to be a self-portrait by Velázquez.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2010 it was reported that a damaged painting long relegated to a basement of the <a href="/wiki/Yale_University_Art_Gallery" title="Yale University Art Gallery">Yale University Art Gallery</a> might be an early work by Velázquez. Thought to have been given to Yale in 1925, the painting has previously been attributed to the 17th-century Spanish school. Some scholars are prepared to attribute the painting to Velázquez, though the <a href="/wiki/Prado_Museum" class="mw-redirect" title="Prado Museum">Prado Museum</a> in Madrid is reserving judgment. The work, which depicts the Virgin Mary being taught to read, will be restored by conservators at Yale.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2011 it was confirmed by art historian Dr. Peter Cherry of Trinity College Dublin through x-ray analysis that a portrait found in the UK in the former collection of the 19th-century painter <a href="/w/index.php?title=Matthew_Shepperson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Matthew Shepperson (page does not exist)">Matthew Shepperson</a> is a previously unknown work by Velázquez. The portrait is of an unidentified man in his fifties or sixties, who could possibly be Juan Mateos, the Master of the Hunt for Velázquez's patron, <a href="/wiki/King_Philip_IV_of_Spain" class="mw-redirect" title="King Philip IV of Spain">King Philip IV of Spain</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> The painting measures 47 x 39&#160;cm and was sold at auction on December 7, 2011, for £3,000,000.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Descendants">Descendants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Descendants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Velázquez, through his daughter Francisca de Silva Velázquez y Pacheco (1619–1658), is an ancestor of the Marquesses of Monteleone, including Enriquetta (Henrietta) Casado de Monteleone (1725–1761) who in 1746 married Heinrich VI, Count <a href="/wiki/Reuss_Younger_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Reuss Younger Line">Reuss zu Köstritz</a> (1707–1783). Through them are descended a number of European royalty, among them <a href="/wiki/King_Felipe_VI_of_Spain" class="mw-redirect" title="King Felipe VI of Spain">King Felipe VI of Spain</a> through his mother <a href="/wiki/Sophia_of_Greece_and_Denmark" class="mw-redirect" title="Sophia of Greece and Denmark">Sophia of Greece and Denmark</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/King_Willem-Alexander_of_the_Netherlands" class="mw-redirect" title="King Willem-Alexander of the Netherlands">King Willem-Alexander of the Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carl_XVI_Gustaf_of_Sweden" title="Carl XVI Gustaf of Sweden">King Carl XVI Gustaf of Sweden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Albert_II_of_Belgium" title="Albert II of Belgium">King Albert II of Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hans-Adam_II,_Prince_of_Liechtenstein" title="Hans-Adam II, Prince of Liechtenstein">Hans-Adam II, Prince of Liechtenstein</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Henri,_Grand_Duke_of_Luxembourg" title="Henri, Grand Duke of Luxembourg">Henri, Grand Duke of Luxembourg</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
