<p><b>Robinson</b> is an <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a> <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a> <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a>, originating in England. It means "son of <a href="/wiki/Robin_(name)" title="Robin (name)">Robin</a> (a diminutive of <a href="/wiki/Robert" title="Robert">Robert</a>)". There are similar surname spellings such as <a href="/wiki/Robison_(name)" title="Robison (name)">Robison</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robeson_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Robeson (disambiguation)">Robeson</a>. Robinson is the 15th-most common surname in the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-AFG_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AFG-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> According to the 1990 United States Census, Robinson was the twentieth most frequently encountered surname among those reported, accounting for 0.23% of the population.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Ireland, Robinson is only really common in <a href="/wiki/Ulster" title="Ulster">Ulster</a>. The two names had been used interchangeably in some areas of the province around the beginning of the 20th century. It can also be an Anglicization of such Jewish surnames as Rabinowitz and Rubinstein.
</p><p>Robinson, the compound word, is a rare <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a>, while its derivative, Robin, has the distinction of being both a masculine and feminine given name.
