	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Short stature</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p><b>Short stature</b> refers to a <a href="/wiki/Height_of_a_human_being" class="mw-redirect" title="Height of a human being">height of a human being</a> which is below typical. Whether a person is considered short depends on the context.  Because of the lack of preciseness, there is often disagreement about the degree of shortness that should be called <i>short</i>.
</p><p>In a medical context, short stature is typically defined as an adult height that is more than two <a href="/wiki/Standard_deviation" title="Standard deviation">standard deviations</a> below the <a href="/wiki/Mean_(statistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mean (statistics)">mean</a> for age and gender, which corresponds to the shortest 2.3% of individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  In developed countries, this typically includes adult men who are shorter than 166 centimetres (5&#160;ft 5&#160;in) tall and adult women who are shorter than 153 centimetres (5&#160;ft 0&#160;in) tall. By comparison, the median or typical adult height in these populations (as the widely abundant statistics from these countries clearly state) is about 177 centimetres (5&#160;ft 10&#160;in) for men and 164 centimetres (5&#160;ft 5&#160;in) for women.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Short_stature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Shortness in children and young adults nearly always results from below-average <a href="/wiki/Human_development_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Human development (biology)">growth</a> in childhood, while shortness in older adults usually results from loss of height due to <a href="/wiki/Kyphosis" title="Kyphosis">kyphosis</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Vertebral_column" title="Vertebral column">spine</a> or collapsed <a href="/wiki/Vertebra" title="Vertebra">vertebrae</a> from <a href="/wiki/Osteoporosis" title="Osteoporosis">osteoporosis</a>. The most common causes of short stature in childhood are constitutional growth delay or familial short stature. 
</p><p>From a medical perspective, severe shortness can be a <a href="/wiki/Human_variability" title="Human variability">variation</a> of normal, resulting from the interplay of multiple familial genes. It can also be due to one or more of many abnormal conditions, such as chronic (prolonged) <a href="/wiki/Growth_hormone" title="Growth hormone">growth hormone</a> or <a href="/wiki/Thyroid_hormone" class="mw-redirect" title="Thyroid hormone">thyroid hormone</a> deficiency, <a href="/wiki/Malnutrition" title="Malnutrition">malnutrition</a>, disease of a major organ system, mistreatment, treatment with certain drugs, <a href="/wiki/Chromosome" title="Chromosome">chromosomal</a> deletions. <a href="/wiki/Human_growth_hormone" class="mw-redirect" title="Human growth hormone">Human growth hormone</a> (HGH) deficiency may occur at any time during infancy or childhood, with the most obvious sign being a noticeable slowing of growth. The deficiency may be genetic. Among children without <a href="/wiki/Growth_hormone_deficiency" title="Growth hormone deficiency">growth hormone deficiency</a>, short stature may be caused by <a href="/wiki/Turner_syndrome" title="Turner syndrome">Turner syndrome</a> or <a href="/wiki/Noonan_syndrome" title="Noonan syndrome">Noonan syndrome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chronic_kidney_disease" title="Chronic kidney disease">chronic kidney disease</a>, being <a href="/wiki/Small_for_gestational_age" title="Small for gestational age">small for gestational age</a> at birth, <a href="/wiki/Prader%E2%80%93Willi_syndrome" title="Prader–Willi syndrome">Prader–Willi syndrome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wiedemann-Steiner_syndrome" class="mw-redirect" title="Wiedemann-Steiner syndrome">Wiedemann-Steiner syndrome</a>, or other conditions. Genetic <a href="/wiki/Skeletal_dysplasias" class="mw-redirect" title="Skeletal dysplasias">skeletal dysplasias</a> also known as <a href="/wiki/Osteochondrodysplasia" title="Osteochondrodysplasia">osteochondrodysplasia</a> usually manifest in short-limbed disproportionate short stature.<sup id="cite_ref-gamal2017_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gamal2017-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Panda2014_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Panda2014-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When the cause is unknown, it is called <i><a href="/wiki/Idiopathic_short_stature" title="Idiopathic short stature">idiopathic short stature</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Allen_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allen-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
Short stature can also be caused by the bone plates fusing at an earlier age than normal, therefore stunting growth. Normally, the <a href="/wiki/Bone_age" title="Bone age">bone age</a> is the same as the biological age but for some people, it is older. For many people with advanced bone ages, they hit a growth spurt early on which propels them to average height but stop growing at an earlier age. However, in some cases, people who are naturally shorter combined with their advanced bone age, end up being even shorter than the height they normally would have been because of their stunted growth.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Short_stature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chronic illnesses, malnutrition, endocrine, metabolic disorders or chromosomal anomalies  are characterized by proportionate short stature. 
On the other hand, most genetic <a href="/wiki/Skeletal_dysplasias" class="mw-redirect" title="Skeletal dysplasias">skeletal dysplasias</a> are known for short stature that may be proportionate or disproportionate. Disproportionate short stature   can be further subdivided as specified by the body segments affected by shortening, namely limbs versus trunk:<sup id="cite_ref-gamal2017_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gamal2017-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Panda2014_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Panda2014-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Short-limb short stature in which there is limb shortening as achondroplasia, hypochondroplasia, pseudoachondroplasia and multiple epiphyseal dysplasia.</li>
<li>Short-trunk short stature in which there is trunk shortening as spondyloepiphyseal dysplasia and  mucopolysaccharidosis</li></ul>
<p>Short-limb short stature can be further subcategorised in accordance with limb segment affected by shortening. These subcategories of limb shortening include, rhizomelic (humerus and femur), mesomelic (radius, ulna, tibia and fibula) and acromelic (hands and feet). Anthropometric measurements provide are very beneficial tools to the diagnostic process of genetic <a href="/wiki/Skeletal_dysplasias" class="mw-redirect" title="Skeletal dysplasias">skeletal dysplasias</a>. The anthropometric measurements include height, sitting height, arm span, upper/ lower-body segment ratio, sitting height/height ratio, and arm span/height ratio for age. They also aid in the differential diagnosis of <a href="/wiki/Skeletal_dysplasia" class="mw-redirect" title="Skeletal dysplasia">skeletal dysplasia</a> subtypes.<sup id="cite_ref-gamal2017_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gamal2017-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Short_stature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The decision to treat is based on a belief that the child will be disabled by being extremely short as an adult, so that the risks of treatment (including sudden death<sup id="cite_ref-Allen_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allen-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>) will outweigh the risks of not treating the <a href="/wiki/Symptom" title="Symptom">symptom</a> of short stature.  Although short children commonly report being teased about their height, most adults who are very short are not physically or psychologically disabled by their height.<sup id="cite_ref-Allen_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allen-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> However, there is some evidence to suggest that there is an inverse linear relationship with height and with risk of suicide.<sup id="cite_ref-psychiatry_online_Strong_Inverse_Association_Between_Height_and_Suicide_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-psychiatry_online_Strong_Inverse_Association_Between_Height_and_Suicide-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Treatment is expensive and requires many years of injections with human growth hormones.  The result depends on the cause, but is typically an increase in final height of about 5 to 10 centimetres (2.0 to 3.9&#160;in) taller than predicted.<sup id="cite_ref-Allen_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allen-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  Thus, treatment takes a child who is expected to be much shorter than a typical adult and produces an adult who is still obviously shorter than average.  For example, several years of successful treatment in a girl who is predicted to be 145 centimetres (4&#160;ft 9&#160;in) as an adult may result in her being 150 centimetres (4&#160;ft 11&#160;in) instead.
</p><p>Increasing final height in children with short stature may be beneficial and could enhance health-related quality of life outcomes, barring troublesome side effects and excessive cost of treatments.<sup id="cite_ref-urlHeight_and_Health-related_Quality_of_Life_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urlHeight_and_Health-related_Quality_of_Life-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cost">Cost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Short_stature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The cost of treatment depends on the amount of growth hormone given, which in turn depends on the child's weight and age.  One year's worth of drugs normally costs about US$20,000 for a small child and over $50,000 for a teenager.<sup id="cite_ref-Allen_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allen-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  These drugs are normally taken for five or more years.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_issues">Cultural issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Short_stature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cultural issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From a social perspective shortness can be a problem independently of the cause. In many societies there are advantages associated with taller stature and disadvantages associated with shorter stature, and vice versa.
</p><p>Pharmaceutical companies <a href="/wiki/Genentech" title="Genentech">Genentech</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eli_Lilly_and_Company" title="Eli Lilly and Company">Eli Lilly</a>, makers of human growth hormone, have worked to <a href="/wiki/Medicalize" class="mw-redirect" title="Medicalize">medicalize</a> short stature by convincing the public that short stature is a <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">disease</a> rather than a natural variation in human height.<sup id="cite_ref-Brody_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brody-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  Limiting sales of the hormone to children diagnosed with <a href="/wiki/Growth_hormone_deficiency" title="Growth hormone deficiency">growth hormone deficiency</a>, rather than being short for any reason, limited their sales market.<sup id="cite_ref-Brody_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brody-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  Expanding it to all children whose height was below the third percentile would create 90,000 new customers and US$10 billion in revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-Valenstein_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Valenstein-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  In the early 1990s, they paid two US charities, the Human Growth Foundation and the <a href="/wiki/MAGIC_Foundation" title="MAGIC Foundation">MAGIC Foundation</a>, to measure the height of thousands of American children in schools and public places, and to send letters urging medical consultations for children whose height was deemed low.<sup id="cite_ref-Brody_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brody-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  Parents and schools were not told that the charities were being paid by the drug companies to do this.<sup id="cite_ref-Brody_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brody-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Valenstein_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Valenstein-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Paired with a campaign to advertise the hormone to physicians, the campaign was successful, and tens of thousands of children began receiving HGH.<sup id="cite_ref-Valenstein_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Valenstein-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  About half of them do not have <a href="/wiki/Growth_hormone_deficiency" title="Growth hormone deficiency">growth hormone deficiency</a>, and consequently benefited very little, if at all, from the hormone injections.<sup id="cite_ref-Valenstein_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Valenstein-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  Criticism of the <a href="/wiki/Universal_screening" class="mw-redirect" title="Universal screening">universal screening</a> program eventually resulted in its end.<sup id="cite_ref-Valenstein_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Valenstein-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Advantage">Advantage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Short_stature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Advantage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Short stature decreases the risk of <a href="/wiki/Chronic_venous_insufficiency" title="Chronic venous insufficiency">venous insufficiency</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Short_stature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> in Britain, the minimum height for soldiers was 5&#160;feet 3&#160;inches (160&#160;cm). Thus thousands of men under this height were denied the ability to fight in the war. As a result of pressure to allow them entry, special "<a href="/wiki/Bantam_(military)" title="Bantam (military)">Bantam Battalions</a>" were created composed of men who were 4&#160;feet 10&#160;inches (147&#160;cm) to 5&#160;feet 3&#160;inches (160&#160;cm). By the end of the war there were 29 Bantam Battalions of about 1,000 men each. Officers were of normal size.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
