<p><b>Pan-European identity</b> is the sense of personal identification with <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, in a cultural or political sense. The concept is discussed in the context of <a href="/wiki/European_integration" title="European integration">European integration</a>, 
historically in connection with <a href="/wiki/Ideas_of_European_unity_before_1945" title="Ideas of European unity before 1945">hypothetical proposals</a>, but since the formation of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> (EU) in the 1990s increasingly with regards to the project of ever-increasing <a href="/wiki/Federalisation_of_the_European_Union" title="Federalisation of the European Union">federalisation of the EU</a>.
</p><p><i>Pan-European identity</i> has roots as far back as the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, when poet and political advisor <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante Alighieri</a> claimed, "My country is the whole world."<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Much of its foundational definition emerged during <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">the Renaissance</a>. Artists and scholars of that period collaborated across national boundaries, travelling to centres of activity in their respective fields and believing that freedom came from common bonds and individualism in a way that transcended national allegiances.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Developing of <i>Pan-European identity</i> continued during <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">the Enlightenment</a>. During this time, leading philosophical and political thinkers in Europe articulated a form of nationalism that acknowledged local cultural differences while incorporating a sense of shared, universal values based on the application of <a href="/wiki/Reason" title="Reason">reason</a>. Towards the end of the Eighteenth Century, philosopher <a href="/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Rousseau" title="Jean-Jacques Rousseau">Jean-Jacques Rousseau</a> remarked that "there are no longer any Frenchmen, Germans, Spaniards, or even Englishmen; there are only Europeans."<sup id="cite_ref-Davies_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davies-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Irish statesman and political theorist <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Burke" title="Edmund Burke">Edmund Burke</a> spoke of a "European Commonwealth" brought together by commercial and economic bonds, and in 1796 wrote: "No citizen of Europe could be altogether an exile in any part of it... When a man travelled or resided for health, pleasure, business or necessity, from his country, he never felt himself quite abroad."<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Davies_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davies-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The model of a "pan-European" union is the <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_Empire" title="Carolingian Empire">Carolingian Empire</a>, which united "Europe" in the sense of <a href="/wiki/Latin_Church" title="Latin Church">Latin Christendom</a>.
</p><p>The original proposal for a <a href="/wiki/Paneuropean_Union" title="Paneuropean Union">Paneuropean Union</a> was made in 1922 by <a href="/wiki/Richard_von_Coudenhove-Kalergi" title="Richard von Coudenhove-Kalergi">Richard von Coudenhove-Kalergi</a>. The term "Pan-European" is to be understood not as referring to the modern geographic definition of the <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">continent of Europe</a> but in the historical sense of the western parts of continental Europe sharing the common history of <a href="/wiki/Latin_Church" title="Latin Church">Latin Christendom</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_Empire" title="Carolingian Empire">Carolingian Empire</a> and the early modern <a href="/wiki/House_of_Habsburg#Holy_Roman_Emperors" title="House of Habsburg">Habsburg Empire</a>. Coudenhove-Kalergi saw the Pan-European state as a future "fifth great power", in explicit opposition to the Soviet Union, "Asia", Great Britain and the United States (as such explicitly excluding both the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a> from his notion of "Pan-European").<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After 1945, an accelerating process of <a href="/wiki/European_integration" title="European integration">European integration</a> culminated in the formation of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> (EU) in 1993. 
In the period of 1995&#8211;2013, the EU has been <a href="/wiki/Enlargement_of_the_European_Union" title="Enlargement of the European Union">enlarged</a> from 12 to 28 member states, far beyond the area originally envisaged for the "pan-European" state by Coudenhove-Kalergi (with the exception of <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>), its member states accounting for a population of some 510 million, or two thirds of the population of the entire continent.
</p><p>In the 1990s to 2000s, there was an active movement towards a <a href="/wiki/Federalisation_of_the_European_Union" title="Federalisation of the European Union">federalisation of the European Union</a>, with the introduction of symbols and institutions usually reserved for sovereign states, such as
<a href="/wiki/Citizenship_of_the_European_Union" title="Citizenship of the European Union">citizenship</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Eurozone" title="Eurozone">common currency</a> (used by 19 out of 28 members), a <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Europe" title="Flag of Europe">flag</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Anthem_of_Europe" title="Anthem of Europe">anthem</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Motto_of_the_European_Union" title="Motto of the European Union">motto</a> (<i>In Varietate Concordia</i>, "United in Diversity"). 
An attempt to introduce a <a href="/wiki/Treaty_establishing_a_Constitution_for_Europe" title="Treaty establishing a Constitution for Europe">European Constitution</a> was made in 2004, but it failed to be ratified; instead, the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Lisbon" title="Treaty of Lisbon">Treaty of Lisbon</a> was signed in 2007 in order to salvage some of the reforms that had been envisaged in the constitution.
</p><p>A debate on the feasibility and desirability of a "pan-European identity" or "European identity" has taken place in parallel to this process of political integration. 
The ideology of <a href="/wiki/Pan-European_nationalism" title="Pan-European nationalism">pan-European nationalism</a>, which had been a hallmark of <a href="/wiki/Neo-fascist" class="mw-redirect" title="Neo-fascist">neo-fascist</a> or <a href="/wiki/Far-right" class="mw-redirect" title="Far-right">far-right</a> currents of European politics during the 1950s to 1970s, has been largely abandoned in favour of a resurgence of national identity paired with "<a href="/wiki/Euroscepticism" title="Euroscepticism">Euroscepticism</a>", while the proponents of European integration do not connect the "European idea" with nationalism, but rather with a "postmodern world order" characterised by "diversity of identity" combined with a "commonality of values",<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> while the remaining loyalties to national or cultural identities are seen as a threat to the "supranational prospect" of European integration.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A possible future "European identity" is seen at best as one aspect of a "multifaceted identity" still involving national or regional loyalties. Two authors writing in 1998 concluded that "In the short-term it seems that the influence of this project [of European integration] will only influence European identity in certain limited niches and in a very modest way. It is doubtful if this will do to ensure a smooth process of
ongoing European integration and successfully address the challenges of the multicultural European societies."<sup id="cite_ref-Utrecht_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Utrecht-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Even at that time, the development of a common European identity was viewed as rather a by-product than the main goal of the European integration process, even though it was actively promoted by both EU bodies and non-governmental initiatives, such as the <a href="/wiki/Directorate-General_for_Education_and_Culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Directorate-General for Education and Culture">Directorate-General for Education and Culture</a> of the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a>.
<sup id="cite_ref-Utrecht_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Utrecht-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
With the rise of EU-scepticism and opposition to continued European integration by the early 2010s, the feasibility and desirability of such a "European identity" has been called into question.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pan-European_nationalism" title="Pan-European nationalism">Pan-European nationalism</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Ideas_of_European_unity_before_1945" title="Ideas of European unity before 1945">Ideas of European unity before 1945</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_European_Union" title="History of the European Union">History of the European Union</a></div>
<p>Pan-Europeanism, as it emerged in the wake of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, derived a sense of European identity from the idea of a <a href="/wiki/History_of_Europe" title="History of Europe">shared history</a>, taken to be the source of a set of fundamental "European values".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Typically<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> the 'common history' includes a combination of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greece</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Ancient Rome</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudalism</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic League</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Age of Enlightenment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Classical_liberalism" title="Classical liberalism">19th century liberalism</a> and different forms of <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Secularism" title="Secularism">secularism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">colonialism</a> and the <a href="/wiki/World_Wars" class="mw-redirect" title="World Wars">World Wars</a>.
</p><p>The oldest European unification movement is the <a href="/wiki/Paneuropean_Union" title="Paneuropean Union">Paneuropean Union</a>, founded in 1923 with the publishment of <a href="/wiki/Richard_von_Coudenhove-Kalergi" title="Richard von Coudenhove-Kalergi">Richard von Coudenhove-Kalergi</a>'s book <i>Paneuropa</i>, who also became its first president (1926–1972), followed by <a href="/wiki/Otto_von_Habsburg" title="Otto von Habsburg">Otto von Habsburg</a> (1973–2004) and <a href="/wiki/Alain_Terrenoire" title="Alain Terrenoire">Alain Terrenoire</a> (from 2004).
This movement initiated and supported the "<a href="/wiki/European_integration" title="European integration">integration process</a>" pursued after World War II, which eventually led to the formation of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>. 
Notable "Paneuropeans" include <a href="/wiki/Konrad_Adenauer" title="Konrad Adenauer">Konrad Adenauer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Schuman" title="Robert Schuman">Robert Schuman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alcide_De_Gasperi" title="Alcide De Gasperi">Alcide De Gasperi</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="European_values"><i>European values</i><span id="European_values"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pan-European_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: European values">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Europeanism" title="Europeanism">Europeanism</a> and <a href="/wiki/European_integration" title="European integration">European integration</a></div>
<p>Especially in France, "the European idea" (<i>l'idée d'Europe</i>) is associated with <a href="/wiki/Value_(ethics)" title="Value (ethics)">political values</a> derived from the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Age of Enlightenment</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Republicanism" title="Republicanism">Republicanism</a> growing out of the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_of_1848" title="Revolutions of 1848">Revolutions of 1848</a> rather than with personal or individual identity formed by culture or ethnicity (let alone a "pan-European" construct including those areas of the continent never affected by 18th-century rationalism or Republicanism).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The phrase "European values" arises as a political neologism in the 1980s in the context of the project of <a href="/wiki/European_integration" title="European integration">European integration</a> and the future formation of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>.
The phrase was popularised by the <a href="/wiki/European_Values_Study" class="mw-redirect" title="European Values Study">European Values Study</a>, a long term research program started in 1981, aiming to document the outlook on "basic human values"  in European populations. The 
project had grown out of a study group on "values and social change in Europe" initiated by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jan_Kerkhofs&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jan Kerkhofs (page does not exist)">Jan Kerkhofs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ruud_de_Moor" title="Ruud de Moor">Ruud de Moor</a> (<a href="/wiki/Catholic_University_in_Tilburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic University in Tilburg">Catholic University in Tilburg</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
The claim that the people of Europe have a distinctive set of political, economic and social norms and values which are gradually replacing national values has also been named "Europeanism" by McCormick (2010).<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>"European values" were contrasted to non-European values in international relations, especially in the <a href="/wiki/East%E2%80%93West_dichotomy" title="East–West dichotomy">East–West dichotomy</a>, "European values" encompassing individualism and the idea of <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> in contrast to Eastern tendencies of collectivism. However, "European values" were also viewed critically, their "darker" side not necessarily leading more peaceful outcomes in international relations.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The association of "European values" with <a href="/wiki/European_integration" title="European integration">European integration</a> as pursued by the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> came to the fore with the <a href="/wiki/Enlargement_of_the_European_Union#Eastern_enlargement" title="Enlargement of the European Union">eastern enlargement</a> of the EU in the aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>.
<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Lisbon" title="Treaty of Lisbon">Treaty of Lisbon</a> (2007) in article 2 lists a number of "values of the Union", 
including "respect for <a href="/wiki/Freedom" title="Freedom">freedom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democracy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_equality" title="Social equality">equality</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_law" title="Rule of law">rule of law</a> and respect for <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> including the <a href="/wiki/Minority_rights" title="Minority rights">rights of persons belonging to minorities</a>", invoking "a society in which <a href="/wiki/Pluralism_(political_philosophy)" title="Pluralism (political philosophy)">pluralism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Non-discrimination" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-discrimination">non-discrimination</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toleration" title="Toleration">tolerance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Justice" title="Justice">justice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solidarity" title="Solidarity">solidarity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gender_equality" title="Gender equality">equality between women and men</a> prevail".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 2012 <a href="/wiki/Eurobarometer" title="Eurobarometer">Eurobarometer</a> survey reported that 49% of those surveyed described the EU member states as "close" in terms of "shared values" (down from 54% in 2008), 42% described them as "different" (up from 34% in 2008).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Identity_factors">Identity factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pan-European_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Identity factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It has been for long a matter of discussions<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> to know whether or not this feeling of belonging was shared by a majority of Europeans, geographically speaking, and the strength of this feeling. 
</p><p>There are discussions as well about the question of the objective factors or "Europeanness". An approach<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> underlines how, for being European,  a person would at least have to: 
</p>
<ul><li>be a citizen of a state, located by <a href="/wiki/Stipulation" title="Stipulation">stipulation</a>, to be geographically within Europe;</li>
<li>speak a <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Europe" title="Languages of Europe">language</a> which is officially accepted as one of the official languages of that state;</li>
<li>share an historical destiny with other people, within that state, speaking the aforementioned language;</li>
<li>share a cultural pattern with other such people, where the cultural pattern is seen as consisting of similar cognitive, evaluative and emotional elements".</li></ul>
<p>Usually four steps are considered as conditions in the building of cultural and political identity:
</p>
<ul><li>The recognition of a “self” distinct from others, “them”.</li>
<li>The recognition that this “self,” this “identification” is in opposition to “them.” In order for an identity to thrive there must be a challenge, a competitive edge or conflicts of interests.</li>
<li>The establishment of a separate political identity involves a cognitive simplification of the world, where most events are interpreted in dual categories such as “European” versus “non-European”.</li>
<li>The establishment of common expected and desired goals. Such goals can be elaborated as utopian systems or models, like the federalist and <a href="/wiki/Confederation" title="Confederation">confederalist</a> conceptions of a new European order, or as partial working solutions to pragmatically felt needs, such as those postulated by <a href="/wiki/Neofunctionalism" title="Neofunctionalism">neo-functionalists</a>.</li></ul>
<p>One of the clearly stated political objectives of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> is the deepening of the European identity feeling<sup id="cite_ref-:1_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_and_linguistic_identity">Cultural and linguistic identity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pan-European_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cultural and linguistic identity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Defining a European identity is a very complex processes. From outside, "Europeanness" would be a thing for a Chinese or an American, but on the internal plan geography is not sufficient to define Europe in the eyes of Europeans. According to <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Duroselle" title="Jean-Baptiste Duroselle">Jean-Baptiste Duroselle</a><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>, "there has been, since men think, an immense variety of Europes". <a href="/wiki/Paul_Val%C3%A9ry" title="Paul Valéry">Paul Valéry</a> cites three major heritages to define the European identity&#160;: the <a href="/wiki/Greek_democracy" title="Greek democracy">Greek democracy</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">Roman Law</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Judeo-Christian" title="Judeo-Christian">Judeo-Christian</a> tradition<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>. Yet <a href="/wiki/Emmanuel_Berl" title="Emmanuel Berl">Emmanuel Berl</a><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> criticizes this thesis as reductive, since it supposes a level of "europeanness", decreasing for West to East. According to him, Europe is shape-shifting, and no culture historically prevails over another, and <a href="/wiki/Islam_in_Europe" title="Islam in Europe">European Islam</a>, which concerns around 8% of the population, is one of the many sides of European identity.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eurobarometer_surveys_on_identity">Eurobarometer surveys on identity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pan-European_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Eurobarometer surveys on identity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Eurobarometer" title="Eurobarometer">Eurobarometer</a> surveys show that European and national identities tend to add rather than rule themselves out. In 2009, 3 French out of 5 felt French and European, a feeling that dominated in every socio-political group except the <a href="/wiki/National_Rally_(France)" title="National Rally (France)">National Front</a> supporters. Yet this tendency is not geographically homogeneous&#160;: 63&#160;% of Britons favoured their sole nationalities (which has been one of the main explanations of the <a href="/wiki/Brexit" title="Brexit">Brexit</a> vote), against 27% Luxembourgian. During these surveys, the respondents are asked which notions they spontaneously associate with the EU. <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">Democracy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">Human Rights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_movement" title="Freedom of movement">Freedom of movement</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a> are the most cited. There are divergences between generations&#160;: those who knew war directly or through their parents narrations mention peace, while the younger evoke market economy. The idea that identity is built through opposition to other groups is also confirmed since 60&#160;% Europeans state they rather or fully agree with the idea that "compared with other continents, it is distinctly easier to see what Europeans have in common in terms of values"<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistic_diversity">Linguistic diversity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pan-European_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Linguistic diversity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Five languages have more than 50 million native speakers in Europe: <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>, <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> and <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>. While Russian has the largest number of native speakers (more than 100 million in Europe), English has the largest number of speakers in total, including some 200 million speakers of <a href="/wiki/English_as_a_second_language" class="mw-redirect" title="English as a second language">English as a second language</a><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>. There is no final account of all <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Europe" title="Languages of Europe">European languages</a>, but the sole EU recognizes 24 official languages. For some, the linguistic diversity is constituent of European identity<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>. 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pan-European_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aspects of an emerging "European identity" in popular culture may be seen in the introduction of "Pan-European" competitions such as the <a href="/wiki/Eurovision_Song_Contest" title="Eurovision Song Contest">Eurovision Song Contest</a> (since 1956), the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_European_Championship" title="UEFA European Championship">UEFA European Championship</a> (since 1958) or, more recently, the <a href="/wiki/2015_European_Games" title="2015 European Games">European Games</a> (2015). In these competitions, it is still teams or representatives of the individual nations of Europe that are competing against one another, but a "European identity" may argued to arise from the definition the "European" participants (often loosely defined, e.g. including Morocco, Israel and Australia in the case of the Eurovision Song Contest), and the emergence of "cultural rites" associated with these events.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
In the 1990s and 2000s, participation in the Eurovision Song Contest was to some extent perceived as a politically significant confirmation of nationhood and of "belonging to Europe" by the then-recently independent nations of Eastern Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Pan-European events not organised along national lines include the <a href="/wiki/European_Film_Awards" title="European Film Awards">European Film Awards</a>, presented annually since 1988 by the <a href="/wiki/European_Film_Academy" title="European Film Academy">European Film Academy</a> to recognize excellence in European cinematic achievements. The awards are given in over ten categories, of which the most important is the Film of the year. They are restricted to <a href="/wiki/European_cinema" class="mw-redirect" title="European cinema">European cinema</a> and European producers, directors, and actors.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Ryder_Cup" title="Ryder Cup">Ryder Cup</a> golf competition is a biennial event, originally between a British and an American team, but since 1979 admitting continental European players to form a "Team Europe".
The <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Europe" title="Flag of Europe">flag of Europe</a> was used to represent "Team Europe" since 1991, but reportedly most European participants preferred to use their own national flags.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
There have also been attempts to <a href="/wiki/Astroturfing" title="Astroturfing">use popular culture</a> for the propagation of "identification with the EU" on the behalf of the EU itself. These attempts have proven controversial. In 1997, the European Commission distributed a comic strip titled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Raspberry_Ice_Cream_War" title="The Raspberry Ice Cream War">The Raspberry Ice Cream War</a></i>, aimed at children in schools. The EU office in London declined to distribute this in the UK, due to an expected unsympathetic reception for such views.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Captain_Euro" title="Captain Euro">Captain Euro</a>, a cartoon character superhero mascot of Europe, was developed in the 1990s by branding strategist <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_De_Santis" title="Nicolas De Santis">Nicolas De Santis</a> to support the launch of the <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">Euro</a> currency.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> In 2014, London branding think tank, Gold Mercury International, launched the <a href="/wiki/Brand_EU" title="Brand EU">Brand EU</a> Centre, with the purpose of solving Europe's identity crisis and creating a strong brand of Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
There have been proposals<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (April 2017)">by whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="Need the year this event took place (April 2017)">year&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> to create a European Olympic Team, which would break with the existing organisation through <a href="/wiki/National_Olympic_Committee" title="National Olympic Committee">National Olympic Committees</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> 
In 2007, <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> President <a href="/wiki/Romano_Prodi" title="Romano Prodi">Romano Prodi</a> suggested that EU teams should carry the EU flag, alongside the national flag, at the <a href="/wiki/2008_Summer_Olympics" title="2008 Summer Olympics">2008 Summer Olympics</a> – a proposal which angered eurosceptics.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> According to Eurobarometer surveys, only 5% of respondents think that a European Olympic team would make them feel more of a 'European citizen'.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Institutional_actions_to_promote_European_identity">Institutional actions to promote European identity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pan-European_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Institutional actions to promote European identity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Institutions_of_the_European_Union" title="Institutions of the European Union">European institutions</a> made several concrete attempts to reinforce two things: identity contents (what is Europe in people’s minds?) and identity formation (what makes people feel European?)<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>. 
The <a href="/wiki/.eu" title=".eu">.eu</a> domain name extension was introduced in 2005 as a new symbol of European Union identity on the World Wide Web. The .eu domain's introduction campaign specifically uses the tagline <i>"Your European Identity"</i>. Registrants must be located within the European Union. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_policies">Direct policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pan-European_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Direct policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On the cultural plan, the European Union began a policy in the 70's with the <a href="/wiki/Directive_(European_Union)" title="Directive (European Union)">directive</a> "Television without Frontiers", which allowed free trade of TV programs and guaranteed more than half of the air time to European operas<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>. The Culture program finances other cultural activities in order to stregthen the European common identity. The European Union also bet on <a href="/wiki/Symbols_of_the_European_Union" title="Symbols of the European Union">symbols</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Europe" title="Flag of Europe">flag</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Anthem" title="Anthem">anthem</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Ode_to_Joy" title="Ode to Joy">Ode to Joy</a>" from the final <a href="/wiki/Movement_(music)" title="Movement (music)">movement</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Beethoven's</a> <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._9_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 9 (Beethoven)">9th Symphony</a>), the motto "<a href="/wiki/Motto_of_the_European_Union" title="Motto of the European Union">In varietate concordia</a>", the <a href="/wiki/Europe_Day" title="Europe Day">Europe day</a>. Great cultural unifying events are organised, such as the <a href="/wiki/European_Heritage_Days" title="European Heritage Days">European heritage days</a>, or the election of the <a href="/wiki/European_Capital_of_Culture" title="European Capital of Culture">Capital of Culture.</a> The youth mobility has been encouraged since the launching of the <a href="/wiki/Erasmus_Programme" title="Erasmus Programme">Erasmus programme</a> in 1987, which has permitted students to go to 33 European countries. 
</p><p>The challenge of communication, to make the European project more understandable to the 500 million citizens, in 24 languages, has also been addressed: in 2004, the first Vice-President of the Commission has the <a href="/wiki/List_of_European_Commission_portfolios#Communication_strategy" title="List of European Commission portfolios">Communication Strategy</a> portfolio. The common values are reasserted through the judicial action of the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a>. Linked to this, the European Union funds many surveys (such as <a href="/wiki/Eurobarometer" title="Eurobarometer">Eurobarometer</a>) and scientific studies, to improve its identity-building policies. A collection of such studies is for example <i>The development of European Identity/Identities&#160;: Unfinished Business</i><sup id="cite_ref-:1_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_boundaries_of_European_identity">The boundaries of European identity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pan-European_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The boundaries of European identity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Just as every sociological identity, the European identity is not as much defined by its contents than by its boundaries<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>.  There are today heated political debates on whether to allow or not immigrants coming to Europe, on which criteria. The debate is also on whether to integrate or assimilate people that come form very different cultures, and how to do it. Many European right-wing politicians<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> are now advocating a vision of European identity (often seen as a White and Christian one) as a citadel being threaten by immigration, and thus needing to be defended by harsher policies on this matter. A new far right movement even baptized itself the <a href="/wiki/Identitarian_movement" title="Identitarian movement">Identitarians</a>. Their adversaries often say that this vision of Europe is <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racist</a><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>, and that it symbolically excludes people who are already European by law.
</p><p>The geographical definitions of Europe do not seem to be a matter of discussion any more,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> but the question of a European identity arises concerning countries that are geographically part of Asia as well, such as <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> or <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>. The question also arises when it comes to European territories outside of (geographical) Europe, such as the <a href="/wiki/French_overseas_departments" class="mw-redirect" title="French overseas departments">French overseas departments</a>. <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guianese</a>, for example, are European citizen even though they are born and live in South America.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pan-European_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The risk, defining a European identity, is to close up from other cultures that would not correspond to pre-defined criteria. To face this difficulty, vagueness is necessary&#160;: the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Lisbon" title="Treaty of Lisbon">Treaty of Lisbon</a> mentions for example "cultural, religious and humanist inheritance"<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>.
Moreover, it would be illusory to impose a principle of cultural homogeneity to states with various national identities. <a href="/wiki/Jean-Marc_Ferry" title="Jean-Marc Ferry">Jean-Marc Ferry</a> considers<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> that the European construction developed new differentiation, between citizenship and nationality for example, with the birth of post national citizenship<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> in 1992. According to <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Aron" title="Raymond Aron">Raymond Aron</a><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>, the construction can predate the European sentiment, but the last is essential to avoid a fictional Europe, a Europe that would only be a meaningless word in which the people do not recognize themselves. This idea is backed by <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Delors" title="Jacques Delors">Jacques Delors</a> in 1992 who writes that it is needed to "give Europe a soul, (...) a spirituality, a meaning" beyond the simple economic and administrative realities<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>.
</p>
