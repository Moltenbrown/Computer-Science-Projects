<p>
Biologically, a <b>child</b> (plural: <b>children</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> being between the stages of <a href="/wiki/Childbirth" title="Childbirth">birth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Puberty" title="Puberty">puberty</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Child_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Child-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mosby_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mosby-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> or between the <a href="/wiki/Development_of_the_human_body" title="Development of the human body">developmental period</a> of <a href="/wiki/Infancy" class="mw-redirect" title="Infancy">infancy</a> and puberty.<sup id="cite_ref-Rathus_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rathus-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The legal definition of <i>child</i> generally refers to a <a href="/wiki/Minor_(law)" title="Minor (law)">minor</a>, otherwise known as a person younger than the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_majority" title="Age of majority">age of majority</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Child_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Child-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Child</i> may also describe a relationship with a parent (such as <a href="/wiki/Son" title="Son">sons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Daughter" title="Daughter">daughters</a> of any age)<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> or, metaphorically, an <a href="/wiki/Authority_figure" class="mw-redirect" title="Authority figure">authority figure</a>, or signify group membership in a clan, tribe, or religion; it can also signify being strongly affected by a specific time, place, or circumstance, as in "a child of nature" or "a child of the Sixties".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span id="Biological.2C_legal_and_social_definitions"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological,_legal_and_social_definitions">Biological, legal and social definitions</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Children_games_Louvre_Ma99_n2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Children_games_Louvre_Ma99_n2.jpg/220px-Children_games_Louvre_Ma99_n2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Children_games_Louvre_Ma99_n2.jpg/330px-Children_games_Louvre_Ma99_n2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Children_games_Louvre_Ma99_n2.jpg/440px-Children_games_Louvre_Ma99_n2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3775" data-file-height="2120" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Children_games_Louvre_Ma99_n2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Children playing ball games, Roman artwork, 2nd century AD</div></div></div>
<p>Biologically, a child is a person between birth and puberty,<sup id="cite_ref-Child_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Child-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mosby_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mosby-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> or between the <a href="/wiki/Development_of_the_human_body" title="Development of the human body">developmental period</a> of <a href="/wiki/Infancy" class="mw-redirect" title="Infancy">infancy</a> and puberty.<sup id="cite_ref-Rathus_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rathus-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Legally, the term <i>child</i> may refer to anyone below the age of majority or some other age limit. The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_Rights_of_the_Child" title="Convention on the Rights of the Child">Convention on the Rights of the Child</a> defines <i>child</i> as "a human being below the age of 18 years unless under the <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a> applicable to the child, <a href="/wiki/Age_of_majority" title="Age of majority">majority</a> is attained earlier".<sup id="cite_ref-un_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-un-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> This is ratified by 192 of 194 member countries. The term <i>child</i> may also refer to someone below another legally defined age limit unconnected to the age of majority. In <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, for example, a <i>child</i> is legally defined as someone under the age of 14 under the "Children and Young Persons Act" whereas the age of majority is 21.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  In U.S. Immigration Law, a child refers to anyone who is under the age of 21.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some English definitions of the word <i>child</i> include the <a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">fetus</a> (sometimes termed <i>the unborn</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-SeeShorter_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SeeShorter-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In many cultures, a child is considered an adult after undergoing a <a href="/wiki/Rite_of_passage" title="Rite of passage">rite of passage</a>, which may or may not correspond to the time of puberty.
</p><p>Children generally have fewer rights than adults and are classed as unable to make serious decisions, and legally must always be under the care of a responsible adult or <a href="/wiki/Child_custody" title="Child custody">child custody</a>, whether their parents divorce or not. Recognition of childhood as a state different from adulthood began to emerge in the 16th and 17th centuries. Society began to relate to the child not as a miniature adult but as a person of a lower level of maturity needing adult protection, love and nurturing. This change can be traced in paintings: In the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, children were portrayed in art as miniature adults with no childlike characteristics. In the 16th century, images of children began to acquire a distinct childlike appearance. From the late 17th century onwards, children were shown playing with toys and later literature for children also began to develop at this time.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span></h2>
<p>According to Professor Peter Jones of <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_university" class="mw-redirect" title="Cambridge university">Cambridge university</a> development of the brain continues long past legal definitions of adulthood so "to have a definition of when you move from childhood to adulthood looks increasingly absurd. It's a much more nuanced transition that takes place over three decades."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Children go through stages of <a href="/wiki/Child_development" title="Child development">social development</a>. Children learn initially through <a href="/wiki/Play_(activity)" title="Play (activity)">play</a> and later in most societies through formal schooling. As a child is growing they are learning how to do some tasks in chronological order. They learn how to prioritize their goals and actions. Their behavior is transcending as they learn new perspectives from other people. They learn how to represent certain things symbolically and learn new behavior.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span></h2>
<p>Children with <a href="/wiki/Attention_deficit_hyperactivity_disorder" title="Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder">ADHD</a> and learning disabilities may need extra help to develop social skills. The impulsive characteristics of an ADHD child may lead to poor peer relationships. Children with poor attention spans may not tune into social cues in their environment, making it difficult for them to learn social skills through experience.<sup id="cite_ref-Social_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Social-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Health issues affecting children are generally managed separately from those affecting adults, by <a href="/wiki/Pediatrics" title="Pediatrics">pediatricians</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Age_of_responsibility">Age of responsibility</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Age_of_consent" title="Age of consent">Age of consent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Age_of_majority" title="Age of majority">Age of majority</a>, <a href="/wiki/Age_of_criminal_responsibility" class="mw-redirect" title="Age of criminal responsibility">Age of criminal responsibility</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Marriageable_age" title="Marriageable age">Marriageable age</a></div>
<p>The age at which children are considered responsible for their society-bound actions (e. g. marriage, voting, etc.) has also changed over time, and this is reflected in the way they are treated in courts of law. In Roman times, children were regarded as not culpable for crimes, a position later adopted by the Church. In the 19th century, children younger than seven years old were believed incapable of crime. Children from the age of seven forward were considered responsible for their actions. Therefore, they could face criminal charges, be sent to adult prison, and be punished like adults by whipping, branding or hanging.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Minimum employment age and marriage age also vary. The age limit of voluntary/involuntary military service is also disputed at the international level.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Child_mortality">Child mortality</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Child_mortality" title="Child mortality">Child mortality</a> and <a href="/wiki/Infant_mortality" title="Infant mortality">Infant mortality</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:362px;"><a href="/wiki/File:2012_Infant_mortality_rate_per_1000_live_births,_under-5,_world_map.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/2012_Infant_mortality_rate_per_1000_live_births%2C_under-5%2C_world_map.svg/360px-2012_Infant_mortality_rate_per_1000_live_births%2C_under-5%2C_world_map.svg.png" decoding="async" width="360" height="185" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/2012_Infant_mortality_rate_per_1000_live_births%2C_under-5%2C_world_map.svg/540px-2012_Infant_mortality_rate_per_1000_live_births%2C_under-5%2C_world_map.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/2012_Infant_mortality_rate_per_1000_live_births%2C_under-5%2C_world_map.svg/720px-2012_Infant_mortality_rate_per_1000_live_births%2C_under-5%2C_world_map.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="863" data-file-height="443" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:2012_Infant_mortality_rate_per_1000_live_births,_under-5,_world_map.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>World infant mortality rates in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-unicef2013_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unicef2013-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>During the early 17th century in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, about two-thirds of all children died before the age of four.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, the life expectancy of children increased dramatically.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>, and this has continued. Child mortality rates have fallen across the world. About 12.6 million under-five infants died worldwide in 1990, which declined to 6.6 million in 2012. The infant mortality rate dropped from 90 deaths per 1,000 live births in 1990, to 48 in 2012. The highest average infant mortality rates are in sub-Saharan Africa, at 98 deaths per 1,000 live births – over double the world's average.<sup id="cite_ref-unicef2013_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unicef2013-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kuito_class.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Kuito_class.jpg/220px-Kuito_class.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Kuito_class.jpg/330px-Kuito_class.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Kuito_class.jpg/440px-Kuito_class.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kuito_class.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Children in an outdoor classroom in <a href="/wiki/Bi%C3%A9_(province)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bié (province)">Bié</a>, <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a></div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">Education</a></div>
<p>Education, in the general sense, refers to the act or process of imparting or acquiring general knowledge, developing the powers of reasoning and judgment, and preparing intellectually for mature life.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Formal education most often takes place through <a href="/wiki/Schooling" class="mw-redirect" title="Schooling">schooling</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Right_to_education" title="Right to education">right to education</a> has been recognized by some governments. At the global level, Article 13 of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>' 1966 <a href="/wiki/International_Covenant_on_Economic,_Social_and_Cultural_Rights" title="International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights">International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights</a> recognizes the right of everyone to an education.<sup id="cite_ref-ICESCR-art13.1_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ICESCR-art13.1-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Compulsory_education" title="Compulsory education">Education is compulsory</a> in most places up to a certain age, but attendance at <a href="/wiki/School" title="School">school</a> may not be, with alternative options such as <a href="/wiki/Home-schooling" class="mw-redirect" title="Home-schooling">home-schooling</a> or <a href="/wiki/E-learning" class="mw-redirect" title="E-learning">e-learning</a> being recognized as valid forms of education in certain jurisdictions.
</p><p>Children in some countries (especially in parts of Africa and Asia) are often kept out of school, or attend only for short periods. Data from <a href="/wiki/UNICEF" title="UNICEF">UNICEF</a> indicate that in 2011, 57 million children were out of school; and more than 20% of African children have never attended primary school or have left without completing primary education.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> According to a UN report, <a href="/wiki/Warfare" class="mw-redirect" title="Warfare">warfare</a> is preventing 28 million children worldwide from receiving an education, due to the risk of sexual violence and attacks in schools.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Other factors that keep children out of school include poverty, child labor, social attitudes, and long distances to school.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Attitudes_toward_children">Attitudes toward children</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Group_of_Breaker_boys._Smallest_is_Sam_Belloma.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Group_of_Breaker_boys._Smallest_is_Sam_Belloma.jpg/220px-Group_of_Breaker_boys._Smallest_is_Sam_Belloma.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Group_of_Breaker_boys._Smallest_is_Sam_Belloma.jpg/330px-Group_of_Breaker_boys._Smallest_is_Sam_Belloma.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Group_of_Breaker_boys._Smallest_is_Sam_Belloma.jpg/440px-Group_of_Breaker_boys._Smallest_is_Sam_Belloma.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4806" data-file-height="3276" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Group_of_Breaker_boys._Smallest_is_Sam_Belloma.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Group of <a href="/wiki/Breaker_boy" title="Breaker boy">breaker boys</a> in Pittston, Pennsylvania, 1911. <a href="/wiki/Child_labor" class="mw-redirect" title="Child labor">Child labor</a> was widespread until the early 20th century. Currently,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (May 2019)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> child labor rates are highest in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.</div></div></div><p> Social attitudes toward children differ around the world in various cultures and change over time. A 1988 study on European attitudes toward the centrality of children found that Italy was more child-centric and the Netherlands less child-centric, with other countries, such as Austria, Great Britain, Ireland and <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a> falling in between.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2013, <a href="/wiki/Child_marriage" title="Child marriage">child marriage</a> rates reached 75% in Niger, 68% in Central African Republic and Chad, 66% in Bangladesh, and 47% in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Protection of children from <a href="/wiki/Child_abuse" title="Child abuse">abuse</a> is considered an important contemporary goal. This includes protecting children from exploitation such as <a href="/wiki/Child_labor" class="mw-redirect" title="Child labor">child labor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Child_trafficking" class="mw-redirect" title="Child trafficking">child trafficking</a> and selling, <a href="/wiki/Child_sexual_abuse" title="Child sexual abuse">child sexual abuse</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Child_prostitution" title="Child prostitution">child prostitution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Child_pornography" title="Child pornography">child pornography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Military_use_of_children" class="mw-redirect" title="Military use of children">military use of children</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Child_laundering" title="Child laundering">child laundering</a> in illegal <a href="/wiki/Adoption" title="Adoption">adoptions</a>. There exist several international instruments for these purposes, such as:
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Emergencies_and_conflicts">Emergencies and conflicts</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Declaration_on_the_Protection_of_Women_and_Children_in_Emergency_and_Armed_Conflict" title="Declaration on the Protection of Women and Children in Emergency and Armed Conflict">Declaration on the Protection of Women and Children in Emergency and Armed Conflict</a>, <a href="/wiki/Children_in_the_Israeli%E2%80%93Palestinian_conflict" title="Children in the Israeli–Palestinian conflict">Children in the Israeli–Palestinian conflict</a>, <a href="/wiki/Save_the_Children" title="Save the Children">Save the Children</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inter-Agency_Network_for_Education_in_Emergencies" title="Inter-Agency Network for Education in Emergencies">Inter-Agency Network for Education in Emergencies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Military_use_of_children" class="mw-redirect" title="Military use of children">Military use of children</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trafficking_of_children" title="Trafficking of children">Trafficking of children</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_child_abduction" title="International child abduction">International child abduction</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Refugee_children" title="Refugee children">Refugee children</a></div>
<p>Emergencies and conflicts pose detrimental risks to the <a href="/wiki/Child_Health" class="mw-redirect" title="Child Health">health</a>, safety, and well-being of children. There are many different kinds of conflicts and emergencies, for example, violence, armed conflicts, <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">war</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_disaster" title="Natural disaster">natural disasters</a>, etc. As of 2010 approximately 13 million children are displaced by <a href="/wiki/Armed_conflicts" class="mw-redirect" title="Armed conflicts">armed conflicts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Violence" title="Violence">violence</a> around the world.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Where violent conflicts are the norm, the lives of young children are significantly disrupted and their families have great difficulty in offering the sensitive and consistent care that young children need for their healthy development.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Studies on the effect of emergencies and conflict on the <a href="/wiki/Physical_health" class="mw-redirect" title="Physical health">physical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mental_health" title="Mental health">mental health</a> of children between birth and 8 years old show that where the disaster is natural, the rate of <a href="/wiki/Posttraumatic_stress_disorder" title="Posttraumatic stress disorder">PTSD</a> occurs in anywhere from 3 to 87 percent of affected children.<sup id="cite_ref-Shannon,_M._P._1994._pp._80-93_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shannon,_M._P._1994._pp._80-93-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> However, rates of PTSD for children living in chronic conflict conditions varies from 15 to 50 percent.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
