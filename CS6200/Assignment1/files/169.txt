<p><b>Meir Bar-Ilan</b> (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886049626">.mw-parser-output .script-hebrew,.mw-parser-output .script-Hebr{font-size:1.15em;font-family:"Ezra SIL","Ezra SIL SR","Keter Aram Tsova","Taamey Ashkenaz","Taamey David CLM","Taamey Frank CLM","Frank Ruehl CLM","Keter YG","Shofar","David CLM","Hadasim CLM","Simple CLM","Nachlieli","SBL BibLit","SBL Hebrew",Cardo,Alef,"Noto Serif Hebrew","Noto Sans Hebrew","David Libre",David,"Times New Roman",Gisha,Arial,FreeSerif,FreeSans}</style><span class="script-hebrew" style="font-size: 115%;" dir="rtl">מֵאִיר בַּר-אִילָן</span>; <span style="display:none">(<span class="bday">1880-04-10</span>)</span>April 10, 1880 &#8211; <span style="display:none">(<span class="dday deathdate">1949-04-17</span>)</span>April 17, 1949) was an
<a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Judaism" title="Orthodox Judaism">Orthodox</a> <a href="/wiki/Rabbi" title="Rabbi">rabbi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Author" title="Author">author</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Religious_Zionism" title="Religious Zionism">Religious Zionist</a> activist, who served as leader of the <a href="/wiki/Mizrachi_(religious_Zionism)" title="Mizrachi (religious Zionism)">Mizrachi</a> movement in the United States and <a href="/wiki/Mandatory_Palestine" title="Mandatory Palestine">Mandatory Palestine</a>. <a href="/wiki/Bar-Ilan_University" title="Bar-Ilan University">Bar-Ilan University</a>, founded in 1955, was named in his honour.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meir_Bar-Ilan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meir_Bar-Ilan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Chaimwalkinvolozin.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Chaimwalkinvolozin.jpg/250px-Chaimwalkinvolozin.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="185" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Chaimwalkinvolozin.jpg/375px-Chaimwalkinvolozin.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Chaimwalkinvolozin.jpg/500px-Chaimwalkinvolozin.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3206" data-file-height="2373" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Chaimwalkinvolozin.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bar-Ilan (standing, second from right) with members of his family.</div></div></div>
<p>Bar-Ilan was born Meir Berlin in 1880, the youngest son of Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Naftali_Zvi_Yehuda_Berlin" title="Naftali Zvi Yehuda Berlin">Naftali Zvi Yehuda Berlin</a> and his second wife Batya Miriam Berlin (<a href="/wiki/Given_name#Name_at_birth" title="Given name"><span title="Name at birth">n&#233;e</span></a>&#160;Epshtein). Bar-Ilan's father was the head of the famous <a href="/wiki/Volozhin_Yeshiva" class="mw-redirect" title="Volozhin Yeshiva">Volozhin Yeshiva</a> in Lithuania.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Bar-Ilan was also a descendant of Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Meir_ben_Isaac_Katzenellenbogen" class="mw-redirect" title="Meir ben Isaac Katzenellenbogen">Meir Katzenellenbogen</a>, the Maharam of Padua.<sup id="cite_ref-unbroken_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unbroken-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He studied at the Volozhin Yeshiva and, after his father's death in 1894, at the traditional yeshivas of <a href="/wiki/Telz_Yeshiva" class="mw-redirect" title="Telz Yeshiva">Telshe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brisk_yeshivas" class="mw-redirect" title="Brisk yeshivas">Brisk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Novardok_yeshiva" class="mw-redirect" title="Novardok yeshiva">Novardok</a>, where he learned with his maternal grandfather, Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Yechiel_Michel_Epstein" title="Yechiel Michel Epstein">Yechiel Michel Epstein</a>. After gaining <i><a href="/wiki/Semicha" class="mw-redirect" title="Semicha">semicha</a></i> in 1902 at the age of twenty-two, Bar-Ilan travelled to Germany to attend the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Berlin" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Berlin">University of Berlin</a>. There, he became acquainted with a more modern form of <a href="/wiki/Modern_Orthodox_Judaism" title="Modern Orthodox Judaism">Orthodox Judaism</a> that had a more tolerant attitude to secular education and to political <a href="/wiki/Zionism" title="Zionism">Zionism</a> (although such attitudes were also present in the Lithuania of his youth). Bar-Ilan was deeply influenced by the local religious community and its philosophy of <i><a href="/wiki/Torah_im_Derech_Eretz" title="Torah im Derech Eretz">Torah im Derech Eretz</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Mizrachi_movement_and_Ha.E2.80.99Ivri"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Mizrachi_movement_and_Ha’Ivri">Mizrachi movement and <i>Ha’Ivri</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meir_Bar-Ilan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mizrachi movement and Ha’Ivri">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1905 Bar-Ilan joined the <a href="/wiki/Mizrachi_(Religious_Zionism)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mizrachi (Religious Zionism)">Mizrachi movement</a>, representing it at the <a href="/wiki/Zionist_Congress" class="mw-redirect" title="Zionist Congress">Seventh Zionist Congress</a>, at which he voted against the <a href="/wiki/Uganda_Proposal" class="mw-redirect" title="Uganda Proposal">Uganda Proposal</a> to create a temporary Jewish homeland in <a href="/wiki/British_East_Africa" class="mw-redirect" title="British East Africa">British East Africa</a>, as suggested by Great Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-Meir_Bar-Ilan_1880-1949_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meir_Bar-Ilan_1880-1949-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1911, he founded the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a> <a href="/wiki/Weekly_newspaper" title="Weekly newspaper">weekly newspaper</a> <i>Ha’Ivri</i> in Berlin as a "non-party paper dedicated to all the affairs of Israel, faithful in its spirit to our religious tradition and to our national renaissance."<sup id="cite_ref-brown_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brown-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> That same year, Bar-Ilan was appointed secretary of the world Mizrachi movement. In 1913 he came to the United States and developed local Mizrachi groups into a national organisation, chairing the first <a href="/wiki/Religious_Zionists_of_America" title="Religious Zionists of America">American Mizrachi</a> convention, held in <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati" title="Cincinnati">Cincinnati</a> in May 1914.<sup id="cite_ref-medoff_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-medoff-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Bar-Ilan settled in New York in 1914, becoming president of the American Mizrachi movement the following year, a position he held until 1928.<sup id="cite_ref-Meir_Bar-Ilan_1880-1949_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meir_Bar-Ilan_1880-1949-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In his absence  <i>Ha’Ivri</i> ceased publication in April 1914, but was re-established under Bar-Ilan's direction in New York in January 1916. Published until 1921, the paper's contributors included such prominent writers as <a href="/wiki/S._Y._Agnon" class="mw-redirect" title="S. Y. Agnon">S. Y. Agnon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Opatoshu" title="Joseph Opatoshu">Joseph Opatoshu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reuben_Brainin" title="Reuben Brainin">Reuben Brainin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eliezer_Ben-Yehuda" title="Eliezer Ben-Yehuda">Eliezer Ben-Yehuda</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Yehuda_Leib_Maimon" title="Yehuda Leib Maimon">Yehuda Leib Maimon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-brown_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brown-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Bar-Ilan was also an active member of the <a href="/wiki/American_Jewish_Joint_Distribution_Committee" title="American Jewish Joint Distribution Committee">Joint Distribution Committee</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, and served as vice president of the Central Relief Committee of New York City in 1916. He founded the Mizrachi Teachers Institute in 1917. In 1923, Bar-Ilan briefly served as acting president for what is now <a href="/wiki/Yeshiva_University" title="Yeshiva University">Yeshiva University</a> during the temporary absence of its then-president, <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Revel" title="Bernard Revel">Bernard Revel</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_in_Israel">Life in Israel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meir_Bar-Ilan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Life in Israel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In 1923 he moved to <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>. In Israel, Bar-Ilan founded the daily newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/HaTzofe" title="HaTzofe">Hatzofeh</a></i> and initiated the <i>Encyclopedia Talmudit</i>, a Hebrew encyclopedia summarizing <a href="/wiki/Halacha" class="mw-redirect" title="Halacha">halachic</a> topics in the Talmud, forty-two volumes of which have been published to date.<sup id="cite_ref-medoff_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-medoff-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Bar-Ilan also served on the board of directors of the <a href="/wiki/Bank_Mizrahi-Tefahot" title="Bank Mizrahi-Tefahot">Mizrachi Bank</a> and, in 1925, became a member of the Board of Directors of the <a href="/wiki/Jewish_National_Fund" title="Jewish National Fund">Jewish National Fund</a>, devoted to financing the rebuilding of the Jewish homeland in the then-<a href="/wiki/Mandatory_Palestine" title="Mandatory Palestine">British Mandate of Palestine</a>.
</p><p>He was a vocal opponent of the 1937 <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Partition_Plan_for_Palestine" title="United Nations Partition Plan for Palestine">United Nations Partition Plan for Palestine</a> and the 1939 British <a href="/wiki/White_Paper_of_1939" title="White Paper of 1939">White Paper</a>, and advocated <a href="/wiki/Civil_disobedience" title="Civil disobedience">civil disobedience</a> and non-cooperation by the <a href="/wiki/Jew" class="mw-redirect" title="Jew">Jews</a> with the British.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At the beginning of 1943, Bar-Ilan visited the United States to lobby the American government to rescue <a href="/wiki/Holocaust" class="mw-redirect" title="Holocaust">Jewish refugees</a> and help establish a Jewish state. He secured meetings with leading politicians and foreign ambassadors, including Vice President <a href="/wiki/Henry_A._Wallace" title="Henry A. Wallace">Henry Wallace</a>, Senator <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Wagner" title="Robert F. Wagner">Robert Wagner</a>, Senate Majority Leader <a href="/wiki/Alben_W._Barkley" title="Alben W. Barkley">Alben Barkley</a>, and House Minority Leader <a href="/wiki/Joseph_W._Martin_Jr." title="Joseph W. Martin Jr.">Joseph Martin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-medoff_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-medoff-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scholarship">Scholarship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meir_Bar-Ilan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Scholarship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Along with Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Shlomo_Yosef_Zevin" title="Shlomo Yosef Zevin">Shlomo Yosef Zevin</a>, Bar-Ilan was the editor of the <i>Talmudical Encyclopedia</i> (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span lang="he" dir="rtl">אנציקלופדיה תלמודית</span>&#8206;), Volumes I (Jerusalem, 1946) and II (published posthumously in 1949).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> He also wrote articles on Talmudic subjects for various periodicals. Notable works of Bar-Ilan include:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Eretz Yisroel in der milḥome un nokh der milḥome</i> (<a href="/wiki/Yiddish_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Yiddish language">Yiddish</a>: <span lang="yi" dir="rtl">ארץ ישראל אין דער מלחמה און נאָך דער מלחמה</span>&#8206;; New York, 1934)</li>
<li><i>Fun Volozhin biz Yerushalayim</i> (<a href="/wiki/Yiddish_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Yiddish language">Yiddish</a>: <span lang="yi" dir="rtl">פון וואָלאָז'ין בּיז ירושלים</span>&#8206;; in Yiddish, Yew York, 1933; in Hebrew, Tel Aviv, 1939-40), autobiography in two volumes</li>
<li><i>Bishvil ha-Techiah</i> (Tel Aviv, 1940)</li>
<li><i>Raban shel Yisrael</i> (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span lang="he" dir="rtl">רבן של ישראל</span>&#8206;; New York, 1943)</li></ul>
<p>After 1948, his activities were scholastically oriented. He organized a committee of scholars to examine the legal problems of the new state in the light of Jewish law and founded an institute for the publication of a new complete edition of the Talmud.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meir_Bar-Ilan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bar-Ilan inspired the founding of <a href="/wiki/Bar-Ilan_University" title="Bar-Ilan University">Bar-Ilan University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ramat_Gan" title="Ramat Gan">Ramat Gan</a> by the American Mizrachi movement, named in his honour. The moshav <a href="/wiki/Beit_Meir" title="Beit Meir">Beit Meir</a> is named in his honour, as are the Meir Forest in the <a href="/wiki/Mount_Hebron" title="Mount Hebron">Hebron Hills</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bar-Ilan_Street" title="Bar-Ilan Street">Bar-Ilan Street</a> in Jerusalem.
</p>
