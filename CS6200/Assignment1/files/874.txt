<p>The <b>Basilica of San Vitale</b> is a church in <a href="/wiki/Ravenna" title="Ravenna">Ravenna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, and one of the most important surviving examples of early Christian <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_art" title="Byzantine art">Byzantine art</a> and <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_architecture" title="Byzantine architecture">architecture</a> in Europe. The Roman Catholic Church has designated the building a "basilica", the honorific title bestowed on church buildings of exceptional historic and ecclesial importance, although it is not of architectural <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">basilica</a> form. It is one of eight Ravenna structures inscribed on the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_List" class="mw-redirect" title="World Heritage List">World Heritage List</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Basilica_of_San_Vitale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Emilia_Ravenna4_tango7174.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Emilia_Ravenna4_tango7174.jpg/250px-Emilia_Ravenna4_tango7174.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Emilia_Ravenna4_tango7174.jpg/375px-Emilia_Ravenna4_tango7174.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Emilia_Ravenna4_tango7174.jpg/500px-Emilia_Ravenna4_tango7174.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Emilia_Ravenna4_tango7174.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The interior of the dome, with Baroque frescoes from the late 18th century.</div></div></div>
<p>The church was begun by Bishop Ecclesius in 526, when Ravenna was under the rule of the <a href="/wiki/Ostrogoths" title="Ostrogoths">Ostrogoths</a> and completed by the 27th Bishop of Ravenna, <a href="/wiki/Maximianus_of_Ravenna" title="Maximianus of Ravenna">Maximian</a>, in 547 preceding the Byzantine <a href="/wiki/Exarchate_of_Ravenna" title="Exarchate of Ravenna">Exarchate of Ravenna</a>.
</p><p>The construction of the church was sponsored by Julius Argentarius, a banker and architect, of whom very little is known, except that he also sponsored the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_Sant%27Apollinare_in_Classe" title="Basilica of Sant&#39;Apollinare in Classe">Basilica of Sant'Apollinare in Classe</a> at around the same time.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> (A <a href="/wiki/Donor_portrait" title="Donor portrait">donor portrait</a> of Julius Argentarius may appear among the courtiers on the Justinian mosaic.) The final cost amounted to 26,000 <i>solidi</i> (gold pieces).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> equal to 36.11 pds. of gold. It has been suggested that Julian originated in the eastern part of the Byzantine Empire, where there was a long-standing tradition of public benefactions.
</p><p>The central vault used a western technique of hollow tubes inserted into each other, rather than bricks. This method was the first recorded structural use of terra-cotta forms, which later evolved into modern <a href="/wiki/Structural_clay_tile" title="Structural clay tile">structural clay tile</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Ambulatory" title="Ambulatory">ambulatory</a> and gallery were vaulted only later in the Middle Ages.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> frescoes on the dome were made between 1778 and 1782 by S. Barozzi, <a href="/wiki/Ubaldo_Gandolfi" title="Ubaldo Gandolfi">U. Gandolfi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jacopo_Guarana" title="Jacopo Guarana">E. Guarana</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The church has an octagonal plan. The building combines <a href="/wiki/Roman_architecture" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman architecture">Roman elements</a>: the dome, shape of doorways, and stepped towers; with <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_architecture" title="Byzantine architecture">Byzantine elements</a>: polygonal apse, capitals, narrow bricks, and an early example of <a href="/wiki/Flying_buttress" title="Flying buttress">flying buttresses</a>. The church is most famous for its wealth of Byzantine <a href="/wiki/Mosaics" class="mw-redirect" title="Mosaics">mosaics</a>, the largest and best preserved outside of <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>. The church is of extreme importance in Byzantine art, as it is the only major church from the period of the Emperor <a href="/wiki/Justinian_I" title="Justinian I">Justinian I</a> to survive virtually intact to the present day. Furthermore, it is thought to reflect the design of the <a href="/wiki/Chrysotriklinos" title="Chrysotriklinos">Byzantine Imperial Palace Audience Chamber</a>, of which nothing at all survives. The belltower has four bells, the tenor one dating from the 16th century. According to legends, the church was erected on the site of the martyrdom of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Vitalis_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Saint Vitalis (disambiguation)">Saint Vitalis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  However, there is some confusion as to whether this is the Saint <a href="/wiki/Vitalis_of_Milan" title="Vitalis of Milan">Vitalis of Milan</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/Saints_Vitalis_and_Agricola" title="Saints Vitalis and Agricola">Saint Vitale</a> whose body was discovered together with that of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Agricola" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Agricola">Saint Agricola</a>, by <a href="/wiki/Saint_Ambrose" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Ambrose">Saint Ambrose</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bologna" title="Bologna">Bologna</a> in 393.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mosaic_art">Mosaic art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Basilica_of_San_Vitale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mosaic art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The central section is surrounded by two superposed <a href="/wiki/Ambulatory" title="Ambulatory">ambulatories</a>. The upper one, the <i>matrimoneum</i>, was possibly reserved for married women. A series of mosaics in the lunettes above the <a href="/wiki/Triforium" title="Triforium">triforia</a> depict sacrifices from the Old Testament:<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> the story of <a href="/wiki/Abraham" title="Abraham">Abraham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Melchizedek" title="Melchizedek">Melchizedek</a>, and the Sacrifice of <a href="/wiki/Isaac" title="Isaac">Isaac</a>; the story of <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Burning_Bush" class="mw-redirect" title="Burning Bush">Burning Bush</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jeremiah" title="Jeremiah">Jeremiah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isaiah" title="Isaiah">Isaiah</a>, representatives of the twelve tribes of Israel, and the story of <a href="/wiki/Abel" class="mw-redirect" title="Abel">Abel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cain" class="mw-redirect" title="Cain">Cain</a>. A pair of angels, holding a medallion with a cross, crowns each lunette. On the side walls the corners, next to the mullioned windows, have mosaics of the <a href="/wiki/Four_Evangelists" title="Four Evangelists">Four Evangelists</a>, under their symbols (angel, lion, ox and eagle), and dressed in white. Especially the portrayal of the lion is remarkable in its ferocity.
</p><p>The cross-ribbed vault in the <a href="/wiki/Presbytery_(architecture)" class="mw-redirect" title="Presbytery (architecture)">presbytery</a> is richly ornamented with mosaic festoons of leaves, fruit and flowers, converging on a crown encircling the <a href="/wiki/Lamb_of_God" title="Lamb of God">Lamb of God</a>. The crown is supported by four <a href="/wiki/Angel" title="Angel">angels</a>, and every surface is covered with a profusion of flowers, stars, birds and animals, including many <a href="/wiki/Peacock" class="mw-redirect" title="Peacock">peacocks</a>. Above the arch, on both sides, two angels hold a disc and beside them a representation of the cities of Jerusalem and Bethlehem. They symbolize the human race (Jerusalem representing the Jews, and Bethlehem the Gentiles).
</p><p>All these mosaics are executed in the Hellenistic-Roman tradition: lively and imaginative, with rich colors and a certain perspective, and with a vivid depiction of the landscape, plants and birds. They were finished when Ravenna was still under <a href="/wiki/Ostrogothic_Kingdom" title="Ostrogothic Kingdom">Gothic</a> rule. The apse is flanked by two chapels, the <i><a href="/wiki/Prothesis_(altar)" title="Prothesis (altar)">prothesis</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Diaconicon" title="Diaconicon">diaconicon</a></i>, typical for Byzantine architecture.
</p><p>Inside, the intrados of the great <a href="/wiki/Triumphal_arch" title="Triumphal arch">triumphal arch</a> is decorated with fifteen mosaic medallions, depicting <a href="/wiki/Jesus_Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesus Christ">Jesus Christ</a>, the twelve <a href="/wiki/Twelve_Apostles" class="mw-redirect" title="Twelve Apostles">Apostles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Gervasius" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Gervasius">Saint Gervasius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Protasius" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Protasius">Saint Protasius</a>, the sons of Saint Vitale. The <a href="/wiki/Theophany" title="Theophany">theophany</a> was begun in 525 under bishop Ecclesius. It has a great gold fascia with twining flowers, birds, and horns of plenty. Jesus Christ appears, seated on a blue globe in the summit of the vault, robed in purple, with his right hand offering the martyr's crown to Saint Vitale. On the left, Bishop Ecclesius offers a model of the church.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Justinian_and_Theodora_panels">Justinian and Theodora panels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Basilica_of_San_Vitale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Justinian and Theodora panels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the foot of the <a href="/wiki/Apse" title="Apse">apse</a> side walls are two famous mosaic panels, completed in 547.  On the right is a mosaic depicting the East Roman Emperor <a href="/wiki/Justinian_I" title="Justinian I">Justinian I</a>, clad in <a href="/wiki/Tyrian_purple" title="Tyrian purple">Tyrian purple</a> with a golden halo, standing next to court officials, <a href="/wiki/Maximianus_of_Ravenna" title="Maximianus of Ravenna">Bishop Maximian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scholae_Palatinae" title="Scholae Palatinae">palatinae</a> guards and deacons. The halo around his head gives him the same aspect as Christ in the dome of the apse, but is part of the tradition of rendering the imperial family with haloes describe by Ernst <a href="/wiki/Kantorowicz" title="Kantorowicz">Kantorowicz</a> in the <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Two_Bodies" class="mw-redirect" title="King&#39;s Two Bodies">King's Two Bodies</a>.  Justinian himself stands in the middle, with soldiers on his right and clergy on his left, emphasizing that Justinian is the leader of both church and state of his empire. The later insertion of the Bishop Maximian's name above his head suggests that the mosaic may have been modified in 547, replacing the representation of the prior bishop with that of the Maximian.
</p><p>The gold background of the mosaic shows that Justinian and his entourage are inside the church. The figures are placed in a V shape; Justinian is placed in the front and in the middle to show his importance with Bishop Maximian on his left and lesser individuals being placed behind them. This placement can be seen through the overlapping feet of the individuals present in the mosaic.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another panel shows <a href="/wiki/Empress_Theodora" class="mw-redirect" title="Empress Theodora">Empress Theodora</a> solemn and formal, with golden halo, crown and jewels, and a group of court women as well as <a href="/wiki/Eunuchs" class="mw-redirect" title="Eunuchs">eunuchs</a>. The Empress holds the Eucharistic vessel for wine, and her panel differs from that of Justinian in having a more complex background, with a fountain, cupola, and lavish hangings.
</p>
