	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Relativism</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For the physics theory, see <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">Theory of relativity</a>.</div>
<p><b>Relativism</b> is the idea that <a href="/wiki/Points_of_view_(philosophy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Points of view (philosophy)">views</a> are relative to differences in perception and consideration. There is no universal, objective truth according to relativism; rather each point of view has its own truth.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The major categories of relativism vary in their degree of scope and controversy.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Moral_relativism" title="Moral relativism">Moral relativism</a></i> encompasses the differences in moral judgments among people and cultures.<sup id="cite_ref-stanford_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stanford-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Truth" title="Truth">Truth</a> relativism</i> is the doctrine that there are no <a href="/wiki/Universality_(philosophy)" title="Universality (philosophy)">absolute truths</a>, i.e., that truth is always relative to some particular frame of reference, such as a language or a culture (<a href="/wiki/Cultural_relativism" title="Cultural relativism">cultural relativism</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Descriptive relativism seeks to describe the differences among cultures and people without evaluation, while normative relativism evaluates the morality or truthfulness of views within a given framework.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forms_of_relativism">Forms of relativism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relativism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Forms of relativism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anthropological_versus_philosophical_relativism">Anthropological versus philosophical relativism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relativism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Anthropological versus philosophical relativism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Anthropological_relativism" class="mw-redirect" title="Anthropological relativism">Anthropological relativism</a> refers to a <a href="/wiki/Methodology" title="Methodology">methodological</a> stance, in which the researcher suspends (or brackets) his or her own cultural biases while attempting to understand beliefs and behaviors in their local contexts. This has become known as <a href="/wiki/Methodological_relativism" class="mw-redirect" title="Methodological relativism">methodological relativism</a>, and concerns itself specifically with avoiding <a href="/wiki/Ethnocentrism" title="Ethnocentrism">ethnocentrism</a> or the application of one's own cultural standards to the assessment of other cultures.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> This is also the basis of the so-called "<a href="/wiki/Emic" class="mw-redirect" title="Emic">emic</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Etic" class="mw-redirect" title="Etic">etic</a>" distinction, in which:
</p>
<ul><li>An <b>emic</b> or insider account of behavior is a description of a society in terms that are meaningful to the participant or actor's own culture; an emic account is therefore culture-specific, and typically refers to what is considered "<a href="/wiki/Common_sense" title="Common sense">common sense</a>" within the culture under observation.</li>
<li>An <b>etic</b> or outsider account is a description of a society by an observer, in terms that can be applied to other cultures; that is, an etic account is culturally neutral, and typically refers to the conceptual framework of the social scientist. (This is complicated when it is scientific research itself that is under study, or when there is theoretical or terminological disagreement within the social sciences.)</li></ul>
<p>Philosophical relativism, in contrast, asserts that the truth of a proposition depends on the metaphysical, or theoretical frame, or the instrumental method, or the context in which the proposition is expressed, or on the person, groups, or culture who interpret the proposition.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Methodological relativism and philosophical relativism can exist independently from one another, but most anthropologists base their methodological relativism on that of the philosophical variety.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Descriptive_versus_normative_relativism">Descriptive versus normative relativism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relativism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Descriptive versus normative relativism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The concept of relativism also has importance both for <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophers</a> and for <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropologists</a> in another way. In general, anthropologists engage in descriptive relativism ("how things are" or "how things seem"), whereas philosophers engage in <a href="/wiki/Norm_(philosophy)" title="Norm (philosophy)">normative</a> relativism ("how things ought to be"), although there is some overlap (for example, descriptive relativism can pertain to concepts, normative relativism to truth).
</p><p>Descriptive relativism assumes that certain cultural groups have different modes of thought, standards of reasoning, and so forth, and it is the anthropologist's task to describe, but not to evaluate the validity of these principles and practices of a cultural group. It is possible for an anthropologist in his or her fieldwork to be a descriptive relativist about some things that typically concern the philosopher (e.g., ethical principles) but not about others (e.g., logical principles). However, the descriptive relativist's empirical claims about epistemic principles, moral ideals and the like are often countered by anthropological arguments that such things are universal, and much of the recent literature on these matters is explicitly concerned with the extent of, and evidence for, cultural or moral or linguistic or human universals.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The fact that the various species of descriptive relativism are empirical claims, may tempt the philosopher to conclude that they are of little philosophical interest, but there are several reasons why this isn't so. First, some philosophers, notably Kant, argue that certain sorts of cognitive differences between human beings (or even all rational beings) are impossible, so such differences could never be found to obtain in fact, an argument that places a priori limits on what empirical inquiry could discover and on what versions of descriptive relativism could be true. Second, claims about actual differences between groups play a central role in some arguments for normative relativism (for example, arguments for normative ethical relativism often begin with claims that different groups in fact have different moral codes or ideals). Finally, the anthropologist's descriptive account of relativism helps to separate the fixed aspects of human nature from those that can vary, and so a descriptive claim that some important aspect of experience or thought does (or does not) vary across groups of human beings tells us something important about human nature and the human condition.
</p><p>Normative relativism concerns normative or <a href="/wiki/Value_(ethics)" title="Value (ethics)">evaluative</a> claims that modes of thought, standards of reasoning, or the like are only right or wrong relative to a framework. ‘Normative’ is meant in a general sense, applying to a wide range of views; in the case of beliefs, for example, normative correctness equals truth. This does not mean, of course, that framework-relative correctness or truth is always clear, the first challenge being to explain what it amounts to in any given case (e.g., with respect to concepts, truth, epistemic norms). Normative relativism (say, in regard to normative ethical relativism) therefore implies that things (say, ethical claims) are not simply true in themselves, but only have <a href="/wiki/Truth_value" title="Truth value">truth values</a> relative to broader frameworks (say, moral codes). (Many normative ethical relativist arguments run from premises about ethics to conclusions that assert the relativity of truth values, bypassing general claims about the nature of truth, but it is often more illuminating to consider the type of relativism under question directly.)<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Postmodernism_and_relativism">Postmodernism and relativism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relativism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Postmodernism and relativism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "relativism" often comes up in debates over <a href="/wiki/Postmodernism" title="Postmodernism">postmodernism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poststructuralism" class="mw-redirect" title="Poststructuralism">poststructuralism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phenomenology_(philosophy)" title="Phenomenology (philosophy)">phenomenology</a>. Critics of these perspectives often identify advocates with the label "relativism". For example, the <a href="/wiki/Sapir%E2%80%93Whorf_hypothesis" class="mw-redirect" title="Sapir–Whorf hypothesis">Sapir–Whorf hypothesis</a> is often considered a relativist view because it posits that linguistic categories and structures shape the way people view the world. <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Fish" title="Stanley Fish">Stanley Fish</a> has defended postmodernism and relativism.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>These perspectives do not strictly count as relativist in the philosophical sense, because they express agnosticism on the nature of reality and make <a href="/wiki/Epistemological" class="mw-redirect" title="Epistemological">epistemological</a> rather than <a href="/wiki/Ontological" class="mw-redirect" title="Ontological">ontological</a> claims. Nevertheless, the term is useful to differentiate them from <a href="/wiki/Philosophical_realism" title="Philosophical realism">realists</a> who believe that the purpose of philosophy, science, or literary critique is to locate externally true meanings. Important philosophers and theorists such as <a href="/wiki/Michel_Foucault" title="Michel Foucault">Michel Foucault</a>, <a href="/wiki/Max_Stirner" title="Max Stirner">Max Stirner</a>, political movements such as <a href="/wiki/Post-anarchism" title="Post-anarchism">post-anarchism</a> or <a href="/wiki/Post-Marxism" title="Post-Marxism">post-Marxism</a> can also be considered as relativist in this sense - though a better term might be <a href="/wiki/Social_constructivist" class="mw-redirect" title="Social constructivist">social constructivist</a>.
</p><p>The spread and popularity of this kind of "soft" relativism varies between academic disciplines. It has wide support in <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a> and has a majority following in cultural studies. It also has advocates in political theory and political science, sociology, and <a href="/wiki/Continental_philosophy" title="Continental philosophy">continental philosophy</a> (as distinct from Anglo-American analytical philosophy). It has inspired empirical studies of the social construction of meaning such as those associated with labelling theory, which defenders can point to as evidence of the validity of their theories (albeit risking accusations of <a href="/wiki/Performative_contradiction" title="Performative contradiction">performative contradiction</a> in the process). Advocates of this kind of relativism often also claim that recent developments in the natural sciences, such as Heisenberg's <a href="/wiki/Uncertainty_principle" title="Uncertainty principle">uncertainty principle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaos theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Complex_systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Complex systems">complexity theory</a> show that science is now becoming relativistic. However, many scientists who use these methods continue to identify as realist or <a href="/wiki/Post-positivist" class="mw-redirect" title="Post-positivist">post-positivist</a>, and some sharply criticize the association.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_and_contrasting_positions">Related and contrasting positions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relativism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Related and contrasting positions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Relational_theory" title="Relational theory">Relationism</a> is the theory that there are only relations between individual entities, and no intrinsic
properties. Despite the similarity in name, it is held by some to be a position distinct from relativism—for instance, because "statements about relational properties [...] assert an absolute truth about things in the world".<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
On the other hand, others wish to equate relativism, relationism and even <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">relativity</a>, which is a precise theory of relationships between physical objects:<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Nevertheless, "This confluence of relativity theory with relativism became a strong contributing factor in the increasing prominence of relativism".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Whereas previous investigations of science only sought sociological or psychological explanations of failed scientific theories or pathological science, the '<a href="/wiki/Strong_programme" title="Strong programme">strong programme</a>' is more relativistic, assessing scientific truth and falsehood equally in a historic and cultural context.
</p><p>Relativism is not <a href="/wiki/Skepticism" title="Skepticism">skepticism</a>, which superficially resembles relativism, because they both doubt absolute notions of truth. However, whereas skeptics go on to doubt all notions of truth, relativists replace absolute truth with a positive theory of many equally valid relative truths. For the relativist, there is no more to
truth than the right context, or the right personal or cultural belief, so there is a lot of truth in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Catholic_Church_and_relativism">Catholic Church and relativism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relativism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Catholic Church and relativism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>, especially under <a href="/wiki/John_Paul_II" class="mw-redirect" title="John Paul II">John Paul II</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XVI" title="Pope Benedict XVI">Pope Benedict XVI</a>, has identified relativism as one of the most significant problems for faith and morals today.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to the Church and to some theologians, relativism, as a denial of absolute truth, leads to moral license and a denial of the possibility of <a href="/wiki/Sin" title="Sin">sin</a> and of <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a>. Whether moral or epistemological, relativism constitutes a denial of the capacity of the human mind and reason to arrive at truth. Truth, according to Catholic theologians and philosophers (following Aristotle) consists of <i>adequatio rei et intellectus</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Correspondence_theory_of_truth" title="Correspondence theory of truth">correspondence</a> of the mind and reality. Another way of putting it states that the <a href="/wiki/Mind" title="Mind">mind</a> has the same form as reality. This means when the form of the computer in front of someone (the type, color, shape, capacity, etc.) is also the form that is in their mind, then what they know is true because their mind corresponds to objective reality.
</p><p>The denial of an absolute reference, of an <i>axis mundi,</i> denies God, who equates to Absolute Truth, according to these Christian theologians. They link relativism to <a href="/wiki/Secularism" title="Secularism">secularism</a>, an obstruction of religion in <a href="/wiki/Human_condition" title="Human condition">human life.</a>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leo_XIII">Leo XIII</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relativism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Leo XIII">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_XIII" title="Pope Leo XIII">Pope Leo XIII</a> (1810–1903) was the first known Pope to use the word relativism in the encyclical <i><a href="/wiki/Humanum_genus" title="Humanum genus">Humanum genus</a></i> (1884). Leo XIII condemned <a href="/wiki/Freemasonry" title="Freemasonry">Freemasonry</a> and claimed that its philosophical and political system was largely based on relativism.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="John_Paul_II">John Paul II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relativism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: John Paul II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">John Paul II</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Veritatis_Splendor" class="mw-redirect" title="Veritatis Splendor">Veritatis Splendor</a></i>
</p>
<dl><dd>As is immediately evident, the crisis of truth is not unconnected with this development. Once the idea of a universal truth about the good, knowable by human reason, is lost, inevitably the notion of conscience also changes. Conscience is no longer considered in its primordial reality as an act of a person's intelligence, the function of which is to apply the universal knowledge of the good in a specific situation and thus to express a judgment about the right conduct to be chosen here and now. Instead, there is a tendency to grant to the individual conscience the prerogative of independently determining the criteria of good and evil and then acting accordingly. Such an outlook is quite congenial to an individualist ethic, wherein each individual is faced with his own truth, different from the truth of others. Taken to its extreme consequences, this individualism leads to a denial of the very idea of human nature.</dd></dl>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Evangelium_Vitae" class="mw-redirect" title="Evangelium Vitae">Evangelium Vitae</a></i> (The Gospel of Life), he says:
</p>
<dl><dd>Freedom negates and destroys itself, and becomes a factor leading to the destruction of others, when it no longer recognizes and respects its essential link with the truth. When freedom, out of a desire to emancipate itself from all forms of tradition and authority, shuts out even the most obvious evidence of an objective and universal truth, which is the foundation of personal and social life, then the person ends up by no longer taking as the sole and indisputable point of reference for his own choices the truth about good and evil, but only his subjective and changeable opinion or, indeed, his selfish interest and whim.</dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Benedict_XVI">Benedict XVI</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relativism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Benedict XVI">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In April 2005, in his homily<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> during Mass prior to the conclave which would elect him as <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Pope</a>, then <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_Joseph_Ratzinger" class="mw-redirect" title="Cardinal Joseph Ratzinger">Cardinal Joseph Ratzinger</a> talked about the world "moving towards a dictatorship of relativism":
</p>
<dl><dd>How many winds of doctrine we have known in recent decades, how many ideological currents, how many ways of thinking. The small boat of thought of many Christians has often been tossed about by these waves – thrown from one extreme to the other: from Marxism to liberalism, even to libertinism; from collectivism to radical individualism; from atheism to a vague religious mysticism; from agnosticism to syncretism, and so forth. Every day new sects are created and what <a href="/wiki/Saint_Paul" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Paul">Saint Paul</a> says about human trickery comes true, with cunning which tries to draw those into error (cf <a href="/wiki/Ephesians" class="mw-redirect" title="Ephesians">Ephesians</a> 4, 14). Having a clear Faith, based on the Creed of the Church, is often labeled today as a fundamentalism. Whereas, relativism, which is letting oneself be tossed and "swept along by every wind of teaching", looks like the only attitude acceptable to today's standards. We are moving towards a dictatorship of relativism which does not recognize anything as certain and which has as its highest goal one's own ego and one's own desires. However, we have a different goal: the Son of God, true man. He is the measure of true humanism. Being an "Adult" means having a faith which does not follow the waves of today's fashions or the latest novelties. A faith which is deeply rooted in friendship with <a href="/wiki/Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Christ">Christ</a> is adult and mature. It is this friendship which opens us up to all that is good and gives us the knowledge to judge true from false, and deceit from truth.</dd></dl>
<p>On June 6, 2005, Pope Benedict XVI told educators:
</p>
<dl><dd>Today, a particularly insidious obstacle to the task of education is the massive presence in our society and culture of that relativism which, recognizing nothing as definitive, leaves as the ultimate criterion only the self with its desires. And under the semblance of freedom it becomes a prison for each one, for it separates people from one another, locking each person into his or her own 'ego'.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>Then during the <a href="/wiki/World_Youth_Day_2005" title="World Youth Day 2005">World Youth Day</a> in August 2005, he also traced to relativism the problems produced by the communist and sexual revolutions, and provided a counter-counter argument.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>In the last century we experienced revolutions with a common programme–expecting nothing more from God, they assumed total responsibility for the cause of the world in order to change it. And this, as we saw, meant that a human and partial point of view was always taken as an absolute guiding principle. Absolutizing what is not absolute but relative is called totalitarianism. It does not liberate man, but takes away his dignity and enslaves him. It is not ideologies that save the world, but only a return to the living God, our Creator, the Guarantor of our freedom, the Guarantor of what is really good and true.</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relativism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span id="paradox_of_relativism"></span>A common argument<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> against relativism suggests that it inherently <a href="/wiki/Self-refuting_idea" title="Self-refuting idea">contradicts, refutes, or stultifies itself</a>: the statement "all is relative" classes either as a relative statement or as an absolute one. If it is relative, then this statement does not rule out absolutes. If the statement is <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/absolute" class="extiw" title="wikt:absolute">absolute</a>, on the other hand, then it provides an example of an absolute statement, proving that not all truths are relative. However, this argument against relativism only applies to relativism that positions truth as relative–i.e. epistemological/truth-value relativism. More specifically, it is only extreme forms of epistemological relativism that can come in for this criticism as there are many epistemological relativists<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (July 2015)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> who posit that some aspects of what is regarded as factually "true" are not universal, yet still accept that other universal truths exist (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Gas_laws" title="Gas laws">gas laws</a> or moral laws).
</p><p>Another argument against relativism posits a <a href="/wiki/Natural_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural Law">Natural Law</a>. Simply put, the physical universe works under basic principles: the "Laws of Nature". Some contend that a natural Moral Law may also exist, for example as argued by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Dawkins" title="Richard Dawkins">Richard Dawkins</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_God_Delusion" title="The God Delusion">The God Delusion</a></i> (2006)<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> and addressed by <a href="/wiki/C._S._Lewis" title="C. S. Lewis">C. S. Lewis</a> in "<a href="/wiki/Mere_Christianity" title="Mere Christianity">Mere Christianity</a>" (1952).<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Dawkins said "I think we face an equal but much more sinister challenge from the left, in the shape of cultural relativism - the view that scientific truth is only one kind of truth and it is not to be especially privileged".<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
Philosopher <a href="/wiki/Hilary_Putnam" title="Hilary Putnam">Hilary Putnam</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> among others,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> states that some forms of relativism make it impossible to believe one is in error. If there is no truth beyond an individual's <a href="/wiki/Belief" title="Belief">belief</a> that something is true, then an individual cannot hold their own beliefs to be false or mistaken. A related criticism is that relativizing truth to individuals destroys the distinction between truth and belief.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Views">Views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relativism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indian_religions">Indian religions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relativism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Indian religions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Indian_religions" title="Indian religions">Indian religions</a> tend to view the perceivable universe and cosmos as relativistic.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> <a href="/wiki/Mahavira" title="Mahavira">Mahavira</a> (599-527 BC), the 24th <a href="/wiki/Tirthankara" title="Tirthankara">Tirthankara</a> of <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">Jainism</a>, developed an early philosophy regarding relativism and <a href="/wiki/Subjectivism" title="Subjectivism">subjectivism</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Anekantavada" title="Anekantavada">Anekantavada</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hindu religion</a> has no theological difficulties in accepting degrees of truth in other religions. A <a href="/wiki/Rig_Veda" class="mw-redirect" title="Rig Veda">Rig Vedic</a> hymn states that "Truth is One, though the sages tell it variously." (Ékam sat vipra bahudā vadanti)
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Madhyamaka" title="Madhyamaka">Madhyamaka Buddhism</a>, which forms the basis for many <a href="/wiki/Mahayana" title="Mahayana">Mahayana</a> Buddhist schools and was founded by <a href="/wiki/Nagarjuna" title="Nagarjuna">Nagarjuna</a>, discerns two levels of truth, absolute and relative. The <a href="/wiki/Two_truths_doctrine" title="Two truths doctrine">two truths doctrine</a> states that there is <i>Relative</i> or common-sense truth, which describes our daily experience of a concrete world, and <i>Ultimate</i> truth, which describes the ultimate reality as <a href="/wiki/Sunyata" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunyata">sunyata</a>, empty of concrete and inherent characteristics. The conventional truth may be interpreted as "obscurative truth" or "that which obscures the true nature" as a result. It is constituted by the appearances of mistaken awareness. Conventional truth would be the appearance that includes a duality of apprehender and apprehended, and objects perceived within that. Ultimate truths, are phenomena free from the duality of apprehender and apprehended.<sup id="cite_ref-LevinsonAug06_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LevinsonAug06-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Sikhism" title="Sikhism">Sikhism</a> the <a href="/wiki/Sikh_Gurus" class="mw-redirect" title="Sikh Gurus">Gurus</a> (spiritual teacher ) have propagated the message of "many paths" leading to the <a href="/wiki/Ek_Onkar" class="mw-redirect" title="Ek Onkar">one God</a> and ultimate <a href="/wiki/Salvation" title="Salvation">salvation</a> for all souls who tread on the path of <a href="/wiki/Righteousness" title="Righteousness">righteousness</a>. They have supported the view that proponents of all faiths can, by doing good and virtuous deeds and by remembering the <a href="/wiki/Lord" title="Lord">Lord</a>, certainly achieve salvation. The students of the Sikh faith are told to accept all leading faiths as possible vehicles for attaining spiritual enlightenment provided the faithful study, ponder and practice the teachings of their prophets and leaders. The holy book of the <a href="/wiki/Sikh" class="mw-redirect" title="Sikh">Sikhs</a> called the <a href="/wiki/Sri_Guru_Granth_Sahib" class="mw-redirect" title="Sri Guru Granth Sahib">Sri Guru Granth Sahib</a> says: "Do not say that the Vedas, the Bible and the Koran are false. Those who do not contemplate them are false." <a href="/wiki/Guru_Granth_Sahib" title="Guru Granth Sahib">Guru Granth Sahib</a> page 1350;<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> later stating "The seconds, minutes, and hours, days, weeks and months, and the various seasons originate from the one Sun; O nanak, in just the same way, the many forms originate from the Creator." <a href="/wiki/Guru_Granth_Sahib" title="Guru Granth Sahib">Guru Granth Sahib</a> page 12,13.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sophists">Sophists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relativism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Sophists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sophists" class="mw-redirect" title="Sophists">Sophists</a> are considered the founding fathers of relativism in the <a href="/wiki/Western_World" class="mw-redirect" title="Western World">Western World</a>. Elements of relativism emerged among the <a href="/wiki/Sophist" title="Sophist">Sophists</a> in the 5th century <a href="/wiki/Before_Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Before Christ">BC</a>. Notably, it was <a href="/wiki/Protagoras" title="Protagoras">Protagoras</a> who coined the phrase, "Man is the measure of all things: of things which are, that they are, and of things which are not, that they are not." The thinking of the Sophists is mainly known through their opponents, <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> and <a href="/wiki/Socrates" title="Socrates">Socrates</a>.  In a well known paraphrased dialogue with Socrates, Protagoras said: "What is true for you is true for you, and what is true for me is true for me."<sup id="cite_ref-socratesdialogue_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-socratesdialogue-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bernard_Crick">Bernard Crick</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relativism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Bernard Crick">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another important advocate of relativism, <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Crick" title="Bernard Crick">Bernard Crick</a>, a British political scientist, wrote the book <i>In Defence of Politics</i> (first published in 1962), suggesting the inevitability of moral conflict between people. Crick stated that only <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethics</a> could resolve such conflict, and when that occurred in public it resulted in <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a>. Accordingly, Crick saw the process of <a href="/wiki/Dispute_resolution" title="Dispute resolution">dispute resolution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harms_reduction" class="mw-redirect" title="Harms reduction">harms reduction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mediation" title="Mediation">mediation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Peacemaking" title="Peacemaking">peacemaking</a> as central to all of moral philosophy. He became an important influence on the <a href="/wiki/Feminists" class="mw-redirect" title="Feminists">feminists</a> and later on the <a href="/wiki/Green_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Green movement">Greens</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Paul_Feyerabend">Paul Feyerabend</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relativism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Paul Feyerabend">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The philosopher of science <a href="/wiki/Paul_Feyerabend" title="Paul Feyerabend">Paul Feyerabend</a> is often considered to be a relativist, though he denied being one.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Feyerabend argued that modern science suffers from being methodologically monistic (the belief that only a single methodology can produce <a href="/wiki/Scientific_progress" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific progress">scientific progress</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Feyerabend summarises his case in his work <a href="/wiki/Against_Method" title="Against Method">Against Method</a> with the phrase "anything goes".<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>In an aphorism [Feyerabend] often repeated, "potentially every culture is all cultures". This is intended to convey that world views are not hermetically closed, since their leading concepts have an "ambiguity" - better, an open-endedness - which enables people from other cultures to engage with them. [...] It follows that relativism, understood as the doctrine that truth is relative to closed systems, can get no purchase.  [...] For Feyerabend, both hermetic relativism and its absolutist rival [realism] serve, in their different ways, to "devalue human existence". The former encourages that unsavoury brand of political correctness which takes the refusal to criticise "other cultures" to the extreme of condoning murderous dictatorship and barbaric practices. The latter, especially in its favoured contemporary form of "scientific realism", with the excessive prestige it affords to the abstractions of "the monster 'science'", is in bed with a politics which likewise disdains variety, richness and everyday individuality - a politics which likewise "hides" its norms behind allegedly neutral facts, "blunts choices and imposes laws".<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thomas_Kuhn">Thomas Kuhn</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relativism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Thomas Kuhn">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Samuel_Kuhn" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Samuel Kuhn">Thomas Kuhn</a>'s philosophy of science, as expressed in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Structure_of_Scientific_Revolutions" title="The Structure of Scientific Revolutions">The Structure of Scientific Revolutions</a></i> is often interpreted as relativistic. He claimed that as well as progressing steadily and incrementally ("<a href="/wiki/Normal_science" title="Normal science">normal science</a>"), science undergoes periodic revolutions or "<a href="/wiki/Paradigm_shift" title="Paradigm shift">paradigm shifts</a>", leaving scientists working in different paradigms with difficulty in even communicating. Thus the truth of a claim, or the existence of a posited entity is relative to the paradigm employed. However, it isn't necessary for him to embrace relativism because every paradigm presupposes the prior, building upon itself through history and so on. This leads to there being a fundamental, incremental, and referential structure of development which is not relative but again, fundamental.
</p>
<dl><dd>From these remarks, one thing is however certain: Kuhn is not saying that incommensurable theories cannot be compared - what they can’t be is compared in terms of a system of common measure. He very plainly says that they can be compared, and he reiterates this repeatedly in later work, in a (mostly in vain) effort to avert the crude and sometimes catastrophic misinterpretations he suffered from mainstream philosophers and post-modern relativists alike.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>But Thomas Kuhn denied the accusation of being a relativist later in his postscript.
</p>
<dl><dd>scientific development is ... a unidirectional and irreversible process. Latter scientific theories are better than earlier ones for solving puzzles ... That is not a relativist's position, and it displays the sense in which I am a convinced believer in scientific progress.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>Some have argued that one can also read Kuhn's work as essentially positivist in its ontology: the revolutions he posits are epistemological, lurching toward a presumably 'better' understanding of an objective reality through the lens presented by the new paradigm. However, a number of passages in Structures do indeed appear to be distinctly relativist, and to directly challenge the notion of an objective reality and the ability of science to progress towards an ever-greater grasp of it, particularly through the process of paradigm change.
</p>
<dl><dd>In the sciences there need not be progress of another sort. We may, to be more precise, have to relinquish the notion, explicit or implicit, that changes of paradigm carry scientists and those who learn from them closer and closer to the truth.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
<dl><dd>We are all deeply accustomed to seeing science as the one enterprise that draws constantly nearer to some goal set by nature in advance. But need there be any such goal? Can we not account for both science’s existence and its success in terms of evolution from the community’s state of knowledge at any given time? Does it really help to imagine that there is some one full, objective, true account of nature and that the proper measure of scientific achievement is the extent to which it brings us closer to that ultimate goal?<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="George_Lakoff_and_Mark_Johnson">George Lakoff and Mark Johnson</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relativism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: George Lakoff and Mark Johnson">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/George_Lakoff" title="George Lakoff">George Lakoff</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mark_Johnson_(professor)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mark Johnson (professor)">Mark Johnson</a> define relativism in their book <i><a href="/wiki/Metaphors_We_Live_By" title="Metaphors We Live By">Metaphors We Live By</a></i> as the rejection of both <a href="/wiki/Subject_(philosophy)" title="Subject (philosophy)">subjectivism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metaphysical_realism" class="mw-redirect" title="Metaphysical realism">metaphysical objectivism</a> in order to focus on the relationship between them, i.e. the <a href="/wiki/Metaphors" class="mw-redirect" title="Metaphors">metaphor</a> by which we relate our current experience to our previous experience. In particular, Lakoff and Johnson characterize "objectivism" as a "<a href="/wiki/Straw_man" title="Straw man">straw man</a>", and, to a lesser degree, criticize the views of <a href="/wiki/Karl_Popper" title="Karl Popper">Karl Popper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">Kant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (September 2010)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Robert_Nozick">Robert Nozick</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relativism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Robert Nozick">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In his book <i><a href="/wiki/Invariances" title="Invariances">Invariances</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Nozick" title="Robert Nozick">Robert Nozick</a> expresses a complex set of theories about the absolute and the relative. He thinks the absolute/relative distinction should be recast in terms of an invariant/variant distinction, where there are many things a proposition can be invariant with regard to or vary with. He thinks it is coherent for truth to be relative, and speculates that it might vary with time. He thinks necessity is an unobtainable notion, but can be approximated by robust invariance across a variety of conditions—although we can never identify a proposition that is invariant with regard to everything. Finally, he is not particularly warm to one of the most famous forms of relativism, <a href="/wiki/Moral_relativism" title="Moral relativism">moral relativism</a>, preferring an evolutionary account.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Joseph_Margolis">Joseph Margolis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relativism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Joseph Margolis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Joseph_Margolis" title="Joseph Margolis">Joseph Margolis</a> advocates a view he calls "robust relativism" and defends it in his books: <i>Historied Thought, Constructed World</i>, Chapter 4 (California, 1995) and <i>The Truth about Relativism</i> (Blackwells, 1991). He opens his account by stating that our logics should depend on what we take to be the nature of the sphere to which we wish to apply our logics. Holding that there can be no distinctions which are not "privileged" between the <a href="/wiki/Alethic_possibility" class="mw-redirect" title="Alethic possibility">alethic</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ontic" title="Ontic">ontic</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Epistemic" class="mw-redirect" title="Epistemic">epistemic</a>, he maintains that a <a href="/wiki/Many_valued_logic" class="mw-redirect" title="Many valued logic">many valued logic</a> just might be the most apt for <a href="/wiki/Aesthetics" title="Aesthetics">aesthetics</a> or <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a> since, because in these practices, we are loath to hold to simple <a href="/wiki/Principle_of_bivalence" title="Principle of bivalence">binary logic</a>; and he also holds that many-valued logic is relativistic. (This is perhaps an unusual definition of "relativistic". Compare with his comments on "relationism"). "True" and "False" as mutually exclusive and exhaustive judgements on <a href="/wiki/Hamlet" title="Hamlet">Hamlet</a>, for instance, really does seem absurd. A many valued logic—"apt", "reasonable", "likely", and so on—seems intuitively more applicable to Hamlet interpretation. Where apparent contradictions arise between such interpretations, we might call the interpretations "incongruent", rather than dubbing either "false", because using many-valued logic implies that a measured value is a mixture of two extreme possibilities. Using the subset of many-valued logic, <a href="/wiki/Fuzzy_logic" title="Fuzzy logic">fuzzy logic</a>, it can be said that various interpretations can be represented by membership in more than one possible truth sets simultaneously. Fuzzy logic is therefore probably the best mathematical structure for understanding "robust relativism" and has been interpreted by <a href="/wiki/Bart_Kosko" title="Bart Kosko">Bart Kosko</a> as philosophically being related to Zen Buddhism.
</p><p>It was <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> who held that relativism implied we should, sticking with appearances only, end up contradicting ourselves somewhere if we could apply all attributes to all <i>ousiai</i> (<a href="/wiki/Being" title="Being">beings</a>). Aristotle, however, made non-contradiction dependent upon his <a href="/wiki/Essentialism" title="Essentialism">essentialism</a>. If his essentialism is false, then so too is his ground for disallowing relativism. (Subsequent philosophers have found other reasons for supporting the principle of non-contradiction).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (December 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Beginning with <a href="/wiki/Protagoras" title="Protagoras">Protagoras</a> and invoking <a href="/wiki/Charles_Sanders_Peirce" title="Charles Sanders Peirce">Charles Sanders Peirce</a>, Margolis shows that the historic struggle to discredit relativism is an attempt to impose an unexamined belief in the world's essentially rigid rule-like nature. Plato and Aristotle merely attacked "relationalism"—the doctrine of true-for l or true for k, and the like, where l and k are different speakers or different worlds, or the something similar (Most philosophers would call this position "relativism"). For Margolis, "true" means true; that is, the alethic use of "true" remains untouched. However, in real world contexts, and context is ubiquitous in the real world, we must apply truth values. Here, in epistemic terms, we<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (July 2011)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> might retire "true" <i>tout court</i> as an evaluation and keep "false". The rest of our value-judgements could be graded from "extremely plausible" down to "false". Judgements which on a bivalent logic would be incompatible or contradictory are further seen as "incongruent", though one may well have more weight than the other. In short, relativistic logic is not, or need not be, the bugbear it is often presented to be. It may simply be the best type of logic to apply to certain very uncertain spheres of real experiences in the world (although some sort of logic needs to be applied to make that judgement). Those who swear by <a href="/wiki/Bivalent_logic" class="mw-redirect" title="Bivalent logic">bivalent</a> logic might simply be the ultimate keepers of the great fear of the flux.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Richard_Rorty">Richard Rorty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relativism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Richard Rorty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Philosopher <a href="/wiki/Richard_Rorty" title="Richard Rorty">Richard Rorty</a> has a somewhat <a href="/wiki/Paradox" title="Paradox">paradoxical</a> role in the debate over relativism: he is criticized for his relativistic views by many commentators, but has always denied that relativism applies to much anybody, being nothing more than a Platonic scarecrow.  Rorty claims, rather, that he is a <a href="/wiki/Pragmatism" title="Pragmatism">pragmatist</a>, and that to construe pragmatism as relativism is to <a href="/wiki/Beg_the_question" class="mw-redirect" title="Beg the question">beg the question</a>.
</p>
<dl><dd>'"Relativism" is the traditional epithet applied to pragmatism by realists'<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
<dl><dd>'"Relativism" is the view that every belief on a certain topic, or perhaps about any topic, is as good as every other. No one holds this view. Except for the occasional cooperative freshman, one cannot find anybody who says that two incompatible opinions on an important topic are equally good. The philosophers who get called 'relativists' are those who say that the grounds for choosing between such opinions are less algorithmic than had been thought.'<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
<dl><dd>'In short, my strategy for escaping the self-referential difficulties into which "the Relativist" keeps getting himself is to move everything over from epistemology and metaphysics into cultural politics, from claims to knowledge and appeals to self-evidence to suggestions about what we should try.'<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>Rorty takes a <a href="/wiki/Deflationary_theory_of_truth" title="Deflationary theory of truth">deflationary</a> attitude to <a href="/wiki/Truth" title="Truth">truth</a>, believing there is nothing of interest to be said about truth in general, including the contention that it is generally subjective. He also argues that the notion of <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_justification" title="Theory of justification">warrant</a> or justification can do most of the work traditionally assigned to the concept of truth, and that justification <i>is</i> relative; justification is justification to an audience, for Rorty.
</p><p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Contingency,_Irony,_and_Solidarity" title="Contingency, Irony, and Solidarity">Contingency, Irony, and Solidarity</a></i> he argues that the debate between so-called relativists and so-called objectivists is beside the point because they don't have enough premises in common for either side to prove anything to the other.
</p>
