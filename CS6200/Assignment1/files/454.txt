	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Grandparent</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Grandad", "Grandfather", "Grandma", and "Grandpa" redirect here. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Grandad_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Grandad (disambiguation)">Grandad (disambiguation)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grandfather_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Grandfather (disambiguation)">Grandfather (disambiguation)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grandma_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Grandma (disambiguation)">Grandma (disambiguation)</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Grandpa_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Grandpa (disambiguation)">Grandpa (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Favorite_by_Georgios_Iakovidis.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/The_Favorite_by_Georgios_Iakovidis.jpg/220px-The_Favorite_by_Georgios_Iakovidis.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="295" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/The_Favorite_by_Georgios_Iakovidis.jpg/330px-The_Favorite_by_Georgios_Iakovidis.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/The_Favorite_by_Georgios_Iakovidis.jpg/440px-The_Favorite_by_Georgios_Iakovidis.jpg 2x" data-file-width="959" data-file-height="1284" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Favorite_by_Georgios_Iakovidis.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>The Favorite</i> – Grandfather and Grandson, by <a href="/wiki/Georgios_Jakobides" title="Georgios Jakobides">Georgios Jakobides</a> (1890)</div></div></div>
<p><b>Grandparents</b> are the <a href="/wiki/Parent" title="Parent">parents</a> of a person's <a href="/wiki/Father" title="Father">father</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mother" title="Mother">mother</a> – <a href="/wiki/Paternal" class="mw-redirect" title="Paternal">paternal</a> or <a href="/wiki/Maternal" class="mw-redirect" title="Maternal">maternal</a>.  Every sexually-reproducing living organism who is not a <a href="/wiki/Genetic_chimera" class="mw-redirect" title="Genetic chimera">genetic chimera</a> has a maximum of four <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetic</a> grandparents, eight genetic great-grandparents, sixteen genetic great-great-grandparents, thirty-two genetic great-great-great-grandparents, etc.  In the history of modern humanity, around 30,000 years ago, the number of modern humans who lived to be a grandparent increased.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  It is not known for certain what spurred this increase in longevity but largely results in the improved medical technology and living standard,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> but it is generally believed that a key consequence of three <a href="/wiki/Generation" title="Generation">generations</a> being alive together was the preservation of information which could otherwise have been lost; an example of this important information might have been where to find water in times of drought.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In cases where parents are unwilling or unable to provide adequate care for their children (e.g., death of the parents, financial obstacles, marriage problems<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>), grandparents often take on the role of primary caregivers. Even when this is not the case, and particularly in traditional cultures, grandparents often have a direct and clear role in relation to the raising, care and <a href="/wiki/Nurture" class="mw-redirect" title="Nurture">nurture</a> of <a href="/wiki/Child" title="Child">children</a>. Grandparents are second-degree relatives and share 25% genetic overlap.
</p><p>A step-grandparent can be the step-parent of the parent or the step-parent's parent or the step-parent's step-parent (though technically this might be called a step-step-grandparent). The various words for grandparents at times may also be used to refer to any elderly person, especially the terms <i>gramps</i>, <i>granny</i>, <i>grandfather</i>, <i>grandmother</i>, <i>nan</i>, <i>maw-maw</i>, <i>paw-paw</i> and others which families make up themselves.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Titles">Titles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grandparent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Titles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:277px;"><a href="/wiki/File:GrandfatherAndScooter.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/GrandfatherAndScooter.JPG/275px-GrandfatherAndScooter.JPG" decoding="async" width="275" height="210" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/GrandfatherAndScooter.JPG/413px-GrandfatherAndScooter.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/GrandfatherAndScooter.JPG/550px-GrandfatherAndScooter.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2203" data-file-height="1685" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:GrandfatherAndScooter.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A grandfather teaching his granddaughter to use a <a href="/wiki/Kick_scooter" title="Kick scooter">kick scooter</a></div></div></div>
<p>When used as a noun (e.g., "... a grandparent walked by"), grandfather and grandmother are usually used, although forms such as grandma/grandpa, granny/granddaddy or even nan/pop are sometimes used. When preceded by "my ..." (e.g., "... my grandpa walked by"), all forms are common (anywhere from "... my grandfather ..." to "... my Gramps ..."). All forms can be used in plural, but Gramps (<a href="/wiki/Plural" title="Plural">plural</a> Gramps) is rare.
</p><p>In writing, <i>Grandfather</i> and <i>Grandmother</i> are most common, but very rare as a form of address. In speech, <i>Grandpa</i> and <i>Grandma</i> are commonly used in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. In <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and, particularly prevalent in the Canadian province of <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland and Labrador</a>, <i>Nan</i>, <i>Nana</i>, <i>Nanna</i>, <i>Nanny</i>, <i>Gran</i> and <i>Granny</i> and other variations are often used for grandmother in both writing and speech.
</p><p>In many parts of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, maternal grandparents are called <i>Nana</i> and <i>Nani</i>. Similarly, paternal grandparents are called <i>Dada</i> and <i>Dadi</i>. 
One's parents' maternal grandparents are called <i>Par-nani</i> and <i>Par-nana</i>. On similar lines, parents' paternal grandparents are called <i>Par-dadi</i> and <i>Par-dada.</i>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Child_pushing_grandmother_on_plastic_tricycle.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Child_pushing_grandmother_on_plastic_tricycle.jpg/220px-Child_pushing_grandmother_on_plastic_tricycle.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Child_pushing_grandmother_on_plastic_tricycle.jpg/330px-Child_pushing_grandmother_on_plastic_tricycle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Child_pushing_grandmother_on_plastic_tricycle.jpg/440px-Child_pushing_grandmother_on_plastic_tricycle.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Child_pushing_grandmother_on_plastic_tricycle.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A grandmother playing with her grandson</div></div></div>
<p>Numerous other variants exist, such as <i>Gramp</i>, <i>Gramps</i>, <i>Grampa</i>, <i>Grandpap</i>, <i>Granda</i>, <i>Grampy</i>, <i>Granddad</i>, <i>Grandad</i>, <i>Granddaddy</i>, <i>Grandpappy</i>, <i>Pop(s)</i>,  <i>Pap,</i> <i>Pappy</i>, and <i>Pawpaw</i> for grandfather; <i>Grandmom</i>, <i>Grandmama</i>, <i>Grama</i>, <i>Granny</i>, <i>Gran</i>, <i>Nanny</i>, <i>Nan</i>, <i>Mammaw</i> and <i>Grammy</i> for grandmother. <i>Gogo</i> can be used for either, etc.
</p><p>Given that people may have two living sets of grandparents, some confusion arises from calling two people "grandma" or "grandpa", so often two of the other terms listed above are used for one set of grandparents. Another common solution is to call grandparents by their first names ("Grandpa George", "Grandma Anne", etc.) or by their family names ("Grandpa Jones", "Grandma Smith"). In North America, many families call one set of grandparents by their ethnic names (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Hispanic" title="Hispanic">Hispanic</a> grandparents might be called <i>abuelo</i> and <i>abuela</i> or "abuelito" and "abuelita", <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a> grandparents might be called <i>papi</i> and <i>mamie</i>, <a href="/wiki/Italian_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian people">Italian</a> grandparents might be called <i>nonno</i> and <i>nonna</i>, or <a href="/wiki/Dutch_people" title="Dutch people">Dutch</a> and <a href="/wiki/German_people" class="mw-redirect" title="German people">German</a> grandparents might be called <i>Opa</i> and <i>Oma</i>. In <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a> pepee or petje and memee or metje are most used). In <a href="/wiki/Friesland" title="Friesland">Friesland</a>, a common pair is pake and beppe. <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">Mandarin</a>-speaking <a href="/wiki/Chinese_people" title="Chinese people">Chinese</a> Americans refer to maternal grandparents as <i>wài pó</i> (外婆) and <i>wài gōng</i> (外公) and paternal grandparents as <i>nǎi nǎi</i> (奶奶) and <i>yé yé</i> (爷爷). In the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, grandparents are called <i>lolo</i> (grandfather) and <i>lola</i> (grandmother), respectively.
</p><p>Languages and cultures with more specific <a href="/wiki/Kinship_terminology" title="Kinship terminology">kinship terminology</a> than English may distinguish between paternal grandparents and maternal grandparents. For example, in the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish language</a> there is no single word for "grandmother"; the mother's mother is termed <i>mormor</i> and the father's mother is termed <i>farmor</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
However, the other <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Scandinavian languages">Scandinavian languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian</a>, use words which specifies the kinship like in Swedish (identically spelled among all three languages), as well as using common terms similar to grandmother (Danish: bedstemor, Norwegian: bestemor).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Great-grandparents_and_beyond">Great-grandparents and beyond</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grandparent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Great-grandparents and beyond">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Babur_Seeks_His_Grandmothers_Advice.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Babur_Seeks_His_Grandmothers_Advice.jpg/220px-Babur_Seeks_His_Grandmothers_Advice.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="342" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Babur_Seeks_His_Grandmothers_Advice.jpg/330px-Babur_Seeks_His_Grandmothers_Advice.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Babur_Seeks_His_Grandmothers_Advice.jpg/440px-Babur_Seeks_His_Grandmothers_Advice.jpg 2x" data-file-width="662" data-file-height="1028" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Babur_Seeks_His_Grandmothers_Advice.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Timurid_dynasty" title="Timurid dynasty">Timurid</a> conqueror <a href="/wiki/Babur" title="Babur">Babur</a> seeks the advice of his grandmother.</div></div></div>
<p>The parents of a grandparent, or the grandparents of a parent, are called the same names as grandparents (grandfather/-mother, grandpa/-ma, granddad/-ma, etc.) with the prefix <i>great-</i> added, with an additional <i>great-</i> added for each additional generation. One's great-grandparent's parents would be "great-great-grandparents".
</p><p>To avoid a proliferation of "greats" when discussing genealogical trees, one may also use <a href="/wiki/Ordinal_number_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ordinal number (linguistics)">ordinals</a> instead of multiple "greats"; thus a "great-great-grandfather" would be the "second great-grandfather", and a "great-great-great-grandfather" would be a third great-grandfather, and so on. This system is used by some genealogical websites such as Geni.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> One may also use <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_number_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cardinal number (linguistics)">cardinal numbers</a> for numbering greats, for example, great-great-great-grandmother becomes 3×-great-grandmother.
</p><p>Individuals who share the same great-grandparents but are not siblings or <a href="/wiki/Cousin#First_cousins" title="Cousin">first cousins</a> are called "second cousins" to each other, as second cousins have grandparents who are siblings. Similarly, "third cousins" would have great-grandparents who are siblings.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_term">History of the term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grandparent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History of the term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The use of the prefix "grand-" dates from the early 13th century, from the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman_language" title="Anglo-Norman language">Anglo-French</a> <i>graund</i>. The term was used as a translation of <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>magnus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The prefix "great-" represents a direct translation of Anglo-French <i>graund</i> and Latin <i>magnus</i> to English.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a>, the prefixes <i>ealde-</i> (old) and <i>ieldra-</i> (elder) were used (<i>ealdefæder</i>/<i>-mōdor</i> and <i>ieldrafæder</i>/<i>-mōdor</i>). A great-grandfather was called a <i>þridda fæder</i> (third father), a great-great-grandfather a <i>fēowerða fæder</i> (fourth father), etc.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Variation_of_terms">Variation of terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grandparent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Variation of terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><b>maternal grandmother</b>: mother's mother.</li>
<li><b>maternal grandfather</b>: mother's father.</li>
<li><b>paternal grandmother</b>: father's mother.</li>
<li><b>paternal grandfather</b>: father's father.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grandparental_involvement_in_childcare">Grandparental involvement in childcare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grandparent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Grandparental involvement in childcare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Grandparents are changing their roles in contemporary world,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> especially they are becoming increasingly involved in childcare. Since 2007, approximately one-third of children in the U.S. live in a household consisting of both parents and a grandparent.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Around 67% of these households are also maintained by either two grandparents, or a grandmother.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Likewise, more than 40% of grandparents across 11 European countries care for their grandchildren in the absence of the parents.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In Britain, around 63% of grandparents care for their grandchildren who are under 16 years old.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Grandparent involvement is also common in Eastern societies. For instance, 48% of grandparents in Hong Kong reported that they are taking care of their grandchildren.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In China, around 58% of Chinese grandparents who are aged 45 or older are involved in childcare.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In Singapore, 40% of children from birth to three years old are cared by their grandparents and this percentage is still increasing.<sup id="cite_ref-Thang_548–570_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thang_548–570-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In South Korea, 53% of children under the age of 6 years old are cared by their grandparents.<sup id="cite_ref-Kim_1–13_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kim_1–13-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Therefore, grandparents taking care of their grandchildren has become a prevalent phenomenon around the world.
</p><p>There are a few reasons why grandparent involvement is becoming more prevalent. First, life expectancy has increased while fertility rates have decreased. This means that more children are growing up while their grandparents are still alive, whom can become involved in childcare.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In addition, the reduced fertility rates mean that grandparents can devote more attention and resources to their only grandchildren.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Second, more mothers are involved in the workforce, and thus, other caregivers need to be present to care for the child.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> For instance, in Hong Kong, 55% of grandparents reported that they took care of their grandchild because his or her parents have to work.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In South Korea, 53% of working mother reported that they once received child care services from their parents.<sup id="cite_ref-Kim_1–13_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kim_1–13-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Third, the increasing number of single-parent families creates a need for grandparental support.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The degree of grandparent involvement also varies depending on the societal context, such as the social welfare policies. For example, in European countries such as Sweden and Denmark, where formal childcare is widely available, grandparents provide less intensive childcare.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> By contrast, in European countries such as Spain and Italy, where formal childcare is limited, and welfare payment is low, grandparents provide more intensive childcare.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In Singapore, the grandparent caregiver tax relief was established in 2004, which enables working parents (Singapore citizens with children age 12 and below) whose children are being cared for by unemployed grandparents to receive income tax relief of 3,000 Singaporean dollars.<sup id="cite_ref-Thang_548–570_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thang_548–570-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Different_types_of_grandparental_involvement">Different types of grandparental involvement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grandparent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Different types of grandparental involvement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are different types of grandparental involvement, including nonresident grandparents, co-resident grandparents, grandparent-maintained household, and custodial grandparents.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<ul><li>Nonresident grandparents: Grandparents who do not live with their grandchildren, but provide care for them,<sup id="cite_ref-:3_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> such as picking them up from school.</li>
<li>Co-resident grandparents: Grandparents who live with their grandchild, as well as his/her parents. This type of household is also known as three-generational households.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> According to a report that uses data from the 2010 Census, the American Community Survey (ACS), the Current Population Survey (CPS), and the Survey of Income and Program Participation (SIPP), co-resident grandparents are more likely to be in poverty and suffer from an illness or disability.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Grandparent-maintained households: A grandparent who is in charge of the household. In this type of household, the parents may or may not be present.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In the USA, 33% of children who live in a grandparent-maintained household have only the grandparents present; this is comparable to another 30% who live with a grandmother and one or more parents.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Custodial grandparents: Grandparents who raise their grandchildren without the presence of the grandchildren’s parents in the household. This type of involvement is especially common among ethnic minority groups.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> For instance, approximately 50% of custodial grandparents in the USA belong to an ethnic minority group.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>  In general, grandparents adopt the primary caregiving role for various reasons, such as when the grandchild is neglected or abused by his/her parents, when his/her parents suffer from drug and/or alcohol addiction, when his/her parents have relocated due to job demands, died, incarcerated, or deployed. In some cases, parents remain in contact with their children.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_of_grandparental_involvement">Impact of grandparental involvement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grandparent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Impact of grandparental involvement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="On_child_development">On child development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grandparent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: On child development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Grandparents have different functions in child development. Not only do they provide instrumental support such as picking grandchildren from school or feeding them, but they also offer emotional support.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, grandparents protect children from being impacted by negative circumstances, such as harsh parenting, poor economic status, and single-parent families.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:7_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In addition to providing support, grandparents can also help grandchildren with their schoolwork or teach them values that are integral to their society.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Grandparents can have a positive or negative impact on child development. On the one hand, previous research suggests that children and adolescents who have a close relationship with their grandparents tend to have better well-being, experience fewer emotional problems, and demonstrate fewer problematic behaviours.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:7_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> They are also more academically engaged and are more likely to help others.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> On the other hand, there are also research studies indicating that grandparent involvement is associated with more hyperactivity and peer difficulties among young children.<sup id="cite_ref-:12_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:12-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> In other words, children who are cared for by their grandparents can have more interpersonal relationship problems.<sup id="cite_ref-:12_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:12-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Also, children who are under the care of their grandparents have poorer health outcomes such as obesity, and more injuries due to low safety awareness.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="On_grandparents">On grandparents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grandparent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: On grandparents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since taking care of grandchildren could be a highly demanding job that requires constant energy and time devotion,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> grandparental involvement in child raising could have a negative impact on grandparents’ physical and emotional health. For example, taking care of grandchildren can reduce grandparents’ own time for self-care such as missing their medical appointments. Therefore, they are likely to have a higher chance to suffer from physical health issues.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> In the USA, compared with those who do not take care of their grandchildren, grandparents who are involved in childcare are more likely to have poor physical conditions, such as heart disease, hypertension or body pain.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Besides physical health issues, grandparents are also likely to have emotional issues. To be more specific, raising young children again could be a stressful and overwhelming experience and thus results in different kinds of negative emotions such as anxiety or depression.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> In addition to physical and emotional issues, grandparents who are involved in caring for their grandchildren can also suffer socially. For instance, grandparents will be forced to limit their social activities so as to care for their grandchildren. By doing so, grandparents become more isolated from their social relations.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Taking care of grandchildren also means more responsibilities, grandparents would fear for their grandchildren’s future well-being because of their disability and death in the future.<sup id="cite_ref-:11_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:11-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> If grandparents cannot handle the caregiver role of their grandchildren well, this job can eventually become a burden or stressor and bring more severe physical health and emotional issues to grandparents.<sup id="cite_ref-:9_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, there are also positive effects of being involved in grandchildren raising. Compared with grandparents who do not provide caregiving to their grandchildren, those who take care of their grandchildren with long hours are more likely to have better cognitive functions.<sup id="cite_ref-:8_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> To be more specific, taking care of grandchildren helps elder grandparents maintain their mental capacities in later life, they are also less likely to develop diseases such as dementia.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Moreover, frequent interactions with their grandchildren could reduce the cognitive aging process, allowing grandparents a chance to live a more vibrant and active life.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_18-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:8_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Grandparents also get benefits of physically exercising more during this process.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Taking care of grandchildren can also have benefits on grandparents’ emotional health. As an example, many grandparents start to feel a sense of purpose and meaning in life again after their retirement; as another example, their ties with their adult children and grandchildren are also strengthened.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Many grandparents also think of the caregiving experience as positive because it provides another chance for them to make up mistakes they made with their own children and give them more opportunities to educate their grandchildren and improve their parenting styles.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_comparisons_of_grandparent_involvement_in_childcare">Cultural comparisons of grandparent involvement in childcare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grandparent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Cultural comparisons of grandparent involvement in childcare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Grandparental involvement differs between Western and Eastern cultures. Grandparents taking care of their grandchildren is a common phenomenon in China due to Chinese traditions which emphasize family harmony, collective well-being, intergenerational exchanges and filial responsibilities.<sup id="cite_ref-:9_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> China’s unique philosophies, Buddhism and Taoism, play important roles in forming these cultural values. While Chinese Buddhism emphasizes prioritized role of the family in Chinese society and harmonious relations among family members,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Taoism emphasizes the importance of harmony in interpersonal relations and relations between nature and the humans.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> These philosophies underline the important role that families play in Chinese cultures. Besides cultural factors, grandparents taking care of their grandchildren also appears in the context in which their adult children need to work full-time, and the child care services are either too expensive (in big cities) or too scarce (in remote areas).<sup id="cite_ref-:9_34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:10_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Grandparents serving as their grandchildren’s caregiver is particularly common in rural China. Due to the fast development of <a href="/wiki/Urbanization_in_China" title="Urbanization in China">urbanization in China</a> since the 1980s, up to 220 million migrant workers from rural areas move to urban areas to seek for more job opportunities, which leave around 58 million children behind in rural areas,<sup id="cite_ref-:10_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> grandparents, therefore, undertake the role of parents and become caregivers to their grandchildren. A new population named “left-behind grandparents”<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> appears in this context, these grandparents live in rural China, and their main job is to look after their grandchildren, most of these grandparents are facing financial burdens and wish their adult children could come back. The mental and physical health of “left-behind grandparents” needs more attention from the public.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Even though in urban areas where child care services are available, nearly all grandparents still prefer to take care of their grandchildren voluntarily. Not only because this can reduce their adult children’s financial burdens on child care services but also taking care of their own grandchildren is a more effective way to maintain family harmony.<sup id="cite_ref-:9_34-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the USA, taking care of grandchildren is not a necessary responsibility of grandparents. Grandparents taking care of their grandchildren is often caused by involuntary events or crisis, and it is more like a solution to a problem, not an initiative desire, which is a distinct difference from that in China.<sup id="cite_ref-:11_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:11-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> For example, grandparents in the USA often take care of their grandchildren when their adult children get into troubles such as substance abuse, incarceration or parental death.<sup id="cite_ref-:11_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:11-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Differences also exist in different ethnicities in the USA, Caucasian individuals generally regard individual independence as more important, so grandparents are less likely to take care of their grandchildren. However, African American and Latino individuals are more likely to regard looking after grandchildren as a family tradition and are more willing to provide help for their adult children.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Ethnic differences in grandparents looking after their grandchildren reflect different cultural values that different ethnic groups hold. To be more specific, African American grandparents are more likely to provide guidance and discipline to their grandchildren due to their flexible family system in which relatives, nonblood kin are all willing to help each other.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Latino families have a strong preference to live together and keep frequent contact with family members because most of them are immigrants or first-generation born in the USA, they are more likely to live and function as a unit. Grandparents in Latino culture also play important roles in stabilizing the family unit as family leaders.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Although Caucasian grandparents are less likely to raise their grandchildren,<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> they have more cognitive or physical burdens of taking care of grandchildren compared with other ethnic groups,<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> mainly because their caregiver roles are less normative, and they rely more on remote or companionate parenting styles. On the contrary, African American and Latino grandparents rely more on disciplinary and instructional parenting styles and they are less likely to have cognitive or physical burdens when taking care of their grandchildren.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
