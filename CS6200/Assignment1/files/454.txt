	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Hebraization of surnames</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hebraization_of_surnames.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Hebraization_of_surnames.jpg/220px-Hebraization_of_surnames.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="127" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Hebraization_of_surnames.jpg/330px-Hebraization_of_surnames.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Hebraization_of_surnames.jpg/440px-Hebraization_of_surnames.jpg 2x" data-file-width="720" data-file-height="414" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hebraization_of_surnames.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Poster offering assistance in choosing a Hebrew name</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Hebraization of surnames</b> (also <b>Hebraicization</b>)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886049626">.mw-parser-output .script-hebrew,.mw-parser-output .script-Hebr{font-size:1.15em;font-family:"Ezra SIL","Ezra SIL SR","Keter Aram Tsova","Taamey Ashkenaz","Taamey David CLM","Taamey Frank CLM","Frank Ruehl CLM","Keter YG","Shofar","David CLM","Hadasim CLM","Simple CLM","Nachlieli","SBL BibLit","SBL Hebrew",Cardo,Alef,"Noto Serif Hebrew","Noto Sans Hebrew","David Libre",David,"Times New Roman",Gisha,Arial,FreeSerif,FreeSans}</style><span class="script-hebrew" style="font-size: 115%;" dir="rtl">עברות</span>, <i>Ivrut</i>, "Hebraization") is the act of <a href="/wiki/Name_change" title="Name change">adopting</a> a Hebrew surname in exchange for a <a href="/wiki/Kinnui" title="Kinnui">diaspora name</a>. For many <a href="/wiki/Jewish_diaspora" title="Jewish diaspora">diaspora Jews</a> who <a href="/wiki/Aliyah" title="Aliyah">migrated</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Land_of_Israel" title="Land of Israel">Land of Israel</a>, taking a Hebrew <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a> was a way to <a href="/wiki/Negation_of_the_Diaspora" title="Negation of the Diaspora">erase remnants</a> of their diaspora experience and to <a href="/wiki/Israelization" title="Israelization">assimilate</a> into a new shared identity with <a href="/wiki/Mizrahi_Jews" title="Mizrahi Jews">Mizrahi Jews</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palestinian_Jews" title="Palestinian Jews">Palestinian Jews</a> (Jewish residents of <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Palestine" class="mw-redirect" title="Ottoman Palestine">Ottoman Palestine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mandatory_Palestine" title="Mandatory Palestine">Mandatory Palestine</a>) and later as <a href="/wiki/Israeli_Jews" title="Israeli Jews">Israeli Jews</a> (Jewish citizens of the independent <a href="/wiki/State_of_Israel" class="mw-redirect" title="State of Israel">State of Israel</a>).
</p><p>This name-change typically did not apply to <a href="/wiki/Mizrahi_Jews" title="Mizrahi Jews">Mizrahi Jews</a> who came from neighboring countries such as Iran, Iraq, and Egypt. Mizrahis usually kept their surnames.
</p><p>This phenomenon was especially common among <a href="/wiki/Ashkenazi_Jews" title="Ashkenazi Jews">Ashkenazi Jews</a>, because many such families only acquired permanent surnames (rather than <a href="/wiki/Patronym" class="mw-redirect" title="Patronym">patronyms</a>) when surnames were made compulsory by the November 12, 1787 decree by the <a href="/wiki/Habsburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Habsburg">Habsburg</a> emperor <a href="/wiki/Joseph_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Joseph II, Holy Roman Emperor">Joseph II</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> By way of contrast, <a href="/wiki/Sephardi_Jews" title="Sephardi Jews">Sephardi Jews</a> from the Iberian peninsula often had hereditary family names since well before the <a href="/wiki/Alhambra_Decree" title="Alhambra Decree">Spanish Expulsion</a> (e.g., Cordovero, Abrabanel, Shaltiel, de Leon, Alcalai, Toledano, etc.). Very few Hebrew surnames existed before Hebraization, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Cohen_(surname)" title="Cohen (surname)">Cohen</a></i> (priest), <i><a href="/wiki/Moss_(surname)" title="Moss (surname)">Moss</a></i> (Moses) and <i><a href="/wiki/Levi_(surname)" title="Levi (surname)">Levi</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Levite" title="Levite">Levite</a>).  In America, names ending with <i>-berg</i>, "-burg", <i>-stein</i> "-mann" <i>-man</i> are often thought of as Jewish, but are of <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> origin, while suffixes such as <i>-cky</i> <i>-sky</i> and <i>-vitz</i> are <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">Slavic</a>. Similarly,  a few Hebrew surnames, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Katz_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Katz (name)">Katz</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bogoraz" title="Bogoraz">Bogoraz</a></i>, <i>Ohl</i> and <i>Pak</i> are in fact Hebrew <a href="/wiki/Acronym_and_initialism" class="mw-redirect" title="Acronym and initialism">acronyms</a>, even though they sound and are often perceived as being of foreign origin (in these cases, from German, <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>, respectively).
</p><p>The Hebraization of surnames is a unique phenomenon to the Hebrew language. This process began as early as the days of the <a href="/wiki/First_Aliyah" title="First Aliyah">First</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second_Aliyah" title="Second Aliyah">Second</a> <i>Aliyot</i> and continued after the establishment of the State of Israel. The widespread trend towards Hebraization of surnames in the days of the <a href="/wiki/Yishuv" title="Yishuv">Yishuv</a> and immediately after the establishment of the State of Israel was based on the claim that a Hebrew name provided a feeling of belonging to the new state. There was also the wish to distance from the lost and dead past, and from the forced imposition of foreign (e.g. German) names in the previous centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-JAI_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JAI-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This process has not ended: among the thousands of Israelis who currently apply for legal name changes each year, many do it to adopt Hebrew names.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hebraization_of_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_Yishuv">In the Yishuv</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hebraization_of_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In the Yishuv">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Among the <a href="/wiki/Yishuv" title="Yishuv">Yishuv</a> (the first to return to Eretz Yisrael—the <a href="/wiki/Land_of_Israel" title="Land of Israel">Land of Israel</a>), there was a strong feeling of <i>sh'lilat ha'gola</i> (Hebrew: <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049626"/><span class="script-hebrew" style="font-size: 115%;" dir="rtl">שלילת הגולה</span> "negation of the diaspora/Exile"), which often included the exchange of Diaspora surnames for purely Hebrew ones.<sup id="cite_ref-W_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Part of the <a href="/wiki/Zionist_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Zionist movement">Zionist movement</a> was not only Aliyah it was also wanting to create an image of an <a href="/wiki/Israeli_Jews" title="Israeli Jews">Israeli Jew</a> that would be different from the stereotypical perception of <a href="/wiki/Yiddish" title="Yiddish">Yiddish</a>-speaking, <i><a href="/wiki/Shtetl" title="Shtetl">shtetl</a></i>-living, weak <a href="/wiki/Diaspora_Jews" class="mw-redirect" title="Diaspora Jews">Diaspora Jews</a>, and these things were a significant part of the people of the <a href="/wiki/First_Aliyah" title="First Aliyah">First</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second_Aliyah" title="Second Aliyah">Second</a> <i>Aliyot</i>. Some of the immigrants of the First Aliyah (1882–1903) Hebraized their surnames, and the practice became widespread during the Second Aliyah (1904–1914).<sup id="cite_ref-JAI_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JAI-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-forward_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forward-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Jewish_Agency_booklet">Jewish Agency booklet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hebraization_of_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Jewish Agency booklet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>This process started with individuals like <a href="/wiki/Eliezer_Ben-Yehuda" title="Eliezer Ben-Yehuda">Eliezer Ben-Yehuda</a> (Perelman) and was adopted by the <a href="/wiki/New_Yishuv" class="mw-redirect" title="New Yishuv">New Yishuv</a>. Before the founding of the State of Israel, in 1944, the Zionist leadership and the <a href="/wiki/Jewish_National_Council" title="Jewish National Council">Jewish National Council</a> proclaimed it the "Year of naturalization and the Hebrew name".<sup id="cite_ref-JAI_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JAI-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> A special committee under the chairmanship of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mordechai_Nemzabi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mordechai Nemzabi (page does not exist)">Mordechai Nemzabi</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Jewish_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish Agency">Jewish Agency</a> advisor on matters of civilian defense, published a booklet which contained guidelines on the creation on new Hebrew surnames.<sup id="cite_ref-JAI_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JAI-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Changing a foreign surname to Hebrew</dt>
<dd></dd></dl>
<ol><li>Change of vocalization: <i>Leib</i> becomes <i>Lev</i></li>
<li>Change of consonants: <i>Borg</i> or <i>Brog</i> becomes <i>Barak</i></li>
<li>Shortening by omitting the ending: <i>Rosenberg</i> becomes <i>Rosen</i></li>
<li>Shortening a name with a Hebrew meaning, by omitting the foreign suffix: <i>Yakobovitch</i> (<i>Jacobowitz</i>, <i>Jacobowicz</i>) becomes <i>Ya'akovi</i></li>
<li>Translating the foreign name into Hebrew according to the meaning: <i>Abramovich</i> (<i>Abramowicz</i>, <i>Abramowitz</i>) becomes <i>Ben Avraham</i></li></ol>
<dl><dt>First names as surnames</dt>
<dd></dd></dl>
<ol><li>Name of a father or mother who were murdered during the <a href="/wiki/The_Holocaust" title="The Holocaust">Shoah</a>, thus: <i>Bat Miriam</i>, <i>Ben Moshe</i>, <i>Devorin</i></li>
<li>Son or daughter who fell in battle: <i>Avinoam</i></li>
<li>Brother or sister who were killed or fell: <i>Achimeir</i></li>
<li>Beloved or admired <a href="/wiki/Tanakh" class="mw-redirect" title="Tanakh">biblical</a> figure: <i><a href="/wiki/Saul" title="Saul">Shaul</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/David" title="David">Davidi</a></i></li></ol>
<dl><dt>Change of names by names of places, plants or sites in Eretz Yisrael</dt>
<dd></dd></dl>
<ol><li>Places or sites: <i><a href="/wiki/Hermon" class="mw-redirect" title="Hermon">Hermoni</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eilat" title="Eilat">Eilat</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Gilead" title="Gilead">Gilad</a></i></li>
<li>Plants, especially plants of the <a href="/wiki/Land_of_Israel" title="Land of Israel">Land of Israel</a>: <i>Eshel</i> ("orchard", "garden"), <i>Rotem</i> ("desert <a href="/wiki/Broom_(shrub)" class="mw-redirect" title="Broom (shrub)">broom</a>")</li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_the_establishment_of_Israel">After the establishment of Israel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hebraization_of_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: After the establishment of Israel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Israeli_Declaration_of_Independence" title="Israeli Declaration of Independence">Israeli Declaration of Independence</a>, there was still the attitude that the hebraization of family names should continue, in order to get rid of names with a diaspora sound.<sup id="cite_ref-JAI_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JAI-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Hebraization of names became a typical part of the integration process for new immigrants among <a href="/wiki/Ashkenazi_Jews" title="Ashkenazi Jews">Ashkenazi Jews</a>, but also among <a href="/wiki/Sephardi_Jews" title="Sephardi Jews">Sephardi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mizrahi_Jews" title="Mizrahi Jews">Mizrahi</a> Jewish immigrants from Arab and Muslim lands; Sephardi and Mizrahi children were typically given new Hebrew names in school, often without permission from their parents.<sup id="cite_ref-forward_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forward-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/David_Ben-Gurion" title="David Ben-Gurion">David Ben-Gurion</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Israel" title="Prime Minister of Israel">Prime Minister of Israel</a>, was committed to the use of the Hebrew language (he changed his surname from Grün to Ben-Gurion). He tried to convince as many people to change their surnames into "real" Hebrew ones. Ben-Gurion got <a href="/wiki/Herzl_Rosenblum" title="Herzl Rosenblum">Herzl Rosenblum</a> to sign the Israeli Declaration of Independence as  Herzl Vardi, his <a href="/wiki/Pen_name" title="Pen name">pen name</a> (later changed to his legal name), as Ben-Gurion wanted more Hebrew names on the document. Nine more of the signatories of the document would then go on to Hebraize their name, as well.
</p><p>Ben-Gurion, in an order to the <a href="/wiki/Israel_Defense_Forces" title="Israel Defense Forces">Israel Defense Forces</a> soldiers, wrote, "It is desirable that every commanding officer (from Squadron Commander to Chief of Staff) should change his surname, whether German, English, Slavic, French or foreign in general, to a Hebrew surname, in order to be a role model for his soldiers. The Israel Defense Forces must be Hebrew in spirit, vision, and in all internal and external expressions."<sup id="cite_ref-JAI_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JAI-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A binding order of the same issue was issued to the officials of the state in 1950, and particularly to those who represented the State abroad. A "Committee for Hebrew Names" was established to supervise the implementation of the order, whose task was to assist and advise the choice of a Hebrew name.
</p><p>In addition to pressure from the state, tensions between <a href="/wiki/Jewish_ethnic_groups" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish ethnic groups">Jewish ethnic groups</a> caused some people to Hebraize their names to dis-identify with a "stigmatized" ethnic group or to merge into a "collective Israeli identity" and therefore created a desire to Hebraize.<sup id="cite_ref-W_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Supporters_and_opponents">Supporters and opponents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hebraization_of_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Supporters and opponents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Hebraization of surnames was a source of debate in the days of the <a href="/wiki/Yishuv" title="Yishuv">Yishuv</a> and after the <a href="/wiki/Establishment_of_the_State_of_Israel" class="mw-redirect" title="Establishment of the State of Israel">establishment of the State of Israel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Supporters">Supporters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hebraization_of_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Supporters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Among the most significant supporters was <a href="/wiki/Yitzhak_Ben_Zvi" class="mw-redirect" title="Yitzhak Ben Zvi">Yitzhak Ben Zvi</a> (Shimshelevich), leader of the <a href="/wiki/Labor_(Israel)" class="mw-redirect" title="Labor (Israel)">Labor movement</a>, historian and second <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_State_of_Israel" class="mw-redirect" title="President of the State of Israel">President of the State of Israel</a>. He was born in <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> on 24 November 1884. He studied law in Istanbul together with David Ben Gurion. In 1906 he attended the founding conference of the <a href="/wiki/Poalei_Zion" class="mw-redirect" title="Poalei Zion">Poalei Zion</a> and in 1907 he settled in the Land of Israel.<sup id="cite_ref-JAI_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JAI-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> He belonged to the founders of the <a href="/wiki/Ahdut_ha-Avodah" class="mw-redirect" title="Ahdut ha-Avodah">Ahdut ha-Avodah</a> Party, was active in the <a href="/wiki/Haganah" title="Haganah">Haganah</a>, a member of the <a href="/wiki/Jewish_National_Council" title="Jewish National Council">Jewish National Council</a>, and signed the Israeli Declaration of Independence.<sup id="cite_ref-JAI_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JAI-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Ben Zvi died in 1963.
</p><p>Ben Zvi wrote:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Our surnames are mostly of foreign origin, which cling to exile [...] even names based on Hebrew first names were damaged and distorted from the original [...] by German and English suffixes, like "son" or "sohn" and the Slavic "in", "ovich", "ovsky" and "shvili". These surnames fill the air and the pages of our newspaper, the posters and announcements in our streets and public squares [...] it is indeed not really clear if the hardship of this inheritance which remained with us as a result of the Middle Ages and subsequent ghettoization should be tolerated...</p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8201;<cite>Yitzhak Ben Zvi in <i>Collected Writings</i>, vol. 4, pp. 11–14<sup id="cite_ref-JAI_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JAI-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>All rabbinic authorities encourage hebraizing first names (VaYikra Rabba 32, and Kor'ei Sh'mo, pp.&#160;173–181), and some actively encourage last names, as well (Rabbi Shlomo Aviner (Resp. She'elat Shlomo VIII, 67-68), and even did so themselves: among them: Rabbis <a href="/wiki/Menashe_Klein" title="Menashe Klein">Menashe HaKatan</a> (Klein), <a href="/wiki/Maharam_Schick" class="mw-redirect" title="Maharam Schick">Maharam Schick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shlomo_Goren" title="Shlomo Goren">Shlomo Goren</a> (Goronchick), <a href="/wiki/Shaul_Yisraeli" title="Shaul Yisraeli">Shaul Yisraeli</a> (Israelite), <a href="/wiki/Moshe_Zvi_Neria" class="mw-redirect" title="Moshe Zvi Neria">Moshe Zvi Neria</a> (Menkin), <a href="/wiki/Shlomo_Aviner" title="Shlomo Aviner">Shlomo Aviner</a> (Langenauer).
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Opponents">Opponents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hebraization_of_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Opponents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>One of the opponents of the Hebraization of surnames was <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moses_Calvary&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Moses Calvary (page does not exist)">Moses Calvary</a>, a writer and teacher. Born in Germany 1883, he received a traditional, general, and rabbinical education.<sup id="cite_ref-JAI_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JAI-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> He was a member of <a href="/wiki/Ahdut_Ha%27avodah" class="mw-redirect" title="Ahdut Ha&#39;avodah">Ahdut Ha'avodah</a>, an educator in the <a href="/wiki/Meir_Shfeya" title="Meir Shfeya">Meir Shfeya</a> youth village, principal of <a href="/wiki/Gymnasia_Rehavia" title="Gymnasia Rehavia">Gymnasia Rehavia</a> in <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>, and educator in the "Ahava" youth village in <a href="/wiki/Kiryat_Bialik" title="Kiryat Bialik">Kiryat Bialik</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-JAI_4-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JAI-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Yitzhak Ben Zvi demands we disqualify all the foreign names which are close to us. His assumption was that our immigration to Eretz Yisrael is a revolutionary act, a return to one's origins. Let the memory of the Diaspora therefore be erased so that the crown be returned to ancient times, by wrapping our names in the envelope of our language. I have my doubts if this zealousness is appropriate. Our names are part—a significant part—of our history. Bearers of historical names are still alive and among us; the names of figures who mark our history—names like Sasportas or Benbenisti, Abrabanel or Don Yehia, Rappaport or Eibeschitz—there is no reason to delete the chronicle of our national life. Let us preserve the faith of our fathers also in our revival. Do not betray our memories by radical action; and the text does not only relate to the famous: the variety in our names is a sign of our colorful history, a two-thousand year-old history, whose traces cannot be wiped out light-handedly.</p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8201;<cite>Moses Calvary in <i>Between Sowing and Harvest</i>, p. 339<sup id="cite_ref-JAI_4-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JAI-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Some people were emotionally attached to their diaspora last name, for reasons such as it having noble origins (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew" class="mw-redirect" title="Hebrew">Hebrew</a>: יִחוּס  <i>Yichus</i>), or for a desire to continue to identify with their <a href="/wiki/Jewish_ethnic_group" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish ethnic group">ethnic group</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-W_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> There is story of an Israeli diplomat who told <a href="/wiki/David_Ben-Gurion" title="David Ben-Gurion">David Ben-Gurion</a>, "I will change my name if you can find me one non-Jew named Lifshitz."<sup id="cite_ref-W_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Others had names that were entirely Hebrew to begin with.<sup id="cite_ref-W_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The disagreement about the Hebraization of surnames continued. Many people preserved their foreign surname, such as first President of Israel <a href="/wiki/Chaim_Weizmann" title="Chaim Weizmann">Chaim Weizmann</a>, <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Israel" title="Supreme Court of Israel">President of the Supreme Court of Israel</a> <a href="/wiki/Shimon_Agranat" title="Shimon Agranat">Shimon Agranat</a>, and others.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline_of_Hebraization">Decline of Hebraization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hebraization_of_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Decline of Hebraization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This trend moderated with time.<sup id="cite_ref-JAI_4-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JAI-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> By the time of the wave of immigration from the former <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> in the 1990s, the practice was waning. The Soviet immigration wave clearly had the effect of weakening the practice of Hebraizing names—as part of the marked general tendency of these immigrants to cling to their specific Russian linguistic and cultural identity. A conspicuous example is the former (2009–2013) Israeli Minister of Tourism, <a href="/wiki/Stas_Misezhnikov" title="Stas Misezhnikov">Stas Misezhnikov</a>. Though an outspoken Israeli nationalist on other issues, Misezhnikov did not feel impelled to change his clearly Slavic surname (nor his equally Slavic first name), and there was no public pressure on him to do so—as there would have been on an Israeli minister during the country's first decades.
</p><p>However, even today, people continue to Hebraize their surname, especially those serving in the IDF and Israel's diplomatic missions, representing the State of Israel.<sup id="cite_ref-JAI_4-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JAI-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The number of those who do is small but significant; about 15% of American and British immigrants to Israel who come on <a href="/wiki/Nefesh_B%27Nefesh" title="Nefesh B&#39;Nefesh">Nefesh B'Nefesh</a> flights Hebraize their names on arrival.<sup id="cite_ref-forward_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forward-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There is also a trend of reverting to ancestral, non-Hebrew names to return to one's roots and preserve traditions unique to <a href="/wiki/Jewish_ethnic_group" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish ethnic group">each ethnic group</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-JAI_4-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JAI-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> There are people who re-adopt the name their family previously abandoned for the sake of "Israeliness", such as Israeli writer <a href="/wiki/Yitzhak_Orpaz" title="Yitzhak Orpaz">Yitzhak Orpaz</a> who restored his family's original family name of "Averbuch".<sup id="cite_ref-JAI_4-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JAI-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Others kept their name for several reasons. Sometimes, the reason it was kept was because of its religious nature. For examples, names connected with the <a href="/wiki/Kohen" title="Kohen">Kohen</a> (priesthood) such as Cohen, Kohn, Kaplan, Sacerdoti, Katz, Azoulai, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-W_6-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Other times it indicated Levite descent such as Levi, Levy, Weil (<a href="/wiki/Anagram" title="Anagram">anagram</a>), and Segal (Hebrew <a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">acronym</a>). Other times it was synagogue or Jewish community functions such as Gabbai, Chazan, or Rabin.<sup id="cite_ref-W_6-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Sometimes the surname was already Hebrew (Sarfati).
</p><p>Others kept their name for its <i>yichus</i> (meaning that the person descends from something akin to "good stock"), which gave the bearer more reason not to Hebraize it.<sup id="cite_ref-W_6-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Examples include Horowitz (famous rabbinical dynasty), <a href="/wiki/Rothschild_family" title="Rothschild family">Rothschild</a> (famous Jewish banking dynasty), <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Einstein</a> (famous bearer), or Shaltiel (ancient <a href="/wiki/Sephardi_Jews" title="Sephardi Jews">Sephardic</a> family tracing its origins to <a href="/wiki/King_David" class="mw-redirect" title="King David">King David</a>—and it is <a href="/wiki/Shealtiel" title="Shealtiel">already Hebrew</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-W_6-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Others kept their name but the name underwent some mutation because they contained sounds that do not exist in Hebrew.<sup id="cite_ref-W_6-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Examples include Lando (from "Landau"), and Glober (from "Glauber").<sup id="cite_ref-W_6-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Other "Diaspora" Jewish names are Hebrew to begin with (such as Ashkenazi and Yerushalmi), corruptions of Hebrew words (such as Heifetz (from Chafetz)), Hebrew acronyms (such as Shalit (from "<i>Sheyihye le'orekh yamim tovim</i><span style="padding-left:.15em;">"</span>)), or of <a href="/wiki/Aramaic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Aramaic language">Aramaic</a> origin (such as Kahane, or Raban).<sup id="cite_ref-W_6-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
