	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Eastern Aramaic languages</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p><b>Eastern Aramaic languages</b> have developed from the varieties of <a href="/wiki/Aramaic" title="Aramaic">Aramaic</a> that developed in and around <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>, southeast <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, northeast <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> and northwest and southwest <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>), as opposed to western varieties of the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a> (modern Levantine <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>). Most speakers are ethnic <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_people" title="Assyrian people">Assyrians</a>, although there are a minority of <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Kurdistan" title="History of the Jews in Kurdistan">Jews</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mandaeans" title="Mandaeans">Mandaeans</a>, who also speak varieties of Eastern Aramaic.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Speakers">Speakers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_Aramaic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Speakers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Numbers of fluent speakers range from approximately 575,000 to 1,000,000, with the main languages being <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_Neo-Aramaic" title="Assyrian Neo-Aramaic">Assyrian Neo-Aramaic</a>  (235,000 speakers), <a href="/wiki/Chaldean_Neo-Aramaic" title="Chaldean Neo-Aramaic">Chaldean Neo-Aramaic</a> (216,000 speakers) and <a href="/wiki/Turoyo_language" title="Turoyo language">Surayt/Turoyo</a> (250,000 speakers),<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> together with a number of  smaller closely related languages with no more than 5,000 to 10,000 speakers between them.
</p><p>Despite their names, they are not restricted to specific churches; Chaldean Neo-Aramaic being spoken by members of the Chaldean Catholic Church, Assyrian Church of the East, Syriac Orthodox Church, Assyrian Protestant churches, and Assyrian Neo-Aramaic and Turoyo being spoken by members of the Chaldean Catholic Church etc.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MacDonald_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacDonald-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition, there are approximately 25,000 speakers of <a href="/wiki/Jewish_languages" title="Jewish languages">Jewish varieties</a>, and some 5000 fluent speakers of the <a href="/wiki/Mandaic_language" title="Mandaic language">Mandaic language</a><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> among the some 50,000 Mandaeans, an ethno-religious <a href="/wiki/Gnostic" class="mw-redirect" title="Gnostic">gnostic</a> minority in Iraq and Iran.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_Aramaic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Historically, eastern varieties of Aramaic have been more dominant, mainly due to their political acceptance in the <a href="/wiki/Neo-Assyrian_Empire" title="Neo-Assyrian Empire">Neo-Assyrian Empire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Achaemenid Persian</a> empires. With the later loss of political platforms to <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>, Eastern Aramaic continued to be used by the population of Mesopotamia.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Assyria" title="Assyria">Assyria</a>, today's modern northern Iraq, south east Turkey and north east Syria, the local variety of eastern Aramaic, known as <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">Syriac</a> (the terms <i>Syrian</i> and <i>Syriac</i> originally being Indo-European derivatives of <i>Assyrian</i>) had emerged by the 5th century BC, and between the 1st and 4th centuries AD became a standard language among the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Christianity" title="Eastern Christianity">Eastern Rite</a> <a href="/wiki/Christians" title="Christians">Christian</a> <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_people" title="Assyrian people">Assyrians</a>, being used in the <a href="/wiki/Peshitta" title="Peshitta">Peshitta</a> and by the poet <a href="/wiki/Ephrem_the_Syrian" title="Ephrem the Syrian">Ephrem</a>, and in the schools of <a href="/wiki/School_of_Edessa" title="School of Edessa">Edessa</a> and <a href="/wiki/School_of_Nisibis" title="School of Nisibis">Nisibis</a>, and later by the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Thomas_Christians" title="Saint Thomas Christians">Saint Thomas Christians</a> in India.
</p><p>In the region of <a href="/wiki/Babylonia" title="Babylonia">Babylonia</a> (modern Southern Iraq), <a href="/wiki/Rabbi" title="Rabbi">rabbinical</a> schools flourished, producing the <a href="/wiki/Targum" title="Targum">Targumim</a> and <a href="/wiki/Talmud" title="Talmud">Talmud</a>, making the language a standard of religious <a href="/wiki/Jewish" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> scholarship.
</p><p>Among the <a href="/wiki/Mandaeism" title="Mandaeism">Mandaean</a> ethnic community of <a href="/wiki/Kh%C5%ABzest%C4%81n_Province" class="mw-redirect" title="Khūzestān Province">Khuzestan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>, another variety of eastern Aramaic, known as <a href="/wiki/Mandaic_language" title="Mandaic language">Mandaic</a>, became the liturgical language of the religion.
</p><p>These varieties have widely influenced the less prominent <a href="/wiki/Western_Aramaic_languages" title="Western Aramaic languages">Western Aramaic languages</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a>, and the three classical languages outlined above have also influenced numerous vernacular varieties of Eastern Aramaic, some of which are spoken to this day, largely by ethnic Assyrians and Mandaeans (see <a href="/wiki/Neo-Aramaic_languages" title="Neo-Aramaic languages">Neo-Aramaic languages</a>). Since the <a href="/wiki/Muslim_conquest_of_Persia" title="Muslim conquest of Persia">Muslim conquest of Persia</a> of the seventh century, most of the population of the Middle East has undergone a gradual but steady <a href="/wiki/Language_shift" title="Language shift">language shift</a> to <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a>.
</p><p>However there are still between some 550,000 - 1,000,000 fluent speakers among the indigenous ethnic <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_people" title="Assyrian people">Assyrians</a> of northern Iraq, northeast Syria, southeastern Turkey and northwestern Iran, as well as small migrant communities in Lebanon, Israel, Jordan, Armenia, Georgia, southern Russia and Azerbaijan. Most of these are members of the <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_Church_of_the_East" title="Assyrian Church of the East">Assyrian Church of the East</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syriac_Orthodox_Church" title="Syriac Orthodox Church">Syriac Orthodox Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chaldean_Catholic_Church" title="Chaldean Catholic Church">Chaldean Catholic Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Church_of_the_East" title="Ancient Church of the East">Ancient Church of the East</a>, <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_Pentecostal_Church" title="Assyrian Pentecostal Church">Assyrian Pentecostal Church</a> and <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_Evangelical_Church" title="Assyrian Evangelical Church">Assyrian Evangelical Church</a>. A further number may have a more sparse understanding of the language, due to pressures in their homelands to speak Arabic, Turkish, <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a> or Kurdish, and due to the <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_Diaspora" class="mw-redirect" title="Assyrian Diaspora">Assyrian Diaspora</a> to the Western World.
</p>

The Assyrians descent from the population of ancient <a href="/wiki/Assyria" title="Assyria">Assyria</a> (founded in the 24th century BC), and have lived as a <a href="/wiki/Linguistic" class="mw-redirect" title="Linguistic">linguistic</a>, political, religious, and <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_minority" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethnic minority">ethnic minority</a> in Iraq, Iran, Syria and Turkey since the fall of the <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Assyrian Empire">Assyrian Empire</a> in 608 BC. Practices that maintain ethnic and cultural continuity in the <a href="/wiki/Near_East" title="Near East">Near East</a>, the United States and elsewhere include language and residential patterns, ethnically based <a href="/wiki/Christian_denomination" title="Christian denomination">Christian churches</a> characterized by unique holidays and <a href="/wiki/Rite" title="Rite">rites</a>, and culturally specific practices related to life-cycle events and <a href="/wiki/Food_preparation" class="mw-redirect" title="Food preparation">food preparation</a>. The interviews probe parental attitudes and practices related to <a href="/wiki/Cultural_identity" title="Cultural identity">ethnic identity</a> and encouragement of <a href="/wiki/Endogamy" title="Endogamy">endogamy</a>. Results are being analyzed.</q></cite><span title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Ajournal&amp;rft.genre=article&amp;rft.atitle=Socialization+for+Ingroup+Identity+among+Assyrians+in+the+United+States&amp;rft.date=2004-07-29&amp;rft.au=MacDonald%2C+Kevin&amp;rft_id=http%3A%2F%2Fevolution.anthro.univie.ac.at%2Fishe%2Fconferences%2Fpast%2520conferences%2Fghent.html&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3AEastern+Aramaic+languages" class="Z3988"></span> <span class="cs1-hidden-error error citation-comment">Cite journal requires <code class="cs1-code">&#124;journal=</code> (<a href="/wiki/Help:CS1_errors#missing_periodical" title="Help:CS1 errors">help</a>)</span><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886058088"/></span>
