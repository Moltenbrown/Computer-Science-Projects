<p><b>Burgundy</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;b&#39; in &#39;buy&#39;">b</span><span title="/ɜːr/: &#39;ur&#39; in &#39;fur&#39;">ɜːr</span><span title="/ɡ/: &#39;g&#39; in &#39;guy&#39;">ɡ</span><span title="/ən/: &#39;on&#39; in &#39;button&#39;">ən</span><span title="&#39;d&#39; in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span><span title="/i/: &#39;y&#39; in &#39;happy&#39;">i</span></span>/</a></span></span>; French: <i lang="fr">Bourgogne</i> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">[buʁɡɔɲ]</a></span>&#32;<span class="nowrap" style="font-size:85%">(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap;margin-right:.25em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Fr-Bourgogne.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" decoding="async" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a></span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9e/Fr-Bourgogne.ogg" class="internal" title="Fr-Bourgogne.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</span>) is a historical territory and a former <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_France" title="Regions of France">administrative region</a> of east-central France. It takes its name from the <a href="/wiki/Burgundians" title="Burgundians">Burgundians</a>, an <a href="/wiki/East_Germanic_tribes" class="mw-redirect" title="East Germanic tribes">East Germanic people</a> who moved westwards beyond the Rhine during the late Roman period.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Historically, "Burgundy" has referred to numerous political entities, including kingdoms and duchies spanning territory from the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">Low Countries</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources in the body of the article. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burgundy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Bourgogne-Franche-Comt%C3%A9" class="mw-redirect" title="History of Bourgogne-Franche-Comté">History of Bourgogne-Franche-Comté</a></div>
<p>The first recorded inhabitants of the area that became Burgundy were <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a>, who were eventually incorporated in the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> as <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Roman" class="mw-redirect" title="Gallo-Roman">Gallo-Romans</a>.
</p><p>During the 4th century, the  <a href="/wiki/Burgundians" title="Burgundians">Burgundians</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic</a> people, who may have originated in <a href="/wiki/Bornholm" title="Bornholm">Bornholm</a> (on the Baltic Sea), settled in the western Alps. They founded the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Burgundians" title="Kingdom of the Burgundians">Kingdom of the Burgundians</a>, which was conquered in the 6th century by another Germanic tribe, the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a>. 
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Burgundy_province.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a7/Burgundy_province.png/100px-Burgundy_province.png" decoding="async" width="100" height="101" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a7/Burgundy_province.png/150px-Burgundy_province.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a7/Burgundy_province.png/200px-Burgundy_province.png 2x" data-file-width="894" data-file-height="899" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Burgundy_province.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of France showing Burgundy and provincial boundaries in 1789</div></div></div>
<p>Under Frankish dominion, the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Burgundy" title="Kingdom of Burgundy">Kingdom of Burgundy</a> continued for several centuries.
</p><p>Later, the region was divided between the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Burgundy" title="Duchy of Burgundy">Duchy of Burgundy</a> (to the west) and the <a href="/wiki/Free_County_of_Burgundy" class="mw-redirect" title="Free County of Burgundy">Free County of Burgundy</a> (to the east). The Duchy of Burgundy is the better-known of the two, later becoming the French province of Burgundy, while the County of Burgundy became the French province of <a href="/wiki/Franche-Comt%C3%A9" title="Franche-Comté">Franche-Comté</a>, literally meaning <i>free county</i>.
</p><p>Burgundy's modern existence is rooted in the dissolution of the <a href="/wiki/Frankish_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankish Empire">Frankish Empire</a>. In the 880s, there were four Burgundies, which were the Kingdom of Upper and Lower Burgundy, the duchy and the county.
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, Burgundy was home to some of the most important Western churches and <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monasteries</a>, including those of <a href="/wiki/Cluny_Abbey" title="Cluny Abbey">Cluny</a>, <a href="/wiki/C%C3%AEteaux_Abbey" title="Cîteaux Abbey">Cîteaux</a>, and <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A9zelay_Abbey" title="Vézelay Abbey">Vézelay</a>. Cluny, founded in 910, exerted a strong influence in Europe for centuries. The first <a href="/wiki/Cistercian" class="mw-redirect" title="Cistercian">Cistercian</a> abbey was founded in 1098 in Cîteaux. Over the next century, hundreds of Cistercian abbeys were founded throughout Europe, in a large part due to the charisma and influence of <a href="/wiki/Bernard_of_Clairvaux" title="Bernard of Clairvaux">Bernard of Clairvaux</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Abbey_of_Fontenay" title="Abbey of Fontenay">Abbey of Fontenay</a>, a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO_World_Heritage_site" class="mw-redirect" title="UNESCO World Heritage site">UNESCO World Heritage site</a>, is today the best-preserved Cistercian abbey in Burgundy. The Abbey of Vezelay, also a UNESCO site, is still a starting point for pilgrimages to <a href="/wiki/Santiago_de_Compostela" title="Santiago de Compostela">Santiago de Compostela</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ParisDigest_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ParisDigest-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Cluny was almost totally destroyed during the French Revolution.
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Years%27_War" title="Hundred Years&#39; War">Hundred Years' War</a>, King <a href="/wiki/John_II_of_France" title="John II of France">John II of France</a> gave the duchy to his youngest son, <a href="/wiki/Philip_the_Bold" title="Philip the Bold">Philip the Bold</a>. The duchy soon became a major rival to the crown. The court in <a href="/wiki/Dijon" title="Dijon">Dijon</a> outshone the French court both economically and culturally. In 1477, at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Nancy" title="Battle of Nancy">battle of Nancy</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Burgundian_Wars" title="Burgundian Wars">Burgundian Wars</a>, the last duke <a href="/wiki/Charles_the_Bold" title="Charles the Bold">Charles the Bold</a> was killed in battle, and the Duchy itself was annexed by France and became a province. However the northern part of the empire was taken by the Austrian Habsburgs.
</p><p>With the French Revolution in the end of the 18th century, the administrative units of the provinces disappeared, but were reconstituted as regions during the <a href="/wiki/French_Fifth_Republic" title="French Fifth Republic">Fifth Republic</a> in the 1970s. The modern-day administrative region comprises most of the former duchy.
</p>

<p>The region of Burgundy is both larger than the old <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Burgundy" title="Duchy of Burgundy">Duchy of Burgundy</a> and smaller than the area ruled by the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Burgundy" title="Duke of Burgundy">Dukes of Burgundy</a>, from the modern <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> to the border of <a href="/wiki/Auvergne_(region)" class="mw-redirect" title="Auvergne (region)">Auvergne</a>. Today, Burgundy is made up of the old provinces:
</p>
<ul><li>Burgundy: <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B4te-d%27Or" title="Côte-d&#39;Or">Côte-d'Or</a>, Saône-et-Loire, and southern half of <a href="/wiki/Yonne" title="Yonne">Yonne</a>. This corresponds to the old <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Burgundy" title="Duke of Burgundy">duchy of Burgundy</a> (later called <i>province of Burgundy</i>). However, the old <a href="/wiki/County_of_Burgundy" title="County of Burgundy">county of Burgundy</a> (later called <i>province of Franche-Comté</i>) is not included inside the Burgundy region, but it makes up the <a href="/wiki/Franche-Comt%C3%A9" title="Franche-Comté">Franche-Comté</a> region. Also, a small part of the duchy of Burgundy (province of Burgundy) is now inside the <a href="/wiki/Champagne-Ardenne" title="Champagne-Ardenne">Champagne-Ardenne</a> region.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nivernais" title="Nivernais">Nivernais</a>: now the department of <a href="/wiki/Ni%C3%A8vre" title="Nièvre">Nièvre</a>.</li>
<li>the northern half of <a href="/wiki/Yonne" title="Yonne">Yonne</a> is a territory that was not part of Burgundy (at least not since the 11th century), and was a frontier between <a href="/wiki/Champagne_(province)" title="Champagne (province)">Champagne</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%8Ele-de-France" title="Île-de-France">Île-de-France</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Orl%C3%A9anais" title="Orléanais">Orléanais</a>, being part of each of these provinces at different times in history.</li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burgundy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The climate of this region is essentially oceanic (Cfb in <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_classification" class="mw-redirect" title="Köppen classification">Köppen classification</a>), with a continental influence (sometimes called a "half-continental climate").<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Regional_council_of_Burgundy" class="mw-redirect" title="Regional council of Burgundy">regional council of Burgundy</a> was the legislative assembly of the region, located in the capital city <a href="/wiki/Dijon" title="Dijon">Dijon</a> at 17 boulevard de la Trémouille until its merger to form the <a href="/wiki/Regional_council_of_Bourgogne-Franche-Comt%C3%A9" title="Regional council of Bourgogne-Franche-Comté">regional council of Bourgogne-Franche-Comté</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture_and_livelihoods">Culture and livelihoods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burgundy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Culture and livelihoods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Burgundy_wine" title="Burgundy wine">Burgundy wine</a></div>
<p>Burgundy is one of France's main wine producing areas. It is well known for both its red and white wines, mostly made from <a href="/wiki/Pinot_noir" title="Pinot noir">Pinot noir</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chardonnay" title="Chardonnay">Chardonnay</a> grapes, respectively, although other grape varieties can be found, including <a href="/wiki/Gamay" title="Gamay">Gamay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aligote" class="mw-redirect" title="Aligote">Aligote</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pinot_blanc" title="Pinot blanc">Pinot blanc</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sauvignon_blanc" title="Sauvignon blanc">Sauvignon blanc</a>. The region is divided into the <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B4te-d%27Or" title="Côte-d&#39;Or">Côte-d'Or</a>, where the most expensive and prized Burgundies are found, and <a href="/wiki/Beaujolais" title="Beaujolais">Beaujolais</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chablis" title="Chablis">Chablis</a>, the <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B4te_Chalonnaise" title="Côte Chalonnaise">Côte Chalonnaise</a> and <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A2con" title="Mâcon">Mâcon</a>. The reputation and quality of the top wines, together with the fact that they are often produced in small quantities, has led to high demand and high prices, with some Burgundies ranking among the most expensive wines in the world.
</p><p>With regard to cuisine, the region is famous for the Burgundian dishes <a href="/wiki/Coq_au_vin" title="Coq au vin">coq au vin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beef_bourguignon" title="Beef bourguignon">beef bourguignon</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%89poisses_de_Bourgogne" title="Époisses de Bourgogne">époisses de Bourgogne</a></i> cheese.
</p><p>Tourist sites of Burgundy include the <a href="/wiki/Rock_of_Solutr%C3%A9" title="Rock of Solutré">Rock of Solutré</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tournus" title="Tournus">Tournus</a> cathedral, <a href="/wiki/Cluny" title="Cluny">Cluny</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Brancion&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Brancion (page does not exist)">Brancion</a>, the castles of <a href="/wiki/Cormatin" title="Cormatin">Cormatin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Couches" title="Couches">Couches</a>, the palace of the dukes of Burgundy in <a href="/wiki/Dijon" title="Dijon">Dijon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Arboretum_de_P%C3%A9zanin" title="Arboretum de Pézanin">Pézanin Arboretum</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Dompierre-les-Ormes" title="Dompierre-les-Ormes">Dompierre-les-Ormes</a>), and <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A9zelay_Abbey" title="Vézelay Abbey">Vézelay Abbey</a>.
</p><p>Earlier, the southeastern part of Burgundy was heavily industrial, with coal mines near <a href="/wiki/Montceau-les-Mines" title="Montceau-les-Mines">Montceau-les-Mines</a> and iron foundries and crystal works in <a href="/wiki/Le_Creusot" title="Le Creusot">Le Creusot</a>. These industries declined in the second half of the twentieth century, and Le Creusot has tried to reinvent itself as a tourist town.
</p>
