<p>The <b>Maison Carrée</b> (<small>French pronunciation:&#160;&#8203;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">[mɛzɔ̃ kaʁe]</a></span>; French for "square house") is an ancient building in <a href="/wiki/N%C3%AEmes" title="Nîmes">Nîmes</a>, southern <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>; it is one of the best preserved <a href="/wiki/Roman_temple" title="Roman temple">Roman temple</a> façades to be found in the territory of the former <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maison_Carr%C3%A9e&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Acta_Eruditorum_-_I_iscrizioni,_1760_%E2%80%93_BEIC_13444805.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Acta_Eruditorum_-_I_iscrizioni%2C_1760_%E2%80%93_BEIC_13444805.jpg/220px-Acta_Eruditorum_-_I_iscrizioni%2C_1760_%E2%80%93_BEIC_13444805.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Acta_Eruditorum_-_I_iscrizioni%2C_1760_%E2%80%93_BEIC_13444805.jpg/330px-Acta_Eruditorum_-_I_iscrizioni%2C_1760_%E2%80%93_BEIC_13444805.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Acta_Eruditorum_-_I_iscrizioni%2C_1760_%E2%80%93_BEIC_13444805.jpg/440px-Acta_Eruditorum_-_I_iscrizioni%2C_1760_%E2%80%93_BEIC_13444805.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1274" data-file-height="944" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Acta_Eruditorum_-_I_iscrizioni,_1760_%E2%80%93_BEIC_13444805.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illustration of critique of <i>Dissertation sur l'ancienne inscription de la Maison-Carrée de Nismes</i> published in <a href="/wiki/Acta_Eruditorum" title="Acta Eruditorum">Acta Eruditorum</a>, 1760</div></div></div>
<p>In about 4-7 AD,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> the Maison Carrée was dedicated or rededicated to <a href="/wiki/Gaius_Caesar" title="Gaius Caesar">Gaius Caesar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lucius_Caesar" title="Lucius Caesar">Lucius Caesar</a>, grandsons and adopted heirs of <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a> who both died young. The inscription dedicating the temple to Gaius and Lucius was removed in <a href="/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">medieval</a> times.  However, a local scholar, <a href="/wiki/Jean-Fran%C3%A7ois_S%C3%A9guier" title="Jean-François Séguier">Jean-François Séguier</a>, was able to reconstruct the inscription in 1758 from the order and number of the holes on the front <a href="/wiki/Frieze" title="Frieze">frieze</a> and <a href="/wiki/Architrave" title="Architrave">architrave</a>, to which the bronze letters had been affixed by projecting tines.  According to Séguier's reconstruction, the text of the dedication read (in translation): "To Gaius Caesar, son of Augustus, Consul; to Lucius Caesar, son of Augustus, Consul designate; to the princes of youth."<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> During the 19th century the temple slowly began to recover its original splendour, due to the efforts of Victor Grangent.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maison_Carr%C3%A9e&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MaisonCarr%C3%A9eFace.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/MaisonCarr%C3%A9eFace.JPG/220px-MaisonCarr%C3%A9eFace.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="215" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/MaisonCarr%C3%A9eFace.JPG/330px-MaisonCarr%C3%A9eFace.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/MaisonCarr%C3%A9eFace.JPG/440px-MaisonCarr%C3%A9eFace.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1720" data-file-height="1680" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MaisonCarr%C3%A9eFace.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Front view</div></div></div>
<p>The Maison Carrée is an example of <a href="/wiki/Vitruvius" title="Vitruvius">Vitruvian</a> architecture.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  Raised on a 2.85 m high podium, the temple dominated the <a href="/wiki/Forum_(Roman)" title="Forum (Roman)">forum</a> of the Roman city, forming a rectangle almost twice as long as it is wide, measuring 26.42 m by 13.54 m.  The <a href="/wiki/Facade" title="Facade">façade</a> is dominated by a deep <a href="/wiki/Portico" title="Portico">portico</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pronaos" class="mw-redirect" title="Pronaos">pronaos</a> almost a third of the building's length.  It is a <a href="/wiki/Hexastyle" class="mw-redirect" title="Hexastyle">hexastyle</a> design with six <a href="/wiki/Corinthian_order" title="Corinthian order">Corinthian columns</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Pediment" title="Pediment">pediment</a> at either end,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Pseudoperipteral" class="mw-redirect" title="Pseudoperipteral">pseudoperipteral</a> in that twenty <a href="/wiki/Engaged_column" title="Engaged column">engaged columns</a> are embedded along the walls of the <a href="/wiki/Cella" title="Cella">cella</a>.  Above the columns, the architrave is divided by two recessed rows of petrified water drips into three levels with ratios of 1:2:3.  <a href="/wiki/Egg-and-dart" title="Egg-and-dart">Egg-and-dart</a> decoration divides the architrave from the frieze.  On three sides the frieze is decorated with fine ornamental relief carvings of rosettes and <a href="/wiki/Acanthus_(ornament)" title="Acanthus (ornament)">acanthus leaves</a> beneath a row of very fine <a href="/wiki/Dentil" title="Dentil">dentils</a>.
</p><p>A large door (6.87 m high by 3.27 m wide) leads to the surprisingly small and windowless interior, where the shrine was originally housed. This is now used to house a tourist oriented <a href="/wiki/3-D_film" class="mw-redirect" title="3-D film">film</a> on the Roman history of Nîmes. No ancient decoration remains inside the cella.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Restorations">Restorations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maison_Carr%C3%A9e&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Restorations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The building has undergone extensive restoration over the centuries.  Until the 19th century, it formed part of a larger complex of adjoining buildings.  These were demolished when the Maison Carrée housed what is now the <a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_des_Beaux-Arts_de_N%C3%AEmes" title="Musée des Beaux-Arts de Nîmes">Musée des Beaux-Arts de Nîmes</a> (from 1821 to 1907), restoring it to the isolation it would have enjoyed in Roman times.  The pronaos was restored in the early part of the 19th century when a new ceiling was provided, designed in the Roman style.  The present door was made in 1824.
</p>

<p>It underwent a further restoration between 1988–1992, during which time it was re-roofed and the square around it was cleared, revealing the outlines of the forum.  <a href="/wiki/Norman_Foster,_Baron_Foster_of_Thames_Bank" title="Norman Foster, Baron Foster of Thames Bank">Sir Norman Foster</a> was commissioned to build a modern art gallery and public library, known as the <a href="/wiki/Carr%C3%A9_d%27Art" title="Carré d&#39;Art">Carré d'Art</a>, on the far side of the square, to replace the city theater of Nîmes, which had burnt down in 1952.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  This provides a startling contrast to the Maison Carrée but renders many of its features, such as the portico and columns, in steel and glass.  The contrast of its modernity is thus muted by the physical resemblance between the two buildings, representing architectural styles 2000 years apart.
</p><p>The Maison Carrée inspired the <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_architecture" title="Neoclassical architecture">neoclassical</a> <a href="/wiki/%C3%89glise_de_la_Madeleine" class="mw-redirect" title="Église de la Madeleine">Église de la Madeleine</a> in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, St. Marcellinus Church in <a href="/wiki/Rogalin" title="Rogalin">Rogalin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, and in the United States the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_State_Capitol" title="Virginia State Capitol">Virginia State Capitol</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> which was designed by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">Thomas Jefferson</a>, who had a stucco model made of the Maison Carrée while he was minister to France in 1785.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
