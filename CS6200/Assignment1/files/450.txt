<p>A <b>sibling</b> is one of two or more individuals having one or both parents in common.  A full sibling is a <a href="/wiki/First-degree_relative" class="mw-redirect" title="First-degree relative">first-degree relative</a>. A male sibling is a <a href="/wiki/Brother" title="Brother">brother</a>, and a female sibling is a <a href="/wiki/Sister" title="Sister">sister</a>. In most societies throughout the world, siblings often grow up together, thereby facilitating the development of strong <a href="/wiki/Human_bonding" title="Human bonding">emotional bonds</a>. The emotional bond between siblings is often complicated and is influenced by factors such as parental treatment, <a href="/wiki/Birth_order" title="Birth order">birth order</a>, <a href="/wiki/Personality_psychology" title="Personality psychology">personality</a>, and personal experiences outside the family.<sup id="cite_ref-psychtoday_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-psychtoday-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Identical_twins" class="mw-redirect" title="Identical twins">Identical twins</a> share 100% of their <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Understanding_genetics_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Understanding_genetics-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Full siblings are first-degree <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">relatives</a> and, on average, share 50% of their genes out of those that <a href="/wiki/Human_genetic_variation" title="Human genetic variation">vary among humans</a>, assuming that the parents share none of those genes.<sup id="cite_ref-Understanding_genetics_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Understanding_genetics-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Half-siblings are genetically second-degree relatives and have, on average, a 25% overlap in their human genetic variation.<sup id="cite_ref-Why_half_siblings_share_25%_of_their_DNA_-_Understanding_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Why_half_siblings_share_25%_of_their_DNA_-_Understanding-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Full">Full</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Full">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Haitian_brothers.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Haitian_brothers.jpg/220px-Haitian_brothers.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Haitian_brothers.jpg/330px-Haitian_brothers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Haitian_brothers.jpg/440px-Haitian_brothers.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4368" data-file-height="2912" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Haitian_brothers.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Two brothers from <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Full_Siblings.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/47/Full_Siblings.PNG/220px-Full_Siblings.PNG" decoding="async" width="220" height="136" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/47/Full_Siblings.PNG/330px-Full_Siblings.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/47/Full_Siblings.PNG/440px-Full_Siblings.PNG 2x" data-file-width="496" data-file-height="307" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Full_Siblings.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Full siblings.</div></div></div>
<p><i>Full siblings</i> (full brothers or full sisters; or brother and sister) have the same biological parents and are 50% related (full siblings share 50% of their genes out of those that <a href="/wiki/Human_genetic_variation" title="Human genetic variation">vary among humans</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Understanding_genetics_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Understanding_genetics-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Why_half_siblings_share_25%_of_their_DNA_-_Understanding_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Why_half_siblings_share_25%_of_their_DNA_-_Understanding-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Identical_twin" class="mw-redirect" title="Identical twin">Identical twins</a> by definition are 100% related.<sup id="cite_ref-Understanding_genetics_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Understanding_genetics-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Full siblings are also the most common type of siblings.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Twins">Twins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Twins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There are two types of twins: <a href="/wiki/Identical_twins" class="mw-redirect" title="Identical twins">identical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fraternal_twins" class="mw-redirect" title="Fraternal twins">fraternal</a>. Identical twins have exactly the same genes; fraternal twins are no more similar than regular siblings. Often, twins with a close relationship will develop a <a href="/wiki/Twin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Twin language">twin language</a> from infanthood, a language only shared and understood between the two. Studies suggest that identical twins appear to display more twin talk than fraternal twins. At about 3 years of age, twin talk usually ends.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Researchers were interested in subjects who were in the later years of life. They knew that past studies suggested that genetics played a larger role in one's personality in the earlier years of their life. However, they were curious about whether or not this was true later on in life. They gathered subjects with a mean age of 59, who included 99 pairs of identical twins, and 229 pairs of fraternal twins who were all reared apart. They also gathered twins who were reared together: 160 pairs of identical twins, and 212 pairs of fraternal twins. They studied the most heritable traits in regard to personality, which are emotionality, activity level and sociability; also known as EAS. This study found that identical twins resembled each other twice as much as fraternal twins, due to genetic factors. Furthermore, environment influences personality substantially; however, it has little to do with whether they are reared together or apart. This study also suggests that heritability is substantial, but not as substantial as for younger subjects; it has less significance later on in life.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Half"><span id="Half-sibling"></span> Half</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Half">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Half_Siblings.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/da/Half_Siblings.PNG/220px-Half_Siblings.PNG" decoding="async" width="220" height="64" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/da/Half_Siblings.PNG/330px-Half_Siblings.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/da/Half_Siblings.PNG/440px-Half_Siblings.PNG 2x" data-file-width="635" data-file-height="186" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Half_Siblings.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Half-siblings</div></div></div>
<p><i>Half-siblings</i> are people who share one parent. They may share the same mother but different fathers (in which case they are known as <i>uterine</i> siblings or <i>maternal half-brothers/half-sisters</i>), or they may have the same father but different mothers (in which case, they are known as <i>agnate</i> siblings or <i>paternal half-brothers/half-sisters</i>. In law, the term <i><a href="/wiki/Consanguinity" title="Consanguinity">consanguine</a></i> is used in place of agnate). They share only one parent instead of two as full siblings do and are on average 25% related.<sup id="cite_ref-Why_half_siblings_share_25%_of_their_DNA_-_Understanding_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Why_half_siblings_share_25%_of_their_DNA_-_Understanding-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Theoretically, there is a chance that they might not share genes.  This is very rare and is due to there being a smaller possibility of inheriting the same chromosomes from the shared parent. However, the same is also theoretically possible for full siblings, albeit (comparatively) much less likely. Because of the formation of <a href="/wiki/Chiasma_(genetics)" title="Chiasma (genetics)">Chiasma</a> in late prophase II (cross-over events), both previous statements are generally impossible. <sup id="cite_ref-Why_half_siblings_share_25%_of_their_DNA_-_Understanding_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Why_half_siblings_share_25%_of_their_DNA_-_Understanding-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In law (and especially <a href="/wiki/Inheritance" title="Inheritance">inheritance</a> law), half-siblings have often been accorded treatment unequal to that of full siblings. Old English <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> at one time incorporated inequalities into the laws of <a href="/wiki/Intestacy" title="Intestacy">intestate succession</a>, with half-siblings taking only half as much property of their intestate siblings' estates as siblings of full-blood. Unequal treatment of this type has been wholly abolished in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> but still exists in the U.S. state of Florida.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Three-quarter"><span id="3quarters"></span> Three-quarter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Three-quarter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Three-quarter siblings</b> have one common parent, while their unshared parents have a <a href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_relationship#Human_genealogy" title="Coefficient of relationship">mean consanguinity</a> of 50%. This means the unshared parents are either siblings or parent and child (similar terminology is <a href="/wiki/Horse_breeding#Terminology" title="Horse breeding">used in horse breeding</a>, where it occurs more frequently). Three-quarter siblings are likely to share more genes than half siblings, but fewer than full siblings.
</p>

<p>In this case the unshared parents are full siblings. Furthermore, the three-quarter siblings are also first cousins.
</p><p>In the case where the unshared parents are <a href="/wiki/Identical_twins" class="mw-redirect" title="Identical twins">identical twins</a>, the children share as much genetic material as full siblings do.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Step"><span id="step"></span> Step</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Step">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"Stepsiblings" (stepbrothers or stepsisters) are the children of one's <a href="/wiki/Stepfamily" title="Stepfamily">stepparent</a> from a previous relationship. They are unrelated by blood.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foster">Foster</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Foster">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"Foster siblings" are children who are raised in the same <a href="/wiki/Foster_home" class="mw-redirect" title="Foster home">foster home</a>, foster children of the person's parents, or foster parents' biological children.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adoptive">Adoptive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Adoptive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Two "adoptive siblings" are raised by a person who is the <a href="/wiki/Adoption" title="Adoption">adoptive</a> parent of one and the adoptive or biological parent of the other. Adoptive siblings are legally related but need not be blood-related or biologically related.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In-law">In-law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: In-law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sibling-in-law" title="Sibling-in-law">Sibling-in-law</a></div>
<p>One's sibling-in-law is the sibling of one's spouse or the spouse of one's sibling.
</p>

<p>They are the children of the godfather or godmother or the godchildren of the father or mother. They are unrelated by blood.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Milk">Milk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Milk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In cultures with <a href="/wiki/Milk_kinship" title="Milk kinship">milk kinship</a>, a milk sibling is a person who is not one's biological sibling but was <a href="/wiki/Breastfeeding" title="Breastfeeding">nursed</a> by the same woman as oneself. The concept exists in <a href="/wiki/Islamic_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic law">Islamic law</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cross">Cross</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Cross">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cross-siblings are individuals who share one or more half-siblings; if one person has at least one maternal half-sibling and at least one paternal half-sibling, the maternal and paternal half-siblings are cross-siblings to each other. Stepsiblings are not cross-siblings unless their married parents have a child together. Alternatively, cross-siblings may not be stepsiblings at all, in the event that the respective parents have a child without marrying, or the cross-siblings are born after the parents of the mutual half-sibling have separated. Cross-siblings are not biologically related, unless the parents have a biological relationship irrespective of their children's cross-sibling status.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pseudo">Pseudo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Pseudo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A pseudosibling is someone that is not a sibling but has a relationship like a sibling, or that one considers to be like a sibling. Pseudosiblings are often not related by blood (consanguinity) and are typically very close friends.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ex-step">Ex-step</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Ex-step">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>An ex-stepsibling is someone who was once one's stepsibling but no longer is. Ex-stepsiblings are neither related by blood nor by law, so they fall under the category of "pseudosiblings".
</p>
<dl><dt>Example</dt></dl>
<pre>Karen has a daughter named Amanda, and Steve has a son named Kevin. While Karen and Steve are married, Amanda and Kevin are stepsiblings, but when Karen and Steve divorce, Amanda and Kevin become ex-stepsiblings.
</pre>
<p>Another definition of an ex-stepsibling is "someone who was previously the stepchild of one's parent". This definition is broader because it includes people who were never stepsiblings. 
</p>
<dl><dt>Example</dt></dl>
<pre>Mary is married to James and has a stepson named Samuel (Samuel is James's son). Mary and James divorce, making Samuel her ex-stepson. Mary then has a son named Ryan with another man. Ryan and Samuel are ex-stepsiblings.
</pre>
<p>In this scenario, the two children are ex-stepsiblings by definition, even though they were never stepsiblings. The term "ex-stepsibling" is less commonly used in situations like these, but is still accurate nonetheless.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Birth_order">Birth order</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Birth order">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Birth_order" title="Birth order">Birth order</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:P_S_Kr%C3%B8yer_1897_-_D%C3%B8trene_Benzon.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/P_S_Kr%C3%B8yer_1897_-_D%C3%B8trene_Benzon.jpg/170px-P_S_Kr%C3%B8yer_1897_-_D%C3%B8trene_Benzon.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="209" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/P_S_Kr%C3%B8yer_1897_-_D%C3%B8trene_Benzon.jpg/255px-P_S_Kr%C3%B8yer_1897_-_D%C3%B8trene_Benzon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/P_S_Kr%C3%B8yer_1897_-_D%C3%B8trene_Benzon.jpg/340px-P_S_Kr%C3%B8yer_1897_-_D%C3%B8trene_Benzon.jpg 2x" data-file-width="880" data-file-height="1082" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:P_S_Kr%C3%B8yer_1897_-_D%C3%B8trene_Benzon.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>The Benzon Daughters</i> by <a href="/wiki/Peder_Severin_Kr%C3%B8yer" title="Peder Severin Krøyer">Peder Severin Krøyer</a></div></div></div>
<p>Birth order is a person's rank by age among his or her siblings. Typically, researchers classify siblings as "eldest", "middle child", and "youngest" or simply distinguish between "firstborn" and "later-born" children.
</p><p>Birth order is commonly believed in <a href="/wiki/Pop_psychology" class="mw-redirect" title="Pop psychology">pop psychology</a> and popular culture to have a profound and lasting effect on psychological development and <a href="/wiki/Personality_psychology" title="Personality psychology">personality</a>. For example, firstborns are seen as conservative and high achieving, middle children as natural mediators, and youngest children as charming and outgoing. Despite its lasting presence in the public domain, studies have failed to consistently produce clear, valid, and compelling findings. Therefore, it has honed the title of a <a href="/wiki/Pseudoscience" title="Pseudoscience">pseudo-psychology</a> amongst the scientific psychological community.<sup id="cite_ref-Psychology_Around_Us_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Psychology_Around_Us-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The theorizing and study of birth order can be traced back to <a href="/wiki/Francis_Galton" title="Francis Galton">Francis Galton</a>'s (1822–1911) theory of birth order and <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/eminence" class="extiw" title="wikt:eminence">eminence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Adler" title="Alfred Adler">Alfred Adler</a>'s (1870–1937) theory of birth order and personality characteristics.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Galton">Galton</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Galton">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In his book <i>English Men of Science: Their Nature and Nurture</i> (1874), Galton noted that prominent composers and scientists are over-represented as first-borns.<sup id="cite_ref-intelltheory.com_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-intelltheory.com-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> He theorized three main reasons as to why first-borns are generally more eminent:
</p>
<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Primogeniture" title="Primogeniture">Primogeniture</a> laws: first-borns have access to their parents' financial resources to continue their education.<sup id="cite_ref-intelltheory.com_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-intelltheory.com-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>First-borns are given more responsibility than their younger siblings and are treated more as companions by their parents.<sup id="cite_ref-intelltheory.com_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-intelltheory.com-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>First-borns are given more attention and nourishment in families with limited financial resources.<sup id="cite_ref-intelltheory.com_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-intelltheory.com-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></li></ol>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Adler">Adler</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Adler">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul><li>First Borns: Fulfilling family roles of leadership and authority, obedient of protocol and hierarchy. Seek out and prefer order, structure and adherence to norms and rules. They partake in goal-striving behaviour as their lives are centred around achievement and accomplishment themes. They fear the loss of their position in the top of the hierarchy.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Middle Children: Feel like outcasts of families as they lack primacy of the first child and the "attention garnering recency" of the youngest. These children often go to great lengths to de-identify themselves with their siblings, in an attempt to make a different and individualized identity for themselves as they feel like they were "squeezed out" of their families.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Youngest Children: Feel disadvantaged compared to older siblings, are often perceived as less capable or experienced and are therefore indulged and spoiled. Because of this, they are skilled in coaxing/charming others to do things for them or provide. This contributes to the image of them being popular and outgoing, as they engage in attention-seeking behaviour to meet their needs.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_findings">Contemporary findings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Contemporary findings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Today, the flaws and inconsistencies in birth order research eliminate its validity. It is very difficult to control solely for factors related to birth order, and therefore most studies produce ambiguous results.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Embedded into theories of birth order is a debate of nature versus nurture. It has been disproved that there is something innate in the position one is born into, and therefore creating a preset role. Birth order has no genetic basis.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The social interaction that occurs as a result of birth order however is the most notable. Older siblings often become role models of behaviour, and younger siblings become learners and supervisees. Older siblings are at a developmental advantage both cognitively and socially. The role of birth order also depends greatly and varies greatly on family context. Family size, sibling identification, age gap, modeling, parenting techniques, gender, class, race, and temperament are all confounding variables that can influence behaviour and therefore perceived behaviour of specific birth categories.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The research on birth order does have stronger correlations, however, in areas such as intelligence and physical features, but are likely caused by other factors other than the actual position of birth. Some research has found that firstborn children have slightly higher <a href="/wiki/IQ" class="mw-redirect" title="IQ">IQs</a> on average than later born children.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> However, other research finds no such effect.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> It has been found that first-borns score three points higher compared to second borns and that children born earlier in a family are on average, taller and weigh more than those born later.<sup id="cite_ref-Psychology_Around_Us_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Psychology_Around_Us-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> However, it is impossible to generalize birth order characteristics and apply them universally to all individuals in that subgroup.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_explanations_for_IQ_findings">Contemporary explanations for IQ findings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Contemporary explanations for IQ findings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Resource_dilution_model">Resource dilution model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Resource dilution model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>(Blake, 1981) provide three potential reasons for the higher scoring of older siblings on IQ tests:<sup id="cite_ref-intelltheory.com_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-intelltheory.com-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>Parental resources are finite, first-born children get full and primary access to these resources.<sup id="cite_ref-intelltheory.com_17-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-intelltheory.com-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>As the number of a children in a family goes up, the more resources must be shared.<sup id="cite_ref-intelltheory.com_17-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-intelltheory.com-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>These parental resources have an important impact on a child’s educational success.<sup id="cite_ref-intelltheory.com_17-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-intelltheory.com-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></li></ol>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Confluence_model">Confluence model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Confluence model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p><a href="/wiki/Robert_Zajonc" title="Robert Zajonc">Robert Zajonc</a> proposed that the intellectual environment within a family is ever-changing due to three factors, and therefore more permissive of first-born children's intellectual advancement:<sup id="cite_ref-intelltheory.com_17-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-intelltheory.com-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>Firstborns do not need to share parental attention and have their parents' complete absorption. More siblings in the family limit the attention devoted to each of them.<sup id="cite_ref-intelltheory.com_17-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-intelltheory.com-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Firstborns are exposed to more adult language. Later-borns are exposed to the less-mature speech of their older siblings.<sup id="cite_ref-intelltheory.com_17-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-intelltheory.com-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Firstborns and older siblings must answer questions and explain things to younger siblings, acting as tutors. This advances their cognitive processing of information and language skills.<sup id="cite_ref-intelltheory.com_17-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-intelltheory.com-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></li></ol>
<p>In 1996, interest in the science behind birth order was re-sparked when <a href="/wiki/Frank_Sulloway" title="Frank Sulloway">Frank Sulloway</a>’s book <i>Born To Rebel</i> was published. In this book, Sulloway argues that firstborns are more conscientious, more socially dominant, less agreeable, and less open to new ideas compared to later-borns. While being seemingly empirical and academic, as many studies are cited throughout the book, it is still often criticized as a biased and incomplete account of the whole picture of siblings and birth order. Because it is a novel, the research and theories proposed throughout were not criticized and peer-reviewed by other academics before its release.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Literature_review" title="Literature review">Literature reviews</a> that have examined many studies and attempted to control for confounding variables tend to find minimal effects for birth order on personality.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
In her review of the scientific literature, <a href="/wiki/Judith_Rich_Harris" title="Judith Rich Harris">Judith Rich Harris</a> suggests that birth order effects may exist within the context of the family of origin, but that they are not enduring aspects of personality.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In practice, systematic birth order research is a challenge because it is difficult to control for all of the variables that are statistically related to birth order. For example, large families are generally lower in socioeconomic status than small families, so third-born children are more likely than first-born children to come from poorer families. Spacing of children, parenting style, and gender are additional variables to consider.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regressive_behavior_at_birth">Regressive behavior at birth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Regressive behavior at birth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Trio_Of_Boys_(54780902).jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Trio_Of_Boys_%2854780902%29.jpeg/220px-Trio_Of_Boys_%2854780902%29.jpeg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Trio_Of_Boys_%2854780902%29.jpeg/330px-Trio_Of_Boys_%2854780902%29.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Trio_Of_Boys_%2854780902%29.jpeg/440px-Trio_Of_Boys_%2854780902%29.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1365" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Trio_Of_Boys_(54780902).jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>big brothers and a <a href="/wiki/Newborn" class="mw-redirect" title="Newborn">newborn</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Four_Sisters_MET_DP72050.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Four_Sisters_MET_DP72050.jpg/220px-Four_Sisters_MET_DP72050.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="153" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Four_Sisters_MET_DP72050.jpg/330px-Four_Sisters_MET_DP72050.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Four_Sisters_MET_DP72050.jpg/440px-Four_Sisters_MET_DP72050.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2040" data-file-height="1417" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Four_Sisters_MET_DP72050.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Four Sisters (<a href="/wiki/Frank_Eugene" title="Frank Eugene">Frank Eugene</a>, about 1900)</div></div></div>
<p>The arrival of a new baby is especially stressful for firstborns and for siblings between 3 and 5 years old. Regressive behavior and aggressive behavior, such as handling the baby roughly, can also occur. All of these symptoms are considered to be typical and developmentally appropriate for children between the ages of 3–5.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> While some can be prevented, the remainder can be improved within a few months. Regressive behavior may include demand for a bottle, thumb sucking, requests to wear diapers (even if toilet-trained), or requests to carry a <a href="/wiki/Security_blanket" class="mw-redirect" title="Security blanket">security blanket</a>.
</p><p>Regressive behaviors are the child's way of demanding the parents' love and attention.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Pediatrics" title="American Academy of Pediatrics">American Academy of Pediatrics</a> suggests<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> that instead of protesting or telling children to act their age, parents should simply grant their requests without becoming upset. The affected children will soon return to their normal routine when they realize that they now have just as important a place in the family as the new sibling. Most of the behaviors can be improved within a few months.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Michigan_Health_System" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Michigan Health System">University of Michigan Health System</a> advises<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> that most occurrences of regressive behavior are mild and to be expected; however, it recommends parents to contact a pediatrician or child psychologist if the older child tries to hurt the baby, if regressive behavior does not improve within 2 or 3 months, or if the parents have other questions or concerns.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rivalry">Rivalry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Rivalry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sibling_rivalry" title="Sibling rivalry">Sibling rivalry</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sir_Joshua_Reynolds_004.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Sir_Joshua_Reynolds_004.jpg/170px-Sir_Joshua_Reynolds_004.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="213" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Sir_Joshua_Reynolds_004.jpg/255px-Sir_Joshua_Reynolds_004.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Sir_Joshua_Reynolds_004.jpg/340px-Sir_Joshua_Reynolds_004.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4795" data-file-height="6000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sir_Joshua_Reynolds_004.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Portrait_of_Lady_Cockburn_and_her_Three_Eldest_Sons" class="mw-redirect" title="Portrait of Lady Cockburn and her Three Eldest Sons">Portrait of Lady Cockburn and her Three Eldest Sons</a></i> (1773-1775) by <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Reynolds" title="Joshua Reynolds">Joshua Reynolds</a></div></div></div>
<p>"Sibling rivalry" is a type of competition or animosity among brothers and sisters. It appears to be particularly intense when children are very close in age or of the same gender.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Sibling rivalry can involve <a href="/wiki/Aggression" title="Aggression">aggression</a>; however, it is not the same as <a href="/wiki/Sibling_abuse" title="Sibling abuse">sibling abuse</a> where one child victimizes another.
</p><p>Sibling rivalry usually starts right after, or before, the arrival of the second child. While siblings will still love each other, it is not uncommon for them to bicker and be malicious to each other.<sup id="cite_ref-newbaby_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newbaby-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Children are sensitive from the age of 1 year to differences in parental treatment and by 3 years they have a sophisticated grasp of family rules and can evaluate themselves in relation to their siblings.<sup id="cite_ref-psychtoday_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-psychtoday-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Sibling rivalry often continues throughout childhood and can be very frustrating and stressful to parents.<sup id="cite_ref-michigan_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-michigan-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> One study found that the age group 10–15 reported the highest level of competition between siblings.<sup id="cite_ref-mcnerney_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcnerney-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Sibling rivalry can continue into adulthood and sibling relationships can change dramatically over the years. Approximately one-third of adults describe their relationship with siblings as rivalrous or distant. However, rivalry often lessens over time and at least 80% of siblings over age 60 enjoy close ties.<sup id="cite_ref-psychtoday_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-psychtoday-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Each child in a family competes to define who they are as persons and want to show that they are separate from their siblings. Sibling rivalry increases when children feel they are getting unequal amounts of their parents' attention, where there is stress in the parents' and children's lives, and where fighting is accepted by the family as a way to resolve conflicts.<sup id="cite_ref-michigan_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-michigan-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sigmund_Freud" title="Sigmund Freud">Sigmund Freud</a> saw the sibling relationship as an extension of the <a href="/wiki/Oedipus_complex" title="Oedipus complex">Oedipus complex</a>, where brothers were in competition for their mother's attention and sisters for their father's.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_psychologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Evolutionary psychologist">Evolutionary psychologists</a> explain sibling rivalry in terms of <a href="/wiki/Parental_investment" title="Parental investment">parental investment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kin_selection" title="Kin selection">kin selection</a>: a parent is inclined to spread resources equally among all children in the family, but a child wants most of the resources for him or herself.<sup id="cite_ref-mcnerney_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcnerney-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationships">Relationships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Relationships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Sibling_relationship" title="Sibling relationship">Sibling relationship</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jealousy">Jealousy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Jealousy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Attachment_theory#Attachment_patterns" title="Attachment theory">Attachment theory § Attachment patterns</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jealousy" title="Jealousy">Jealousy</a> is not a single emotion.  The basic emotions expressed in jealous interactions are <a href="/wiki/Fear" title="Fear">fear</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anger" title="Anger">anger</a>, relief, <a href="/wiki/Sadness" title="Sadness">sadness</a>, and anxiety.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>  Jealousy occurs in a social triangle of relationships which do not require a third person. The social triangle involves the relationships between the jealous individual and the parent, the relationship between the parent and the rival, and the relationship between jealous individual and the rival.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Newborn">Newborn</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Newborn">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>First-borns' attachment to their parents is directly related to their jealous behaviour. In a study by Volling, four classes of children were identified based on their different responses of jealousy to new infant siblings and parent interactions.
<b>Regulated Exploration Children</b>: 60% of children fall into this category.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> These children closely watch their parents interact with their newborn sibling, approach them positively and sometimes join the interaction.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> They show fewer behaviour problems in the months following the new birth and do not display problematic behaviours during the parent-infant interaction.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> These children are considered <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/secure" class="extiw" title="wikt:secure">secure</a> as they act how a child would be expected to act in a familiar home setting with their parents present as secure bases to explore the environment.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
<b>Approach-Avoidant Children</b>: 30% of children fall into this category.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> These children observe parent-infant interaction closely and are less likely to approach the infant and the parent. They are anxious to explore the new environment as they tend to seek little comfort from their parents.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
<b><a href="/wiki/Anxious" class="mw-redirect" title="Anxious">Anxious</a>-Clingy Children</b>:  6% of children fell into this category.  These children have an intense interest in parent-infant interaction and a strong desire to seek proximity and contact with the parent, and sometimes intrude on parent-child interaction.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
<b>Disruptive Children</b>: 2.7% of children fall into this category.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> These children are emotionally reactive and aggressive. They have difficulty regulating their negative emotions and may be likely to externalize it as negative behaviour around the newborn.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Parental_effect">Parental effect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Parental effect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Children are more jealous of the interactions between newborns and their mothers than they are with newborns and their fathers.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> This is logical as up until the birth of the infant, the first-born child had the mother as his or her primary care-giver all to his or herself. Some research has suggested that children display less jealous reactions over father-newborn interactions because fathers tend to punish negative <a href="/wiki/Emotion" title="Emotion">emotion</a> and are less tolerant than mothers of clinginess and visible distress, although this is hard to generalize.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Children that have parents with a better marital relationship are better at regulating their jealous emotions.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Children are more likely to express jealousy when their parents are directing their attention to the sibling as opposed to when the parents are solely interacting with them.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Parents who are involved in good marital communication help their children cope adaptively with jealousy.  They do this by modelling problem-solving and conflict resolution for their children.  Children are also less likely to have jealous feelings when they live in a home in which everyone in the family shares and expresses love and happiness.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Implicit_theories">Implicit theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Implicit theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Implicit theories about relationships are associated with the ways children think of strategies to deal with a new situation. 
Children can fall into two categories of implicit theorizing. They may be malleable theorists and believe that they can affect change on situations and people. Alternatively, they may be fixed theorists, believing situations and people are not changeable.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> These implicit beliefs determine both the intensity of their jealous feelings, and how long those jealous feelings last.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
<b>Malleable Theorists</b> display engaging behaviours, like interacting with the parent or sibling in an attempt to improve the situation.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> They tend to have more intense and longer-lasting feelings of jealousy because they spend more time ruminating on the situation and constructing ways to make it better.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_34-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
<b>Fixed Theorists</b> display non-engaging behaviours, for example retreating to their room because they believe none of their actions will affect or improve the situation.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_34-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> They tend to have less intense and shorter lasting feelings of jealousy than malleable theorists.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_34-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Different_ages">Different ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Different ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Older children tend to be less jealous than their younger sibling.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>  This is due to their ability to mentally process the social situation in a way that gives them more positive, empathetic feelings toward their younger sibling.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Older children are better able to cope with their jealous feelings toward their younger sibling due to their understanding of the necessary relationship between the parent and younger sibling.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Older children are also better at self-regulating their emotions and are less dependent on their caregivers for external regulation as opposed to their younger siblings.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
Younger siblings' feelings of jealousy are overpowered by feelings of anger.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The quality of the relationship between the younger child and the older child is also a factor in jealousy, as the better the relationship the less jealous feelings occurred and vice versa.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conflict">Conflict</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Conflict">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sibling conflict is pervasive, and often shrugged off as an accepted part of sibling dynamics. In spite of the broad variety of conflict that siblings are often involved in, sibling conflicts can be grouped into two broader categories.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceD_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceD-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The first category is conflict about equality or fairness. It is not uncommon to see siblings who think that their sibling is favored by their teachers, peers, or especially their parents. In fact it is not uncommon to see siblings who both think that their parents favor the other sibling.  Perceived inequalities in the division of resources such as who got a larger dessert also fall into this category of conflict. This form of conflict seems to be more prevalent in the younger sibling.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceD_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceD-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The second category of conflict involves an invasion of a child’s perceived personal domain by their sibling. An example of this type of conflict is when a child enters their sibling’s room when they are not welcome, or when a child crosses over into their sibling’s side of the car in a long road trip. These types of fights seem to be more important to older siblings due to their larger desire for independence.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceD_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceD-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Warmth">Warmth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Warmth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sibling warmth is a term for the degree of affection and companionship shared by siblings. Sibling warmth seems to have an effect on siblings. Higher sibling warmth is related to better <a href="/wiki/Social_skill" class="mw-redirect" title="Social skill">social skill</a> and higher perceived social competence. Even in cases where there is a high level of sibling conflict if there is also a high level of sibling warmth then social skills and competence remain unaffected.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceE_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceE-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Negative_effects_of_conflict">Negative effects of conflict</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Negative effects of conflict">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sibling_Conflict.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Sibling_Conflict.jpg/220px-Sibling_Conflict.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Sibling_Conflict.jpg/330px-Sibling_Conflict.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Sibling_Conflict.jpg/440px-Sibling_Conflict.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2816" data-file-height="2112" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sibling_Conflict.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sibling physical conflict</div></div></div>
<p>The saying that people "fight like siblings" shows just how charged sibling conflict can be and how well recognized sibling squabbles are. In spite of how widely acknowledged these squabbles can be, sibling conflict can have several impacts on the sibling pair. It has been shown that increased levels of sibling conflict are related to higher levels of anxiety and depression in siblings, along with lower levels of self-worth and lower levels of academic competence. In addition, sibling warmth is not a protective factor for the negative effects of <a href="/wiki/Anxiety" title="Anxiety">anxiety</a>, <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">depression</a>, lack of <a href="/wiki/Self-worth" class="mw-redirect" title="Self-worth">self-worth</a> and lower levels of academic competence. This means that sibling warmth does not counteract these negative effects.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceE_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceE-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Sibling conflict is also linked to an increase in more risky behavior including: smoking cigarettes, skipping days of school, contact with the police, and other behaviors in Caucasian sibling pairs with the exception of firstborns with younger brothers. Except for the elder brother in this pair sibling conflict is positively correlated with risky behavior, thus sibling conflict may be a <a href="/wiki/Risk_factor" title="Risk factor">risk factor</a> for behavioral problems.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> 
A study on what the topic of the fight was (invasion of personal domain or inequality) also shows that the topic of the fight may have a result on the effects of the conflict. This study showed that sibling conflict over personal domain were related to lower levels of self-esteem, and sibling conflict over perceived inequalities seem to be more related to depressive symptoms. However, the study also showed that greater depressive and anxious symptoms were also related to more frequent sibling conflict and more intense sibling conflict.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceD_35-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceD-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parental_management_techniques_of_conflict">Parental management techniques of conflict</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Parental management techniques of conflict">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Techniques used by parents to manage their children’s conflicts include parental non-intervention, child-centered parental intervention strategies, and more rarely the encouragement of physical conflict between siblings. Parental non-intervention included techniques in which the parent ignores the siblings' conflict and lets them work it out between themselves without outside guidance. In some cases this technique is chosen to avoid situations in which the parent decides which sibling is in the right and may favor one sibling over the other, however, by following this technique the parent may sacrifice the opportunity to instruct their children on how to deal with conflict. Child-centered parental interventions include techniques in which the parent mediates the argument between the two children and helps them come to an agreement. Using this technique, parents may help model how the children can deal with conflicts in the future; however, parents should avoid dictating the outcome to the children, and make sure that they are mediating the argument making suggestions, allowing the children to decide the outcome. Techniques in which parents encourage physical aggression between siblings may be chosen by the parents to help children deal with aggression in the future, however, this technique does not appear to be effective as it is linked to greater conflict levels between children. Parental non-intervention is also linked to higher levels of sibling conflict, and lower levels of sibling warmth. It appears that child-centered parental interventions have the best effect on sibling's relationship with a link to greater levels of sibling warmth and lower levels of sibling conflict.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Long-term_effects_of_presence">Long-term effects of presence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Long-term effects of presence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Studies on social skill and personality differences between only children and children with siblings suggest that overall the presence of a sibling does not have any effect on the child as an adult.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gender_roles_among_children_and_parents">Gender roles among children and parents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Gender roles among children and parents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There have always been some differences between siblings, especially different sex siblings. Often, different sex sibling may consider things to be unfair because their brother or sister is allowed to do certain things because of their gender, while they get to do something less fun or just different. McHale and her colleague conducted a <a href="/wiki/Longitudinal_study" title="Longitudinal study">longitudinal study</a> using middle-childhood aged children and observed the way in which the parents contributed to stereotypical attitudes in their kids.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> In their study the experimenters analysed two different types of families, one with the same sex siblings, and the other with different sex siblings, as well as the children’s birth order.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The experiment was conducted using phone interviews, in which the experimenters would ask the children about the activities they performed throughout their day outside of school.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Surprisingly, the experimenters found that in the homes where there were mixed gender kids, and the father held traditional values, the kids also held traditional values and therefore also played gender based roles in the home.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> In contrast, in homes where the father did not hold traditional values, the house chores were divided more equally among his kids.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> However, if fathers had two male children, the younger male tended to help more with household chores, but as he reached his teenage years the younger child stopped being as helpful around the house.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> There are two important factors that need to be taken into account from this study. First, in cases where the father figure had more traditional values it was found that he may have also had less education than the other dads who participated in the study. Secondly, the mother’s attitudes did not have a noticeable impact on her children’s gender role values.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Altogether this experiment is a good example of the way in which environment and <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">kinship</a> help develop certain perspectives on gender role association in children.
</p><p>In a similar study, Croft and colleagues observed the mother and father's gender roles and examined whether their attitudes would have a long-term effect on the future occupation of their children.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> In this study mothers and fathers were asked a series of questions regarding their work hours and their chores at home, including who looked after the children more often.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> The study demonstrated that mothers felt like they were performing more household duties and they tended to look more after the children.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Something that is quite remarkable in this experiment is the way in which the kids perceive their parents' gender roles. When the kids were asked which parent they would be like when they grew up, some kids did not associate themselves with either male or female occupations, but rather remained neutral, while others leaned towards being more like their same gender parent. However, similar to the study conducted by McHale and her colleagues, this often depended on the father’s traditional values.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> It was also found that girls who observed their parents playing out gendered roles envisioned themselves playing a role similar to their mothers', while daughters who lived in a home where parents did not have sex oriented tasks viewed themselves as working women and family oriented females in the future.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Altogether Croft and her team agreed that in order to create more equality regarding work occupation, the house work also needs to be divided equally.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Westermarck_effect">Westermarck effect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=42" title="Edit section: Westermarck effect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Edvard_Westermarck" title="Edvard Westermarck">Edvard Westermarck</a> found that children who are brought up together as siblings are desensitized to <a href="/wiki/Sexual_attraction" title="Sexual attraction">sexual attraction</a> to one another later in life. This is known as the <a href="/wiki/Westermarck_Effect" class="mw-redirect" title="Westermarck Effect">Westermarck Effect</a>. It can be seen in biological and adoptive families, but also in other situations where children are brought up in close contact, such as the <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israeli</a> <a href="/wiki/Kibbutz" title="Kibbutz">kibbutz</a> system and the Chinese <a href="/wiki/Shim-pua_marriage" class="mw-redirect" title="Shim-pua marriage">shim-pua marriage</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
