<p><b>Euclid</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/juː/: &#39;u&#39; in &#39;cute&#39;">juː</span><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;d&#39; in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Greek language">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%95%E1%BD%90%CE%BA%CE%BB%CE%B5%CE%AF%CE%B4%CE%B7%CF%82" class="extiw" title="wikt:Εὐκλείδης">Εὐκλείδης</a></span> – <i>Eukleídēs</i>, <small>pronounced&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Greek" title="Help:IPA/Greek">[eu̯.kleː.dɛːs]</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Floruit" title="Floruit">fl.</a> 300 BC), sometimes called <b>Euclid of Alexandria</b><sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> to distinguish him from <a href="/wiki/Euclid_of_Megara" title="Euclid of Megara">Euclid of Megara</a>, was a <a href="/wiki/Greek_mathematics" title="Greek mathematics">Greek mathematician</a>, often referred to as the "founder of <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a>"<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> or the "father of geometry". He was active in <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a> during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy_I_Soter" title="Ptolemy I Soter">Ptolemy&#160;I</a> (323–283 BC). His <i><a href="/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements" title="Euclid&#39;s Elements">Elements</a></i> is one of the most influential works in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_mathematics" title="History of mathematics">history of mathematics</a>, serving as the main textbook for teaching <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> (especially <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a>) from the time of its publication until the late 19th or early 20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In the <i>Elements</i>, Euclid deduced the theorems of what is now called <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_geometry" title="Euclidean geometry">Euclidean geometry</a> from a small set of <a href="/wiki/Axiom" title="Axiom">axioms</a>. Euclid also wrote works on <a href="/wiki/Perspective_(visual)" class="mw-redirect" title="Perspective (visual)">perspective</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conic_section" title="Conic section">conic sections</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spherical_geometry" title="Spherical geometry">spherical geometry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Number_theory" title="Number theory">number theory</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rigour#Mathematical_proof" title="Rigour"> mathematical rigour</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> name <i>Euclid</i> is the anglicized version of the Greek name Εὐκλείδης, which means "renowned, glorious".<sup id="cite_ref-Etymology_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Etymology-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span></h2>
<p>Very few original references to Euclid survive, so little is known about his life. He was likely born c. 325 BC, although the place and circumstances of both his birth and death are unknown and may only be estimated roughly relative to other people mentioned with him. He is mentioned by name, though rarely, by other Greek mathematicians from <a href="/wiki/Archimedes" title="Archimedes">Archimedes</a> (c. 287 BC – c. 212 BC) onward, and is usually referred to as "ὁ στοιχειώτης" ("the author of Elements").<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The few historical references to Euclid were written by <a href="/wiki/Proclus" title="Proclus">Proclus</a> c. 450 AD, centuries after Euclid lived.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A detailed biography of Euclid is given by Arabian authors, mentioning, for example, a birth town of <a href="/wiki/Tyre,_Lebanon" title="Tyre, Lebanon">Tyre</a>. This biography is generally believed to be fictitious.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> If he came from Alexandria, he would have known the <a href="/wiki/Serapeum_of_Alexandria" title="Serapeum of Alexandria">Serapeum of Alexandria</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Alexandria" title="Library of Alexandria">Library of Alexandria</a>, and may have worked there during his time. Euclid's arrival in Alexandria came about ten years after its founding by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a>, which means he arrived c. 322 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Proclus introduces Euclid only briefly in his <i>Commentary on the Elements</i>. According to Proclus, Euclid supposedly belonged to <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>'s "persuasion" and brought together the <i>Elements</i>, drawing on prior work of <a href="/wiki/Eudoxus_of_Cnidus" title="Eudoxus of Cnidus">Eudoxus of Cnidus</a> and of several pupils of Plato (particularly <a href="/wiki/Theaetetus_(mathematician)" title="Theaetetus (mathematician)">Theaetetus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philip_of_Opus" title="Philip of Opus">Philip of Opus</a>.) Proclus believes that Euclid is not much younger than these, and that he must have lived during the time of <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy_I_Soter" title="Ptolemy I Soter">Ptolemy I</a> (c. 367 BC – 282 BC) because he was mentioned by Archimedes. Although the apparent citation of Euclid by Archimedes has been judged to be an interpolation by later editors of his works, it is still believed that Euclid wrote his works before Archimedes wrote his.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Proclus later retells a story that, when Ptolemy I asked if there was a shorter path to learning geometry than Euclid's <i>Elements</i>, "Euclid replied there is no royal road to geometry."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> This anecdote is questionable since it is similar to a story told about <a href="/wiki/Menaechmus" title="Menaechmus">Menaechmus</a> and Alexander the Great.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Euclidis_quae_supersunt_omnia.tif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Euclidis_quae_supersunt_omnia.tif/lossy-page1-220px-Euclidis_quae_supersunt_omnia.tif.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="326" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Euclidis_quae_supersunt_omnia.tif/lossy-page1-330px-Euclidis_quae_supersunt_omnia.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Euclidis_quae_supersunt_omnia.tif/lossy-page1-440px-Euclidis_quae_supersunt_omnia.tif.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3966" data-file-height="5878" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Euclidis_quae_supersunt_omnia.tif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Euclidis quae supersunt omnia</i> (1704)</div></div></div>
<p>Euclid died c. 270 BC, presumably in Alexandria.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In the only other key reference to Euclid, <a href="/wiki/Pappus_of_Alexandria" title="Pappus of Alexandria">Pappus of Alexandria</a> (c. 320 AD) briefly mentioned that <a href="/wiki/Apollonius_of_Perga" title="Apollonius of Perga">Apollonius</a> "spent a very long time with the pupils of Euclid at Alexandria, and it was thus that he acquired such a scientific habit of thought" c. 247–222&#160;BC.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Because the lack of biographical information is unusual for the period (extensive biographies being available for most significant Greek mathematicians several centuries before and after Euclid), some researchers have proposed that Euclid was not a historical personage, and that his works were written by a team of mathematicians who took the name Euclid from <a href="/wiki/Euclid_of_Megara" title="Euclid of Megara">Euclid of Megara</a> (à la <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Bourbaki" title="Nicolas Bourbaki">Bourbaki</a>). However, this hypothesis is not well accepted by scholars and there is little evidence in its favor.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

</p>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements" title="Euclid&#39;s Elements">Euclid's Elements</a></div>
<p>Although many of the results in <i>Elements</i> originated with earlier mathematicians, one of Euclid's accomplishments was to present them in a single, logically coherent framework, making it easy to use and easy to reference, including a system of rigorous <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_proof" title="Mathematical proof">mathematical proofs</a> that remains the basis of mathematics 23 centuries later.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There is no mention of Euclid in the earliest remaining copies of the <i>Elements</i>. Most of the copies say they are "from the edition of <a href="/wiki/Theon_of_Alexandria" title="Theon of Alexandria">Theon</a>" or the "lectures of Theon",<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> while the text considered to be primary, held by the Vatican, mentions no author. Proclus provides the only reference ascribing the <i>Elements</i> to Euclid.
</p><p>Although best known for its geometric results, the <i>Elements</i> also includes <a href="/wiki/Number_theory" title="Number theory">number theory</a>. It considers the connection between <a href="/wiki/Perfect_numbers" class="mw-redirect" title="Perfect numbers">perfect numbers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mersenne_primes" class="mw-redirect" title="Mersenne primes">Mersenne primes</a> (known as the <a href="/wiki/Euclid%E2%80%93Euler_theorem" title="Euclid–Euler theorem">Euclid–Euler theorem</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Euclid%27s_theorem" title="Euclid&#39;s theorem">infinitude of prime numbers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Euclid%27s_lemma" title="Euclid&#39;s lemma">Euclid's lemma</a> on factorization (which leads to the <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_arithmetic" title="Fundamental theorem of arithmetic">fundamental theorem of arithmetic</a> on uniqueness of <a href="/wiki/Integer_factorization" title="Integer factorization">prime factorizations</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_algorithm" title="Euclidean algorithm">Euclidean algorithm</a> for finding the <a href="/wiki/Greatest_common_divisor" title="Greatest common divisor">greatest common divisor</a> of two numbers.
</p><p>The geometrical system described in the <i>Elements</i> was long known simply as <i><a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a></i>, and was considered to be the only geometry possible. Today, however, that system is often referred to as <i><a href="/wiki/Euclidean_geometry" title="Euclidean geometry">Euclidean geometry</a></i> to distinguish it from other so-called <i><a href="/wiki/Non-Euclidean_geometry" title="Non-Euclidean geometry">non-Euclidean geometries</a></i> discovered in the 19th century.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fragments">Fragments</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Oxyrhynchus_Papyri" title="Oxyrhynchus Papyri">Oxyrhynchus Papyri</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Papyrus_Oxyrhynchus_29" title="Papyrus Oxyrhynchus 29">Papyrus Oxyrhynchus 29</a> (P. Oxy. 29) is a fragment of the second book of the <i><a href="/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements" title="Euclid&#39;s Elements">Elements</a></i> of Euclid, unearthed by <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Grenfell" class="mw-redirect" title="Bernard Grenfell">Grenfell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Surridge_Hunt" title="Arthur Surridge Hunt">Hunt</a> 1897 in <a href="/wiki/Oxyrhynchus" title="Oxyrhynchus">Oxyrhynchus</a>. More recent scholarship suggests a date of 75–125 AD.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The fragment contains the statement of the 5th proposition of Book 2, which in the translation of <a href="/wiki/T._L._Heath" class="mw-redirect" title="T. L. Heath">T. L. Heath</a> reads:<sup id="cite_ref-Cass_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cass-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>If a straight line be cut into equal and unequal segments, the rectangle contained by the unequal segments of the whole together with the square on the straight line between the points of section is equal to the square on the half.
</p></blockquote>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_works">Other works</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Euclid_Dodecahedron_1.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Euclid_Dodecahedron_1.svg/220px-Euclid_Dodecahedron_1.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="249" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Euclid_Dodecahedron_1.svg/330px-Euclid_Dodecahedron_1.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Euclid_Dodecahedron_1.svg/440px-Euclid_Dodecahedron_1.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="750" data-file-height="850" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Euclid_Dodecahedron_1.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Euclid's construction of a regular <a href="/wiki/Dodecahedron" title="Dodecahedron">dodecahedron</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cube_to_dodecahedron_240px.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Cube_to_dodecahedron_240px.gif/220px-Cube_to_dodecahedron_240px.gif" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/58/Cube_to_dodecahedron_240px.gif 1.5x" data-file-width="240" data-file-height="240" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cube_to_dodecahedron_240px.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Construction of a dodecahedron by placing faces on the edges of a cube.</div></div></div>
<p>In addition to the <i>Elements</i>, at least five works of Euclid have survived to the present day. They follow the same logical structure as <i>Elements</i>, with definitions and proved propositions.
</p>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Data_(Euclid)" title="Data (Euclid)">Data</a></i> deals with the nature and implications of "given" information in geometrical problems; the subject matter is closely related to the first four books of the <i>Elements</i>.</li>
<li><i>On Divisions of Figures</i>, which survives only partially in <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> translation, concerns the division of geometrical figures into two or more equal parts or into parts in given <a href="/wiki/Ratio" title="Ratio">ratios</a>. It is similar to a first-century AD work by <a href="/wiki/Heron_of_Alexandria" class="mw-redirect" title="Heron of Alexandria">Heron of Alexandria</a>.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Catoptrics" title="Catoptrics">Catoptrics</a></i>, which concerns the mathematical theory of mirrors, particularly the images formed in plane and spherical concave mirrors. The attribution is held to be anachronistic however by J J O'Connor and E F Robertson who name <a href="/wiki/Theon_of_Alexandria" title="Theon of Alexandria">Theon of Alexandria</a> as a more likely author.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Phaenomena</i>, a treatise on <a href="/wiki/Spherical_astronomy" title="Spherical astronomy">spherical astronomy</a>, survives in Greek; it is quite similar to <i>On the Moving Sphere</i> by <a href="/wiki/Autolycus_of_Pitane" title="Autolycus of Pitane">Autolycus of Pitane</a>, who flourished around 310 BC.</li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:EuclidStatueOxford.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/EuclidStatueOxford.jpg/170px-EuclidStatueOxford.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="270" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/EuclidStatueOxford.jpg/255px-EuclidStatueOxford.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/EuclidStatueOxford.jpg/340px-EuclidStatueOxford.jpg 2x" data-file-width="922" data-file-height="1462" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:EuclidStatueOxford.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>19th-century statue of Euclid by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Durham" title="Joseph Durham">Joseph Durham</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University_Museum_of_Natural_History" title="Oxford University Museum of Natural History">Oxford University Museum of Natural History</a></div></div></div>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Euclid%27s_Optics" title="Euclid&#39;s Optics">Optics</a></i> is the earliest surviving Greek treatise on perspective. In its definitions Euclid follows the Platonic tradition that vision is caused by <a href="/wiki/Emission_theory_(vision)" title="Emission theory (vision)">discrete rays which emanate from the eye</a>. One important definition is the fourth: "Things seen under a greater angle appear greater, and those under a lesser angle less, while those under equal angles appear equal." In the 36 propositions that follow, Euclid relates the apparent size of an object to its distance from the eye and investigates the apparent shapes of cylinders and cones when viewed from different angles. Proposition 45 is interesting, proving that for any two unequal magnitudes, there is a point from which the two appear equal. <a href="/wiki/Pappus_of_Alexandria" title="Pappus of Alexandria">Pappus</a> believed these results to be important in astronomy and included Euclid's <i>Optics</i>, along with his <i>Phaenomena</i>, in the <i>Little Astronomy</i>, a compendium of smaller works to be studied before the <i>Syntaxis</i> (<i>Almagest</i>) of <a href="/wiki/Claudius_Ptolemy" class="mw-redirect" title="Claudius Ptolemy">Claudius Ptolemy</a>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lost_works">Lost works</span></h3>
<p>Other works are credibly attributed to Euclid, but have been lost.
</p>
<ul><li><i>Conics</i> was a work on <a href="/wiki/Conic_section" title="Conic section">conic sections</a> that was later extended by <a href="/wiki/Apollonius_of_Perga" title="Apollonius of Perga">Apollonius of Perga</a> into his famous work on the subject. It is likely that the first four books of Apollonius's work come directly from Euclid. According to Pappus, "Apollonius, having completed Euclid's four books of conics and added four others, handed down eight volumes of conics." The Conics of Apollonius quickly supplanted the former work, and by the time of Pappus, Euclid's work was already lost.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Porism" title="Porism">Porisms</a></i> might have been an outgrowth of Euclid's work with conic sections, but the exact meaning of the title is controversial.</li>
<li><i>Pseudaria</i>, or <i>Book of Fallacies</i>, was an elementary text about errors in <a href="/wiki/Reasoning" class="mw-redirect" title="Reasoning">reasoning</a>.</li>
<li><i>Surface Loci</i> concerned either <a href="/wiki/Locus_(mathematics)" title="Locus (mathematics)">loci</a> (sets of points) on surfaces or loci which were themselves surfaces; under the latter interpretation, it has been hypothesized that the work might have dealt with <a href="/wiki/Quadric" title="Quadric">quadric surfaces</a>.</li>
<li>Several works on <a href="/wiki/Mechanics" title="Mechanics">mechanics</a> are attributed to Euclid by Arabic sources. <i>On the Heavy and the Light</i> contains, in nine definitions and five propositions, Aristotelian notions of moving bodies and the concept of specific gravity. <i>On the Balance</i> treats the theory of the lever in a similarly Euclidean manner, containing one definition, two axioms, and four propositions. A third fragment, on the circles described by the ends of a moving lever, contains four propositions. These three works complement each other in such a way that it has been suggested that they are remnants of a single treatise on mechanics written by Euclid.</li></ul>
