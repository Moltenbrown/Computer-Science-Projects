<p>The <b>O'Brien dynasty</b> (Classical Irish <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>: <i lang="ga">Ua Briain</i>, (Modern Irish <i>Ó Briain</i>, <small>IPA:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Irish" title="Help:IPA/Irish">[oːˈbʲɾʲiənʲ]</a></span>), genitive <i>Uí Bhriain</i>, <small>IPA:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Irish" title="Help:IPA/Irish">[iːˈβʲɾʲiənʲ]</a></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Royal_family" title="Royal family">royal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">noble</a> house founded in the 10th century by <a href="/wiki/Brian_Boru" title="Brian Boru">Brian Boru</a> of the <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1l_gCais" class="mw-redirect" title="Dál gCais">Dál gCais</a> or Dalcassians. After becoming <a href="/wiki/King_of_Munster" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Munster">King of Munster</a>, through conquest he established himself as <i>Ard Rí na hÉireann</i> (<a href="/wiki/High_King_of_Ireland" title="High King of Ireland">High King of Ireland</a>). Brian's descendants thus carried the name Ó Briain, continuing to rule the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Munster" title="Kingdom of Munster">Kingdom of Munster</a> until the 12th century where their territory had shrunk to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Thomond" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Thomond">Kingdom of Thomond</a> which they would hold for just under five centuries.
</p><p>In total, four Ó Briains ruled in Munster, and two held the High Kingship of Ireland (with opposition). After the partition of Munster into <a href="/wiki/Thomond" title="Thomond">Thomond</a> and the MacCarthy <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Desmond" title="Kingdom of Desmond">Kingdom of Desmond</a> by <a href="/wiki/Tairrdelbach_Ua_Conchobair" title="Tairrdelbach Ua Conchobair">Tairrdelbach Ua Conchobair</a> in the 12th century, the dynasty would go on to provide around thirty monarchs of Thomond until 1542. During part of this period in the late 13th century they had a rivalry with the Norman <a href="/wiki/De_Clare" title="De Clare">de Clare</a> house, disputing the throne of Thomond. The last Ó Briain to reign in Thomond was <a href="/w/index.php?title=Murrough_%C3%93_Briain,_1st_Earl_of_Thomond&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Murrough Ó Briain, 1st Earl of Thomond (page does not exist)">Murrough Ó Briain</a> who surrendered his sovereignty to the new <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland" title="Kingdom of Ireland">Kingdom of Ireland</a> under <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a> of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Tudor" title="House of Tudor">House of Tudor</a>, becoming instead <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Thomond" title="Earl of Thomond">Earl of Thomond</a> and maintaining a role in governance. Today the head carries the title of <a href="/wiki/Irish_nobility" title="Irish nobility">Prince of Thomond</a>, and depending on succession sometimes also <a href="/wiki/Baron_Inchiquin" title="Baron Inchiquin">Baron Inchiquin</a>.
</p><p>Throughout the time that the Ó Briains ruled in medieval Ireland, the system of <a href="/wiki/Tanistry" title="Tanistry">tanistry</a> was used to decide succession, rather than <a href="/wiki/Primogeniture" title="Primogeniture">primogeniture</a> used by much of feudal <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. The system in effect was a dynastic monarchy but <a href="/wiki/Elective_monarchy" title="Elective monarchy">family-elected</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy_(class)" title="Aristocracy (class)">aristocratic</a>, in the sense that the royal family chose the most suitable male candidate from close paternal relations—<i>roydammna</i> (those of kingly material) rather than the crown automatically passing to the eldest son. This sometimes led to bitter quarrels and in-family warring. Since 1542, the head of the Ó Briain house adopted primogeniture to decide succession of noble titles instead.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=O%27Brien_dynasty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Ó Brian emerged as chiefs of the <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1l_gCais" class="mw-redirect" title="Dál gCais">Dál gCais</a> tribe from the south-west of Ireland — a cohesive set of <a href="/wiki/Sept" title="Sept">septs</a>, related by blood, all claiming descent in tradition from a common ancestor of Cas,sixth in descent from <a href="/wiki/Cormac_Cas" class="mw-redirect" title="Cormac Cas">Cormac Cas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cormac_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cormac-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In the <i><a href="/wiki/Annals_of_the_Four_Masters" title="Annals of the Four Masters">Annals of the Four Masters</a></i>, the father of Cormac Cas was said to be <a href="/wiki/Ailill_Aulom" title="Ailill Aulom">Oilioll Olum</a>, who was according to tradition <a href="/wiki/King_of_Munster" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Munster">King of Munster</a> and <a href="/wiki/King_of_Leinster" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Leinster">King of Leinster</a> in the 3rd century.<sup id="cite_ref-cormac_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cormac-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Such a connection would have meant that the tribe held kinship with the <a href="/wiki/E%C3%B3ganachta" title="Eóganachta">Eoghanachta</a> who had dominated Munster since the earliest times.<sup id="cite_ref-medi_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-medi-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> While founder mythologies were very common in antiquity and the medieval world, such a connection is generally regarded as fanciful and politically motivated in the context of the rise to prominence of the Dalcassians.<sup id="cite_ref-medi_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-medi-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Instead, academic histories generally accept the Dalcassians as being the <a href="/w/index.php?title=D%C3%A9isi_Tuaisceart&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Déisi Tuaisceart (page does not exist)">Déisi Tuaisceart</a>, after adopting a new name — first recorded under their newly adopted name under the year 934 in the <i><a href="/wiki/Annals_of_Inisfallen" title="Annals of Inisfallen">Annals of Inisfallen</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-medi_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-medi-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9isi" title="Déisi">Déisi</a>, a people whose name means literally <i>vassals</i>, were originally located where today is <a href="/wiki/County_Waterford" title="County Waterford">Waterford</a>, south <a href="/wiki/County_Tipperary" title="County Tipperary">Tipperary</a> and <a href="/wiki/County_Limerick" title="County Limerick">Limerick</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-fitzroyal_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fitzroyal-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/O%27Rahilly%27s_historical_model" title="O&#39;Rahilly&#39;s historical model">O'Rahilly's historical model</a> counts them as ethnically <a href="/wiki/%C3%89rainn" class="mw-redirect" title="Érainn">Érainn</a>; the sept split into the <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9isi" title="Déisi">Déisi Muman</a> who continued to hold territory in Waterford and Tipperary, while the west Déisi controlled areas either side of the <a href="/wiki/River_Shannon" title="River Shannon">River Shannon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-fitzroyal_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fitzroyal-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> During the 8th century, the latter was further divided into the Déisi Deiscirt and the Déisi Tuaisceart who would become the Dalcassians.<sup id="cite_ref-medi_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-medi-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-koch_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-koch-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Prehistoric ancestors of the Déisi Tuisceart and Dál gCais may have been a once prominent Érainn people called the <a href="/wiki/Mairtine" title="Mairtine">Mairtine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It was during this century that the tribe annexed to Munster the area today known as <a href="/wiki/County_Clare" title="County Clare">Clare</a> and made it their home. Taken from the weakened <a href="/wiki/U%C3%AD_Fiachrach_Aidhne" title="Uí Fiachrach Aidhne">Uí Fiachrach Aidhne</a> it had previously been part of <a href="/wiki/Connacht" title="Connacht">Connacht</a> but was renamed <a href="/wiki/Thomond" title="Thomond">Thomond</a> (<i>Tuamhain</i>, meaning North Munster). After gaining influence over other tribes in the area such as the <a href="/wiki/Corcu_Mruad" class="mw-redirect" title="Corcu Mruad">Corcu Mruad</a> and <a href="/wiki/Corcu_Baiscinn" class="mw-redirect" title="Corcu Baiscinn">Corcu Baiscinn</a>, the Dalcassians were able to crown <a href="/wiki/Cenn%C3%A9tig_mac_Lorc%C3%A1in" title="Cennétig mac Lorcáin">Cennétig mac Lorcáin</a> as <a href="/wiki/King_of_Thomond" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Thomond">King of Thomond</a>, he died in 951.<sup id="cite_ref-koch_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-koch-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> His son <a href="/wiki/Mathgamain_mac_Cenn%C3%A9tig" title="Mathgamain mac Cennétig">Mathgamain mac Cennétig</a> was to expand their territory further according to the <i><a href="/wiki/Annals_of_Ulster" title="Annals of Ulster">Annals of Ulster</a></i>; capturing the <a href="/wiki/Rock_of_Cashel" title="Rock of Cashel">Rock of Cashel</a> capital of the Eoghanachta, the Dalcassians became <a href="/wiki/Kings_of_Munster" class="mw-redirect" title="Kings of Munster">Kings of Cashel and Munster</a> over their previous overlords for the first time in history.<sup id="cite_ref-medi_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-medi-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Mathgamain along with his younger brother <a href="/wiki/Brian_Boru" title="Brian Boru">Brian Boru</a> began military campaigns such as the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Sulcoit" title="Battle of Sulcoit">Battle of Sulcoit</a>, against the <a href="/wiki/Norse_Gaels" class="mw-redirect" title="Norse Gaels">Norse Vikings</a> of the settlement <a href="/wiki/Limerick" title="Limerick">Limerick</a>, ruled by <a href="/wiki/Ivar_of_Limerick" title="Ivar of Limerick">Ivar</a>. The Dalcassians were successful, plundering spoils of jewels, gold and silver, saddles, finding "soft, youthful, bright girls, booming silk-clad women and active well-formed boys".<sup id="cite_ref-medi_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-medi-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mary_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mary-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The males fit for war were executed at Saingel, while the rest were taken as slaves.<sup id="cite_ref-mary_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mary-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Through much of his reign Mathgamain was competing with his Eoghanachta rival <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1el_Muad_mac_Brain" title="Máel Muad mac Brain">Máel Muad mac Brain</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-koch_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-koch-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Mathgamain was only defeated in the end by a piece of treachery; he believed he was attending a friendly meeting, but was betrayed at <a href="/wiki/Donnub%C3%A1n_mac_Cathail" title="Donnubán mac Cathail">Donnubán mac Cathail</a>'s house, handed over to his enemies and executed in 976.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  The crown of Munster was briefly back in the hands of the Eoghanachta for two years until Brian Boru had thoroughly avenged his brother,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> with the defeat and slaying of Máel Muad in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Belach_Lechta" title="Battle of Belach Lechta">Battle of Belach Lechta</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rise_of_Brian_Boru">Rise of Brian Boru</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=O%27Brien_dynasty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rise of Brian Boru">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Brian_Boru,_King_of_Munster.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Brian_Boru%2C_King_of_Munster.jpg/150px-Brian_Boru%2C_King_of_Munster.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="228" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Brian_Boru%2C_King_of_Munster.jpg/225px-Brian_Boru%2C_King_of_Munster.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9a/Brian_Boru%2C_King_of_Munster.jpg 2x" data-file-width="266" data-file-height="404" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Brian_Boru,_King_of_Munster.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Brian_Boru" title="Brian Boru">Brian Boru</a>, High King.</div></div></div>

<p>The following year Brian came to blows with the Norsemen of Limerick at <a href="/wiki/Scattery_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="Scattery Island">Scattery Island</a> where a monastery was located. Whilst all parties were <a href="/wiki/Christians" title="Christians">Christians</a>, when their king Ivar and his sons took refuge in the monastery, Brian desecrated it and killed them in the <a href="/wiki/Sanctuary" title="Sanctuary">sanctuary</a>; the Vikings of Limerick had earlier killed Brian's mother.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Following this the Dalcassians came into conflict with those responsible for the death of Mathgamain, the Eoghanachta represented by Donovan and Molloy. A message was sent to Molloy, where Boru's son Murrough would challenge him in single combat; eventually the Battle of Belach Lechta took place where Molloy along with 1200 of his soldiers were slain. Donovan was destroyed together with Aralt, his brother-in-law and Ivar's remaining son, newly elected king of the Danes and Foreigners of Munster, in Donovan's fortress of Cathair Cuan, which Brian razed. With this Brian Boru was now the <a href="/wiki/King_of_Munster" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Munster">King of Munster</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mary_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mary-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Brian's rise did not go unnoticed, however; <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1el_Sechnaill_mac_Domnaill" title="Máel Sechnaill mac Domnaill">Máel Sechnaill II</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Clann_Cholm%C3%A1in" title="Clann Cholmáin">Clann Cholmáin</a> sept of the <a href="/wiki/U%C3%AD_N%C3%A9ill" title="Uí Néill">Uí Néill</a>, as reigning <a href="/wiki/King_of_Mide" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Mide">king of Mide</a> and <a href="/wiki/High_King_of_Ireland" title="High King of Ireland">High King of Ireland</a> marched an army down to Munster to send a warning to the Dalcassians. His army cut down the tree of <i>Magh Adhair</i>, which was sacred to the Dalcassians as it was used as their site of royal inaugurations. This sparked a conflict between Máel Sechnaill and Brian, the object of both men to be recognised as High King.
</p><p>A treaty would eventually be reached between Máel Sechnaill and Brian which split the areas of influence in Ireland between them. Brian gained control over a large portion of the island's south including Dublin. The peace didn't last long as Brian used the newly acquired forces of Dublin and Leinster to spearhead an attack against Máel Sechnaill which ended in their defeat and forced Brian to reconsider pressing any further North. The war dragged on but Brian would eventually force Máel Sechnaill to accept his authority when northern branch of the Uí Néill clan refused to support him. Despite his fall in position Máel Sechnaill would become one of Brian's most important allies. Eventually the northern Uí Néill branch would accept Brian's rule as well, unusually for the time this was done peacefully, their submission to Brian was negotiated by the clergy rather than forced in battle. With the most powerful Kings in Ireland now accepting Brian as the High King it was a much easier task for Brian to force the remaining Kings to submit to his rule and though it may have been tenuous he eventually was acknowledged as High King by all the rulers in Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-Kings_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kings-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
