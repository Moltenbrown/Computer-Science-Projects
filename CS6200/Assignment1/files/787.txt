	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Epithet</h1>

<p>An <b>epithet</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el">ἐπίθετον</span> <i>epitheton</i>, neuter of <span lang="el" title="Greek language text">ἐπίθετος</span> <i>epithetos</i>, "attributed, added"<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>) is a <b>byname</b>, or a descriptive term (word or phrase), accompanying or occurring in place of a name and having entered common usage. It has various shades of meaning when applied to seemingly real or fictitious people, divinities, objects, and <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial nomenclature</a>. It can also be a descriptive title: for example, <a href="/wiki/Athena" title="Athena">Pallas Athena</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alfred_the_Great" title="Alfred the Great">Alfred the Great</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suleiman_the_Magnificent" title="Suleiman the Magnificent">Suleiman the Magnificent</a> or <a href="/wiki/W%C5%82adys%C5%82aw_I_the_Elbow-high" title="Władysław I the Elbow-high">Władysław I the Elbow-high</a>.
</p><p>In contemporary use, <i>epithet</i> often refers to an abusive, defamatory, or derogatory phrase, such as a <a href="/wiki/Racial_epithet" class="mw-redirect" title="Racial epithet">racial</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Animal_epithet" title="Animal epithet">animal epithet</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Herzfeld2016_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Herzfeld2016-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> This use as a <a href="/wiki/Euphemism" title="Euphemism">euphemism</a> is criticized by Martin Manser and other proponents of <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_prescription" title="Linguistic prescription">linguistic prescription</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistics">Linguistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Epithet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Linguistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Epithets are sometimes attached to a person's name or appear in place of his or her name, as what might be described as a glorified nickname or <a href="/wiki/Sobriquet" title="Sobriquet">sobriquet</a>.  An epithet is linked to its noun by long-established usage.  Not every adjective is an epithet. An epithet is especially recognizable when its function is largely decorative, such as if "cloud-gathering Zeus" is employed other than in reference to conjuring up a storm. "The epithets are decorative insofar as they are neither essential to the immediate context nor modeled especially for it.  Among other things, they are extremely helpful to fill out a half-verse", <a href="/wiki/Walter_Burkert" title="Walter Burkert">Walter Burkert</a> has noted.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some epithets are known by the Latin term <i>epitheton necessarium</i> because they are required to distinguish the bearers, e.g. as an alternative to numbers after a prince's name—such as Richard the Lionheart (<a href="/wiki/Richard_I_of_England" title="Richard I of England">Richard I of England</a>), or <a href="/wiki/Charles_the_Fat" title="Charles the Fat">Charles the Fat</a> alongside <a href="/wiki/Charles_the_Bald" title="Charles the Bald">Charles the Bald</a>. The same epithet can be used repeatedly joined to different names, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Constantine_the_Great" title="Constantine the Great">Constantine the Great</a>.
</p><p>Other epithets can easily be omitted without serious risk of confusion, and are therefore known (again in Latin) as <i>epitheton ornans</i>. Thus the classical Roman author Virgil systematically called his main hero <i>pius Aeneas</i>, the epithet being <i>pius</i>, which means religiously observant, humble and wholesome, as well as calling the armsbearer of Aeneas <i>fidus Achates</i>, the epithet being <i>fidus</i>, which means faithful or loyal.
</p><p>There are also specific types of epithets, such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Kenning" title="Kenning">kenning</a></i> which appears in works such as <i><a href="/wiki/Beowulf" title="Beowulf">Beowulf</a></i>. An example of a kenning would be using the term <i><a href="/wiki/Whale-road" class="mw-redirect" title="Whale-road">whale-road</a></i> instead of the word "sea".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Epithet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Epithets are characteristic of the style of ancient <a href="/wiki/Epic_poetry" title="Epic poetry">epic poetry</a>, notably in that of <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a> or the northern European sagas (see above, as well as <i><a href="/wiki/Epithets_in_Homer" title="Epithets in Homer">Epithets in Homer</a></i>).  When <a href="/wiki/James_Joyce" title="James Joyce">James Joyce</a> uses the phrase "the snot-green sea" he is playing on Homer's familiar epithet "the wine-dark sea". The phrase "Discreet Telemachus" is also considered an epithet.
</p><p>The Greek term <a href="/wiki/Antonomasia" title="Antonomasia">antonomasia</a>, in rhetoric, means substituting any epithet or phrase for a proper name, as <i>Pelides</i>, signifying the "son of Peleus", to identify Achilles. An opposite substitution of a proper name for some generic term is also sometimes called <i>antonomasia</i>, as a Cicero for an orator. The use of a father's name or ancestor's name, such as "Pelides" in the case of Achilles, or "Saturnia" in the case of the goddess Juno in Vergil's <i>Aeneid</i>, is specifically called a patronymic device and is in its own class of epithet.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>'s play <i><a href="/wiki/Romeo_and_Juliet" title="Romeo and Juliet">Romeo and Juliet</a></i>, epithets are used in the prologue, such as "star-cross'd lovers" and "death-mark'd love."
</p><p>Epithets were in layman's terms glorified nicknames that could be used to represent one's style, artistic nature, or even geographical reference. They originated to simply serve the purpose of dealing with names that were hard to pronounce or just unpleasant.<sup id="cite_ref-LKW_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LKW-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It from there went to something that could be very significant assigned by elders or counterparts to represent one's position in the community or it could be a representation of whomever one wanted to be or thought he was.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The elegance of this movement was used throughout history and even modern day with many examples ranging from "Aphrodite the Heavenly &amp; Zeus the Protector of Guests" all the way to "Johnny Football &amp; King James".<sup id="cite_ref-LKW_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LKW-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/American_comic_book" title="American comic book">American comic books</a> tend to give epithets to <a href="/wiki/Superhero" title="Superhero">superheroes</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/The_Phantom" title="The Phantom">The Phantom</a> being "The Ghost Who Walks", <a href="/wiki/Superman" title="Superman">Superman</a> called "The Man of Steel", and "The Dynamic Duo" <a href="/wiki/Batman" title="Batman">Batman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robin_(comics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Robin (comics)">Robin</a>, who are individually known as "The Dark Knight" and "The Boy Wonder".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Additionally, epíteto, the Spanish version of epithet, is commonly used throughout poems in Castilian literature.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Epithet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In many <a href="/wiki/Polytheistic" class="mw-redirect" title="Polytheistic">polytheistic</a> religions, such as those of ancient Greece and Rome, a deity's epithets generally reflected a particular aspect of that god's essence and role, for which his influence may be obtained for a specific occasion: Apollo <i>Musagetes</i> is "<a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a>, [as] leader of the <a href="/wiki/Muse" class="mw-redirect" title="Muse">Muses</a>" and therefore patron of the arts and sciences<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> while <i>Phoibos</i> Apollo is the same deity, but as shining sun-god. "<a href="/wiki/Athena" title="Athena">Athena</a> protects the city as <i>polias</i>, oversees handicrafts as <i>ergane</i>, joins battle as <i><a href="/wiki/Athena_Promachos" title="Athena Promachos">promachos</a></i> and grants victory as <i>nike</i>."<sup id="cite_ref-special_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-special-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Alternatively the epithet may identify a particular and <i>localized</i> aspect of the god, such as a reference to the mythological place of birth or <a href="/wiki/Numen" title="Numen">numinous presence</a> at a specific sanctuary: sacrifice might be offered on one and the same occasion to Pythian Apollo (<i>Apollo Pythios</i>) and Delphic Apollo (<i>Apollo Delphinios</i>). A localizing epithet refers simply to a particular center of veneration and the cultic tradition there, as the god manifested at a particular festival, for example: Zeus Olympios, Zeus as present at Olympia, or Apollo Karneios, Apollo at the Spartan <a href="/wiki/Carneian_festival" class="mw-redirect" title="Carneian festival">Carneian festival</a>.
</p><p>Often the epithet is the result of fusion of the Olympian divinity with an older one: <a href="/wiki/Erectheus" class="mw-redirect" title="Erectheus">Poseidon Erechtheus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Artemis_Orthia" class="mw-redirect" title="Artemis Orthia">Artemis Orthia</a>, reflect intercultural equations of a divinity with an older one, that is generally considered its pendant; thus most Roman gods and goddesses, especially the <a href="/wiki/Twelve_Olympians" title="Twelve Olympians">Twelve Olympians</a>, had traditional counterparts in Greek, Etruscan, and most other Mediterranean pantheons, such as <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_(mythology)" title="Jupiter (mythology)">Jupiter</a> as head of the Olympian Gods with <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a>, but in specific cults there may be a different equation, based on one specific aspect of the divinity. Thus the Greek word <i>Trismegistos</i>: "thrice grand" was first used as a Greek name for the Egyptian god of science and invention, <a href="/wiki/Thoth" title="Thoth">Thoth</a>, and later as an <i>epitheton</i> for the Greek <a href="/wiki/Hermes" title="Hermes">Hermes</a> and, finally, the fully equated Roman Mercurius <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(mythology)" title="Mercury (mythology)">Mercury</a> (both were messenger of the gods). Among the Greeks, T. H. Price notes<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> the nurturing power of <i>Kourotrophos</i> might be invoked in sacrifices and recorded in inscription, without specifically identifying <a href="/wiki/Hera" title="Hera">Hera</a> or <a href="/wiki/Demeter" title="Demeter">Demeter</a>.
</p><p>Some epithets were applied to several deities of a same pantheon rather accidentally if they had a common characteristic, or deliberately, emphasizing their blood- or other ties; thus in pagan Rome, several divinities gods, and heroes were given the <i>epitheton</i> <i><a href="/wiki/Comes" title="Comes">Comes</a></i> as companion of another (usually major) divinity. An epithet can even be meant for collective use, e.g. in Latin <i>pilleati</i> 'the felt hat-wearers' for the brothers <a href="/wiki/Castor_and_Pollux" title="Castor and Pollux">Castor and Pollux</a>. Some epithets resist explanation.<sup id="cite_ref-special_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-special-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Catholics, <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Orthodox">Eastern Orthodox</a> Christians, and Christians of other churches practice the use of epithets in the veneration of <a href="/wiki/Jesus_Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesus Christ">Jesus</a> (e.g., "Christ"; "<a href="/wiki/Christian_messianic_prophecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian messianic prophecies">Prince of Peace</a>"; "<a href="/wiki/Good_Shepherd" title="Good Shepherd">The Good Shepherd</a>"), of <a href="/wiki/Mary,_Mother_of_Jesus" class="mw-redirect" title="Mary, Mother of Jesus">Mary, Mother of Jesus</a> (e.g. "<a href="/wiki/Theotokos" title="Theotokos">Mother of God</a>"; "<a href="/wiki/Panagia" title="Panagia">Panagia</a>"), and of the saints (e.g. "<a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">Pope Saint John Paul the Great</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theophan_the_Recluse" title="Theophan the Recluse">Saint Theophan the Recluse</a>"). "<a href="/wiki/Our_Lady_of_Lourdes" title="Our Lady of Lourdes">Our Lady of Lourdes</a>" is essentially <a href="/wiki/Periphrasis" title="Periphrasis">periphrasis</a>, except where some aspect of the Virgin is invoked.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rhetoric">Rhetoric</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Epithet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rhetoric">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An epithet is an adjective or adjectival phrase that characterizes a place, a thing, or a person that helps make the characteristics of this thing more prominent. These descriptive phrases can be used in a positive or negative way that benefits the orator. "It will generally happen, that the Epithets employed by a skillful orator, will be found to be, in fact, so many abridged arguments, the force of which is sufficiently conveyed by a mere hint; e.g. if any one says, 'We ought to take warning from the bloody revolution of France,' the Epithet suggests one of the reasons for our being warned; and that, not less clearly, and more forcibly, than if the argument had been stated at length."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> With persuasion being a key component of rhetoric, it is rational to use epithets. The use of persuasive wording gives leverage to one's arguments. Knowledge along with descriptive words or phrases can be a powerful tool. This is supported in Bryan Short's article when he states, "The New Rhetoric derives its empiricist flavor from a pervasive respect for clarity and directness of language."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Rhetors use epithets to direct their audience to see their point of view, using verbal forms of imagery as a persuasive tactic.
</p><p>Orators have a variety of epithets that they can employ that have different meanings.  The most common are fixed epithets and transferred epithets. A fixed epithet is the repetitive use of the same word or phrase for the same person or object. A transferred epithet qualifies a noun other than the person or thing it is describing.  This is also known as a hypallage. This can often involves shifting a modifier from the animate to the inanimate; for example, "cheerful money" and "suicidal sky".
</p><p>Orators take special care when using epithets as to not use them as smear words.  Orators could be accused of racial or abusive epithets if used incorrectly. American journalist <a href="/wiki/William_Safire" title="William Safire">William Safire</a> discussed the use of the word in a 2008 column in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>: "'I am working on a piece about nationalism with a focus on epithet as a smear word,' writes David Binder, my longtime Times colleague, 'which was still a synonym for 'delineation' or 'characterization' in my big 1942 Webster's but now seems to be almost exclusively a synonym for 'derogation' or 'smear word.' ... In the past century, [epithet] blossomed as 'a word of abuse,' today gleefully seized upon to describe political smears."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics_and_military">Politics and military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Epithet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Politics and military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In historical, journalistic, and other writings, epithets often carry a political message. These differ from official titles as they express no legal status; however, they may confer prestige, especially if bestowed by an authority or legislature, and may be used for propaganda purposes. Examples of such epithets are the various traditions of <a href="/wiki/Victory_title" title="Victory title">victory titles</a> awarded to generals and rulers or to entire military units, such as the adjective "<i>Fidelis</i>" ("loyal") bestowed on various Roman legions.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_prior_to_surnames">Usage prior to surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Epithet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Usage prior to surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">May 2019</span>)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Descriptive bynames were given to a person to distinguish them from other persons of the same name.  In England by-names were used during the period when the use of <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Surname" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Surname">surnames</a> had not been extensively adopted. As an example the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a> of 1085 identifies 40 individuals with the given name of "Richard".  Most (40%), such as "Richard of Coursey"  are identified with a locational by-name, indicating where they came from, or in some cases where they lived.  Others (25%), such as "Richard the butler" and "Richard the bald" are identified with an occupational or a personally descriptive by-name. Some of the "Richard's" identified in the Domesday Book, such as Richard Basset, made use of what we would recognize as a surname.  
</p><p>The distinction between a by-name and a surname lies in the fact that the by-name is not usually heritable, and may change for any given person as his circumstances change.  Richard the bald, for example, was presumably not always bald, and Richard of Brampton may not have always lived at Brampton.
</p><p>The use of by-names did not end with the adoption of surnames.  In some cases, before the adoption of middle names, government records, such as taxes lists, included persons with both the same given name and the same surname.  This led to the use of by-names to further distinguish the person.  For example one "John Smith" might be described as "John Smith of the mill", while another might be described as "John Smith the short".
</p>
