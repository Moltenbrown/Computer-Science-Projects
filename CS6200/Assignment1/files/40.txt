<p>In <a href="/wiki/Sociolinguistics" title="Sociolinguistics">sociolinguistics</a>, a <b>T–V distinction</b> (from the Latin <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">pronouns</a> <i lang="la" title="Latin language text"><b>t</b>u</i> and <i lang="la" title="Latin language text"><b>v</b>os</i>) is a contrast, within one language, between various forms of addressing one's conversation partner or partners that are specialized for varying levels of politeness, <a href="/wiki/Social_distance" title="Social distance">social distance</a>, courtesy, familiarity, age or insult toward the addressee. Many languages lack this type of distinction, instead relying on more explicit wording to convey these meanings. The <a href="/wiki/Morphosyntax" class="mw-redirect" title="Morphosyntax">morphosyntactic</a> T–V distinction, though, is found in a variety of languages around the world.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Modern_English" title="Modern English">Modern English</a> no longer has a T–V distinction, with the exception of a few dialects. There previously was one with the pronouns <i><a href="/wiki/Thou" title="Thou">thou</a></i> and <i>you</i>, with the familiar <i>thou</i> disappearing from <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern_English" title="Early Modern English">Early Modern English</a>. Additionally British commoners have historically spoken to nobility and royalty using the third person rather than the second person, a practice that has fallen out of favor. English speakers today often employ <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">semantic</a> analogues to convey the mentioned attitudes towards the addressee, such as whether to address someone by <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given</a> or <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a>, or whether to use <i><a href="/wiki/Sir" title="Sir">sir</a></i>/<i><a href="/wiki/Madam" title="Madam">ma'am</a></i>. Under a broader classification, <b>T</b> and <b>V</b> forms are examples of <a href="/wiki/Honorifics_(linguistics)" title="Honorifics (linguistics)">honorifics</a>.
</p><p>The T–V distinction is expressed in a variety of forms. Two particularly common means are
</p>
<ol><li>Addressing a single individual using the second-person plural forms in the language, instead of the singular (e.g. in French).</li>
<li>Addressing individuals with another pronoun with its own verb conjugations (e.g. in Spanish).</li></ol>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_usage">History and usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=T%E2%80%93V_distinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The terms <b>T</b> and <b>V</b>, based on the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> pronouns <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">tu</i> and <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">vos</i>, were first used in a paper by the social psychologist <a href="/wiki/Roger_Brown_(psychologist)" title="Roger Brown (psychologist)">Roger Brown</a> and the Shakespearian scholar <a href="/wiki/Roger_Brown_(psychologist)#Personal_life" title="Roger Brown (psychologist)">Albert Gilman</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> This was a historical and contemporary survey of the uses of pronouns of address, seen as <a href="/wiki/Semantic" class="mw-redirect" title="Semantic">semantic</a> markers of social relationships between individuals. The study considered mainly <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> and <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>. The paper was highly influential<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and with few exceptions, the terms <b>T</b> and <b>V</b> have been used in subsequent studies.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=T%E2%80%93V_distinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>, <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">tu</i> was originally the singular, and <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">vos</i> the plural, with no distinction for honorific or familiar. According to Brown and Gilman, usage of the plural to the <a href="/wiki/Roman_emperor" title="Roman emperor">Roman emperor</a> began in the 4th century AD. They mention the possibility that this was because there were two emperors at that time (in <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>), but also mention that "plurality is a very old and ubiquitous metaphor for power". This usage was extended to other powerful figures, such as <a href="/wiki/Pope_Gregory_I" title="Pope Gregory I">Pope Gregory I</a> (590–604). However, Brown and Gilman note that it was only between the 12th and 14th centuries that the norms for the use of T- and V-forms crystallized. Less commonly, the use of the plural may be extended to other <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">persons</a>, such as the "<a href="/wiki/Royal_we" title="Royal we">royal <i>we</i></a>" (<a href="/wiki/Majestic_plural" class="mw-redirect" title="Majestic plural">majestic plural</a>) in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>.
</p><p>Brown and Gilman argued that the choice of form is governed by either relationships of 'power' and/or 'solidarity', depending on the culture of the speakers, showing that 'power' had been the dominant predictor of form in Europe until the 20th century. Thus, it was quite normal for a powerful person to use a T-form but expect a V-form in return. However, in the twentieth century the dynamic shifted in favour of solidarity, so that people would use T-forms with those they knew, and V-forms in service encounters, with <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/reciprocity" class="extiw" title="wikt:reciprocity">reciprocal</a> usage being the norm in both cases.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history:_the_power_semantic">Early history: the power semantic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=T%E2%80%93V_distinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early history: the power semantic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">Early Middle Ages</a> (the 5th century to the 10th century), the pronoun <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">vos</i> was used to address the most exalted figures, emperors and popes, who would use the pronoun <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">tu</i> to address a subject. This use was progressively extended to other states and societies, and down the social hierarchy as a mark of respect to individuals of higher rank, religious authority, greater wealth, or seniority within a family. The development was slow and erratic, but a consistent pattern of use is estimated to have been reached in different European societies by the period 1100 to 1500. Use of V spread to upper-class individuals of equal rank, but not to lower class individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modification:_the_solidarity_semantic">Modification: the solidarity semantic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=T%E2%80%93V_distinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Modification: the solidarity semantic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Speakers developed greater flexibility of pronoun use by redefining relationships between individuals. Instead of defining the father–son relationship as one of power, it could be seen as a shared family relationship. Brown and Gilman term this the semantics of solidarity. Thus a speaker might have a choice of pronoun, depending on how they perceived the relationship with the person addressed. Thus a speaker with superior power might choose <b>V</b> to express fellow feeling with a subordinate. For example, a restaurant customer might use <b>V</b> to their favourite waiter. Similarly a subordinate with a friendly relationship of long standing might use <b>T</b>. For example, a child might use <b>T</b> to express affection for their parent.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This may be represented as:

<p>These choices were available not only to reflect permanent relationships, but to express momentary changes of attitude. This allowed playwrights such as <a href="/wiki/Jean_Racine" title="Jean Racine">Racine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moli%C3%A8re" title="Molière">Molière</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ben_Jonson" title="Ben Jonson">Ben Johnson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Marlowe" title="Christopher Marlowe">Marlowe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shakespeare" class="mw-redirect" title="Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a> to express a character's inner changes of mood through outward changes of pronoun.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For centuries, it was the more powerful individual who chose to address a subordinate either with <b>T</b> or with <b>V</b>, or to allow the subordinate to choose. For this reason, the pronouns were traditionally defined as the <i>pronoun of either condescension or intimacy</i> (<b>T</b>) and <i>the pronoun of reverence or formality</i> (<b>V</b>). Brown and Gilman argue that modern usage no longer supports these definitions.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_history">Modern history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=T%E2%80%93V_distinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Modern history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Developments from the nineteenth century have seen the solidarity semantic more consistently applied. It has become less acceptable for a more powerful individual to exercise the choice of pronoun. Officers in most armies are not permitted to address a soldier as <b>T</b>. Most European parents cannot oblige their children to use <b>V</b>. <p>The tendency to promote the solidarity semantic may lead to the abolition of any choice of address pronoun. During the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a> attempts were made to abolish <b>V</b>. In 17th century England the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Friends" class="mw-redirect" title="Society of Friends">Society of Friends</a> obliged its members to use only <b>T</b> to everyone, and some continue to use <b>T</b> (<i>thee</i>) to one another.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In most Modern English dialects the choice of <b>T</b> no longer exists outside of poetry.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Changes_in_progress">Changes in progress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=T%E2%80%93V_distinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Changes in progress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It was reported in 2012 that use of the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> <i lang="fr" title="French language text">vous</i> and the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> <i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">usted</i> are in decline in <a href="/wiki/Social_media" title="Social media">social media</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bbcnewsmag_lawn_2012_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbcnewsmag_lawn_2012-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> An explanation offered was that such online communications favour the philosophy of <a href="/wiki/Social_equality" title="Social equality">equality</a>, regardless of usual formal distinctions. Similar tendencies were observed in <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>, <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Estonian_language" title="Estonian language">Estonian</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bbcnewsmag_lawn_2012_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbcnewsmag_lawn_2012-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_use_in_individual_languages">History of use in individual languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=T%E2%80%93V_distinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History of use in individual languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="English">English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=T%E2%80%93V_distinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> and <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_English#Early_Middle_English" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Middle English">Early Middle English</a> <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">second person</a> pronouns <i lang="enm" title="Middle English language text">thou</i> and <i lang="enm" title="Middle English language text">ye</i> (with variants) were used for singular and plural reference respectively with no <b>T–V</b> distinction. The earliest entry in the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a> for <i>ye</i> as a <b>V</b> pronoun in place of the singular <i>thou</i> exists in a <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> text of 1225 composed in 1200.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The usage may have started among the <a href="/wiki/Norman_French" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman French">Norman French</a> nobility in imitation of French. It made noticeable advances during the second half of the 13th century. During the 16th century, the distinction between the <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">subject</a> form <i>ye</i> and the <a href="/wiki/Object_(grammar)" title="Object (grammar)">object</a> form <i>you</i> was largely lost, leaving <i>you</i> as the usual <b>V</b> pronoun (and plural pronoun). After 1600, the use of <i>ye</i> in standard English was confined to literary and religious contexts or as a consciously archaic usage.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/David_Crystal" title="David Crystal">David Crystal</a> summarises <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern_English" title="Early Modern English">Early Modern English</a> usage thus:
</p><p><b>V</b> would normally be used
</p>
<ul><li>by people of lower social status to those above them</li>
<li>by the upper classes when talking to each other, even if they were closely related</li>
<li>as a sign of a change (contrasting with <i>thou</i>) in the emotional temperature of an interaction</li></ul>
<p><b>T</b> would normally be used
</p>
<ul><li>by people of higher social status to those below them</li>
<li>by the lower classes when talking to each other</li>
<li>in addressing God/Jesus</li>
<li>in talking to ghosts, witches, and other supernatural beings</li>
<li>in an imaginary address to someone who was absent</li>
<li>as a sign of a change (contrasting with <i>you</i>) in the emotional temperature of an interaction<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The <b>T–V</b> distinction was still well preserved when Shakespeare began writing at the end of the 16th century. However, other playwrights of the time made less use of <b>T–V</b> contrasts than Shakespeare. The infrequent use of <b>T</b> in popular writing earlier in the century such as the <a href="/wiki/Paston_Letters" title="Paston Letters">Paston Letters</a> suggest that the distinction was already disappearing from gentry speech. In the first half of the 17th century, <i>thou</i> disappeared from <a href="/wiki/Standard_English" title="Standard English">Standard English</a>, although the <b>T–V</b> distinction was preserved in many <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">regional dialects</a>. When the <a href="/wiki/Quakers" title="Quakers">Quakers</a> began using <i>thou</i> again in the middle of the century, many people were still aware of the old <b>T–V</b> distinction and responded with derision and physical violence.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In the 19th century, one aspect of the <b>T–V</b> distinction was restored to some English dialects in the form of a pronoun that expressed friendly solidarity, written as <i><a href="/wiki/Y%27all" title="Y&#39;all">y'all</a></i>. Unlike earlier <i>thou</i>, it was used primarily for plural address, and in some dialects for singular address as well.<sup id="cite_ref-Schneider_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schneider-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The pronoun was first observed in the southern states of the US among <a href="/wiki/African-American" class="mw-redirect" title="African-American">African-American</a> speakers, although its precise origin is obscure. The pronoun spread rapidly to <a href="/wiki/White_people" title="White people">White</a> speakers in those southern states, and (to a lesser extent) other regions of the US and beyond. This pronoun is not universally accepted, and may be regarded as either nonstandard or a regionalism.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Yous(e)</i> (pron. <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/j/: &#39;y&#39; in &#39;yes&#39;">j</span><span title="/uː/: &#39;oo&#39; in &#39;goose&#39;">uː</span><span title="&#39;z&#39; in &#39;zoom&#39;">z</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/j/: &#39;y&#39; in &#39;yes&#39;">j</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="&#39;z&#39; in &#39;zoom&#39;">z</span></span>/</a></span></span>) as a plural is found mainly in <a href="/wiki/English_language_in_Northern_England" title="English language in Northern England">(Northern) England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_English" title="Scottish English">Scotland</a>, parts of <a href="/wiki/Hiberno-English" title="Hiberno-English">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australian_English" title="Australian English">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_English" title="New Zealand English">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_African_English" title="South African English">South Africa</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a> and parts of <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> in Canada and parts of the northeastern United States (especially areas where there was historically Irish or Italian immigration), including in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York,_New_York" class="mw-redirect" title="New York, New York">New York</a>, and scattered throughout working class Italian-American communities in the American <a href="/wiki/Rust_Belt" title="Rust Belt">Rust Belt</a>. It also occurs in <a href="/wiki/Scouse" title="Scouse">Scouse</a> (the regional dialect of the <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a> area).
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="French">French</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=T%E2%80%93V_distinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: French">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> texts, the pronouns <i lang="fr" title="French language text">tu</i> and <i lang="fr" title="French language text">vous</i> are often used interchangeably to address an individual, sometimes in the same sentence. However, some emerging pattern of use has been detected by recent scholars.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Between characters equal in age or rank, <i lang="fr" title="French language text">vous</i> was more common than <i lang="fr" title="French language text">tu</i> as a singular address. However, <i lang="fr" title="French language text">tu</i> was sometimes used to put a young man in his place, or to express temporary anger. There may also have been variation between <a href="/wiki/Parisian_(person)" class="mw-redirect" title="Parisian (person)">Parisian</a> use and that of other regions.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Middle_French" title="Middle French">Middle French</a> period a relatively stable <b>T–V</b> distinction emerged. <i>Vous</i> was the <b>V</b> form used by upper-class speakers to address one another, while <i lang="fr" title="French language text">tu</i> was the <b>T</b> form used among lower class speakers. Upper-class speakers could choose to use either <b>T</b> or <b>V</b> when addressing an inferior. Inferiors would normally use <b>V</b> to a superior. However, there was much variation; in 1596 <span lang="fr" style="font-style:normal;" title="French language text"><a href="/wiki/%C3%89tienne_Pasquier" title="Étienne Pasquier">Étienne Pasquier</a></span> in his comprehensive survey <i lang="fr" title="French language text">Recherches de la France</i> observed that the French sometimes used <i lang="fr" title="French language text">vous</i> to inferiors as well as to superiors "<i lang="fr" title="French language text">selon la facilité de nos naturels</i>" ("according to our natural tendencies"). In poetry, <i lang="fr" title="French language text">tu</i> was often used to address kings or to speak to God.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_names">Use of names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=T%E2%80%93V_distinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Use of names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The boundaries between formal and informal language differ from language to language, as well as within social groups of the speakers of a given language. In some circumstances, it is not unusual to call other people by first name and the respectful form, or last name and familiar form. For example, German teachers use the former construct with upper-secondary students, while Italian teachers typically use the latter (switching to a full V-form with university students). This can lead to constructions denoting an intermediate level of formality in T–V-distinct languages that sound awkward to English-speakers. In Italian, <i lang="it" title="Italian language text">(Signor) Vincenzo Rossi</i> can be addressed with the <i lang="it" title="Italian language text">tu</i> (familiar) form or the <i lang="it" title="Italian language text">Lei</i> (formal) one, but complete addresses range from <i lang="it" title="Italian language text">Tu, Vincenzo</i> (peer to peer or family) and <i lang="it" title="Italian language text">Tu, Rossi</i> (teacher or fellow student to high-school student, as stated above) to <i lang="it" title="Italian language text">Lei, signor Vincenzo</i> (live-in servant to master or master's son) and <i lang="it" title="Italian language text">Lei, Rossi</i> (senior staff member to junior) and <i lang="it" title="Italian language text">Lei, signor Rossi</i> (among peers).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Translation_issues">Translation issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=T%E2%80%93V_distinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Translation issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The use of these forms may be an issue for <a href="/wiki/Untranslatability#Compensation" title="Untranslatability">compensating translation</a> of dialogue into English if the translator does not wish to use the "thou" pronoun to translate. For example, a character in a French film or novel saying "<i lang="fr" title="French language text">Tutoie-moi&#8201;!</i>" ("Use [the informal pronoun] <i lang="fr" title="French language text">tu</i> when addressing me!") might be translated "Do not be so formal!" or "Call me by my first name!"
</p><p>Conversely, when translating from English to a T–V language, the translator must decide again and again throughout the work which second-person form the reader would deem the more appropriate in a given situation. In the current German DVD release of <i><a href="/wiki/Gone_with_the_Wind_(film)" title="Gone with the Wind (film)">Gone with the Wind</a></i>, the translators of the subtitles and dubbing artists sometimes make opposite decisions; the actors' dubbed voices speak with the familiar form, while the subtitles for the same scene are more formal.
</p>
<h2><span id="Singular.2C_plural_and_other_ways_of_distinction"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Singular,_plural_and_other_ways_of_distinction">Singular, plural and other ways of distinction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=T%E2%80%93V_distinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Singular, plural and other ways of distinction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In many languages, the respectful singular pronoun derives from a plural form. Some Romance languages have familiar forms derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a> singular <i lang="la" title="Latin language text"><b>t</b>u</i> and respectful forms derived from Latin plural <i lang="la" title="Latin language text"><b>v</b>os</i>, sometimes via a circuitous route. Sometimes, singular V-form derives from a third-person pronoun; in <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> and some <a href="/wiki/Nordic_countries" title="Nordic countries">Nordic</a> languages, it is the third-person plural. Some languages have separate T and V forms for both singular and plural, others have the same form and others have a T–V distinction only in the singular.
</p><p>Different languages distinguish pronoun uses in different ways. Even within languages, there are differences between groups (older people and people of higher status tending both to use and to expect more respectful language) and between various aspects of one language. For example, in Dutch, V form <i lang="nl" title="Dutch language text">u</i> is slowly falling into disuse in the plural and so one could sometimes address a group as T form <i lang="nl" title="Dutch language text">jullie</i>, which clearly expresses the plural when one would address each member individually as <i lang="nl" title="Dutch language text">u</i>, which has the disadvantage of being ambiguous. In Latin American Spanish, the opposite change has occurred—having lost the T form <i lang="nl" title="Dutch language text">vosotros</i>, Latin Americans address all groups as <i lang="nl" title="Dutch language text">ustedes</i>, even if the group is composed of friends whom they would call <i lang="nl" title="Dutch language text">tú</i> or <i lang="nl" title="Dutch language text"><a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">vos</a></i> (both T forms).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In Standard Peninsular Spanish, however, <i lang="nl" title="Dutch language text">vosotros</i> (literally "you others") is still regularly used in informal conversation. In some cases, the V-form is likely to be capitalized when it is written.
</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/T%E2%80%93V_distinction_in_the_world%27s_languages" title="T–V distinction in the world&#39;s languages">T–V distinction in the world's languages</a></div>
<h2><span id="Related_verbs.2C_nouns_and_pronouns"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_verbs,_nouns_and_pronouns">Related verbs, nouns and pronouns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=T%E2%80%93V_distinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Related verbs, nouns and pronouns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some languages have a verb to describe the fact of using either a <i>T</i> or a <i>V</i> form. Some also have a related noun or pronoun. The English words are used to refer only to English usage in the past, not to usage in other languages. The analogous distinction may be expressed as "to use first names" or "to be on familiar terms (with someone)".
