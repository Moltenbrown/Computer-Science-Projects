<p>A <b>clan</b> is a group of <a href="/wiki/People" title="People">people</a> united by <a href="/wiki/Consanguinity" title="Consanguinity">actual</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fictive_kinship" title="Fictive kinship">perceived</a> <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">kinship</a><sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Ancestry" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancestry">descent</a>. Even if lineage details are unknown, clan members may be organized around a founding member or <a href="/wiki/Apical_ancestor" class="mw-redirect" title="Apical ancestor">apical ancestor</a>. Clans, in indigenous societies, tend to be <a href="/wiki/Exogamy" title="Exogamy">exogamous</a>, meaning that their members cannot marry one another. Clans preceded more centralized forms of community organization and government, and exist in every country. Members may identify with a <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coat of arms</a> or other symbol to show that they are an independent clan. The kinship-based bonds may also have a symbolic ancestor, whereby the clan shares a "stipulated" common ancestor that is a symbol of the clan's unity. When this "ancestor" is non-human, it is referred to as a <a href="/wiki/Totem" title="Totem">totem</a>, which is frequently an animal.
</p><p>The word clan is derived from the Gaelic <i lang="gd" title="Scottish Gaelic language text">clann</i><sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> meaning "children" or "progeny"; it is not from the word for "family" in either <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a><sup id="cite_ref-Focloir_Dineen_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Focloir_Dineen-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Focloir_De_Bhaldraithe_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Focloir_De_Bhaldraithe-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a>. According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i>, the word was introduced into English in around 1425, as a label for the nature of the society of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Scottish Highlands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The Irish and Scottish Gaelic term for <i>clan</i> is <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">fine</i> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Irish" title="Help:IPA/Irish">[ˈfʲɪnʲə]</a></span>; <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">teaghlach</i> means "family" in the sense of the nuclear family, though it can include other family members if living in the house, <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">líon tí</i> means either "family" in the sense of "household", or everyone who lives in the house, including non-family; and <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">muintir</i> means "family" in the sense of "kinsfolk".<sup id="cite_ref-Focloir_De_Bhaldraithe_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Focloir_De_Bhaldraithe-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clans_as_political_units">Clans as political units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Clans as political units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In different cultures and situations, a clan usually has different meaning than other kin-based groups, such as <a href="/wiki/Tribe" title="Tribe">tribes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Band_society" title="Band society">bands</a>. Often, the distinguishing factor is that a clan is a smaller, integral part of a larger <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a> such as a <a href="/wiki/Tribe" title="Tribe">tribe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chiefdom" title="Chiefdom">chiefdom</a>, or a <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">state</a>. In some societies, clans may have an official leader such as a <a href="/wiki/Tribal_chief" title="Tribal chief">chief</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matriarchy" title="Matriarchy">matriarch</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Patriarch" title="Patriarch">patriarch</a>; in others, leadership positions may have to be achieved, or people may say that "elders" make decisions.
</p><p>Examples include <a href="/wiki/Irish_clans" title="Irish clans">Irish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_clan" title="Scottish clan">Scottish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_clan" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese clan">Chinese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bon-gwan" title="Bon-gwan">Korean</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Japanese_clans" title="Japanese clans">Japanese</a> clans, which exist as <a href="/wiki/Kin_group" class="mw-redirect" title="Kin group">kin groups</a> within their respective nations. Note, however, that tribes and bands can also be components of larger societies. The early <a href="/wiki/Norse_clans" title="Norse clans">Norse clans</a>, the <i lang="non" title="Old Norse language text">ætter</i>, are often translated as "house" or "line". The Biblical 'tribes' of Israel were composed of many clans.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tribes_of_Arabia" title="Tribes of Arabia">Arab clans</a> are sub-tribal groups within Arab society. Native American and <a href="/wiki/First_Nations" title="First Nations">First Nations</a> peoples also have clans. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Ojibwa" class="mw-redirect" title="Ojibwa">Ojibwa</a> bands are smaller parts of the <a href="/wiki/Ojibwa_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Ojibwa people">Ojibwa people</a> or tribe people in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. The many Native American peoples are distinguished by language and culture, and most have clans and bands as the basic kinship organizations. In some cases tribes recognized each other's clans; for instance, both the <a href="/wiki/Chickasaw" title="Chickasaw">Chickasaw</a> and <a href="/wiki/Choctaw" title="Choctaw">Choctaw</a> tribes of the Southeast United States had fox and bear clans, who felt a kinship that reached beyond their respective tribes. 
</p><p>Apart from these different historical traditions of kinship, conceptual confusion arises from colloquial usages of the term. In post-<a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> countries, for example, it is quite common to speak of "clans" in reference to informal networks within the economic and political sphere. This usage reflects the assumption that their members act towards each other in a particularly close and mutually supportive way approximating the solidarity among kinsmen. Similar usage of the term applies to specific groups, of various cultures and nationalities, involved in organized <a href="/wiki/Crime_family" title="Crime family">crime</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Polish</a> clans differ from most others as they are a collection of <a href="/wiki/Families" class="mw-redirect" title="Families">families</a> who bear the same <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coat of arms</a>, as opposed to claiming a common descent (see <a href="/wiki/Polish_heraldry" title="Polish heraldry">Polish heraldry</a>). There are multiple closely related clans in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/South_India" title="South India">South India</a>.
</p>
