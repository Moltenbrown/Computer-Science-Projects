	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Barbarian</h1>

<p>A <b>barbarian</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> who is perceived to be either <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">uncivilized</a> or <a href="/wiki/Primitive_culture" title="Primitive culture">primitive</a>. The designation is usually applied as <a href="/wiki/Generalization" title="Generalization">generalization</a> based on a popular <a href="/wiki/Stereotype" title="Stereotype">stereotype</a>; barbarians can be any member of a <a href="/wiki/Nation" title="Nation">nation</a> judged by some to be less civilized or orderly (such as a <a href="/wiki/Tribal_society" class="mw-redirect" title="Tribal society">tribal society</a>), but may also be part of a certain "primitive" <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">cultural group</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Nomad" title="Nomad">nomads</a>) or <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">social class</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Bandit" class="mw-redirect" title="Bandit">bandits</a>) both within and outside one's own nation. Alternatively, they may instead be admired and romanticised as <a href="/wiki/Noble_savage" title="Noble savage">noble savages</a>. In idiomatic or figurative usage, a "barbarian" may also be an individual reference to a brutal, cruel, warlike, and insensitive person.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The term originates from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el">Œ≤Œ¨œÅŒ≤Œ±œÅŒøœÇ</span> (<i>barbaros</i> pl. Œ≤Œ¨œÅŒ≤Œ±œÅŒøŒπ <i>barbaroi</i>). In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greece</a>, the Greeks used the term towards those who did not speak Greek and follow classical Greek customs.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Ancient Rome</a>, the Romans used the term towards tribal non-Romans such as the <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gauls" title="Gauls">Gauls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iberians" title="Iberians">Iberians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thracians" title="Thracians">Thracians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illyrians" title="Illyrians">Illyrians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berbers" title="Berbers">Berbers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parthian_Empire" title="Parthian Empire">Parthians</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sarmatians" title="Sarmatians">Sarmatians</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_period" title="Early modern period">early modern period</a> and sometimes later, the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Greeks" title="Byzantine Greeks">Byzantine Greeks</a> used it for the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_people" title="Turkish people">Turks</a>, in a clearly <a href="/wiki/Pejorative" title="Pejorative">pejorative</a> manner.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbarian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Invasions_of_the_Roman_Empire_1.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Invasions_of_the_Roman_Empire_1.png/220px-Invasions_of_the_Roman_Empire_1.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="153" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Invasions_of_the_Roman_Empire_1.png/330px-Invasions_of_the_Roman_Empire_1.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Invasions_of_the_Roman_Empire_1.png/440px-Invasions_of_the_Roman_Empire_1.png 2x" data-file-width="1954" data-file-height="1362" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Invasions_of_the_Roman_Empire_1.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Routes taken by barbarian invaders during the <a href="/wiki/Migration_Period" title="Migration Period">Migration Period</a>, 5th century AD</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Genghis_Khan_empire-en.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Genghis_Khan_empire-en.svg/220px-Genghis_Khan_empire-en.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="142" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Genghis_Khan_empire-en.svg/330px-Genghis_Khan_empire-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Genghis_Khan_empire-en.svg/440px-Genghis_Khan_empire-en.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1017" data-file-height="658" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Genghis_Khan_empire-en.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Routes taken by <a href="/wiki/Mongol_Empire" title="Mongol Empire">Mongol invaders</a>, 13th century AD</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> name <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B2%CE%AC%CF%81%CE%B2%CE%B1%CF%81%CE%BF%CF%82" class="extiw" title="wikt:Œ≤Œ¨œÅŒ≤Œ±œÅŒøœÇ">Œ≤Œ¨œÅŒ≤Œ±œÅŒøœÇ</a> (<i>barbaros</i>), "barbarian", was an <a href="/wiki/Antonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Antonym">antonym</a> for <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%80%CE%BF%CE%BB%CE%AF%CF%84%CE%B7%CF%82" class="extiw" title="wikt:œÄŒøŒªŒØœÑŒ∑œÇ">œÄŒøŒªŒØœÑŒ∑œÇ</a> (<i>politƒìs</i>), "citizen" (from œÄœåŒªŒπœÇ ‚Äì <i><a href="/wiki/Polis" title="Polis">polis</a></i>, "city-state"). The earliest attested form of the word is the <a href="/wiki/Mycenaean_Greek" title="Mycenaean Greek">Mycenaean Greek</a> <span lang="gmy" title="Mycenaean Greek language text">êÄûêÄûêÄ´</span>, <i>pa-pa-ro</i>, written in <a href="/wiki/Linear_B" title="Linear B">Linear B</a> syllabic script.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <i>Greeks</i> used the term <i>barbarian</i> for all non-Greek-speaking peoples, including the <a href="/wiki/Egyptians" title="Egyptians">Egyptians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Persian_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Persian people">Persians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medes" title="Medes">Medes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phoenicians" class="mw-redirect" title="Phoenicians">Phoenicians</a>, emphasizing their otherness. According to Greek writers, this was because the language they spoke sounded to Greeks like <a href="/wiki/Gibberish" title="Gibberish">gibberish</a> represented by the sounds "bar..bar..;"  the alleged root of the word <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B2%CE%AC%CF%81%CE%B2%CE%B1%CF%81%CE%BF%CF%82" class="extiw" title="wikt:Œ≤Œ¨œÅŒ≤Œ±œÅŒøœÇ">Œ≤Œ¨œÅŒ≤Œ±œÅŒøœÇ</a>, which is an echomimetic or <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeia" title="Onomatopoeia">onomatopoeic</a> word. However, in various occasions, the term was also used by Greeks, especially the <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athenians</a>, to deride other Greek tribes and states (such as Epirotes, Eleans, Macedonians, Boeotians and Aeolic-speakers) but also fellow Athenians, in a pejorative and politically motivated manner.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The term also carried a cultural dimension to its dual meaning.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The verb <span lang="el" title="Greek language text">Œ≤Œ±œÅŒ≤Œ±œÅŒØŒ∂œâ</span> (<i>barbar√≠z≈ç</i>) in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">ancient Greek</a> meant to behave or talk like a barbarian, or to hold with the barbarians.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> (<i>Statesman</i> 262de) rejected the Greek‚Äìbarbarian dichotomy as a logical absurdity on just such grounds: dividing the world into Greeks and non-Greeks told one nothing about the second group, yet Plato used the term barbarian frequently in his seventh letter.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>'s works, the term appeared only once (<i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i> 2.867), in the form <span lang="el" title="Greek language text">Œ≤Œ±œÅŒ≤Œ±œÅœåœÜœâŒΩŒøœÇ</span> (<i lang="el" title="Greek language text">barbarophonos</i>) ("of incomprehensible speech"), used of the <a href="/wiki/Carians" title="Carians">Carians</a> fighting for <a href="/wiki/Troy" title="Troy">Troy</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Trojan_War" title="Trojan War">Trojan War</a>. In general, the concept of <i>barbaros</i> did not figure largely in archaic literature before the 5th century <a href="/wiki/Anno_Domini" title="Anno Domini">BC</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  It has been suggested that the "barbarophonoi" in the <i>Iliad</i> signifies not those who spoke a non-Greek language but simply those who spoke Greek badly.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A change occurred in the connotations of the word after the <a href="/wiki/Greco-Persian_Wars" title="Greco-Persian Wars">Greco-Persian Wars</a> in the first half of the 5th century BC. Here a hasty coalition of Greeks defeated the vast <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Persian Empire</a>. Indeed, in the Greek of this period 'barbarian' is often used expressly to refer to Persians, who were enemies of the Greeks in this war.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:272px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Barbarian_prisoner_MAN_Napoli_Inv6116.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Barbarian_prisoner_MAN_Napoli_Inv6116.jpg/270px-Barbarian_prisoner_MAN_Napoli_Inv6116.jpg" decoding="async" width="270" height="630" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Barbarian_prisoner_MAN_Napoli_Inv6116.jpg/405px-Barbarian_prisoner_MAN_Napoli_Inv6116.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Barbarian_prisoner_MAN_Napoli_Inv6116.jpg/539px-Barbarian_prisoner_MAN_Napoli_Inv6116.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1735" data-file-height="4050" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Barbarian_prisoner_MAN_Napoli_Inv6116.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A preconnesian marble depiction of a barbarian. Second century AD.</div></div></div>
<p>The Romans used the term <i>barbarus</i> for uncivilised people, opposite to Greek or Roman, and in fact, it became a common term to refer to all foreigners among Romans after Augustus age (as, among the Greeks, after the Persian wars, the Persians), including the Germanic peoples, Persians, Gauls, Phoenicians and Carthaginians.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Greek term <i>barbaros</i> was the etymological source for many words meaning "barbarian", including English <i>barbarian</i>, which was first recorded in 16th century <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a>.
</p><p>A word <i>barbara-</i> is also found in the <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> of ancient India, with the primary meaning of "stammering" implying someone with an unfamiliar language.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The Greek word <i>barbaros</i> is related to Sanskrit <i>barbaras</i> (stammering).<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> This Indo-European root is also found in Latin <i>balbus</i> for "stammering" and Czech blblati "to stammer".<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Aramaic, Old Persian and Arabic context, the root refers to "babble confusedly". It appears as <i>barbary</i> or in Old French <i>barbarie</i>, itself derived from the Arabic <i>Barbar</i>, <i>Berber</i>, which is an ancient Arabic term for the North African inhabitants west of Egypt. The Arabic word might be ultimately from Greek <i>barbaria</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Semantics">Semantics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbarian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Semantics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Germaniae_antiquae_libri_tres,_Plate_17,_Cl%C3%BCver.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Germaniae_antiquae_libri_tres%2C_Plate_17%2C_Cl%C3%BCver.jpg/220px-Germaniae_antiquae_libri_tres%2C_Plate_17%2C_Cl%C3%BCver.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="346" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Germaniae_antiquae_libri_tres%2C_Plate_17%2C_Cl%C3%BCver.jpg/330px-Germaniae_antiquae_libri_tres%2C_Plate_17%2C_Cl%C3%BCver.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Germaniae_antiquae_libri_tres%2C_Plate_17%2C_Cl%C3%BCver.jpg/440px-Germaniae_antiquae_libri_tres%2C_Plate_17%2C_Cl%C3%BCver.jpg 2x" data-file-width="719" data-file-height="1130" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Germaniae_antiquae_libri_tres,_Plate_17,_Cl%C3%BCver.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"<a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic</a> warriors" as depicted in <a href="/wiki/Philipp_Cl%C3%BCver" title="Philipp Cl√ºver">Philipp Cl√ºver</a>'s <i>Germania Antiqua</i> (1616)</div></div></div>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> gives five definitions of the noun <i>barbarian</i>, including an obsolete <a href="/wiki/Barbary" class="mw-redirect" title="Barbary">Barbary</a> usage.
</p>
<ul><li><b>1.</b> <i>Etymologically</i>, A foreigner, one whose language and customs differ from the speaker's.</li>
<li><b>2.</b> <i>Hist</i>. <b>a.</b> One not a Greek. <b>b.</b> One living outside the pale of the Roman Empire and its civilization, applied especially to the northern nations that overthrew them. <b>c.</b> One outside the pale of <a href="/wiki/Christian_civilization" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian civilization">Christian civilization</a>. <b>d.</b> With the Italians of the Renaissance: One of a nation outside of Italy.</li>
<li><b>3.</b> A rude, wild, uncivilized person. <b>b.</b> Sometimes distinguished from <i>savage</i> (perh. with a glance at 2). <b>c.</b> Applied by the Chinese contemptuously to foreigners.</li>
<li><b>4.</b> An uncultured person, or one who has no sympathy with literary culture.</li>
<li>‚Ä†<b>5.</b> A native of Barbary. [See <a href="/wiki/Barbary_Coast" title="Barbary Coast">Barbary Coast</a>.] <i>Obs</i>. ‚Ä†<b>b.</b> <a href="/wiki/Barbary_pirates" title="Barbary pirates">Barbary pirates</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Barb_horse" title="Barb horse">A Barbary horse</a>. <i>Obs</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The <i>OED</i> <i>barbarous</i> entry summarizes the semantic history. "The sense-development in ancient times was (with the Greeks) 'foreign, non-Hellenic,' later 'outlandish, rude, brutal'; (with the Romans) 'not Latin nor Greek,' then 'pertaining to those outside the Roman Empire'; hence 'uncivilized, uncultured,' and later 'non-Christian,' whence 'Saracen, heathen'; and generally 'savage, rude, savagely cruel, inhuman.'"
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_classical_Greco-Roman_contexts">In classical Greco-Roman contexts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbarian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In classical Greco-Roman contexts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Barbarian_kingdoms" title="Barbarian kingdoms">Barbarian kingdoms</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_developments">Historical developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbarian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Historical developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Roman_collared_slaves_-_Ashmolean_Museum.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Roman_collared_slaves_-_Ashmolean_Museum.jpg/220px-Roman_collared_slaves_-_Ashmolean_Museum.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Roman_collared_slaves_-_Ashmolean_Museum.jpg/330px-Roman_collared_slaves_-_Ashmolean_Museum.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Roman_collared_slaves_-_Ashmolean_Museum.jpg/440px-Roman_collared_slaves_-_Ashmolean_Museum.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1752" data-file-height="1168" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Roman_collared_slaves_-_Ashmolean_Museum.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Slaves in chains, relief found in Smyrna (present day <a href="/wiki/%C4%B0zmir" title="ƒ∞zmir">ƒ∞zmir</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>), 200 AD</div></div></div>
<p>Greek attitudes towards "barbarians" developed in parallel with the growth of <a href="/wiki/Chattel_slavery" class="mw-redirect" title="Chattel slavery">chattel slavery</a> - especially in <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>. Although the enslavement of Greeks for non-payment of <a href="/wiki/Debt" title="Debt">debts</a> continued in most Greek states, Athens banned this practice under <a href="/wiki/Solon" title="Solon">Solon</a> in the early 6th century BC. Under the <a href="/wiki/Athenian_democracy" title="Athenian democracy">Athenian democracy</a> established ca. 508 BC, <a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_antiquity" title="Slavery in antiquity">slavery</a> came into use on a scale never before seen among the Greeks. Massive concentrations of slaves worked under especially brutal conditions in the silver mines at <a href="/wiki/Laurium" title="Laurium">Laureion</a> in south-eastern Attica after the discovery of a major vein of silver-bearing ore there in 483 BC, while the phenomenon of skilled slave craftsmen producing manufactured goods in small factories and workshops became increasingly common.
</p><p>Furthermore, slave-ownership no longer became the preserve of the rich: all but the poorest of Athenian households came to have slaves in order to supplement the work of their free members. The slaves of Athens that had "barbarian" origins were coming especially from lands around the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thrace</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taurica" class="mw-redirect" title="Taurica">Taurica</a> (<a href="/wiki/Crimea" title="Crimea">Crimea</a>), while <a href="/wiki/Lydia" title="Lydia">Lydians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phrygians" title="Phrygians">Phrygians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carians" title="Carians">Carians</a> came from <a href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" class="mw-redirect" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a>. <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Politics_(Aristotle)" title="Politics (Aristotle)">Politics</a></i> 1.2‚Äì7; 3.14) characterises barbarians as slaves by nature.
</p><p>From this period, words like <i>barbarophonos</i>, cited above from Homer, came into use not only for the sound of a foreign language but also for foreigners who spoke Greek improperly. In the Greek language, the word <i><a href="/wiki/Logos" title="Logos">logos</a></i> expressed both the notions of "language" and "reason", so Greek-speakers readily conflated speaking poorly with stupidity.
</p><p>Further changes occurred in the connotations of <i>barbari</i>/<i>barbaroi</i> in <a href="/wiki/Late_Antiquity" class="mw-redirect" title="Late Antiquity">Late Antiquity</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> when <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishops</a> and <a href="/wiki/Catholicos" title="Catholicos"><i>catholikoi</i></a> were appointed to sees connected to cities among the "civilized" <i>gentes barbaricae</i> such as in <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a> or <a href="/wiki/Persia" class="mw-redirect" title="Persia">Persia</a>, whereas bishops were appointed to supervise entire peoples among the less settled.
</p><p>Eventually the term found a hidden meaning through the <a href="/wiki/Folk_etymology" title="Folk etymology">folk etymology</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cassiodorus" title="Cassiodorus">Cassiodorus</a> (c. 485 ‚Äì c. 585). He stated that the word <i>barbarian</i> was "made up of <i>barba</i> (beard) and <i>rus</i> (flat land); for barbarians did not live in cities, making their abodes in the fields like wild animals".<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hellenic_stereotypes">Hellenic stereotypes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbarian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hellenic stereotypes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Head_of_a_barbarian_Athens.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Head_of_a_barbarian_Athens.jpg/220px-Head_of_a_barbarian_Athens.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="282" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Head_of_a_barbarian_Athens.jpg/330px-Head_of_a_barbarian_Athens.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Head_of_a_barbarian_Athens.jpg/440px-Head_of_a_barbarian_Athens.jpg 2x" data-file-width="515" data-file-height="660" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Head_of_a_barbarian_Athens.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Head of a barbarian, Probably a <a href="/wiki/Germanic_Kingship" class="mw-redirect" title="Germanic Kingship">Client King</a>. <a href="/wiki/Acropolis_Museum" title="Acropolis Museum">Acropolis Museum</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>From classical origins the Hellenic stereotype of barbarism evolved: barbarians are like children, unable to speak or reason properly, cowardly, effeminate, luxurious, cruel, unable to control their appetites and desires, politically unable to govern themselves. Writers voiced these stereotypes with much shrillness - <a href="/wiki/Isocrates" title="Isocrates">Isocrates</a> in the 4th century B.C., for example,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> called for a war of conquest against <a href="/wiki/Persian_empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Persian empire">Persia</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Panacea" title="Panacea">panacea</a> for Greek problems.
</p><p>However, the disparaging Hellenic stereotype of barbarians did not totally dominate Hellenic attitudes. <a href="/wiki/Xenophon" title="Xenophon">Xenophon</a> (died 354 B.C.), for example, wrote the <i><a href="/wiki/Cyropaedia" title="Cyropaedia">Cyropaedia</a></i>, a laudatory fictionalised account of <a href="/wiki/Cyrus_the_Great" title="Cyrus the Great">Cyrus the Great</a>, the founder of the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Empire" title="Persian Empire">Persian Empire</a>, effectively a <a href="/wiki/Utopia" title="Utopia">utopian</a> text. In his <i><a href="/wiki/Anabasis_(Xenophon)" title="Anabasis (Xenophon)">Anabasis</a></i>, Xenophon's accounts of the Persians and other non-Greeks who he knew or encountered show few traces of the stereotypes.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Protagoras_(dialogue)" title="Protagoras (dialogue)">Protagoras</a></i>, Prodicus of Ceos calls "barbarian" the <a href="/wiki/Aeolic_Greek" title="Aeolic Greek">Aeolian</a> dialect that <a href="/wiki/Pittacus_of_Mytilene" title="Pittacus of Mytilene">Pittacus of Mytilene</a> spoke.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The renowned <a href="/wiki/Orator" title="Orator">orator</a> <a href="/wiki/Demosthenes" title="Demosthenes">Demosthenes</a> (384‚Äì322 B.C.) made derogatory comments in his speeches, using the word "barbarian".
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible's</a> <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_of_Tarsus" class="mw-redirect" title="Paul of Tarsus">St. Paul</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Tarsus,_Mersin" title="Tarsus, Mersin">Tarsus</a>) - lived about A.D. 5 to about A.D. 67) uses the word <i>barbarian</i> in its Hellenic sense to refer to non-Greeks (<i><a href="/wiki/Epistle_to_the_Romans" title="Epistle to the Romans">Romans</a> 1:14</i>), and he also uses it to characterise one who merely speaks a different language (<i><a href="/wiki/First_Epistle_to_the_Corinthians" title="First Epistle to the Corinthians">1 Corinthians</a> 14:11</i>).
</p><p>About a hundred years after Paul's time, <a href="/wiki/Lucian" title="Lucian">Lucian</a> ‚Äì a native of <a href="/wiki/Samosata" title="Samosata">Samosata</a>, in the former kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Commagene" class="mw-redirect" title="Commagene">Commagene</a>, which had been absorbed by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> and made part of the province of <a href="/wiki/History_of_Syria" title="History of Syria">Syria</a> ‚Äì used the term "barbarian" to describe himself. Because he was a noted satirist, this could have indicated self-deprecating irony. It might also have suggested descent from Samosata's original <a href="/wiki/Semitic_people" title="Semitic people">Semitic</a> population ‚Äì who were likely called "barbarians by later Hellenistic, <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek-speaking</a> settlers", and might have eventually taken up this appellation themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The term retained its standard usage in the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek language</a> throughout the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>; <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Greeks" title="Byzantine Greeks">Byzantine Greeks</a> used it widely until the fall of the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Roman_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Roman Empire">Eastern Roman Empire</a>, (later named the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a>) in the 15th century (1453 with the fall of capital city <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>}.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a> (106-43 BC) described the mountain area of inner <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a> as "a land of barbarians", with these inhabitants also known by the manifestly pejorative term <i>latrones mastrucati</i> ("thieves with a rough garment in wool").
The region, still known as "<a href="/wiki/Barbagia" title="Barbagia">Barbagia</a>" (in <a href="/wiki/Sardinian_language" title="Sardinian language">Sardinian</a> <i>Barb√†gia</i> or <i>Barb√†za</i>), preserves this old "barbarian" designation in its name ‚Äì but it no longer consciously retains "barbarian" associations: the inhabitants of the area themselves use the name naturally and unaffectedly.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Dying_Galatian_statue">The Dying Galatian statue</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbarian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Dying Galatian statue">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dying_Galatian" class="mw-redirect" title="Dying Galatian">Dying Galatian</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dying_gaul.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Dying_gaul.jpg/220px-Dying_gaul.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="179" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Dying_gaul.jpg/330px-Dying_gaul.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Dying_gaul.jpg/440px-Dying_gaul.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1458" data-file-height="1186" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dying_gaul.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <i>Dying Galatian</i>, <a href="/wiki/Capitoline_Museums" title="Capitoline Museums">Capitoline Museums</a>, Rome</div></div></div>
<p>The statue of the <i><a href="/wiki/Dying_Galatian" class="mw-redirect" title="Dying Galatian">Dying Galatian</a></i> provides some insight into the Hellenistic perception of and attitude towards "Barbarians". <a href="/wiki/Attalus_I" title="Attalus I">Attalus I</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pergamon" title="Pergamon">Pergamon</a> (ruled 241-197 BC) commissioned (220s BC) a statue to celebrate his victory (ca 232 BC) over the Celtic <a href="/wiki/Galatia" title="Galatia">Galatians</a> in <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a> (the bronze original is lost, but a <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a> copy was found in the 17th century).<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The statue depicts with remarkable realism a dying Celt warrior with a typically Celtic hairstyle and moustache. He sits on his fallen shield while a sword and other objects lie beside him. He appears to be fighting against death, refusing to accept his fate.
</p><p>The statue serves both as a reminder of the Celts' defeat, thus demonstrating the might of the people who defeated them, and a memorial to their bravery as worthy adversaries. As <a href="/wiki/H._W._Janson" title="H. W. Janson">H. W. Janson</a> comments, the sculpture conveys the message that "they knew how to die, barbarians that they were".<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Utter_barbarism.2C_civilisation.2C_and_the_noble_savage"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Utter_barbarism,_civilisation,_and_the_noble_savage">Utter barbarism, civilisation, and the noble savage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbarian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Utter barbarism, civilisation, and the noble savage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Greeks admired <a href="/wiki/Scythia" title="Scythia">Scythians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galatia" title="Galatia">Galatians</a> as heroic individuals ‚Äì and even (as in the case of <a href="/wiki/Anacharsis" title="Anacharsis">Anacharsis</a>) as philosophers ‚Äì but they regarded their culture as barbaric. The <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Romans</a> indiscriminately characterised the various <a href="/wiki/Germanic_tribes" class="mw-redirect" title="Germanic tribes">Germanic tribes</a>, the settled <a href="/wiki/Gauls" title="Gauls">Gauls</a>, and the raiding <a href="/wiki/Hun" class="mw-redirect" title="Hun">Huns</a> as barbarians,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and subsequent classically oriented historical narratives depicted the migrations associated with the end of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a> as the "<a href="/wiki/Barbarian_invasions" class="mw-redirect" title="Barbarian invasions">barbarian invasions</a>".
</p><p>The Romans adapted the term in order to refer to anything that was non-Roman. The German cultural historian Silvio Vietta points out that the meaning of the word "barbarous" has undergone a semantic change in modern times, after <a href="/wiki/Michel_de_Montaigne" title="Michel de Montaigne">Michel de Montaigne</a> used it to characterize the activities of the Spaniards in the New World ‚Äì supposedly representatives of the "higher" European culture ‚Äì as "barbarous," in a satirical essay published in the year 1580.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>  It was not the supposedly "uncivilized" Indian tribes who were "barbarous", but the conquering Spaniards. Montaigne argued that Europeans noted the barbarism of other cultures but not the crueler and more brutal actions of their own societies, particularly (in his time) during the so-called <a href="/wiki/European_wars_of_religion" title="European wars of religion">religious wars</a>. In Montaigne's view, his own people ‚Äì the Europeans ‚Äì were the real "barbarians". In this way, the <a href="/wiki/Eurocentrism" title="Eurocentrism">Eurocentric</a> argument was turned around and applied to the European invaders. With this shift in meaning, a whole literature arose in Europe that characterized the indigenous Indian peoples as innocent, and the militarily superior Europeans as "barbarous" intruders invading a paradisical world.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_international_historical_contexts">In international historical contexts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbarian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In international historical contexts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Historically, the term <i>barbarian</i> has seen widespread use, in English. Many peoples have dismissed alien cultures and even rival civilizations, because they were unrecognizably strange. For instance, the nomadic <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_nomads" title="Eurasian nomads">steppe peoples</a> north of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Pechenegs" title="Pechenegs">Pechenegs</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kipchaks" title="Kipchaks">Kipchaks</a>, were called barbarians by the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Greeks" title="Byzantine Greeks">Byzantines</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_East_and_North_Africa">Middle East and North Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbarian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Middle East and North Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Purchase_of_Christian_captives_from_the_Barbary_States.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Purchase_of_Christian_captives_from_the_Barbary_States.jpg/220px-Purchase_of_Christian_captives_from_the_Barbary_States.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="160" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Purchase_of_Christian_captives_from_the_Barbary_States.jpg/330px-Purchase_of_Christian_captives_from_the_Barbary_States.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Purchase_of_Christian_captives_from_the_Barbary_States.jpg/440px-Purchase_of_Christian_captives_from_the_Barbary_States.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2237" data-file-height="1623" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Purchase_of_Christian_captives_from_the_Barbary_States.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ransom of Christian slaves held in Barbary, 17th century</div></div></div>
<p>The native <i><a href="/wiki/Berber_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Berber people">Berbers</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> were among the many peoples called "Barbarian" by the early Romans. The term continued to be used by medieval <a href="/wiki/Arab" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab">Arabs</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Berber_(Etymology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Berber (Etymology)">Berber etymology</a>) before being replaced by "<a href="/wiki/Amazigh" class="mw-redirect" title="Amazigh">Amazigh</a>". In English, the term "Berber" continues to be used as an <a href="/wiki/Exonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Exonym">exonym</a>. The geographical term <a href="/wiki/Barbary" class="mw-redirect" title="Barbary">Barbary</a> or <a href="/wiki/Barbary_Coast" title="Barbary Coast">Barbary Coast</a>, and the name of the <a href="/wiki/Barbary_pirates" title="Barbary pirates">Barbary pirates</a> based on that coast (and who were not necessarily Berbers) were also derived from it.
</p><p>The term has also been used to refer to people from <a href="/wiki/Barbary" class="mw-redirect" title="Barbary">Barbary</a>, a region encompassing most of <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>. The name of the region, <i>Barbary,</i> comes from the Arabic word <i>Barbar,</i> possibly from the Latin word <i>barbaricum,</i> meaning "land of the barbarians."
</p><p>Many languages define the "Other" as those who do not speak one's language; Greek <i>barbaroi</i> was paralleled by <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Ajam" title="Ajam">ajam</a></i> "non-Arabic speakers; non-Arabs; (especially) <a href="/wiki/Persian_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Persian people">Persians</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Asia">South Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbarian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: South Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Dasa" title="Dasa">Dasa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mleccha" title="Mleccha">Mleccha</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dalit" title="Dalit">Dalit</a></div>
<p>In the ancient Indian epic <a href="/wiki/Mahabharata" title="Mahabharata">Mahabharata</a>, the Sanskrit word <i>barbara-</i> meant "stammering, wretch, foreigner, sinful people, low and barbarous".<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to Romila Thapar, the Indo-Aryan semi-nomadic people viewed the indigenous people as barbarians when they arrived.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Indo-Aryan used the term <i><a href="/wiki/Mleccha" title="Mleccha">mleccha</a></i> in referring to people "outside the caste system and ritual ambience." <sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="East_Asia">East Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbarian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: East Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbarian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>The term "Barbarian" in traditional Chinese culture had several aspects. For one thing, Chinese has more than one historical "barbarian" <a href="/wiki/Exonym_and_endonym" title="Exonym and endonym">exonym</a>. Several historical <a href="/wiki/Chinese_characters" title="Chinese characters">Chinese characters</a> for non-Chinese peoples were <a href="/wiki/Graphic_pejoratives_in_written_Chinese" title="Graphic pejoratives in written Chinese">graphic pejoratives</a>, the character for the <a href="/wiki/Yao_people" title="Yao people">Yao people</a>, for instance, was changed from <i>yao</i> Áå∫ "jackal" to <i>yao</i> Áë§ "precious jade" in the modern period.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The original <a href="/wiki/Hua%E2%80%93Yi_distinction" title="Hua‚ÄìYi distinction">Hua‚ÄìYi distinction</a> between "Chinese" and "barbarian" was based on culture and power but not on race.
</p><p>Historically, the Chinese used various words for foreign ethnic groups. They include terms like Â§∑ <i>Yi</i>, which is often translated as "barbarians." Despite this conventional translation, there are also other ways of translating <i>Yi</i> into English. Some of the examples include "foreigners,"<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> "ordinary others,"<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> "wild tribes,"<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> "uncivilized tribes,"<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> and so forth.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_terminology">History and terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbarian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: History and terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Chinese historical records mention what may now perhaps be termed "barbarian" peoples for over four millennia, although this considerably predates the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek language</a> origin of the term "barbarian", at least as is known from the thirty-four centuries of written records in the Greek language. The sinologist <a href="/wiki/Herrlee_Glessner_Creel" class="mw-redirect" title="Herrlee Glessner Creel">Herrlee Glessner Creel</a> said, "Throughout Chinese history "the barbarians" have been a constant motif, sometimes minor, sometimes very major indeed. They figure prominently in the Shang oracle inscriptions, and the dynasty that came to an end only in 1912 was, from the Chinese point of view, barbarian."<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Shang_dynasty" title="Shang dynasty">Shang dynasty</a> (1600‚Äì1046 BC) <a href="/wiki/Oracle_bone_script" title="Oracle bone script">oracles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_bronze_inscriptions" title="Chinese bronze inscriptions">bronze inscriptions</a> first recorded specific Chinese <a href="/wiki/Exonyms" class="mw-redirect" title="Exonyms">exonyms</a> for foreigners, often in contexts of warfare or tribute. King <a href="/wiki/Wu_Ding" title="Wu Ding">Wu Ding</a> (r. 1250‚Äì1192 BC), for instance, fought with the  <a href="/wiki/Guifang" title="Guifang">Guifang</a> È¨ºÊñπ, <a href="/wiki/Di_(Wu_Hu)" class="mw-redirect" title="Di (Wu Hu)">Di</a> Ê∞ê, and <a href="/wiki/Qiang_people" title="Qiang people">Qiang</a> Áæå "barbarians."
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Spring_and_Autumn_period" title="Spring and Autumn period">Spring and Autumn period</a> (771‚Äì476 BC), the meanings of four exonyms were expanded. "These included Rong, Yi, Man, and Di‚Äîall general designations referring to the barbarian tribes."<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> These <i><a href="/wiki/Four_Barbarians" title="Four Barbarians">Siyi</a></i> ÂõõÂ§∑ "Four Barbarians", most "probably the names of ethnic groups originally,"<sup id="cite_ref-Creel_1970,_197_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Creel_1970,_197-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> were the Yi or <a href="/wiki/Dongyi" title="Dongyi">Dongyi</a> Êù±Â§∑ "eastern barbarians," Man or <a href="/wiki/Nanman" title="Nanman">Nanman</a> ÂçóË†ª "southern barbarians," Rong or <a href="/wiki/Xirong" title="Xirong">Xirong</a> Ë•øÊàé "western barbarians," and Di or <a href="/wiki/Beidi" title="Beidi">Beidi</a> ÂåóÁãÑ "northern barbarians." The Russian anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Kryukov" title="Mikhail Kryukov">Mikhail Kryukov</a> concluded.
</p>
<blockquote><p> Evidently, the barbarian tribes at first had individual names, but during about the middle of the first millennium B.C., they were classified schematically according to the four cardinal points of the compass. This would, in the final analysis, mean that once again territory had become the primary criterion of the we-group, whereas the consciousness of common origin remained secondary. What continued to be important were the factors of language, the acceptance of certain forms of material culture, the adherence to certain rituals, and, above all, the economy and the way of life. Agriculture was the only appropriate way of life for the <a href="/wiki/Huaxia" title="Huaxia">Hua-Hsia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Raising_an_army.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Raising_an_army.jpg/220px-Raising_an_army.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="132" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Raising_an_army.jpg/330px-Raising_an_army.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Raising_an_army.jpg/440px-Raising_an_army.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1040" data-file-height="624" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Raising_an_army.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A scene of the Chinese campaign against the <a href="/wiki/Miao_people" title="Miao people">Miao</a> in Hunan, 1795</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Chinese_classics" title="Chinese classics">Chinese classics</a> use compounds of these four generic names in localized "barbarian tribes" exonyms such as "west and north" <i>Rongdi</i>, "south and east" <i>Manyi</i>, <i>Nanyibeidi</i> "barbarian tribes in the south and the north," and <i>Manyirongdi</i> "all kinds of barbarians." Creel says the Chinese evidently came to use <i>Rongdi</i> and <i>Manyi</i> "as generalized terms denoting 'non-Chinese,' 'foreigners,' 'barbarians'," and a statement such as "the Rong and Di are wolves" (<i>Zuozhuan</i>, Min 1) is "very much like the assertion that many people in many lands will make today, that 'no foreigner can be trusted'."
</p>
<blockquote><p> The Chinese had at least two reasons for vilifying and depreciating the non-Chinese groups. On the one hand, many of them harassed and pillaged the Chinese, which gave them a genuine grievance. On the other, it is quite clear that the Chinese were increasingly encroaching upon the territory of these peoples, getting the better of them by trickery, and putting many of them under subjection. By vilifying them and depicting them as somewhat less than human, the Chinese could justify their conduct and still any qualms of conscience.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>This word <i>Yi</i> has both specific references, such as to <i>Huaiyi</i> Ê∑ÆÂ§∑ peoples in the <a href="/wiki/Huai_River" title="Huai River">Huai River</a> region, and generalized references to "barbarian; foreigner; non-Chinese." <i><a href="/wiki/Lin_Yutang%27s_Chinese-English_Dictionary_of_Modern_Usage" title="Lin Yutang&#39;s Chinese-English Dictionary of Modern Usage">Lin Yutang's Chinese-English Dictionary of Modern Usage</a></i> translates <i>Yi</i> as "Anc[ient] barbarian tribe on east border, any border or foreign tribe."<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> The sinologist <a href="/wiki/Edwin_G._Pulleyblank" title="Edwin G. Pulleyblank">Edwin G. Pulleyblank</a> says the name <i>Yi</i> "furnished the primary Chinese term for 'barbarian',"  but "Paradoxically the Yi were considered the most civilized of the non-Chinese peoples.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Idealization">Idealization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbarian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Idealization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Some Chinese classics romanticize or idealize barbarians, comparable to the western <a href="/wiki/Noble_savage" title="Noble savage">noble savage</a> construct. For instance, the Confucian <i><a href="/wiki/Analects" title="Analects">Analects</a></i> records:
</p>
<ul><li>The Master said, The [Â§∑ÁãÑ] barbarians of the East and North have retained their princes. They are not in such a state of decay as we in China.</li>
<li>The Master said, The Way makes no progress. I shall get upon a raft and float out to sea.</li>
<li>The Master wanted to settle among the [‰πùÂ§∑] Nine Wild Tribes of the East. Someone said, I am afraid you would find it hard to put up with their lack of refinement. The Master said, Were a true gentleman to settle among them there would soon be no trouble about lack of refinement.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The translator <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Waley" title="Arthur Waley">Arthur Waley</a> noted that, "A certain idealization of the 'noble savage' is to be found fairly often in early Chinese literature", citing the <i><a href="/wiki/Zuo_Zhuan" class="mw-redirect" title="Zuo Zhuan">Zuo Zhuan</a></i> maxim, "When the Emperor no longer functions, learning must be sought among the 'Four Barbarians,' north, west, east, and south."<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> Professor Creel said,
</p>
<blockquote><p>From ancient to modern times the Chinese attitude toward people not Chinese in culture‚Äî"barbarians"‚Äîhas commonly been one of contempt, sometimes tinged with fear&#160;... It must be noted that, while the Chinese have disparaged barbarians, they have been singularly hospitable both to individuals and to groups that have adopted Chinese culture. And at times they seem to have had a certain admiration, perhaps unwilling, for the rude force of these peoples or simpler customs.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>In a somewhat related example, <a href="/wiki/Mencius" title="Mencius">Mencius</a> believed that Confucian practices were universal and timeless, and thus followed by both Hua and Yi, "<a href="/wiki/Shun_(Chinese_leader)" class="mw-redirect" title="Shun (Chinese leader)">Shun</a> was an Eastern barbarian; he was born in Chu Feng, moved to Fu Hsia, and died in Ming T'iao. <a href="/wiki/King_Wen" class="mw-redirect" title="King Wen">King Wen</a> was a Western barbarian; he was born in Ch'i Chou and died in Pi Ying. <h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Pejorative_Chinese_characters">Pejorative Chinese characters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbarian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Pejorative Chinese characters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>

<p>Some <a href="/wiki/Chinese_characters" title="Chinese characters">Chinese characters</a> used to <a href="/wiki/Transcription_into_Chinese_characters" title="Transcription into Chinese characters">transcribe</a> non-Chinese peoples were graphically pejorative <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_slurs" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethnic slurs">ethnic slurs</a>, in which the insult derived not from the Chinese word but from the character used to write it. Chinese <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">orthography</a> (symbols used to write a language) can provide unique opportunities to write ethnic insults <a href="/wiki/Logograph" class="mw-redirect" title="Logograph">logographically</a> that do not exist alphabetically. For the Yao ethnic group, there is a difference between the transcriptions <i>Yao</i> Áå∫ "jackal" and <i>Yao</i> Áë§ "jade" but none between the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_romanization" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese romanization">romanizations</a> <i>Yao</i> and <i>Yau</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>

<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_and_racial_barbarianism">Cultural and racial barbarianism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbarian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Cultural and racial barbarianism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_wall_of_china-mutianyu_3.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Great_wall_of_china-mutianyu_3.JPG/220px-Great_wall_of_china-mutianyu_3.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Great_wall_of_china-mutianyu_3.JPG/330px-Great_wall_of_china-mutianyu_3.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Great_wall_of_china-mutianyu_3.JPG/440px-Great_wall_of_china-mutianyu_3.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_wall_of_china-mutianyu_3.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The purpose of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Wall_of_China" title="Great Wall of China">Great Wall of China</a> was to stop the "barbarians" from crossing the northern border of China.</div></div></div>
<p>According to the archeologist William Meacham, it was only by the time of the late <a href="/wiki/Shang_dynasty" title="Shang dynasty">Shang dynasty</a> that one can speak of "<a href="/wiki/Chinese_people" title="Chinese people">Chinese</a>," "<a href="/wiki/Chinese_culture" title="Chinese culture">Chinese culture</a>," or "Chinese civilization." "There is a sense in which the traditional view of ancient Chinese history is correct (and perhaps it originated ultimately in the first appearance of dynastic civilization): those on the fringes and outside this esoteric event were "barbarians" in that they did not enjoy (or suffer from) the fruit of civilization until they were brought into close contact with it by an imperial expansion of the civilization itself."<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
In a similar vein, Creel explained the significance of Confucian <i><a href="/wiki/Li_(Confucian)" class="mw-redirect" title="Li (Confucian)">li</a></i> "ritual; rites; propriety".
</p>
<blockquote><p>The fundamental criterion of "Chinese-ness," anciently and throughout history, has been cultural. The Chinese have had a particular way of life, a particular complex of usages, sometimes characterized as <i>li</i>. Groups that conformed to this way of life were, generally speaking, considered Chinese. Those that turned away from it were considered to cease to be Chinese.&#160;... It was the process of acculturation, transforming barbarians into Chinese, that created the great bulk of the Chinese people. The barbarians of Western Chou times were, for the most part, future Chinese, or the ancestors of future Chinese. This is a fact of great importance.&#160;... It is significant, however, that we almost never find any references in the early literature to physical differences between Chinese and barbarians. Insofar as we can tell, the distinction was purely cultural.<sup id="cite_ref-Creel_1970,_197_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Creel_1970,_197-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Dik√∂tter says,
</p>
<blockquote><p>Thought in ancient China was oriented towards the world, or <i><a href="/wiki/Tianxia" title="Tianxia">tianxia</a></i>, "all under heaven." The world was perceived as one homogenous unity named "great community" (<i><a href="/wiki/Great_Unity" title="Great Unity">datong</a></i>) The Middle Kingdom [China], dominated by the assumption of its cultural superiority, measured outgroups according to a yardstick by which those who did not follow the "Chinese ways" were considered "barbarians." A Theory of "using the Chinese ways to transform the barbarian" as strongly advocated. It was believed that the barbarian could be culturally assimilated. In the Age of Great Peace, the barbarians would flow in and be transformed: the world would be one.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> </p></blockquote>
<p>According to the Pakistani academic <a href="/wiki/M._Shahid_Alam" title="M. Shahid Alam">M. Shahid Alam</a>, "The centrality of culture, rather than race, in the Chinese world view had an important corollary. Nearly always, this translated into a civilizing mission rooted in the premise that 'the barbarians could be culturally assimilated'"; namely <i>laihua</i> ‰æÜÂåñ "come and be transformed" or <i>Hanhua</i> Êº¢Âåñ "become Chinese; be sinicized."<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Two millennia before the French anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Claude_L%C3%A9vi-Strauss" title="Claude L√©vi-Strauss">Claude L√©vi-Strauss</a> wrote <i><a href="/wiki/The_Raw_and_the_Cooked" title="The Raw and the Cooked">The Raw and the Cooked</a></i>, the Chinese differentiated "raw" and "cooked" categories of barbarian peoples who lived in China. The <i>shufan</i> ÁÜüÁï™ "cooked [food eating] barbarians" are sometimes interpreted as Sinicized, and the <i>shengfan</i> ÁîüÁï™ "raw [food eating] barbarians" as not Sinicized.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
The <i><a href="/wiki/Liji" class="mw-redirect" title="Liji">Liji</a></i> gives this description.
</p>
<blockquote><p>The people of those five regions ‚Äì the Middle states, and the [Rong], [Yi] (and other wild tribes around them) ‚Äì had all their several natures, which they could not be made to alter. The tribes on the east were called [Yi]. They had their hair unbound, and tattooed their bodies. Some of them ate their food without its being cooked with fire. Those on the south were called Man. They tattooed their foreheads, and had their feet turned toward each other. Some of them ate their food without its being cooked with fire. Those on the west were called [Rong]. They had their hair unbound, and wore skins. Some of them did not eat grain-food. Those on the north were called [Di]. They wore skins of animals and birds, and dwelt in caves. Some of them did not eat grain-food.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Dik√∂tter explains the close association between <a href="/wiki/Nature_and_nurture" class="mw-redirect" title="Nature and nurture">nature and nurture</a>. "The <i>shengfan</i>, literally 'raw barbarians', were considered savage and resisting. The <i>shufan</i>, or 'cooked barbarians', were tame and submissive. The consumption of raw food was regarded as an infallible sign of savagery that affected the physiological state of the barbarian."<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some <a href="/wiki/Warring_States_period" title="Warring States period">Warring States period</a> texts record a belief that the respective natures of the Chinese and the barbarian were incompatible. Mencius, for instance, once stated: "I have heard of the Chinese converting barbarians to their ways, but not of their being converted to barbarian ways."<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> Dik√∂tter says, "The nature of the Chinese was regarded as impermeable to the evil influences of the barbarian; no retrogression was possible. Only the barbarian might eventually change by adopting Chinese ways."<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, different thinkers and texts convey different opinions on this issue. The prominent Tang Confucian Han Yu, for example, wrote in his essay <i>Yuan Dao</i> the following: "When Confucius wrote the <i>Chunqiu</i>, he said that if the feudal lords use Yi ritual, then they should be called Yi; If they use Chinese rituals, then they should be called Chinese." Han Yu went on to lament in the same essay that the Chinese of his time might all become Yi because the Tang court wanted to put Yi laws above the teachings of the former kings.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> Therefore, Han Yu's essay shows the possibility that the Chinese can lose their culture and become the uncivilized outsiders, and that the uncivilized outsiders have the potential to become Chinese.
</p><p>After the Song Dynasty, many of China's rulers in the north were of Inner Asia ethnicities, such as the Khitans, Juchens, and Mongols of the Liao, Jin and Yuan Dynasties, the latter ended up ruling over the entire China. Hence, the historian <a href="/wiki/John_King_Fairbank" class="mw-redirect" title="John King Fairbank">John King Fairbank</a> wrote, "the influence on China of the great fact of alien conquest under the Liao-Jin-Yuan dynasties is just beginning to be explored."<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> During the Qing Dynasty, the rulers of China adopted Confucian philosophy and Han Chinese institutions to show that the Manchu rulers had received the Mandate of Heaven to rule China. At the same time, they also tried to retain their own indigenous culture.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> Due to the Manchus' adoption of Han Chinese culture, most Han Chinese (though not all) did accept the Manchus as the legitimate rulers of China. Similarly, according to Fudan University historian Yao Dali, even the supposedly "patriotic" hero Wen Tianxiang of the late Song and early Yuan period did not believe the Mongol rule to be illegitimate. In fact, Wen was willing to live under Mongol rule as long as he was not forced to be a Yuan dynasty official, out of his loyalty to the Song dynasty. Yao explains that Wen chose to die in the end because he was forced to become a Yuan official. So, Wen chose death due to his loyalty to his dynasty, not because he viewed the Yuan court as a non-Chinese, illegitimate regime and therefore refused to live under their rule. Yao also says that many Chinese who were living in the Yuan-Ming transition period also shared Wen's beliefs of identifying with and putting loyalty towards one's dynasty above racial/ethnic differences. Many Han Chinese writers did not celebrate the collapse of the Mongols and the return of the Han Chinese rule in the form of the Ming dynasty government at that time. Many Han Chinese actually chose not to serve in the new Ming court at all due to their loyalty to the Yuan. Some Han Chinese also committed suicide on behalf of the Mongols as a proof of their loyalty.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> We should note that the founder of the Ming Dynasty, Zhu Yuanzhang, also indicated that he was happy to be born in the Yuan period and that the Yuan did legitimately receive the Mandate of Heaven to rule over China.  On a side note, one of his key advisors, Liu Ji, generally supported the idea that while the Chinese and the non-Chinese are different, they are actually equal. Liu was therefore arguing against the idea that the Chinese were and are superior to the "Yi."<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>These things show that many times, pre-modern Chinese did view culture (and sometimes politics) rather than race and ethnicity as the dividing line between the Chinese and the non-Chinese. In many cases, the non-Chinese could and did become the Chinese and vice versa, especially when there was a change in culture.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_reinterpretations">Modern reinterpretations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbarian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Modern reinterpretations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>According to the historian <a href="/wiki/Frank_Dik%C3%B6tter" title="Frank Dik√∂tter">Frank Dik√∂tter</a>, "The delusive myth of a Chinese antiquity that abandoned racial standards in favour of a concept of cultural universalism in which all barbarians could ultimately participate has understandably attracted some modern scholars. </p><p>The politician, historian, and diplomat <a href="/wiki/K._C._Wu" title="K. C. Wu">K. C. It carried the connotation of people ignorant of Chinese culture and, therefore, 'barbarians'."<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Christopher I. Beckwith makes the extraordinary claim that the name "barbarian" should only be used for Greek historical contexts, and is inapplicable for all other "peoples to whom it has been applied either historically or in modern times."<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> Beckwith notes that most specialists in East Asian history, including him, have translated Chinese exonyms as English "<i>barbarian</i>." He believes that after academics read his published explanation of the problems, except for direct quotations of "earlier scholars who use the word, it should no longer be used as a term by any writer."<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The first problem is that, "it is impossible to translate the word <i>barbarian</i> into Chinese because the concept does not exist in Chinese," meaning a single "completely generic" <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">loanword</a> from Greek <i>barbar-</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> "Until the Chinese borrow the word <i>barbarian</i> or one of its relatives, or make up a new word that explicitly includes the same basic ideas, they cannot express the idea of the 'barbarian' in Chinese.".<sup id="cite_ref-Beckwith,_358_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beckwith,_358-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> The usual <a href="/wiki/Standard_Chinese" title="Standard Chinese">Standard Chinese</a> translation of English <i>barbarian</i> is <i>yemanren</i> (<a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant" title="Chinese language text">ÈáéË†ª‰∫∫</span>&#x3b;&#x20;<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans" title="Chinese language text">ÈáéËõÆ‰∫∫</span>&#x3b;&#x20;<a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i>yƒõm√°nr√©n</i>), which Beckwith claims, "actually means 'wild man, savage'. That is very definitely not the same thing as 'barbarian'."<sup id="cite_ref-Beckwith,_358_81-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beckwith,_358-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> Despite this semantic hypothesis, Chinese-English dictionaries regularly translate <i>yemanren</i> as "barbarian" or "barbarians."<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> Beckwith concedes that the early Chinese "apparently disliked foreigners in general and looked down on them as having an inferior culture," and pejoratively wrote some exonyms. However, he purports, "The fact that the Chinese did not <i>like</i> foreigner Y and occasionally picked a transcriptional character with negative meaning (in Chinese) to write the sound of his ethnonym, is irrelevant."<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Beckwith's second problem is with linguists and lexicographers of Chinese. "If one looks up in a Chinese-English dictionary the two dozen or so partly generic words used for various foreign peoples throughout Chinese history, one will find most of them defined in English as, in effect, 'a kind of barbarian'. </p>
<blockquote><p>The word "<i>FƒÅn</i>" was formerly used by the Chinese almost innocently in the sense of 'aborigines' to refer to ethnic groups in South China, and Mao Zedong himself once used it in 1938 in a speech advocating equal rights for the various minority peoples. But that term has now been so systematically purged from the language that it is not to be found (at least in that meaning) even in large dictionaries, and all references to Mao's 1938 speech have excised the offending word and replaced it with a more elaborate locution, "Yao, Yi, and Yu."<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>The Tang Dynasty Chinese also had a derogatory term for foreigners, <i>lu</i> (<a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant" title="Chinese language text">Ëôú</span>&#x3b;&#x20;<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans" title="Chinese language text">Ëôè</span>&#x3b;&#x20;<a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i>l«î</i>) "prisoner, slave, captive". Beckwith says it means something like "those miscreants who should be locked up," therefore, "The word does not even mean 'foreigner' at all, let alone 'barbarian'."<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Christopher I. Beckwith's 2009 "The Barbarians" epilogue provides many references, but overlooks H. G. Creel's 1970 "The Barbarians" chapter. Creel descriptively wrote, "Who, in fact, were the barbarians? The Chinese have no single term for them. But they were all the non-Chinese, just as for the Greeks the barbarians were all the non-Greeks."<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup> Beckwith prescriptively wrote, "The Chinese, however, have still not yet borrowed Greek <i>barbar</i>-. There is also no single native Chinese word for 'foreigner', no matter how pejorative," which meets his strict definition of "barbarian.".<sup id="cite_ref-Beckwith,_358_81-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beckwith,_358-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Barbarian_puppet_drinking_game">Barbarian puppet drinking game</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbarian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Barbarian puppet drinking game">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Tang_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Tang Dynasty">Tang Dynasty</a> houses of pleasure, where drinking games were common, small puppets in the aspect of Westerners, in a ridiculous state of drunkenness, were used in one popular permutation of the drinking game; so, in the form of blue-eyed, pointy nosed, and peak-capped barbarians, these puppets were manipulated in such a way as to occasionally fall down: then, whichever guest to whom the puppet pointed after falling was then obliged by honor to empty his cup of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_alcoholic_beverages" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese alcoholic beverages">Chinese wine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbarian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>When Europeans came to <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, they were called <i>nanban</i><span style="font-weight: normal">&#32;(<span lang="ja">ÂçóËõÆ</span>)</span>, literally <i>Barbarians from the South</i>, because the <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a> ships appeared to sail from the South. The <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a>, who arrived later, were also called either <i>nanban</i> or <i>k≈çm≈ç</i><span style="font-weight: normal">&#32;(<span lang="ja">Á¥ÖÊØõ</span>)</span>, literally meaning "Red Hair."
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-Colombian_Americas">Pre-Colombian Americas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbarian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Pre-Colombian Americas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Mesoamerica the <a href="/wiki/Aztec" class="mw-redirect" title="Aztec">Aztec</a> civilization used the word "<a href="/wiki/Chichimeca" title="Chichimeca">Chichimeca</a>" to denominate a group of nomadic hunter-gatherer tribes that lived on the outskirts of the <a href="/wiki/Aztec_Triple_Alliance" class="mw-redirect" title="Aztec Triple Alliance">Triple Alliance</a>'s Empire, in the north of Modern Mexico, and whom the Aztec people saw as primitive and uncivilized. One of the meanings attributed to the word "Chichimeca" is "dog people".
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Incas" class="mw-redirect" title="Incas">Incas</a> of South America used the term "puruma auca" for all peoples living outside the rule of their empire (see <a href="/wiki/Promaucaes" class="mw-redirect" title="Promaucaes">Promaucaes</a>).
</p><p>The British, and later the white colonial settlers of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, referred to Native Americans as "savages."
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Barbarian_mercenaries">Barbarian mercenaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbarian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Barbarian mercenaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The entry of "barbarians" into <a href="/wiki/Mercenary" title="Mercenary">mercenary</a> service in a metropole repeatedly occurs in history as a standard way in which peripheral peoples from and beyond <a href="/wiki/Frontier" title="Frontier">frontier</a> regions relate to "civilised" imperial powers as part of a (semi-)foreign militarised proletariat.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup>
Examples include:
</p>
<ul><li>nomadic frontier tribes serving in <a href="/wiki/Military_history_of_China_before_1911" title="Military history of China before 1911">pre-modern China</a><sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>mainly <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic</a> soldiery in the <a href="/wiki/Structural_history_of_the_Roman_military" title="Structural history of the Roman military">armies</a> of the declining <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a><sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Viking <a href="/wiki/Varangian_guard" class="mw-redirect" title="Varangian guard">Varangian guards</a> in imperial <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_army" title="Byzantine army">Byzantium</a><sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Turkic mercenaries in the <a href="/wiki/Abbasid_Caliphate" title="Abbasid Caliphate">Abbasid Caliphate</a><sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Widespread use of ethnic mercenary forces in pre-historic <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">Mesoamerica</a><sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cossacks" title="Cossacks">Cossack</a> units in the armies of (for example) <a href="/wiki/Military_of_the_Polish%E2%80%93Lithuanian_Commonwealth" title="Military of the Polish‚ÄìLithuanian Commonwealth">Poland-Lithuania</a> and of <a href="/wiki/Military_history_of_Russia" title="Military history of Russia">pre-Soviet Russia</a><sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gurkha" title="Gurkha">Gurkha</a> <a href="/wiki/Brigade_of_Gurkhas" title="Brigade of Gurkhas">units</a> in the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British</a> and Indian armies<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">&#91;104&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Modern_period">Early Modern period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbarian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Early Modern period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Viking_revival" title="Viking revival">Viking revival</a>, <a href="/wiki/Noble_savage" title="Noble savage">Noble savage</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Philistinism" title="Philistinism">Philistinism</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:IMG_7039_-_Milano_-_Casa_degli_omenoni_-_Sarmata_-_Foto_Giovanni_Dall%27Orto_-_8-Mar-2007_crop.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/IMG_7039_-_Milano_-_Casa_degli_omenoni_-_Sarmata_-_Foto_Giovanni_Dall%27Orto_-_8-Mar-2007_crop.jpg/220px-IMG_7039_-_Milano_-_Casa_degli_omenoni_-_Sarmata_-_Foto_Giovanni_Dall%27Orto_-_8-Mar-2007_crop.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="390" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/IMG_7039_-_Milano_-_Casa_degli_omenoni_-_Sarmata_-_Foto_Giovanni_Dall%27Orto_-_8-Mar-2007_crop.jpg/330px-IMG_7039_-_Milano_-_Casa_degli_omenoni_-_Sarmata_-_Foto_Giovanni_Dall%27Orto_-_8-Mar-2007_crop.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/IMG_7039_-_Milano_-_Casa_degli_omenoni_-_Sarmata_-_Foto_Giovanni_Dall%27Orto_-_8-Mar-2007_crop.jpg/440px-IMG_7039_-_Milano_-_Casa_degli_omenoni_-_Sarmata_-_Foto_Giovanni_Dall%27Orto_-_8-Mar-2007_crop.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1090" data-file-height="1933" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:IMG_7039_-_Milano_-_Casa_degli_omenoni_-_Sarmata_-_Foto_Giovanni_Dall%27Orto_-_8-Mar-2007_crop.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A defeated <a href="/wiki/Sarmatian" class="mw-redirect" title="Sarmatian">Sarmatian</a> barbarian serves as an <a href="/wiki/Atlas_(architecture)" title="Atlas (architecture)">atlas</a> on a 16th-century <a href="/wiki/Villa" title="Villa">villa</a> in <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>. <p>Italians in the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> often called anyone who lived outside of their country a barbarian. As an example, there is the last chapter of <a href="/wiki/The_Prince" title="The Prince">The Prince</a> by <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Machiavelli" title="Niccol√≤ Machiavelli">Niccol√≤ Machiavelli</a>, "Exhortatio ad Capesendam Italiam in Libertatemque a Barbaris Vinsicandam" (in English: Exhortation to take Italy and free her from the barbarians) in which he appeals to <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_de%27_Medici,_Duke_of_Urbino" title="Lorenzo de&#39; Medici, Duke of Urbino">Lorenzo de' Medici, Duke of Urbino</a> to unite Italy and stop the "barbarian invasions" led by other European rulers, such as <a href="/wiki/Charles_VIII_of_France" title="Charles VIII of France">Charles VIII</a> and <a href="/wiki/Louis_XII_of_France" title="Louis XII of France">Louis XII</a>, both of France, and <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">Ferdinand II of Aragon</a>.
</p><p>Spanish sea captain <a href="/wiki/Francisco_de_Cuellar" title="Francisco de Cuellar">Francisco de Cuellar</a>, who sailed with the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Armada" title="Spanish Armada">Spanish Armada</a> in 1588, used the term 'savage' ('salvaje') to describe the <a href="/wiki/Irish_people" title="Irish people">Irish people</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Marxist_use_of_.22Barbarism.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Marxist_use_of_&quot;Barbarism&quot;">Marxist use of "Barbarism"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbarian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Marxist use of &quot;Barbarism&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><p>
In her 1916 anti-war pamphlet <i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.marxists.org/archive/luxemburg/1915/junius/">The Crisis of German Social Democracy</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxist</a> theorist <a href="/wiki/Rosa_Luxemburg" title="Rosa Luxemburg">Rosa Luxemburg</a> writes: </p><blockquote><p>Bourgeois society stands at the crossroads, either transition to Socialism or regression into Barbarism.<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote><p>Luxemburg attributed it to <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Engels" title="Friedrich Engels">Friedrich Engels</a>, though ‚Äì as shown by <a href="/wiki/Michael_L%C3%B6wy" title="Michael L√∂wy">Michael L√∂wy</a> ‚Äì Engels had not used the term "Barbarism" but a less resounding formulation: <i>If the whole of modern society is not to perish, a revolution in the mode of production and distribution must take place.</i> <sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup> The case has been made that Luxemburg had remembered a passage from <i><a href="/wiki/Erfurt_Program" title="Erfurt Program">The Erfurt Program</a></i>, written in 1892 by <a href="/wiki/Karl_Kautsky" title="Karl Kautsky">Karl Kautsky</a>, and mistakenly attributed it to Engels:</p><blockquote><p>As things stand today capitalist civilization cannot continue; we must either move forward into socialism or fall back into barbarism.<sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108">&#91;108&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote><p>Luxemburg went on to explain what she meant by "Regression into Barbarism": "A look around us at this moment [i.e., 1916 Europe] shows what the regression of bourgeois society into Barbarism means. This World War is a regression into Barbarism. The triumph of Imperialism leads to the annihilation of civilization. At first, this happens sporadically for the duration of a modern war, but then when the period of unlimited wars begins it progresses toward its inevitable consequences. Today, we face the choice exactly as Friedrich Engels foresaw it a generation ago: either the triumph of Imperialism and the collapse of all civilization as in ancient Rome, depopulation, desolation, degeneration ‚Äì a great cemetery. Or the victory of Socialism, that means the conscious active struggle of the International Proletariat against Imperialism and its method of war."
</p><p>"Socialism or Barbarism" becomes, and remains, an often quoted and influential concept in <a href="/wiki/Marxist" class="mw-redirect" title="Marxist">Marxist</a> literature. "Barbarism" is variously interpreted as meaning either a technologically advanced but extremely exploitative and oppressive society (e.g. a victory and world domination by <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Fascism" title="Fascism">Fascist</a> allies); a collapse of technological civilization due to Capitalism causing a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear War">Nuclear War</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ecological_disaster" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecological disaster">ecological disaster</a>; or the one form of barbarism bringing on the other.
</p><p>The Internationalist Communist Tendency considers "Socialism or Barbarism" to be a variant of the earlier "<a href="/wiki/Give_me_Liberty,_or_give_me_Death!" class="mw-redirect" title="Give me Liberty, or give me Death!">Liberty or Death</a>", used by revolutionaries of different stripes since the late 18th century <sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109">&#91;109&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Socialisme_ou_Barbarie" title="Socialisme ou Barbarie">Socialisme ou Barbarie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Socialism_or_Barbarism" title="Socialism or Barbarism">Socialism or Barbarism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Socialismo_e_Barbarie" title="Socialismo e Barbarie">Socialismo e Barbarie</a></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_popular_culture">Modern popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbarian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Modern popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Modern popular culture contains such fantasy barbarians as <a href="/wiki/Conan_the_Barbarian" title="Conan the Barbarian">Conan the Barbarian</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">&#91;110&#93;</a></sup> In such fantasy, the negative connotations traditionally associated with "Barbarian" are often inverted. For example, "<a href="/wiki/The_Phoenix_on_the_Sword" title="The Phoenix on the Sword">The Phoenix on the Sword</a>" (1932), the first of <a href="/wiki/Robert_E._Howard" title="Robert E. Howard">Robert E. Howard</a>'s "Conan" series, is set soon after the "Barbarian" protagonist had forcibly seized the turbulent kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Aquilonia_(Conan)" title="Aquilonia (Conan)">Aquilonia</a> from King Numedides, whom he strangled upon his throne. The story is clearly slanted to imply that the kingdom greatly benefited by power passing from a decadent and tyrannical hereditary monarch to a strong and vigorous Barbarian usurper.
</p>

Prejudice toward Greeks on the part of Greeks was not limited to those who lived on the fringes of the Greek world. The Boeotians, inhabitants of central Greece, whose credentials were impeccable, were routinely mocked for their stupidity and gluttony. Ethnicity is a fluid concept even at the best of times. When it suited their purposes, the Greeks also divided themselves into Ionians and Dorians. The distinction was emphasized at the time of the Peloponnesian War, when the Ionian Athenians fought against the Dorian Spartans.
