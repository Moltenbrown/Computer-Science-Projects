<p>A <b>concerto</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/tʃ/: &#39;ch&#39; in &#39;China&#39;">tʃ</span><span title="/ɛər/: &#39;are&#39; in &#39;bare&#39;">ɛər</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span><span title="/oʊ/: &#39;o&#39; in &#39;code&#39;">oʊ</span></span>/</a></span></span>; plural <i>concertos</i>, or <i>concerti</i> from the Italian plural) is a <a href="/wiki/Musical_composition" title="Musical composition">musical composition</a> generally composed of three <a href="/wiki/Movement_(music)" title="Movement (music)">movements</a>, in which, either one <a href="/wiki/Solo_(music)" title="Solo (music)">solo</a> instrument (for instance, a <a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Violin" title="Violin">violin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cello" title="Cello">cello</a> or <a href="/wiki/Flute" title="Flute">flute</a>), or a group of soloists (<a href="/wiki/Concertino_(group)" class="mw-redirect" title="Concertino (group)"><i>concertino</i></a>) is <a href="/wiki/Accompaniment" title="Accompaniment">accompanied</a> by an <a href="/wiki/Orchestra" title="Orchestra">orchestra</a> or <a href="/wiki/Concert_band" title="Concert band">concert band</a>. Its characteristics and definition have changed over time. In the 17th century, sacred works for voices and orchestra were typically called concertos, as reflected by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">J.&#160;S. Bach</a>'s usage of the title "concerto" for many of the works that we know as <a href="/wiki/Cantata" title="Cantata">cantatas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wolf186_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wolf186-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concerto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <i>concerto</i> comes from <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>; its etymology is uncertain, but it seems to originate from the conjunction of two Latin words: <i>conserere</i> (meaning to tie, to join, to weave) and <i>certamen</i> (competition, fight). The idea is that the two parts in a concerto—the soloist and the orchestra or concert band—alternate between episodes of opposition, cooperation, and independence to create a sense of flow.
</p><p>The concerto, as understood in this modern way, arose in the <a href="/wiki/Baroque_music" title="Baroque music">Baroque</a> period, in parallel to the <i><a href="/wiki/Concerto_grosso" title="Concerto grosso">concerto grosso</a></i>, which contrasted a small group of instruments called a <a href="/wiki/Concertino_(group)" class="mw-redirect" title="Concertino (group)"><i>concertino</i></a> with the rest of the orchestra, called the <i><a href="/wiki/Ripieno" title="Ripieno">ripieno</a></i>. The popularity of the <i>concerto grosso</i> declined after the Baroque period, and the genre was not revived until the 20th century. The solo concerto, however, has remained a vital musical force from its inception to this day.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early-Baroque_concerto">Early-Baroque concerto</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concerto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early-Baroque concerto">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "concerto" was initially used to denote works that involved voices and instruments in which the instruments had independent parts—as opposed to the Renaissance common practice in which instruments that accompanied voices only doubled the voice parts.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  Examples of this earlier form of concerto include <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Gabrieli" title="Giovanni Gabrieli">Giovanni Gabrieli</a>'s "In Ecclesiis" or <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Sch%C3%BCtz" title="Heinrich Schütz">Heinrich Schütz</a>'s "Saul, Saul, was verfolgst du mich".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Late-Baroque_concerto">Late-Baroque concerto</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concerto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Late-Baroque concerto">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ripieno_concerto" title="Ripieno concerto">Ripieno concerto</a></div>
<p>The concerto began to take its modern shape in the late-<a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> period, beginning with the <i>concerto grosso</i> form developed by <a href="/wiki/Arcangelo_Corelli" title="Arcangelo Corelli">Arcangelo Corelli</a>. Corelli's concertino group was two violins, a cello and harpsichord.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In J. S. Bach's Fifth <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg_Concertos" title="Brandenburg Concertos">Brandenburg Concerto</a>, for example, the concertino is a flute, a violin, and a harpsichord;<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> although the harpsichord is a featured solo instrument, it also sometimes plays with the <i>ripieno</i>, functioning as a continuo keyboard accompaniment.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Later, the concerto approached its modern form, in which the concertino usually reduces to a single solo instrument playing with (or against) an orchestra. The main composers of concertos of the baroque were <a href="/wiki/Tommaso_Albinoni" class="mw-redirect" title="Tommaso Albinoni">Tommaso Albinoni</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Vivaldi" title="Antonio Vivaldi">Antonio Vivaldi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georg_Philipp_Telemann" title="Georg Philipp Telemann">Georg Philipp Telemann</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">Johann Sebastian Bach</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/George_Frideric_Handel" title="George Frideric Handel">George Frideric Handel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Locatelli" title="Pietro Locatelli">Pietro Locatelli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Tartini" title="Giuseppe Tartini">Giuseppe Tartini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Geminiani" title="Francesco Geminiani">Francesco Geminiani</a> and <a href="/wiki/Johann_Joachim_Quantz" title="Johann Joachim Quantz">Johann Joachim Quantz</a>.
The concerto was intended as a composition typical of the Italian style of the time, and all the composers were studying how to compose in the Italian fashion (<i>all'Italiana</i>).
</p><p>The Baroque concerto was mainly for a string instrument (<a href="/wiki/Violin" title="Violin">violin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Viola" title="Viola">viola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cello" title="Cello">cello</a>, seldom <a href="/wiki/Viola_d%27amore" title="Viola d&#39;amore">viola d'amore</a> or <a href="/wiki/Harp" title="Harp">harp</a>) or a wind instrument (<a href="/wiki/Flute" title="Flute">flute</a>, <a href="/wiki/Recorder_(musical_instrument)" title="Recorder (musical instrument)">recorder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oboe" title="Oboe">oboe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bassoon" title="Bassoon">bassoon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_horn" title="Natural horn">horn</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Trumpet" title="Trumpet">trumpet</a>,). Bach also wrote a concerto for two violins and orchestra.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> During the Baroque period, before the invention of the piano, keyboard concertos were comparatively rare, with the exception of the <a href="/wiki/Pipe_organ" title="Pipe organ">organ</a> and some <a href="/wiki/Harpsichord_concerto" title="Harpsichord concerto">harpsichord concertos</a> by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">Johann Sebastian Bach</a>. As the harpsichord evolved into the <a href="/wiki/Fortepiano" title="Fortepiano">fortepiano</a>, and in the end to the modern <a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a>, the increased volume and the richer sound of the new instrument allowed the keyboard instrument to better compete with a full orchestra.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Cello concertos have been written since the Baroque era, if not earlier. Among the works from that period, those by <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Vivaldi" title="Antonio Vivaldi">Antonio Vivaldi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Tartini" title="Giuseppe Tartini">Giuseppe Tartini</a> are still part of the standard <a href="/wiki/Musical_repertoire" title="Musical repertoire">repertoire</a> today.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_concerto">Classical concerto</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concerto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Classical concerto">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Classical_concerto_sonata_form.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Classical_concerto_sonata_form.png/400px-Classical_concerto_sonata_form.png" decoding="async" width="400" height="84" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Classical_concerto_sonata_form.png/600px-Classical_concerto_sonata_form.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Classical_concerto_sonata_form.png/800px-Classical_concerto_sonata_form.png 2x" data-file-width="1038" data-file-height="218" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Classical_concerto_sonata_form.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sonata form in the Classical Concerto.<sup id="cite_ref-White_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> See: <a href="/wiki/Trill_(music)" title="Trill (music)">trill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cadenza" title="Cadenza">cadenza</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Coda_(music)" title="Coda (music)">coda</a>. For <a href="/wiki/Exposition_(music)" title="Exposition (music)">exposition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Musical_development" title="Musical development">development</a> and <a href="/wiki/Recapitulation_(music)" title="Recapitulation (music)">recapitulation</a>, see <a href="/wiki/Sonata_form" title="Sonata form">sonata form</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The concertos of the sons of <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">Johann Sebastian Bach</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Carl_Philipp_Emanuel_Bach" title="Carl Philipp Emanuel Bach">C. P. E. Bach</a>, are perhaps the best links between those of the Baroque period and those of the Classical era.
</p><p>It is conventional to state that the first movements of concertos from the Classical period onwards follow the structure of <a href="/wiki/Sonata_form" title="Sonata form">sonata form</a>. Final movements are often in <a href="/wiki/Rondo" title="Rondo">rondo</a> form, as in J.S. Bach's <a href="/wiki/Violin_Concerto_in_E_major_(Bach)" title="Violin Concerto in E major (Bach)">E Major Violin Concerto</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-White_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Violin_concertos">Violin concertos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concerto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Violin concertos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mozart" class="mw-redirect" title="Mozart">Mozart</a> wrote five violin concertos, all in 1775. They show a number of influences, notably Italian and <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austrian</a>. Several passages have leanings towards <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk music</a>, as manifested in Austrian <a href="/wiki/Serenade" title="Serenade">serenades</a>.
Mozart also wrote the highly regarded <a href="/wiki/Sinfonia_Concertante_for_Violin,_Viola_and_Orchestra_(Mozart)" title="Sinfonia Concertante for Violin, Viola and Orchestra (Mozart)">Sinfonia Concertante</a> for violin, viola, and orchestra.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Beethoven</a> wrote only one violin concerto, under-appreciated until revealed as a masterpiece in a performance by violin virtuoso <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Joachim" title="Joseph Joachim">Joseph Joachim</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cello_concertos">Cello concertos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concerto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cello concertos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Haydn wrote at least two cello concertos (for cello, oboes, horns, and strings), which are the most important works in that genre of the classical era. However, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Philipp_Emanuel_Bach" title="Carl Philipp Emanuel Bach">C. P. E. Bach's</a> three cello concertos and <a href="/wiki/Luigi_Boccherini" title="Luigi Boccherini">Boccherini's</a> twelve concertos are also noteworthy.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Keyboard_concertos">Keyboard concertos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concerto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Keyboard concertos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>C.P.E. Bach's keyboard concertos contain some virtuosic solo writing. Some of them have movements that run into one another without a break, and there are frequent cross-movement thematic references.
</p><p>Mozart, as a child, made arrangements for keyboard and orchestra of four sonatas by now little-known composers. Then he arranged three sonata movements by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Christian_Bach" title="Johann Christian Bach">Johann Christian Bach</a>. By the time he was twenty, Mozart was able to write concerto ritornelli that gave the orchestra admirable opportunity for asserting its character in an exposition with some five or six sharply contrasted themes, before the soloist enters to elaborate on the material. Of <a href="/wiki/Mozart_piano_concertos" class="mw-redirect" title="Mozart piano concertos">his 27 piano concertos</a>, the last 22 are highly appreciated.
</p><p>A dozen cataloged keyboard concertos are attributed to Haydn, of which only three or four are considered genuine.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Concertos_for_other_instruments">Concertos for other instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concerto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Concertos for other instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>C. P. E. Bach wrote four flute concertos and two oboe concertos.
</p><p>Bohemian composer <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Rosetti" title="Antonio Rosetti">Francesco Antonio Rosetti</a> composed several solo and double horn concertos. He was a significant contributor to the genre of horn concertos in the 18th century. Most of his outstanding horn concertos were composed between 1782 and 1789 for the Bohemian duo Franz Zwierzina and Joseph Nage while at the Bavarian court of Oettingen-Wallerstein. One of his best-known works in this genre is his Horn Concerto in E flat major C49/K III:36. It consists of three movements: 1. Allegro moderato 2. Romance 3. Rondo.
</p><p>Many common features of the <a href="/wiki/Galant_style" title="Galant style">galant style</a> are present in Rosetti's music and composing style. In his E-flat horn concerto, we hear periodic and short phrases, galant harmonic rhythm and melodic line reduction.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (November 2017)">original research?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Rosetti's influence on the 18th century composers, musicians and music was considerable. At the Bavarian court of Oettingen-Wallerstein, his music was often performed by the Wallerstein ensembles. In Paris, his compositions were performed by the best ensembles of the city, including the orchestra of the Concert Spirituel. His publishers were Le Menu et Boyer and Sieber. According to <a href="/wiki/H._C._Robbins_Landon" title="H. C. Robbins Landon">H. C. Robbins Landon</a> (Mozart scholar),<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A complete citation is needed (November 2016)">full citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Rosetti's horn concertos might have been a model for Mozart's horn concertos.
</p><p>Mozart wrote one concerto each for flute, <a href="/wiki/Oboe_Concerto_(Mozart)" title="Oboe Concerto (Mozart)">oboe</a> (later rearranged for flute and known as Flute Concerto No. 2), <a href="/wiki/Clarinet_Concerto_(Mozart)" title="Clarinet Concerto (Mozart)">clarinet</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bassoon_Concerto_(Mozart)" title="Bassoon Concerto (Mozart)">bassoon</a>, four for <a href="/wiki/Natural_horn" title="Natural horn">horn</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Category:Articles_contradicting_other_articles" title="Category:Articles contradicting other articles"><span title="This text contradicts material elsewhere on this page. (November 2017)">contradictory</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> a <a href="/wiki/Concerto_for_Flute,_Harp,_and_Orchestra_(Mozart)" title="Concerto for Flute, Harp, and Orchestra (Mozart)">Concerto for Flute, Harp, and Orchestra</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Exsultate,_jubilate" title="Exsultate, jubilate">Exsultate, jubilate</a></i>, a <i>de facto</i> concerto for soprano voice.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> They all exploit and explore the characteristics of the solo instrument(s).
</p><p>Haydn wrote an important <a href="/wiki/Trumpet_concerto_(Haydn)" class="mw-redirect" title="Trumpet concerto (Haydn)">trumpet concerto</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Sinfonia_Concertante_(Haydn)" title="Sinfonia Concertante (Haydn)"><i>Sinfonia Concertante</i> for violin, cello, oboe and bassoon</a> as well as two horn concertos.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Romantic_concerto">Romantic concerto</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concerto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Romantic concerto">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Violin_concertos_2">Violin concertos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concerto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Violin concertos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Violin_concerto" title="Violin concerto">Violin concerto</a></div>
<p>In the 19th century the concerto as a vehicle for <a href="/wiki/Virtuoso" title="Virtuoso">virtuosic</a> display flourished as never before. It was an age in which the artist was seen as hero, to be worshipped with rapture. Early Romantic traits can be found in the violin concertos of <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Viotti" title="Giovanni Battista Viotti">Viotti</a>, but it is <a href="/wiki/Louis_Spohr" title="Louis Spohr">Spohr's</a> twelve violin concertos, written between 1802 and 1827, that truly embrace the Romantic spirit with their melodic as well as their dramatic qualities.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cello_concertos_2">Cello concertos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concerto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Cello concertos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cello_concerto" title="Cello concerto">Cello concerto</a></div>
<p>Since the Romantic era, the cello has received as much attention as the piano and violin as a concerto instrument, and many great Romantic and even more 20th-century composers left examples.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Anton%C3%ADn_Dvo%C5%99%C3%A1k" title="Antonín Dvořák">Antonín Dvořák</a>'s cello concerto ranks among the supreme examples from the Romantic era while <a href="/wiki/Robert_Schumann" title="Robert Schumann">Robert Schumann</a>'s focuses on the lyrical qualities of the instrument. The instrument was also popular with composers of the Franco-Belgian tradition: <a href="/wiki/Camille_Saint-Sa%C3%ABns" title="Camille Saint-Saëns">Saint-Saëns</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henri_Vieuxtemps" title="Henri Vieuxtemps">Vieuxtemps</a> wrote two cello concertos each and <a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Lalo" title="Édouard Lalo">Lalo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Jongen" title="Joseph Jongen">Jongen</a> one. <a href="/wiki/Elgar" class="mw-redirect" title="Elgar">Elgar</a>'s popular concerto, while written in the early 20th century, belongs to the late romantic period stylistically.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Beethoven</a> contributed to the repertoire with a <i><a href="/wiki/Triple_Concerto_(Beethoven)" title="Triple Concerto (Beethoven)"> Triple Concerto</a></i> for piano, violin, cello and orchestra while later in the century, <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Brahms" title="Johannes Brahms">Brahms</a> wrote a <i><a href="/wiki/Double_Concerto_(Brahms)" title="Double Concerto (Brahms)"> Double Concerto</a></i> for violin, cello and orchestra.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Pyotr_Ilyich_Tchaikovsky" title="Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky">Tchaikovsky</a>'s contribution to the genre is a series of <a href="/wiki/Variations_on_a_Rococo_Theme" title="Variations on a Rococo Theme">Variations on a Rococo Theme</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> He also left very fragmentary sketches of a projected Cello Concerto. Cellist <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yuriy_Leonovich&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Yuriy Leonovich (page does not exist)">Yuriy Leonovich</a> and Tchaikovsky researcher <a href="/w/index.php?title=Brett_Langston&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Brett Langston (page does not exist)">Brett Langston</a> published <a href="/wiki/Cello_Concerto_(Tchaikovsky/Leonovich)" title="Cello Concerto (Tchaikovsky/Leonovich)">their completion</a> of the piece in 2006.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Carl_Reinecke" title="Carl Reinecke">Carl Reinecke</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Popper" title="David Popper">David Popper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Julius_Klengel" title="Julius Klengel">Julius Klengel</a> also wrote cello concertos that were popular in their time and are still played occasionally nowadays.
</p><p>Today's 'core' repertoire—performed the most of any cello concertos—are by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Elgar" title="Edward Elgar">Elgar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anton%C3%ADn_Dvo%C5%99%C3%A1k" title="Antonín Dvořák">Dvořák</a>, Saint-Saëns, Haydn, <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Shostakovich" title="Dmitri Shostakovich">Shostakovich</a> and Schumann, but many more concertos are performed nearly as often (see below: cello concertos in the 20th century).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Piano_concertos">Piano concertos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concerto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Piano concertos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Piano_concerto" title="Piano concerto">Piano concerto</a></div>
<p>Beethoven's five piano concertos increase the technical demands made on the soloist. The last two are particularly remarkable, integrating the concerto into a large symphonic structure with movements that frequently run into one another. His <a href="/wiki/Piano_Concerto_No._4_(Beethoven)" title="Piano Concerto No. 4 (Beethoven)">Piano Concerto No. 4</a> starts, against tradition, with a statement by the piano, after which the orchestra enters in a foreign key, to present what would normally have been the opening <a href="/wiki/Tutti" title="Tutti">tutti</a>. The work has an essentially lyrical character. The slow movement is a dramatic dialogue between the soloist and the orchestra. His <a href="/wiki/Piano_Concerto_No._5_(Beethoven)" title="Piano Concerto No. 5 (Beethoven)">Piano Concerto No. 5</a> has the basic rhythm of a Viennese military <a href="/wiki/March_(music)" title="March (music)">march</a>. There is no lyrical second subject, but in its place a continuous development of the opening material.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (November 2017)">original research?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The piano concertos of <a href="/wiki/Johann_Baptist_Cramer" title="Johann Baptist Cramer">Cramer</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Field_(composer)" title="John Field (composer)">Field</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jan_Ladislav_Dussek" title="Jan Ladislav Dussek">Düssek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_W%C3%B6lfl" title="Joseph Wölfl">Woelfl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Ries" title="Ferdinand Ries">Ries</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Johann_Nepomuk_Hummel" title="Johann Nepomuk Hummel">Hummel</a> provide a link from the Classical concerto to the Romantic concerto.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Chopin" title="Frédéric Chopin">Chopin</a> wrote two piano concertos in which the orchestra is very much relegated to an accompanying role. Schumann, despite being a pianist-composer, wrote a piano concerto in which virtuosity is never allowed to eclipse the essential lyrical quality of the work. The gentle, expressive melody heard at the beginning on woodwind and horns (after the piano's heralding introductory chords) bears the material for most of the argument in the first movement. In fact, argument in the traditional developmental sense is replaced by a kind of variation technique in which soloist and orchestra interweave their ideas.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (November 2017)">original research?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Franz_Liszt" title="Franz Liszt">Liszt</a>'s mastery of piano technique matched that of <a href="/wiki/Paganini" class="mw-redirect" title="Paganini">Paganini</a> for the violin. His concertos <a href="/wiki/Piano_Concerto_No._1_(Liszt)" title="Piano Concerto No. 1 (Liszt)">No. 1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Piano_Concerto_No._2_(Liszt)" title="Piano Concerto No. 2 (Liszt)">No. 2</a> left a deep impression on the style of piano concerto writing, influencing <a href="/wiki/Rubinstein" title="Rubinstein">Rubinstein</a>, and especially <a href="/wiki/Pyotr_Ilyich_Tchaikovsky" title="Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky">Tchaikovsky</a>, whose <a href="/wiki/Piano_Concerto_No._1_(Tchaikovsky)" title="Piano Concerto No. 1 (Tchaikovsky)">First Piano Concerto's</a> rich chordal opening is justly famous.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Edvard_Grieg" title="Edvard Grieg">Grieg's</a> concerto likewise begins in a striking manner after which it continues in a lyrical vein.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Johannes_Brahms" title="Johannes Brahms">Brahms</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Piano_Concerto_No._1_(Brahms)" title="Piano Concerto No. 1 (Brahms)">First Piano Concerto</a> in D minor (pub 1861) was the result of an immense amount of work on a mass of material originally intended for a symphony. His <a href="/wiki/Piano_Concerto_No._2_(Brahms)" title="Piano Concerto No. 2 (Brahms)">Second Piano Concerto</a> in B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span> major (1881) has four movements and is written on a larger scale than any earlier concerto. Like his violin concerto, it is symphonic in proportions.
</p><p>Fewer piano concertos were written in the late Romantic Period.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> But <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Rachmaninoff" title="Sergei Rachmaninoff">Sergei Rachmaninoff</a> wrote four piano concertos between 1891 and 1926. His <a href="/wiki/Piano_Concerto_No._2_(Rachmaninoff)" title="Piano Concerto No. 2 (Rachmaninoff)">Second</a> and <a href="/wiki/Piano_Concerto_No._3_(Rachmaninoff)" title="Piano Concerto No. 3 (Rachmaninoff)">Third</a>, being the most popular of the four, went on to become among the most famous in the piano repertoire.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Other romantic piano concertos, like those by <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Kalkbrenner" title="Friedrich Kalkbrenner">Kalkbrenner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henri_Herz" title="Henri Herz">Henri Herz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ignaz_Moscheles" title="Ignaz Moscheles">Moscheles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sigismond_Thalberg" title="Sigismond Thalberg">Thalberg</a> were also very popular in the Romantic era, but not today.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="20th-century_concerto">20th-century concerto</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concerto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 20th-century concerto">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many of the concertos written in the early 20th century belong more to the late Romantic school than to any modernistic movement.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (April 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Masterpieces were written by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Elgar" title="Edward Elgar">Edward Elgar</a> (a violin concerto and a cello concerto), <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Rachmaninoff" title="Sergei Rachmaninoff">Sergei Rachmaninoff</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Medtner" title="Nikolai Medtner">Nikolai Medtner</a> (four and three piano concertos, respectively), <a href="/wiki/Jean_Sibelius" title="Jean Sibelius">Jean Sibelius</a> (a violin concerto), <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Delius" title="Frederick Delius">Frederick Delius</a> (a violin concerto, a <a href="/wiki/Cello_Concerto_(Delius)" title="Cello Concerto (Delius)">cello concerto</a>, a piano concerto and a <a href="/wiki/Double_concerto_for_violin_and_cello" class="mw-redirect" title="Double concerto for violin and cello">double concerto for violin and cello</a>), <a href="/wiki/Karol_Szymanowski" title="Karol Szymanowski">Karol Szymanowski</a> (two violin concertos and a "Symphonie Concertante" for piano), and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Strauss" title="Richard Strauss">Richard Strauss</a> (two horn concertos, a violin concerto, <i>Don Quixote</i>—a tone poem that features the cello as a soloist—and among later works, an <a href="/wiki/Oboe_concerto" title="Oboe concerto">oboe concerto</a>).
</p><p>However, in the first decades of the 20th century, several composers such as <a href="/wiki/Debussy" class="mw-redirect" title="Debussy">Debussy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Schoenberg" title="Arnold Schoenberg">Schoenberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alban_Berg" title="Alban Berg">Berg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hindemith" class="mw-redirect" title="Hindemith">Hindemith</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stravinsky" class="mw-redirect" title="Stravinsky">Stravinsky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prokofiev" class="mw-redirect" title="Prokofiev">Prokofiev</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bart%C3%B3k" class="mw-redirect" title="Bartók">Bartók</a> started experimenting with ideas that were to have far-reaching consequences for the way music is written and, in some cases, performed. Some of these innovations include a more frequent use of <a href="/wiki/Musical_mode" class="mw-redirect" title="Musical mode">modality</a>, the exploration of non-western <a href="/wiki/Musical_scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Musical scale">scales</a>, the development of <a href="/wiki/Atonality" title="Atonality">atonality</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neotonality" title="Neotonality">neotonality</a>, the wider acceptance of <a href="/wiki/Consonance_and_dissonance" title="Consonance and dissonance">dissonances</a>, the invention of the <a href="/wiki/Twelve-tone_technique" title="Twelve-tone technique">twelve-tone technique</a> of composition and the use of <a href="/wiki/Polyrhythm" title="Polyrhythm">polyrhythms</a> and complex <a href="/wiki/Time_signature" title="Time signature">time signatures</a>.
</p><p>These changes also affected the concerto as a musical form. Beside more or less radical effects on musical language, they led to a redefinition of the concept of virtuosity that included new and extended instrumental techniques and a focus on previously neglected aspects of sound such as <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(music)" title="Pitch (music)">pitch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Timbre" title="Timbre">timbre</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dynamics_(music)" title="Dynamics (music)">dynamics</a>. In some cases, they also brought about a new approach to the role of soloists and their relation to the orchestra.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Violin_concertos_3">Violin concertos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concerto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Violin concertos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Two great innovators of early 20th-century music, <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Schoenberg" title="Arnold Schoenberg">Schoenberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Igor_Stravinsky" title="Igor Stravinsky">Stravinsky</a>, both wrote violin concertos. The material in Schoenberg's concerto, like that in <a href="/wiki/Alban_Berg" title="Alban Berg">Berg's</a>, is linked by the <a href="/wiki/Twelve-tone_technique" title="Twelve-tone technique">twelve-tone serial</a> method. <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9la_Bart%C3%B3k" title="Béla Bartók">Bartók</a>, another major 20th-century composer, wrote two important concertos for violin. Russian composers <a href="/wiki/Prokofiev" class="mw-redirect" title="Prokofiev">Prokofiev</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Shostakovich" title="Dmitri Shostakovich">Shostakovich</a> each wrote two concertos while <a href="/wiki/Khachaturian" title="Khachaturian">Khachaturian</a> wrote a concerto and a Concerto-Rhapsody for the instrument. <a href="/wiki/Paul_Hindemith" title="Paul Hindemith">Hindemith</a>'s concertos hark back to the forms of the 19th century, even if the harmonic language he used was different.
</p><p>Three violin concertos from <a href="/wiki/David_Diamond_(composer)" title="David Diamond (composer)">David Diamond</a> show the form in neoclassical style.
</p><p>In 1950 <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Ch%C3%A1vez" title="Carlos Chávez">Carlos Chávez</a> completed a substantial <a href="/wiki/Violin_Concerto_(Ch%C3%A1vez)" title="Violin Concerto (Chávez)">Violin Concerto</a> with an enormous central cadenza for the unaccompanied violin.
</p><p>More recently, <a href="/wiki/Dutilleux" class="mw-redirect" title="Dutilleux">Dutilleux</a>'s <i>L'Arbre des songes</i> has proved an important addition to the repertoire and a fine example of the composer's atonal yet melodic style.
</p><p>Other composers of major violin concertos include <a href="/wiki/John_Adams_(composer)" title="John Adams (composer)">John Adams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Barber" title="Samuel Barber">Samuel Barber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Britten" title="Benjamin Britten">Benjamin Britten</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Maxwell_Davies" title="Peter Maxwell Davies">Peter Maxwell Davies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miguel_del_Aguila" class="mw-redirect" title="Miguel del Aguila">Miguel del Aguila</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philip_Glass" title="Philip Glass">Philip Glass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crist%C3%B3bal_Halffter" title="Cristóbal Halffter">Cristóbal Halffter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gy%C3%B6rgy_Ligeti" title="György Ligeti">György Ligeti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Martin_(composer)" title="Frank Martin (composer)">Frank Martin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bohuslav_Martin%C5%AF" title="Bohuslav Martinů">Bohuslav Martinů</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Nielsen" title="Carl Nielsen">Carl Nielsen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Piston" title="Walter Piston">Walter Piston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Schnittke" title="Alfred Schnittke">Alfred Schnittke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean_Sibelius" title="Jean Sibelius">Jean Sibelius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Vaughan_Williams" title="Ralph Vaughan Williams">Ralph Vaughan Williams</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Walton" title="William Walton">William Walton</a>,
<a href="/wiki/John_Williams" title="John Williams">John Williams</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roger_Sessions" title="Roger Sessions">Roger Sessions</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cello_concertos_3">Cello concertos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concerto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Cello concertos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 20th century, particularly after the Second World War, the cello enjoyed an unprecedented popularity. As a result, its concertante repertoire caught up with those of the piano and the violin both in terms of quantity and quality.
</p><p>An important factor in this phenomenon was the rise of virtuoso cellist <a href="/wiki/Mstislav_Rostropovich" title="Mstislav Rostropovich">Mstislav Rostropovich</a>. His outstanding technique and passionate playing prompted dozens of composers to write pieces for him, first in his native Soviet Union and then abroad. Among such compositions may be listed <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Prokofiev" title="Sergei Prokofiev">Sergei Prokofiev</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Symphony-Concerto_(Prokofiev)" title="Symphony-Concerto (Prokofiev)">Symphony-Concerto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Shostakovich" title="Dmitri Shostakovich">Dmitri Shostakovich</a>'s two cello concertos, <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Britten" title="Benjamin Britten">Benjamin Britten</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Cello_Symphony_(Britten)" title="Cello Symphony (Britten)">Cello-Symphony</a> (which emphasizes, as its title suggests, the equal importance of soloist and orchestra), <a href="/wiki/Henri_Dutilleux" title="Henri Dutilleux">Henri Dutilleux</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Tout_un_monde_lointain..." title="Tout un monde lointain...">Tout un monde lointain...</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Crist%C3%B3bal_Halffter" title="Cristóbal Halffter">Cristóbal Halffter</a>'s two cello concertos, <a href="/wiki/Witold_Lutos%C5%82awski" title="Witold Lutosławski">Witold Lutosławski</a>'s cello concerto, <a href="/wiki/Dmitry_Kabalevsky" title="Dmitry Kabalevsky">Dmitry Kabalevsky</a>'s two cello concertos, <a href="/wiki/Aram_Khachaturian" title="Aram Khachaturian">Aram Khachaturian</a>'s <i>Concerto-Rhapsody</i>, <a href="/wiki/Arvo_P%C3%A4rt" title="Arvo Pärt">Arvo Pärt</a>'s <i>Pro et Contra</i>, <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Schnittke" title="Alfred Schnittke">Alfred Schnittke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Jolivet" title="André Jolivet">André Jolivet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Krzysztof_Penderecki" title="Krzysztof Penderecki">Krzysztof Penderecki</a> second cello concertos, <a href="/wiki/Sofia_Gubaidulina" title="Sofia Gubaidulina">Sofia Gubaidulina</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Canticle_of_the_Sun_(Gubaidulina)" title="The Canticle of the Sun (Gubaidulina)">Canticles of the Sun</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Luciano_Berio" title="Luciano Berio">Luciano Berio</a>'s <i>Ritorno degli Snovidenia</i>, <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Bernstein" title="Leonard Bernstein">Leonard Bernstein</a>'s <i>Three Meditations</i>, <a href="/wiki/James_MacMillan" title="James MacMillan">James MacMillan</a>'s cello concerto and <a href="/wiki/Olivier_Messiaen" title="Olivier Messiaen">Olivier Messiaen</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Concert_%C3%A0_quatre" title="Concert à quatre">Concert à quatre</a></i> (a quadruple concerto for cello, piano, oboe, flute and orchestra).
</p><p>In addition, several important composers who were not directly influenced by Rostropovich wrote cello concertos: <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Barber" title="Samuel Barber">Samuel Barber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elliott_Carter" title="Elliott Carter">Elliott Carter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Ch%C3%A1vez" title="Carlos Chávez">Carlos Chávez</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miguel_del_Aguila" class="mw-redirect" title="Miguel del Aguila">Miguel del Aguila</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Glazunov" title="Alexander Glazunov">Alexander Glazunov</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hans_Werner_Henze" title="Hans Werner Henze">Hans Werner Henze</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Hindemith" title="Paul Hindemith">Paul Hindemith</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Honegger" title="Arthur Honegger">Arthur Honegger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erich_Wolfgang_Korngold" title="Erich Wolfgang Korngold">Erich Wolfgang Korngold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gy%C3%B6rgy_Ligeti" title="György Ligeti">György Ligeti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bohuslav_Martin%C5%AF" title="Bohuslav Martinů">Bohuslav Martinů</a>, <a href="/wiki/Darius_Milhaud" title="Darius Milhaud">Darius Milhaud</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Myaskovsky" title="Nikolai Myaskovsky">Nikolai Myaskovsky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Einojuhani_Rautavaara" title="Einojuhani Rautavaara">Einojuhani Rautavaara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joaqu%C3%ADn_Rodrigo" title="Joaquín Rodrigo">Joaquín Rodrigo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toru_Takemitsu" title="Toru Takemitsu">Toru Takemitsu</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Walton" title="William Walton">William Walton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heitor_Villa-Lobos" title="Heitor Villa-Lobos">Heitor Villa-Lobos</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bernd_Alois_Zimmermann" title="Bernd Alois Zimmermann">Bernd Alois Zimmermann</a> for instance.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Piano_concertos_2">Piano concertos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concerto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Piano concertos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Igor_Stravinsky" title="Igor Stravinsky">Igor Stravinsky</a> wrote three works for solo piano and orchestra: <a href="/wiki/Concerto_for_Piano_and_Wind_Instruments" class="mw-redirect" title="Concerto for Piano and Wind Instruments">Concerto for Piano and Wind Instruments</a>, <a href="/wiki/Capriccio_for_Piano_and_Orchestra" title="Capriccio for Piano and Orchestra">Capriccio for Piano and Orchestra</a>, and Movements for Piano and Orchestra. <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Prokofiev" title="Sergei Prokofiev">Sergei Prokofiev</a>, another Russian composer, wrote five piano concertos, which he himself performed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Shostakovich" title="Dmitri Shostakovich">Dmitri Shostakovich</a> composed two. Fellow Soviet composer <a href="/wiki/Aram_Khachaturian" title="Aram Khachaturian">Aram Khachaturian</a> contributed to the repertoire with a <a href="/wiki/Piano_Concerto_(Khachaturian)" title="Piano Concerto (Khachaturian)">piano concerto</a> and a Concerto-Rhapsody.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Arnold_Schoenberg" title="Arnold Schoenberg">Arnold Schoenberg</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Piano_Concerto_(Schoenberg)" title="Piano Concerto (Schoenberg)">Piano Concerto</a> is a well-known example of a <a href="/wiki/Dodecaphonic" class="mw-redirect" title="Dodecaphonic">dodecaphonic</a> piano concerto.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9la_Bart%C3%B3k" title="Béla Bartók">Béla Bartók</a> also wrote three piano concertos. Like their violin counterparts, they show the various stages in his musical development. Bartok's also rearranged his chamber piece, <a href="/wiki/Sonata_for_Two_Pianos_and_Percussion" title="Sonata for Two Pianos and Percussion">Sonata for Two Pianos and Percussion</a>, into a <i>Concerto for Two Pianos and Percussion</i>, adding orchestral accompaniment.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Crist%C3%B3bal_Halffter" title="Cristóbal Halffter">Cristóbal Halffter</a> wrote a prize-winning neoclassical Piano Concerto in 1953, and a second Piano Concerto in 1987–88.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ralph_Vaughan_Williams" title="Ralph Vaughan Williams">Ralph Vaughan Williams</a> wrote a concerto for piano, though it was later reworked as a concerto for two pianos and orchestra—both versions have been recorded—while <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Britten" title="Benjamin Britten">Benjamin Britten</a>'s concerto for piano (1938) is a prominent work from his early period.
</p><p>Important piano concertos by Latin-American composers included one by <a href="/wiki/Piano_Concerto_(Ch%C3%A1vez)" title="Piano Concerto (Chávez)">Carlos Chávez</a>, two by <a href="/wiki/Alberto_Ginastera" title="Alberto Ginastera">Alberto Ginastera</a>, and five by <a href="/wiki/Heitor_Villa-Lobos" title="Heitor Villa-Lobos">Heitor Villa-Lobos</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Gy%C3%B6rgy_Ligeti" title="György Ligeti">György Ligeti</a>'s concerto (1988) has a synthetic quality: it mixes complex rhythms, the composer's Hungarian roots and his experiments with micropolyphony from the 1960s and 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Witold_Lutos%C5%82awski" title="Witold Lutosławski">Witold Lutosławski</a>'s piano concerto, completed in the same year, alternates between playfulness and mystery. It also displays a partial return to melody after the composer's aleatoric period.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Russian composer <a href="/wiki/Rodion_Shchedrin" title="Rodion Shchedrin">Rodion Shchedrin</a> has written six piano concertos. Finnish composer <a href="/wiki/Einojuhani_Rautavaara" title="Einojuhani Rautavaara">Einojuhani Rautavaara</a> wrote three piano concertos, the third one dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Ashkenazy" title="Vladimir Ashkenazy">Vladimir Ashkenazy</a>, who played and conducted the world première.
</p><p>French composer <a href="/wiki/Germaine_Tailleferre" title="Germaine Tailleferre">Germaine Tailleferre</a> and Czech composers <a href="/wiki/Bohuslav_Martin%C5%AF" title="Bohuslav Martinů">Bohuslav Martinů</a> and <a href="/wiki/V%C3%ADt%C4%9Bzslava_Kapr%C3%A1lov%C3%A1" title="Vítězslava Kaprálová">Vítězslava Kaprálová</a> wrote piano concertos.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Concertos_for_other_instruments_2">Concertos for other instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concerto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Concertos for other instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 20th century also witnessed a growth of the concertante repertoire of instruments, some of which had seldom or never been used in this capacity, and even a concerto for wordless coloratura soprano by <a href="/wiki/Reinhold_Gli%C3%A8re" title="Reinhold Glière">Reinhold Glière</a>. As a result, almost all classical instruments now have a concertante repertoire. Examples include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Accordion_concerto" title="Accordion concerto">Accordion concerto</a>: <a href="/wiki/Alan_Hovhaness" title="Alan Hovhaness">Hovhaness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sofia_Gubaidulina" title="Sofia Gubaidulina">Sofia Gubaidulina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toshio_Hosokawa" title="Toshio Hosokawa">Toshio Hosokawa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kalevi_Aho" title="Kalevi Aho">Kalevi Aho</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alto_saxophone" title="Alto saxophone">Alto saxophone</a> Concerto: <a href="/wiki/John_Adams_(composer)" title="John Adams (composer)">Adams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Creston" title="Paul Creston">Creston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ingolf_Dahl" title="Ingolf Dahl">Dahl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edison_Denisov" title="Edison Denisov">Denisov</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Max_Dubois" class="mw-redirect" title="Pierre Max Dubois">Dubois</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Glazunov" title="Alexander Glazunov">Glazunov</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karel_Husa" title="Karel Husa">Husa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Ibert" title="Jacques Ibert">Ibert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erland_von_Koch" title="Erland von Koch">Koch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lars-Erik_Larsson" title="Lars-Erik Larsson">Larsson</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Maslanka" title="David Maslanka">Maslanka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Muczynski" title="Robert Muczynski">Muczynski</a>, <a href="/wiki/Esa-Pekka_Salonen" title="Esa-Pekka Salonen">Salonen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Ticheli" title="Frank Ticheli">Ticheli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henri_Tomasi" title="Henri Tomasi">Tomasi</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Williams" title="John Williams">J. Williams</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Worley" title="John Worley">Worley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Takashi_Yoshimatsu" title="Takashi Yoshimatsu">Yoshimatsu</a></li>

<p>Among the works of the prolific composer <a href="/wiki/Alan_Hovhaness" title="Alan Hovhaness">Alan Hovhaness</a> may be noted <i>Prayer of St. Gregory</i> for trumpet and strings, though it is not a concerto in the usual sense of the term.
</p><p>In the later 20th century the concerto tradition was continued by composers such as <a href="/wiki/Peter_Maxwell_Davies" title="Peter Maxwell Davies">Maxwell Davies</a>, whose series of <a href="/wiki/Strathclyde_Concertos" title="Strathclyde Concertos">Strathclyde Concertos</a> exploit some of the instruments less familiar as soloists.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concertos_for_orchestra_or_concert_band">Concertos for orchestra or concert band</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concerto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Concertos for orchestra or concert band">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Concerto_for_Orchestra" title="Concerto for Orchestra">Concerto for Orchestra</a></div>
<p>In the 20th and 21st centuries, several composers wrote concertos for <a href="/wiki/Orchestra" title="Orchestra">orchestra</a> or <a href="/wiki/Concert_band" title="Concert band">concert band</a>. In these works, different sections and/or instruments of the orchestra or concert band are treated at one point or another as soloists with emphasis on solo sections and/or instruments changing during the piece. Some examples include those written by:
</p><p><b>Orchestra</b>:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Bart%C3%B3k" class="mw-redirect" title="Bartók">Bartók</a> – <a href="/wiki/Concerto_for_Orchestra_(Bart%C3%B3k)" title="Concerto for Orchestra (Bartók)">Concerto for Orchestra</a> – 1945</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Elliott_Carter" title="Elliott Carter">Carter</a> – 1969</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hindemith" class="mw-redirect" title="Hindemith">Hindemith</a> – Op. 38, 1925</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Knussen" class="mw-redirect" title="Knussen">Knussen</a> – 1969</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kod%C3%A1ly" class="mw-redirect" title="Kodály">Kodály</a> – 1940</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Magnus_Lindberg_(Finnish_composer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnus Lindberg (Finnish composer)">Lindberg</a> – 2003</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lutoslawski" class="mw-redirect" title="Lutoslawski">Lutoslawski</a> – <a href="/wiki/Concerto_for_Orchestra_(Lutos%C5%82awski)" title="Concerto for Orchestra (Lutosławski)">Concerto for Orchestra</a> – 1954</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rodion_Shchedrin" title="Rodion Shchedrin">Shchedrin</a> – No. 1 <i>Naughty Limericks</i> (1963), No. 2 <i>The Chimes</i> (1968), No. 3 <i>Old Russian Circus Music</i> (1989), No. 4 <i>Round Dances (Khorovody)</i> (1989), No. 5 <i>Four Russian Songs</i> (1998)</li></ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Henri_Dutilleux" title="Henri Dutilleux">Dutilleux</a> has also described his <i>Métaboles</i> as a concerto for orchestra.
</p><p><b>Concert band:</b>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Steven_Bryant_(composer)" title="Steven Bryant (composer)">Bryant</a> – 2007–2010</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lukas_Foss" title="Lukas Foss">Foss</a> – 2002</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Karel_Husa" title="Karel Husa">Husa</a> – 1982</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gordon_Jacob" title="Gordon Jacob">Jacob</a> – 1974</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Robert_E._Jager" title="Robert E. Jager">Jager</a> – 1982</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concertos_for_two_or_more_instruments">Concertos for two or more instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concerto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Concertos for two or more instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many composers also wrote concertos for two or more soloists.
</p><p>In the Baroque era:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Antonio_Vivaldi" title="Antonio Vivaldi">Vivaldi</a>'s concertos for 2, 3 or 4 violins, for 2 cellos, for 2 mandolins, for 2 trumpets, for 2 flutes, for oboe and bassoon, for cello and bassoon... etc..  Some of Vivaldi's concertos were written for a very large number of soloists, including the extraordinary <a href="/wiki/Ryom_Verzeichnis" class="mw-redirect" title="Ryom Verzeichnis">RV</a>555, which features 3 violins, an oboe, 2 recorders, 2 viole all'inglese, a <a href="/wiki/Chalumeau" title="Chalumeau">chalumeau</a>, 2 cellos, 2 harpsichords and 2 trumpets.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bach" class="mw-redirect" title="Bach">Bach</a>'s concertos for 2 violins, for 2, 3, or 4 harpsichords as well as several of his <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg_concertos" class="mw-redirect" title="Brandenburg concertos">Brandenburg concertos</a>.</li></ul>
<p>In the Classical era:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Joseph_Haydn" title="Joseph Haydn">Haydn</a>'s concerto for violin and keyboard (usually referred to as the Keyboard Concerto No. 6) and <a href="/wiki/Sinfonia_Concertante_(Haydn)" title="Sinfonia Concertante (Haydn)">Sinfonia concertante</a> for violin, cello, oboe and bassoon.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Mozart</a>'s concertos for two pianos and three pianos, the <a href="/wiki/Sinfonia_Concertante_for_Violin,_Viola_and_Orchestra_(Mozart)" title="Sinfonia Concertante for Violin, Viola and Orchestra (Mozart)">Sinfonia concertante</a> for violin and viola, and his concerto for flute and harp.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Antonio_Salieri" title="Antonio Salieri">Salieri</a>'s <i>Triple Concerto</i> for oboe, violin and cello, and his double concerto for flute and oboe.</li></ul>
<p>In the Romantic era:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Beethoven</a>'s triple concerto for piano, violin, and cello.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Johannes_Brahms" title="Johannes Brahms">Brahms</a>'s double concerto for violin and cello.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Max_Bruch" title="Max Bruch">Bruch</a>'s double concerto for viola and clarinet and one for 2 pianos.</li></ul>
<p>In the 20th century:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Arnold" title="Malcolm Arnold">Malcolm Arnold</a>'s concerto for piano duet and strings, as well as his <a href="/wiki/Concerto_for_Two_Violins_and_String_Orchestra_(Arnold)" title="Concerto for Two Violins and String Orchestra (Arnold)">concerto for two violins and string orchestra</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9la_Bart%C3%B3k" title="Béla Bartók">Béla Bartók</a>'s concerto for two pianos and percussion</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Samuel_Barber" title="Samuel Barber">Samuel Barber</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Capricorn_Concerto" title="Capricorn Concerto">Capricorn Concerto</a></i> for flute, oboe and trumpet.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Britten" title="Benjamin Britten">Benjamin Britten</a>'s double concerto for violin and viola.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Elliott_Carter" title="Elliott Carter">Elliott Carter</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Double_Concerto_(Carter)" title="Double Concerto (Carter)">Double Concerto for Harpsichord and Piano with Two Chamber Orchestras</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Peter_Maxwell_Davies" title="Peter Maxwell Davies">Peter Maxwell Davies</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Strathclyde_Concertos" title="Strathclyde Concertos">Strathclyde Concerto</a> No. 3 for horn, trumpet and orchestra, No. 4 for violin, viola and string orchestra and No. 9 for piccolo, alto flute, cor anglais, E-flat clarinet, bass clarinet, contrabassoon and string orchestra.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Frederick_Delius" title="Frederick Delius">Frederick Delius</a>'s double concerto for violin and cello.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Flagello" title="Nicolas Flagello">Nicolas Flagello</a>'s <i>Concerto Sinfonico</i> for saxophone quartet and orchestra.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jean_Fran%C3%A7aix" title="Jean Françaix">Jean Françaix</a>'s concerto for two pianos and another for two harps, as well as his <i>Divertissement</i> for string trio and orchestra, his <i>Quadruple Concerto</i> for flute, oboe, clarinet, bassoon and orchestra, his <i>Double Concerto</i> for flute and clarinet, and his <i>Concerto for 15 Soloists and Orchestra</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Philip_Glass" title="Philip Glass">Philip Glass</a>'s concerto for <a href="/wiki/Saxophone_quartet" title="Saxophone quartet">saxophone quartet</a> and orchestra.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Crist%C3%B3bal_Halffter" title="Cristóbal Halffter">Cristóbal Halffter</a>'s <i>Concierto a cuatro</i> for saxophone quartet and orchestra.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hans_Werner_Henze" title="Hans Werner Henze">Hans Werner Henze</a>'s double concerto for oboe and harp.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Paul_Hindemith" title="Paul Hindemith">Paul Hindemith</a>'s concerto for flute, oboe, clarinet, bassoon, harp, and orchestra as well as his concerto for trumpet, bassoon, and strings.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gustav_Holst" title="Gustav Holst">Gustav Holst</a>'s <i>Fugal Concerto</i> for flute, oboe and string orchestra.</li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Gy%C3%B6rgy_Kurt%C3%A1g" title="György Kurtág">György Kurtág</a>'s double concerto for piano and cello.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lowell_Liebermann" title="Lowell Liebermann">Lowell Liebermann</a>'s concerto for flute and harp</li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Jon_Lord" title="Jon Lord">Jon Lord</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Concerto_for_Group_and_Orchestra" title="Concerto for Group and Orchestra">Concerto for Group and Orchestra</a></i> for <a href="/wiki/Rock_band" class="mw-redirect" title="Rock band">rock band</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Witold_Lutos%C5%82awski" title="Witold Lutosławski">Witold Lutosławski</a>'s concerto for oboe and harp.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mikl%C3%B3s_Maros" title="Miklós Maros">Miklós Maros</a>'s <i>Concerto Grosso</i> for <a href="/wiki/Saxophone_quartet" title="Saxophone quartet">saxophone quartet</a> and orchestra.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Frank_Martin_(composer)" title="Frank Martin (composer)">Frank Martin</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Petite_Symphonie_Concertante" class="mw-redirect" title="Petite Symphonie Concertante">Petite Symphonie Concertante</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Concerto_for_seven_wind_instruments,_timpani,_percussion,_and_string_orchestra" title="Concerto for seven wind instruments, timpani, percussion, and string orchestra">Concerto for seven wind instruments, timpani, percussion, and string orchestra</a>.</li>
