<p><b>Reynolds</b> is a surname in the English language. Among the earliest recorded use of the surname is from the early 14th century; <a href="/wiki/Walter_Reynolds" title="Walter Reynolds">Walter Reynolds</a> of <a href="/wiki/Worcester" title="Worcester">Worcester</a>, England.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="English_Reynolds">English Reynolds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reynolds_(surname)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: English Reynolds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:160px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Reynolds_Family_Crest.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/f/f5/Reynolds_Family_Crest.JPG" decoding="async" width="158" height="250" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="158" data-file-height="250" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Reynolds_Family_Crest.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Reynolds Family Crest</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ReynellArms.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/ReynellArms.PNG/200px-ReynellArms.PNG" decoding="async" width="200" height="229" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/ReynellArms.PNG/300px-ReynellArms.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/ReynellArms.PNG/400px-ReynellArms.PNG 2x" data-file-width="933" data-file-height="1068" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ReynellArms.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Arms of Reynell, adopted at the start of the age of <a href="/wiki/Heraldry" title="Heraldry">heraldry</a> (c. 1200–1215): <i>Argent, masonry sable a chief indented of the second</i><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Joshua_Reynolds_Self_Portrait.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Joshua_Reynolds_Self_Portrait.jpg/220px-Joshua_Reynolds_Self_Portrait.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="281" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7f/Joshua_Reynolds_Self_Portrait.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="313" data-file-height="400" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Joshua_Reynolds_Self_Portrait.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Joshua Reynolds – self-portrait</div></div></div>
<p>Reynolds is a <a href="/wiki/Patronymic_surname" title="Patronymic surname">patronymic surname</a> meaning "son of Reynold", where the given name of the father, "<a href="/wiki/Reynold" title="Reynold">Reynold</a>", or "<a href="/wiki/Reginald_(given_name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Reginald (given name)">Reginald</a>",<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> was a Germanic name composed of <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Germanic/Raginawald%C4%85" class="extiw" title="wikt:Reconstruction:Proto-Germanic/Raginawaldą">*raginą + *waldą</a>, meaning "Powerful Ruler"<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> (possibly an alteration of the Old French name <b>Reinold</b>). The addition of "s" to the father's first name makes Reynolds a simple <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">genitive case</a> patronymic.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Possessors of these names arrived in England with the <a href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman Conquest">Norman Conquest</a> of 1066,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
and early <a href="/wiki/List_of_English_chronicles" title="List of English chronicles">English chronicles</a> indicate a Norman origin, with the name appearing in England from about 1066. Early records of the name mention <a href="/wiki/Willemus_filius_Raunaldi" class="mw-redirect" title="Willemus filius Raunaldi">Willemus filius Raunaldi</a> who was listed in the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a> of 1086, in which "Rainald-us" is a common Christian name.<sup id="cite_ref-SurnamesOf_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SurnamesOf-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The alternative <a href="/wiki/Saxon" class="mw-redirect" title="Saxon">Saxon</a> origin is less commonly cited (in this etymology, the name is constructed from the Saxon words <i>Rhein</i>, pure, and <i>hold</i>, love).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The name <b>Reynolds</b> appeared in many references, but spellings included <a href="/wiki/Reynell_(surname)" title="Reynell (surname)">Reynell</a>, <b>Reynalds</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-SurnamesOf_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SurnamesOf-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  <b>Renals</b>, <b>Rennels</b> and many more. Scribes recorded and <a href="/wiki/Phonemic_orthography#Loan_words" title="Phonemic orthography">spelled the name as it sounded</a>. Hence, a person would appear to be born with one spelling, married with another, and buried with still another.
</p><p>Reynolds emerged as a notable family name in the county of <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a> where Sir <a href="/wiki/Richard_Reynell_(knight)" title="Richard Reynell (knight)">Richard Reynell</a> (died pre-1213) was seated at <a href="/wiki/Pitney" title="Pitney">Pitney</a> in Somerset and was given custody of the Castles at <a href="/wiki/Exeter" title="Exeter">Exeter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Launceston,_Cornwall" title="Launceston, Cornwall">Launceston</a> by <a href="/wiki/King_Richard_I" class="mw-redirect" title="King Richard I">King Richard I</a> when he went to the Holy Land in 1191. In 1198 Godfrey, Robert and Torketil <b>Renoldus</b> were registered in <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a>.
</p><p>Meanwhile, many of the junior branches had joined the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Pembroke" title="Earl of Pembroke">Earl of Pembroke</a>'s Irish invasion, where they became the Earls of <a href="/wiki/Cavan" title="Cavan">Cavan</a>, of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lisburne&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lisburne (page does not exist)">Lisburne</a>, of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mountmorris&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mountmorris (page does not exist)">Mountmorris</a>. In England the main line was forfeited but Sir Richard's son recovered the lands. This distinguished west country family also branched to other locations in England, mostly under the name Reynolds; for example, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Reynolds" title="Walter Reynolds">Walter Reynolds</a> (died 1327) was the son of a Windsor baker, who became the favourite of <a href="/wiki/King_Edward_II" class="mw-redirect" title="King Edward II">King Edward II</a> and <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Archbishop of Canterbury</a> (1313–1327).
</p><p>During the 12th century many of these Norman families moved north to Scotland in the train of the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Huntingdon" title="Earl of Huntingdon">Earl of Huntingdon</a>, later to become <a href="/wiki/David_I_of_Scotland" title="David I of Scotland">King David of Scotland</a>.
</p><p>In Ireland, settlers became known as the "Adventurers for land." They "undertook" to keep the Protestant faith, and were granted lands previously held by the Irish. This family migrated to Ireland during two periods, first with the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Normans" title="Anglo-Normans">Anglo-Norman</a> invasion of Ireland, and later, in the 17th century giving rise to the <b>Reynells</b> of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Reynell_castle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Reynell castle (page does not exist)">Reynell castle</a>. In some cases it is difficult to distinguish early migrants from native Irish Reynolds who derive their name from <a href="/w/index.php?title=McRannell&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="McRannell (page does not exist)">McRannell</a>, formerly spelled "Mac Ragnaill".
</p><p>Among colonisers of North America bearing the family name Reynolds were Henry, Samuel, Thomas Reynold who joined the colonisation of the Barbados in 1688; <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Reynolds_(politician)" title="Christopher Reynolds (politician)">Christopher Reynolds</a> settled in Virginia in 1622; Nathaniel Reynold settled in Salem in 1630; Robert Reynolds settled in Salem in 1630 with Mary and his four children; Cornelius, Edward, George, Hannah, James, Mary, Nicholas, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Reynolds" title="Richard Reynolds">Richard Reynolds</a> also settled in Virginia. During the American War of Independence some declared their loyalty to the Crown and moved into Canada as <a href="/wiki/United_Empire_Loyalists" class="mw-redirect" title="United Empire Loyalists">United Empire Loyalists</a>.
</p><p>More recently, notable bearers of the Reynolds surname include: <a href="/wiki/Sir_Joshua_Reynolds" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Joshua Reynolds">Sir Joshua Reynolds</a> (1723–1792), painter; <a href="/wiki/John_Reynolds_(US_politician)" class="mw-redirect" title="John Reynolds (US politician)">John Reynolds (US politician)</a> (1788–1865), American politician, Governor of Illinois; <a href="/wiki/James_Clark_McReynolds" title="James Clark McReynolds">James Clark McReynolds</a> (1862–1946), US Attorney General (1913–14) and Associate Justice of the US Supreme Court (1914–41); <a href="/wiki/Albert_Reynolds" title="Albert Reynolds">Albert Reynolds</a> (b. 1935), Irish politician, Prime Minister of the Republic of Ireland (1992–95); <a href="/wiki/Debbie_Reynolds" title="Debbie Reynolds">Debbie Reynolds</a> (1932-2016), American actress, dancer and singer; <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Augustine_Reynolds" title="Christopher Augustine Reynolds">Christopher Reynolds</a>, Australian Archbishop of Adelaide (1873–1893); as well as <a href="/wiki/Burt_Reynolds" title="Burt Reynolds">Burt Reynolds</a> (1936-2018), American actor.
Rebecca Reynolds moved to New York then to Atlanta from Sydney Australia.
The most ancient grant<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (July 2016)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> of a Coat of Arms found was a silver shield with a portcullis and three blue bars; A fox was the <a href="/wiki/Canting_arms" title="Canting arms">canting</a> crest (French: <i>renard</i> = fox).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mottos">Mottos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reynolds_(surname)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mottos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The ancient family Motto for this distinguished name was:
<i>Jus meum tuebor</i> ("I will defend my right"<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>)
</p><p>Or, "<i>Favente Deo</i> ("With God favouring").<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Irish_Reynolds">Irish Reynolds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reynolds_(surname)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Irish Reynolds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Ireland, the <i>Reynolds</i> surname originates from <a href="/wiki/Muintir_Eolais" title="Muintir Eolais">Muintir Eolais</a>, the primary <a href="/wiki/Conmaicne" class="mw-redirect" title="Conmaicne">Conmaicne</a> sept of south <a href="/wiki/County_Leitrim" title="County Leitrim">County Leitrim</a>. 
</p><p>Throughout Ireland's rich history, the Reynolds family name was a prominent one, and even today County Leitrim is the principal stronghold of the name, nearly half the people in Ireland so called hailing from that area.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish language</a>, the surname is rendered <i><a href="/wiki/Mac_Raghnaill" title="Mac Raghnaill">Mac Raghnaill</a></i>, and the name is ultimately derived from the Old Norse <i><a href="/wiki/Rognvald" title="Rognvald">Rognvald</a></i> a Latin borrowing of the two words regal and valor. It was also a surname of <a href="/wiki/Huguenots" title="Huguenots">Irish Huguenots</a> who came to Ireland from France to evade religious persecution in the 1600s; The original French surnames being either Renaud or Renault; Or a combination of both, respectively.
</p><p>Like many Irish families, the Reynolds began emigrating from Ireland in two fronts: early in America's history, as they settled in the northeast prior to the American Revolution; and later, in the 19th century, during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Hunger" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Hunger">Great Irish Hunger</a>, when millions of Irish Catholics came to North America. The first wave of Irish immigrants were mostly Anglo-Irish <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> converts from <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, which differentiates them distinctly from the second wave of Irish potato famine refugees, who were evidently <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> and from <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Mainland Ireland</a>.
</p><p>There is also a branch of the family which traces its origin to Phillipe D'Reynald, a templar knight who was required to return from the Holy Land and take up the legacy of his deceased brother William. However, this branch is more difficult to locate but is believed to have moved through Normandy and Somerset and on to Ireland.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Portuguese_Reynolds">Portuguese Reynolds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reynolds_(surname)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Portuguese Reynolds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Reynolds_bras%C3%A3o.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Reynolds_bras%C3%A3o.jpg/150px-Reynolds_bras%C3%A3o.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="136" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Reynolds_bras%C3%A3o.jpg/225px-Reynolds_bras%C3%A3o.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1b/Reynolds_bras%C3%A3o.jpg 2x" data-file-width="255" data-file-height="232" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Reynolds_bras%C3%A3o.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Reynolds Family Crest (Portugal)</div></div></div>
<p>Originally from Maidstone, Kent, England, the first Reynolds that related to Portugal, Thomas Johnson William Reynolds, born in 1786, was a naval officer but withdrawn, settled later in Chatham, also in Kent, as an importer of fruit, wine corks and virgin <a href="/wiki/Cork_(material)" title="Cork (material)">cork</a> from Spain and <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, until, because of a liver disease, on the advice of a doctor, that Reynolds began a sea voyage that brought him to <a href="/wiki/Porto" title="Porto">Porto</a>, where he saw a good opportunity to expand his business. With him came his sons, Thomas, William and Robert Hunter Reynolds, born respectively in 1811 and 1820. His daughter had already been born in Port Elizabeth in 1828.
</p><p>Portugal proved to be a wealth of opportunities for these Reynolds. His son Robert, was the person that brought him to the <a href="/wiki/Alentejo" title="Alentejo">Alentejo</a> region, where he began his purchase of cork bark still on the tree, paying in advance, sometimes several years, with risk but with superb profits. Thus came the Alentejo and setting properties in <a href="/wiki/Estremoz" title="Estremoz">Estremoz</a>, accompanied by a nephew, son of Thomas, born in 1842, named William Reynolds.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
