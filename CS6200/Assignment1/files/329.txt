<p>A <b>military junta</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;h&#39; in &#39;hi&#39;">h</span><span title="/ʊ/: &#39;u&#39; in &#39;push&#39;">ʊ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span></span>,<span class="wrap"> </span><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/dʒ/: &#39;j&#39; in &#39;jam&#39;">dʒ</span><span title="/ʌ/: &#39;u&#39; in &#39;cut&#39;">ʌ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span></span>-/</a></span></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a> led by a committee of <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a> leaders. The term <i><a href="/wiki/Junta_(governing_body)" title="Junta (governing body)">junta</a></i> means "meeting" or "committee" and originated in the <a href="/wiki/Junta_(Peninsular_War)" title="Junta (Peninsular War)">national and local junta organized by the Spanish resistance</a> to <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_War" title="Peninsular War">Napoleon's invasion of Spain</a> in 1808.<sup id="cite_ref-EB_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The term is now used to refer to an <a href="/wiki/Authoritarian" class="mw-redirect" title="Authoritarian">authoritarian</a> <a href="/wiki/Form_of_government" class="mw-redirect" title="Form of government">form of government</a> characterized by <a href="/wiki/Oligarchic" class="mw-redirect" title="Oligarchic">oligarchic</a> <a href="/wiki/Military_dictatorship" title="Military dictatorship">military dictatorship</a>, as distinguished from other categories of authoritarian rule, specifically <a href="/wiki/Strongman_(politics)" title="Strongman (politics)">strongman</a> (autocratic military dictatorships); machine (<a href="/wiki/Oligarchic" class="mw-redirect" title="Oligarchic">oligarchic</a> party dictatorships); and bossism (autocratic party dictatorships).<sup id="cite_ref-LaiSlater_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LaiSlater-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>A junta often comes to power as a result of a <a href="/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="Coup d&#39;état">coup d'état</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EB_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The junta may either formally take power as the nation's governing body, with the power to <a href="/wiki/Rule_by_decree" title="Rule by decree">rule by decree</a>, or may exercise power by exercising binding (but informal) control over a nominally civilian government.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooker2009_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooker2009-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> These two forms of junta rule are sometimes called <i>open rule</i> and <i>disguised rule</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooker2014_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooker2014-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Disguised rule may take the form of either <i>civilianization</i> or <i>indirect rule</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooker2014_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooker2014-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Civilization occurs when a junta publicly ends its obviously military features, but continues its dominance.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooker2014_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooker2014-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> For example, the junta may terminate <a href="/wiki/Martial_law" title="Martial law">martial law</a>, forgo military uniforms in favor of civilian attire, "colonize" government with former military officers, and make use of <a href="/wiki/Political_parties" class="mw-redirect" title="Political parties">political parties</a> or mass organizations.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooker200953_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooker200953-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> "Indirect rule" involves the junta's exertion of concealed, behind-the-scenes control over a civilian <a href="/wiki/Puppet_government" class="mw-redirect" title="Puppet government">puppet</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooker2014_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooker2014-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Indirect rule by the military can include either broad control over the government or control over a narrower set of policy areas, such as military or <a href="/wiki/National_security" title="National security">national security</a> matters.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooker2014_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooker2014-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Since the 1920s, military juntas have been frequently seen in <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a>, typically in the form of an "institutionalized, highly corporate/professional junta" headed by the commanding officers of the different <a href="/wiki/Military_branch" title="Military branch">military branches</a> (army, navy, and <a href="/wiki/Air_force" title="Air force">air force</a>), and sometimes joined by the head of the national <a href="/wiki/Police" title="Police">police</a> or other key bodies.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooker2009_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooker2009-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Political scientist <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Finer" title="Samuel Finer">Samuel Finer</a>, writing in 1988, noted that juntas in Latin America tended to be smaller than juntas elsewhere; the median junta had 11 members, while Latin American juntas typically had three or four.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooker2009_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooker2009-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  "Corporate" military coups have been distinguished from "factional" military coups. The former are carried out by the armed forces as an institution, led by senior commanders at the top of the military hierarchy, while the latter are carried out by a segment of the armed forces and are often led by mid-ranking officers.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooker2009_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooker2009-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Well-known_military_juntas">Well-known military juntas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_junta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Well-known military juntas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Argentina — <a href="/wiki/National_Reorganization_Process" title="National Reorganization Process">National Reorganization Process</a> (1976–1983)</li>
<li>Bolivia — <a href="/wiki/Bolivian_military_juntas_of_1970-1982_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Bolivian military juntas of 1970-1982 (disambiguation)">Bolivian military juntas</a> (1970–1971 and 1980–1982)</li>
<li>Brazil — <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Military_Junta_of_1930" title="Brazilian Military Junta of 1930">Military Junta of 1930</a></li>
<li>Brazil — <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Military_Junta_of_1969" title="Brazilian Military Junta of 1969">Military junta of 1969</a></li>
<li>Chile — <a href="/wiki/Government_Junta_of_Chile_(1973)" title="Government Junta of Chile (1973)">Government Junta</a> (1973–1990)</li>
<li>El Salvador — <a href="/wiki/Revolutionary_Government_Junta_of_El_Salvador" title="Revolutionary Government Junta of El Salvador">Revolutionary Government Junta</a> (1979–1982)</li>
<li>Ethiopia — <a href="/wiki/Derg" title="Derg">Derg</a> (1974–1987)</li>
<li>Fiji — military government of <a href="/wiki/Frank_Bainimarama" title="Frank Bainimarama">Frank Bainimarama</a> (2006–2014)<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Greece &#8212; <a href="/wiki/Greek_military_junta_of_1967%E2%80%931974" title="Greek military junta of 1967–1974">Regime of the Colonels</a> (1967–1974)</li>
<li>Guinea — <a href="/wiki/National_Council_for_Democracy_and_Development" title="National Council for Democracy and Development">National Council for Democracy and Development</a> (2008–2010)</li>
<li>Haiti — Junta that took control in the <a href="/wiki/1991_Haitian_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="1991 Haitian coup d&#39;état">1991 coup</a> and was <a href="/wiki/Operation_Uphold_Democracy" title="Operation Uphold Democracy">deposed in 1994</a> (1991–1994)</li>
<li>Myanmar (Burma) — <a href="/wiki/State_Peace_and_Development_Council" title="State Peace and Development Council">State Peace and Development Council</a> (1988–2011), known as the <i>State Law and Order Restoration Council</i> from 1988 to 1997.</li>
<li>Nicaragua — <a href="/wiki/Junta_of_National_Reconstruction" title="Junta of National Reconstruction">Junta of National Reconstruction</a> (1979–1985)</li>
<li>Nigeria &#8212; <a href="/wiki/Nigerian_military_juntas_of_1966%E2%80%931979_and_1983%E2%80%931998" class="mw-redirect" title="Nigerian military juntas of 1966–1979 and 1983–1998">Military juntas</a> (1966–1979 and 1983–1998)</li>
<li>Peru — <a href="/wiki/Peruvian_Military_Junta_of_1968-1980" title="Peruvian Military Junta of 1968-1980">Military junta of 1968-1980</a> (1968–1980)</li>
<li>Poland — <a href="/wiki/Military_Council_of_National_Salvation" title="Military Council of National Salvation">Military Council of National Salvation</a> (1981–1983)</li>
<li>Portugal — <a href="/wiki/National_Salvation_Junta" title="National Salvation Junta">National Salvation Junta</a> (1974–1975)</li>
<li>South Korea — <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Council_for_National_Reconstruction" title="Supreme Council for National Reconstruction">Supreme Council for National Reconstruction</a> (1961–1963)</li>
<li>Thailand — <a href="/wiki/Council_for_National_Security" title="Council for National Security">Council for National Security</a> (2006–2008)</li></ul>
