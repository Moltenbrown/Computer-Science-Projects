	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">History of biology</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For the video game, see <a href="/wiki/History_of_Biology_(video_game)" title="History of Biology (video game)">History of Biology (video game)</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">History of the study of life from ancient to modern times </div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Erasmus_Darwin_Temple_of_Nature.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Erasmus_Darwin_Temple_of_Nature.jpg/180px-Erasmus_Darwin_Temple_of_Nature.jpg" decoding="async" width="180" height="265" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Erasmus_Darwin_Temple_of_Nature.jpg/270px-Erasmus_Darwin_Temple_of_Nature.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a4/Erasmus_Darwin_Temple_of_Nature.jpg 2x" data-file-width="319" data-file-height="469" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Erasmus_Darwin_Temple_of_Nature.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The frontispiece to <a href="/wiki/Erasmus_Darwin" title="Erasmus Darwin">Erasmus Darwin</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a>-themed poem <i>The Temple of Nature</i> shows a goddess pulling back the veil from nature (in the person of <a href="/wiki/Artemis" title="Artemis">Artemis</a>). Allegory and metaphor have often played an important role in the history of biology.</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>history of biology</b> traces the study of the <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">living world</a> from <a href="/wiki/Ancient" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient">ancient</a> to <a href="/wiki/Modernity" title="Modernity">modern</a> times. Although the concept of <i><a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a></i> as a single coherent field arose in the 19th century, the biological sciences emerged from <a href="/wiki/History_of_medicine" title="History of medicine">traditions of medicine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">natural history</a> reaching back to <a href="/wiki/Ayurveda" title="Ayurveda">ayurveda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_medicine" title="Ancient Egyptian medicine">ancient Egyptian medicine</a> and the works of <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galen" title="Galen">Galen</a> in the ancient <a href="/wiki/Greco-Roman_world" title="Greco-Roman world">Greco-Roman world</a>. This ancient work was further developed in the Middle Ages by <a href="/wiki/Islamic_medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic medicine">Muslim physicians</a> and scholars such as <a href="/wiki/Avicenna" title="Avicenna">Avicenna</a>. During the European <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> and early modern period, biological thought was revolutionized in Europe by a renewed interest in <a href="/wiki/Empiricism" title="Empiricism">empiricism</a> and the discovery of many novel organisms. Prominent in this movement were <a href="/wiki/Vesalius" class="mw-redirect" title="Vesalius">Vesalius</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Harvey" title="William Harvey">Harvey</a>, who used experimentation and careful observation in physiology, and naturalists such as <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Georges-Louis_Leclerc,_Comte_de_Buffon" title="Georges-Louis Leclerc, Comte de Buffon">Buffon</a> who began to <a href="/wiki/Scientific_classification" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific classification">classify the diversity of life</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Fossil_record" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil record">fossil record</a>, as well as the development and behavior of organisms. <a href="/wiki/Antonie_van_Leeuwenhoek" title="Antonie van Leeuwenhoek">Antonie van Leeuwenhoek</a> revealed by means of <a href="/wiki/Microscopy" title="Microscopy">microscopy</a> the previously unknown world of microorganisms, laying the groundwork for <a href="/wiki/Cell_theory" title="Cell theory">cell theory</a>. The growing importance of <a href="/wiki/Natural_theology" title="Natural theology">natural theology</a>, partly a response to the rise of <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_philosophy" title="Mechanical philosophy">mechanical philosophy</a>, encouraged the growth of natural history (although it entrenched the <a href="/wiki/Teleological_argument" title="Teleological argument">argument from design</a>).
</p><p>Over the 18th and 19th centuries, biological sciences such as <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">botany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">zoology</a> became increasingly professional <a href="/wiki/Scientific_discipline" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific discipline">scientific disciplines</a>. <a href="/wiki/Lavoisier" class="mw-redirect" title="Lavoisier">Lavoisier</a> and other physical scientists began to connect the animate and inanimate worlds through physics and chemistry. Explorer-naturalists such as <a href="/wiki/Alexander_von_Humboldt" title="Alexander von Humboldt">Alexander von Humboldt</a> investigated the interaction between organisms and their environment, and the ways this relationship depends on geography—laying the foundations for <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">biogeography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethology" title="Ethology">ethology</a>. Naturalists began to reject <a href="/wiki/Essentialism" title="Essentialism">essentialism</a> and consider the importance of <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a> and the <a href="/wiki/History_of_evolutionary_thought" title="History of evolutionary thought">mutability of species</a>. <a href="/wiki/Cell_theory" title="Cell theory">Cell theory</a> provided a new perspective on the fundamental basis of life. These developments, as well as the results from <a href="/wiki/Embryology" title="Embryology">embryology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paleontology" title="Paleontology">paleontology</a>, were synthesized in <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a>'s theory of <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a> by <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a>. The end of the 19th century saw the fall of <a href="/wiki/Spontaneous_generation" title="Spontaneous generation">spontaneous generation</a> and the rise of the <a href="/wiki/Germ_theory_of_disease" title="Germ theory of disease">germ theory of disease</a>, though the mechanism of <a href="/wiki/Biological_inheritance" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological inheritance">inheritance</a> remained a mystery.
</p><p>In the early 20th century, the rediscovery of <a href="/wiki/Gregor_Mendel" title="Gregor Mendel">Mendel's</a> work led to the rapid development of <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a> by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hunt_Morgan" title="Thomas Hunt Morgan">Thomas Hunt Morgan</a> and his students, and by the 1930s the combination of <a href="/wiki/Population_genetics" title="Population genetics">population genetics</a> and natural selection in the "<a href="/wiki/Modern_synthesis_(20th_century)" title="Modern synthesis (20th century)">neo-Darwinian synthesis</a>". New disciplines developed rapidly, especially after <a href="/wiki/James_D._Watson" class="mw-redirect" title="James D. Watson">Watson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francis_Crick" title="Francis Crick">Crick</a> proposed the structure of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>. Following the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Central_Dogma" class="mw-redirect" title="Central Dogma">Central Dogma</a> and the cracking of the <a href="/wiki/Genetic_code" title="Genetic code">genetic code</a>, biology was largely split between <i>organismal biology</i>—the fields that deal with whole organisms and groups of organisms—and the fields related to <i><a href="/wiki/Cell_biology" title="Cell biology">cellular</a> and <a href="/wiki/Molecular_biology" title="Molecular biology">molecular biology</a></i>. By the late 20th century, new fields like <a href="/wiki/Genomics" title="Genomics">genomics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Proteomics" title="Proteomics">proteomics</a> were reversing this trend, with organismal biologists using molecular techniques, and molecular and cell biologists investigating the interplay between genes and the environment, as well as the genetics of natural populations of organisms.
</p>

<h2><span id="Etymology_of_.22biology.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_of_&quot;biology&quot;">Etymology of "biology"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology of &quot;biology&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <i>biology</i> is formed by combining the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B2%CE%AF%CE%BF%CF%82" class="extiw" title="wikt:βίος">βίος</a> (bios), meaning "life", and so the suffix '-logy', meaning "science of", "knowledge of", "study of", "about of", based on the Greek verb <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BB%CE%AD%CE%B3%CF%89" class="extiw" title="wikt:λέγω">λέγειν</a>, 'legein' "to select", "to gather" (cf. the noun <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BB%CF%8C%CE%B3%CE%BF%CF%82" class="extiw" title="wikt:λόγος">λόγος</a>, 'logos' "word"). The term <i>biology</i> in its modern sense appears to have been introduced independently by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Beddoes" title="Thomas Beddoes">Thomas Beddoes</a> (in 1799),<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Karl_Friedrich_Burdach" title="Karl Friedrich Burdach">Karl Friedrich Burdach</a> (in 1800), <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_Reinhold_Treviranus" title="Gottfried Reinhold Treviranus">Gottfried Reinhold Treviranus</a> (<i>Biologie oder Philosophie der lebenden Natur</i>, 1802) and <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Lamarck" title="Jean-Baptiste Lamarck">Jean-Baptiste Lamarck</a> (<i>Hydrogéologie</i>, 1802).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The word itself appears in the title of Volume 3 of <a href="/wiki/Michael_Christoph_Hanow" title="Michael Christoph Hanow">Michael Christoph Hanow</a>'s <i>Philosophiae naturalis sive physicae dogmaticae: Geologia, biologia, phytologia generalis et dendrologia</i>, published in 1766.
</p><p>Before <i>biology</i>, there were several terms used for the study of animals and plants. <i><a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">Natural history</a></i> referred to the descriptive aspects of biology, though it also included <a href="/wiki/Mineralogy" title="Mineralogy">mineralogy</a> and other non-biological fields; from the Middle Ages through the Renaissance, the unifying framework of natural history was the <i>scala naturae</i> or <a href="/wiki/Great_Chain_of_Being" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Chain of Being">Great Chain of Being</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Natural_philosophy" title="Natural philosophy">Natural philosophy</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Natural_theology" title="Natural theology">natural theology</a></i> encompassed the conceptual and metaphysical basis of plant and animal life, dealing with problems of why organisms exist and behave the way they do, though these subjects also included what is now <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>. Physiology and (botanical) pharmacology were the province of medicine. <i>Botany</i>, <i>zoology</i>, and (in the case of fossils) <i>geology</i> replaced <i>natural history</i> and <i>natural philosophy</i> in the 18th and 19th centuries before <i>biology</i> was widely adopted.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> To this day, "botany" and "zoology" are widely used, although they have been joined by other sub-disciplines of biology.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_and_medieval_knowledge">Ancient and medieval knowledge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancient and medieval knowledge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_cultures">Early cultures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early cultures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_world" title="History of the world">History of the world</a>, <a href="/wiki/History_of_agriculture" title="History of agriculture">History of agriculture</a>, and <a href="/wiki/History_of_medicine" title="History of medicine">History of medicine</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Earliest_humans" class="mw-redirect" title="Earliest humans">earliest humans</a> must have had and passed on knowledge about <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a> to increase their chances of survival. This may have included knowledge of human and animal anatomy and aspects of animal behavior (such as migration patterns). However, the first major turning point in biological knowledge came with the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_Revolution" title="Neolithic Revolution">Neolithic Revolution</a> about 10,000 years ago. Humans first domesticated plants for farming, then <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a> animals to accompany the resulting sedentary societies.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The ancient cultures of <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Egypt</a>, the <a href="/wiki/History_of_India" title="History of India">Indian subcontinent</a>, and <a href="/wiki/History_of_China" title="History of China">China</a>, among others, produced renowned surgeons and students of the natural sciences such as <a href="/wiki/Susruta" class="mw-redirect" title="Susruta">Susruta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Zhongjing" title="Zhang Zhongjing">Zhang Zhongjing</a>, reflecting independent sophisticated systems of natural philosophy. However, the roots of modern biology are usually traced back to the <a href="/wiki/Secular" class="mw-redirect" title="Secular">secular</a> tradition of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_philosophy" title="Ancient Greek philosophy">ancient Greek philosophy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Mesopotamia">Ancient Mesopotamia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ancient Mesopotamia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Babylonian_medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Babylonian medicine">Babylonian medicine</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:112px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Divinatory_livers_Louvre_AO19837.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Divinatory_livers_Louvre_AO19837.jpg/110px-Divinatory_livers_Louvre_AO19837.jpg" decoding="async" width="110" height="315" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Divinatory_livers_Louvre_AO19837.jpg/165px-Divinatory_livers_Louvre_AO19837.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Divinatory_livers_Louvre_AO19837.jpg/220px-Divinatory_livers_Louvre_AO19837.jpg 2x" data-file-width="588" data-file-height="1686" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Divinatory_livers_Louvre_AO19837.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Clay models of animal livers dating between the nineteenth and eighteenth centuries BCE, found in the royal palace at <a href="/wiki/Mari,_Syria" title="Mari, Syria">Mari</a></div></div></div>
<p>The Mesopotamians seem to have had little interest in the natural world as such, preferring to study how the gods had ordered the universe. <a href="/wiki/Animal_physiology" class="mw-redirect" title="Animal physiology">Animal physiology</a> was studied for <a href="/wiki/Divination" title="Divination">divination</a>, including especially the anatomy of the <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a>, seen as an important organ in <a href="/wiki/Haruspicy" class="mw-redirect" title="Haruspicy">haruspicy</a>. <a href="/wiki/Animal_behavior" class="mw-redirect" title="Animal behavior">Animal behavior</a> too was studied for divinatory purposes. Most information about the training and domestication of animals was probably transmitted orally, but one text dealing with the training of horses has survived.<sup id="cite_ref-McIntosh2005_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McIntosh2005-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The ancient Mesopotamians had no distinction between "rational science" and <a href="/wiki/Magic_(paranormal)" class="mw-redirect" title="Magic (paranormal)">magic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Farber1995_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farber1995-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Abusch_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Abusch-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brown_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> When a person became ill, doctors prescribed both magical formulas to be recited and medicinal treatments.<sup id="cite_ref-Farber1995_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farber1995-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Abusch_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Abusch-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brown_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The earliest medical prescriptions appear in <a href="/wiki/Sumerian_language" title="Sumerian language">Sumerian</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Third_Dynasty_of_Ur" title="Third Dynasty of Ur">Third Dynasty of Ur</a> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 2112 – <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 2004 BCE).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The most extensive Babylonian medical text, however, is the <i>Diagnostic Handbook</i> written by the <i>ummânū</i>, or chief scholar, <a href="/wiki/Esagil-kin-apli" title="Esagil-kin-apli">Esagil-kin-apli</a> of <a href="/wiki/Borsippa" title="Borsippa">Borsippa</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Stol-99_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stol-99-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> during the reign of the Babylonian king <a href="/wiki/Adad-apla-iddina" title="Adad-apla-iddina">Adad-apla-iddina</a> (1069 – 1046 BCE).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/East_Semitic" class="mw-redirect" title="East Semitic">East Semitic</a> cultures, the main medicinal authority was an exorcist-healer known as an <i><a href="/wiki/Asipu" class="mw-redirect" title="Asipu">āšipu</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Farber1995_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farber1995-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Abusch_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Abusch-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brown_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The profession was passed down from father to son and was held in high regard.<sup id="cite_ref-Farber1995_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farber1995-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Of less frequent recourse was the <i>asu</i>, a healer who treated physical symptoms using remedies composed of herbs, animal products, and minerals, as well as potions, enemas, and ointments or <a href="/wiki/Poultices" class="mw-redirect" title="Poultices">poultices</a>. These physicians, who could be either male or female, also dressed wounds, set limbs, and performed simple surgeries. The ancient Mesopotamians also practiced <a href="/wiki/Prophylaxis" class="mw-redirect" title="Prophylaxis">prophylaxis</a> and took measures to prevent the spread of disease.<sup id="cite_ref-McIntosh2005_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McIntosh2005-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Chinese_traditions">Ancient Chinese traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ancient Chinese traditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In ancient China, biological topics can be found dispersed across several different disciplines, including the work of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_herbology" title="Chinese herbology">herbologists</a>, physicians, alchemists, and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_philosophy" title="Chinese philosophy">philosophers</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Taoism" title="Taoism">Taoist</a> tradition of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_alchemy" title="Chinese alchemy">Chinese alchemy</a>, for example, can be considered part of the life sciences due to its emphasis on health (with the ultimate goal being the <a href="/wiki/Elixir_of_life" title="Elixir of life">elixir of life</a>). The system of <a href="/wiki/Classical_Chinese_medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Classical Chinese medicine">classical Chinese medicine</a> usually revolved around the theory of <a href="/wiki/Yin_and_yang" title="Yin and yang">yin and yang</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Wu_Xing" class="mw-redirect" title="Wu Xing">five phases</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Taoist philosophers, such as <a href="/wiki/Zhuang_Zhou" title="Zhuang Zhou">Zhuangzi</a> in the 4th century BCE, also expressed ideas related to <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a>, such as denying the fixity of biological species and speculating that species had developed differing attributes in response to differing environments.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Indian_traditions">Ancient Indian traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ancient Indian traditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>One of the oldest organised systems of medicine is known from the Indian subcontinent in the form of <a href="/wiki/Ayurveda" title="Ayurveda">Ayurveda</a> which originated around 1500 BCE from <a href="/wiki/Atharvaveda" title="Atharvaveda">Atharvaveda</a> (one of the four most ancient books of Indian knowledge, wisdom and culture).
</p><p>The ancient Indian <a href="/wiki/Ayurveda" title="Ayurveda">Ayurveda</a> tradition independently developed the concept of three humours, resembling that of the <a href="/wiki/Humorism" title="Humorism">four humours</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_medicine" title="Ancient Greek medicine">ancient Greek medicine</a>, though the Ayurvedic system included further complications, such as the body being composed of <a href="/wiki/Classical_element" title="Classical element">five elements</a> and seven basic <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">tissues</a>. Ayurvedic writers also classified living things into four categories based on the method of birth (from the womb, eggs, heat &amp; moisture, and seeds) and explained the conception of a <a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">fetus</a> in detail. They also made considerable advances in the field of <a href="/wiki/Surgery" title="Surgery">surgery</a>, often without the use of human <a href="/wiki/Dissection" title="Dissection">dissection</a> or animal <a href="/wiki/Vivisection" title="Vivisection">vivisection</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> One of the earliest Ayurvedic treatises was the <i><a href="/wiki/Sushruta_Samhita" title="Sushruta Samhita">Sushruta Samhita</a></i>, attributed to Sushruta in the 6th century BCE. It was also an early <a href="/wiki/Materia_medica" title="Materia medica">materia medica</a>, describing 700 medicinal plants, 64 preparations from mineral sources, and 57 preparations based on animal sources.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Egyptian_traditions">Ancient Egyptian traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ancient Egyptian traditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Over a dozen <a href="/wiki/Medical_papyri" class="mw-redirect" title="Medical papyri">medical papyri</a> have been preserved, most notably the <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Smith_Papyrus" title="Edwin Smith Papyrus">Edwin Smith Papyrus</a> (the oldest extant surgical handbook) and the <a href="/wiki/Ebers_Papyrus" title="Ebers Papyrus">Ebers Papyrus</a> (a handbook of preparing and using materia medica for various diseases), both from the 16th century BCE.
</p><p>Ancient Egypt is also known for developing <a href="/wiki/Embalming" title="Embalming">embalming</a>, which was used for <a href="/wiki/Mummy" title="Mummy">mummification</a>, in order to preserve human remains and forestall <a href="/wiki/Decomposition" title="Decomposition">decomposition</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Greek_and_Roman_traditions">Ancient Greek and Roman traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ancient Greek and Roman traditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_medicine" title="Ancient Greek medicine">Ancient Greek medicine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aristotle%27s_biology" title="Aristotle&#39;s biology">Aristotle's biology</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:161Theophrastus_161_frontespizio.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/161Theophrastus_161_frontespizio.jpg/170px-161Theophrastus_161_frontespizio.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="270" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/161Theophrastus_161_frontespizio.jpg/255px-161Theophrastus_161_frontespizio.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/161Theophrastus_161_frontespizio.jpg/340px-161Theophrastus_161_frontespizio.jpg 2x" data-file-width="437" data-file-height="693" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:161Theophrastus_161_frontespizio.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Frontispiece to a 1644 version of the expanded and illustrated edition of <i><a href="/wiki/Historia_Plantarum_(Theophrastus)" title="Historia Plantarum (Theophrastus)">Historia Plantarum</a></i>, originally written by <a href="/wiki/Theophrastus" title="Theophrastus">Theophrastus</a> around 300 BC</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pre-Socratic_philosophy" title="Pre-Socratic philosophy">pre-Socratic philosophers</a> asked many questions about life but produced little systematic knowledge of specifically biological interest—though the attempts of the <a href="/wiki/Atomists" class="mw-redirect" title="Atomists">atomists</a> to explain life in purely physical terms would recur periodically through the history of biology. However, the medical theories of <a href="/wiki/Hippocrates" title="Hippocrates">Hippocrates</a> and his followers, especially <a href="/wiki/Humorism" title="Humorism">humorism</a>, had a lasting impact.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The philosopher <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> was the most influential scholar of the living world from <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">classical antiquity</a>. Though his early work in natural philosophy was speculative, <a href="/wiki/Aristotle%27s_biology" title="Aristotle&#39;s biology">Aristotle's later biological writings</a> were more empirical, focusing on biological causation and the diversity of life. He made countless observations of nature, especially the habits and <a href="/wiki/Abstraction" title="Abstraction">attributes</a> of <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a> in the world around him, which he devoted considerable attention to <a href="/wiki/Categorization" title="Categorization">categorizing</a>. In all, Aristotle classified 540 animal species, and dissected at least 50. He believed that intellectual purposes, <a href="/wiki/Formal_cause" class="mw-redirect" title="Formal cause">formal causes</a>, guided all natural processes.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Aristotle, and nearly all Western scholars after him until the 18th century, believed that creatures were arranged in a graded scale of perfection rising from plants on up to humans: the <i>scala naturae</i> or <a href="/wiki/Great_Chain_of_Being" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Chain of Being">Great Chain of Being</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Aristotle's successor at the <a href="/wiki/Lyceum" title="Lyceum">Lyceum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theophrastus" title="Theophrastus">Theophrastus</a>, wrote a series of books on botany—the <i><a href="/wiki/Historia_Plantarum_(Theophrastus)" title="Historia Plantarum (Theophrastus)">History of Plants</a></i>—which survived as the most important contribution of antiquity to botany, even into the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. Many of Theophrastus' names survive into modern times, such as <i>carpos</i> for fruit, and <i>pericarpion</i> for seed vessel. <a href="/wiki/Dioscorides" class="mw-redirect" title="Dioscorides">Dioscorides</a> wrote a pioneering and <a href="/wiki/Encyclopaedia" class="mw-redirect" title="Encyclopaedia">encyclopaedic</a> <a href="/wiki/Pharmacopoeia" title="Pharmacopoeia">pharmacopoeia</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/De_Materia_Medica_(Dioscorides)" class="mw-redirect" title="De Materia Medica (Dioscorides)">De Materia Medica</a></i>, incorporating descriptions of some 600 plants and their uses in <a href="/wiki/Roman_medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman medicine">medicine</a>. <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a>, in his <i><a href="/wiki/Natural_History_(Pliny)" title="Natural History (Pliny)">Natural History</a></i>, assembled a similarly encyclopaedic account of things in nature, including accounts of many plants and animals.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A few scholars in the <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">Hellenistic period</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_dynasty" title="Ptolemaic dynasty">Ptolemies</a>—particularly <a href="/wiki/Herophilos" title="Herophilos">Herophilus of Chalcedon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Erasistratus" title="Erasistratus">Erasistratus of Chios</a>—amended Aristotle's physiological work, even performing dissections and vivisections.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Galen" title="Galen">Claudius Galen</a> became the most important authority on medicine and anatomy. Though a few ancient <a href="/wiki/Atomism" title="Atomism">atomists</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Lucretius" title="Lucretius">Lucretius</a> challenged the <a href="/wiki/Teleology" title="Teleology">teleological</a> Aristotelian viewpoint that all aspects of life are the result of design or purpose, teleology (and after the rise of <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_theology" title="Natural theology">natural theology</a>) would remain central to biological thought essentially until the 18th and 19th centuries. <a href="/wiki/Ernst_W._Mayr" class="mw-redirect" title="Ernst W. Mayr">Ernst W. Mayr</a> argued that "Nothing of any real consequence happened in biology after Lucretius and Galen until the Renaissance."<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The ideas of the Greek traditions of natural history and medicine survived, but they were generally taken unquestioningly in <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">medieval Europe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_and_Islamic_knowledge">Medieval and Islamic knowledge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Medieval and Islamic knowledge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Islamic_medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic medicine">Islamic medicine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_medicine" title="Byzantine medicine">Byzantine medicine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Medieval_medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval medicine">Medieval medicine</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ibn_al-nafis_page.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Ibn_al-nafis_page.jpg/170px-Ibn_al-nafis_page.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="268" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Ibn_al-nafis_page.jpg/255px-Ibn_al-nafis_page.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Ibn_al-nafis_page.jpg/340px-Ibn_al-nafis_page.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="1895" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ibn_al-nafis_page.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A biomedical work by <a href="/wiki/Ibn_al-Nafis" title="Ibn al-Nafis">Ibn al-Nafis</a>, an early adherent of experimental dissection who discovered the <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_circulation" title="Pulmonary circulation">pulmonary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coronary_circulation" title="Coronary circulation">coronary circulation</a></div></div></div>
<p>The decline of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> led to the disappearance or destruction of much knowledge, though physicians still incorporated many aspects of the Greek tradition into training and practice. In <a href="/wiki/Byzantium" title="Byzantium">Byzantium</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Islamic" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic">Islamic</a> world, many of the Greek works were translated into <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a> and many of the works of Aristotle were preserved.<sup id="cite_ref-Mayr-91-94_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mayr-91-94-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Frederick_II_and_eagle.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Frederick_II_and_eagle.jpg/170px-Frederick_II_and_eagle.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="235" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Frederick_II_and_eagle.jpg/255px-Frederick_II_and_eagle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Frederick_II_and_eagle.jpg/340px-Frederick_II_and_eagle.jpg 2x" data-file-width="593" data-file-height="818" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Frederick_II_and_eagle.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/De_arte_venandi" class="mw-redirect" title="De arte venandi">De arte venandi</a></i>, by <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor</a>, was an influential medieval natural history text that explored bird <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">High Middle Ages</a>, a few European scholars such as <a href="/wiki/Hildegard_of_Bingen" title="Hildegard of Bingen">Hildegard of Bingen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Albertus_Magnus" title="Albertus Magnus">Albertus Magnus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick II</a> wrote on natural history. The <a href="/wiki/History_of_European_research_universities" class="mw-redirect" title="History of European research universities">rise of European universities</a>, though important for the development of physics and philosophy, had little impact on biological scholarship.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Renaissance_and_early_modern_developments">Renaissance and early modern developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Renaissance and early modern developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_anatomy" title="History of anatomy">History of anatomy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scientific_Revolution" title="Scientific Revolution">Scientific Revolution</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/European_Renaissance" class="mw-redirect" title="European Renaissance">European Renaissance</a> brought expanded interest in both empirical natural history and physiology. In 1543, <a href="/wiki/Andreas_Vesalius" title="Andreas Vesalius">Andreas Vesalius</a> inaugurated the modern era of Western medicine with his seminal <a href="/wiki/Human_anatomy" class="mw-redirect" title="Human anatomy">human anatomy</a> treatise <i><a href="/wiki/De_humani_corporis_fabrica" title="De humani corporis fabrica">De humani corporis fabrica</a></i>, which was based on dissection of corpses. Vesalius was the first in a series of anatomists who gradually replaced <a href="/wiki/Scholasticism" title="Scholasticism">scholasticism</a> with <a href="/wiki/Empiricism" title="Empiricism">empiricism</a> in physiology and medicine, relying on first-hand experience rather than authority and abstract reasoning. Via <a href="/wiki/Herbalism" class="mw-redirect" title="Herbalism">herbalism</a>, medicine was also indirectly the source of renewed empiricism in the study of plants. <a href="/wiki/Otto_Brunfels" title="Otto Brunfels">Otto Brunfels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hieronymus_Bock" title="Hieronymus Bock">Hieronymus Bock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leonhart_Fuchs" title="Leonhart Fuchs">Leonhart Fuchs</a> wrote extensively on wild plants, the beginning of a nature-based approach to the full range of plant life.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bestiaries" class="mw-redirect" title="Bestiaries">Bestiaries</a>—a genre that combines both the natural and figurative knowledge of animals—also became more sophisticated, especially with the work of <a href="/wiki/William_Turner_(naturalist)" title="William Turner (naturalist)">William Turner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Belon" title="Pierre Belon">Pierre Belon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guillaume_Rondelet" title="Guillaume Rondelet">Guillaume Rondelet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conrad_Gessner" title="Conrad Gessner">Conrad Gessner</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ulisse_Aldrovandi" title="Ulisse Aldrovandi">Ulisse Aldrovandi</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Artists such as <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht Dürer">Albrecht Dürer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>, often working with naturalists, were also interested in the bodies of animals and humans, studying physiology in detail and contributing to the growth of anatomical knowledge.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The traditions of <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">alchemy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_magic" title="Natural magic">natural magic</a>, especially in the work of <a href="/wiki/Paracelsus" title="Paracelsus">Paracelsus</a>, also laid claim to knowledge of the living world. Alchemists subjected organic matter to chemical analysis and experimented liberally with both biological and mineral <a href="/wiki/Pharmacology" title="Pharmacology">pharmacology</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> This was part of a larger transition in world views (the rise of the <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_philosophy" title="Mechanical philosophy">mechanical philosophy</a>) that continued into the 17th century, as the traditional metaphor of <i>nature as organism</i> was replaced by the <i>nature as machine</i> metaphor.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seventeenth_and_eighteenth_centuries">Seventeenth and eighteenth centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Seventeenth and eighteenth centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_plant_systematics" title="History of plant systematics">History of plant systematics</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Scientific_classification" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific classification">Systematizing</a>, naming and classifying dominated natural history throughout much of the 17th and 18th centuries. <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> published a basic <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology)" title="Taxonomy (biology)">taxonomy</a> for the natural world in 1735 (variations of which have been in use ever since), and in the 1750s introduced <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">scientific names</a> for all his species.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> While Linnaeus conceived of species as unchanging parts of a designed hierarchy, the other great naturalist of the 18th century, <a href="/wiki/Georges-Louis_Leclerc,_Comte_de_Buffon" title="Georges-Louis Leclerc, Comte de Buffon">Georges-Louis Leclerc, Comte de Buffon</a>, treated species as artificial categories and living forms as malleable—even suggesting the possibility of <a href="/wiki/Common_descent" title="Common descent">common descent</a>. Though he was opposed to evolution, Buffon is a key figure in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_evolutionary_thought" title="History of evolutionary thought">history of evolutionary thought</a>; his work would influence the evolutionary theories of both <a href="/wiki/Lamarck" class="mw-redirect" title="Lamarck">Lamarck</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Darwin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The discovery and description of new species and the <a href="/wiki/Collecting" title="Collecting">collection</a> of specimens became a passion of scientific gentlemen and a lucrative enterprise for entrepreneurs; many naturalists traveled the globe in search of scientific knowledge and adventure.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Musei_Wormiani_Historia.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Musei_Wormiani_Historia.jpg/220px-Musei_Wormiani_Historia.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="175" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Musei_Wormiani_Historia.jpg/330px-Musei_Wormiani_Historia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Musei_Wormiani_Historia.jpg/440px-Musei_Wormiani_Historia.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1167" data-file-height="928" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Musei_Wormiani_Historia.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_curiosities" title="Cabinet of curiosities">Cabinets of curiosities</a>, such as that of <a href="/wiki/Ole_Worm" title="Ole Worm">Ole Worm</a>, were centers of biological knowledge in the early modern period, bringing organisms from across the world together in one place. Before the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Exploration" class="mw-redirect" title="Age of Exploration">Age of Exploration</a>, naturalists had little idea of the sheer scale of biological diversity.</div></div></div>
<p>Extending the work of Vesalius into experiments on still living bodies (of both humans and animals), <a href="/wiki/William_Harvey" title="William Harvey">William Harvey</a> and other natural philosophers investigated the roles of blood, veins and arteries. Harvey's <i><a href="/wiki/De_motu_cordis" class="mw-redirect" title="De motu cordis">De motu cordis</a></i> in 1628 was the beginning of the end for Galenic theory, and alongside <a href="/wiki/Santorio_Santorio" title="Santorio Santorio">Santorio Santorio</a>'s studies of metabolism, it served as an influential model of quantitative approaches to physiology.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the early 17th century, the micro-world of biology was just beginning to open up. A few lensmakers and natural philosophers had been creating crude <a href="/wiki/Microscope" title="Microscope">microscopes</a> since the late 16th century, and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hooke" title="Robert Hooke">Robert Hooke</a> published the seminal <i><a href="/wiki/Micrographia" title="Micrographia">Micrographia</a></i> based on observations with his own compound microscope in 1665. But it was not until <a href="/wiki/Antonie_van_Leeuwenhoek" title="Antonie van Leeuwenhoek">Antonie van Leeuwenhoek</a>'s dramatic improvements in lensmaking beginning in the 1670s—ultimately producing up to 200-fold magnification with a single lens—that scholars discovered <a href="/wiki/Spermatozoa" class="mw-redirect" title="Spermatozoa">spermatozoa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Infusoria" title="Infusoria">infusoria</a> and the sheer strangeness and diversity of microscopic life. Similar investigations by <a href="/wiki/Jan_Swammerdam" title="Jan Swammerdam">Jan Swammerdam</a> led to new interest in <a href="/wiki/Entomology" title="Entomology">entomology</a> and built the basic techniques of microscopic dissection and <a href="/wiki/Staining" title="Staining">staining</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cork_Micrographia_Hooke.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Cork_Micrographia_Hooke.png/170px-Cork_Micrographia_Hooke.png" decoding="async" width="170" height="245" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Cork_Micrographia_Hooke.png/255px-Cork_Micrographia_Hooke.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Cork_Micrographia_Hooke.png/340px-Cork_Micrographia_Hooke.png 2x" data-file-width="811" data-file-height="1170" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cork_Micrographia_Hooke.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In <i><a href="/wiki/Micrographia" title="Micrographia">Micrographia</a></i>, Robert Hooke had applied the word <i>cell</i> to biological structures such as this piece of <a href="/wiki/Cork_cambium" title="Cork cambium">cork</a>, but it was not until the 19th century that scientists considered cells the universal basis of life.</div></div></div>
<p>As the microscopic world was expanding, the macroscopic world was shrinking. Botanists such as <a href="/wiki/John_Ray" title="John Ray">John Ray</a> worked to incorporate the flood of newly discovered organisms shipped from across the globe into a coherent taxonomy, and a coherent theology (<a href="/wiki/Natural_theology" title="Natural theology">natural theology</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Debate over another flood, the <a href="/wiki/Noachian_flood" class="mw-redirect" title="Noachian flood">Noachian</a>, catalyzed the development of <a href="/wiki/Paleontology" title="Paleontology">paleontology</a>; in 1669 <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Steno" class="mw-redirect" title="Nicholas Steno">Nicholas Steno</a> published an essay on how the remains of living organisms could be trapped in layers of sediment and mineralized to produce <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a>. Although Steno's ideas about fossilization were well known and much debated among natural philosophers, an organic origin for all fossils would not be accepted by all naturalists until the end of the 18th century due to philosophical and theological debate about issues such as the age of the earth and <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century:_the_emergence_of_biological_disciplines">19th century: the emergence of biological disciplines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 19th century: the emergence of biological disciplines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Up through the 19th century, the scope of biology was largely divided between medicine, which investigated questions of form and function (i.e., physiology), and natural history, which was concerned with the diversity of life and interactions among different forms of life and between life and non-life. By 1900, much of these domains overlapped, while natural history (and its counterpart <a href="/wiki/Natural_philosophy" title="Natural philosophy">natural philosophy</a>) had largely given way to more specialized scientific disciplines—<a href="/wiki/Cell_biology" title="Cell biology">cytology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bacteriology" title="Bacteriology">bacteriology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Embryology" title="Embryology">embryology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Alexander_von_Humboldt_-_Geographie_der_Pflanzen_in_den_Tropen-Laendern_-_stacked.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Alexander_von_Humboldt_-_Geographie_der_Pflanzen_in_den_Tropen-Laendern_-_stacked.jpg/220px-Alexander_von_Humboldt_-_Geographie_der_Pflanzen_in_den_Tropen-Laendern_-_stacked.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="114" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Alexander_von_Humboldt_-_Geographie_der_Pflanzen_in_den_Tropen-Laendern_-_stacked.jpg/330px-Alexander_von_Humboldt_-_Geographie_der_Pflanzen_in_den_Tropen-Laendern_-_stacked.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Alexander_von_Humboldt_-_Geographie_der_Pflanzen_in_den_Tropen-Laendern_-_stacked.jpg/440px-Alexander_von_Humboldt_-_Geographie_der_Pflanzen_in_den_Tropen-Laendern_-_stacked.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5285" data-file-height="2742" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Alexander_von_Humboldt_-_Geographie_der_Pflanzen_in_den_Tropen-Laendern_-_stacked.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In the course of his travels, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_von_Humboldt" title="Alexander von Humboldt">Alexander von Humboldt</a> mapped the distribution of plants across landscapes and recorded a variety of physical conditions such as pressure and temperature.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_history_and_natural_philosophy">Natural history and natural philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Natural history and natural philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Humboldtian_science" title="Humboldtian science">Humboldtian science</a></div>
<p>Widespread travel by naturalists in the early-to-mid-19th century resulted in a wealth of new information about the diversity and distribution of living organisms. Of particular importance was the work of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_von_Humboldt" title="Alexander von Humboldt">Alexander von Humboldt</a>, which analyzed the relationship between organisms and their environment (i.e., the domain of <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">natural history</a>) using the quantitative approaches of <a href="/wiki/Natural_philosophy" title="Natural philosophy">natural philosophy</a> (i.e., <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>). Humboldt's work laid the foundations of <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">biogeography</a> and inspired several generations of scientists.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology_and_paleontology">Geology and paleontology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Geology and paleontology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_geology" title="History of geology">History of geology</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_paleontology" title="History of paleontology">History of paleontology</a></div>
<p>The emerging discipline of geology also brought natural history and natural philosophy closer together; the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Stratigraphy" title="Stratigraphy">stratigraphic column</a> linked the spatial distribution of organisms to their temporal distribution, a key precursor to concepts of evolution. <a href="/wiki/Georges_Cuvier" title="Georges Cuvier">Georges Cuvier</a> and others made great strides in <a href="/wiki/Comparative_anatomy" title="Comparative anatomy">comparative anatomy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paleontology" title="Paleontology">paleontology</a> in the late 1790s and early 19th century. In a series of lectures and papers that made detailed comparisons between living mammals and <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> remains Cuvier was able to establish that the fossils were remains of species that had become <a href="/wiki/Extinct" class="mw-redirect" title="Extinct">extinct</a>—rather than being remains of species still alive elsewhere in the world, as had been widely believed.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Fossils discovered and described by <a href="/wiki/Gideon_Mantell" title="Gideon Mantell">Gideon Mantell</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Buckland" title="William Buckland">William Buckland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mary_Anning" title="Mary Anning">Mary Anning</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Owen" title="Richard Owen">Richard Owen</a> among others helped establish that there had been an 'age of reptiles' that had preceded even the prehistoric mammals. These discoveries captured the public imagination and focused attention on the history of life on earth.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Most of these geologists held to <a href="/wiki/Catastrophism" title="Catastrophism">catastrophism</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lyell" title="Charles Lyell">Charles Lyell</a>'s influential <i>Principles of Geology</i> (1830) popularised <a href="/wiki/James_Hutton" title="James Hutton">Hutton's</a> <a href="/wiki/Uniformitarianism_(science)" class="mw-redirect" title="Uniformitarianism (science)">uniformitarianism</a>, a theory that explained the geological past and present on equal terms.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution_and_biogeography">Evolution and biogeography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Evolution and biogeography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_evolutionary_thought" title="History of evolutionary thought">History of evolutionary thought</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_speciation" title="History of speciation">History of speciation</a></div>
<p>The most significant evolutionary theory before Darwin's was that of <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Lamarck" title="Jean-Baptiste Lamarck">Jean-Baptiste Lamarck</a>; based on the <a href="/wiki/Inheritance_of_acquired_characteristics" class="mw-redirect" title="Inheritance of acquired characteristics">inheritance of acquired characteristics</a> (an inheritance mechanism that was widely accepted until the 20th century), it described a chain of development stretching from the lowliest microbe to humans.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> The British naturalist <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a>, combining the biogeographical approach of Humboldt, the uniformitarian geology of Lyell, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Malthus" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Malthus">Thomas Malthus</a>'s writings on population growth, and his own morphological expertise, created a more successful evolutionary theory based on <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a>; similar evidence led <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Russel_Wallace" title="Alfred Russel Wallace">Alfred Russel Wallace</a> to independently reach the same conclusions.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 1859 publication of Darwin's theory in <i><a href="/wiki/On_the_Origin_of_Species_by_Means_of_Natural_Selection,_or_the_Preservation_of_Favoured_Races_in_the_Struggle_for_Life" class="mw-redirect" title="On the Origin of Species by Means of Natural Selection, or the Preservation of Favoured Races in the Struggle for Life">On the Origin of Species by Means of Natural Selection, or the Preservation of Favoured Races in the Struggle for Life</a></i> is often considered the central event in the history of modern biology. Darwin's established credibility as a naturalist, the sober tone of the work, and most of all the sheer strength and volume of evidence presented, allowed <i>Origin</i> to succeed where previous evolutionary works such as the anonymous <i><a href="/wiki/Vestiges_of_Creation" class="mw-redirect" title="Vestiges of Creation">Vestiges of Creation</a></i> had failed. Most scientists were convinced of evolution and <a href="/wiki/Common_descent" title="Common descent">common descent</a> by the end of the 19th century. However, natural selection would not be accepted as the primary mechanism of evolution until well into the 20th century, as most contemporary theories of heredity seemed incompatible with the inheritance of random variation.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Darwins_first_tree.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Darwins_first_tree.jpg/220px-Darwins_first_tree.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="221" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Darwins_first_tree.jpg/330px-Darwins_first_tree.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Darwins_first_tree.jpg/440px-Darwins_first_tree.jpg 2x" data-file-width="610" data-file-height="612" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Darwins_first_tree.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a>'s first sketch of an evolutionary tree from his <i>First Notebook on Transmutation of Species</i> (1837)</div></div></div>
<p>Wallace, following on earlier work by <a href="/wiki/A.P._de_Candolle" class="mw-redirect" title="A.P. de Candolle">de Candolle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_von_Humboldt" title="Alexander von Humboldt">Humboldt</a> and Darwin, made major contributions to <a href="/wiki/Zoogeography" title="Zoogeography">zoogeography</a>. Because of his interest in the transmutation hypothesis, he paid particular attention to the geographical distribution of closely allied species during his field work first in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> and then in the <a href="/wiki/Malay_archipelago" class="mw-redirect" title="Malay archipelago">Malay archipelago</a>. While in the archipelago he identified the <a href="/wiki/Wallace_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Wallace line">Wallace line</a>, which runs through the <a href="/wiki/Maluku_Islands" title="Maluku Islands">Spice Islands</a> dividing the fauna of the archipelago between an Asian zone and a <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a>/Australian zone. His key question, as to why the fauna of islands with such similar climates should be so different, could only be answered by considering their origin. In 1876 he wrote <i>The Geographical Distribution of Animals</i>, which was the standard reference work for over half a century, and a sequel, <i>Island Life</i>, in 1880 that focused on island biogeography. He extended the six-zone system developed by <a href="/wiki/Philip_Sclater" title="Philip Sclater">Philip Sclater</a> for describing the geographical distribution of birds to animals of all kinds. His method of tabulating data on animal groups in geographic zones highlighted the discontinuities; and his appreciation of evolution allowed him to propose rational explanations, which had not been done before.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The scientific study of <a href="/wiki/Heredity" title="Heredity">heredity</a> grew rapidly in the wake of Darwin's <i>Origin of Species</i> with the work of <a href="/wiki/Francis_Galton" title="Francis Galton">Francis Galton</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Biometry" class="mw-redirect" title="Biometry">biometricians</a>. The origin of <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a> is usually traced to the 1866 work of the <a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">monk</a> <a href="/wiki/Gregor_Mendel" title="Gregor Mendel">Gregor Mendel</a>, who would later be credited with the <a href="/wiki/Laws_of_inheritance" class="mw-redirect" title="Laws of inheritance">laws of inheritance</a>. However, his work was not recognized as significant until 35 years afterward. In the meantime, a variety of theories of inheritance (based on <a href="/wiki/Pangenesis" title="Pangenesis">pangenesis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orthogenesis" title="Orthogenesis">orthogenesis</a>, or other mechanisms) were debated and investigated vigorously.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Embryology" title="Embryology">Embryology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> also became central biological fields, especially as linked to evolution and popularized in the work of <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Haeckel" title="Ernst Haeckel">Ernst Haeckel</a>. Most of the 19th century work on heredity, however, was not in the realm of natural history, but that of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Experimental_physiology&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Experimental physiology (page does not exist)">experimental physiology</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physiology">Physiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Physiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Over the course of the 19th century, the scope of physiology expanded greatly, from a primarily medically oriented field to a wide-ranging investigation of the physical and chemical processes of life—including plants, animals, and even microorganisms in addition to man. <i>Living things as machines</i> became a dominant metaphor in biological (and social) thinking.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Albert_Edelfelt_-_Louis_Pasteur_-_1885.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Albert_Edelfelt_-_Louis_Pasteur_-_1885.jpg/220px-Albert_Edelfelt_-_Louis_Pasteur_-_1885.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="268" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Albert_Edelfelt_-_Louis_Pasteur_-_1885.jpg/330px-Albert_Edelfelt_-_Louis_Pasteur_-_1885.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Albert_Edelfelt_-_Louis_Pasteur_-_1885.jpg/440px-Albert_Edelfelt_-_Louis_Pasteur_-_1885.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2082" data-file-height="2532" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Albert_Edelfelt_-_Louis_Pasteur_-_1885.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Innovative <a href="/wiki/Laboratory_glassware" title="Laboratory glassware">laboratory glassware</a> and experimental methods developed by <a href="/wiki/Louis_Pasteur" title="Louis Pasteur">Louis Pasteur</a> and other biologists contributed to the young field of <a href="/wiki/Bacteriology" title="Bacteriology">bacteriology</a> in the late 19th century.</div></div></div>
<h4><span id="Cell_theory.2C_embryology_and_germ_theory"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Cell_theory,_embryology_and_germ_theory">Cell theory, embryology and germ theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Cell theory, embryology and germ theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Advances in <a href="/wiki/Microscopy" title="Microscopy">microscopy</a> also had a profound impact on biological thinking. In the early 19th century, a number of biologists pointed to the central importance of the <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cell</a>. In 1838 and 1839, <a href="/wiki/Matthias_Jakob_Schleiden" title="Matthias Jakob Schleiden">Schleiden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Theodor_Schwann" title="Theodor Schwann">Schwann</a> began promoting the ideas that (1) the basic unit of organisms is the cell and (2) that individual cells have all the characteristics of <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a>, though they opposed the idea that (3) all cells come from the division of other cells. Thanks to the work of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Remak" title="Robert Remak">Robert Remak</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Virchow" title="Rudolf Virchow">Rudolf Virchow</a>, however, by the 1860s most biologists accepted all three tenets of what came to be known as <a href="/wiki/Cell_theory" title="Cell theory">cell theory</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cell theory led biologists to re-envision individual organisms as interdependent assemblages of individual cells. Scientists in the rising field of <a href="/wiki/Cell_biology" title="Cell biology">cytology</a>, armed with increasingly powerful microscopes and new <a href="/wiki/Staining" title="Staining">staining</a> methods, soon found that even single cells were far more complex than the homogeneous fluid-filled chambers described by earlier microscopists. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Brown_(Scottish_botanist_from_Montrose)" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Brown (Scottish botanist from Montrose)">Robert Brown</a> had described the <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">nucleus</a> in 1831, and by the end of the 19th century cytologists identified many of the key cell components: <a href="/wiki/Chromosome" title="Chromosome">chromosomes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Centrosome" title="Centrosome">centrosomes</a> <a href="/wiki/Mitochondria" class="mw-redirect" title="Mitochondria">mitochondria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplasts</a>, and other structures made visible through staining. Between 1874 and 1884 <a href="/wiki/Walther_Flemming" title="Walther Flemming">Walther Flemming</a> described the discrete stages of mitosis, showing that they were not <a href="/wiki/Artifact_(observational)" class="mw-redirect" title="Artifact (observational)">artifacts</a> of staining but occurred in living cells, and moreover, that chromosomes doubled in number just before the cell divided and a daughter cell was produced. Much of the research on cell reproduction came together in <a href="/wiki/August_Weismann" title="August Weismann">August Weismann</a>'s theory of heredity: he identified the nucleus (in particular chromosomes) as the hereditary material, proposed the distinction between <a href="/wiki/Somatic_cell" title="Somatic cell">somatic cells</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germ_cell" title="Germ cell">germ cells</a> (arguing that chromosome number must be halved for germ cells, a precursor to the concept of <a href="/wiki/Meiosis" title="Meiosis">meiosis</a>), and adopted <a href="/wiki/Hugo_de_Vries" title="Hugo de Vries">Hugo de Vries</a>'s theory of <a href="/wiki/Pangene" class="mw-redirect" title="Pangene">pangenes</a>. Weismannism was extremely influential, especially in the new field of experimental <a href="/wiki/Embryology" title="Embryology">embryology</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By the mid-1850s the <a href="/wiki/Miasma_theory_of_disease" class="mw-redirect" title="Miasma theory of disease">miasma theory of disease</a> was largely superseded by the <a href="/wiki/Germ_theory_of_disease" title="Germ theory of disease">germ theory of disease</a>, creating extensive interest in microorganisms and their interactions with other forms of life. By the 1880s, <a href="/wiki/Bacteriology" title="Bacteriology">bacteriology</a> was becoming a coherent discipline, especially through the work of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Koch" title="Robert Koch">Robert Koch</a>, who introduced methods for growing pure cultures on <a href="/wiki/Agar_plate" title="Agar plate">agar gels</a> containing specific nutrients in <a href="/wiki/Petri_dish" title="Petri dish">Petri dishes</a>. The long-held idea that living organisms could easily originate from nonliving matter (<a href="/wiki/Spontaneous_generation" title="Spontaneous generation">spontaneous generation</a>) was attacked in a series of experiments carried out by <a href="/wiki/Louis_Pasteur" title="Louis Pasteur">Louis Pasteur</a>, while debates over <a href="/wiki/Vitalism" title="Vitalism">vitalism</a> vs. <a href="/wiki/Mechanism_(philosophy)" title="Mechanism (philosophy)">mechanism</a> (a perennial issue since the time of Aristotle and the Greek atomists) continued apace.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rise_of_organic_chemistry_and_experimental_physiology">Rise of organic chemistry and experimental physiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Rise of organic chemistry and experimental physiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In chemistry, one central issue was the distinction between organic and inorganic substances, especially in the context of organic transformations such as <a href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">fermentation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Putrefaction" title="Putrefaction">putrefaction</a>. Since Aristotle these had been considered essentially biological (<i><a href="/wiki/Vitalism" title="Vitalism">vital</a></i>) processes. However, <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_W%C3%B6hler" title="Friedrich Wöhler">Friedrich Wöhler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Justus_Liebig" class="mw-redirect" title="Justus Liebig">Justus Liebig</a> and other pioneers of the rising field of <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a>—building on the work of Lavoisier—showed that the organic world could often be analyzed by physical and chemical methods. In 1828 Wöhler showed that the organic substance <a href="/wiki/Urea" title="Urea">urea</a> could be created by chemical means that do not involve life, providing a powerful challenge to <a href="/wiki/Vitalism" title="Vitalism">vitalism</a>. Cell extracts ("ferments") that could effect chemical transformations were discovered, beginning with <a href="/wiki/Diastase" title="Diastase">diastase</a> in 1833. By the end of the 19th century the concept of <a href="/wiki/Enzymes" class="mw-redirect" title="Enzymes">enzymes</a> was well established, though equations of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_kinetics" title="Chemical kinetics">chemical kinetics</a> would not be applied to enzymatic reactions until the early 20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Physiologists such as <a href="/wiki/Claude_Bernard" title="Claude Bernard">Claude Bernard</a> explored (through vivisection and other experimental methods) the chemical and physical functions of living bodies to an unprecedented degree, laying the groundwork for <a href="/wiki/Endocrinology" title="Endocrinology">endocrinology</a> (a field that developed quickly after the discovery of the first <a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">hormone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Secretin" title="Secretin">secretin</a>, in 1902), <a href="/wiki/Biomechanics" title="Biomechanics">biomechanics</a>, and the study of <a href="/wiki/Nutrition" title="Nutrition">nutrition</a> and <a href="/wiki/Digestion" title="Digestion">digestion</a>. The importance and diversity of experimental physiology methods, within both medicine and biology, grew dramatically over the second half of the 19th century. The control and manipulation of life processes became a central concern, and experiment was placed at the center of biological education.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>At the beginning of the 20th century, biological research was largely a professional endeavour. Most work was still done in the <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">natural history</a> mode, which emphasized morphological and phylogenetic analysis over experiment-based causal explanations. However, anti-<a href="/wiki/Vitalism" title="Vitalism">vitalist</a> experimental physiologists and embryologists, especially in Europe, were increasingly influential. The tremendous success of experimental approaches to development, heredity, and metabolism in the 1900s and 1910s demonstrated the power of experimentation in biology. In the following decades, experimental work replaced natural history as the dominant mode of research.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology_and_environmental_science">Ecology and environmental science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Ecology and environmental science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_ecology" title="History of ecology">History of ecology</a></div>
<p>In the early 20th century, naturalists were faced with increasing pressure to add rigor and preferably experimentation to their methods, as the newly prominent laboratory-based biological disciplines had done. <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">Ecology</a> had emerged as a combination of biogeography with the <a href="/wiki/Biogeochemical_cycle" title="Biogeochemical cycle">biogeochemical cycle</a> concept pioneered by chemists; field biologists developed quantitative methods such as the <a href="/wiki/Quadrat" title="Quadrat">quadrat</a> and adapted laboratory instruments and cameras for the field to further set their work apart from traditional natural history. Zoologists and botanists did what they could to mitigate the unpredictability of the living world, performing laboratory experiments and studying semi-controlled natural environments such as gardens; new institutions like the <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Station_for_Experimental_Evolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Carnegie Station for Experimental Evolution">Carnegie Station for Experimental Evolution</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Marine_Biological_Laboratory" title="Marine Biological Laboratory">Marine Biological Laboratory</a> provided more controlled environments for studying organisms through their entire life cycles.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Ecological_succession" title="Ecological succession">ecological succession</a> concept, pioneered in the 1900s and 1910s by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Chandler_Cowles" title="Henry Chandler Cowles">Henry Chandler Cowles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frederic_Clements" title="Frederic Clements">Frederic Clements</a>, was important in early plant ecology.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Lotka" class="mw-redirect" title="Alfred Lotka">Alfred Lotka</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Predator-prey_equations" class="mw-redirect" title="Predator-prey equations">predator-prey equations</a>, <a href="/wiki/G._Evelyn_Hutchinson" title="G. Evelyn Hutchinson">G. Evelyn Hutchinson</a>'s studies of the biogeography and biogeochemical structure of lakes and rivers (<a href="/wiki/Limnology" title="Limnology">limnology</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Sutherland_Elton" title="Charles Sutherland Elton">Charles Elton's</a> studies of animal <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chains</a> were pioneers among the succession of quantitative methods that colonized the developing ecological specialties. Ecology became an independent discipline in the 1940s and 1950s after <a href="/wiki/Eugene_P._Odum" class="mw-redirect" title="Eugene P. Odum">Eugene P. Odum</a> synthesized many of the concepts of <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_ecology" title="Ecosystem ecology">ecosystem ecology</a>, placing relationships between groups of organisms (especially material and energy relationships) at the center of the field.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1960s, as evolutionary theorists explored the possibility of multiple <a href="/wiki/Units_of_selection" class="mw-redirect" title="Units of selection">units of selection</a>, ecologists turned to evolutionary approaches. In <a href="/wiki/Population_ecology" title="Population ecology">population ecology</a>, debate over <a href="/wiki/Group_selection" title="Group selection">group selection</a> was brief but vigorous; by 1970, most biologists agreed that natural selection was rarely effective above the level of individual organisms. The evolution of ecosystems, however, became a lasting research focus. Ecology expanded rapidly with the rise of the environmental movement; the <a href="/wiki/International_Biological_Program" title="International Biological Program">International Biological Program</a> attempted to apply the methods of <a href="/wiki/Big_science" class="mw-redirect" title="Big science">big science</a> (which had been so successful in the physical sciences) to ecosystem ecology and pressing environmental issues, while smaller-scale independent efforts such as <a href="/wiki/Island_biogeography" class="mw-redirect" title="Island biogeography">island biogeography</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hubbard_Brook_Experimental_Forest" title="Hubbard Brook Experimental Forest">Hubbard Brook Experimental Forest</a> helped redefine the scope of an increasingly diverse discipline.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Classical_genetics.2C_the_modern_synthesis.2C_and_evolutionary_theory"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_genetics,_the_modern_synthesis,_and_evolutionary_theory">Classical genetics, the modern synthesis, and evolutionary theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Classical genetics, the modern synthesis, and evolutionary theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_genetics" title="History of genetics">History of genetics</a>, <a href="/wiki/History_of_model_organisms" title="History of model organisms">History of model organisms</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Modern_synthesis_(20th_century)" title="Modern synthesis (20th century)">Modern synthesis (20th century)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Morgan_crossover_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Morgan_crossover_1.jpg/220px-Morgan_crossover_1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Morgan_crossover_1.jpg/330px-Morgan_crossover_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Morgan_crossover_1.jpg/440px-Morgan_crossover_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1174" data-file-height="910" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Morgan_crossover_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hunt_Morgan" title="Thomas Hunt Morgan">Thomas Hunt Morgan</a>'s illustration of <a href="/wiki/Chromosomal_crossover" title="Chromosomal crossover">crossing over</a>, part of the Mendelian-chromosome theory of heredity</div></div></div>
<p>1900 marked the so-called <i>rediscovery of Mendel</i>: <a href="/wiki/Hugo_de_Vries" title="Hugo de Vries">Hugo de Vries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Correns" title="Carl Correns">Carl Correns</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Erich_von_Tschermak" title="Erich von Tschermak">Erich von Tschermak</a> independently arrived at <a href="/wiki/Mendel%27s_laws" class="mw-redirect" title="Mendel&#39;s laws">Mendel's laws</a> (which were not actually present in Mendel's work).<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Soon after, cytologists (cell biologists) proposed that <a href="/wiki/Chromosome" title="Chromosome">chromosomes</a> were the hereditary material. Between 1910 and 1915, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hunt_Morgan" title="Thomas Hunt Morgan">Thomas Hunt Morgan</a> and the "<a href="/wiki/Drosophilists" class="mw-redirect" title="Drosophilists">Drosophilists</a>" in his fly lab forged these two ideas—both controversial—into the "Mendelian-chromosome theory" of heredity.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> They quantified the phenomenon of genetic linkage and postulated that genes reside on chromosomes like beads on string; they hypothesized <a href="/wiki/Chromosomal_crossover" title="Chromosomal crossover">crossing over</a> to explain linkage and constructed <a href="/wiki/Genetic_map" class="mw-redirect" title="Genetic map">genetic maps</a> of the fruit fly <i><a href="/wiki/Drosophila_melanogaster" title="Drosophila melanogaster">Drosophila melanogaster</a></i>, which became a widely used <a href="/wiki/Model_organism" title="Model organism">model organism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hugo de Vries tried to link the new genetics with evolution; building on his work with heredity and <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridization</a>, he proposed a theory of <a href="/wiki/Mutationism" title="Mutationism">mutationism</a>, which was widely accepted in the early 20th century. <a href="/wiki/Lamarckism" title="Lamarckism">Lamarckism</a>, or the theory of inheritance of acquired characteristics also had many adherents. <a href="/wiki/Darwinism" title="Darwinism">Darwinism</a> was seen as incompatible with the continuously variable traits studied by <a href="/wiki/Biometry" class="mw-redirect" title="Biometry">biometricians</a>, which seemed only partially heritable. In the 1920s and 1930s—following the acceptance of the Mendelian-chromosome theory— the emergence of the discipline of <a href="/wiki/Population_genetics" title="Population genetics">population genetics</a>, with the work of <a href="/wiki/R.A._Fisher" class="mw-redirect" title="R.A. Fisher">R.A. Fisher</a>, <a href="/wiki/J.B.S._Haldane" class="mw-redirect" title="J.B.S. Haldane">J.B.S. Haldane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sewall_Wright" title="Sewall Wright">Sewall Wright</a>, unified the idea of evolution by <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a> with <a href="/wiki/Mendelian_inheritance" title="Mendelian inheritance">Mendelian genetics</a>, producing the <a href="/wiki/Modern_synthesis_(20th_century)" title="Modern synthesis (20th century)">modern synthesis</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Inheritance_of_acquired_characters" class="mw-redirect" title="Inheritance of acquired characters">inheritance of acquired characters</a> was rejected, while mutationism gave way as genetic theories matured.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the second half of the century the ideas of population genetics began to be applied in the new discipline of the genetics of behavior, <a href="/wiki/Sociobiology" title="Sociobiology">sociobiology</a>, and, especially in humans, <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_psychology" title="Evolutionary psychology">evolutionary psychology</a>. In the 1960s <a href="/wiki/W.D._Hamilton" class="mw-redirect" title="W.D. Hamilton">W.D. Hamilton</a> and others developed <a href="/wiki/Game_theory" title="Game theory">game theory</a> approaches to explain <a href="/wiki/Altruism" title="Altruism">altruism</a> from an evolutionary perspective through <a href="/wiki/Kin_selection" title="Kin selection">kin selection</a>. The possible origin of higher organisms through <a href="/wiki/Endosymbiosis" class="mw-redirect" title="Endosymbiosis">endosymbiosis</a>, and contrasting approaches to molecular evolution in the <a href="/wiki/Gene-centered_view_of_evolution" title="Gene-centered view of evolution">gene-centered view</a> (which held selection as the predominant cause of evolution) and the <a href="/wiki/Neutral_theory_of_molecular_evolution" title="Neutral theory of molecular evolution">neutral theory</a> (which made <a href="/wiki/Genetic_drift" title="Genetic drift">genetic drift</a> a key factor) spawned perennial debates over the proper balance of <a href="/wiki/Adaptationism" title="Adaptationism">adaptationism</a> and contingency in evolutionary theory.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1970s <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Jay_Gould" title="Stephen Jay Gould">Stephen Jay Gould</a> and <a href="/wiki/Niles_Eldredge" title="Niles Eldredge">Niles Eldredge</a> proposed the theory of <a href="/wiki/Punctuated_equilibrium" title="Punctuated equilibrium">punctuated equilibrium</a> which holds that stasis is the most prominent feature of the fossil record, and that most evolutionary changes occur rapidly over relatively short periods of time.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> In 1980 <a href="/wiki/Luis_Walter_Alvarez" title="Luis Walter Alvarez">Luis Alvarez</a> and <a href="/wiki/Walter_Alvarez" title="Walter Alvarez">Walter Alvarez</a> proposed the hypothesis that an <a href="/wiki/Impact_event" title="Impact event">impact event</a> was responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_extinction_event" title="Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event">Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> Also in the early 1980s, statistical analysis of the fossil record of marine organisms published by <a href="/wiki/Jack_Sepkoski" title="Jack Sepkoski">Jack Sepkoski</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_M._Raup" title="David M. Raup">David M. Raup</a> led to a better appreciation of the importance of <a href="/wiki/Mass_extinction_events" class="mw-redirect" title="Mass extinction events">mass extinction events</a> to the history of life on earth.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Biochemistry.2C_microbiology.2C_and_molecular_biology"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Biochemistry,_microbiology,_and_molecular_biology">Biochemistry, microbiology, and molecular biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Biochemistry, microbiology, and molecular biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_biochemistry" title="History of biochemistry">History of biochemistry</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_molecular_biology" title="History of molecular biology">History of molecular biology</a></div>
<p>By the end of the 19th century all of the major pathways of <a href="/wiki/Drug_metabolism" title="Drug metabolism">drug metabolism</a> had been discovered, along with the outlines of protein and fatty acid metabolism and urea synthesis.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> In the early decades of the 20th century, the minor components of foods in human nutrition, the <a href="/wiki/Vitamins" class="mw-redirect" title="Vitamins">vitamins</a>, began to be isolated and synthesized. Improved laboratory techniques such as <a href="/wiki/Chromatography" title="Chromatography">chromatography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electrophoresis" title="Electrophoresis">electrophoresis</a> led to rapid advances in physiological chemistry, which—as <i>biochemistry</i>—began to achieve independence from its medical origins. In the 1920s and 1930s, biochemists—led by <a href="/wiki/Hans_Adolf_Krebs" title="Hans Adolf Krebs">Hans Krebs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carl_Ferdinand_Cori" title="Carl Ferdinand Cori">Carl</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gerty_Cori" title="Gerty Cori">Gerty Cori</a>—began to work out many of the central <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_pathways" class="mw-redirect" title="Metabolic pathways">metabolic pathways</a> of life: the <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid_cycle" title="Citric acid cycle">citric acid cycle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glycogenesis" title="Glycogenesis">glycogenesis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glycolysis" title="Glycolysis">glycolysis</a>, and the synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Steroid" title="Steroid">steroids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Porphyrin" title="Porphyrin">porphyrins</a>. Between the 1930s and 1950s, <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Lipmann" class="mw-redirect" title="Fritz Lipmann">Fritz Lipmann</a> and others established the role of <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a> as the universal carrier of energy in the cell, and <a href="/wiki/Mitochondria" class="mw-redirect" title="Mitochondria">mitochondria</a> as the powerhouse of the cell. Such traditionally biochemical work continued to be very actively pursued throughout the 20th century and into the 21st.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_of_molecular_biology">Origins of molecular biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Origins of molecular biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Following the rise of classical genetics, many biologists—including a new wave of physical scientists in biology—pursued the question of the gene and its physical nature. <a href="/wiki/Warren_Weaver" title="Warren Weaver">Warren Weaver</a>—head of the science division of the <a href="/wiki/Rockefeller_Foundation" title="Rockefeller Foundation">Rockefeller Foundation</a>—issued grants to promote research that applied the methods of physics and chemistry to basic biological problems, coining the term <i><a href="/wiki/Molecular_biology" title="Molecular biology">molecular biology</a></i> for this approach in 1938; many of the significant biological breakthroughs of the 1930s and 1940s were funded by the Rockefeller Foundation.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:TMV_virus_under_magnification.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/TMV_virus_under_magnification.jpg/220px-TMV_virus_under_magnification.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/TMV_virus_under_magnification.jpg/330px-TMV_virus_under_magnification.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/TMV_virus_under_magnification.jpg/440px-TMV_virus_under_magnification.jpg 2x" data-file-width="675" data-file-height="675" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:TMV_virus_under_magnification.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Wendell_Meredith_Stanley" title="Wendell Meredith Stanley">Wendell Stanley</a>'s crystallization of <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_mosaic_virus" title="Tobacco mosaic virus">tobacco mosaic virus</a> as a pure <a href="/wiki/Nucleoprotein" title="Nucleoprotein">nucleoprotein</a> in 1935 convinced many scientists that heredity might be explained purely through physics and chemistry.</div></div></div>
<p>Like biochemistry, the overlapping disciplines of <a href="/wiki/Bacteriology" title="Bacteriology">bacteriology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Virology" title="Virology">virology</a> (later combined as <i>microbiology</i>), situated between science and medicine, developed rapidly in the early 20th century. <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9lix_d%27Herelle" title="Félix d&#39;Herelle">Félix d'Herelle</a>'s isolation of <a href="/wiki/Bacteriophage" title="Bacteriophage">bacteriophage</a> during World War I initiated a long line of research focused on phage viruses and the bacteria they infect.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The development of standard, genetically uniform organisms that could produce repeatable experimental results was essential for the development of <a href="/wiki/Molecular_genetics" title="Molecular genetics">molecular genetics</a>. After early work with <i>Drosophila</i> and <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a>, the adoption of simpler <a href="/wiki/Scientific_modelling" title="Scientific modelling">model systems</a> like the bread mold <i><a href="/wiki/Neurospora_crassa" title="Neurospora crassa">Neurospora crassa</a></i> made it possible to connect genetics to biochemistry, most importantly with <a href="/wiki/George_Wells_Beadle" class="mw-redirect" title="George Wells Beadle">Beadle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edward_Lawrie_Tatum" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward Lawrie Tatum">Tatum</a>'s <a href="/wiki/One_gene-one_enzyme_hypothesis" class="mw-redirect" title="One gene-one enzyme hypothesis">one gene-one enzyme hypothesis</a> in 1941. Genetics experiments on even simpler systems like <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_mosaic_virus" title="Tobacco mosaic virus">tobacco mosaic virus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bacteriophage" title="Bacteriophage">bacteriophage</a>, aided by the new technologies of <a href="/wiki/Electron_microscope" title="Electron microscope">electron microscopy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ultracentrifuge" title="Ultracentrifuge">ultracentrifugation</a>, forced scientists to re-evaluate the literal meaning of <i>life</i>; virus heredity and reproducing <a href="/wiki/Nucleoprotein" title="Nucleoprotein">nucleoprotein</a> cell structures outside the nucleus ("plasmagenes") complicated the accepted Mendelian-chromosome theory.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Crick%27s_1958_central_dogma.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Crick%27s_1958_central_dogma.svg/220px-Crick%27s_1958_central_dogma.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="214" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Crick%27s_1958_central_dogma.svg/330px-Crick%27s_1958_central_dogma.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Crick%27s_1958_central_dogma.svg/440px-Crick%27s_1958_central_dogma.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="720" data-file-height="700" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Crick%27s_1958_central_dogma.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The "<a href="/wiki/Central_dogma_of_molecular_biology" title="Central dogma of molecular biology">central dogma of molecular biology</a>" (originally a "dogma" only in jest) was proposed by Francis Crick in 1958.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> This is Crick's reconstruction of how he conceived of the central dogma at the time. The solid lines represent (as it seemed in 1958) known modes of information transfer, and the dashed lines represent postulated ones.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Oswald_Avery" title="Oswald Avery">Oswald Avery</a> showed in 1943 that <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> was likely the genetic material of the chromosome, not its protein; the issue was settled decisively with the 1952 <a href="/wiki/Hershey%E2%80%93Chase_experiment" title="Hershey–Chase experiment">Hershey–Chase experiment</a>—one of many contributions from the so-called <a href="/wiki/Phage_group" title="Phage group">phage group</a> centered around physicist-turned-biologist <a href="/wiki/Max_Delbr%C3%BCck" title="Max Delbrück">Max Delbrück</a>. In 1953 <a href="/wiki/James_Watson" title="James Watson">James Watson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francis_Crick" title="Francis Crick">Francis Crick</a>, building on the work of <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Wilkins" title="Maurice Wilkins">Maurice Wilkins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rosalind_Franklin" title="Rosalind Franklin">Rosalind Franklin</a>, suggested that the structure of DNA was a double helix. In their famous paper "<a href="/wiki/Molecular_structure_of_Nucleic_Acids" class="mw-redirect" title="Molecular structure of Nucleic Acids">Molecular structure of Nucleic Acids</a>", Watson and Crick noted coyly, "It has not escaped our notice that the specific pairing we have postulated immediately suggests a possible copying mechanism for the genetic material."<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> After the 1958 <a href="/wiki/Meselson%E2%80%93Stahl_experiment" title="Meselson–Stahl experiment">Meselson–Stahl experiment</a> confirmed the <a href="/wiki/Semiconservative_replication" title="Semiconservative replication">semiconservative replication</a> of DNA, it was clear to most biologists that nucleic acid sequence must somehow determine <a href="/wiki/Peptide_sequence" class="mw-redirect" title="Peptide sequence">amino acid sequence</a> in proteins; physicist <a href="/wiki/George_Gamow" title="George Gamow">George Gamow</a> proposed that a fixed <a href="/wiki/Genetic_code" title="Genetic code">genetic code</a> connected proteins and DNA. Between 1953 and 1961, there were few known biological sequences—either DNA or protein—but an abundance of proposed code systems, a situation made even more complicated by expanding knowledge of the intermediate role of <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a>. To actually decipher the code, it took an extensive series of experiments in biochemistry and bacterial genetics, between 1961 and 1966—most importantly the work of <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Warren_Nirenberg" title="Marshall Warren Nirenberg">Nirenberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Har_Gobind_Khorana" title="Har Gobind Khorana">Khorana</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion_of_molecular_biology">Expansion of molecular biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Expansion of molecular biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In addition to the Division of Biology at <a href="/wiki/Caltech" class="mw-redirect" title="Caltech">Caltech</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Laboratory_of_Molecular_Biology" title="Laboratory of Molecular Biology">Laboratory of Molecular Biology</a> (and its precursors) at <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Cambridge University">Cambridge</a>, and a handful of other institutions, the <a href="/wiki/Pasteur_Institute" title="Pasteur Institute">Pasteur Institute</a> became a major center for molecular biology research in the late 1950s.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> Scientists at Cambridge, led by <a href="/wiki/Max_Perutz" title="Max Perutz">Max Perutz</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Kendrew" title="John Kendrew">John Kendrew</a>, focused on the rapidly developing field of <a href="/wiki/Structural_biology" title="Structural biology">structural biology</a>, combining <a href="/wiki/X-ray_crystallography" title="X-ray crystallography">X-ray crystallography</a> with <a href="/wiki/Molecular_modelling" title="Molecular modelling">Molecular modelling</a> and the new computational possibilities of <a href="/wiki/History_of_computing_hardware" title="History of computing hardware">digital computing</a> (benefiting both directly and indirectly from the <a href="/wiki/Military_funding_of_science" class="mw-redirect" title="Military funding of science">military funding of science</a>). A number of biochemists led by <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Sanger" title="Frederick Sanger">Frederick Sanger</a> later joined the Cambridge lab, bringing together the study of <a href="/wiki/Macromolecule" title="Macromolecule">macromolecular</a> structure and function.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> At the Pasteur Institute, <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Jacob" title="François Jacob">François Jacob</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Monod" title="Jacques Monod">Jacques Monod</a> followed the 1959 <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Pardee#The_PaJaMo_experiment" title="Arthur Pardee">PaJaMo experiment</a> with a series of publications regarding the <a href="/wiki/Lac_operon" title="Lac operon"><i>lac</i></a> <a href="/wiki/Operon" title="Operon">operon</a> that established the concept of <a href="/wiki/Gene_regulation" class="mw-redirect" title="Gene regulation">gene regulation</a> and identified what came to be known as <a href="/wiki/Messenger_RNA" title="Messenger RNA">messenger RNA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> By the mid-1960s, the intellectual core of molecular biology—a model for the molecular basis of metabolism and reproduction— was largely complete.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The late 1950s to the early 1970s was a period of intense research and institutional expansion for molecular biology, which had only recently become a somewhat coherent discipline. In what organismic biologist <a href="/wiki/E._O._Wilson" title="E. O. Wilson">E. O. Wilson</a> called "The Molecular Wars", the methods and practitioners of molecular biology spread rapidly, often coming to dominate departments and even entire disciplines.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> Molecularization was particularly important in <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Immunology" title="Immunology">immunology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Embryology" title="Embryology">embryology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Neurobiology" class="mw-redirect" title="Neurobiology">neurobiology</a>, while the idea that life is controlled by a "<a href="/wiki/Genetic_program" title="Genetic program">genetic program</a>"—a metaphor Jacob and Monod introduced from the emerging fields of <a href="/wiki/Cybernetics" title="Cybernetics">cybernetics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>—became an influential perspective throughout biology.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> Immunology in particular became linked with molecular biology, with innovation flowing both ways: the <a href="/wiki/Clonal_selection_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Clonal selection theory">clonal selection theory</a> developed by <a href="/wiki/Niels_Jerne" class="mw-redirect" title="Niels Jerne">Niels Jerne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frank_Macfarlane_Burnet" class="mw-redirect" title="Frank Macfarlane Burnet">Frank Macfarlane Burnet</a> in the mid-1950s helped shed light on the general mechanisms of protein synthesis.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Resistance to the growing influence of molecular biology was especially evident in <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_biology" title="Evolutionary biology">evolutionary biology</a>. <a href="/wiki/Protein_sequencing" title="Protein sequencing">Protein sequencing</a> had great potential for the quantitative study of evolution (through the <a href="/wiki/Molecular_clock_hypothesis" class="mw-redirect" title="Molecular clock hypothesis">molecular clock hypothesis</a>), but leading evolutionary biologists questioned the relevance of molecular biology for answering the big questions of evolutionary causation. Departments and disciplines fractured as organismic biologists asserted their importance and independence: <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_Dobzhansky" title="Theodosius Dobzhansky">Theodosius Dobzhansky</a> made the famous statement that "<a href="/wiki/Nothing_in_biology_makes_sense_except_in_the_light_of_evolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Nothing in biology makes sense except in the light of evolution">nothing in biology makes sense except in the light of evolution</a>" as a response to the molecular challenge. The issue became even more critical after 1968; <a href="/wiki/Motoo_Kimura" title="Motoo Kimura">Motoo Kimura</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Neutral_theory_of_molecular_evolution" title="Neutral theory of molecular evolution">neutral theory of molecular evolution</a> suggested that <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a> was not the ubiquitous cause of evolution, at least at the molecular level, and that molecular evolution might be a fundamentally different process from <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphological</a> evolution. (Resolving this "molecular/morphological paradox" has been a central focus of molecular evolution research since the 1960s.)<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Biotechnology.2C_genetic_engineering.2C_and_genomics"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Biotechnology,_genetic_engineering,_and_genomics">Biotechnology, genetic engineering, and genomics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Biotechnology, genetic engineering, and genomics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_biotechnology" title="History of biotechnology">History of biotechnology</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">Biotechnology</a> in the general sense has been an important part of biology since the late 19th century. With the industrialization of <a href="/wiki/Brewing" title="Brewing">brewing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, chemists and biologists became aware of the great potential of human-controlled biological processes. In particular, <a href="/wiki/Industrial_fermentation" title="Industrial fermentation">fermentation</a> proved a great boon to chemical industries. By the early 1970s, a wide range of biotechnologies were being developed, from drugs like <a href="/wiki/Penicillin" title="Penicillin">penicillin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steroids" class="mw-redirect" title="Steroids">steroids</a> to foods like <i><a href="/wiki/Chlorella" title="Chlorella">Chlorella</a></i> and single-cell protein to <a href="/wiki/Gasohol" class="mw-redirect" title="Gasohol">gasohol</a>—as well as a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrid</a> <a href="/w/index.php?title=High-yield_crop&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="High-yield crop (page does not exist)">high-yield crops</a> and agricultural technologies, the basis for the <a href="/wiki/Green_Revolution" title="Green Revolution">Green Revolution</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:E_coli_at_10000x,_original.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/E_coli_at_10000x%2C_original.jpg/220px-E_coli_at_10000x%2C_original.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="160" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/E_coli_at_10000x%2C_original.jpg/330px-E_coli_at_10000x%2C_original.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/E_coli_at_10000x%2C_original.jpg/440px-E_coli_at_10000x%2C_original.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2598" data-file-height="1889" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:E_coli_at_10000x,_original.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Carefully engineered <a href="/wiki/Strain_(biology)" title="Strain (biology)">strains</a> of the bacterium <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">Escherichia coli</a></i> are crucial tools in biotechnology as well as many other biological fields.</div></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Recombinant_DNA">Recombinant DNA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Recombinant DNA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Biotechnology in the modern sense of <a href="/wiki/Genetic_engineering" title="Genetic engineering">genetic engineering</a> began in the 1970s, with the invention of <a href="/wiki/Recombinant_DNA" title="Recombinant DNA">recombinant DNA</a> techniques.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Restriction_enzyme" title="Restriction enzyme">Restriction enzymes</a> were discovered and characterized in the late 1960s, following on the heels of the isolation, then duplication, then synthesis of viral <a href="/wiki/Genes" class="mw-redirect" title="Genes">genes</a>. Beginning with the lab of <a href="/wiki/Paul_Berg" title="Paul Berg">Paul Berg</a> in 1972 (aided by <i><a href="/wiki/EcoRI" title="EcoRI">EcoRI</a></i> from <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Boyer" title="Herbert Boyer">Herbert Boyer</a>'s lab, building on work with <a href="/wiki/Ligase" title="Ligase">ligase</a> by <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Kornberg" title="Arthur Kornberg">Arthur Kornberg</a>'s lab), molecular biologists put these pieces together to produce the first <a href="/wiki/Transgenic_organisms" class="mw-redirect" title="Transgenic organisms">transgenic organisms</a>. Soon after, others began using <a href="/wiki/Plasmid" title="Plasmid">plasmid</a> <a href="/wiki/Vector_(molecular_biology)" title="Vector (molecular biology)">vectors</a> and adding genes for <a href="/wiki/Antibiotic_resistance" class="mw-redirect" title="Antibiotic resistance">antibiotic resistance</a>, greatly increasing the reach of the recombinant techniques.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Wary of the potential dangers (particularly the possibility of a prolific bacteria with a viral cancer-causing gene), the scientific community as well as a wide range of scientific outsiders reacted to these developments with both enthusiasm and fearful restraint. Prominent molecular biologists led by Berg suggested a temporary moratorium on recombinant DNA research until the dangers could be assessed and policies could be created. This moratorium was largely respected, until the participants in the 1975 <a href="/wiki/Asilomar_Conference_on_Recombinant_DNA" title="Asilomar Conference on Recombinant DNA">Asilomar Conference on Recombinant DNA</a> created policy recommendations and concluded that the technology could be used safely.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Following Asilomar, new genetic engineering techniques and applications developed rapidly. <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequencing" title="DNA sequencing">DNA sequencing</a> methods improved greatly (pioneered by <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Sanger" title="Frederick Sanger">Frederick Sanger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Walter_Gilbert" title="Walter Gilbert">Walter Gilbert</a>), as did <a href="/wiki/Oligonucleotide" title="Oligonucleotide">oligonucleotide</a> synthesis and <a href="/wiki/Transfection" title="Transfection">transfection</a> techniques.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> Researchers learned to control the expression of <a href="/wiki/Transgene" title="Transgene">transgenes</a>, and were soon racing—in both academic and industrial contexts—to create organisms capable of expressing human genes for the production of human hormones. However, this was a more daunting task than molecular biologists had expected; developments between 1977 and 1980 showed that, due to the phenomena of split genes and <a href="/wiki/Splicing_(genetics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Splicing (genetics)">splicing</a>, higher organisms had a much more complex system of <a href="/wiki/Gene_expression" title="Gene expression">gene expression</a> than the bacteria models of earlier studies.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> The first such race, for synthesizing human <a href="/wiki/Insulin" title="Insulin">insulin</a>, was won by <a href="/wiki/Genentech" title="Genentech">Genentech</a>. This marked the beginning of the biotech boom (and with it, the era of <a href="/wiki/Gene_patent" class="mw-redirect" title="Gene patent">gene patents</a>), with an unprecedented level of overlap between biology, industry, and law.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Molecular_systematics_and_genomics">Molecular systematics and genomics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Molecular systematics and genomics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_molecular_evolution" title="History of molecular evolution">History of molecular evolution</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cycler.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Cycler.jpg/170px-Cycler.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="206" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Cycler.jpg/255px-Cycler.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Cycler.jpg/340px-Cycler.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1738" data-file-height="2109" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cycler.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Inside of a 48-well <a href="/wiki/Thermal_cycler" title="Thermal cycler">thermal cycler</a>, a device used to perform <a href="/wiki/Polymerase_chain_reaction" title="Polymerase chain reaction">polymerase chain reaction</a> on many samples at once</div></div></div>
<p>By the 1980s, protein sequencing had already transformed methods of <a href="/wiki/Scientific_classification" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific classification">scientific classification</a> of organisms (especially <a href="/wiki/Cladistics" title="Cladistics">cladistics</a>) but biologists soon began to use RNA and DNA sequences as <a href="/wiki/Trait_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Trait (biology)">characters</a>; this expanded the significance of <a href="/wiki/Molecular_evolution" title="Molecular evolution">molecular evolution</a> within evolutionary biology, as the results of <a href="/wiki/Molecular_systematics" class="mw-redirect" title="Molecular systematics">molecular systematics</a> could be compared with traditional evolutionary trees based on <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a>. Following the pioneering ideas of <a href="/wiki/Lynn_Margulis" title="Lynn Margulis">Lynn Margulis</a> on <a href="/wiki/Endosymbiotic_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Endosymbiotic theory">endosymbiotic theory</a>, which holds that some of the <a href="/wiki/Organelles" class="mw-redirect" title="Organelles">organelles</a> of <a href="/wiki/Eukaryotic" class="mw-redirect" title="Eukaryotic">eukaryotic</a> cells originated from free living <a href="/wiki/Prokaryotic" class="mw-redirect" title="Prokaryotic">prokaryotic</a> organisms through <a href="/wiki/Symbiotic" class="mw-redirect" title="Symbiotic">symbiotic</a> relationships, even the overall division of the tree of life was revised. Into the 1990s, the five domains (Plants, Animals, Fungi, Protists, and Monerans) became three (the <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">Archaea</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">Bacteria</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Eukarya" class="mw-redirect" title="Eukarya">Eukarya</a>) based on <a href="/wiki/Carl_Woese" title="Carl Woese">Carl Woese</a>'s pioneering <a href="/wiki/Molecular_systematics" class="mw-redirect" title="Molecular systematics">molecular systematics</a> work with <a href="/wiki/16S_ribosomal_RNA" title="16S ribosomal RNA">16S rRNA</a> sequencing.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The development and popularization of the <a href="/wiki/Polymerase_chain_reaction" title="Polymerase chain reaction">polymerase chain reaction</a> (PCR) in mid-1980s (by <a href="/wiki/Kary_Mullis" title="Kary Mullis">Kary Mullis</a> and others at <a href="/wiki/Cetus_Corp." class="mw-redirect" title="Cetus Corp.">Cetus Corp.</a>) marked another watershed in the history of modern biotechnology, greatly increasing the ease and speed of genetic analysis.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup> Coupled with the use of <a href="/wiki/Expressed_sequence_tags" class="mw-redirect" title="Expressed sequence tags">expressed sequence tags</a>, PCR led to the discovery of many more genes than could be found through traditional biochemical or genetic methods and opened the possibility of sequencing entire genomes.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The unity of much of the <a href="/wiki/Morphogenesis" title="Morphogenesis">morphogenesis</a> of organisms from fertilized egg to adult began to be unraveled after the discovery of the <a href="/wiki/Homeobox" title="Homeobox">homeobox</a> genes, first in fruit flies, then in other insects and animals, including humans. These developments led to advances in the field of <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_developmental_biology" title="Evolutionary developmental biology">evolutionary developmental biology</a> towards understanding how the various <a href="/wiki/Body_plan" title="Body plan">body plans</a> of the animal phyla have evolved and how they are related to one another.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Human_Genome_Project" title="Human Genome Project">Human Genome Project</a>—the largest, most costly single biological study ever undertaken—began in 1988 under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/James_D._Watson" class="mw-redirect" title="James D. Watson">James D. Watson</a>, after preliminary work with genetically simpler model organisms such as <i><a href="/wiki/E._coli" class="mw-redirect" title="E. coli">E. coli</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/S._cerevisiae" class="mw-redirect" title="S. cerevisiae">S. cerevisiae</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Caenorhabditis_elegans" title="Caenorhabditis elegans">C. elegans</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Shotgun_sequencing" title="Shotgun sequencing">Shotgun sequencing</a> and gene discovery methods pioneered by <a href="/wiki/Craig_Venter" title="Craig Venter">Craig Venter</a>—and fueled by the financial promise of gene patents with <a href="/wiki/Celera_Genomics" class="mw-redirect" title="Celera Genomics">Celera Genomics</a>— led to a public–private sequencing competition that ended in compromise with the first draft of the human DNA sequence announced in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Twenty-first_century_biological_sciences">Twenty-first century biological sciences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Twenty-first century biological sciences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the beginning of the 21st century, biological sciences converged with previously differentiated new and classic disciplines like <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">Physics</a> into research fields like <a href="/wiki/Biophysics" title="Biophysics">Biophysics</a>.  Advances were made in <a href="/wiki/Analytical_chemistry" title="Analytical chemistry">analytical chemistry</a> and physics instrumentation including improved sensors, optics, tracers, instrumentation, signal processing, networks, robots, satellites, and compute power for data collection, storage, analysis, modeling, visualization, and simulations.  These technology advances allowed theoretical and experimental research including internet publication of molecular biochemistry, <a href="/wiki/Biological_system" title="Biological system">biological systems</a>, and ecosystems science.  This enabled worldwide access to better measurements, theoretical models, complex simulations, theory predictive model experimentation, analysis, worldwide internet observational <a href="/wiki/Data_reporting" title="Data reporting">data reporting</a>, open peer-review, collaboration, and internet publication.  New fields of biological sciences research emerged including <a href="/wiki/Bioinformatics" title="Bioinformatics">Bioinformatics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neuroscience" title="Neuroscience">Neuroscience</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_biology" class="mw-redirect" title="Theoretical biology">Theoretical biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computational_genomics" title="Computational genomics">Computational genomics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Astrobiology" title="Astrobiology">Astrobiology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_Biology" class="mw-redirect" title="Synthetic Biology">Synthetic Biology</a>.
</p>
