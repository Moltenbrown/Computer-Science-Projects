	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Consanguinity</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p><b>Consanguinity</b> ("blood relation", from the Latin <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/consanguinitas" class="extiw" title="wikt:consanguinitas">consanguinitas</a></i>) is the property of being from the same <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">kinship</a> as another person. In that aspect, consanguinity is the quality of being descended from the same ancestor as another person.
</p><p>The laws of many jurisdictions set out the <a href="/wiki/Degree_of_relationship" class="mw-redirect" title="Degree of relationship">degree of consanguinity</a> in relation to prohibited sexual relations and marriage parties. Such rules are also used to determine heirs of an estate according to statutes that govern intestate succession, which vary from jurisdiction to jurisdiction. In some places and times, <a href="/wiki/Cousin_marriage" title="Cousin marriage">cousin marriage</a> is approved and expected; in others, it is as taboo as <a href="/wiki/Incest" title="Incest">incest</a>.
</p><p>The degree of relative consanguinity can be illustrated with a <b>consanguinity table</b> in which each level of lineal consanguinity (<i><a href="/wiki/Generation" title="Generation">generation</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Meiosis" title="Meiosis">meiosis</a></i>) appears as a row, and individuals with a collaterally consanguineous relationship share the same row.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The Knot System is a numerical notation that defines consanguinity.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_secular_law">Modern secular law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consanguinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Modern secular law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The degree of kinship between two people may give raise to several legal issues. Some laws prohibit <a href="/wiki/Sexual_relations" class="mw-redirect" title="Sexual relations">sexual relations</a> between closely related people, referred to as incestuous. Laws may also bar marriage between closely related people, which are almost universally prohibited to the second degree of consanguinity. Some jurisdictions forbid marriage between <a href="/wiki/Cousin#First_cousin" title="Cousin">first cousins</a>, while others do not. Marriage with aunts and uncles (<a href="/wiki/Avunculate_marriage" title="Avunculate marriage">avunculate marriage</a>) is legal in several countries.
</p><p>Consanguinity is also relevant to inheritance, particularly with regard to <a href="/wiki/Intestate_succession" class="mw-redirect" title="Intestate succession">intestate succession</a>. In general, laws tend to favor inheritance by persons closely related to the deceased.
</p><p>Some jurisdictions ban citizens from service on a jury on the basis of consanguinity and also affinity with persons involved in the case.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In many countries, laws prohibiting <a href="/wiki/Nepotism" title="Nepotism">nepotism</a> ban employment of, or certain kinds of contracts with, the near relations of public officers or employees.
</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Libellus_responsionum" title="Libellus responsionum">Libellus responsionum</a></div>
<p>Under <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">Roman civil law</a>, which early <a href="/wiki/Canon_law_(Catholic_Church)" class="mw-redirect" title="Canon law (Catholic Church)">canon law of the Catholic Church</a> followed, couples were forbidden to marry if they were within four degrees of consanguinity.<sup id="cite_ref-Bouchard2010_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bouchard2010-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In the ninth century the church raised the number of prohibited degrees to seven and changed the method by which they were calculated.<sup id="cite_ref-Bouchard2010_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bouchard2010-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Eventually the nobility became too interrelated to marry as the pool of non-related prospective spouses became smaller. They had to either defy the church's position or look elsewhere for eligible marriage candidates.<sup id="cite_ref-Bouchard2010_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bouchard2010-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In 1215 the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Council_of_the_Lateran" title="Fourth Council of the Lateran">Fourth Lateran Council</a> made what they believed was a necessary change to canon law reducing the number of prohibited degrees of consanguinity from seven back to four.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Baldwin1994_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baldwin1994-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The method of calculating prohibited degrees was changed also: Instead of the former practice of counting up to the common ancestor then down to the proposed spouse, the new law computed consanguinity by counting back to the common ancestor.<sup id="cite_ref-Bouchard2010_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bouchard2010-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>, unknowingly marrying a closely consanguineous blood relative was grounds for a <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_nullity" title="Declaration of nullity">declaration of nullity</a>, but during the eleventh and twelfth centuries <a href="/wiki/Dispensation_(canon_law)" title="Dispensation (canon law)">dispensations</a> were granted with increasing frequency due to the thousands of persons encompassed in the prohibition at seven degrees and the hardships this posed for finding potential spouses.<sup id="cite_ref-LSCS356_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LSCS356-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After 1215, the general rule was that while fourth cousins could marry without dispensation, generally the need for dispensations was greatly reduced.<sup id="cite_ref-LSCS356_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LSCS356-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In fourteenth century England, for example, papal dispensations for annulments due to consanguinity (and <a href="/wiki/Affinity_(canon_law)" title="Affinity (canon law)">affinity</a>) were relatively few.<sup id="cite_ref-Helmholz2007_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Helmholz2007-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The connotations of degree of consanguinity varies by context, though most cultures define a degree of consanguinity within which sexual interrelationships are regarded as incestuous  or the "<a href="/wiki/Prohibited_degree_of_kinship" title="Prohibited degree of kinship">prohibited degree of kinship</a>".
</p><p>Among the Christian <i>Habesha</i> highlanders of <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eritrea" title="Eritrea">Eritrea</a> (the predominantly orthodox Christian <a href="/wiki/Amhara_people" title="Amhara people">Amhara</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tigray-Tigrinya_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Tigray-Tigrinya people">Tigray-Tigrinya</a>), it is a tradition to be able to recount one's paternal ancestors at least seven generations away starting from early childhood, because "those with a common patrilineal ancestor less than seven generations away are considered 'brother and sister' and may not marry." The rule is less strict on the mother's side, where the limit is about four generations back, but still determined patrilinearly. This rule does not apply to Muslims or other ethnic groups.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Islam">Islam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consanguinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Islam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Qisas" title="Qisas">Qisas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Watta_satta" title="Watta satta">Watta satta</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a> at 4:22-24 states. "Forbidden to you in marriage are: your mothers, your daughters, your sisters, your father's sisters, your mother's sisters, your brother's daughters, your sister's daughters."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Therefore, the list of forbidden marriage partners, as read in the Qur'an, Surah 4:23, does not include first cousins.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a> himself married his first cousin <a href="/wiki/Zaynab_bint_Jahsh" title="Zaynab bint Jahsh">Zaynab bint Jahsh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (November 2018)">better&#160;source&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Financial incentives to discourage consangineous marriages exist in some countries:  mandatory premarital screening for inherited blood disorders exist in the UAE since 2004, Qatar in 2009, where couples with positive results will not receive their marriage grant.<sup id="cite_ref-mid_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mid-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Genetically, consanguinity derives from the reduction in variation due to <a href="/wiki/Meiosis" title="Meiosis">meiosis</a> that occurs because of the smaller number of near ancestors. Since all humans share between 99.6% and 99.9% of their <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> consanguinity only affects a very small part of the sequence. If two siblings have a child, the child only has two rather than four grandparents. In these circumstances the probability that the child inherits two copies of a harmful <a href="/wiki/Recessive_gene" class="mw-redirect" title="Recessive gene">recessive gene</a> (<a href="/wiki/Allele" title="Allele">allele</a>) rather than one which would not have immediate effects is much increased.
</p><p>Genetic consanguinity is expressed as defined 1922 by Wright<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> with the <a href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_relationship" title="Coefficient of relationship">coefficient of relationship</a> <i>r</i>, where <i>r</i> is defined as the fraction of <a href="/wiki/Zygosity" title="Zygosity">homozygous</a> due to the consanguinity under discussion. Thus, a parent and child pair has a value of <i>r</i>=0.5 (sharing 50% of genes), siblings have a value of <i>r</i>=0.5, a parent's sibling has <i>r</i>=0.25 (25% of genes), and first cousins have <i>r</i>=0.125 (12,5% of genes). These are often expressed in terms of a percentage of shared DNA.
</p><p>As a working definition, unions contracted between persons biologically related as second cousins or closer (<i>r</i> ≥ 0.03125) are categorized as consanguineous. This arbitrary limit has been chosen because the genetic influence in marriages between couples related to a lesser degree would usually be expected to differ only slightly from that observed in the general population. Globally it is estimated that at least 8.5% of children have consanguineous parents.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In clinical genetics, consanguinity is defined as a union between two individuals who are related as second cousins or closer, with the inbreeding coefficient (F) equal or higher than 0.0156.where (F) represents the proportion of genetic loci at which the child of a consanguineous couple might inherit identical gene copies from both parents.<sup id="cite_ref-hamamy_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hamamy-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is common to distinguish first-degree cousins, second-degree cousins, and often also third-degree cousins. Since comparatively few people can trace their full family tree for more than four generations, the identity of fourth-degree cousins often cannot be established.
Also, at a genetic level, half-fourth cousins typically do not exhibit greater genetic similarity with one another than with any other individual from the same population.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_factors_in_favor">Cultural factors in favor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consanguinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cultural factors in favor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Reasons favoring consanguinous marriage have been listed as higher compatibility between husband and wife sharing same social relationships, couples stability, enforcing family solidarity, easier financial negotiations and others.<sup id="cite_ref-hamamy_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hamamy-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>187</span></sup>
Consanguinity is a deeply rooted phenomenon in 20% of the world population mostly the Middle East, West Asia and North Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-hamamy_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hamamy-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Globally, the most common form of consanguineous union is between first cousins, in which the spouses share &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>8</sub></span> of their genes inherited from a common ancestor, and so their progeny are homozygous (or more correctly autozygous) at &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>16</sub></span> of all loci (<i>r</i> = 0.0625).<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Due to variation in geographical and ethnic background and the loci chosen to genotype there is some 2.4% variation expected.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consanguinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Historically, some European nobles cited a close degree of consanguinity when they required convenient grounds for divorce, especially in contexts where religious doctrine forbade the voluntary dissolution of an unhappy or childless marriage.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Conversely, the consanguinity law of <a href="/wiki/Order_of_succession" title="Order of succession">Order of succession</a> requires the next monarch to be of the same blood of the previous one; allowing, for example, <a href="/wiki/Illegitimacy" class="mw-redirect" title="Illegitimacy">illegitimate</a> children to inherit.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Muslim_countries">Muslim countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consanguinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Muslim countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the Arab world today<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (November 2018)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> the practice of marrying relatives is common. According to the Centre for Arabic Genomic Research, between 40% and 54% of UAE nationals’ marriages are between family members, up from 39% in the previous generation. Between 21% and 28% of marriages of UAE nationals were between first cousins.<sup id="cite_ref-mid_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mid-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pmid24294299_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid24294299-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Consanguineous marriage is much less prevalent in Christian Arabs as they do not practice arranged marriages.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, an <a href="/wiki/Dispensation_(canon_law)#General_powers_of_dispensation" title="Dispensation (canon law)">indult dispensation</a> is required to marriages contracted between first cousins or closer in Arab Christian denominations in communion with the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox_Church" title="Greek Orthodox Church">Greek Orthodox Church</a>; there are no similar regulations that apply to first-cousin marriages in the Coptic Orthodox Church.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Egypt, around 40% of the population marry a cousin. A 1992 survey in Jordan found that 32% were married to a first cousin; a further 17.3% were married to more distant relatives.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> 67% of marriages in <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a> are between close relatives as are 54% of all marriages in <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwait</a>, whereas 18% of all <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanese</a> were between blood relatives.The incidence of consanguinity was 54.3% among Kuwaiti natives and higher among Bedouins.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It has been estimated that 55% of marriages between Pakistani Muslim immigrants in the United Kingdom are between first cousins,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> where preferential patrilateral parallel <a href="/wiki/Cousin_marriage" title="Cousin marriage">cousin marriage</a>, i.e. a boy marrying the daughter of his father's brother is favored.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cousin#Additional_terms" title="Cousin">Double first cousins</a> are descended from two pairs of siblings, and have the same genetic similarity as half-siblings. In unions between double first cousins the highest <a href="/wiki/Inbreeding" title="Inbreeding">inbreeding</a> coefficients are reached, with an( F) of 0.125, for example in among Arabs and uncle-niece marriages in South India.<sup id="cite_ref-hamamy_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hamamy-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetic_disorders">Genetic disorders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consanguinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Genetic disorders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Inbreeding" title="Inbreeding">Inbreeding</a></div>
<p>The phenomenon of inbreeding increases the level of homozygotes for autosomal genetic disorders and generally leads to a decreased <a href="/wiki/Biological_fitness" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological fitness">biological fitness</a> of a population known as <a href="/wiki/Inbreeding_depression" title="Inbreeding depression">inbreeding depression</a>, a major objective in clinical studies.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> While the risks of <a href="/wiki/Inbreeding" title="Inbreeding">inbreeding</a> are well-known, informing <a href="/wiki/Minority_group" title="Minority group">minority group</a> families with a <a href="/wiki/Tradition" title="Tradition">tradition</a> of <a href="/wiki/Endogamy" title="Endogamy">endogamy</a> and changing their behavior is a challenging task for <a href="/wiki/Genetic_counseling" title="Genetic counseling">genetic counseling</a> in the health care system. <sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The offspring of consanguineous relationships are at greater risk of certain genetic disorders. <a href="/wiki/Autosomal_recessive" class="mw-redirect" title="Autosomal recessive">Autosomal recessive</a> disorders occur in individuals who are <a href="/wiki/Homozygous" class="mw-redirect" title="Homozygous">homozygous</a> for a particular recessive gene <a href="/wiki/Mutation" title="Mutation">mutation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CBMCA920_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CBMCA920-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> This means that they carry two copies (<a href="/wiki/Alleles" class="mw-redirect" title="Alleles">alleles</a>) of the same gene.<sup id="cite_ref-CBMCA920_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CBMCA920-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Except in certain rare circumstances (new mutations or <a href="/wiki/Uniparental_disomy" title="Uniparental disomy">uniparental disomy</a>) both parents of an individual with such a disorder will be carriers of the gene.<sup id="cite_ref-CBMCA920_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CBMCA920-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Such carriers are not affected and will not display any signs that they are carriers, and so may be unaware that they carry the mutated gene. As relatives share a proportion of their genes, it is much more likely that related parents will be carriers of an autosomal recessive gene, and therefore their children are at a higher risk of an autosomal recessive disorder.<sup id="cite_ref-GACA138_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GACA138-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The extent to which the risk increases depends on the degree of genetic relationship between the parents; so the risk is greater in mating relationships where the parents are close relatives, but for relationships between more distant relatives, such as second cousins, the risk is lower (although still greater than the general population).<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Consanguinity in a population increases its susceptibility to infectious <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis" title="Hepatitis">hepatitis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
