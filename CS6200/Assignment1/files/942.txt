	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Dictionnaire philosophique</i></h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p>The <i><b>Dictionnaire philosophique</b></i> (<i><b>Philosophical Dictionary</b></i>) is an <a href="/wiki/Encyclopedic_dictionary" title="Encyclopedic dictionary">encyclopedic dictionary</a> published by <a href="/wiki/Voltaire" title="Voltaire">Voltaire</a> in 1764. The alphabetically arranged articles often criticize the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>, and other institutions. The first edition, released in June 1764, went by the name of <i>Dictionnaire philosophique portatif</i>. It was 344 pages and consisted of 73 articles. Later versions were expanded into two volumes consisting of 120 articles.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The first editions were published anonymously in <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a> by Gabriel Grasset. Due to the volatile content of the <i>Dictionnaire</i>, Voltaire chose Grasset over his usual publisher to ensure his own anonymity.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> There were many editions and reprints of the <i>Dictionnaire</i> during Voltaire's lifetime,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> but only four of them contained additions and modifications.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, another work published in 1770, <i>Questions sur l'Encyclopédie</i>, which contained reshaped and modified articles from the <a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A9die" title="Encyclopédie">Encyclopédie</a> always in alphabetical order, led many following editors to join this and the <i>Dictionnaire</i> (plus other minor works) in a unique opus.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
The <i>Dictionnaire</i> was a lifelong project for Voltaire. It represents the culmination of his views on <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morality" title="Morality">morality</a> and other subjects.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_origins">History and origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dictionnaire_philosophique&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Voltaire_-_Philospohical_dictionary.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Voltaire_-_Philospohical_dictionary.jpg/300px-Voltaire_-_Philospohical_dictionary.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="259" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Voltaire_-_Philospohical_dictionary.jpg/450px-Voltaire_-_Philospohical_dictionary.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/89/Voltaire_-_Philospohical_dictionary.jpg 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="432" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Voltaire_-_Philospohical_dictionary.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Voltaire; <i>A Philosophical Dictionary</i>. 2nd ed., London: Printed for J. and H.L. Hunt, 1824</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a> saw the creation of a new way of structuring <a href="/wiki/Information" title="Information">information</a> in <a href="/wiki/Book" title="Book">books</a>. The first work to employ this method was the <i><a href="/wiki/Dictionnaire_Historique_et_Critique" title="Dictionnaire Historique et Critique">Dictionnaire Historique et Critique</a></i> (1697) by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Bayle" title="Pierre Bayle">Pierre Bayle</a>, in which the information is ordered alphabetically. Other important works using a similar structure followed, such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A9die" title="Encyclopédie">Encyclopédie</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Denis_Diderot" title="Denis Diderot">Diderot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d%27Alembert" title="Jean le Rond d&#39;Alembert">Jean d'Alembert</a>. Having witnessed first-hand the popularity and many advantages of this form, Voltaire used this information while preparing the <i>Philosophical Dictionary</i> in 1752, although it was not completed until 1764.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Having had the opportunity to write his <i>Dictionary</i> at a later point in time, Voltaire saw that there were certain problems with previous dictionaries, chiefly that they were all lengthy, and thus very expensive and unaccessible for much of the population. Voltaire sought to create a text which would fit in one's pocket and be affordable because "revolutionary material must be small enough for people to carry with them".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> What he created is a text which educated and amused at the same time.<sup id="cite_ref-Voltaire_p._56_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voltaire_p._56-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Voltaire's motivations for writing the <i>Philosophical Dictionary</i> can be seen as serendipitous. The idea was spawned at a dinner party in the court of Prince <a href="/wiki/Frederick_the_Great" title="Frederick the Great">Frederick II of Prussia</a> in 1752, during which he and other guests each agreed to write an article and share them the next morning. Voltaire consequently was the only guest to take the game seriously and the idea cascaded to form the <i>Philosophical Dictionary</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Voltaire_p._56_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voltaire_p._56-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dictionnaire_philosophique&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>Philosophical Dictionary</i> is structured in the tradition of Bayle, Diderot and d'Alembert — that is to say, alphabetically ordered. Although this order helps readers more easily find articles, this was not meant to be a dictionary or encyclopaedia in the same totalizing way of d'Alembert's project. Voltaire's writing is neither objective nor varied in opinion; the same arguments are made throughout the <i>Philosophical Dictionary</i> emphasizing the point of his discontent.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Themes">Themes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dictionnaire_philosophique&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Themes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many of the themes addressed by Voltaire in this book are addressed or touched upon in his work <i>L'Infame</i>. In this and other works, Voltaire is very concerned about the injustices of the Catholic Church, which he sees as intolerant and fanatical. At the same time, his work espouses deism (although, in the entry <i><a href="/wiki/Theism" title="Theism">Theism</a></i>, he argues in favour of that which we actually mean as <i><a href="/wiki/Deism" title="Deism">deism</a></i>),  tolerance  and freedom of the press.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">Influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dictionnaire_philosophique&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because this work was written late in the author's life, coupled with the fact that it is compiled of articles written over an extended period of time, the work had many influences, which noticeably change, depending on the theme which the article addresses.
</p><p>The first major influence on the <i>Philosophical Dictionary</i> is Voltaire's visit to England, which gave him the opportunity to compare the problems in France with a place that had free press and to become better acquainted with important and influential thinkers such as Locke and Newton. Newtonian theory is influential in many of the articles that deal with tolerance stating that if we "do not know the essence of things we will not persecute others",<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> for those things.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Jean_Calas" title="Jean Calas">Calas affair</a> was an event that shaped Voltaire during the creation of the <i>Philosophical Dictionary</i>. Jean Calas was a <a href="/wiki/Calvinism" title="Calvinism">Calvinist</a> who was wrongfully convicted of killing his son. This occurred because there had been a rumour that the murder was fueled by Calas' son conversion to Catholicism. David de Beaudrige, who was in charge of the case, upon hearing this rumour, had the Calas family arrested without inquiry.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Voltaire used this theme in the <i>Philosophical Dictionary</i> to fight against the Catholic Church.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dictionnaire_philosophique&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The reception of the <i>Philosophical Dictionary</i> at the time was mixed. On one hand, the book saw a very strong reception on the part of the general public. The first edition was quickly sold out;<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> many more editions were needed to keep up with the demand. Enlightened rulers such as <a href="/wiki/Frederick_the_Great" title="Frederick the Great">Frederick II</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Prussia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Catherine_the_Great" title="Catherine the Great">Catherine II</a> of <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russia</a> both have been recorded as supporters of the book.
</p><p>On the other hand, the <i>Philosophical Dictionary</i> was despised by religious authorities, who had a very important influence over what works were to be censored. The <i>Philosophical Dictionary</i> was censored in many countries, including Switzerland (<a href="/wiki/Canton_of_Geneva" title="Canton of Geneva">Geneva</a>) and France.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In these countries, all available copies of the book were collected and burned in the town square.<sup id="cite_ref-Orieux,_p._409_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Orieux,_p._409-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Voltaire, who remained an anonymous author, was repeatedly asked if he minded that the <i>Portatif</i> was being burned, but he calmly replied that he had no reason to be upset.<sup id="cite_ref-Orieux,_p._409_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Orieux,_p._409-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
