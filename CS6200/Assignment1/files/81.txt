<p><b>Smith</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> originating in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. It is the most prevalent surname in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BRN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BRN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and the fifth most common surname in the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>. The surname Smith is particularly prevalent among those of English, Scottish and Irish descent,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> but is also a common surname among <a href="/wiki/African_American" class="mw-redirect" title="African American">African Americans</a>, which can be attributed either to <a href="/wiki/Black_slaves" class="mw-redirect" title="Black slaves">black slaves</a> being given the surname during slavery and never changing the name upon the end of the <a href="/wiki/Slave_trade" class="mw-redirect" title="Slave trade">era of slavery</a> and after the issuance of the <a href="/wiki/Emancipation_Proclamation" title="Emancipation Proclamation">Emancipation Proclamation</a> or to descendants of interracial marriages.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (June 2017)">dubious</span></a>&#32;<span class="metadata"> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Talk:Smith_(surname)#Occupation-based_names_common_among_freedmen_and_slaves" title="Talk:Smith (surname)">discuss</a></span></i>&#93;</sup> 2,376,206 Americans shared the surname Smith during the <a href="/wiki/2000_US_census" class="mw-redirect" title="2000 US census">2000 census</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and more than 500,000 people share it in the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  At the turn of the 20th century, the surname was sufficiently prevalent in England to have prompted the statement: "Common to every village in England, north, south, east and west";<sup id="cite_ref-Bardsley1901_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bardsley1901-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and sufficiently common on the (European) continent (in various forms) to be "common in most countries of Europe".<sup id="cite_ref-Anderson1863_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson1863-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_history">Etymology and history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smith_(surname)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name refers to a <a href="/wiki/Metalsmith" title="Metalsmith">smith</a>, originally deriving from <i>smið</i> or <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/smitan" class="extiw" title="wikt:smitan">smiþ</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> term meaning <i>one who works in metal</i> related to the word <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Smitan" class="extiw" title="wikt:Smitan">smitan</a></i>, the Old English form of <i>smite</i>, which also meant <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/strike" class="extiw" title="wikt:strike">strike</a></i> (as in early 17th century Biblical English: the verb "to smite" = to hit). The Old English word <i>smiþ</i> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" class="mw-redirect" title="Proto-Germanic">Proto-Germanic</a> word <i>smiþaz</i>. <i>Smithy</i> comes from the Old English word <i>smiðē</i> from the Proto-Germanic <i>smiðjon</i>. The use of <i>Smith</i> as an <a href="/wiki/Occupational_surname" class="mw-redirect" title="Occupational surname">occupational surname</a> dates back to <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon">Anglo-Saxon</a> times, when inherited surnames were still unknown: Ecceard Smith of <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">County Durham</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_East_England" title="North East England">North East England</a>, was recorded in 975.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the name is derived from a common occupation, many later Smiths had no connection to that occupation, but adopted or were given the surname precisely because of its commonness. For example:
</p>
<ul><li>It is common for people in English-speaking countries to adopt the surname Smith in order to maintain a <a href="/wiki/Secret_identity" title="Secret identity">secret identity</a>, when they wish to avoid being found. Smith is an extremely common name among English <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Gypsies</a>; see also <a href="/wiki/John_Smith_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="John Smith (disambiguation)">John Smith</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li>
<li>During the colonization of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, some <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Native Americans</a> took the name for use in dealing with colonists.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li>
<li>During the period of <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slavery</a> in the United States, many other slaves were known by the surname of their masters, or adopted those surnames upon their <a href="/wiki/Emancipation_Proclamation" title="Emancipation Proclamation">emancipation</a>.</li>
<li>During the <a href="/wiki/World_war" title="World war">world wars</a>, many <a href="/wiki/German_American" class="mw-redirect" title="German American">German Americans</a> <a href="/wiki/Anglicise" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglicise">anglicised</a> the common and equivalent German surname <i><a href="/wiki/Schmidt_(surname)" title="Schmidt (surname)">Schmidt</a></i> or <i>Schmitz</i> to <i>Smith</i> to avoid discrimination.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ellis_island_1902.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Ellis_island_1902.jpg/200px-Ellis_island_1902.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="137" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Ellis_island_1902.jpg/300px-Ellis_island_1902.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Ellis_island_1902.jpg/400px-Ellis_island_1902.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1185" data-file-height="812" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ellis_island_1902.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Immigrants arriving at <a href="/wiki/Ellis_Island" title="Ellis Island">Ellis Island</a>; some chose more "American" surnames, like "Smith", on arrival.</div></div></div>
<p>A popular misconception holds that at the beginning of the 20th century, when many new immigrants were entering the U.S., <a href="/wiki/Civil_servant" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil servant">civil servants</a> at <a href="/wiki/Ellis_Island" title="Ellis Island">Ellis Island</a> responsible for cataloging the entry of such persons sometimes arbitrarily assigned new surnames if the immigrants' original surname was particularly lengthy, or difficult for the processor to spell or pronounce.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> While such claims are likely vastly exaggerated,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> many immigrants did choose to begin their American lives with more "American" names, particularly with <a href="/wiki/Anglicised" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglicised">Anglicised</a> versions of their birth names; the German <i>Schmidt</i> was often Anglicized to <i>Smith</i> not only during the <a href="/wiki/World_war" title="World war">world wars</a>, but also commonly in times of peace, and the equivalent <a href="/wiki/Polish_American" class="mw-redirect" title="Polish American">Polish</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Kowalski" title="Kowalski">Kowalski</a></i> was Anglicized to <i>Smith</i> as well.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographical_distribution">Geographical distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smith_(surname)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geographical distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of 2014, 64.3% of all known bearers of the surname <i>Smith</i> were residents of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> (frequency 1:121), 13.7% of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> (1:88), 4.2% of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> (1:191), 4.0% of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> (1:130), 3.9% of <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> (1:295), 1.4% of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> (1:84) and 1.0% of <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a> (1:62).
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, the frequency of the surname was higher than average (1:84) in the following council areas:
</p>
<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 22em; -webkit-column-width: 22em; column-width: 22em;">

<li>2. <a href="/wiki/Outer_Hebrides" title="Outer Hebrides">Outer Hebrides</a> (1:46)</li>
<li>3. <a href="/wiki/Moray" title="Moray">Moray</a> (1:49)</li>
<li>4. <a href="/wiki/Angus,_Scotland" title="Angus, Scotland">Angus</a> (1:50)</li>

<li>6. <a href="/wiki/Dundee" title="Dundee">Dundee</a> (1:70)</li>
<li>7. <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen" title="Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a> (1:71)</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations">Variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smith_(surname)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Variations of the surname Smith also remain very common. These include different spellings of the English term, and versions in other languages.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="English_variations">English variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smith_(surname)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: English variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is some disagreement about the origins of the numerous variations of the name Smith. The addition of an <i>e</i> at the end of the name is sometimes considered an affectation, but may have arisen either as an attempt to spell <i>smithy</i> or as the Middle English adjectival form of <i>smith</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-cottle_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cottle-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> which would have been used in surnames based on location rather than occupation (in other words, for someone living near or at the smithy).<sup id="cite_ref-Lower1860_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lower1860-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Likewise, the replacement of the <i>i</i> with a <i>y</i> in <i>Smyth</i> or <i>Smythe</i> is also often considered an affectation but may have originally occurred because of the difficulty of reading <a href="/wiki/Blackletter" title="Blackletter">blackletter</a> text, where <i>Smith</i> might look like <i>Snuth</i> or <i>Simth</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-cottle_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cottle-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> However, Charles Bardsley wrote in 1901, "The y in Smyth is the almost invariable spelling in early rolls, so that it cannot exactly be styled a modern affectation."<sup id="cite_ref-Bardsley1901_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bardsley1901-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Some variants (such as <a href="/wiki/Smijth" class="mw-redirect" title="Smijth">Smijth</a>) were adopted by individuals for personal reasons, while others may have arisen independently or as offshoots from the <i>Smith</i> root. Names such as <a href="/wiki/Smither" title="Smither">Smither</a> and <a href="/wiki/Smithers_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Smithers (name)">Smithers</a> may in some cases be variants of <i>Smith</i> but in others independent surnames based on a meaning of <i>light and active</i> attributed to <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/smyther" class="extiw" title="wikt:smyther">smyther</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lower1860_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lower1860-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Additional derivatives include Smithman, Smithson and Smithfield (see below).<sup id="cite_ref-Lower1860_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lower1860-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Athersmith may derive from <i>at the Smith</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Other variations focus on specialisms within the profession; for example <a href="/wiki/Blacksmith" title="Blacksmith">Blacksmith</a>, from those who worked predominantly with iron, <a href="/wiki/Whitesmith" title="Whitesmith">Whitesmith</a>, from those who worked with tin (and the more obvious <a href="/wiki/Tinsmith" title="Tinsmith">Tinsmith</a>), Brownsmith and Redsmith, from those who worked with copper (<a href="/wiki/Coppersmith" title="Coppersmith">Coppersmith</a> and Greensmith; copper is green when oxidised), <a href="/wiki/Silversmith" title="Silversmith">Silversmith</a> and <a href="/wiki/Goldsmith" title="Goldsmith">Goldsmith</a> – and those based on the goods produced, such as Hammersmith, <a href="/wiki/Bladesmith" title="Bladesmith">Bladesmith</a>, Naismith (<a href="/wiki/Nail_(fastener)" title="Nail (fastener)">nail</a>-smith), Arrowsmith which in turn was shortened to Arsmith,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Shoesmith" title="Shoesmith">Shoesmith</a> (referring to <a href="/wiki/Horseshoe" title="Horseshoe">horseshoes</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Lower1860_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lower1860-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sixsmith" title="Sixsmith">Sixsmith</a> is variant spelling of a <a href="/wiki/Sickle" title="Sickle">sickle</a>- or <a href="/wiki/Scythe" title="Scythe">scythe</a>-smith.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Wildsmith in turn is a corruption of wheelsmith<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a> practice of attaching <i>son</i> to the end of a name to indicate that the bearer is the child of the original holder has also led to the surnames <a href="/wiki/Smithson" title="Smithson">Smithson</a> and Smisson. Historically, "Smitty" has been a common nickname given to someone with the surname, Smith; in some instances, this usage has passed into "Smitty" being used as a surname itself.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, the word for smith, <i>gobha</i>, is found in the surname "MacGouren"/<i>MacGouran</i>/<i>MacGowan</i>/<i><a href="/wiki/McGowan" title="McGowan">McGowan</a></i>.
