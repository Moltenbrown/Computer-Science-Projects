<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methodology">Methodology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Methodology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quantitative">Quantitative</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Quantitative">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the best known sources for gathering both historical and contemporary data on families is the national <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census survey.</a> In the United States, the national census occurs in every household every 10 years. There are smaller surveys taken in between called the <a href="/wiki/American_Community_Survey" title="American Community Survey">American Community Survey</a>. Both are held by the larger <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">U.S. Census Bureau</a> and its related subsidiaries in each state. The Census Bureau collects data about American families for the nation, states and communities. Their data provides statistics on trends in household and family composition, and show the number of children, young adults and couples living in the United States. Their wave on Families and Living Arrangements is organized into clusters: childcare, children, <a href="/wiki/Child_support_in_the_United_States" title="Child support in the United States">child suppor</a>t, families and households, fertility, grandparents and grandchildren, marriage and divorce, and <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_relationship" title="Same-sex relationship">same-sex couples</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Qualitative">Qualitative</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Qualitative">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another method is <a href="/wiki/Ethnographic" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethnographic">ethnographic</a> or participatory observation research of families, which usually reduces the <a href="/wiki/Sample_size_determination" title="Sample size determination">sample size</a> to have a more intimate analysis of the <a href="/wiki/Conjugal_family" title="Conjugal family">conjugal</a> or other family structure. In general, a qualitative approach to research is an excellent way to investigate group dynamics and family relationships. Specifically, qualitative research on the topic of families is particularly useful when looking at: 1) deeper meanings about family interactions and relationships 2) learning more about the insider views about relational processes and observing interactions 3) looking at the family from within a greater context and 4) providing a voice for marginalized family members (e.g. case of abuse). Often, <a href="/wiki/Qualitative_data" class="mw-redirect" title="Qualitative data">qualitative data</a> is able to provide ample data that is rich and meaningful, especially for structurally diverse families.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sociology_of_interracial_intimacy">Sociology of interracial intimacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sociology of interracial intimacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The construction of race in Western society and, to a degree, globally, has led to a distinct view of <a href="/wiki/Interracial_marriage" title="Interracial marriage">interracial</a> intimacy. Although interracial relationships and marriages have become far more popular and socially acceptable in the United States and Western Europe since the Civil Rights era, these unions continue to be viewed with less than total acceptance by significant portions of the population. More historically, <i>American Families</i> by <a href="/wiki/Stephanie_Coontz" title="Stephanie Coontz">Stephanie Coontz</a> treats the difficulties these couples went through during the time before <i><a href="/wiki/Loving_v._Virginia" title="Loving v. Virginia">Loving v. Virginia</a></i>, when interracial marriage bans were declared unconstitutional. These bans functioned to enforce the <a href="/wiki/One-drop_rule" title="One-drop rule">one-drop rule</a> and reenforce <a href="/wiki/Racial_identity" class="mw-redirect" title="Racial identity">identity</a> and privilege. Internationally, the <a href="/wiki/Far_right" class="mw-redirect" title="Far right">far right</a> continues to promote ideas of <a href="/wiki/Racial_hygiene" title="Racial hygiene">racial purity</a> by working against the normalization of interracial couples and families.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-modern_family_life_and_religious_discourse">Pre-modern family life and religious discourse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pre-modern family life and religious discourse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Historically, religious discourses have played a significant role in constituting family members and constructing particular forms of behavior in families, and religion has been particularly important in discourses on female sexuality. An example of the role of religion in this respect was the'witchcraft craze' in Medieval Europe. According to Turner,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> this was a device to regulate the behavior of women, and the attack on women as witches was principally 'a critique of their sexuality'. 'Women were closely associated with witchcraft, because it was argued that they were particularly susceptible to the sexual advances of the devil. ...Women were seen to be irrational, emotional and lacking in self-restraint; they were especially vulnerable to satanic temptation.'
</p><p>Turner argues that attempts to regulate female sexuality through religious discourse have, in the case of Western Europe, to be understood in the context of concerns about managing private property and ensuring its continuity. Thus, for the land-owning aristocracy, the point of marriage was to produce a male heir to the property of the household. Since child mortality was common, women had to be more or less continuously pregnant during their marriage to guarantee a living male heir. Furthermore, this heir had to be legitimate, if disputes over inheritance were to be avoided. This legitimacy could only be ensured by the heads of households marrying virgins and ensuring the chastity of their wives for the duration of the marriage. Equally,daughters had to be sexually pure if they were to be eligible for marriage to other property-holding families. Such marriages were prompted solely by the need to produce children and had none of the elements of eroticism and sexual compatibility of contemporary marriages.
</p><p>In pre-modern Europe, these interests were reflected in the character of marriages. They were private, arranged contracts that could be easily dissolved in the event of child production being compromised by the woman's infertility or infidelity. With the entry of the Church into marriage arrangements, different definitions of marriage emerged. Lifelong marriages were demanded, but with a concern to regulate sexuality, particularly the sexuality of women.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sociology_of_marriage">Sociology of marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sociology of marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the year 2015, <a href="/wiki/Judeo-Christian" title="Judeo-Christian">Judeo-Christian</a> <a href="/wiki/Belief_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Belief system">belief system</a> marriage is modeled after <a href="/wiki/Adam_and_Eve" title="Adam and Eve">Adam and Eve</a>'s lifetime commitment between man and woman. The married couple produces children, constituting the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_family" title="Nuclear family">nuclear family</a>. Some sociologists now dispute the degree to which this idealized arrangement has and does reflect the true structure of families in American society. In her 1995 article <i>The American Family and the Nostalgia Trap</i>, sociologist <a href="/wiki/Stephanie_Coontz" title="Stephanie Coontz">Stephanie Coontz</a> first posited that the <a href="/wiki/American_family" class="mw-redirect" title="American family">American family</a> has always been defined first and foremost by its economic needs. For instance, in colonial times families often relied on slaves or indentured servants to support themselves economically. The modern "breadwinner-<a href="/wiki/Homemaker" class="mw-redirect" title="Homemaker">homemaker</a> model", argues Coontz, then has little historical basis. Only in the 1950s did the myth of the happy, nuclear family as the correct family structure arise.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>"The modern family is increasingly complex and has changed profoundly, with greater acceptance for unmarried cohabitation, divorce, single-parent families, same-sex partnerships and complex extended family relations. Grandparents are also doing their bit."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>To paraphrase the quote, family structure is changing drastically and there is a vast variety of different family structures.
</p><p>Yet Coontz argues in <i>Marriage, A History</i> that during the 20th century, marriages have become <a href="/wiki/Divorce_in_the_United_States" title="Divorce in the United States">increasingly unstable</a> in the United States as individuals have begun to seek unions for the ideals of love and affection rather than social or economic expediency.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> This transition has blurred the division of labor within the breadwinner-homemaker model, such that maintenance of the household and childcare, called the "second shift", are now topics for debate between marital partners. Sociologist <a href="/wiki/Arlie_Russell_Hochschild" title="Arlie Russell Hochschild">Arlie Russell Hochschild</a> argues in <i>The Second Shift</i> that despite changes in perceptions of the purpose of marriage and the economic foundations for marriage, women continue to do the bulk of care work to the detriment of the American family. Hochschild illustrates the ways in which an unequal division of the second shift undermines family welfare by reducing marital equality and spousal satisfaction.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Families and marriage have increasingly become areas where gender matters. However, it is important to note that gender differences in marriage have too often been perceived as merely an "individual struggle and depoliticized by reducing social inequalities to differences".<sup id="cite_ref-Ferree2010_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferree2010-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>422</span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_marriage_market:_determining_who_marries_whom">The marriage market: determining who marries whom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The marriage market: determining who marries whom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are many theoretical models to describe how people determine who to marry. An important gender-focused approach is an <a href="/wiki/Intersectional" class="mw-redirect" title="Intersectional">intersectional</a> approach that combines education level and gender. Men and women operate in a "marriage market" that is influenced by many competing factors. One of the most decisive factors is education level. Studies have shown that men and women tend to marry partners that have attained a level of education similar to their own. In the study by Bruze, Svarer, and Weiss, low education is defined as a high school education or less, medium education is defined as vocational education, and high education is defined as a college education (2015).<sup id="cite_ref-Bruze2015_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bruze2015-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Marriages" class="mw-redirect" title="Marriages">Marriages</a> depend on the expectations of two people and are "formed and terminated" based on those expectations. Consequently, individuals "are selected into and out of the marriage market on the basis of their education".<sup id="cite_ref-Bruze2015_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bruze2015-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The most distinct marital trend is that men with low education are slowly selected out of the marriage market, instead remaining single. The driving force behind this process is that a marriage in which both partners or only the husband have low education end with divorce at a substantially higher rate than marriages where both partners or the husband do not have low education. Young women with medium education levels tend to have the highest rates of marriage. Highly educated men tend to marry highly educated women. Moreover, men and women who have attained high levels of education delay marriage past the age when other individuals typically marry. This trend becomes stronger with age: the proportion of married with high educations who are married to women with similarly high educations reaches 64% when the men are 46 years old.<sup id="cite_ref-Bruze2015_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bruze2015-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another important intersectional factor to consider in relation to gender and marriage is marriage markets.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Marriage market means how economics affects who marries, whose bonds endure and what this means for future generations of workers and parents. Analyzing marriage markets as they pertain to marriage has several benefits. First, marriage market conditions are forces that influence marriage from outside they subjects affect, which means they impact the general trends of marriage decisions.<sup id="cite_ref-Harknett2011_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harknett2011-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>42</span></sup> In other words, individual circumstances cause people to make decisions about their marriages that might be specific to their personal situation; marriage markets impact all peoples' decisions about marriage from a macro level, which means stead incomes and job make for sound marriage. In addition, Job stability benefits both employers through greater productivity and families though more cohesion. Second, marriage market conditions may capture many economic influences.<sup id="cite_ref-Harknett2011_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harknett2011-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>43</span></sup> Empirical findings indicate that financial stability is an important requisite for marriage. In weak marriage markets (when there is high unemployment) couples who would like to get married may delay doing so due to unemployment or financial troubles. Furthermore, even couples that are already married may face doubts about the future economic status of themselves or their partners, which can create marital instability. Conversely, strong labor markets (when unemployment is low) may improve the employment situation and financial situation of either partner, which may facilitate marriage and increase economic stability.<sup id="cite_ref-Harknett2011_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harknett2011-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>44</span></sup> Thus, when marriage markets are strong and unemployment is low, marriage may be perceived as more attractive to individuals than when marriage markets and weak and unemployment is high.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intersection_of_class_and_gender">Intersection of class and gender</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Intersection of class and gender">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">Social class</a> interacts with gender to impact the male-female dynamic in marriage, particularly with respect to "temporal flexibility at work and home".<sup id="cite_ref-Gertsel2014_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gertsel2014-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>397</span></sup> Research shows that class advantaged men and women use their class privilege and the flexibility it provides them in ways that support conventional <a href="/wiki/Gender_roles" class="mw-redirect" title="Gender roles">gender roles</a>. Conversely, men and women who do not have access to such flexibility and control of their time are pressured to weaken conventional gender expectations regarding marriage, family, and jobs. Gertsel and Clawson conducted a study in which they collected data from four groups of paid care workers, divided by class and gender (2014).<sup id="cite_ref-Gertsel2014_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gertsel2014-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The two class-advantaged groups were <a href="/wiki/Nurses" class="mw-redirect" title="Nurses">nurses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">doctors</a>. The nurses were almost exclusively women and the doctors were almost exclusively men. This group had a number of choices about work hours and their ability to utilize family-friendly workplace policies. The two class-disadvantaged groups were female <a href="/wiki/Nursing_assistants" class="mw-redirect" title="Nursing assistants">nursing assistants</a> (CNAs) and male <a href="/wiki/Emergency_medical_technicians" class="mw-redirect" title="Emergency medical technicians">emergency medical technicians</a> (EMTs). The class-disadvantaged group had fewer choices regarding their work hours and faced greater constraints in flexibility and control of their time. Women in particular need flexible work hours in order to meet the inflexible demands that marriage and a family place upon them, as traditional gender expectations stipulate that the woman be the primary caregiver.<sup id="cite_ref-Gertsel2014_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gertsel2014-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>405</span></sup> The results of this study demonstrate that class, intersecting with gender, influences the ability of men and women to obtain and utilize flexibility with their time.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Furthermore, gender shapes the particular variety of flexibility demanded. In advantaged occupations, both men and women are able to acquire the flexibility they so desire. However, they choose to use the control that this affords them in different manners. Women cut back on paid work hours and take leaves to handle domestic labor and child-care. In other words, they make job sacrifices. On the other hand, men are less likely to utilize family-friendly policies to make work sacrifices; they spend less at home and more time working. In essence, both men and women of class-advantaged occupations use the flexibility that their status provides them to "enact neotraditional gender expectations".<sup id="cite_ref-Gertsel2014_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gertsel2014-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>424</span></sup>
</p><p>Class-disadvantaged men and women do not have the same temporal flexibility that allows them to make decisions on how to allocate their time. They face stricter constraints on their work hours and policies, thus making it impossible for them to choose whether to spend more time at work or more time at home. For example, even if a class-disadvantaged woman wanted to spend less time at work and more time with her children or in the home, she might not be able due to the inability to get time off from work or take a leave of absence.<sup id="cite_ref-Gertsel2014_19-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gertsel2014-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Thus, class-disadvantage makes it more difficult for both men and women to adhere to traditional gender expectations. The researchers showed that class advantage is used to "do gender" in traditional ways, while class disadvantage may lead to a violation of traditional gender expectations in a way that "undoes gender".<sup id="cite_ref-Gertsel2014_19-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gertsel2014-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>431</span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gender_and_work-family_balance_in_marriage">Gender and work-family balance in marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Gender and work-family balance in marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Research indicates that three principal factors predict how well men and women perceive their work-life balance in marriage: job characteristics, family characteristics, and spillover between work and family.<sup id="cite_ref-Keene2004_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Keene2004-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>2</span></sup> Job characteristics determine workers' freedom to balance multiple demands and obligations in their marriage. As demonstrated by Gertsel and Clawson, higher-level occupations are generally more accommodating to family life than are lower level occupations (2014).<sup id="cite_ref-Gertsel2014_19-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gertsel2014-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, the number of hours worked and the work spillover into family life are the most telling predictors of perceived imbalance in marriage. Keene and Quadagno found a greater likelihood of perceived imbalance when work duties caused men or women to miss a family event or make it difficult to maintain their home (2004).<sup id="cite_ref-Keene2004_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Keene2004-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Additional research by Keene and Quadagno suggests that the gender expectations that men should prioritize their work lives and women should prioritize their marriage and home life no longer exist.<sup id="cite_ref-Keene2004_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Keene2004-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> However, there persists an unequal division of labor in the home between men and women. One theoretical approach to explain this concept is the "gender similarity" approach, which "predicts that the convergence in men's and women's work and family demands should lead to a convergence in attitudes toward work and family responsibilities and feelings of work-family balance".<sup id="cite_ref-Keene2004_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Keene2004-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4</span></sup> In contrast, the "gender differences" approach stipulates that "normative differences between men and women remain, with the family still primarily defined as women's sphere and paid work as men's domain".<sup id="cite_ref-Keene2004_20-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Keene2004-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4</span></sup> There is empirical evidence in support of both theories. Some research supports the convergence of men's and women's work experiences: both men and women make adjustments in their marriage and personal lives to meet their employer's expectations, while also making adjustments at work to maintain their marital and family obligations. However, the analysis from the abovementioned study supports the gender differences model. Gender differences exist in the division of household labor and chores, with men working more hours and women spending more time on domestic and child-care responsibilities.<sup id="cite_ref-Keene2004_20-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Keene2004-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Divorce">Divorce</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Divorce">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trends">Trends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Trends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Divorce" title="Divorce">divorce</a> rate in western countries has generally increased over time. Divorce rates have however started to decrease over the last twenty years.  In the US, divorce rate changed from l.2 per 1000 marriages in 1860 to 3.0, 4.0 and 7.7 in 1890, 1900, 1920 then to, 5.3, 4.7, 4.1 and 3.7 per 1000 marriages in 1979, 1990, 2000 and 2004 respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> People are less inclined to stay in unhappy relationships to keep the family unit intact and maintain consistency in their children's lives, the way previous generations did.
</p><p>Divorce rates in Canada and the United States fluctuated in a similar pattern, though the United States still has the highest divorce rate in the world (50% higher than Canada's).<sup id="cite_ref-Macionis_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macionis-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The following are several possible causes for the increased rate of divorce:
</p>
<ol><li>Individualism: In today's society, families spend more time apart than they do together. Some individuals in a family focus more on personal happiness and earning income to support their family that it consumes the time actual spent with their family.</li>
<li>Feelings are no longer mutual: Many people end marriages because they are no longer satisfied by sexual needs or merely because they have lost feelings for one another. This often happens when one partner finds a more exciting relationship and chooses to move forward with that new relationship. In some cases, a partner may even commit adultery which also may result in a divorce as a partner discovers their partner being unfaithful to them.</li>
<li>Women have become more independent: Now that women have equal rights and have proven over time that they have the potential and ability to support themselves, women find it much easier to leave unhappy marriages. They are also more work focused, thus giving them less time to cope with their relationship.</li>
<li>Stress: Stress is a big factor in marriages. Working to support a family, while trying to stabilize finances is a big factor of stress. Also, with both partners working (in most cases), leaves less "family time" which makes raising children difficult. This often happens in the stage where couples are raising young children.</li>
<li>Socially acceptable: In today's generation, divorce is now more socially acceptable. Now, instead of discouraging a divorce in an unsatisfying relationship, it is more widely accepted and sometimes even encouraged. Not only is it now more acceptable, but it is also easier to get a divorce legally than it was in previous years according to the Divorce Act of 1968.</li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parents:_Falling_out_of_love">Parents: Falling out of love</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Parents: Falling out of love">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many scholars have attempted to explain why humans enter relationships, stay in relationships and end relationships. Levinger's (1965, 1976) theory on divorce is based on a theoretical tradition consisting of three basic components: attractions, barriers and alternatives.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Attraction in this theory is proportional to the rewards one gets from the relationship minus the cost of the relationship. All the things that can be seen as gains from the relationship such as love, sex, companionship, emotional support and daily assistance are the rewards of the relationship. The costs would the negative aspects of the relationship such as <a href="/wiki/Domestic_violence" title="Domestic violence">domestic violence</a>, infidelity, quarrels and limitations on personal freedom. Generally people tend to stay in high rewards and low cost relationships. However, the reverse situation, that is, a costly marriage with few benefits does not automatically lead to divorce. Couples must overcome barriers such as religious beliefs, social stigma, and financial dependence or law restrictions before they successfully dissolve their marriage.
</p><p>Another theory to explain why relationships end is the "Mate ejection theory", by Brian Boutwell, J.C. Barnes and K.M Beaver.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The Mate ejection theory looks at the dissolution of marriage from an evolutionary point of view, where all species seek to successfully reproduce. According to this theory there are gender differences in the process of ejection. For example, a woman will be more upset when her husband emotionally cheats on her and a man will be more upset when his wife physically cheats on him. The reason for this stems from evolutionary roots, a man emotionally cheating on his wife equates to a loss or reduction in resources for the wife to raise the children whereas an act of physical <a href="/wiki/Infidelity" title="Infidelity">infidelity</a> by the wife threatens the husband's chance to pass on his genes to the next generation via reproduction. Both these circumstances call for mate ejection. "Ancestral conditions that favored the dissolution of a mateship constituted a recurrent adaptive problem over human evolutionary history and thus imposed selection pressures for the evolution of strategic solutions." Put differently, the capability of emancipating themselves from certain relationships could have conferred a fitness benefit for ancestral humans.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_of_divorce_on_children">Effect of divorce on children</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Effect of divorce on children">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Three <a href="/wiki/Longitudinal_studies" class="mw-redirect" title="Longitudinal studies">longitudinal studies</a> on divorce: The Marin County Project (the clinical study of 60 families that began in 1971), The Virginia County Study (a series of longitudinal studies on marriage, divorce and remarriage) and The Binuclear Family Studies of 98 families have helped expand the literature on divorce. The Binuclear study was based on the findings from the Marin County Project and Virginia County Study.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> This research has been used to understand the <a href="/wiki/Implications_of_divorce" class="mw-redirect" title="Implications of divorce">implications of divorce</a> on children later on in life.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Judith_Wallerstein" title="Judith Wallerstein">Judith Wallerstein</a>, an influential psychologist's research on the effect of divorce (based on the Marin County Project), on children suggests that, "children with divorced parents often reach adulthood as psychologically troubled individuals who find it difficult to maintain satisfying relationships with others". A lot of quantitative research done by other scholars agrees with Wallerstein's conclusion. It has been shown that the children with divorced parents have an increased risk of: experiencing psychological problems, having troubled marriages, divorcing and having poor relationships with parents especially the father. Wallerstein, however, has a disputed 'extreme version' of her theory where she claims that the difference between the children with divorced and continuously married parents is dramatic and pervasive.
</p><p>One such opponent of Wallerstein's extreme theory is Mavis Hetherington who argues that the negative effects of divorce on children have been exaggerated and that most children grow up without long-term harm. Hetherington's data showed that 25% of children with divorced parents reach adulthood with a serious social, emotional or psychological problem, compared to 10% of children with continuously married parents. 75% of the children grow up to be well-functioning adults.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Twenty years after the 98 families from the binuclear study were interviewed; the offspring from these families were interviewed. Eighty-five percent of the offspring were interviewed and out of those, 23% had completed postgraduate training, 33% had completed college, 31% had completed post-secondary training, 10% had received their high school diplomas and the majority (85%) of the interviewed children were employed.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sociology_of_motherhood">Sociology of motherhood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Sociology of motherhood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_theories_surrounding_motherhood">Contemporary theories surrounding motherhood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Contemporary theories surrounding motherhood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mothering" class="mw-redirect" title="Mothering">Mothering</a> is the social practice of nurturing and caring for dependent children. It is a dynamic process of social interactions and relationships. Mothering is typically associated with women since it is typically women who mother their children. However, "not all women mother, and mothering as nurturing and caring work is not inevitable the exclusive domain of women".<sup id="cite_ref-Investigating_Motherhood_2000_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Investigating_Motherhood_2000-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Some argue that Mothering as a female role is a <a href="/wiki/Social_construction" class="mw-redirect" title="Social construction">social construction</a> that is highly influenced by gender belief systems. The roles associated with motherhood are variable across time and culture.<sup id="cite_ref-Investigating_Motherhood_2000_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Investigating_Motherhood_2000-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Universalist_approach">Universalist approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Universalist approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The universalist approach to motherhood is aimed at conceptualizing the work that mothers do. This approach identifies mothers through what they do, rather than how they feel. Mothers share a set of activities known as "maternal practice", that are universal, even though they vary as individuals and across cultures. These activities include nurturing, protecting, and training their children. An individual's mothering actions are shaped by their beliefs about family, individuality, the nature of childhood, and the nature of their child. These are also often shaped by their own childhood and past experiences with children. The dynamic interactions between the mother and child create deep and meaningful connections.<sup id="cite_ref-Investigating_Motherhood_2000_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Investigating_Motherhood_2000-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Particularistic_approach">Particularistic approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Particularistic approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The particularistic approach to mothering suggests that the role of a mother, their activities, and understandings cannot be separated from the context in which they live. According to this theory, mothering takes place within "<i>specific historical contexts framed by interlocking structures of race, class, and gender'<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, a mother's strategies and meanings that she develops are influenced by different social locations, such as the intersections of regional and local political economy with class, ethnicity, culture, and sexual preference.<sup id="cite_ref-Investigating_Motherhood_2000_28-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Investigating_Motherhood_2000-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup></i>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conventional_notions_of_motherhood">Conventional notions of motherhood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Conventional notions of motherhood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Motherhood ideology is influenced by the idealization of the family structure and perpetuates the image of a heterosexual couple with children.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Some sociologists refer to this as the "<a href="/wiki/Bourgeois" class="mw-redirect" title="Bourgeois">bourgeois</a> family", which arose out of typical 16th- and 17th-century European households and is often considered the "traditional Western" structure. In this family model the father acts as the economic support and sometimes disciplinarian of the family, while the <a href="/wiki/Mother" title="Mother">mother</a> or other female relative oversees most of the child-rearing.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western</a> traditional families, fathers were the heads of the families, which meant that his duties included providing financial support and making critical decisions, some of which must have been obeyed without question by the rest of the family members.  "Some Asian American men are brought up under stringent gender role expectations such as a focus on group harmony and filial piety, carrying on their family name and conforming to the expectations of the parents."<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The mother's role in the family is celebrated on <a href="/wiki/Mother%27s_Day" title="Mother&#39;s Day">Mother's Day</a>. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Reeves_Jarvis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Anna Reeves Jarvis (page does not exist)">Anna Reeves Jarvis</a> was a woman who originally organized Mother's Work Day's protesting the lack of cleanliness and sanitation in the work place.<sup id="cite_ref-slate.com_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-slate.com-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Jarvis died in 1905 and her daughter created a National Mother's Day to honor her mother.<sup id="cite_ref-slate.com_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-slate.com-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Mothers frequently have a very important role in raising offspring and the title can be given to a non-biological mother that fills this role. This is common in <a href="/wiki/Stepfamily" title="Stepfamily">stepmothers</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deviancy_discourses">Deviancy discourses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Deviancy discourses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are many cultural contradictions and diverse arrangements and practices that challenge the intensive mothering ideology. However, they are considered deviant discourses since they do not conform to the script of full-time motherhood in the context of marriage. These include single mothers, welfare mothers, minority mothers, immigrant mothers, and lesbian mothers. These types of motherhood categories are not mutually exclusive.<sup id="cite_ref-Investigating_Motherhood_2000_28-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Investigating_Motherhood_2000-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, women who cannot or choose not to be mothers deal with many internal and external pressures.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Motherhood_Mandate_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Motherhood_Mandate-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Motherhood_statistics">Motherhood statistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Motherhood statistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the United States, 82.5 million women are mothers of all ages, while the national average age of first child births is 25.1 years.  In 2008, 10% of births were to <a href="/wiki/Teenage_pregnancy" title="Teenage pregnancy">teenage girls</a>, and 14% were to women ages 35 and older.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> In the United States, a study found that the average woman spends 5 years working and building a career before having children, and mothers working non-salary jobs began having children at age 27, compared to mothers with salary positions, who became pregnant at age 31.<sup id="cite_ref-forbes.com_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbes.com-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The study shows that the difference in age of child birth is related to education, since the longer a woman has been in school, the older she will be when she enters the workforce.<sup id="cite_ref-forbes.com_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbes.com-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sociology_of_fatherhood">Sociology of fatherhood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Sociology of fatherhood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vater_mit_Toechtern_03.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Vater_mit_Toechtern_03.jpg/220px-Vater_mit_Toechtern_03.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Vater_mit_Toechtern_03.jpg/330px-Vater_mit_Toechtern_03.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Vater_mit_Toechtern_03.jpg/440px-Vater_mit_Toechtern_03.jpg 2x" data-file-width="472" data-file-height="317" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vater_mit_Toechtern_03.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Father with his two daughters in relationship</div></div></div>
<p>According to anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Godelier" title="Maurice Godelier">Maurice Godelier</a>, a critical novelty in human society, compared to humans' closest biological relatives (<a href="/wiki/Common_chimpanzee" class="mw-redirect" title="Common chimpanzee">chimpanzees</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bonobos" class="mw-redirect" title="Bonobos">bonobos</a>), is the parental role assumed by the males, which were unaware of their "father" connection.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In many cultures, especially traditional western, a father is usually the husband in a <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couple</a>. Many times fathers have a very important role in raising offspring and the title can be given to a non-biological father that fills this role. This is common in <a href="/wiki/Stepfamily" title="Stepfamily">stepfathers</a> (males married to biological mothers).
In <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western</a> traditional families, fathers are the heads of the families, which means that their duties include providing financial support and making critical decisions, some of which must be obeyed without question by the rest of the family members.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>As with cultural concepts of family, the specifics of a father's role vary according to cultural <a href="/wiki/Folkways_(sociology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Folkways (sociology)">folkways</a>. In what some sociologists term the "<a href="/wiki/Bourgeois" class="mw-redirect" title="Bourgeois">bourgeois</a> family", which arose out of typical 16th- and 17th-century European households, the father's role has been somewhat limited. In this family model the father acts as the economic support and sometimes disciplinarian of the family, while the <a href="/wiki/Mother" title="Mother">mother</a> or other female relative oversees most of the child-rearing. This structure is enforced, for example, in societies which legislate "<a href="/wiki/Maternity_leave" class="mw-redirect" title="Maternity leave">maternity leave</a>" but do not have a corresponding "<a href="/wiki/Paternity_leave" class="mw-redirect" title="Paternity leave">paternity leave</a>".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>However, this limited role has increasingly been called into question. Since the 1950s, social scientists as well as feminists have increasingly criticized gendered arrangements of work and care, and the male breadwinner role, and policies are increasingly targeting men as fathers, as a tool of changing gender relations.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_of_parenting">Science of parenting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Science of parenting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Described as 'the science of male parenting', the study of 'father craft' emerged principally in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a> and the United States (but also throughout <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>) in the 1920s. "Male adjuncts to <a href="/wiki/Maternity" class="mw-redirect" title="Maternity">Maternity</a> and Infant Welfare Centers â€“ reacted to the maternal dominance in infant welfare and parenting in interwar Britain by arguing that fathers should play a crucial role in the upbringing of children."<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Were such a study to be conducted into the science of female parenting, it would be called mother craft.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The words 'ma ma' and 'mom', usually regarded as <a href="/wiki/Terms_of_endearment" class="mw-redirect" title="Terms of endearment">terms of endearment</a> directed towards a mother figure, are generally one of the first words a child speaks. While 'da da' or 'dad' often precede it, this does not reflect a stronger bond between the father and child than that of the mother and child, it is merely simpler to pronounce than 'mummy' or 'mum' which require greater control over the mouth muscles.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Children tend to remember 'daddy' more because, according to research, they are more exciting to the child.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_theories">Contemporary theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Contemporary theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A number of studies have been given to the American public to determine how men view and define <a href="/wiki/Fatherhood" class="mw-redirect" title="Fatherhood">fatherhood</a>. Specifically, studies have focused on why men choose to become fathers and the relationship between fatherhood and contemporary <a href="/wiki/Masculinity" title="Masculinity">masculinity</a>. Not surprisingly, recent research on fatherhood is framed by <a href="/wiki/Social_identity_theory" title="Social identity theory">identity theory</a> and has focused on the salience, centrality, and importance of the father identity in men's lives, especially as it may be linked to men's involvement with their children. According to identity theory, the more salient and central the identity, the more likely individuals are to engage in behaviors associated with it. <a href="/wiki/Salience_(neuroscience)" title="Salience (neuroscience)">Salience</a> refers to the readiness to act out an identity in a particular situation. <a href="/wiki/Centrality" title="Centrality">Centrality</a> refers to the importance of an identity in relation to other identities. The centrality of the father identity is usually held at a higher level (as opposed to brother, husband, etc.) due to the gendered expectation that men must be "good" fathers. Men who view their role as a father central and crucial to who they are as a person are more likely to engage with their children and strive to participate in <a href="/wiki/Responsible_fatherhood" title="Responsible fatherhood">responsible fatherhood</a>. Men who fail to successfully become fathers or are unable to have children view the lack of fatherhood as a threat to their masculinity. As a result, the threat to masculinity serves as a driving force for men to possibly become fathers because they never want to be seen as infertile or effeminate.<sup id="cite_ref-Stryker1994_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stryker1994-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Men_who_do_not_choose_fatherhood">Men who do not choose fatherhood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Men who do not choose fatherhood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Studies on men who choose not to be fathers often focus on how the role of fatherhood is crucial to masculinity and a man's central identity. Many men blame economic difficulties, cultural differences, and life situations as potential factors that deter them from fatherhood.<sup id="cite_ref-Stryker1994_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stryker1994-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Economic difficulties, see <a href="/wiki/Economic_problem" title="Economic problem">economic problem</a>, serve as a primary explanation for men to avoid fatherhood. For men, it is difficult to separate occupational success from fatherhood because financially providing for one's family has been central to the identity of being a father in the United States. As a result, a complex relationship is formed between economic struggles and the importance of fatherhood. Men who are not employed or have low earnings often feel as if they have failed as both fathers and men. On the other hand, men who have a low socioeconomic status find fatherhood very appealing because it gives them a measure of accomplishment denied to them by the occupational world.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In terms of the cultural importance of fatherhood, white men and men of color have differing views on fatherhood that can affect how many of these men participate in fatherhood.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Lastly, some men blame life situations as the primary factor for their decision not to pursue fatherhood. Life situations are defined as an individual's relationship status (single or married) and their age. Studies have shown that men who are older and married tend to be more likely to pursue fatherhood. It has been proposed that men continue to view <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">marriage</a>, work and fatherhood as a "package deal"<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> meaning that lacking one of these components, like work or marriage, may result in the decision not to have children.
</p><p>It has also been proposed that married men feel as if they are expected to pursue fatherhood as a part of their marriage though they personally may not want to have children. On the other hand, men who are single and younger do not feel the same desire because they are not "prepared" to emotionally and financially support a child.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternate_family_forms">Alternate family forms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Alternate family forms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The number of <a href="/wiki/Married_couples" class="mw-redirect" title="Married couples">married couples</a> raising children has decreased over the years. In Canada, married and <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> couples with children under the age of 25 represented 44% of all families in 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-Macionis_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macionis-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> This statistic has lowered since 1991, when married and common law couples raising children under the age of 25 represented 49 percent of all Canadian families.<sup id="cite_ref-Macionis_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macionis-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> There are various family forms which are becoming increasingly popular in society.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Single_parent_families">Single parent families</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Single parent families">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Canada, one parent families have become popular since 1961 when only 8.4 percent of children were being raised by a single parent.<sup id="cite_ref-Macionis_22-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macionis-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> In 2001, 15.6 percent of children were being raised by a single parent.<sup id="cite_ref-Macionis_22-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macionis-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The number of single parent families continue to rise, while it is four times more likely that the mother is the parent raising the child. The high percentage of mothers becoming the sole parent is sometimes due to the result of a divorce, unplanned pregnancy or the inability to find a befitting partner.
Children who are raised by a single parent are commonly at a disadvantage due to the characteristics of parenting. A mother and father both make significant contributions to the development of a child, therefore one parent's ability to raise a child on her or his own may be hindered.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cohabitation">Cohabitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Cohabitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A residence containing an unmarried couple is called <a href="/wiki/Cohabitation" title="Cohabitation">cohabitation</a>. This type of family style is becoming increasingly accepted in Canada and has increased from 8% in 1981 to 16.4% in 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-Macionis_22-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macionis-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> In the last few decades, living with your significant other has become normalized in society. Cohabitation has drastically increased in the United States within the last 50 years, increasing by nearly 900 percent. Data from a 2012 Census showed that 7.8 million couples are living together without first getting married, compared to 2.9 million in 1996. And two-thirds of couples married in 2012 shared a home together for more than two years before their marriage. <sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><br />
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gay_and_lesbian_couples">Gay and lesbian couples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Gay and lesbian couples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gay and Lesbian couples are categorized as same sex relationships. In 1989, Denmark was the first nation to allow same sex couples to get married and to provide equal rights to all citizens.<sup id="cite_ref-Macionis_22-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macionis-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> After this many nations began to allow same sex marriages to occur such as Canada and Spain (2005).<sup id="cite_ref-Macionis_22-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macionis-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> A United States Supreme Court ruling mandated that same sex marriage is constitutional and therefore allowed in all 50 states in the United States (2015).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Child-rearing_by_same-sex_couples">Child-rearing by same-sex couples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Child-rearing by same-sex couples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Children of same-sex couples either come from past relationships or through other opportunities like adoption or artificial insemination.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> From the data collected in the 2000 U.S. Census, it was suggested that more than 250,000 children in the United States were being raised by lesbian and gay couples.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In the 2010 U.S. Census, it was reported that 20% of lesbian and gay couple or partnership households are raising children (115,064 out of 594,000 same-sex households). The trend of child-rearing amongst gay and lesbian couples or partnerships is on the rise. Also, the support from the general public for gay and lesbian couples or partnerships to raising children is at its all-time high since the 1990s.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In 1994, the idea of homosexual partnerships parenting children evenly divided Americans for support. When Americans were asked, "Do you think homosexual couples should or should not have the legal right to adopt a child", 28% of Americans said they should, and 65% said they shouldn't.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In 2003, the idea of homosexual partnerships parenting children evenly divided Americans for support. When Americans were asked the same question about the right of homosexual partnerships to raise children through adoption, 49% of Americans said they should, and 48% said they shouldn't.  In 2014, Americans were asked a very similar question. The results were almost more polarizing than the results found in 1994. Twenty years later, 63% of Americans said lesbian and gay couples or partnerships, and 35% said they shouldn't.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>There are no federal laws prohibiting the adoption of a child by a homosexual couple or partnership.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> But there are some states, one being Florida, that depend on the opinions of the county judge in charge of the case, and county judges base their decisions on "the best interest of the child", in regards to child adoption by same-sex couple.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The "best interest of the child" seems to be the driving force behind the push back and reasoning for the remaining lack of support for homosexual couples or partnerships adopting or raising children. The central argument in the debate for legal rights, policies and overall support is related to the idea of same-sex couples raising children is the well-being of children raised in those families. There are concerns like about the mental, emotional and even the social development of children who are raised in same sex couple or partnership households. There has been a plethora of research conducted that provides insight into a range of issues, including the personal development, gender development, peer relationships, and family relationships of children with same-sex parents.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Research suggests that sexual identities (including gender identity, gender-role behavior, and sexual orientation) develop in much the same ways among children of lesbian mothers as they do among children of heterosexual parents.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Evidence also suggests that children of lesbian and gay parents have normal social relationships with peers and adults.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> There have also been studies of other aspects of personal development (including personality, self-concept, and conduct) that similarly reveal few differences between children of lesbian mothers and children of heterosexual parents.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> These differences are not significant but are noticeable. For example, there was a study that examined and compared particular behaviors and ideas/belief performed by sons and daughters of lesbian mothers. Studies found that 53% of the daughters of lesbian mothers aspired to pursue careers as physicians, attorneys, and engineers compared with only 21% of the daughters of heterosexual mothers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The sons of lesbian mothers also tended to be less aggressive and more nurturing than the sons of heterosexual mothers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  The general concern about homosexual couples or partnerships parenting children doesn't have any grounds for their argument, "children who grow up with one or two gay and/or lesbian parents fare as well in emotional, cognitive, social, and sexual functioning as do children whose parents are heterosexual. Children's optimal development seems to be influenced more by the nature of the relationships and interactions within the family unit than by the particular structural form it takes."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sociology_of_Childhood">Sociology of Childhood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Sociology of Childhood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The values learned during childhood are important in the development and socialization of children. The family is considered to be the agency of <a href="/wiki/Primary_socialisation" title="Primary socialisation">primary socialisation</a> and the first <a href="/wiki/Focal_socialisation_agency" title="Focal socialisation agency">focal socialisation agency</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <p>Since the 2000s, a new subfield, sociology of childhood has gained increasing attention and triggered numerous empirical studies as well as intensive theoretical disputes, starting in the Scandinavian and the English-speaking countries. Up to this time, sociology had approached children and childhood mainly from a socialization perspective, and the emergence of the new childhood sociological paradigm ran parallel to the feminist critique of sociological traditions. Childhood sociologists attacked the "adultocentric" approach and the "separative view" of sociology towards children. Not surprisingly, then, the key works in the sociology of childhood are quite interdisciplinary, linking <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cultural_studies" title="Cultural studies">cultural studies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethnomethodology" title="Ethnomethodology">ethnomethodology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pedagogy" title="Pedagogy">pedagogy</a>. Key texts include James and Prout's <i>Constructing and Reconstructing Childhood</i> (1990/1997), James, Jenks and Prout <i>Theorizing Childhood</i> (1998)and Prout's <i>The Future of Childhood</i> (2005). On methodological issues in research with children see <i>Research with Children</i>, edited by Christensen and James (2008).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_trends">Recent trends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Recent trends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The current Sociology of childhood is organized around three central discussions:
</p><p><b>The child as a social actor</b>: This approach derives from youth sociology as well as ethnography. Focusing on everyday life and the ways children orient themselves in society, it engages with the cultural performances and the social worlds they construct and take part in. Theory and research methodology approach children as active participants and members of society right from the beginning.<sup id="cite_ref-Mackay1975_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mackay1975-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Speier1973_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Speier1973-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Speier1976_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Speier1976-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> Thus they are neither analyzed as outsiders to society nor as merely 'emergent' members of society.
Therefore, the sociology of childhood distinguishes itself from the established concepts of <a href="/wiki/Socialisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Socialisation">socialisation</a><sup id="cite_ref-Cromdal2006_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cromdal2006-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> research and <a href="/wiki/Developmental_psychology" title="Developmental psychology">developmental psychology</a> of the last decades.
</p><p><b>The generational order</b>: The second approach centers on socio-structural and socio-theoretical questions concerning <a href="/wiki/Social_equality" title="Social equality">social equality</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_order" title="Social order">social order</a> in a society, which categorizes their members by age and segregates them in many respects (rights, deeds, economical participation, ascribed needs etc.). These issues can be summarized under the overall concept of the <b>generational order</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Thus the categorization of societal members by age is far from being an innocent representation of natural distinctions, but rather a social construction of such a "natural truth". It is, therefore, a relevant component of <a href="/wiki/Social_order" title="Social order">social order</a> and deeply connected to other dimensions of social inequality.
Social and economic changes and socio-political interventions thus become central topics in childhood sociology. The analysis of these issues has increased awareness of the generational inequality of societies.
</p><p><b>The Hybridity of Childhood</b>: This discussion is more critical (though not dismissive) of the social constructionist approaches that have dominated the sociology of childhood since the 1990s. More open to materialist perspectives, it seeks an interdisciplinary path that recognizes the biological as well as the social and cultural shaping of childhood and holds open the possibility of an interdisciplinary Childhood Studies emergent from current multi-disciplinary efforts. This scholarship has two important influences. Firstly, a so-called 'new wave' of childhood studies,<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> heavily influenced by Alan Prout's (2005) seminal book <i>The Future of Childhood</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> In this work, Prout examines how childhoods are not merely constructed socially â€“ via discourses, laws or institutions â€“ but materially, through toys, food and medicines. Since then, sociologists such as Nick Lee have offered important analyses of the ways in which the 'entanglements' between children and non-human materialities and technologies have become ever-more important to the governance and regulation of children's lives, through what he terms the 'biopoliticisation' of childhood.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> Secondly, nonrepresentational approaches to <a href="/wiki/Children%27s_geographies" title="Children&#39;s geographies">Children's geographies</a> have offered a commensurate and (arguably) broader series of approaches that move beyond social constructivism. Scholars such as Peter Kraftl, John Horton and Affrica Taylor have been particularly influential in examining how childhoods are produced and experienced through complex intersections of emotion, affect, embodiment and materiality.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> Somewhat problematically, there has been relatively little overlap between these two strands of scholarship, despite their sharing common conceptual foundations in the work of <a href="/wiki/Post-structuralism" title="Post-structuralism">Post-structuralism</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_materialism" class="mw-redirect" title="New materialism">New materialism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Posthumanism" title="Posthumanism">Posthumanism</a>. Nevertheless, during the mid-2010s, a so-called 'spatial turn' in childhood and education studies saw increasing cross-fertilisation between these fields and the take-up of children's geographers' work by sociologists and others. Therefore, the prospects for cross-disciplinary scholarship around hybridity, spatiality and a 'new wave' remain very promising â€“ perhaps most evident in a recent volume by Julie Seymour, Abigail Hackett and Lisa Procter.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gender_and_childhood">Gender and childhood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Gender and childhood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There has been much research and discussion about the effects of society on the assumption of gender roles in childhood, and how societal norms perpetuate gender-differentiated interactions with children. <a href="/wiki/Psychologist" title="Psychologist">Psychologists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociologists</a> suggest that <a href="/wiki/Gender_identity" title="Gender identity">self-gender identity</a> is a result of social learning from peers, <a href="/wiki/Role_model" title="Role model">role modeling</a> within the family unit, and genetic predisposition.<sup id="cite_ref-Raley_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raley-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> The sociological implications are as follows:
</p><p><b>Peer interactions</b>:
</p><p>There are significant gender differences in the relationship styles among children which particularly begin to emerge after early childhood and at the onset of middle childhood around age 6 and grow more prevalent with age. Boys tend to play in larger groups than girls, and friends of boys are more likely to become friends with each other which, in turn leads to more density in social networks among boys. Boys also have more well-defined dominance hierarchies than girls within their peer groups. In terms of <a href="/wiki/Dyad_(sociology)" title="Dyad (sociology)">dyadic relationships</a>, girls are more likely to have longer-lasting relationships of this nature, but no literature suggests that girls engage in more dyadic relationships than boys. Girls are also more <a href="/wiki/Prosocial_behavior" title="Prosocial behavior">prosocial</a> in conflict situations and are better at collaborative work and play than boys. They also spend more time in social conversations than boys and are more likely to <a href="/wiki/Self-disclosure" title="Self-disclosure">self-disclose</a> among their peers than boys. On the other hand, boys are more likely than girls to engage in organized play such as sports and activities with well-defined rules. One theory suggests that because of this, boys have more opportunities to exhibit their strength and skill and compare theirs to that of their peers during these competitive activities. Girls' peer groups are characterized by strong interpersonal relations, empathy for others, and working towards connection-oriented goals, while boys focus more on asserting their own <a href="/wiki/Dominance_hierarchy" title="Dominance hierarchy">dominance</a> in the peer group and agenda-oriented goals.<sup id="cite_ref-Rose_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rose-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Significant social differences also exist between boys and girls when experiencing and dealing with social stress. Boys experience more social stress among their peers than girls in the form of verbal and physical abuse, but girls experience more social stress through strains in their friendships and social networks. To deal with social stress, girls do more support-seeking, express more emotions to their friends, and <a href="/wiki/Rumination_(psychology)" title="Rumination (psychology)">ruminate</a> more than boys. Boys use humor as a distraction from stress and seek less emotional support within their friendships and social networks.<sup id="cite_ref-Rose_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rose-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Family interactions</b>:
</p><p>Overall, the literature implies that the biological gender of children affects how parents interact with them. Differentials in interaction range from the amount of time spent with children to how much parents invest financially in their children's futures. On average, <a href="/wiki/Father" title="Father">fathers</a> tend to exhibit more differential treatment than <a href="/wiki/Mother" title="Mother">mothers</a>, and fathers tend to be more invested in families with sons than families with daughters in terms of both time and money. However, the association of gender with father investment has been weakening over the years, and the differentials are not large. Parents tend to enroll their daughters in more cultural activities than their sons (e.g. art classes, dance classes, and musical instrument lessons), and tend to be more invested in school-related parent involvement programs for their sons than their daughters.<sup id="cite_ref-Raley_65-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raley-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sons and daughters are not only treated differently by their parents based on gender, but also receive different benefits from their parents based on gender. Parents, both fathers and mothers, may be less invested in their daughters' higher education than their sons' and tend to save more money on average in anticipation for their sons' enrollment in educational institutions after high school graduation. However, this may not lead to more academic or work success for sons later in life. Parents are also more likely to underestimate daughters' abilities in math and science while overestimating that of sons. Daughters also, on average, also do more housework than sons, which reflects <a href="/wiki/Occupational_sexism" title="Occupational sexism">gendered divisions in the workplace</a> and household in society.<sup id="cite_ref-Raley_65-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raley-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sibling" title="Sibling">Sibling relations</a>, unlike parental relations, show no consensus in the literature about being gender-differentiated in interactions and benefits. However, sex-minority siblings may have more difficulty receiving necessary sex-specific treatment from parents.<sup id="cite_ref-Raley_65-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raley-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_tasks">Current tasks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_of_the_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Current tasks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Questions about socialization practices and institutions remain central in childhood research. But, they are being dealt with in a new, more sociological way. To analyze socialization processes means, therefore, to reconstruct the historically and culturally varying conceptions, processes and institutions of <a href="/wiki/Discipline" title="Discipline">disciplining</a> and <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilization</a> of the offspring. In addition, the strategies of <a href="/wiki/Habitus_(sociology)" title="Habitus (sociology)">habitus</a> formation and the practices of <a href="/wiki/Social_status" title="Social status">status</a> (re-)production are considered. The sociology of social inequality and the sociology of the family and private life are, therefore, important fields for childhood sociologists. Children's own action, their resistance, cooperation, and collective action among <a href="/wiki/Peer_group" title="Peer group">peers</a> has to be taken into account. Meanwhile, widespread anthropological assumptions concerning a universal human nature, based on a view of individual and society as opposed to each other, should be omitted from the conceptual repertoire of sociological <a href="/w/index.php?title=Childhood_research&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Childhood research (page does not exist)">childhood research</a>. They are the legacy of the older socialization approach and they legitimate some forms of childhood and education practices as indispensable and even as a "natural" requirement of society, while devaluing others. In this way they generally legitimate western <a href="/wiki/Middle_class" title="Middle class">middle class</a> childhood and mask <a href="/wiki/Social_inequality" title="Social inequality">inequality</a> and the interests of <a href="/wiki/Social_order" title="Social order">social order</a>.
</p>
