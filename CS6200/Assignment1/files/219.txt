	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Farm (revenue leasing)</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Farm_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Farm (disambiguation)">Farm (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Farming</b> or <b>tax-farming</b> is  a technique of <a href="/wiki/Financial_management" title="Financial management">financial management</a> in which the management of a variable revenue stream is assigned by <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">legal contract</a> to a third party and the holder of the revenue stream receives fixed periodic rents from the contractor. It is most commonly used in <a href="/wiki/Public_finance" title="Public finance">public finance</a>, where governments (the <a href="/wiki/Lease" title="Lease">lessors</a>) lease or assign the right to collect and retain the whole of the tax revenue to a private financier (the farmer), who is charged with paying fixed sums (sometimes called "rents", but with a different meaning from the common modern term) into the <a href="/wiki/Treasury" title="Treasury">treasury</a>. Sometimes, as in the case of <a href="/wiki/Miguel_de_Cervantes" title="Miguel de Cervantes">Miguel de Cervantes</a>, the tax farmer was a government employee, paid a salary, and all money collected went to the government.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Farming in this sense has nothing to do with <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, other than metaphorically.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Farm_(revenue_leasing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are two possible origins for <i>farm</i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Derivation_from_classical_Latin">Derivation from classical Latin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Farm_(revenue_leasing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Derivation from classical Latin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some sources derive "farm" with its French version <i>ferme</i>, most notably used in the context of the <a href="/wiki/Farmers-General" class="mw-redirect" title="Farmers-General">Fermiers Generaux</a>,  from the mediaeval Latin <i>firma</i>, meaning "a fixed agreement, contract", ultimately from the classical Latin adjective <i>firmus</i>, <i>firma</i>, <i>firmum</i>, meaning "firm, strong, stout, steadfast,  immoveable, sure, to be relied upon".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  The modern agricultural sense of the word stems from the same origin, in that  a medieval land-"holder" (none "owned" land but the king himself under his <a href="/wiki/Allodial" class="mw-redirect" title="Allodial">allodial</a> title) under <a href="/wiki/Feudal_land_tenure" class="mw-redirect" title="Feudal land tenure">feudal land tenure</a> might let  it (i.e. lease it out) under a contract as a <a href="/wiki/Going_concern" title="Going concern">going concern</a> (not as a <a href="/wiki/Subinfeudation" title="Subinfeudation">sub-infeudated</a> <a href="/wiki/Fief" title="Fief">fee</a>), that is to say as a unit producing a revenue stream, together with its workers, livestock and deadstock (i.e. implements), for exploitation by a tenant who was licensed by the contract, or <i>firma</i>, to keep all the revenue he could extract from the holding in exchange for fixed rents. Thus the rights to the revenue stream produced by the land had been farmed by the lessor (therefore strictly perhaps the "farmor") and the tenant became the "holder of the farm", or to coin a word, "farmee". Because this was the form of the farming transaction most known to popular society, the word "farmer" became synonymous with a tenant of an agricultural holding.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Derivation_from_Old_English">Derivation from Old English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Farm_(revenue_leasing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Derivation from Old English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to other sources, the word <i>farm</i> comes from <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> <i>ferme</i> ("farm, rent, revenue; revenue collected from a farmer; factor, stewardship, meal, feast"), from <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>feorm, farm</i> ("provision, stores of food, supplies, possessions; provisions supplied to the king or a lord by a tenant or vassal; rent, feast, benefit, assylum"), from <a href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" class="mw-redirect" title="Proto-Germanic">Proto-Germanic</a> *<i>firmō, *firχumō</i> ("means of living, subsistence"), from <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a> *<i>perk<sup>w</sup>u-</i> ("life, strength, force"). It is related to other Old English words such as <i>feormehām</i> ("farm"), <i>feormere</i> ("purveyor, grocer"), <i>feormian</i> ("to provision, sustain"), and <i>feorh</i> ("life, spirit"). The Old English word is stated by these sources as having unusually been  borrowed by <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a> as <i>firma</i> or <i>ferma</i> and to have provided the <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> <i>ferme</i> "farm", <a href="/wiki/Occitan" class="mw-redirect" title="Occitan">Occitan</a> <i>ferma</i> "farm". This is refuted by those sources which state <i>firma</i> to derive from classical Latin <i>firmus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The word continued the same senses of "rent, farmed office, source of revenue, feast". The meaning "rent, fixed payment", which was already present in the Old English word, was further strengthened due to the word's resemblance to the unrelated (so say these sources) <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a> <i>firmus</i> ("firm, solid"), and <i>firmitas</i> ("security, firmness").<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Valuation_of_a_farm">Valuation of a farm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Farm_(revenue_leasing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Valuation of a farm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The tenant of a farm can only make a profit if he makes a careful assessment of its value. Although modern <a href="/wiki/Finance" title="Finance">financial management theory</a> has reduced such calculations to scientific formulae, the mind of the astute financier of past ages would have well understood the calculations involved, whether they were performed mentally or by making marks in the sand. An estimate is made of the long-term average yearly gross value of the revenue stream in question, which can be derived from examination of past records and accounts, adjusted for any new circumstances affecting the future. Then a discount for a risk element is deducted with a further discount deducted for the <a href="/wiki/Time_value_of_money" title="Time value of money">time value of money</a>.  The risk in question relates to the possibility of some of the debts forming the revenue stream being defaulted on or paid late. This causes variability in the revenue stream. These concepts must always have been second-nature to the successful merchant throughout the ages. The resultant figure forms the maximum rent the tenant is willing to pay to the lessee of the farm. His profit becomes the excess of whatever revenues he can extract from the farm less the rents payable, less his administration, levying and collection expenses. The skill of the tenant of a farm is therefore firstly in negotiating a favourable rent which he does by overstating the riskiness (variability) of the cash flow stream in question and secondly in his management of the debts thus assigned to him, that is to say his skills as a debt-collector and  manager in general. He must also be satisfied that he has the ability to enforce payment of the debts, ultimately  by use of a court of law, in which he must pay the standard fee for bringing a suit, under the legal system generally instituted by the government authority which is the lessor of the farm. He does not act as the lessor's agent but as a principal.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_use">Historical use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Farm_(revenue_leasing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Historical use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_Empire">Roman Empire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Farm_(revenue_leasing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Roman Empire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Publican" title="Publican">Publican</a></div>
<p>Tax farming was originally a <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> practice whereby the burden of <a href="/wiki/Tax_collection" class="mw-redirect" title="Tax collection">tax collection</a> was reassigned by the Roman State to private individuals or groups. In essence, these individuals or groups paid the taxes for a certain area and for a certain period of time and then attempted to cover their outlay by collecting money or saleable goods from the people within that area.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The system was set up by <a href="/wiki/Gaius_Gracchus" title="Gaius Gracchus">Gaius Gracchus</a> in 123 BC primarily to increase the efficiency of tax collection within <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> itself but the system quickly spread to the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Province" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Province">Provinces</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Within the Roman Empire, these private individuals and groups which collected taxes in lieu of the bid (i.e. rent) they had paid to the state were known as <i><a href="/wiki/Publicani" class="mw-redirect" title="Publicani">publicani</a></i>, of whom the best known is  the <a href="/wiki/Disciple_(Christianity)" title="Disciple (Christianity)">disciple</a> <a href="/wiki/Matthew_the_Evangelist" class="mw-redirect" title="Matthew the Evangelist">Matthew</a>, a <i>publicanus</i> in the village of <a href="/wiki/Capernaum" title="Capernaum">Capernaum</a> in the province of <a href="/wiki/Galilee" title="Galilee">Galilee</a>. The system was widely abused, and reforms were enacted by <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diocletian" title="Diocletian">Diocletian</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  Tax farming practices are believed to have contributed to the <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_the_Western_Roman_Empire" title="Fall of the Western Roman Empire">fall of the Western Roman Empire</a> in Western Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feudal_England">Feudal England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Farm_(revenue_leasing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Feudal England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mediaeval English kings frequently made grants "in <a href="/wiki/Fief" title="Fief">fee</a>-farm", a form of <a href="/wiki/Feudal_land_tenure" class="mw-redirect" title="Feudal land tenure">feudal tenure</a>. An example is the following <a href="/wiki/Writ" title="Writ">writ</a> of King <a href="/wiki/William_II_of_England" title="William II of England">William II</a> (1087–1100) granting a <a href="/wiki/Hundred_(county_subdivision)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hundred (county subdivision)">hundred court</a> to be held in fee-farm by  <a href="/wiki/Thorney_Abbey" title="Thorney Abbey">Thorney Abbey</a>:
</p>
<blockquote><p>William, king of the English, to all the <a href="/wiki/Sheriff" title="Sheriff">sheriffs</a> and <a href="/wiki/English_feudal_barony" title="English feudal barony">barons</a> of <a href="/wiki/Huntingdonshire" title="Huntingdonshire">Huntingdonshire</a>, greeting. Know that I have granted the hundred of Normancross to the <a href="/wiki/Abbot" title="Abbot">abbot</a> and monks of Thorney to be held in fee-farm for an annual rent of 100 <a href="/wiki/Shilling" title="Shilling">shillings</a> which I order them to pay to my sheriff at Huntingdon. And I forbid any of my officers to do them injury or insult in respect of this.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Farm_(revenue_leasing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Besides the Romans, historical examples include the tax collection methods of the <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_Egypt" class="mw-redirect" title="Ptolemaic Egypt">Ptolemies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seljuks" class="mw-redirect" title="Seljuks">Seljuks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mamluk_Sultanate_(Cairo)" title="Mamluk Sultanate (Cairo)">Mamluks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottomans</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ancien_R%C3%A9gime" title="Ancien Régime">French State prior to Louis XVI</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Ferme_g%C3%A9n%C3%A9rale" title="Ferme générale">ferme générale</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russia</a> prior to 1862. In many cases, such as the <a href="/wiki/Abbasid_Caliphate" title="Abbasid Caliphate">Abbasid</a> practice of <a href="/wiki/Iqta" class="mw-redirect" title="Iqta">Iqta</a>, these rights were granted by an authority, in this example the caliph, for services rendered or promised. In the Byzantine <i>pronoia</i> system, similar rights were often purchased from the crown. Though such arrangements in some respects seem similar to the feudal system, there are significant disparities, including continuance of state power and, at least in the case of <i>pronoia</i>, theoretical time limits on the grant. In many cases, including those mentioned, tax rights were not transferable or divisible, unlike feudal fiefdoms.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages">Advantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Farm_(revenue_leasing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Advantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tax farming was an important step in the history of <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">economic development</a> by providing a method for collecting taxes across a large area without the need for a tax-collecting <a href="/wiki/Bureaucracy" title="Bureaucracy">bureaucracy</a>, or during periods when such a bureaucracy is unworkable or impossible to maintain. Systems of tax farming similar to the Roman model were used in <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_Egypt" class="mw-redirect" title="Ptolemaic Egypt">Ptolemaic Egypt</a>, various medieval Western European countries, the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mughal_Empire" title="Mughal Empire">Mughal</a> empires, and in <a href="/wiki/Qing_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Qing Dynasty">Qing Dynasty</a> China.  As states become stronger, buoyed up by revenues brought in by tax farming, the practice was discontinued in favour of centralized tax collection systems. In part this was because tax farming systems tended to rely on wealthy individuals outside the state machinery, <a href="/wiki/Gang" title="Gang">gangs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Secret_societies" class="mw-redirect" title="Secret societies">secret societies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disadvantages">Disadvantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Farm_(revenue_leasing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Disadvantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The key flaw in the tax farming system is the tension between the state, which seeks a long-term source of taxation revenue, and the tax farmers, who seek to make a profit on their investment in as short a time as possible. As a result, tax-farmers often abuse the taxpayers in various ways, tending them to switch their economic activity from strategic long-term projects to short-term revenue generation. A common abuse by tax farmers is the undervaluation of  goods received  in lieu of taxes, allowing the tax-farmer to re-sell the goods to create a second profit source. Such abuses stifle economic growth by restricting the ability of the tradesman to reinvest in his business, thereby limiting the quantity of taxes generated over the long-term.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern-day_tax_farming">Modern-day tax farming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Farm_(revenue_leasing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Modern-day tax farming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indian_sub-continent">Indian sub-continent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Farm_(revenue_leasing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Indian sub-continent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Bangladesh and India tolls on bridges and roads and dues from public properties such as lakes and forests are often leased to private persons or firms.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disambiguation">Disambiguation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Farm_(revenue_leasing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Disambiguation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Privatized_tax_collection">Privatized tax collection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Farm_(revenue_leasing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Privatized tax collection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tax farming is not synonymous with modern <a href="/wiki/Privatized_tax_collection" title="Privatized tax collection">privatized tax collection</a>, where private individuals or companies collect taxes and pass them to the state in return for a commission or fee, without bearing any risk consequent of default by the taxpayer. Tax farming is <a href="/wiki/Speculation" title="Speculation">speculative</a>, meaning that the tenant of the farm bears the full risk of defaulted debts. In addition, a tenant is often required as a term of the lease to make an early rent payment, which must be financed from his own resources until the revenue stream subject to the farm has started to be collected.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Factoring">Factoring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Farm_(revenue_leasing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Factoring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, some tax collection of "lower value debts" by <a href="/wiki/HMRC" class="mw-redirect" title="HMRC">HMRC</a> has been outsourced to <a href="/wiki/Debt_collection_agency" class="mw-redirect" title="Debt collection agency">debt collection agencies</a> from July 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> However, debt collection agencies, like invoice factors, are not truly farmers of revenue streams, as they do not bear any risk of default. Rather they make loans in expectation of future receipts, such loans being always recoverable and secured on the income stream itself.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Simple_commutation">Simple commutation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Farm_(revenue_leasing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Simple commutation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1999 the National Board of Revenue in Bangladesh (NBR) negotiated with cigarette producing firms a minimum amount of <a href="/wiki/Value_Added_Tax" class="mw-redirect" title="Value Added Tax">Value Added Tax</a> (VAT) that should be paid per month even though  VAT is an <i><a href="/wiki/Ad_valorem_tax" title="Ad valorem tax">ad valorem</a></i> tax, that is to say of variable yield. The NBR took this step because under the self-clearance system monitoring of production and sales of cigarettes proved to be difficult. It was agreed that if the cigarette producing firms paid the minimum revenue fixed by the NBR, physical monitoring would be withdrawn. The NBR resorted to this technique of financial management  to avoid the large costs of monitoring while gaining more in revenue with certainty.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
