<p><b>Kinship terminology</b> is the system used in <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">languages</a> to refer to the persons to whom an individual is related through <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">kinship</a>. Different societies classify kinship relations differently and therefore use different systems of kinship terminology; for example, some languages distinguish between <a href="/wiki/Consanguine" class="mw-redirect" title="Consanguine">consanguine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Affinal_kin" class="mw-redirect" title="Affinal kin">affinal</a> uncles (i.e. the brothers of one's parents and the husbands of the sisters of one's parents, respectively), whereas others have only one word to refer to both a father and his brothers. Kinship terminologies include the terms of address used in different languages or communities for different relatives and the terms of reference used to identify the relationship of these relatives to ego or to each other.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_view">Historical view</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship_terminology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historical view">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Anthropologist" title="Anthropologist">Anthropologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Lewis_H._Morgan" title="Lewis H. Morgan">Lewis Henry Morgan</a> (1818–1881) performed the first survey of kinship terminologies in use around the world. Though much of his work is now considered dated, he argued that <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">kinship</a> terminologies reflect different sets of distinctions. For example, most kinship terminologies distinguish between sexes (the difference between a brother and a sister) and between generations (the difference between a child and a parent). Moreover, he argued, kinship terminologies distinguish between relatives by blood and <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">marriage</a> (although recently some anthropologists have argued that many societies define kinship in terms other than blood).
</p><p>However, Morgan also observed that different languages (and, by extension, societies) organize these distinctions differently. He proposed to describe kinship terms and terminologies as either descriptive or <a href="/wiki/Classificatory_kinship" title="Classificatory kinship">classificatory</a>. When a descriptive term is used, it can only represent one type of relationship between two people, while a classificatory term represents one of many different types of relationships. For example, the word <i><a href="/wiki/Brother" title="Brother">brother</a></i> in English-speaking societies indicates a son of the same parent; thus, English-speaking societies use the word <i>brother</i> as a descriptive term.  A person's male first cousin could be the mother's brother's son, mother's sister's son, father's brother's son, father's sister's son, and so on; English-speaking societies therefore use the word <i>cousin</i> as a classificatory term.
</p><p>Morgan discovered that a descriptive term in one society can become a classificatory term in another society.  For example, in some societies, one would refer to many different people as "mother" (the woman who gave birth to oneself, as well as her sister and husband's sister, and also one's father's sister). Moreover, some societies do not group together relatives which the English-speaking societies classify together. For example, some languages have no one-word equivalent to <i>cousin</i>, because different terms refer to one's mother's sister's children and to one's father's sister's children.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Six_basic_patterns_of_kinship">Six basic patterns of kinship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship_terminology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Six basic patterns of kinship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Armed with these different terms, Morgan identified six basic patterns of kinship terminologies:
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a href="/wiki/File:Kinship_Systems_vertical.svg" class="image" title="Charts of the six systems."><img alt="Charts of the six systems." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Kinship_Systems_vertical.svg/350px-Kinship_Systems_vertical.svg.png" decoding="async" width="350" height="728" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Kinship_Systems_vertical.svg/525px-Kinship_Systems_vertical.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Kinship_Systems_vertical.svg/700px-Kinship_Systems_vertical.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="667" data-file-height="1388" /></a></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hawaiian_kinship">Hawaiian kinship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship_terminology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hawaiian kinship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_kinship" title="Hawaiian kinship">Hawaiian kinship</a>: the most classificatory; only distinguishes between sex and generation.  Thus, siblings and cousins are not distinguished (the same terms are used for both types of relatives).</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sudanese_kinship">Sudanese kinship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship_terminology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sudanese kinship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Sudanese_kinship" title="Sudanese kinship">Sudanese kinship</a>: the most descriptive; no two types of relatives share the same term.  Siblings are distinguished from cousins, and different terms are used for each type of cousin (i.e. father's brother's children, father's sister's children, mother's sister's children and mother's brother's children).</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Eskimo_kinship">Eskimo kinship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship_terminology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Eskimo kinship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Eskimo_kinship" title="Eskimo kinship">Eskimo kinship</a>: has both classificatory and descriptive terms; in addition to sex and generation, it also distinguishes between lineal relatives (those related directly by a line of descent) and collateral relatives (those related by blood, but not directly in the line of descent). Lineal relatives have highly descriptive terms; collateral relatives have highly classificatory terms.  Thus, siblings are distinguished from cousins, while all types of cousins are grouped together.  The system of English-language kinship terms falls into the Eskimo type.</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Iroquois_kinship_and_its_variations">Iroquois kinship and its variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship_terminology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Iroquois kinship and its variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Iroquois_kinship" title="Iroquois kinship">Iroquois kinship</a>: has both classificatory and descriptive terms; in addition to sex and generation, it also distinguishes between siblings of opposite sexes in the parental generation. A genealogical relationship traced through a pair of siblings of the same sex is classed as a blood relationship, but one traced though a pair of siblings of the opposite sex can be considered an in-law relationship. In other words, siblings are grouped together with <a href="/wiki/Parallel_cousin" class="mw-redirect" title="Parallel cousin">parallel cousins</a>, while separate terms are used for <a href="/wiki/Cross-cousin" class="mw-redirect" title="Cross-cousin">cross-cousins</a>.  Also, one calls one's mother's sister "mother" and one's father's brother "father".  However, one refers to one's mother's brother and one's father's sister by separate terms (often the terms for <i>father-in-law</i> and <i>mother-in-law</i>, since cross-cousins can be preferential marriage partners).</li></ul>
<ul><li>The basic principles of Crow and Omaha terminologies are symmetrical and opposite, with Crow systems having a matrilineal emphasis and Omaha systems a patrilineal emphasis.</li></ul>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Crow_kinship">Crow kinship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship_terminology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Crow kinship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Crow_kinship" title="Crow kinship">Crow kinship</a>: like Iroquois, but further distinguishes between one's mother's side and one's father's side. Relatives on the mother's side of the family have more descriptive terms, and relatives on the father's side have more classificatory terms.  Thus, Crow kinship is like Iroquois kinship, with the addition that a number of relatives belonging to one's father's matrilineage are grouped together, ignoring generational differences, so that the same term is used for both one's father's sister and one's father's sister's daughter, etc.</li></ul>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Omaha_kinship">Omaha kinship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship_terminology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Omaha kinship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Omaha_kinship" title="Omaha kinship">Omaha kinship</a>: like Iroquois, but further distinguishes between one's mother's side and one's father's side. Relatives on the mother's side of the family have more classificatory terms, and relatives on the father's side have more descriptive terms.  Thus, Omaha kinship is like Iroquois, with the addition that a number of relatives belonging to one's mother's patrilineage are grouped together, ignoring generational differences, so that the same term is used for both one's mother's brother and one's mother's brother's son, etc.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relative_age">Relative age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship_terminology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Relative age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some languages, such as <a href="/wiki/Kannada" title="Kannada">Kannada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Telugu_language" title="Telugu language">Telugu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">Tamil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sinhalese_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Sinhalese language">Sinhalese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Chinese_kinship" title="Chinese kinship">Chinese kinship</a>), <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Khmer_language" title="Khmer language">Khmer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_language" title="Vietnamese language">Vietnamese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tagalog_language" title="Tagalog language">Tagalog</a> (<a href="/wiki/Filipino_language" title="Filipino language">Filipino</a>), <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">Bulgarian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nepali_language" title="Nepali language">Nepalese,</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nahuatl_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Nahuatl language">Nahuatl</a> add another dimension to some relations: relative age. Rather than one term for "brother", there exist, for example, different words for "older brother" and "younger brother". In Tamil, an older male sibling is referred to as <i>Annan</i> and a younger male sibling as <i>Thambi</i>, whereas older and younger female siblings are called <i>Akka</i> and <i>Thangai</i> respectively.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Identification_of_alternating_generations">Identification of alternating generations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship_terminology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Identification of alternating generations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Other languages, such as <a href="/wiki/Chiricahua_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Chiricahua language">Chiricahua</a>, use the same terms of address for alternating generations. So Chiricahua children (male or female) call their paternal grandmother <i>-ch’iné</i>, and likewise this grandmother will call her son's children <i>-ch’iné</i>. Similar features are seen also in <a href="/wiki/Huichol_language" title="Huichol language">Huichol</a><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>, some descendant languages of <a href="/wiki/Proto-Austronesian_language" title="Proto-Austronesian language">Proto-Austronesian</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Fordata_language" title="Fordata language">Fordata</a><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>, <a href="/wiki/Kei_language" title="Kei language">Kei</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>, and <a href="/wiki/Yamdena_language" title="Yamdena language">Yamdena</a><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>)<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>, <a href="/wiki/Bislama_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Bislama language">Bislama</a><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>, and <a href="/wiki/Usarufa_language" title="Usarufa language">Usarufa</a><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>. Terms that recognize alternating generations and the prohibition of marriage within one's own set of alternate generation relatives (0, ±2, ±4, etc.) are common in <a href="/wiki/Australian_Aboriginal_kinship" title="Australian Aboriginal kinship">Australian Aboriginal kinship</a>.
</p><p>The relative age and alternating-generations systems are combined in some languages.  For instance,
<a href="/wiki/Tagalog_language" title="Tagalog language">Tagalog</a> borrows the relative age system of the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_kinship" title="Chinese kinship">Chinese kinship</a> and follows the generation system of kinship. <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Kinship" class="mw-redirect" title="Philippine Kinship">Philippine kinship</a> distinguishes between generation, age and in some cases, gender.
</p>
<h2><span id="Tamil_.2FDravidian"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Tamil_/Dravidian"><span id="Dravidian_kinship"></span> Tamil /Dravidian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship_terminology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Tamil /Dravidian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Floyd Lounsbury described<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> a seventh, Dravidian, type of terminological system that had been conflated with Iroquois in Morgan’s typology of kin-term systems because both systems distinguish relatives by marriage from relatives by descent, although both are <a href="/wiki/Classificatory_kinship" title="Classificatory kinship">classificatory</a> categories rather than being based on biological descent. The basic idea is that of applying an even/odd distinction to relatives that takes into account the gender of every linking relative for ego’s kin relation to any given person. A MFBD(C), for example, is a mother’s father’s brother’s daughter’s child. If each female link (M,D) is assigned a 0 and each male (F,B) a 1, the number of 1s is either even or odd; in this case, even. However, variant criteria exist.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In a Dravidian or Tamil system with a patrilineal <a href="/wiki/Modular_arithmetic" title="Modular arithmetic">modulo</a>-2 counting system, marriage is prohibited with this relative, and a marriageable relative must be modulo-2 odd. There exists also a version of this logic with a matrilineal bias. Discoveries of systems that use modulo-2 logic, as in South Asia, Australia, and many other parts of the world, marked a major advance in the understanding of kinship terminologies that differ from kin relations and terminologies employed by Europeans.
</p><p>The Dravidian or Tamil kinship system involves selective cousinhood. One's father's brother's children and one's mother's sister's children are <i>not</i> cousins but brothers and sisters one step removed. They are considered <i>consanguineous</i> (<i lang="ta" title="Tamil language text">pangali</i> in Tamil), and marriage with them is strictly forbidden as incestuous. However, one's father's sister's children and one's mother's brother's children are considered cousins and potential mates (<i lang="ta" title="Tamil language text">muraicherugu</i> in Tamil). Marriages between such cousins are allowed and encouraged. There is a clear distinction between <a href="/wiki/Cross_cousin" class="mw-redirect" title="Cross cousin">cross cousins,</a> who are one's true cousins and <a href="/wiki/Parallel_cousin" class="mw-redirect" title="Parallel cousin">parallel cousins,</a> who are, in fact, siblings.
</p><p>Like Iroquois people, Dravidians use the same words to refer to their father's sister and mother-in-law (<i lang="ta" title="Tamil language text">atthai</i> in Tamil and <i lang="kn" title="Kannada language text">atthe</i> in Kannada) and their mother's brother and father-in-law (<i lang="ta" title="Tamil language text">maamaa</i> in Tamil and <i lang="kn" title="Kannada language text">maava</i> in Kannada).  In Kannada, distinction between these relationships may be made because <i lang="kn" title="Kannada language text">sodara</i> is added before <i lang="kn" title="Kannada language text">atthe</i> and <i lang="kn" title="Kannada language text">maava</i> to specifically refer to one's father's sister and mother's brother respectively, although this term is not used in direct address.  In Tamil, however, only one's mother's brother is captioned with <i lang="ta" title="Tamil language text">thaai</i> before <i lang="ta" title="Tamil language text">maamaa</i> because of the honor accorded this relationship.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Abbreviations_for_genealogical_relationships">Abbreviations for genealogical relationships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinship_terminology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Abbreviations for genealogical relationships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The genealogical terminology used in many genealogical charts describes relatives of the subject in question. Using the abbreviations below, genealogical relationships may be distinguished by single or compound relationships, such as BC for a brother's children, MBD for a mother's brother's daughter, and so forth.
</p>
<ul><li>B = Brother</li>
<li>C = Child(ren)</li>
<li>D = Daughter</li>
<li>F = Father</li>
<li>GC = Grandchild(ren)</li>
<li>GP = Grandparent(s)</li>
<li>H = Husband</li>
<li>LA = In-law</li>
<li>M = Mother</li>
<li>P = Parent</li>
<li>S = Son</li>
<li>SI = Siblings</li>
<li>SP = Spouse</li>
<li>W = Wife</li>
<li>Z = Sister</li>
<li>(m.s.) = male speaking</li>
<li>(f.s.) = female speaking</li></ul>
