<p><b>Western law</b> refers to the <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">legal traditions</a> of <a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">Western culture</a>.  Western culture has an idea of the importance of <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a> which has its roots in both <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">Roman law</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canon_law" title="Canon law">canon law</a>.  As Western culture shares a <a href="/wiki/Graeco-Roman" class="mw-redirect" title="Graeco-Roman">Graeco-Roman</a> <a href="/wiki/Classics" title="Classics">Classical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> cultural influence, so do its legal systems.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The rediscovery of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">Justinian Code</a> in the early 10th century rekindled a passion for the discipline of law, initially shared across many of the re-forming boundaries between East and West. Eventually, it was only in the <a href="/wiki/Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Frankish</a> west that <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">Roman law</a> became the foundation of all legal concepts and systems. Its influence can be traced to this day in all Western legal systems, although differing in kind  and degree between the <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common</a> (Anglo-American) and the <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(legal_system)" title="Civil law (legal system)">civil</a> (continental European) legal traditions.  
</p><p>The study of <a href="/wiki/Canon_law" title="Canon law">canon law</a>, the legal system of the Catholic Church, fused with that of Roman law to form the basis for the refounding of Western legal scholarship.   Its principles of <a href="/wiki/Civil_rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil rights">civil rights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_equality" title="Social equality">equality</a> before the <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Equality_of_women" class="mw-redirect" title="Equality of women">equality of women</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Procedural_justice" title="Procedural justice">procedural justice</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democracy</a> as the ideal form of <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a> formed the basis of modern <a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">Western culture</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_legal_culture">Western legal culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Western legal culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Western <a href="/wiki/Legal_culture" title="Legal culture">legal culture</a> is unified in the systematic reliance on legal constructs. Such constructs include <a href="/wiki/Corporations" class="mw-redirect" title="Corporations">corporations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Contracts" class="mw-redirect" title="Contracts">contracts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Estate_(law)" title="Estate (law)">estates</a>, rights and powers to name a few. These concepts are not only nonexistent in primitive or traditional legal systems but they can also be predominately incapable of expression in those language systems which form the basis of such legal cultures.<sup id="cite_ref-Smith_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>   
</p><p>As a general proposition, the concept of legal culture depends on <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a> and <a href="/wiki/Symbols" class="mw-redirect" title="Symbols">symbols</a> and any attempt to analyse non western legal systems in terms of categories of modern western law can result in distortion attributable to differences in language.<sup id="cite_ref-Smith_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  So while legal constructs are unique to classical Roman, modern civil and common law cultures, legal concepts or primitive and archaic law get their meaning from sensed experience based on facts as opposed to theory or abstract. Legal culture therefore in the former group is influenced by academics, learned members of the profession and historically, philosophers. The latter group's culture is harnessed by beliefs, values and religion at a foundational level. Basically the latter group represents primacy of prejudices, myths and taboo as viable means to conduct human society.
</p>
