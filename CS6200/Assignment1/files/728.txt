	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Cultural artifact</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Exhibit_at_Lovre_Museum,_Paris_06.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Exhibit_at_Lovre_Museum%2C_Paris_06.jpg/220px-Exhibit_at_Lovre_Museum%2C_Paris_06.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Exhibit_at_Lovre_Museum%2C_Paris_06.jpg/330px-Exhibit_at_Lovre_Museum%2C_Paris_06.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Exhibit_at_Lovre_Museum%2C_Paris_06.jpg/440px-Exhibit_at_Lovre_Museum%2C_Paris_06.jpg 2x" data-file-width="6000" data-file-height="4000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Exhibit_at_Lovre_Museum,_Paris_06.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A cultural artifact exhibited at Louvre museum, Paris</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>cultural artifact</b>, or <b>cultural artefact</b> (see <a href="/wiki/American_and_British_English_spelling_differences#Miscellaneous_spelling_differences" title="American and British English spelling differences">American and British English spelling differences</a>), is a term used in the <a href="/wiki/Social_sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Social sciences">social sciences</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ethnology" title="Ethnology">ethnology</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> for anything created by <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a> which gives information about the <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a> of its creator and users.  <i>Artifact</i> is the spelling in <a href="/wiki/North_American_English" title="North American English">North American English</a>; <i>artefact</i> is usually preferred elsewhere.  <a href="/wiki/Archeology" class="mw-redirect" title="Archeology">Archeologists</a>, the people who mostly find or describe older artifacts, generally just use the term <a href="/wiki/Artifact_(archaeology)" title="Artifact (archaeology)">artifact</a>.
</p><p>Cultural artifact is a more generic term and should be considered with two words of similar, but narrower, nuance: it can include objects recovered from <a href="/wiki/Archaeological_site" title="Archaeological site">archaeological sites</a>, i.e. <a href="/wiki/Artifact_(archaeology)" title="Artifact (archaeology)">archaeological artifacts</a>, but can also include objects of modern or early-modern society, or <b>social artifacts</b>. For example, in an anthropological context: a 17th-century <a href="/wiki/Lathe" title="Lathe">lathe</a>, a piece of <a href="/wiki/Faience" title="Faience">faience</a>, or a <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a> each provides a wealth of information about the time in which they were manufactured and used.
</p><p>Cultural artifacts, whether ancient or current, have a significance because they offer an insight into: technological processes, economic development and social structure, among other attributes.
</p><p>The philosopher <a href="/wiki/Marx_W._Wartofsky" title="Marx W. Wartofsky">Marx W. Wartofsky</a> categorised artifacts as follows:<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>primary artifacts: used in production (such as a hammer, a fork, a lamp or a camera);</li>
<li>secondary artifacts: relating to primary artifacts (such as a user-manual for a camera);</li>
<li>tertiary artifacts: representations of secondary artifacts (such as a picture of a user-manual for a camera).</li></ul>
<p>Social artifacts, unlike archaeological artifacts, do not need to have a physical form (for example <a href="/wiki/Virtual_artifact" title="Virtual artifact">virtual artifact</a>), nor to be of historical value (items created seconds ago can be classified as social artifacts).
</p>
