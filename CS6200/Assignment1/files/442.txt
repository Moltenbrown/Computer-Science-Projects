<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyfidelity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The practices and beliefs underlying polyfidelity have long existed, but in uncodified fashion. The <a href="/wiki/Oneida_Society" class="mw-redirect" title="Oneida Society">Oneida Commune</a> of the mid-19th century practiced <b>complex marriage</b>, encouraging individual members in the freedom to have multiple ongoing sexual relationships within the community, as an expression of their beliefs and religious faith. This was occasionally referred to as a <a href="/wiki/Group_marriage" title="Group marriage">group marriage</a>, a term brought back to popular recognition by the 1974 publication of <i>Group Marriage: a study of contemporary multilateral marriage</i> by <a href="/wiki/Larry_Constantine" title="Larry Constantine">Larry Constantine</a> and Joan Constantine.
</p><p>The term <b>polyfidelity</b> was coined in the "New Tribe" of the <a href="/wiki/Kerista" title="Kerista">Kerista</a> Commune.<sup id="cite_ref-Miller1999_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miller1999-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  The community at first expected all of its members, within bounds of <a href="/wiki/Gender" title="Gender">gender</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sexual_orientation" title="Sexual orientation">sexual orientation</a>, to be sexually active with all other members, and for <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">exclusive relationships</a> not to be formed within the group. Adding new members would require <a href="/wiki/Consensus" class="mw-redirect" title="Consensus">consensus</a> rather than violate the fundamental compact. 
</p><p>The broader term <i>polyamory</i> was coined later, in the early 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag needs further explanation. (September 2018)">further explanation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyfidelity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Polyfidelitous relationships are, like <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamous</a> relationships, <i>closed</i> in the sense that partners agree not to be sexual with someone not in the relationship. The difference is that more than two people are included in the closed group. New members may generally be added to the group only by unanimous agreement of the existing members, or the group may not be interested in further expansion.
</p><p>While being a subtype of the more general <a href="/wiki/Polyamory" title="Polyamory">polyamory</a>, polyfidelity can resemble monogamy in its relationship power dynamics, attitudes towards autonomy and group consent, as most often polyfidelity develops from an established closed-monogamous couple seeking to add one or more individuals or another couple. In this sense, polyfidelity expands upon standard practices and beliefs of monogamy while still being categorically <a href="/wiki/Polyamory" title="Polyamory">polyamory</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits_and_challenges">Benefits and challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyfidelity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Benefits and challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A commonly cited advantage of polyfidelity is the ability to "fluid bond" among more than two people while maintaining relative safety regarding <a href="/wiki/Sexually_transmitted_disease" class="mw-redirect" title="Sexually transmitted disease">STDs</a>, so long as any new members are sufficiently <a href="/wiki/Sexually_transmitted_infection#Diagnosis" title="Sexually transmitted infection">tested</a> before fluid bonding with the group, and keep their commitments.  This would have health advantages similar to <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamy</a>, although risks rise somewhat with each person added.
</p><p>Some gain a sense of emotional safety from the relatively closed nature of the polyfaithful commitment.
</p><p>Polyfidelity inherently affords less flexibility than other forms of nonmonogamy. For example, <a href="/wiki/Open_relationship" title="Open relationship">open relationships</a> do not necessarily restrict sexual and emotional bonding to such a degree.  
</p><p>As many polyfidelitous people have transitioned directly from closed monogamy, they can encounter problems in learning to communicate intimately with more than one partner.
</p><p>People hoping to create or expand a group marriage mention difficulty finding potential partners with enough mutual compatibility to even consider attempting a relationship.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_usage">Other usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyfidelity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the book <i>Lesbian Polyfidelity</i>, author <a href="/wiki/Celeste_West" title="Celeste West">Celeste West</a> uses the term <i>polyfidelity</i> in much the same way that others use polyamory.  This may represent independent coinage of the same term within a different community, and this usage is not common among polyamorists in general.  West uses the term to emphasize the concept (common in polyamory) that one can be faithful to one's commitments without those commitments including sexual exclusivity.
</p>
