	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Transmission of the Greek Classics</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sanzio_01_cropped.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Sanzio_01_cropped.png/400px-Sanzio_01_cropped.png" decoding="async" width="400" height="223" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Sanzio_01_cropped.png/600px-Sanzio_01_cropped.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Sanzio_01_cropped.png/800px-Sanzio_01_cropped.png 2x" data-file-width="2382" data-file-height="1326" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sanzio_01_cropped.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The ideas of <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>, shown in <a href="/wiki/Raphael" title="Raphael">Raphael</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_School_of_Athens" title="The School of Athens">The School of Athens</a></i>, were partly lost to Western Europeans for centuries.</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>transmission of the Greek Classics</b> to <a href="/wiki/Latin_West" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin West"><i>Latin</i> Western Europe</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> was a key factor in the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_of_the_12th_century" title="Renaissance of the 12th century">development of intellectual life</a> in Western Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-MP_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MP-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Interest in Greek texts and their availability was scarce in the Latin West during the earlier Middle Ages, but as traffic to the East increased so did Western scholarship.
</p><p>Classical <a href="/wiki/Greek_philosophy" class="mw-redirect" title="Greek philosophy">Greek philosophy</a> consisted of various original works ranging from those from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greece</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>) to those Greco-Roman scholars in the classical <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>). Though these works were originally written in Greek, for centuries the language of scholarship in the Mediterranean region, many were translated into <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">Syriac</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a> during the Middle Ages and the original Greek versions were often unknown to the West. With increasing Western presence in the East due to the <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">Crusades</a>, and the gradual collapse of the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a> during the later Middle Ages, many <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Greek" class="mw-redirect" title="Byzantine Greek">Byzantine Greek</a> scholars fled to Western Europe bringing with them many original Greek manuscripts, and providing impetus for <a href="/wiki/Greek_scholars_in_the_Renaissance" title="Greek scholars in the Renaissance">Greek-language education in the West</a> and further translation efforts of Greek scholarship into Latin.<sup id="cite_ref-Vassilev_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vassilev-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The line between <i>Greek</i> scholarship and <i>Arab</i> scholarship in Western Europe was very blurred during the Middle Ages and the early Modern Period. Thus, depending on context, the concept of the transmission of <i>Greek Classics</i> is often used to refer to the collective knowledge that was obtained from the Arab and Byzantine Empires, regardless of where the knowledge actually originated.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_reception_of_Greek_texts">Direct reception of Greek texts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transmission_of_the_Greek_Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Direct reception of Greek texts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As knowledge of Greek declined in the west with the fall of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a>, so did knowledge of the Greek texts, many of which had remained without a Latin translation.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The fragile nature of <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">papyrus</a>, as a writing medium, meant that older texts not copied onto expensive <a href="/wiki/Parchment" title="Parchment">parchment</a> would eventually crumble and be lost.
</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Crusade" title="Fourth Crusade">Fourth Crusade</a> (1202–1204) and the <a href="/wiki/Sack_of_Constantinople" title="Sack of Constantinople">Sack of Constantinople</a> (1204), scholars such as <a href="/wiki/William_of_Moerbeke" title="William of Moerbeke">William of Moerbeke</a> gained access to the original Greek texts of scientists and philosophers, including <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archimedes" title="Archimedes">Archimedes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hero_of_Alexandria" title="Hero of Alexandria">Hero of Alexandria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Proclus" title="Proclus">Proclus</a>, that had been preserved in the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine (Eastern Roman) Empire</a>, and translated them directly into Latin.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The final <a href="/wiki/Byzantine-Ottoman_wars" class="mw-redirect" title="Byzantine-Ottoman wars">decline</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Constantinople" title="Fall of Constantinople">collapse</a> of the Byzantine empire in the fifteenth century heightened contact between its scholars and those of the west.  Translation into Latin of the full range of Greek classics ensued, including the historians, poets, playwrights and non-Aristotelian philosophers.  <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Chrysoloras" title="Manuel Chrysoloras">Manuel Chrysoloras</a> (c. 1355–1415) translated portions of <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Guarino_da_Verona" title="Guarino da Verona">Guarino da Verona</a> (1370–1460) translated <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Poggio_Bracciolini" title="Poggio Bracciolini">Poggio Bracciolini</a> (1380–1459) translated <a href="/wiki/Xenophon" title="Xenophon">Xenophon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lucan" title="Lucan">Lucan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diodorus" class="mw-redirect" title="Diodorus">Diodorus</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Filelfo" title="Francesco Filelfo">Francesco Filelfo</a> (1398–1481) translated portions of <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xenophon" title="Xenophon">Xenophon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lysias" title="Lysias">Lysias</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_Valla" title="Lorenzo Valla">Lorenzo Valla</a> (1407–1457) translated <a href="/wiki/Thucydides" title="Thucydides">Thucydides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Marsilio_Ficino" title="Marsilio Ficino">Marsilio Ficino</a> (1433–1499) and his <a href="/wiki/Platonic_Academy_(Florence)" title="Platonic Academy (Florence)">Platonic Academy</a> translated Plato.  <a href="/wiki/Poliziano" title="Poliziano">Poliziano</a> (1454–1494) translated <a href="/wiki/Herodian" title="Herodian">Herodian</a> and portions of <a href="/wiki/Epictetus" title="Epictetus">Epictetus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a>. <a href="/wiki/Regiomontanus" title="Regiomontanus">Regiomontanus</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_of_Trebizond" title="George of Trebizond">George of Trebizond</a> translated Ptolemy's <a href="/wiki/Almagest" title="Almagest">Almagest</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Important patrons were <a href="/wiki/Basilios_Bessarion" title="Basilios Bessarion">Basilios Bessarion</a> (1403–1472) and Pope <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_V" class="mw-redirect" title="Nicholas V">Nicholas V</a> (1397–1455).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Syriac_translations">Syriac translations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transmission_of_the_Greek_Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Syriac translations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Syriac plays an important role in modern text-criticism even today. The Oxford Classical issue of the Greek text of Aristotle's <i><a href="/wiki/Organon" title="Organon">Organon</a></i> uses the sigla Ρ, Ι, and  Γ, which are texts dating from Christian possessions from the 6th to 8th century.<sup id="cite_ref-minio_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-minio-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">Syriac</a> translations played a major role for the later reception into Arabic. These translators from Syriac were mostly <a href="/wiki/Nestorianism" title="Nestorianism">Nestorian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jacobite_Syrian_Christian_Church" title="Jacobite Syrian Christian Church">Jacobite Christians</a>, working in the two hundred years following the <a href="/wiki/Abbasid" class="mw-redirect" title="Abbasid">Abbasid</a> period.  The most important translator of this group was the Syriac-speaking Christian <a href="/wiki/Hunayn_Ibn_Ishaq" class="mw-redirect" title="Hunayn Ibn Ishaq">Hunayn Ibn Ishaq</a> (809-873), known to the Latins as <i>Joannitius</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_Roman_Empire">Western Roman Empire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transmission_of_the_Greek_Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Western Roman Empire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Classical Greek learning was firmly found in every metropolis of the Roman empire, including in Rome itself.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boethius">Boethius</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transmission_of_the_Greek_Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Boethius">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Rome, <a href="/wiki/Boethius" title="Boethius">Boethius</a> propagated works of Greek classical learning. Boethius intended to pass on the great Greco-Roman culture to future generations by writing manuals on music and astronomy, geometry, and arithmetic.<sup id="cite_ref-po_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-po-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Several of Boethius' writings, which were largely influential during the Middle Ages, drew from the thinking of <a href="/wiki/Porphyry_(philosopher)" title="Porphyry (philosopher)">Porphyry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iamblichus" title="Iamblichus">Iamblichus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-an_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-an-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Boethius wrote a commentary on the <i><a href="/wiki/Isagoge" title="Isagoge">Isagoge</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Porphyry_(philosopher)" title="Porphyry (philosopher)">Porphyry</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-CE_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CE-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  which highlighted the existence of the <a href="/wiki/Problem_of_universals" title="Problem of universals">problem of universals</a>: whether these concepts are subsistent entities which would exist whether anyone thought of them, or whether they only exist as ideas. This topic concerning the <a href="/wiki/Ontological" class="mw-redirect" title="Ontological">ontological</a> nature of universal ideas was one of the most vocal controversies in <a href="/wiki/Medieval_philosophy" title="Medieval philosophy">medieval philosophy</a>.
</p><p>Besides these advanced philosophical works, Boethius is also reported to have translated important Greek texts for the topics of the <a href="/wiki/Quadrivium" title="Quadrivium">quadrivium</a>. His loose translation of <a href="/wiki/Nicomachus" title="Nicomachus">Nicomachus</a>'s treatise on arithmetic (<i>De institutione arithmetica libri duo</i>) and his textbook on music (<i>De institutione musica libri quinque</i>, unfinished) contributed to medieval education.<sup id="cite_ref-CE_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CE-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <i>De arithmetica</i>, begins with modular arithmetic, such as even and odd, evenly even, evenly odd, and oddly even.  He then turns to unpredicted complexity by categorizing numbers and parts of numbers.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>His translations of <a href="/wiki/Euclid" title="Euclid">Euclid</a> on geometry and <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a> on astronomy,<sup id="cite_ref-a_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-a-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  if they were completed, no longer survive. Boethius made Latin translations of Aristotle's <i>De interpretation</i> and <i>Categories</i> with commentaries.  These were widely used during the Middle Ages.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Middle_Ages_in_the_Western_Provinces">Early Middle Ages in the Western Provinces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transmission_of_the_Greek_Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early Middle Ages in the Western Provinces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the Western Provinces (what today is considered the Western Europe's heartland), the collapsing Roman empire lost many Greek manuscripts which were not preserved by monasteries. However, due to the expense and dearth of writing materials, monastic scribes could recycle old parchments.  The parchments could be reused after scraping off the ink of the old texts, and writing new books on the previously used parchment, creating what is called a <a href="/wiki/Palimpsest" title="Palimpsest">palimpsest</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Fortunately for modern scholars, the old writing can still be retrieved, and many extremely valuable works, which would have otherwise been lost, have been recovered in this way. As the language of Roman aristocrats and scholars, Greek died off along with the Roman Empire in the West, and by 500 CE, almost no one in Western Europe was able to read (or translate) Greek texts, and with the rise of the Islamic Empire, the west was further cut off from the language. After a while, only a few <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monasteries</a> in the west had Greek works, and even fewer of them copied these works (mainly the <a href="/wiki/Christian_monasticism#Ireland" title="Christian monasticism">Irish</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Laughlin_139_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Laughlin_139-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Some Irish monks had been taught by Greek and Latin missionaries who probably had brought Greek texts with them.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_Middle_Ages:_William_of_Moerbeke">Late Middle Ages: William of Moerbeke</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transmission_of_the_Greek_Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Late Middle Ages: William of Moerbeke">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/William_of_Moerbeke" title="William of Moerbeke">William of Moerbeke</a> was one of the most prolific and influential translators of Greek philosophical texts in the middle half of the thirteenth century.  Very little is known of William's life.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  He was born probably in 1215 in the village of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moerbeke_(Geraardsbergen)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Moerbeke (Geraardsbergen) (page does not exist)">Moerbeke</a>, now in <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, and probably entered the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominican</a> priory in <a href="/wiki/Leuven" title="Leuven">Leuven</a> as a young man.  Most of his surviving work was done during 1259-72.
</p><p>Though William's contribution to the <a href="/wiki/Recovery_of_Aristotle" title="Recovery of Aristotle">"recovery" of Aristotle</a>, in the 13th century, was not as significant as is sometimes claimed, his work undoubtedly helped in forming a clearer picture of Greek philosophy, and particularly of Aristotle, than was given by the Arabic versions on which they had previously relied, and which had distorted or obscured the relation between Platonic and Aristotelian systems of philosophy.<sup id="cite_ref-Fryde_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fryde-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  William's translation of <a href="/wiki/Proclus" title="Proclus">Proclus</a> was also important, demonstrating that the influential book <i><a href="/wiki/Liber_de_Causis" title="Liber de Causis">Liber de Causis</a></i>, was not a genuine work of Aristotle, but rather derived from Proclus' <i>Elementatio Theologica</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to a tradition originating in the later Middle Ages, William knew <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas" title="Thomas Aquinas">Thomas Aquinas</a> and was commissioned by him to make some of the translations.  But there is no contemporary record of the friendship or the commissions.  If they did meet, it is most likely during the three or four years Aquinas was working at <a href="/wiki/Orvieto" title="Orvieto">Orvieto</a>, i.e. not before the election of Pope Urban IV in August 1261, who invited Aquinas to serve at the Papal court, and not after 1265, when Aquinas left for Rome.  His translation of <i>De motu animalium</i> is cited by Thomas in <i><a href="/wiki/Summa_Contra_Gentiles" class="mw-redirect" title="Summa Contra Gentiles">Summa Contra Gentiles</a></i>, probably completed in 1264.<sup id="cite_ref-Fryde_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fryde-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arabic_translations_and_commentary">Arabic translations and commentary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transmission_of_the_Greek_Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Arabic translations and commentary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Islamic_world_contributions_to_Medieval_Europe" title="Islamic world contributions to Medieval Europe">Islamic world contributions to Medieval Europe</a></div>
<p>Arabic logicians had inherited Greek ideas after they had invaded and conquered <a href="/wiki/Muslim_conquest_of_Egypt" title="Muslim conquest of Egypt">Egypt</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Muslim_conquest_of_the_Levant" title="Muslim conquest of the Levant">Levant</a>. Their translations and commentaries on these ideas worked their way through the Arab West into <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>, which became important centers for this transmission of ideas. <sup id="cite_ref-MP_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MP-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Western Arabic translations of Greek works (found in Iberia and Sicily) originates in the Greek sources preserved by the Byzantines.  These transmissions to the Arab West took place in two main stages.
</p>
<h3><span id="First_period:_Greek.E2.80.93Arabic_translations"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="First_period:_Greek–Arabic_translations">First period: Greek–Arabic translations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transmission_of_the_Greek_Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: First period: Greek–Arabic translations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Early_Islamic_philosophy" title="Early Islamic philosophy">Early Islamic philosophy</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SophoclesTurk.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/ec/SophoclesTurk.gif/220px-SophoclesTurk.gif" decoding="async" width="220" height="238" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/ec/SophoclesTurk.gif/330px-SophoclesTurk.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/ec/SophoclesTurk.gif/440px-SophoclesTurk.gif 2x" data-file-width="510" data-file-height="551" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SophoclesTurk.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An Arab's depiction of <a href="/wiki/Socrates" title="Socrates">Socrates</a> teaching his students.</div></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Umayyads">Umayyads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transmission_of_the_Greek_Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Umayyads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The first period of transmission during 8th and 9th centuries was preceded by a period of conquest, as Arabs took control of previously Hellenized areas such as <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a> in the 7th century.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>  At this point they first began to encounter Greek ideas, though from the beginning, many Arabs were hostile to classical learning.<sup id="cite_ref-ros_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ros-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Because of this hostility, the religious Caliphs could not support scientific translations.  Translators had to seek out wealthy business patrons rather than religious ones.<sup id="cite_ref-ros_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ros-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Until <a href="/wiki/Abbasid" class="mw-redirect" title="Abbasid">Abbasid</a> rule in the 8th century, however, there was little work in translation.  Most knowledge of Greek during Umayyad rule was gained from those scholars of Greek who remained from the Byzantine period, rather than through widespread translation and dissemination of texts. A few scholars argue that translation was more widespread than is thought during this period, but theirs remains the minority view.<sup id="cite_ref-ros_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ros-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Abbasids">Abbasids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transmission_of_the_Greek_Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Abbasids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The main period of translation was during Abbasid rule. The 2nd Abbasid Caliph <a href="/wiki/Al-Mansur" title="Al-Mansur">al-Mansur</a> moved the capital from <a href="/wiki/Damascus" title="Damascus">Damascus</a> to <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">Baghdad</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-anti_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anti-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Here he founded the great library with texts containing Greek Classical texts. Al-Mansur ordered this rich fund of world literature translated into Arabic. Under al-Mansur and by his orders, translations were made from Greek, Syriac, and Persian, the Syriac and Persian books being themselves translations from Greek or Sanskrit.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEO&#39;Leary1922107_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEO&#39;Leary1922107-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 6th-century King of Persia, Anushirvan (<a href="/wiki/Chosroes_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Chosroes I">Chosroes I</a>) the Just, had introduced many Greek ideas into his kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-brick_1_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brick_1-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Aided by this knowledge and juxtaposition of beliefs, the Abbasids considered it valuable to look at Islam with Greek eyes, and to look at the Greeks with Islamic eyes.<sup id="cite_ref-ros_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ros-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>  Abbasid philosophers also pressed the idea that Islam had from the very beginning stressed the gathering of knowledge as important to the religion.  These new lines of thought allowed the work of amassing and translating Greek ideas to expand as it never before had.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span id="Baghdad.27s_House_of_Wisdom"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Baghdad's_House_of_Wisdom">Baghdad's House of Wisdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transmission_of_the_Greek_Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Baghdad&#039;s House of Wisdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/House_of_Wisdom" title="House of Wisdom">House of Wisdom</a></div>
<p>The Caliph al-Mansur was the patron who did most to attract the <a href="/wiki/Nestorian" class="mw-redirect" title="Nestorian">Nestorian</a> physicians to the city of Baghdad which he had founded, and he was also a prince who did much to encourage those who set themselves to prepare Arabic translations of Greek, Syriac, and Persian works. Still more important was the patronage given by the Caliph <a href="/wiki/Al-Ma%27mun" title="Al-Ma&#39;mun">al-Ma'mun</a>, who in <a href="/wiki/Anno_Hegirae" class="mw-redirect" title="Anno Hegirae">A.H.</a> 217 (= A.D. 832) founded a school at Baghdad, suggested no doubt by the Nestorians and Zoroastrian schools already existing, and this he called the <a href="/wiki/Bayt_al-Hikma" class="mw-redirect" title="Bayt al-Hikma">Bayt al-Hikma</a> or "House of Wisdom", and this he placed under the guidance of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yahya_ibn_Masawaih&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Yahya ibn Masawaih (page does not exist)">Yahya ibn Masawaih</a> (d. A.H. 243 = A.D. 857), who was an author both in Syriac and Arabic, and learned also in the use of Greek. His medical treatise on "Fevers" was long in repute and was afterwards translated into Latin and into Hebrew.
</p><p>The most important work of the academy however was done by Yahya's pupils and successors, especially <a href="/wiki/Abu_Zayd_Hunayn_ibn_Ishaq_al-Ibadi" class="mw-redirect" title="Abu Zayd Hunayn ibn Ishaq al-Ibadi">Abu Zayd Hunayn ibn Ishaq al-Ibadi</a> (d. 263 A.H. = A.D. 876), the Nestorian physician to whom we have already referred as translating into Syriac the chief medical authorities as well as parts of Aristotle's <i>Organon</i>. After studying at Baghdad under Yahya he visited Alexandria and returned, not only with the training given at what was then the first medical school, but with a good knowledge of Greek which he employed in making translations in Syriac and Arabic.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEO&#39;Leary1922112_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEO&#39;Leary1922112-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Later the Caliph al-Mamun also sent emissaries to the Byzantines to gather Greek manuscripts for his new university, making it a center for Greek translation work in the Arab world.<sup id="cite_ref-brick_1_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brick_1-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> At first only practical works, such as those on medicine and technology were sought after, but eventually works on philosophy became popular.<sup id="cite_ref-lind_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lind-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-long_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-long-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most scholars agree that during this period rhetoric, poetry, histories, and dramas were not translated into Arabic, since they were viewed as serving political ends which were not to be sought after in Arab states. Instead, philosophical and scientific works were almost the entire focus of translation. This has been disputed by a minority of scholars, however, who argue that stories such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Arabian_Nights" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabian Nights">Arabian Nights</a></i> carry clear parallels to Greek literature—evidence that many Arabs were familiar with Greek humanities more than is thought.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_translation:_Arabic_commentary_on_Greek_works">After translation: Arabic commentary on Greek works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transmission_of_the_Greek_Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: After translation: Arabic commentary on Greek works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="box-Expert_needed plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/40px-Ambox_important.svg.png" decoding="async" width="40" height="40" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/60px-Ambox_important.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/80px-Ambox_important.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="40" data-file-height="40" /></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This article <b>needs attention from an expert in Philosophy</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please add a <i>reason</i> or a <i>talk</i> parameter to this template to explain the issue with the article. <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:WikiProject_Philosophy" title="Wikipedia:WikiProject Philosophy">WikiProject Philosophy</a> may be able to help recruit an expert.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">February 2009</span>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Arabic_aristotle.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Arabic_aristotle.jpg/220px-Arabic_aristotle.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="323" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Arabic_aristotle.jpg/330px-Arabic_aristotle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Arabic_aristotle.jpg/440px-Arabic_aristotle.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1064" data-file-height="1563" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Arabic_aristotle.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A medieval Arabic representation of <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> teaching a student.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Al-Kindi" title="Al-Kindi">Al-Kindi</a> (<i>Alkindus</i>), a famous <a href="/wiki/Logic_in_Islamic_philosophy" title="Logic in Islamic philosophy">logician</a> and prominent figure in the House of Wisdom, is unanimously hailed as the "father of <a href="/wiki/Islamic_philosophy" title="Islamic philosophy">Islamic or Arabic philosophy</a>". His synthesis of Greek philosophy with <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islamic</a> beliefs met with much opposition, and at one point he was flogged by those opposed to his ideas. He argued that one could accept the <a href="/wiki/Koran" class="mw-redirect" title="Koran">Koran</a> and other sacred texts, and work from that point to determine <a href="/wiki/Truth" title="Truth">truth</a>. Whenever he ran into an <a href="/wiki/Impasse" title="Impasse">impasse</a>, he would abandon the Greek ideas in favor of the Islamic faith.<sup id="cite_ref-brick_1_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brick_1-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-laugh_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-laugh-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> He is considered to be largely responsible for pulling the <a href="/wiki/Arab_world" title="Arab world">Arab world</a> out of a mystic and <a href="/wiki/Islamic_theology" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic theology">theological way</a> of thinking into a more rationalistic mode.<sup id="cite_ref-laugh_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-laugh-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Previous to al-Kindi, for example, on the question of how the immaterial God of the Koran could sit on a throne in the same book, one theologist had said, “The sitting is known, its modality is unknown. Belief in it is a necessity, and raising questions regarding it is a heresy.” Few of al-Kindi's writings have survived, making it difficult to judge his work directly, but it is clear from what exists that he carefully worked to present his ideas in a way acceptable to other Muslims.<sup id="cite_ref-laugh_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-laugh-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After Al-Kindi, several philosophers argued more radical views, some of whom even rejected <a href="/wiki/Revelation" title="Revelation">revelation</a>, most notably the <a href="/wiki/Iranian_philosophy" title="Iranian philosophy">Persian logician</a>, <a href="/wiki/Al-Razi" title="Al-Razi">Al-Razi</a> or “Rhazes.” Considered one of the most original thinkers among the Persian philosophers<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (November 2010)">by whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, he challenged both Islamic and Greek ideas in a rationalist manner. Also, where Al-Kindi had focused on <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, Al-Rhazi focused on <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>, introducing his ideas as a contrast.<sup id="cite_ref-laugh_28-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-laugh-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After Al-Kindi, <a href="/wiki/Al-Farabi" title="Al-Farabi">Al-Farabi</a> (<i>Alpharabius</i>) introduced <a href="/wiki/Neoplatonism" title="Neoplatonism">Neoplatonism</a> through his knowledge of the <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Hellenistic culture">Hellenistic culture</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a>. Unlike Al-Kindi or Al-Rhazi, Al-Farabi was hesitant to express his own feelings on issues of religion and philosophy, choosing rather to speak only through the words of the various philosophies he came across.<sup id="cite_ref-laugh_28-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-laugh-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Decades after Al-Farabi, <a href="/wiki/Avicenna" title="Avicenna">Ibn Sina</a> (<i>Avicenna</i>) compiled the ideas of many Muslim philosophers of the previous centuries and established a new school which is known as <a href="/wiki/Avicennism" title="Avicennism">Avicennism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-brick_1_22-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brick_1-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-laugh_28-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-laugh-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>  After this period, Greek philosophy went into a decline in the Islamic world. Theologians such as <a href="/wiki/Al-Ghazali" title="Al-Ghazali">Al-Ghazali</a> argued that many realms of logic only worked in theory, not in reality.<sup id="cite_ref-laugh_28-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-laugh-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> His ideas would later influence Western European religious ideas.<sup id="cite_ref-brick_1_22-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brick_1-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> In response to Al-Ghazali's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Incoherence_of_the_Philosophers" title="The Incoherence of the Philosophers">The Incoherence of the Philosophers</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">Andalusian</a> philosopher <a href="/wiki/Averroes" title="Averroes">Ibn Rushd</a> (<i>Averroes</i>), the most famous commentator on Aristotle and founder of <a href="/wiki/Averroism" title="Averroism">Averroism</a>, wrote a refutation entitled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Incoherence_of_the_Incoherence" title="The Incoherence of the Incoherence">The Incoherence of the Incoherence</a></i>.
</p><p>By 1200, when <a href="/wiki/Islamic_philosophy" title="Islamic philosophy">philosophy was again revived in the Islamic world</a>, Al-Kindi and Al-Farabi were no longer remembered, while Ibn Sina's <a href="/wiki/The_Book_of_Healing" title="The Book of Healing">compilation work</a> still was.<sup id="cite_ref-sina_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sina-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Ibn Sina, otherwise known as Avicenna, would later heavily influence European philosophical, theological and scientific thought, becoming known as “the most famous scientist of Islam” to many Western historians.<sup id="cite_ref-brick_1_22-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brick_1-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_European_reception_of_Greek_ideas_via_the_Arabian_tradition">Western European reception of Greek ideas via the Arabian tradition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transmission_of_the_Greek_Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Western European reception of Greek ideas via the Arabian tradition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While Greek ideas gradually permeated the Islamic world, Muslims conquests extended to the European continent. <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_conquest_of_Hispania" title="Umayyad conquest of Hispania">Spain</a> was <a href="/wiki/Muslim_conquests" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim conquests">conquered by the Arabs</a> around 700 AD, even reaching as far as Poitiers, France by 732 (<a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tours" title="Battle of Tours">Battle of Tours</a>). By 902 <a href="/wiki/Muslim_conquest_of_Sicily" title="Muslim conquest of Sicily">Sicily</a> was conquered. With the aid of Greek and other ideas, <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">Spain</a> in particular quickly became the most heavily populated and thriving area in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-sina_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sina-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> One of the rulers of Muslim Spain, <a href="/wiki/Al-Hakam_II" title="Al-Hakam II">Al-Hakam II</a>,  made an effort to gather books from all over the Arab world, creating a library which would later become a center for translation into Latin.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As books were gathered, so were many <a href="/wiki/Arab" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab">Arab</a> scholars who had studied Greek ideas in the east. For example, <a href="/wiki/Mohammed_ibn_Abdun_Jabali" class="mw-redirect" title="Mohammed ibn Abdun Jabali">Muhammud ibn 'Abdun</a> and 'Abdu'l-Rahman ibn Ismail came to Spain and introduced many ideas about <a href="/wiki/Islamic_medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic medicine">medicine</a> as well as several of the works of Aristotle and <a href="/wiki/Euclid" title="Euclid">Euclid</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Bajjah" class="mw-redirect" title="Ibn Bajjah">Ibn Bajjah</a> (known as "Avempace") and <a href="/wiki/Averroes" title="Averroes">Ibn Rushd</a> (known as “Averroes”) were among the other famous philosophers of Spain who furthered the expansion of Greek ideas in medicine and philosophy.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Prior to Averroes, many Arab philosophers had confused Aristotle with <a href="/wiki/Plotinus" title="Plotinus">Plotinus</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Hellenization" title="Hellenization">Hellenized</a> <a href="/wiki/History_of_Roman_Egypt" class="mw-redirect" title="History of Roman Egypt">Egyptian</a> who founded <a href="/wiki/Neoplatonism" title="Neoplatonism">Neoplatonism</a> and had mixed Aristotle's ideas with Plato's. Averroes rediscovered the “true” Aristotle by translating key texts reintroducing him to <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">Arab Spain</a>. He also challenged Al-Ghazali's largely anti-Greek philosophies and offered some of the best reconciliation of Islam and philosophy of the time.<sup id="cite_ref-Averr_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Averr-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Key to his arguments was the idea that although there was only one truth, that truth could be expressed in many ways, including both philosophy and religion. He even used the <a href="/wiki/Qur%27an" class="mw-redirect" title="Qur&#39;an">Qur'an</a> to back up his arguments in favor of Greek philosophy and logic, especially the passage: “It is He, [O Muhammad] who has revealed the Book to you...some of its verses are unambiguous...and the others are ambiguous...only God and those confirmed in knowledge know its interpretation.”  Averroes argued that “those confirmed in knowledge” were philosophers.<sup id="cite_ref-Averr_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Averr-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Scholasticism" title="Scholasticism">Scholastic</a> philosophers and theologians of the Middle Ages such as <a href="/wiki/Aquinas" class="mw-redirect" title="Aquinas">Aquinas</a> later called Averroes “The Commentator,” and <a href="/wiki/Michael_the_Scot" class="mw-redirect" title="Michael the Scot">Michael the Scot</a> translated several of Averroes' works within fifty years of the Arab's death. However, Averroes' reception in Western Europe contrasted with his ultimate rejection by Arabs in Spain.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>  Soon after Averroes, Greek ideas in the Arab world were largely opposed by those who disliked anything not “truly Arab.”<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Arabic:_Latin_or_Vernacular">Arabic: Latin or Vernacular</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transmission_of_the_Greek_Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Arabic: Latin or Vernacular">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Latin_translations_of_the_12th_century" title="Latin translations of the 12th century">Latin translations of the 12th century</a></div>
<p>While Muslims were busy translating and adding their own ideas to Greek philosophies, the Latin West was still suspicious of pagan ideas. Leaders of the Orthodox Church in the Byzantine Empire also frowned upon philosophy, and the Empire had just gone through a period of plague, famine, and war.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Further west, several key figures in European history who came after Boethius had strengthened the overwhelming shift away from Greek ideas. For centuries, Greek ideas in Europe were all but non-existent, until the Eastern part of the Roman Empire – Byzantine – was sacked during the crusades unlocking numerous Greek texts.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Within Western Europe, only a few monasteries had Greek works, and even fewer of them copied these works.<sup id="cite_ref-Laughlin_139_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Laughlin_139-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There was a brief period of revival, when the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon">Anglo-Saxon</a> monk <a href="/wiki/Alcuin" title="Alcuin">Alcuin</a> and others reintroduced some Greek ideas during the <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_Renaissance" title="Carolingian Renaissance">Carolingian Renaissance</a> of the 8th century.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> After Charlemagne's death, however, intellectual life again fell into decline.<sup id="cite_ref-char_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-char-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> By the 12th century, however, <a href="/wiki/Scholasticism" title="Scholasticism">scholastic</a> thought was beginning to develop, leading to the rise of <a href="/wiki/Universities" class="mw-redirect" title="Universities">universities</a> throughout Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-schol_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-schol-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> These universities gathered what little Greek thought had been preserved over the centuries, including Boethius' commentaries on Aristotle. They also served as places of discussion for new ideas coming from new translations from Arabic throughout Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-schol_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-schol-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By the 12th century, European fear of Islam as a military threat had lessened somewhat. <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Spain" title="Toledo, Spain">Toledo</a>, in Spain, had fallen from Arab hands in 1085, Sicily in 1091, and Jerusalem in 1099.<sup id="cite_ref-watt_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-watt-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-conq_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-conq-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> These linguistic borderlands proved fertile ground for translators. These areas had been conquered by Arab Greek and Latin-speaking peoples over the centuries and contained linguistic abilities from all these cultures.  The small and unscholarly population of the <a href="/wiki/Crusader_Kingdoms" class="mw-redirect" title="Crusader Kingdoms">Crusader Kingdoms</a>  contributed very little to the translation efforts, until the Fourth Crusade took most of the Byzantine Empire. Sicily, still largely Greek-speaking was more productive; it had seen rule under Byzantines, Arabs, and Italians, and many were fluent in Greek, Arabic, and Latin. Sicilians, however, were less influenced by Arabs and instead are noted more for their translations directly from Greek to Latin.<sup id="cite_ref-conq_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-conq-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Spain, on the other hand, was an ideal place for translation from Arabic to Latin because of a combination of rich Latin and Arab cultures living side by side.<sup id="cite_ref-conq_41-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-conq-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Spain_and_Sicily">Spain and Sicily</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transmission_of_the_Greek_Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Spain and Sicily">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>As early as the 10th century, scholars in <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">Andalusia</a> had begun to gather translated texts, and in the latter half of that century began transmitting them to the rest of Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> After the <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a> of the 12th century, however, Spain opened even further for Christian scholars, who were now able to work in “friendly” religious territory.<sup id="cite_ref-spain_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spain-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> As these Europeans encountered Islamic philosophy, their previously held fears turned to admiration, and from Spain came a wealth of Arab knowledge of mathematics and astronomy.<sup id="cite_ref-watt_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-watt-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>   Foreigners came to Spain to translate from all over Europe,<sup id="cite_ref-spain_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spain-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Spain" title="Toledo, Spain">Toledo</a> became a center for such travelers, since so many of its citizens wrote daily in both Arabic and Latin-based languages.
</p><p>Although there was a huge amount of work being accomplished in Spain, there was no central school for translating and no real organized effort, as there had been at times among the Arabs.<sup id="cite_ref-spain_43-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spain-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Translators came from many different backgrounds and translated for many different reasons. For example, non-Christian Jewish scholars participated by translating Arabic works which had already been translated into Hebrew, into Latin and Vulgate languages.<sup id="cite_ref-brick_2_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brick_2-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-jewish_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jewish-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Some scholars, however, have suggested that <a href="/wiki/Raymond_de_Sauvet%C3%A2t" title="Raymond de Sauvetât">Raymond de Sauvetât</a> the Archbishop of Toledo, seems to have started an organized movement of support for translations, and many scholars who seem to be associated with him in documents may have translated two-by-two, working together.<sup id="cite_ref-watt_40-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-watt-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Whether Raimond actually started a truly central, organized effort at translation, later generalized as the <a href="/wiki/Toledo_School_of_Translators" title="Toledo School of Translators">Toledo School of Translators</a>, remains unknown. What is known is that most translations coming out of Spain dealt with either medicine or astronomy. Hugo of Santalla, for example, translated a large selection of Arabic works all dealing with astronomy, as well as tracing the history of astronomic thought through history, underscoring the work of the Greeks, Persians, Hellenists, and Arabs in one large preface to his volume.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By the 13th century, translation had declined in Spain, but it was on the rise in Italy and Sicily, and from there to all of Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-jewish_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jewish-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>  Adelard of Bath, an Englishman, traveled to Sicily and the Arab world, translating works on astronomy and mathematics, including the first complete translation of <a href="/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements" title="Euclid&#39;s Elements">Euclid's Elements</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-brick_2_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brick_2-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-spain_43-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spain-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Powerful Norman kings gathered men of high knowledge from Italy, and other areas, into their courts, as signs of prestige.<sup id="cite_ref-italy_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-italy-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Even the Byzantines experienced an Aristotelian revival in the mid-12th century, and gathered men from Italy as well.<sup id="cite_ref-italy_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-italy-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
