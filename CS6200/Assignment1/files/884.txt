<p><b>Caranus</b> or <b>Karanos</b> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">Κάρανος, Káranos</span>) was the first king of the ancient Greek kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Macedonia_(ancient_kingdom)" title="Macedonia (ancient kingdom)">Macedon</a> according to later traditions. According to <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a>, however, the first king was <a href="/wiki/Perdiccas_I_of_Macedon" title="Perdiccas I of Macedon">Perdiccas I</a>. Caranus is first reported by <a href="/wiki/Theopompus" title="Theopompus">Theopompus</a><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and is the mythical founder of the <a href="/wiki/Argead_dynasty" title="Argead dynasty">Argead dynasty</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Myth">Myth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caranus_of_Macedon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Myth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to a Greek <a href="/wiki/Myth" title="Myth">myth</a>, Caranus was the son of <a href="/wiki/Temenus" title="Temenus">Temenus</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> king of <a href="/wiki/Argos" title="Argos">Argos</a>, who in turn was a <a href="/wiki/Heracleidae" title="Heracleidae">Heraclid</a>, a descendant of <a href="/wiki/Heracles" title="Heracles">Heracles</a>. <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a> agrees on the Heraclid lineage of Caranus and argues that <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a> is a descendant of Heracles through Caranus.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Temenus, along with <a href="/wiki/Cresphontes" title="Cresphontes">Cresphontes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aristodemus" title="Aristodemus">Aristodemus</a> were the three <a href="/wiki/Dorians" title="Dorians">Doric</a> leaders who invaded the <a href="/wiki/Mycenaean_Greece" title="Mycenaean Greece">Mycenean</a> <a href="/wiki/Peloponnese" title="Peloponnese">Peloponnese</a> region. Then they proceeded to divide the conquered territories between them. Cresphontes was given <a href="/wiki/Messenia" title="Messenia">Messenia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sparta" title="Sparta">Sparta</a>; Aristodemus took <a href="/wiki/Laconia" title="Laconia">Laconia</a>; and finally Temenus was given Argos. Following the death of Temenus, the princes argued about who should be king. One of them, <a href="/wiki/Pheidon" title="Pheidon">Pheidon</a>, defeated his brothers in battle and took over the kingship. Caranus then decided to find another kingdom of his own, where he could be king. First, however he went to the <a href="/wiki/Oracle_of_Delphi" class="mw-redirect" title="Oracle of Delphi">Oracle of Delphi</a> to ask <a href="/wiki/Pythia" title="Pythia">Pythia</a>'s advice. "You should find your kingdom there, where you will find plenty of game and domestic animals, she advised."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Inline_citation#When_you_must_use_inline_citations" title="Wikipedia:Inline citation"><span title="The text near this tag needs a citation. (November 2018)">This quote needs a citation</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Thus Caranus and his entourage moved to the North, in search of suitable land to establish his new kingdom. Finally, he discovered a green valley, with a lot of game and goats, whereupon he thought that the <a href="/wiki/Prophecy" title="Prophecy">prophecy</a> of Pythia had been fulfilled. Thus he built a city there, which he named <i>Aigai</i> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el">Αἰγαί</span>), present day <a href="/wiki/Vergina" title="Vergina">Vergina</a>, a site of substantial archaeological activity, as numerous important findings have been unearthed.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="View_of_historians">View of historians</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caranus_of_Macedon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: View of historians">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Justin_(historian)" title="Justin (historian)">Justin</a> (7.1) citing <a href="/wiki/Marsyas_of_Pella" title="Marsyas of Pella">Marsyas of Pella</a>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Caranus also came to <a href="/wiki/Emathia" title="Emathia">Emathia</a> with a large band of Greeks, being instructed by an oracle to seek a home in Macedonia. Here, following a herd of goats running from a downpour, he seized the city of <a href="/wiki/Edessa,_Greece" title="Edessa, Greece">Edessa</a>, the inhabitants being taken unawares because of heavy rain and dense fog. Remembering the oracle’s command to follow the lead of goats in his quest for an empire, Caranus established the city as his capital, and thereafter he made it a solemn observance, wheresoever he took his army, to keep those same goats before his standards in order to have as leaders in his exploits the animals which he had had with him to found the kingdom. He gave the city of Edessa the name Aegae and its people the name Aegeads in memory of this service.
</p></blockquote>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Chronicon_(Eusebius)" title="Chronicon (Eusebius)">Chronicon (Eusebius)</a>
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Before the first Olympiad, Caranus was moved by ambition to collect forces from the Argives and from the rest of the Peloponnese, in order to lead an army into the territory of the Macedonians. At that time the king of the <a href="/wiki/Orestae" class="mw-redirect" title="Orestae">Orestae</a> was at war with his neighbours, the <a href="/wiki/Eordaei" class="mw-redirect" title="Eordaei">Eordaei</a>, and he called on Caranus to come to his aid, promising to give him half of his territory in return, if the Orestae were successful. The king kept his promise, and Caranus took possession of the territory; he reigned there for 30 years, until he died in old age. He was succeeded by his son Coenus, who was king for 28 years.
</p></blockquote>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Livy" title="Livy">Livy</a> (<i>The History of Rome</i>, 45.9.3)
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>From Caranus, the first king, twenty monarchs are enumerated down to <a href="/wiki/Perseus_of_Macedon" title="Perseus of Macedon">Perseus</a>.
</p></blockquote>

<li id="cite_note-3"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-3">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">According to <a href="/wiki/Pausanias_(geographer)" title="Pausanias (geographer)">Pausanias</a> (<i>Description of Greece</i>, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Paus.+9.40&amp;fromdoc=Perseus%3Atext%3A1999.01.0160">9.40, 8-9</a>), Caranus set up a trophy after the Argive fashion for a victory against Cisseus: "The Macedonians say that Caranus, king of Macedonia, overcame  a chieftain in a bordering country in the battle of Cisseus. Caranus realized that it was a mistaken policy to incur the undying hatred of the non-Greeks dwelling around, and so the rule was adopted that no king of Macedonia, neither Caranus himself nor any of his successors, should set up trophies, if they were ever to gain the good-will of their neighbors. This story is confirmed by the fact that Alexander set up no trophies, neither for his victory over Dareius nor for those he won in India."</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-4"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-4">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Plutarch, <i>Alexander</i>, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus%3Atext%3A1999.01.0243%3Achapter%3D2%3Asection%3D1">2.1</a></span>
</li>
