The artistic cultural heritage of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> served as a foundation for later <a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">Western culture</a>, particularly via the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neoclassicism" title="Neoclassicism">Neoclassicism</a> (as exemplified here).</div></div></div>
<p><b>Cultural heritage</b> is the legacy of physical artifacts and intangible attributes of a group or <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a> that is inherited from past generations. Not all legacies of past generations are "heritage", rather heritage is a product of selection by society.<sup id="cite_ref-Logan_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Logan-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cultural heritage includes <a href="/wiki/Cultural_property" title="Cultural property">tangible culture</a> (such as buildings, <a href="/wiki/Monuments" class="mw-redirect" title="Monuments">monuments</a>, landscapes, books, works of art, and artifacts), <a href="/wiki/Intangible_heritage" class="mw-redirect" title="Intangible heritage">intangible culture</a> (such as folklore, traditions, language, and knowledge), and <a href="/wiki/Natural_heritage" title="Natural heritage">natural heritage</a> (including culturally significant landscapes, and <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-repository.jmls.edu_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-repository.jmls.edu-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The deliberate act of keeping cultural and heritage from the present for the future is known as <a href="/wiki/Conservation_(cultural_heritage)" class="mw-redirect" title="Conservation (cultural heritage)">preservation</a> (American English) or <a href="/wiki/Conservation_(cultural_heritage)" class="mw-redirect" title="Conservation (cultural heritage)">conservation</a> (British English), which cultural and historical ethnic <a href="/wiki/Museum" title="Museum">museums</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cultural_center" title="Cultural center">cultural centers</a> promote, though these terms may have more specific or technical meaning in the same contexts in the other dialect. Preserved heritage has become an anchor of the global <a href="/wiki/Tourism_industry" class="mw-redirect" title="Tourism industry">tourism industry</a>, a major contributor economic value to local communities.<sup id="cite_ref-Logan_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Logan-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_ethics_and_rationale_of_cultural_preservation">The ethics and rationale of cultural preservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cultural_heritage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The ethics and rationale of cultural preservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Objects are a part of the study of human history because they provide a concrete basis for ideas, and can validate them. Their preservation demonstrates a recognition of the necessity of the past and of the things that tell its story.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In <i>The Past is a Foreign Country</i>, <a href="/wiki/David_Lowenthal" title="David Lowenthal">David Lowenthal</a> observes that preserved objects also validate <a href="/wiki/Memory" title="Memory">memories</a>. While <a href="/wiki/3D_scanner" class="mw-redirect" title="3D scanner">digital acquisition techniques</a> can provide a technological solution that is able to acquire the shape and the appearance of artifacts with an unprecedented precision<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> in human history, the actuality of the object, as opposed to a reproduction, draws people in and gives them a literal way of touching the past. This unfortunately poses a danger as places and things are damaged by the hands of tourists, the light required to display them, and other risks of making an object known and available. The reality of this risk reinforces the fact that all artifacts are in a constant state of chemical transformation, so that what is considered to be preserved is actually changing – it is never as it once was.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Similarly changing is the value each generation may place on the past and on the artifacts that link it to the past.
</p>

<p>Classical civilizations, and especially the Indian, have attributed supreme importance to the preservation of tradition. Its central idea was that social institutions, scientific knowledge and technological applications need to use a "heritage" as a "resource".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Using contemporary language, we could say that ancient Indians considered, as social resources, both economic assets (like natural resources and their exploitation structure) and factors promoting social integration (like institutions for the preservation of knowledge and for the maintenance of civil order).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Ethics considered that what had been inherited should not be consumed, but should be handed over, possibly enriched, to successive generations. This was a moral imperative for all, except in the final life stage of <a href="/wiki/Sannyasa" title="Sannyasa">sannyasa</a>.
</p><p>What one generation considers "cultural heritage" may be rejected by the next generation, only to be revived by a subsequent generation.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_heritage">Types of heritage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cultural_heritage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types of heritage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_property">Cultural property</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cultural_heritage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cultural property">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Material_culture" title="Material culture">Material culture</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cultural_property" title="Cultural property">Cultural property</a> includes the physical, or "tangible" cultural heritage, such as artworks. These are generally split into two groups of movable and immovable heritage. Immovable heritage includes building so (which themselves may include installed art such as organs, stained glass windows, and frescos), large industrial installations, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://agrawalconstruction.com/sage-heritage/">residential projects</a> or other historic places and <a href="/wiki/Monument" title="Monument">monuments</a>. Moveable heritage includes books, documents, moveable artworks, machines, clothing, and other artifacts, that are considered worthy of preservation for the future. These include objects significant to the archaeology, architecture, science or technology of a specified culture.<sup id="cite_ref-repository.jmls.edu_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-repository.jmls.edu-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Aspects and disciplines of the preservation and conservation of tangible culture include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Museology" title="Museology">Museology</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Archival_science" title="Archival science">Archival science</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Conservation_(cultural_heritage)" class="mw-redirect" title="Conservation (cultural heritage)">Conservation (cultural heritage)</a>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Art_conservation" class="mw-redirect" title="Art conservation">Art conservation</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Archaeological_conservation" class="mw-redirect" title="Archaeological conservation">Archaeological conservation</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Architectural_conservation" title="Architectural conservation">Architectural conservation</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Film_preservation" title="Film preservation">Film preservation</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Phonograph_record#Preservation" title="Phonograph record">Phonograph record preservation</a></li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Digital_preservation" title="Digital preservation">Digital preservation</a></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intangible_culture">Intangible culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cultural_heritage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Intangible culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Anker_Grossvater_erz%C3%A4hlt_eine_Geschichte_1884.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Anker_Grossvater_erz%C3%A4hlt_eine_Geschichte_1884.jpg/220px-Anker_Grossvater_erz%C3%A4hlt_eine_Geschichte_1884.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="154" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Anker_Grossvater_erz%C3%A4hlt_eine_Geschichte_1884.jpg/330px-Anker_Grossvater_erz%C3%A4hlt_eine_Geschichte_1884.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Anker_Grossvater_erz%C3%A4hlt_eine_Geschichte_1884.jpg/440px-Anker_Grossvater_erz%C3%A4hlt_eine_Geschichte_1884.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1579" data-file-height="1105" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Anker_Grossvater_erz%C3%A4hlt_eine_Geschichte_1884.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>The Grandfather tells a story</i>, by <a href="/wiki/Albert_Anker" title="Albert Anker">Albert Anker</a>, ca. 1884.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Intangible_cultural_heritage" title="Intangible cultural heritage">Intangible cultural heritage</a></div>
<p>"Intangible cultural heritage" consists of non-physical aspects of a particular culture, more often maintained by social customs during a specific period in history. The concept includes the ways and means of behavior in a society, and the often formal rules for operating in a particular cultural climate. These include social values and <a href="/wiki/Tradition" title="Tradition">traditions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Convention_(norm)" title="Convention (norm)">customs</a> and practices, <a href="/wiki/Aesthetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Aesthetic">aesthetic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spirituality" title="Spirituality">spiritual</a> beliefs, <a href="/wiki/Artistic_expression" class="mw-redirect" title="Artistic expression">artistic expression</a>, <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a> and other aspects of human activity. The significance of physical artifacts can be interpreted as an act against the backdrop of socioeconomic, political, ethnic, religious and philosophical values of a particular group of people. Naturally, intangible cultural heritage is more difficult to preserve than physical objects.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Aspects of the preservation and conservation of cultural intangibles include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Oral_history" title="Oral history">oral history</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Language_preservation" title="Language preservation">language preservation</a></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_heritage">Natural heritage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cultural_heritage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Natural heritage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Conservation_movement" title="Conservation movement">Conservation movement</a></div>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/Natural_heritage" title="Natural heritage">Natural heritage</a>" is also an important part of a society's heritage, encompassing the <a href="/wiki/Countryside" class="mw-redirect" title="Countryside">countryside</a> and natural environment, including <a href="/wiki/Flora_(plants)" class="mw-redirect" title="Flora (plants)">flora</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fauna_(animals)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fauna (animals)">fauna</a>, scientifically known as <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>, as well as geological elements (including mineralogical, geomorphological, paleontological, etc.), scientifically known as <a href="/wiki/Geodiversity" title="Geodiversity">geodiversity</a>. These kind of heritage sites often serve as an important component in a country's <a href="/wiki/Tourist_industry" class="mw-redirect" title="Tourist industry">tourist industry</a>, attracting many visitors from abroad as well as locally. Heritage can also include cultural landscapes (natural features that may have cultural attributes).
</p><p>Aspects of the preservation and conservation of natural heritage include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Rare_breed_(agriculture)" title="Rare breed (agriculture)">Rare breeds conservation</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Heirloom_plant" title="Heirloom plant">Heirloom plants</a></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_heritage_movement">World heritage movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cultural_heritage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: World heritage movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Plaque_Carthage_Unesco.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Plaque_Carthage_Unesco.JPG/175px-Plaque_Carthage_Unesco.JPG" decoding="async" width="175" height="233" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Plaque_Carthage_Unesco.JPG/263px-Plaque_Carthage_Unesco.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Plaque_Carthage_Unesco.JPG/350px-Plaque_Carthage_Unesco.JPG 2x" data-file-width="768" data-file-height="1024" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Plaque_Carthage_Unesco.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Plaque stating the designation of <a href="/wiki/Carthage" title="Carthage">Carthage</a> as a World Heritage Site.</div></div></div>
<p>Significant was the Convention Concerning the Protection of World Cultural and Natural Heritage that was adopted by the General Conference of <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> in 1972. As of 2011, there are 936 <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Sites" class="mw-redirect" title="World Heritage Sites">World Heritage Sites</a>: 725 cultural, 183 natural, and 28 mixed properties, in 153 countries. Each of these sites is considered important to the international community.
</p><p>The underwater cultural heritage is protected by the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO_Convention_on_the_Protection_of_the_Underwater_Cultural_Heritage" title="UNESCO Convention on the Protection of the Underwater Cultural Heritage">UNESCO Convention on the Protection of the Underwater Cultural Heritage</a>. This convention is a legal instrument helping states parties to improve the protection of their underwater cultural heritage.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition, UNESCO has begun designating <a href="/wiki/Masterpieces_of_the_Oral_and_Intangible_Heritage_of_Humanity" title="Masterpieces of the Oral and Intangible Heritage of Humanity">masterpieces of the Oral and Intangible Heritage of Humanity</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Committee_on_Economic,_Social_and_Cultural_Rights" title="Committee on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights">Committee on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights</a> sitting as part of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Economic_and_Social_Council" title="United Nations Economic and Social Council">United Nations Economic and Social Council</a> with article 15 of its Covenant had sought to instill the principles under which cultural heritage is protected as part of a basic human right.
</p><p>Key international documents and bodies include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Athens_Charter_(Preservation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Athens Charter (Preservation)">Athens Charter</a>, 1931</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Roerich_Pact" title="Roerich Pact">Roerich Pact</a>, 1935</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hague_Convention_for_the_Protection_of_Cultural_Property_in_the_Event_of_Armed_Conflict" title="Hague Convention for the Protection of Cultural Property in the Event of Armed Conflict">Hague Convention for the Protection of Cultural Property in the Event of Armed Conflict</a>, 1954, (with a definition of cultural heritage item adopted by some national law)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Venice_Charter" title="Venice Charter">Venice Charter</a>, 1964</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Barcelona_Charter" title="Barcelona Charter">Barcelona Charter</a>, 2002 (regarding maritime vessel preservation)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/ICOMOS" class="mw-redirect" title="ICOMOS">ICOMOS</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Blue_Shield_International" title="Blue Shield International">The Blue Shield</a>, a network of committees of dedicated individuals across the world that is “<i>committed to the protection of the world’s cultural property, and is concerned with the protection of cultural and natural heritage, tangible and intangible, in the event of armed conflict, natural- or human-made disaster</i>.”</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/International_Institute_for_Conservation" title="International Institute for Conservation">International Institute for Conservation</a></li></ul>
