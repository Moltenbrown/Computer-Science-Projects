<p>An <b>aunt</b> is a person who is the <a href="/wiki/Sister" title="Sister">sister</a> of a parent. Aunts are <a href="/wiki/Second-degree_relative" title="Second-degree relative">second-degree relatives</a> and share 25% genetic overlap when they are the full sister of one of the biological parents. Known alternate terms include Auntie or Aunty. A half-aunt is a half-sister of a parent and is a third-degree relative with 12.5% genetic overlap. An aunt-in-law is a wife of one's uncle, direct genetic overlap will typically be 0%, as this person entered the family through marriage and typically is not a blood relative. A aunt-in-law can also be a aunt of one's spouse. A co-aunt-in-law is a wife of one's uncle of one's spouse.
</p><p>The masculine counterpart of an aunt is an <a href="/wiki/Uncle" title="Uncle">uncle</a>, and the reciprocal relationship is that of a <a href="/wiki/Nephew_and_niece" class="mw-redirect" title="Nephew and niece">nephew or niece</a>.
</p><p>A <b>great-aunt</b><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>/<b>grandaunt</b><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> (sometimes written <b>grand-aunt</b><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>) is the sister of one's grandparent.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aunts_in_popular_culture">Aunts in popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aunt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Aunts in popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="People">People</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aunt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: People">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to the positive image of an old but wise and friendly aunt in many cultures, the word has been used often as an affectionate nickname. Children's TV hosts using "aunt" as their nickname include <a href="/wiki/Hannie_Lips" title="Hannie Lips">Hannie Lips</a> ("Tante Hannie"), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lily_Petersen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lily Petersen (page does not exist)">Lily Petersen</a> ("Tante Lily") and <a href="/wiki/Terry_Van_Ginderen" title="Terry Van Ginderen">Terry Van Ginderen</a> ("Tante Terry"). New Zealand radio presenter <a href="/wiki/Aunt_Daisy" title="Aunt Daisy">Aunt Daisy</a> is another example.
</p><p>Writers using it include <a href="/wiki/Laura_Valentine" title="Laura Valentine">Laura Valentine</a> ("Aunt Louisa"), <a href="/wiki/Harriette_Newell_Woods_Baker" title="Harriette Newell Woods Baker">Harriette Newell Woods Baker</a> ("Aunt Hattie"), Elma Dalhuijsen-Nuis ("Tante Kaat") and <a href="/wiki/Henriette_Roland_Holst" title="Henriette Roland Holst">Henriette Roland Holst</a> ("Tante Jet"). 
</p><p>Musicians using it include Dutch singer Helena Polder (<a href="/wiki/Tante_Leen" title="Tante Leen">Tante Leen</a>), American folk singer <a href="/wiki/Aunt_Molly_Jackson" title="Aunt Molly Jackson">Aunt Molly Jackson</a>, country singer <a href="/wiki/Aunt_Sap" class="mw-redirect" title="Aunt Sap">Aunt Sap</a>, Hawaiian musician <a href="/wiki/Aunty_Genoa_Keawe" class="mw-redirect" title="Aunty Genoa Keawe">Aunty Genoa Keawe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Auntie_Alice_Namakelua" class="mw-redirect" title="Auntie Alice Namakelua">Auntie Alice Namakelua</a> and <a href="/wiki/Auntie_Nona" class="mw-redirect" title="Auntie Nona">Auntie Nona</a>, the rock bands <a href="/wiki/Auntie_Christ" title="Auntie Christ">Auntie Christ</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aunt_Mary" title="Aunt Mary">Aunt Mary</a>, the disco band <a href="/wiki/Aunty_Disco_Project" title="Aunty Disco Project">Aunty Disco Project</a> and the jazz band <a href="/wiki/Aunt_Ruby%27s_Sweet_Jazz_Babies" title="Aunt Ruby&#39;s Sweet Jazz Babies">Aunt Ruby's Sweet Jazz Babies</a>. 
</p><p>Pornographic actress Juliet Anderson named herself <a href="/wiki/Aunt_Peg" class="mw-redirect" title="Aunt Peg">Aunt Peg</a>, while <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> personality Felicia A. O'Dell named herself <a href="/wiki/Auntie_Fee" title="Auntie Fee">Auntie Fee</a>.
</p><p>Due to its inviting image several buildings have also used the word "aunt" in their name, such as the Italian safe house <i><a href="/wiki/Aunt_Anna%27s" title="Aunt Anna&#39;s">Aunt Anna's</a></i> and the Dutch restaurant <i><a href="/wiki/Tante_Koosje" title="Tante Koosje">Tante Koosje</a></i>. Companies like the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Auntie_Beeb" class="mw-redirect" title="Auntie Beeb">Auntie Beeb</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Auntie_Anne%27s" title="Auntie Anne&#39;s">Auntie Anne's</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aunt_Bessie%27s" title="Aunt Bessie&#39;s">Aunt Bessie's</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aunt_Carrie%27s" title="Aunt Carrie&#39;s">Aunt Carrie's</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aunty_Green_Hotel" title="Aunty Green Hotel">Aunty Green Hotel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aunt_Jemima" title="Aunt Jemima">Aunt Jemima</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aunt_Judy%27s_Magazine" title="Aunt Judy&#39;s Magazine">Aunt Judy's Magazine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aunt_Nellie%27s_Red_Cabbage" class="mw-redirect" title="Aunt Nellie&#39;s Red Cabbage">Aunt Nellie's Red Cabbage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spry_Vegetable_Shortening" title="Spry Vegetable Shortening">Spry Vegetable Shortening</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Aunt_Jenny" title="Aunt Jenny">Aunt Jenny</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Aunt_Lute_Books" title="Aunt Lute Books">Aunt Lute Books</a> and <a href="/wiki/Club_Penguin" title="Club Penguin">Club Penguin</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Aunt_Arctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Aunt Arctic">Aunt Arctic</a></i>) use it for the same reasons. An <a href="/wiki/Aunt_Sally" title="Aunt Sally">Aunt Sally</a> is a dummy of an old woman's head, which players throw sticks or battens to.
</p>
