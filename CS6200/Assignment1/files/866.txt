	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Greek East and Latin West</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">&#160;&#160;(Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Greek_East&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Greek East">Greek East</a>)</span></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b><i>Greek East</i> and <i>Latin West</i></b> are terms used to distinguish between the two parts of the <a href="/wiki/Greco-Roman_world" title="Greco-Roman world">Greco-Roman world</a>, specifically the eastern regions where <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> was the <i><a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>) and the western parts where <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> filled this role (The <a href="/wiki/Maghreb" title="Maghreb">Maghreb</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iberia" class="mw-redirect" title="Iberia">Iberia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> and the <a href="/wiki/British_isles" class="mw-redirect" title="British isles">British isles</a>). During the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> a divide had persisted between Latin- and Greek-speaking areas; this divide was encouraged by administrative changes in the empire's structure between the 3rd and 5th centuries, which led ultimately to the establishment of separate administrations for the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Roman_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Roman Empire">Eastern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western</a> halves of the Empire.
</p><p>After the fall of the Western Part, <i>pars occidentalis</i>, of the Empire, the terms <i>Greek East</i> and <i>Latin West</i> are applied to areas that were formerly part of the Eastern or Western Parts of the Empire, and also to areas that fell under the Greek or Latin cultural sphere but that had never been part of the Roman Empire. This has given rise to two modern dichotomies, which are, <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> has been historically split between <a href="/wiki/Western_Christianity" title="Western Christianity">Western Christianity</a> (<a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholicism">Roman Catholicism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Christianity" title="Eastern Christianity">Eastern Christianity</a> (<a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodoxy" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Orthodoxy">Eastern Orthodoxy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodoxy" class="mw-redirect" title="Oriental Orthodoxy">Oriental Orthodoxy</a>, and related traditions). Second, Europeans have traditionally viewed Europe and the Mediterranean as having an East/West cultural split. Cultures associated with the historical <a href="/wiki/Iberians" title="Iberians">Iberians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Goths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungarians" title="Hungarians">Hungarians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lithuanians" title="Lithuanians">Lithuanians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Slavs" title="West Slavs">West Slavs</a> and the historical Roman Catholic Church (Central and Western Europe) have traditionally been considered <i>Western</i>; these cultures adopted Latin as their lingua franca in the Middle Ages. Cultures associated with the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian Empires</a> (<a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greeks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Slavs" class="mw-redirect" title="Orthodox Slavs">Orthodox Slavs</a> (<a href="/wiki/East_Slavs" title="East Slavs">East</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Slavs" title="South Slavs">South Slavs</a>), <a href="/wiki/Romanians" title="Romanians">Romanians</a> and to a lesser extent <a href="/wiki/Albanians" title="Albanians">Albanians</a>) have traditionally been considered <i>Eastern</i>; these cultures all used Greek or Old Church Slavonic as a lingua franca during the early Middle Ages.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_with_regard_to_the_Roman_Empire">Use with regard to the Roman Empire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greek_East_and_Latin_West&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Use with regard to the Roman Empire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">classical context</a>, <i>Greek East</i> refers to the provinces and client states of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> in which the <i><a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a></i> was primarily <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>.
</p><p>This region included the whole Greek peninsula with some other northern parts in the Balkans, the provinces around the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>, those of the Bosphorus, all of <a href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" class="mw-redirect" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a> (in the loosest possible sense, to include <a href="/wiki/Cappadocia_(Roman_province)" title="Cappadocia (Roman province)">Cappadocia</a> and extending to <a href="/wiki/Roman_Armenia" title="Roman Armenia">Armenia Minor</a>), <a href="/wiki/Magna_Graecia" title="Magna Graecia">Magna Graecia</a> (southern part of the Italian peninsula and Sicily), and the other provinces along the eastern rim of the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> (<a href="/wiki/Iudaea_Province" class="mw-redirect" title="Iudaea Province">Judea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syria_(Roman_province)" class="mw-redirect" title="Syria (Roman province)">Syria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyrenaica" title="Cyrenaica">Cyrenaica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Egypt_(Roman_province)" title="Egypt (Roman province)">Egypt</a>). These Roman provinces had been <a href="/wiki/Greek_colonies" class="mw-redirect" title="Greek colonies">Greek colonies</a> or Greek-ruled states during the <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">Hellenistic period</a>, i.e. until the Roman conquests.
</p><p>At the start of <a href="/wiki/Late_antiquity" title="Late antiquity">late antiquity</a>, beginning with the reorganization of the empire's provincial divisions during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Diocletian" title="Diocletian">Diocletian</a> (ruled 284–305), the expression "Greek East" evolved to stand in contradistinction to <i>Latin West</i>. Thereafter, <i>Greek East</i> refers to the Greek-speaking provinces mentioned above (after 395 mostly in the Greek-speaking <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Roman_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Roman Empire">Eastern Roman Empire</a>) in contradistinction to the provinces in Western Europe, <a href="/wiki/Italia_(Roman_province)" class="mw-redirect" title="Italia (Roman province)">Italia</a> (excluding the <a href="/wiki/Catepanate_of_Italy" title="Catepanate of Italy">Catepanate of Italy</a>, where they still spoke Greek) and Northwest Africa (after 395 in the Latin-speaking <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_with_regard_to_Christianity">Use with regard to Christianity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greek_East_and_Latin_West&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Use with regard to Christianity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/East%E2%80%93West_Schism" title="East–West Schism">East–West Schism</a></div>
<p><i>Greek East</i> and <i>Latin West</i> are terms used also to divide <a href="/wiki/Chalcedonian_Christianity" title="Chalcedonian Christianity">Chalcedonian Christianity</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Greek_(language)" class="mw-redirect" title="Greek (language)">Greek</a>-speaking, <a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Orthodox Church">Orthodox</a> peoples of the Eastern <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Basin" title="Mediterranean Basin">Mediterranean Basin</a>, centered on the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>-speaking <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Catholic</a> peoples of <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Here, <i>Latin West</i> applies to regions that were formerly part of the Western Roman Empire, specifically <a href="/wiki/Italia" class="mw-redirect" title="Italia">Italia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gallia</a> (Gaul), <a href="/wiki/Hispania" title="Hispania">Hispania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Africa" class="mw-redirect" title="Northwest Africa">Northwest Africa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Britannia</a>, but also to areas that had never been part of the Empire but which later came under the culture sphere of the Latin West, such as <a href="/wiki/Germania" title="Germania">Magna Germania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hibernia" title="Hibernia">Hibernia</a> (Ireland), <a href="/wiki/Caledonia" title="Caledonia">Caledonia</a> (Scotland). In this sense, the term <i>Latin</i> came to refer to the liturgical and scholarly language of Western Europe, since many of these countries did not actually speak Latin.
</p><p>The term <i>Greek</i> varies in how it is applied. In the most narrow sense, after the rise of the Roman Empire it is only applied to the Eastern Roman (Byzantine) Empire.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Depending on the author it may also be applied to
</p>
<ul><li>Areas where the Greek language was the common scholarly and religious language during classical Roman times and the early Middle Ages, including Syria, Egypt, Palestine, etc.</li>
<li>Areas that have historically been in communion with the (formerly Byzantine) <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Church</a>, which includes <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and much of Eastern Europe, but largely excludes Egypt, Syria, Armenia, etc. which largely opposed the influence of <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> having formed what are now called the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_East" title="Church of the East">Church of the East</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodoxy" class="mw-redirect" title="Oriental Orthodoxy">Oriental Orthodoxy</a>.</li>
<li>Areas that were heavily culturally influenced by the Byzantine Empire, directly or indirectly, during the Middle Ages, including Eastern Europe and the <a href="/wiki/Caliphate" title="Caliphate">Islamic Empires</a>
<ul><li>note: among <a href="/wiki/Muslim_historians" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim historians">Muslim historians</a>, "Greek" and "Roman" are often categories specifically associated with Christians. Though the <a href="/wiki/Sultanate_of_Rum" title="Sultanate of Rum">Sultanate of Rome</a> and, later, the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> would adopt Byzantine titles and describe themselves as rulers of "Rome," this was a geographic designation, much in the same way that the <a href="/wiki/Ghaznavids" title="Ghaznavids">Ghaznavids</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sultanate_of_Delhi" class="mw-redirect" title="Sultanate of Delhi">Delhi Sultans</a> would be known as rulers of "<a href="/wiki/Hindustan" title="Hindustan">Hindustan</a>."</li></ul></li></ul>
<p>The term <i>Latin</i> has survived much longer as a unifying term for the West because the Latin language survived until very recently as a scholarly and liturgical language despite the fragmentation and religious changes in Western Europe. The Greek language, by contrast, died out somewhat quickly in the Arab lands, and the Slavic nations never fully embraced the language despite their long religious affiliation with the Eastern Romans/Byzantines.
</p>
