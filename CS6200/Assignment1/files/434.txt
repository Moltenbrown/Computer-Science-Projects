<p>A <b>household</b> consists of one (or more) people who live in the same <a href="/wiki/Dwelling" title="Dwelling">dwelling</a> and share meals. It may also consist of a single family or another group of people.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> A dwelling is considered to contain multiple households if meals or living spaces are not shared. The household is the basic unit of analysis in many social, <a href="/wiki/Microeconomic" class="mw-redirect" title="Microeconomic">microeconomic</a> and government models,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and is important to <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inheritance" title="Inheritance">inheritance</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Household models include families, <a href="/wiki/Blended_families" class="mw-redirect" title="Blended families">blended families</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roommate" title="Roommate">shared housing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Group_home" title="Group home">group homes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boarding_house" title="Boarding house">boarding houses</a>, <a href="/wiki/House_in_multiple_occupation" title="House in multiple occupation">houses of multiple occupancy</a> (UK), and <a href="/wiki/Single_room_occupancy" title="Single room occupancy">single room occupancy</a> (US). In <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudal</a> societies, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Household" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Household">Royal Household</a> and <a href="/wiki/Medieval_household" title="Medieval household">medieval households</a> of the wealthy included <a href="/wiki/Domestic_worker" title="Domestic worker">servants</a> and other retainers.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_definitions">Government definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Household&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Government definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For statistical purposes in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, a household is defined as "one person or a group of people who have the accommodation as their only or main residence and for a group, either share at least one <a href="/wiki/Meal" title="Meal">meal</a> a day or share the living accommodation, that is, a <a href="/wiki/Living_room" title="Living room">living room</a> or sitting room".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The introduction of legislation to control houses of multiple occupation in the UK Housing Act (2004)<sup id="cite_ref-Housing_Act_2004_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Housing_Act_2004-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> required a tighter definition of a single household. People can be considered a household if they are related: full- or half-blood, foster, step-parent/child, in-laws (and equivalent for unmarried couples), a married couple or unmarried but "living as&#160;..." (same- or different-sex couples).<sup id="cite_ref-www.getrentback.org_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-www.getrentback.org-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census" title="United States Census">United States Census</a> definition also hinges on "separate living quarters": "those in which the occupants live and eat separately from any other persons in the building."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> According to the U.S. census, a householder is the "person (or one of the people) in whose name the housing unit is owned or rented (maintained)"; if no person qualifies, any adult resident of a housing unit is considered a householder. The U.S. government formerly used "head of the household" and "head of the family", but those terms were replaced with "householder" in 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In the census definition of a household, it
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>... includes all the persons who occupy a housing unit. A housing unit is a house, an apartment, a mobile home, a group of rooms, or a single room that is occupied (or if vacant, is intended for occupancy) as separate living quarters. Separate living quarters are those in which the occupants live and eat separately from any other persons in the building and which have direct access from the outside of the building or through a common hall. The occupants may be a single family, one person living alone, two or more families living together, or any other group of related or unrelated persons who share living arrangements. (People not living in households are classified as living in group quarters.)<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>On July 15, 1998, <a href="/wiki/Statistics_Canada" title="Statistics Canada">Statistics Canada</a> said: "A household is generally defined as being composed of a person or group of persons who co-reside in, or occupy, a dwelling."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_definition"><span id="Economic_theories"></span>Economic definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Household&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Economic definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Although a one-income-stream economic theory simplifies modeling, it does not necessarily reflect reality. Many, if not most, households have several income-earning members. Most economic models do not equate households and traditional families, and there is not always a one-to-one relationship between households and families.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_definitions"><span id="Social"></span>Social definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Household&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Social definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Social_work" title="Social work">social work</a>, a household is defined similarly: a residential group in which <a href="/wiki/Homemaking" title="Homemaking">housework</a> is divided and performed by householders. <a href="/wiki/Carer" class="mw-redirect" title="Carer">Care</a> may be delivered by one householder to another, depending upon their respective needs, abilities, and (perhaps) <a href="/wiki/Disability" title="Disability">disabilities</a>. Household composition may affect life and health expectations and outcomes for its members.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-usyd_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usyd-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Eligibility for <a href="/wiki/Home_care" class="mw-redirect" title="Home care">community services</a> and <a href="/wiki/Welfare" title="Welfare">welfare</a> benefits may depend upon household composition.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, household work strategy (a term coined by <a href="/wiki/Ray_Pahl" title="Ray Pahl">Ray Pahl</a> in his 1984 book, <i>Divisions of Labour</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> is the <a href="/wiki/Division_of_labour" title="Division of labour">division of labour</a> among members of a household. Household work strategies vary over the life cycle as household members age, or with the economic environment; they may be imposed by one person, or be decided collectively.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">Feminism</a> examines how <a href="/wiki/Gender_role" title="Gender role">gender roles</a> affect the division of labour in households. In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Second_Shift" title="The Second Shift">The Second Shift</a></i> and <i>The Time Bind</i>, sociologist <a href="/wiki/Arlie_Russell_Hochschild" title="Arlie Russell Hochschild">Arlie Russell Hochschild</a> presents evidence that in two-career couples men and women spend about equal amounts of time working; however, women spend more time on housework.<sup id="cite_ref-Hochschild1_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hochschild1-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hochschild2_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hochschild2-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cathy_Young" title="Cathy Young">Cathy Young</a> (another feminist writer) says that in some cases, women may prevent the equal participation of men in housework and parenting.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Models"><span id="Household_models"></span>Models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Household&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Household models in the <a href="/wiki/English-speaking_world" title="English-speaking world">English-speaking world</a> include traditional and blended families, shared housing, and <a href="/wiki/Group_home" title="Group home">group homes</a> for people with support needs. Other models which may meet definitions of a household include <a href="/wiki/Boarding_house" title="Boarding house">boarding houses</a>, houses in multiple occupation (UK), and <a href="/wiki/Single_room_occupancy" title="Single room occupancy">single room occupancy</a> (US).
</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudal</a> or <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy" title="Aristocracy">aristocratic</a> societies, a household may include servants or retainers who derive their income from the household's principal income.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Housing_conditions">Housing conditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Household&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Housing conditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Belgium"><span id="Historical_housing_conditions_in_Belgium"></span>Belgium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Household&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Belgium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A 1961–62 National Housing Institute survey estimated that 13.8 percent of Belgian dwellings were unfit and incapable of improvement. A further 19.5 percent were unfit but had the potential to be improved, and 54 percent were considered suitable (without alteration or improvement) for modern living standards. Seventy-four percent of dwellings lacked a shower or bath, 19 percent had inadequate sewage disposal, and 3.6 percent lacked a drinking-water supply; 36.8 percent had an indoor <a href="/wiki/Flush_toilet" title="Flush toilet">water closet</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> According to a 1964 study, 13 percent of Belgium's housing consisted of <a href="/wiki/Slum" title="Slum">slums</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="France"><span id="Postwar_housing_conditions_in_France"></span>France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Household&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Between 1954 and 1973, the percentage of French homes with a shower or bath increased from 10 to 65 percent. During that period, the percentage of homes without flush toilets fell from 73 to 30 percent; homes without running water fell from 42 to 3.4 percent. A 1948 law permitted gradual, long-term rent increases for existing flats on the condition that part of the money was spent on repairs. According to <a href="/wiki/John_Ardagh" title="John Ardagh">John Ardagh</a>, the law, "vigorously applied, was partly successful in its twofold aim: to encourage both repairs and new building."<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom"><span id="Postwar_housing_conditions_in_the_United_Kingdom"></span>United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Household&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, a large percentage of British housing was single-family housing. Seventy-eight percent of housing in 1961 consisted of single-family homes, compared to 56 percent in the Netherlands, 49 percent in West Germany and 32 percent in France.<sup id="cite_ref-google4_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-google4-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> In England and Wales in 1964, 6.6 percent of housing units had two or fewer  rooms; 5.8 percent had seven or more rooms, 15.2 percent had six rooms, 35.1 percent had five rooms, 26.3 percent had four rooms, and 11.1 percent had three rooms. These figures included kitchens when they were used for eating meals. Fifty percent of 1964 housing had three bedrooms; 1.9 percent had five or more bedrooms, 6.2 percent had four bedrooms, 10.5 percent had one bedroom or none, and 31.3&#160;percent had two bedrooms. A 1960 social survey estimated that 0.6 percent of households in England and Wales exceeded the statutory overcrowding standard; the 1964 percentage was 0.5 percent. In 1964, 6.9 of all households exceeded one person per room. The 1960 figure was 11 percent, with 1.75 percent having two or more bedrooms below the standard and 9.25 percent having one bedroom below the standard. This declined slightly by 1964 to 9.4 percent of households below the standard, with 8.1 percent having one bedroom below the standard and 1.3 percent having two bedrooms or more below the standard. According to local authorities in 1965, five percent of the housing stock in England and Wales was unfit for habitation.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._and_Canada"><span id="Housing_conditions_in_Canada_and_the_United_States_of_America"></span>U.S. and Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Household&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: U.S. and Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Housing conditions improved in Canada and the U.S. after World War II. In the U.S., 35.4 percent of all 1950 dwellings did not have complete plumbing facilities; the figure fell to 16.8 percent in 1960 and 8.4 percent in 1968. In Canada from 1951 to 1971, the percentage of dwellings with a bath or shower increased from 60.8 to 93.4 percent; the percentage of dwellings with hot and cold running water increased from 56.9 to 93.5 percent.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_30-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> In the United States from 1950 to 1974, the percentage of housing without full plumbing fell from 34 to three percent; during that period, the percentage of housing stock considered dilapidated fell from nine percent to less than four.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
