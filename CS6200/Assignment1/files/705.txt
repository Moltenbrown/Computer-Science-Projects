<p><b>Cultural Christians</b> are <a href="/wiki/Deists" class="mw-redirect" title="Deists">deists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pantheists" class="mw-redirect" title="Pantheists">pantheists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agnostics" class="mw-redirect" title="Agnostics">agnostics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atheists" class="mw-redirect" title="Atheists">atheists</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Antitheists" class="mw-redirect" title="Antitheists">antitheists</a> who adhere to <a href="/wiki/Christian_values" title="Christian values">Christian values</a> and appreciate <a href="/wiki/Christian_culture" title="Christian culture">Christian culture</a>. This kind of identification may be due to various factors, such as family background, personal experiences, and the social and cultural environment in which they grew up.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Contrasting terms are "biblical Christian",<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> "committed Christian",<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> or "believing Christian".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_Europe">Modern Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cultural_Christian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 21st century, outspoken British <a href="/wiki/Atheist" class="mw-redirect" title="Atheist">atheist</a> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Dawkins" title="Richard Dawkins">Richard Dawkins</a> has described himself in several interviews as a "cultural Christian" and a "cultural Anglican".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/President_of_Belarus" title="President of Belarus">President of Belarus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aleksandr_Lukashenko" class="mw-redirect" title="Aleksandr Lukashenko">Aleksandr Lukashenko</a>, has also identified as cultural Christian calling himself an "<a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Christianity" class="mw-redirect" title="Orthodox Christianity">Orthodox</a> <a href="/wiki/Christian_atheism" title="Christian atheism">atheist</a>" in one of his interviews.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Netherlands">The Netherlands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cultural_Christian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Netherlands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The provinces <a href="/wiki/North_Brabant" title="North Brabant">North Brabant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Limburg,_Netherlands" class="mw-redirect" title="Limburg, Netherlands">Limburg</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> are historically mostly <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism_in_the_Netherlands" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholicism in the Netherlands">Roman Catholic</a>, therefore many of their people still use the term and some traditions as a base for their cultural identity rather than as a religious identity. Since the <a href="/wiki/Eighty_Years%27_War" title="Eighty Years&#39; War">War of Independence</a> the Catholics were systematically and officially discriminated against by the Protestant government until the second half of the 20th century, which had a major influence on the economical and cultural development of the southern part of the Netherlands.
</p><p>From the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformation</a> to the 20th century, Dutch Catholics were largely confined to certain southern areas in the Netherlands, and they still tend to form a majority or large minority of the population in the southern provinces of the Netherlands, <a href="/wiki/North_Brabant" title="North Brabant">North Brabant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Limburg_(Netherlands)" title="Limburg (Netherlands)">Limburg</a>.
</p><p>However, with modern population shifts and increasing <a href="/wiki/Secularization" title="Secularization">secularization</a>, these areas tend to be less and less religious Catholic. Since 1960 the emphasis on many Catholic concepts including hell, the devil, sinning and Catholic traditions like confession, kneeling, the teaching of catechism and having the hostia placed on the tongue by the priest rapidly disappeared, and these concepts are nowadays seldom or not at all found in modern Dutch Catholicism. The southern area still has original Catholic traditions including <a href="/wiki/Carnival_in_the_Netherlands" title="Carnival in the Netherlands">Carnival</a>, pilgrimages, rituals like lighting candles for special occasions and field chapels and crucifixes in the landscape, giving the southern part of the Netherlands a distinctive Catholic atmosphere, with which the population identifies in contrast to the rest of the Netherlands. The vast majority of the (self-identifying) Catholic population in the Netherlands is now largely irreligious in practice. Research among Catholics in the Netherlands in 2007 shows that only 27% of the Dutch Catholics can be regarded as <a href="/wiki/Theist" class="mw-redirect" title="Theist">theist</a>, 55% as <a href="/wiki/Ietsism" title="Ietsism">ietsist</a> /<a href="/wiki/Agnostic" class="mw-redirect" title="Agnostic">agnostic</a>/<a href="/wiki/Deist" class="mw-redirect" title="Deist">deist</a> and 17% as atheist.<sup id="cite_ref-Nederland&#39;_2006_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nederland&#39;_2006-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cultural_Christian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In China, the term "Cultural Christian" can refer to intellectuals, openly religious or otherwise,  who are devoted to Christian theology, ethics and literature, and often contribute to a movement known as <a href="/wiki/Sino-Christian_theology" title="Sino-Christian theology">Sino-Christian theology</a>.
</p><p>Traditionally, Christianity has been considered a "foreign religion" (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh">洋教</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>&#58; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">yáng jiào</span></i>, means non-local religions) in China, including all the negative connotations of foreignness common in China. This attitude only started to change at the end of the 20th century. In China, the term "Cultural Christians" (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hans">文化基督徒</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>&#58; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">wénhuà jīdūtú</span></i>) can refer to Chinese intellectuals devoted to the study of Christian theology, ethics, and literature, and often contribute to a movement known as <a href="/wiki/Sino-Christian_theology" title="Sino-Christian theology">Sino-Christian theology</a>. Some of the earliest figures in this movement in the late-1980s and 1990s, such as <a href="/wiki/Liu_Xiaofeng_(academic)" title="Liu Xiaofeng (academic)">Liu Xiaofeng</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=He_Guanghu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="He Guanghu (page does not exist)">He Guanghu</a>, were sympathetic to Christianity but chose not to associate with any local church.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Since the 1990s, a newer generation of these Cultural Christians have been more willing to associate with local churches, and have often drawn on <a href="/wiki/Calvinist_theology" class="mw-redirect" title="Calvinist theology">Calvinist theology</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
